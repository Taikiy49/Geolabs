INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Swimming Pool and Parking Lot Improvements project
located within the Arcadia Retirement Residence near the intersection of Punahou Street
and Interstate Route H 1 in the Makiki area in Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii
The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is located on the eastern flank of Punchbowl Hill The Punchbowl
Hill Puowaina is a tuff cone near the center of Honolulu built against the end of a spur of
the Koolau Range The tuff consists mostly of brown palagonitized vitric ash and lapilli
with scattered fragments of coral limestone and Koolau basalt
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered stiff to very stiff silty soils over medium
dense to dense sandy soils in the borings drilled at the swimming pool site Based on the
generally dense sandy subsoil conditions encountered in our borings we believe that a
shallow foundation system bearing on the dense in situ soils may be used for support of
the new basement walls housing the swimming pool Considering the proximity of the
new structures to the existing building footings for the new basement wall and swimming
pool should not extend below the bottom of footings for the existing building to reduce the
potential for undermining the existing building footings We understand that the bottom of
footings for the existing building is at about elevation 40 feet MSL
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3652 00 Page 8
We understand that the proposed swimming pool will be located at the basement
level at about 10 to 12 feet below the existing ground surface Therefore we anticipate
that an excavation on the order of about 15 feet will be required for the proposed new
basement wall and swimming pool construction Due to the relatively deep excavation
and limited construction space anticipated we believe that temporary shoring of the
excavations will likely be necessary to facilitate construction of the new basement walls
Due to the proximity of the proposed excavation to the existing building footings
ponding of water near the existing footing level may cause saturation and subsequent
loss of strength of the foundation soils below the existing footings Therefore we
recommend that the sides of the excavation at the swimming pool site be protected and
sloped to provide positive drainage during construction to prevent the potential for water
ponding at the excavation bottom It is anticipated that the bottom of the proposed
excavation will be near the bottom of footing elevation for the existing building Therefore
we recommend that settlement markers be installed on the walls of the existing building
prior to commencement of any excavation work to monitor any possible movements of
the existing building The settlement markers should be read throughout the construction
period by a qualified land surveyor

Foundations
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the site we believe that a shallow
foundation system consisting of continuous wall footings bearing on the dense in situ
sands may be used for support of the new basement walls and swimming pool An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 5 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for
the design of foundations bearing on the dense in situ soils This bearing value is for
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3652 00 Page 9
dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as those
caused by wind or seismic forces
Footings for the new basement wall should be embedded a minimum of 30 inches
below the lowest adjacent finish grades Where footings are to be located adjacent to
below grade structures or utilities the footings should extend to a depth below an
imaginary 45 degree plane projected upward from the bottom of the below grade
structure or utility This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below
grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement We also recommend that the new basement walls and swimming pool
footings have a minimum width of 24 inches For the portion of the new swimming pool
wall adjacent to the existing building footings for the new swimming pool walls should not
extend below the bottom of footings for the existing building to reduce the potential for
undermining the existing building footings In addition footings for the new swimming
pool walls should be set back a horizontal distance of at least 3 feet measured from the
outside edges of the new and existing footings The void or gap between the new and
existing footing resulting from the excavation for the new footings should be grouted with
a sand cement grout mixture up to the top of footing elevation of the existing building
The grouting of the void or gap should be done as soon as the new footing is constructed
For footings bearing on the in situ soils the bottom of the footing excavations
should be dense and unyielding prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete If soft
and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations they should
be over excavated to expose the underlying firm dense materials The over excavation
should be backfilled with lean concrete or structural fill material compacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction We recommend that footing excavations be observed
by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete
to confirm the foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths

Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs will be used for the swimming pool deck area
and for walkways on the existing ground surface level In general the concrete slab
subgrade including the bottom of the swimming pool should be scarified to a minimum
depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
For more uniform support of the swimming pool deck and the bottom of the
swimming pool we recommend that the concrete slab be supported on a minimum
4 inch layer of cushion fill The cushion fill layer should consist of open graded gravels
such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation which will also serve as a
capillary moisture break The cushion fill layer should be placed as soon as possible
after the scarification and recompaction of the existing subgrade soils as recommended
above
For concrete slabs located at the existing ground surface level such as the
concrete walkways we recommend that the concrete slab be supported on a 6 inch

Excavation
We understand that the proposed swimming pool deck elevation will be located at
the existing basement level Therefore in order to construct the swimming pool we
anticipate that an excavation on the order of up to about 15 feet below the existing ground

The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the

should be stockpiled no closer to the edge of the excavation than a horizontal distance
equal to the depth of the excavation in order to reduce the potential for ground
movement
Based on our field exploration the project site is underlain by stiff fills and medium
dense to dense sands within the depths of the excavation Due to the limited construction
space and relatively deep excavation anticipated we believe that a temporary shoring
system may be necessary to support the proposed excavation The temporary shoring
support system should conform to the requirements of local state and federal
regulations

about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to
reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the
pipes It is critical that the free draining material be used around the pipes to reduce the
potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support around the sides of the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils or select fill
material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3652 00 Page 19
Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement subgrade should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the new facility should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and
footing foundations
