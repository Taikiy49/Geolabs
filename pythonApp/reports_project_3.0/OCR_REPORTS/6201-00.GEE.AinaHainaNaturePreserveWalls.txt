SECTION 1 GENERAL
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Reconstruction of Retaining Wall Drainage Study for the Aina
Haina Nature Preserve in the Aina Haina area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations retaining
structures and site grading only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 1
Project Considerations
The Aina Haina Nature Preserve encompasses the lower elevation gentle to
moderately inclined hill slopes at the northern end of Wailupe Valley adjacent to the termini
of Hind Iuka Drive and Mona Street in the Honolulu area of the Island of Oahu Hawaii We
understand the City and County of Honolulu acquired a large parcel of undeveloped land
the Aina Haina Nature Preserve northerly of the existing residential development A small
portion of the preserve area about 40 acres encompasses moderately sloping hillside
terrain above a number of existing residential lots on the upslope eastern side of Hind
Iuka Drive and Mona Street The preserve area slopes are assumed to mainly consist of
alluvial and colluvial clay soils with surface and embedded boulders and some areas of
basaltic rock formation
The proposed improvements consist of reconstruction of a retaining wall along the
southern boundary at Hind Iuka Drive The existing CRM retaining
wall is distressed i e irregular alignment leaning etc and seepage water reportedly flows
through and over the wall into the adjacent parcel The City has determined that this
retaining wall should be replaced with a concrete retaining wall This work will include
design of the new wall and construction observations

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age The project site is situated in a valley in the deeply
eroded southern flank of Koolau where chemical weathering in drainageways has
frequently created clay rich soils with relatively high shrink swell potentials Fluvial
processes then move these soils to the toe of the valley walls
2 2
General Soil Conditions
The anticipated surface soils underlying the project site are described by the
U S Soil Conservation Service in their publication Soil Survey of Islands of Kauai Oahu
Maui Molokai and Lanai State of Hawaii 1972 The major soils at the site and some of
their general properties are as follows
Lualualei Series LPE This soil occurs on talus slopes on Oahu and Kauai
Slopes average between about 3 to 35 percent The runoff is slow and these soils
have a slight erosion hazard Their engineering qualities are relatively poor and they
have a high shrink swell potential

Existing Site Conditions
The site is presently vacant land within the existing Aina Haina Nature Preserve The
lot is bordered by Hind Iuka Drive on the west an existing residence on the south and
vacant land to the north and east The site slopes gently up towards the east and the
ground surface is covered with tall grass scattered boulders and occasional trees Ground
surface elevations range from about 277 to about 313 feet above Mean Sea Level

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicates that the site is generally underlain by a surface layer
of expansive soils consisting of very stiff to hard silty clays with some boulders extending to
the maximum depth explored of about 20 feet below the existing ground surface We did
not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the site we believe that shallow
foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the
proposed new retaining walls Foundations for the new walls should bear on new
compacted select granular fills as discussed herein
Based on our laboratory tests the surface clay soils at the site can exhibit moderate
to high shrink swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in soil moisture content
As a result we recommend providing a minimum of 12 inches of non expansive select
granular fill material below the base of the wall foundations Proper surface drainage and
reduction of water infiltration into the subsurface are integral components to the
recommendations provided herein
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils and
new fill materials are critical elements of earthwork Geolabs should perform observations
and soil density tests during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required
degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on each fill lift Our geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of foundations retaining structures
and site grading are further discussed in the following sections

In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades For footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping
ground the footings should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal

loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of permeable material
such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation directly around a
perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of the wall discharging to an
appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product
such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the drainage material The
prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated
pipe at the base of the wall
The backfill should be sloped back away from the wall to a drainage swale to provide
surface drainage
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of select granular fill
material Unless covered by concrete slabs or pavement the upper 12 inches of
backfill should consist of low expansion relatively impervious material to reduce the
potential for water infiltration behind the walls

At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be cleared

Excavation
Based on our exploration at the site conventional earth moving equipment may be
used for the excavation of the wall foundations and subgrade replacement However it
is anticipated that boulders will be encountered during the excavation work Where
boulders result in overbreak below the level of the subgrade replacement the resulting
void should be backfilled with non expansive select granular replacement fill materials
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture placed in level
lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to 90 to 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage
by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling
two borings each extending to a depth of about 20 feet below the existing ground surface
The approximate locations of the test borings and bulk sample are shown on Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a track mounted drill rig equipped with continuous
flight augers
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a near continuous
basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory
Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as
shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1 Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Rock
cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown
on the Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2

ROCK FRACTURE CHARACTERISTICS
The following terms describe general fracture spacing of a rock
Massive Greater than 24 inches apart
Slightly Fractured 12 to 24 inches apart
Moderately Fractured 6 to 12 inches apart
Closely Fractured 3 to 6 inches apart
Severely Fractured Less than 3 inches apart
DEGREE OF WEATHERING
The following terms describe the chemical weathering of a rock
Unweathered Rock shows no sign of discoloration or loss of strength
Slightly Weathered Slight discoloration inwards from open fractures
Moderately Weathered Discoloration throughout and noticeably weakened though not able to break by hand
Highly Weathered Most minerals decomposed with some corestones present in residual soil mass Can be broken by hand
Extremely Weathered Saprolite Mineral residue completely decomposed to soil but fabric and structure preserved
HARDNESS
The following terms describe the resistance of a rock to indentation or scratching
Very Hard Specimen breaks with difficulty after several pinging hammer blows
Example Dense fine grain volcanic rock
Hard Specimen breaks with some difficulty after several hammer blows
Example Vesicular vugular coarse grained rock
