PRELIMINARY GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING EXPLORATION
AIEA INDUSTRIAL SUBDIVISION
LOT NO 6
Dear Mr Criz
This letter report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for Lot No 6 of the Aiea Industrial Subdivision The subdivision is
located within the former Aiea Sugar Mill site located northeast of Ulune Street
northwest of Kulawea Street and southeast of Hakina Street in the District of Aiea on
the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This letter report summarizes our findings and preliminary geotechnical
engineering recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and
engineering analyses These preliminary recommendations are intended for the
preliminary design associated with site grading foundations retaining walls asphalt
pavements and slabs on grade only The findings and preliminary recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Lot No 6 is located at the end of Roads B and C and is located northeast of Lot
and 14 Lot No 6 is the only lot of the industrial subdivision that was not
graded during the recent mass grading operation Lot No 6 is also the only lot of the
industrial subdivision where the buildings that were constructed for the sugar mill were not
demolished It is unknown at this time if the existing structures will be kept intact or
demolished or if new structures will be built on the lot It is also unknown what type of
structure will be built on the lot however we anticipate that some type of warehouse
building will be constructed We anticipate that minor grading less than 2 feet of cuts or
fills will be performed at the site

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
Lot No 6 is a relatively flat lot with pad elevations ranging between about
116 and 121 feet Mean Sea Level MSL At the southwest side of the lot a 2 to 5 foot
high slope exists with a two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V inclination sloping down
toward the property line At the southeast side of the lot a 10 to 15 foot high stream bank
slope exists with a 172H 1V inclination sloping down toward Aiea Stream An old concrete
bridge that crosses over the Aiea Stream exists at the southeast side of the lot At the
northwest and northeast sides of the lot 10 foot high slopes exist with a 2H 1V inclination
sloping up to the property line
Currently the site contains two one story warehouse buildings with high vaulted
ceilings metal frame metal single wall siding and processing tanks at the western end of
the lot Between the two warehouses remnants of some sort of washing facility appear
to be present on the wall of the large warehouse building An asphalt paved driveway
was observed between the two warehouse buildings and along the north east and
south perimeter of the lot
The large warehouse building is about 300 feet by 100 feet in plan dimensions and
appears to have been used for some sugar cane processing operation The floor is
currently covered with thick black molasses The eastern corner of the building is within
the designated flood area
The small warehouse building is about 120 feet by 60 feet in plan dimensions and
appears to have been used for office space and some sugar cane packaging operation
This building appears to be the most recently constructed building on the Aiea Sugar Mill
site
It appears that the lot was constructed primarily with fills of 3 to 5 feet in height
The site contained some grass and weed vegetation especially at the perimeter slopes
at the time of our field exploration

PRELIMINARY ASSESSMENT OF EXISTING STRUCTURES
Large Warehouse Building
The large warehouse building was found to contain an 8 to 8 5 inch thick concrete
slab bearing directly on the on site clayey material The building appears to be in
relatively fair condition with regard to structural integrity of the foundations and slab as
evidenced by the lack of significant concrete cracking However we anticipate that due to
the absence of a capillary break material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No
67 gradation and a moisture vapor membrane moisture infiltration from the ground
through the slab may occur This could pose a problem if floor coverings are placed
directly over the concrete slab especially if floor adhesives are used Note that we do not
recommend that floor coverings be utilized where vehicular traffic will traverse over it
Since the existing slab does not have non expansive soil beneath it we
recommend that a 3 foot wide sidewalk be constructed adjacent to and surrounding the
building A sidewalk with a 12 inch deep thickened edge should help to stabilize the
subgrade soil moisture below the structures and therefore reduce the potential for
swelling shrinking and softening of the subgrade soils
If additional loads are planned for the existing structure we recommend that new
foundations be constructed The new foundations should be constructed in accordance
with the recommendations given in the Site Preparation section of this report

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Shallow Foundations
Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the site we
believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings will likely be suitable for support of
new structures An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot
psf may be utilized for the design of shallow foundations bearing on properly compacted
select granular fill material or recompacted on site material This bearing value is for
those caused by wind or seismic forces

Footings should have a minimum width of 12 inches and should be embedded a
minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade For footings constructed near
tops of slopes or on sloping ground the footings should be embedded deep enough to
provide a minimum horizontal set back distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge
of the footings to the slope face
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations
they should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The
over excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill material compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down
to the underlying competent material
Footings located adjacent to retaining walls should be embedded below a 1H 1V
imaginary plane extended upward from the heel of the retaining wall foundation or the
retaining walls should be designed to resist the additional structural loads
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soil around the
footings is relatively undisturbed or well compacted Unless covered by pavements or
slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected A
coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used between the subgrade soil and concrete
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Inc prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Retaining Walls
In general retaining wall foundations should be designed in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Foundations section of this preliminary letter report
except for the following Wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches and
should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent grade Wall
footings parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed with stepped footings

For the design of free standing retaining walls with level backfill an equivalent fluid
earth pressure of 45 pcf may be utilized Note that free standing walls are defined as
walls that are allowed to rotate about 0 5 percent of the wall height For restrained
retaining walls with level backfill i e split level retaining wall or basement wall an
equivalent fluid earth pressure of 65 pcf may be utilized For sloping backfill conditions
the equivalent fluid earth pressure should be increased by 25 percent
Surcharge and hydrostatic loads are not accounted for in the earth pressures
given Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges live loads and point loads should be
considered in the design
Backfill behind retaining walls should consist of on site soils or imported select
granular fill material compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction
Over compaction of the retaining wall backfill should be avoided
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system consists of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation immediately around
the perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall discharging to appropriate
outlet or weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as
MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used in lieu of the drainage material The prefabricated
drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base
of the wall Unless covered by concrete the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of
impervious material such as the on site clayey materials to reduce the potential for
water infiltration behind the walls

Slabs On Grade
Our field exploration disclosed that Lot No 6 is generally underlain by near surface
clayey soils with moderate expansion potential Unless floor slabs constructed above the
highly expansive soils are properly designed there is a potential for future distress to the
lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of these soils due to
changes in the moisture content To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting
from the swelling of the subgrade soils we recommend that the subgrade soils be
properly prepared and capped with at least 12 inches of non expansive select granular fill
material

Consideration should be given to construction of a 3 foot wide sidewalk adjacent to
and surrounding the structures A sidewalk with a 12 inch deep thickened edge should
help to stabilize the subgrade soil moisture below the structures and therefore reduce the
potentiai for swelling shrinking and softening of the subgrade soils
The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab and

recommendations presented in the Site Grading section of this preliminary letter report
Compaction requirements for the subgrade soils and select granular fill are also
discussed in the same section of this letter report
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a
minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation or Basaltic Termite Barrier BTB be placed below the slabs to serve as
a capillary break and to provide more uniform support of the slabs To reduce the
potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top
of the gravel or BTB cushion layer Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl
should be considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking It is also
recommended that interior walls be designed to incorporate some flexibility to
accommodate a small amount of possible ground movements
For slabs subjected to vehicular traffic the vapor barrier and 4 inch gravel BTB
cushion layer may be omitted For design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade
reaction of 200 pci may be used for the compacted select granular fill material A
minimum slab thickness of 6 inches may be used for preliminary design purposes
Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the slab joints that are
subjected to vehicular traffic The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved area
should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
For exterior slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic the vapor barrier and 4 inch
gravel BTB cushion layer may be omitted Construction joints should be provided at
intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle
intersections

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork vegetation debris rubbish or other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
Soft and yielding areas should be over excavated to expose firm material and the
resulting excavation should be backfilled with properly compacted fill material
The excavated soft soils should be disposed of properly off site
Prior to filling and at finished subgrades the ground should be scarified to a depth
of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction For pavement
subgrades the ground should be scarified to a depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to
at least the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or the subgrade
should be scarified moisture conditioned and recompacted Saturation and subsequent
yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may
require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with properly compacted fill
material Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage
of dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Fills and Backfills
Fill material should consist of well graded on site materials less than 3 inches in
largest dimension and free of vegetation and deleterious materials
Import fill and select granular fill materials should consist of non expansive select
granular material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200
sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell value of 1 percent or less Imported fill materials should be tested by
Geolabs Inc for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project
site for its intended use

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Saturation and subsequent yielding
of the fills due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of
the soft areas and replacement with properly compacted fill material
Base course and select borrow subbase materials should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils are
critical elements of earthwork observations and soil density tests should be performed by
Geolabs Inc during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree
of compaction and the proper moisture content Where compaction is less than required
additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as
necessary to obtain the specified compaction
Slopes
Slopes should not exceed a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical
2H 1V Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design
slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face
Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in
accordance with the above fill placement recommendations

Drainage
The finished grades adjacent to the structures should be sloped to shed water
away from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that
gutter systems be installed around the structures and that the discharge be diverted away
from the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the
foundations and slabs should be avoided Planters next to foundations should also be
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration
into the subsoils The existing drainage swales should be kept intact and maintained
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength of the near surface soils The foundations
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from walls and
foundations
