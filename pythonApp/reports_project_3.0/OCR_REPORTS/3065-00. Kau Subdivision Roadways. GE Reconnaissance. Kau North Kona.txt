INTRODUCTION
In accordance with your request we have performed a geotechnical engineering
reconnaissance for the proposed subdivision road alignments at the Kau Subdivision site
This report presents our findings and recommendations
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to make visual observation and to obtain an
overview of the surface soils and rock conditions along the proposed road alignments in
order to provide data for the formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations
pertinent to the design of the proposed road grading and infrastructure installation

1 Visual reconnaissance of the proposed subdivision road alignments by a

recommendations pertinent to the design of the proposed subdivision roads
and appurtenant utility installation

GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located on the westerly side of Mamalahoa Highway The site
is raw land which was used for pasture of cattle Generally the site is vegetated with
dryland forest and an understory of grasses and shrubs
The terrain generally slopes down in a westerly direction at an average slope ratio
of about five horizontal to one vertical 5H 1V Locally the terrain is rugged with some
steep slopes approaching a ratio of one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V The terrain
typically reflects the original surface of thick basaltic lava flows in both the a a and
pahoehoe forms The ground surface elevations range from about 950 feet Mean Sea
Level MSL at the lower end of the site to approximately 1 825 feet MSL at Mamalahoa
Highway Drainage trends towards the west
Numerous cavities in the rock formation were noted during our field
reconnaissance Of particular note was a large collapse feature or sinkhole at about
Station 78 00 of Road A

Regional Geology
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai one of the five 5
basaltic shield volcanoes which built the island of Hawaii Shield volcanoes are
characterized as being low dome shaped mountains built by quiescent eruptions of very
fluid lavas The general slope of the flanks of the domes range from about five 5 to
about fifteen 15 degrees above horizontal
Hawaiian volcanic activity is typified by an abundance of flank eruptions along rift
zones or zones of weakness in the earth s crust The northwest rift zone of Hualalai is
situated to the north of the site
The project site is underlain by prehistoric basaltic lava flows of the Hualalai
Volcanic Series which were most likely deposited during the late Pleistocene Stearns and
Macdonald 1946
At and in the area of the project site the lava flows are in both the a a and
pahoehoe forms The a a lava is characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly surface of
fragmental clinker underlain by a dense core of massive basalt which frequently has a
thinner less continuous layer of clinker below the massive core
Visual observations of exposures of existing road cuts indicate that the clinker
layers may be as much as ten feet 10 thick with discontinuous ledges of hard basalt
interbedded within the clinker
The pahoehoe flows are characterized by a ropy or billowy surface texture The
rock fabric is usually vesicular and may grade with depth to a dense massive core Lava
tubes or cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our reconnaissance and analyses it is our opinion that development of
the proposed subdivision roads and infrastructure installation is feasible from a
geotechnical engineering point of view The basalt formation encountered at the site
should provide adequate bearing support for the proposed roads and infrastructure The
excavated volcanic materials should provide a suitable source of fill material if they are
processed to reduce the large size fragments to less than 12 inches in size
Site Preparation
Prior to commencement of grading all areas within the roadway and right of way
grading limits should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting
grub and spoil material should be disposed of properly off site or stockpiled for use in
landscaping
Due to the dry and sometimes windy site conditions moisture conditioning of fill
material will be necessary It is also advisable to institute a dust control program during
grading operations
Soft or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below any areas
designated to receive fill should he over excavated to firm natural material and the
resulting depression backfilled with well compacted engineered fill
Fill Placement
In general the excavated and processed rock materials broken down to less than
12 inches in size may be suitable as a source of fill material The basaltic rock material
if reduced to less than six inches 6 in size should be adequate as a source of trench
and structural backfill if needed Imported material if required should conform to the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3065 00 Page 6
requirements stipulated in Section 30 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works
Construction Select Borrow for Subbase Course dated September 1986
Consideration should be given to mobilizing a crusher to the site to aid in reducing
the size of large boulders and fragments It is possible that portions of the dense ledges
could yield aggregate quality material if properly segregated and crushed
if the volcanic rock materials encountered at the site are processed and reduced
to a relatively well graded select granular material with an average maximum size of about
six inches 6 and an absolute maximum size of 12 inches the fills and embankments
may generally be considered to be equivalent to earthfill In this case no special choking
layer would be required if some losses of topsoil are considered acceptable
Generally fills required should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of the maximum dry density
as established by ASTM D 1557 91 Trench and structural backfills should be placed in
level lifts not exceeding eight inches 8 in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent of the maximum dry density per ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 The
upper two feet 2 of fills and backfills below road subgrade level should be compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent of the maximum dry density
Alternately large boulders or rock fragments could be utilized as rockfills or
boulder fills provided that they are not nested and that they are well tracked with a heavy
bulldozer to lock the material into grain to grain contact A choking layer of at least
18 inches thick for yard areas and a minimum of 24 inches thick for road or structural
areas should be used to cap the rockfill to reduce the potential of loss of fines from the
upper portion of the fill The choking layer should consist of a relatively well graded select
granular material with an average maximum size of about six inches 6 and an absolute
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3065 00 Page 7
maximum size of 12 inches However care must be exercised to avoid placement of any
boulder fill in locations which would conflict with future utility lines In general boulder or
rock fragments larger than 24 inches in size should be placed in deep fill embankment
sections and at depths at least four feet 4 below finish subgrade level
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
excavated volcanic material Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of passes
of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be conducted
at the start of the grading phase of the project On the basis of this testing program the
number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction It
is estimated that a minimum of six passes would be needed using a heavy vibratory
compactor

Excavation
The field reconnaissance and visual observations made at the site and in its vicinity
indicate that the site is underlain by basalt formation at shallow depths It is anticipated
that some hard excavation and or ripping may be encountered in localized cemented
areas or when ledges are encountered Cemented clinker and ledge basalt formation will
most likely require some hard excavation such as heavy ripping hoerams or blasting
Generally the excavation method should be left to the contractor Blasting should
be considered to expedite the excavation process Generally consideration should be
given to the economics of blasting to initially reduce the size of the excavated rock

Earthwork Quantities
Based on previous experience in the vicinity of the project site it is anticipated that
the rock materials at the project site will experience a volume change when excavated
processed and used as fill material In general the net change will depend upon whether
the material is loose clinker versus dense basalt the average particle size of the fill
material and the amount of void space in the fill
Discussions with contractors whom have worked with similar materials indicated
that the volume change can range from a factor of about 0 75 for loose clinker to as high
as 1 5 for massive dense basalt The increase would be the greatest when the excavation
encounters a significant amount of dense basalt and this material is used as boulder fill

Infrastructure Installation
It is anticipated that underground utilities will be installed as part of the
development work Based on the results of our field exploration it is anticipated that
relatively hard excavation will be required for utility line trenching

installation However the increased width of the excavated utility trench may result in

the infrastructure systems
Trench shoring design and installation should be left to the contractor However

Cavity Detection
As discussed above numerous cavities were noted during our field
reconnaissance Subsurface cavities and other voids are commonly encountered in
basalt formations To reduce the possibility of foundation or structural settlements
resulting from the collapse of such near surface voids we recommend that a program of
probing and grouting be considered for the project
In general cavity probes for foundation areas should be drilled at each column
location and on ten foot 10 centers for line footings The probe holes should be a
minimum of three inches 8 in diameter and should extend a minimum of ten feet 10
below the footing subgrade The holes should be backfilled with sand cement grout
injected at low pressures
Road subgrades should be probed at ten foot 10 centers across the right of way
and along the road alignment If a cavity or void is suspected additional probes should
be drilled to attempt to delineate the extent of the suspected void
In order to avoid having to drill the probes inside the footing excavations which
would increase drilling costs it is suggested that the probing and grouting work be
performed after either site clearing or site grading but prior to the commencement of
excavation work
The probing and grouting operations should be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii to see that the above recommendations are properly implemented and
to evaluate other solutions to backfill potential voids
As an alternate to conventional probing and grouting potential cavities could be
detected by geophysical methods Our own experience indicates that ground penetrating
radar can be an effective method of detecting cavities in basalt formations at the roadway
subgrade elevations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3065 00 Page 14
This method uses impulse radar technology to obtain a near continuous profile of
the subsurface The impulse propagates through the subsurface materials until it
encounters a soil interface void or embedded object Depending upon the dielectric
constant of the interface void or object a portion of the signal is reflected back This
reflected signal can be intercepted recorded and interpreted
Strong reflections show up as anomalies within the recorded data field Anomalies
may be interpreted as interfaces between materials of different consistency composition
or structure Generally strong anomalies within consolidated coral formations may be
considered to be voids
The site could be geophysically surveyed to map any strong anomalies along the
road alignments Generally the surveys should be performed along both sides of the
road centerline For fill areas the geophysical work should be conducted after the road
area has been prepared but prior to fill placement For cut areas the geophysical
surveys should be conducted when the road is brought to subgrade level
