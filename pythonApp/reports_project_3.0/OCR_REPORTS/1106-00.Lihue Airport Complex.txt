This report presents the results of the additional field
California Bearing Ratio CBR tests performed at the above
referenced project site
In our original field investigation the field CBR test values
were generally variable with approximately half of the values
near 5 and half greater than about 15 There did not appear to
be a trend in the values in terms of horizontal or vertical
location in the apron area Accordingly we had used the lower
CBR value of 5 in the pavement design
Since the CBR value directly affects the thickness of the air
craft pavement section additional field CBR testings were per
formed to help delineate possible trends or sections where a
thinner pavement section might be utilized
Our work was done in general accordance with the scope outlined
in our proposal dated August 3 1982

Field CBR Testing

vated to depths of 3 to 7 feet at a level close to the proposed
subgrade elevation The bottom of the pits were further exca
vated by hand to remove any material disturbed by the backhoe
operation prior to testing
The material encountered in the first seven test pits was a
stiff residual reddish brown clayey silt soil Test pit 8
encountered dark brown clayey silt Ground water was not
encountered in any of the pits

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
ALUN
The recent test results and the test results from the original
investigation were combined in the determination of a design
CBR value per FAA and Asphalt Institute methods Where more
than one field CBR test was performed in a test pit an average
value was used to determine the design CBR values for the
on site subgrade soil
The FAA method presented in the Advisory Circular
recommends a design CBR value which is one standard deviation
below the mean of the test values The mean value of the com
bined data is 12 7 with a standard deviation of 7 7 There
fore using this method the design CBR value would be 5
The Asphalt Institute method presented in Full Depth Asphalt
Pavements for Air Carrier Airports MS 11 dated January 1973
utilizes a satistical plot to select the CBR value that is
equal to or less than 85 percent of the test values The plot
is shown on Plate 4 Based on this method a new design CBR of
6 is obtained

Page 3
It is our opinion that a higher design CBR of 6 obtained from
the Asphalt Institute method is appropriate and shall be used
for the in situ soils
According to the FAA pavement design criteria material with a
CBR value of 6 must be at least 25 inches below the surface of
the final pavement section We believe that by recompacting
the subgrade surface the soils within a depth of 12 inches can
be improved to a higher density to obtain a CBR value of 10
Based on the present availability of heavy compactors we
believe this could be achieved by proofrolling the subgrade
soil and compacting it to a minimum of 95 of its maximum dry
density Assuming that the CBR 6 material will be at least 12
inches below the subgrade level and overlain by compacted
material with a CBR value of at least 10 then the pavement may
be designed using a higher CBR value of 10 with a corresponding
subgrade modulus of 200 psi inch

SITE CONDITIONS
Surface Condition
The site is located on the outskirts of Lihue on the eastern coastline of
Kauai Hawaii The site for the new airport complex is situated to the
southwest of the existing airport and is bounded by Kapule Highway on the
west Ahukini Road to the north the present Taxiway A to the east and cane
fields to the south
The proposed terminal apron will be situated on the southeastern side of the
future airport complex near and parallel to the existing Taxiway A
The area is presently cultivated with sugar cane by the Lihue Plantation Co
Ltd There are two cane haul roads extending into the area Irrigation
ditches cross the site in many directions leaving soft wet areas In addi
tion many weathered basalt boulders were noted along the existing irrigation
ditches
The ground surface elevations generally vary from about 142 to 160 feet
Geology
The island of Kauai was built up by a single large shield volcano During
the Tertiary period basaltic lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series
built a roughly circular island to a height over 17 000 feet above the sur
rounding sea floor Near the end of the Tertiary Period this main shield
building volcanic phase came to a halt
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1106 00 Page 5
Following a long period of erosion and subsidence volcanism was renewed
during the Pleistocene epoch producing the Koloa Volcanic Series These
post erosional volcanics laid a veneer of fresh lavas over the older Waimea
Canyon Series across most of the eastern half of the island
After the cessation of this renewed volcanism the geologic forces of wea
thering erosion and deposition during the late Pleistocene and the Recent
epochs have shaped the island to its present form
At the site the lava rocks of this post erosional volcanic series have been
deeply weathered to form a mantle of stiff reddish brown residual clayey silt
soils

Subsurface Conditions
In general the subsurface conditions consist of a thin layer of topsoil over
a relatively thick mantle of stiff residual soil of reddish brown clayey silt
The topsoil at the surface consists of dark brown Slightly organic clayey
silt The topsoil has been tilled and plowed over and intermixed with some
decayed and burnt vegetation and other organic matter from the cultivation
activities The topsoil layer is estimated to be about 1 foot thick
The residual soil of reddish brown clayey silt below the topsoil layer is
very stiff to hard at shallow depth however it grades somewhat less stiff
with depth generally at 10 to 15 feet Generally the moisture contents tend
to increase and densities tend to decrease with depth In general this trend
appears to stabilize below a depth of about 15 feet The in place clayey
silt soil generally exhibits low swelling potential However some of the
laboratory tests indicate that if the soil is recompacted at moistures below
the optimum moisture content the compacted soil may have moderate swelling
potential

respectively

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration disclosed that the site is underlain by stiff to hard
residual soil The upper 10 to 15 feet is generally harder with standard
penetration resistances of over 30 blows per foot Below the upper hard
crust the soil grades to a stiff consistency with standard penetration
resistances of 10 to 20 blows per foot The finished apron and taxiway
grades will be about 1 to 10 feet below the existing grade and therefore
should encounter stiff residual soil suitable for pavement support
The rough grading for the apron and taxiway areas will be performed under a
separate contract K 1072 in conjunction with the rough grading for the
terminal building parking areas and access roads The rough grading will
consist of cuts from 1 to 10 feet deep to the planned top of pavement eleva
tion No fills are anticipated in the apron and taxiway areas
The condition of the subgrade soils is a critical consideration in the
pavement design due to the relatively heavy aircraft traffic loading To
minimize the pavement section for this project special subgrade preparation
and construction sequence are necessary

Utilizing the FAA Advisory Circular 6C method the

We believe that a design CBR of 11 would be appropriate for com

The laboratory compacted samples also indicated that the perfor
mance of the soils are sensitive to moisture content The soil
samples compacted several percent drier than the optimum mois
ture content 31 exhibited moderate swelling potential The
natural moisture content of the in situ soil is generally about
55 Therefore if the natural soils are not allowed to dry
below the optimum moisture content the swelling potential can be
minimized
Based on the above findings two approaches to the pavement
design have been evaluated First the higher laboratory design
CBR of 11 could be utilized in the pavement design if the soils
below the subgrade level are prepared in the following manner

a Undercut the subgrade to a depth of 6 inches and

b Scarify this resulting surface to a depth of

A second alternative would be to utilize the lower in situ CBR

opinion that by recompacting the subgrade surface the soils

Pavement Drainage
eke ee ee
Surface runoff from the project and pavement areas should be
collected and drained to a suitable discharge point Water
should not be allowed to pond immediately adjacent to the
pavement areas or on top of the embankment slopes The site
should be graded to provide positive drainage away from these
areas
Subsurface drainage is also very critical for the proposed
airport pavement Although the on site residual soils in
general are relatively impermeable the field exploration and
our previous experience at the runway construction indicated that
permeable zones may be encountered in the deeper cut areas We
anticipate that some amount of surface water may infiltrate
through the pavement section
In order to minimize the build up of water beneath the pavement
and the subsequent softening of the clayey subgrade soils a
subdrainage system at the subgrade level is recommended around
the entire apron pavement area to intercept and drain subsurface
water
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1106 00 Page 15
The purpose of the proposed subdrains along the higher north
western and southwestern edges of the apron is to intercept
possible subsurface seepage before it reaches the apron pave
ment The subdrains along the lower northeastern and south
eastern edges of the apron is to provide a drainage outlet for
water accumulated below the pavement section During construc
tion careful control of the subgrade elevation in the apron area
should be exercised to insure that there is sufficient slope to
allow water to flow into the subdrainage system Areas of over
excavation below the subgrade could cause future ponding under
the pavement section This should be avoided by proper grading
control and adequate backfill
The future terminal building will require a drainline along the
northwestern side of the apron It would be preferable to incor
porate a deep subdrainage system in this drainline trench back
i1l to intercept other possible subsurface seepage If this is
utilized a subdrain along the northwestern side of the apron may

Site Grading
The site grading work for this apron and taxiway project will consist of
additional cuts from the finished pavement elevation prepared under Contract
K 1072 to the subgrade level
The surface of the subgrade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 of its
maximum dry density established by ASTM D 1557 70
As discussed in the previous section it is critical that the subgrade
material be kept moist and not allowed to dry out below the optimum moisture
content in order to minimize the swelling potential The contractor should
only be allowed to expose the portion of the subgrade which can be prepared
and covered with the pavement base course layer on that same day
Soft spots encountered at the subgrade level should be removed and replaced
with on site soil compacted to a minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density
If boulders are encountered near the subgrade level they should be removed
for a minimum depth of 12 inches below the subgrade level and the resulting
excavation backfilled with soil compacted to 95 of its maximum dry density
Drainline
A new 36 to 42 inch reinforced concrete pipe drain is planned parallel to and
between the new aircraft apron and existing Taxiway A

The invert of the proposed pipe will be about 10 to 15 feet below the exist
ing ground surface Stiff residual soil with occasional boulders is antici
pated in the trench excavation Although we believe that ground water would
not be a major problem during the construction occasional seepage may be
encountered in deep excavations The surface drainage should be controlled
to prevent rain runoff entering into the excavations
Granular material such as sand should be used for the backfill up to about
one foot above the pipe to reduce the compactive effort required and the
possibility of damaging the pipe
Most of the excavated on site soil may be re used as the upper portion of the
trench backfill from the level one foot above the pipe to subgrade or
finished grade Backfill should be mechanically compacted to a minimum of
90 of its maximum dry density established by ASTM D 1557 The backfill
within 12 inches of subgrade should be compacted to 95 of its maximun dry
density
Where future structures or pavement sections are not anticipated the
backfill compaction requirement may be reduced if desired
