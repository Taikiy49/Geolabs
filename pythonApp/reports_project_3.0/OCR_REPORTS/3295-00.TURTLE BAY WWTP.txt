INTRODUCTION
In accordance with the request of we have conducted a field
observation of the standby injection wells for the Turtle Bay Wastewater Treatment
Plant
Our work was performed in general accordance with our revised fee proposal
dated March 10 1994
This report summarizes our findings from the site observation and short term
injection tests performed on the injection wells
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Turtle Bay Wastewater Treatment Plant injection wells are utilized for the
disposal of secondary treated effluent generated within the Turtle Bay resort
development The majority of the effluent treated at the plant is used for irrigation of
the resort golf course within the Turtle Bay complex Therefore the wells are used for
injection of treated effluent only on a standby basis when the effluent cannot be utilized
for irrigation purposes
As a condition of the Underground Injection Control UIC permit issued for the
facility UIC Permit No UO 1532 it is required that periodic monitoring of the wells be
made by a professional engineer or geologist familiar with injection well operation This
observation is intended to ascertain that the wells remain operational

GENERAL INFORMATION
The facility had been granted a permit to operate injection wells under the
Underground Injection Control program In this permit the facility is identified by
Facility ID No and the wells are identified as Wells A B and C
The Turtle Bay Wastewater Treatment Plant is a privately owned treatment
facility that is used for the treatment of sanitary wastes generated in the Turtle Bay
resort development The plant is generally of the aeration lagoon type and has a
design capacity of about 1 32 million gallons per day mgd The peak hydraulic
capacity is however currently only two of the four ponds are being utilized
with a peak hydraulic capacity of At present the average daily flow through
the plant is about 200 000 gallons per day of domestic wastewater The current
average daily irrigation requirement of the G 2 Links Course is approximately 400 000
gallons per day
The plant is located mauka or southerly of Kamehameha Highway Effluent is
piped to the golf course area which is makai or northerly of the highway for disposal
The wells are used only on a standby basis The primary method of disposal is
by irrigation of the resort golf course area Links Golf Course G 2 If necessary it is
also possible to divert effluent for the irrigation of the Turtle Bay Golf Course G 1
The current irrigation needs of Golf Course G 2 average approximately 400 000 gallons
per day This exceeds the amount of treated effluent presently available

Well Site Conditions
The wells are located on the easterly side of the tee boxes for the 10th Hole of
Golf Course G 2 The wells are situated in an open grass area between the tees and
the moat around Punahoolapa Marsh See Photo No 1 of Appendix C
The weil heads are enclosed in below ground vaults approximately 4 feet by
4 feet in plan dimension The vaults are covered with hinged steel plate doors
Inside of the vaults the well heads consist of steel tees with plates bolted to
their tops for security and to allow the build up of additional head if needed These
plates were removed by plant representatives for the inspection and performance of the
injection tests

Injection System
The injection system is regulated by valves which can be used to isolate the
wells so that any one of the wells or any combination of wells may be used for
injection At the time of our site visit Wells A and B had been used most recently for
the injection of effluent
The system is also equipped with a totalizing meter This meter measures all
flow to the well field rather than to individual wells Flows to individual wells may be
determined by records of which valves are open during injection periods

Short Term Injection Tests
The short term injection tests were performed by injecting treated effluent from
the treatment plant into each well separately for a period of about 8 minutes Each
well was isolated from the others by adjusting the valves controlling the effluent flow
During injection the head level response was measured and recorded Readings from
the totalizing meter were recorded prior to and after each short term injection test to
obtain the total quantity of injected effluent and to estimate the average flow rate into
each well
In general it was observed that the operation of the wells are satisfactory The
average injection rates ranged from about 450 to 619 gallons per minute gpm During
injection at these rates the head levels in Wells A and C were observed to rise steadily
to nearly the top of the well casing within the 8 minute test duration However the
head level in Well B appeared to stabilize about 6 inches below the top of the well
casing This indicates that Well B is the more permeable well of the three Appendix

Recommendations
To facilitate future inspections of the wells we recommend that inspection ports
be placed in the plates on the tops of the well heads This would eliminate the need
to remove the top plates The inspection port should be a minimum of 2 inches in
diameter i e two inch pipe to allow the use of a bailer inside the well
To maintain the injection capacity of the wells we recommend that an operation
and maintenance program be instituted There is a tendency for injection wells to clog
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3295 00 Page 9
over time as a result of suspended solids in the effluent and biological action in the
wells In general to reduce the level of biological activity the use of the wells should
be rotated so that the wells are allowed to rest between extended periods of injection
To partially restore capacity lost by the introduction of suspended solids and gas
accumulation due to biological activity we recommend that the wells be redeveloped
on a periodic basis It is our opinion that airlift pumping could be effective for
redevelopment of the wells Other methods of redevelopment such as jetting or
scrubbing the interior of the well may be too abrasive and may damage the well screen
The method of redevelopment should be carefully evaluated by a professional
experienced with the available well development methods and the construction of
injection wells
Redevelopment should be scheduled and conducted based in part on periodic
field observations recorded by Treatment Plant personnel of the system operation and
head levels within the wells during injection In addition the results of our biannual
short term injection testing of the wells may be used to aid in determining whether the
injection wells could be experiencing a reduced capacity between inspection visits
Generally redevelopment should be conducted if observed head levels indicate an
average increase of about 10 to 15 percent over conditions established after a
redevelopment event
