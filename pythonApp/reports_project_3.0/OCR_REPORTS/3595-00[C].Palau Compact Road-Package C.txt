Based on our literature research site reconnaissance and field exploration
performed for Package C of the proposed Palau Compact Road project we anticipate
that the proposed roadway alignments will traverse various volcanic sedimentary and
alluvial agoonal deposits ranging from very dense volcanic breccia and hard tuff rock
formation to medium stiff to very stiff saprolite and old alluvial soils and to very soft
and or loose recent alluvial mangrove swamp and coralline lagoonal deposits
Based on our field exploration the primary geotechnical engineering
considerations for design of the proposed roadway project include slope failure or
block type landslides of the cut slopes potential for rock excavation high moisture
contents of the in situ soils seepage of groundwater settlement of the fill materials due to
self weight settlement due to soft soil conditions erosion control pavement subgrade
stabilization and design of bridge foundations Special attention should be given to the
recommendations presented in this report to reduce the impacts of these geotechnical
engineering considerations on the proposed roadway project

A Geography
The Palau Islands form a curved chain of islands totaling about 350 in number
and covering approximately 458 square kilometers in area The island chain extends
approximately 161 kilometers in length and 64 kilometers in width Babeldaob the
largest island of the chain is an elongated volcanic island trending north to south and
encompasses about 208 square kilometers of land area Elevations on Babeldaob
range from sea level along the coastal areas to about 242 meters Mean Sea Level
MSL at Mt Ngerekelehuus on the northwestern side of Babeldaob
The Palau Reef encircles the island of Babeldaob as a barrier and fringing reef
creating extensive quiet water lagoons and bays with some deep channels and
passages adjacent to the island Beyond the fringing reef the ocean floor drops
steeply to greater than 300 meters in depth within a distance of about 1 kilometer
The island of Babeldaob is primarily of volcanic origin with some localized
occurrence of limestone rock formation particularly in southern coastal Babeldaob
The relief is generally characterized by hilly terrain of elongated ridgelines frequently
incised by streams and gullies exposing extremely weathered volcanic earth materials
Flatter alluvial areas are restricted mainly to the mouths and banks of larger streams
and along the coastline situated behind sandy beaches where inland hills do not
abruptly terminate at the shoreline More rugged terrain consisting of sharp ridges and
cliff lines occur on the western and southeastern portions of the island Both rounded
hilltops and sharp ridgelines rise above the generally rolling terrain at scattered
locations offering prominent geographic landmarks among the generally homogenous
backdrop
The coastline of Babeldaob is irregularly shaped with many small bays and
elevated headlands Swamp areas consisting of dense mangrove within shallow
tidal influenced mudflats exist throughout approximately 70 percent of the coastline
areas The width of the mangrove swamps along the coastline varies from less than

B Climate
The Palau Islands have a maritime tropical rainy climate characterized by small
seasonal changes Temperatures are generally uniform throughout the year with
abundant rainfall reaching a maximum in July and a minimum from February through
April The mean annual temperature is about 27 degrees Celsius with a relative
humidity of about 82 percent
Rainfall typically occurs as intense showers of relatively short duration and
averages about 250 to 500 mm per month Periods of prolonged heavy rainfall lasting
several days at a time occur as typhoon storm centers frequently pass to the north of
Palau during the summer months Other pronounced rainy periods occur as the
equatorial convergent zone advances northward within the vicinity of Palau around
June and July and again in September and October as the convergent zone retreats
southward

The Philippine Sea and the Pacific Ocean Basin are separated by a series of
elongated submarine ridge structures The Palau Islands are situated at the exposed
crest of the southwestern most submarine ridge of the area The Palau Ridge is part of

A Saprolite Soil Extremely Weathered Rock
Saprolite is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks and is composed
mainly of silty materials The saprolite soil unit is the most dominant soil type traversed
by the West Route and covers approximately half of the East Route of the Palau Compact
Road This soil unit consists predominantly of reddish brown mottled with tan purple
and gray clayey silt and silty clay soils The saprolite soils may contain variations in
color tan grayish white or pinkish tan with varying degrees of mottling or colored
striations depending on the type of parent volcanic rock from which the soils are derived
and the degree of chemical weathering that the material has experienced The soils
are essentially derived from basaltic and andesitic breccia and tuff formations which
may contain intrusive rock bodies of variable composition
Bauxite gravel deposits consisting of a loose surface cover of brown and red
bauxite concretions are often found mantling the residual and saprolite soils Based on
our observations the bauxite gravels are generally confined to the drier upper slopes
and ridgelines of sparse vegetation The deposits are generally absent on lower hill
slopes and areas covered by dense forest vegetation The absence of bauxite gravel in
these areas may be attributed to higher soil moisture content and accelerated chemical
weathering processes The bauxite gravel is generally high in iron and aluminum
content The thickness of the bauxite deposits vary but are generally less than about
300 mm however local deposits in the vicinity of the abandoned mine sites at the
northern half of the West Route may be greater than 2 meters in thickness

boulders or zones of less weathered rocky material localized intrusive volcanic bodies
such as dikes and sills and irregular transitions to underlying rock formation may be
embedded within the saprolite soil type The occurrence of these rock zones within the
saprolite are common but they may be difficult to locate by surface observation and
may not be encountered by test borings

B Swamp Mangrove and Recent Alluvial Deposits
Based on their characteristics and modes of deposition the swamp soils and
recent alluvial soils are closely related to each other in physical properties Both of
these soils generally exist as under consolidated soft to medium stiff deposits as a
result of their sedimentary deposition The swamp soils generally consist of dark brown
or dark gray organic rich silts and clayey silts with some sand and gravel These soils
may contain significant amounts of root and decomposed organic material peat as a
result of organic debris being buried during deposition The swamp and mangrove
deposits are commonly found along the low lying coastal mangrove areas where
streams meet the shoreline environment and where shallow groundwater levels are
largely influenced by tidal fluctuation and stream discharge The ground surface within
the swamp land may be very soft existing as a slurry of mud and water or may be
relatively firm as a result of the intertwined matrix of root growth associated with
mangrove vegetation
The recent alluvial deposits generally consist of tan to brown clayey silts and silty
clays derived from the erosion transportation and re deposition of saprolite and
residual soil materials Alluvial soils are generally found within some stream channels
and bordering some stream banks at the mouths outlets of streams and within broad
valley floors and basin features surrounded by hills These soils may also be found in
close proximity to swamp and lagoonal environments Furthermore recent alluvium
may be found interbedded with the swamp and lagoonal soil deposits

C Volcanic Breccia or Tuff Rock Formation Relatively Unweathered Rock
The volcanic breccia and tuff rock units occur as greenish to brownish gray
slightly fractured to massive hard rock formation The rock formations typically exist as
massive rounded outcroppings showing embedded breccia clasts or as layered
fractured often platy volcanic tuff
Volcanic breccia are angular pyroclastic rock materials erupted from vents as
either flows of superheated material or as blocks of superheated material larger than
60 mm in size that may be cemented with or without a volcanic material matrix
Volcanic tuff is formed by the deposition and cementation of relatively fine grained
pyroclastic materials such as sand and ash which accumulate as airfall deposits
_during volcanic eruptions of superheated materials and gases Tuff formations often
show horizontal or inclined bedding which mimic the underlying topography that the
deposit accumulated These rock formations may be exposed at the ground surface
partially exposed and co existing with some saprolite soil or buried under a relatively
thin mantie of surficial alluvium or residual soil

The on site soils along the majority of the Palau Compact Road alignment generally
exist in a very moist to wet condition Based on our laboratory tests the recompacted
saprolite soils exhibit relatively low California Bearing Ratio CBR values of about 1 to 3
Strengthening of these subgrade soils may be required in certain areas in order to improve
the subsoil workability and to facilitate the project construction This may be accomplished
by the inclusion of additives such as lime or cement treatment and or incorporation of
ground stabilization fabrics at the subgrade level
A Lime and or Cement Stabilization
Lime has been used to improve the properties of fine grained soils for many years
The general objectives of lime stabilization are to modify and improve the
characteristics of the soils thus expediting construction and improving the strength
O 3595 00 C GEOLABS HAWAII Page 91
SECTION V DISCUSSION AND EARTHWORK RECOMMENDATIONS
and durability of the soils In particular it is desired to control the volume stability
swelling and shrinkage characteristics and to reduce the plasticity of the soils
When lime is added to clayey soils several chemical reactions are initiated These
reactions are cation exchange and flocculation agglomeration they are also
pozzolanic In the cation and flocculation agglomeration reactions the clay
particles tend to bond together to form larger sized particles These reactions tend
to decrease the liquid limit increase the plastic limit decrease the plasticity index
increase the workability and improve the strength and deformation properties of the
clay
The pozzolanic reactions involve the formation of cementitious materials between
lime and the silica and alumina of the clay The end product is a series of calcium
silicate hydrates It is important that a sufficient amount of lime be present to
provide a high environment In the long term the high pH causes a
breakdown of the crystal lattice of the clay and the formation of cementitious
products These reactions can continue for a long period of time
It should be noted that the presence of free sulfates in clay soils can be detrimental
With high sulfate content in the soil the initial stabilization may be satisfactory but
subsequent wetting can lead to expansion and breakdown of the cement structure
Attainment of a uniform and intimate mixture of lime with clay and proper curing in
the field are of vital importance and affect the quality of the end results
It is not anticipated that widespread use of lime for subgrade soil stabilization would
be implemented for this project especially since the quick lime needed for soil
Stabilization is not readily available in the Republic of Palau and would need to be
Specifically imported for this application However cement treatment may be
considered since cement also imported may be more readily available in the
Republic of Palau

2 Ground Stabilization Fabrics
Construction on weak and or wet soils can cause delays and added material costs
In addition these types of soils cause rapid deterioration of the paved structures
resulting in significant maintenance costs during the life of the pavement structure
Construction fabrics have been used in recent years to provide a cost effective
solution where stabilization of the subgrade soils is needed in order to facilitate the
project construction and to enhance the long term performance of the pavement
structure Utilization of ground stabilization fabrics helps to solve these soil related
construction and maintenance problems and keep costs down in three ways
e unpaved roads and other areas when designed with ground stabilization
fabrics require less amount of aggregate
e construction schedules are more easily followed because work can continue
during most types of weather and
e ground stabilization fabrics protect the aggregate base course against
subgrade intrusion thus significantly reducing costly maintenance
The benefits resulting from the fabric functions of separation confinement and load
distribution are described as follows

GEOLABS HAWAII

a Separation
Ground stabilization fabrics with puncture and tear resistant properties act
as a separation barrier between the fine grained soils and load distributing
aggregate fill material As a separator it eliminates the loss of costly
aggregate material into the subgrade and prevents the upward pumping of
silt and other contaminating soil fines into the aggregate
b Confinement
Ground stabilization fabrics provide a high friction surface between the
subgrade and the aggregate layer that helps to keep the aggregate in place
This confining action maintains the thickness and hence the intended
load bearing capacity of the aggregate
C Load Distribution
When placed between the subgrade and the aggregate layer ground
stabilization fabric with its high tensile strength and modulus acts to reduce
localized stress by redistributing traffic loads over a wider area of subgrade
In general these subgrade stabilization measures are more cost effective for higher
design traffic requiring thicker pavement sections For the lower design traffic it is
unlikely that ground stabilization fabrics would be cost effective to reduce the
thickness of the pavement structural section However ground stabilization fabrics
may be used as a means to facilitate earthwork construction in the wet
environment

G Erosion Control
Implementation of an adequate erosion control program would be crucial to the
satisfactory performance of the planned cut slopes and fill embankments against potential
failures In general erosion of the existing slopes planned cut slopes or new fill
embankments are caused by surface water run off General guidelines such as
establishment of vegetation and provision of interceptor drainage ditches and earth berms
have been provided and discussed under the Cut Slope Design and Fill Slope Design
subsections of this report However we understand that due to the inherent properties of
the on site soils establishment of vegetation at the slope face would be difficult and may
require a long period of time Therefore other erosion control methods or applications
such as Grouted Rubble Paving GRP and concrete lining may need to be considered as
part of the erosion control program of the proposed roadway project In addition to these
conventional rigid lining systems the use of flexible geosynthetic matting as a viable
solution for slope erosion control has become increasingly more acceptable and may be
considered for this project
Flexible geosynthetic matting provides a flexible ground armor that resists erosion
while allowing natural vegetation to establish Its primary and secondary functions include
the following 1 temporary erosion control 2 mulching 3 permanent erosion control
and 4 run off velocity reduction

1 Temporary Erosion Control
The flexibility weight web structure and porosity features of the flexible
geosynthetic matting make it particularly effective in temporary erosion control
It conforms easily to the ground surface and remains firmly in place as a stable
ground armor The mat shields soil surface from the erosive forces of wind and rain
and prevents soil seed and fertilizer from washing away Finally it provides a
rough surface to retain sediment deposits during run off
2 Mulching
The high porosity web structure of the flexible geosynthetic matting acts as a
non deteriorating mulch It holds soil seed and fertilizer in place and retains
sediment run off as a medium for root growth It also retains moisture and heat
necessary for germination and allows uninhibited growth of grass and other
vegetation
3 Permanent Erosion Control
Once vegetation growth is established the flexible geosynthetic matting continues
to provide permanent erosion control as it becomes embedded in the new stem and
root system It protects new vegetation from washout and reinforces and anchors
the vegetation in place as a cohesive unit
4 Run Off Velocities Reduction
Revegetated slopes and ditches reinforced with the matting reduce run off flow
velocities which is an important advantage that cannot be achieved with concrete
lining It reduces run off flow volume and concentrations and reduces the need for
energy dissipation at the ditch outiet or slope base It also promotes water
percolation and ground water recharge

Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics

parameters used for concrete pipes depend on the chlorides and sulfates found in the soil

the potential for corrosion of the buried concrete structures Chemical analyses including

M Pavement Drainage System
One of the primary distress mechanisms observed in pavement structures is
pumping due to saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Based on our
experience with projects having similar soil conditions there is a strong tendency for water
to accumulate at the interface between the granular subbase course and the underlying
clayey pavement subgrade soils In addition free water voids in the pavement section
repeated heavy wheel loads and an erodible base can also cause pumping conditions
Unless the pavement subgrade soils are properly prepared and adequate drainage is
provided the accumulated water will likely soften the silty and clayey subgrade soils and
cause premature failure of the pavement Therefore paved areas should be sloped and
drainage gradients maintained to carry all surface water off the site Surface water
ponding should not be allowed anywhere on the site during or after construction
In general the primary source of free water in the pavement structure is surface
water infiltration through cracks and joints in the pavement A major source of infiltrated
moisture is the longitudinal pavement shoulder interface Water also enters the pavement

B Stream Bed Material for Scour Analysis

take into consideration the potential for stream scour Scour is the result of erosive action

stream Total scour over a period of time generally consists of three components

scour depend on a number of factors such as the shape and dimensions of a pier or

J Shoring
We envision that temporary excavations may be required for construction of
some of the drainage structures and possibly the bridge foundations Open cut
excavation may be desirable provided that the excavation may be setback away from
existing on grade and below grade structures and utility lines Based on the boring
information we believe that temporary cut slopes on the order of about or flatter
may be used for open cut excavations in the stiff saprolite soils anticipated at the project
site Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal to
the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for
ground movement
Open cut excavations greater than 2 meters deep in the soft and or loose recent
alluvial soils and mangrove swamp deposits are not recommended and should be shored
Excavations into the soft subsoils should be made only with proper shoring installation
Therefore we recommend that the temporary excavations to construct the drainage
structures in soft soils and to remove the soft soils under the drainage structures be
shored One possible shoring method is to use a sheet pile shoring system It should be
noted that the bottom of some of these excavations will encounter dense volcanic breccia
formation and would not readily provide toe embedment for the sheet pile shoring
Therefore other means of providing the passive pressures needed at the toe of the sheet
pile shoring such as a tremie concrete plug or predrilling into the rock formation would be
necessary Other temporary shoring systems may also be considered
It should be noted that during and after the installation of the shoring system minor
settlements may be possible As previously indicated some areas may expose the dense
volcanic breccia rock formation and or boulders in the planned excavation therefore use
of a sheet pile shoring system may not be appropriate in these subsurface conditions
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with all applicable
safety requirements and the adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be
made the sole responsibility of the Contractor His her representative who should be
required to be continuously present on site during excavation and construction work will

CONSTRUCTION PROCEDURES FOR STAGE CONSTRUCTION
STAGE DESCRIPTION
Subgrade Preparation
Clear existing ground surface of vegetation debris and deleterious materials
Soft Ground Stabilization
Place woven geotextile or geogrid as appropriate
Place 0 75 m minimum thickness granular fill or surge rock fill layer as appropriate
For layers greater than 1 m thick place additional geogrids at 0 5 m height intervals
Settlement Gauge and Piezometer Installation
Install settlement gauges and vibrating wire piezometers
Take two baseline readings one day min 24 hours apart 10 days prior to site filling
Take readings on a weekly basis during entire settlement monitoring program
Note Readings of the vibrating wire piezometers are taken near continuously using data loggers
Download data and plot on a weekly basis
Stage 1 Fill Placement Requirements
Place fill at a rate of not more than 1 m per week up to a maximum of 5 m including stabilization layer
Extend settlement gauges as necessary during fill placement
Establish settlement markers at the top of the Stage 1 fill embankment
Allow fill to settle during settlement waiting period for Stage 1 approximately 4 to 6 months
Monitor settlement gauges settlement markers and piezometers on a weekly basis
during entire embankment construction and settlement monitoring period
Proceed with Stage 2 if additional fill required when 70 to 80 percent of pore pressure build up in the
soft soils has dissipated
Stage 2 Fill Placement Requirements
Place fill at a rate of not more than 1 m per week up to a maximum of 8 m above the top elevation of Stage 1
Extend settlement gauges as necessary during fill placement
Establish settlement markers at the top of the Stage 2 fill embankment
Allow fill to settle during settlement waiting period for Stage 2 approximately 1 to 6 months
Monitor settlement gauges settlement markers and piezometers on a weekly basis
during entire embankment construction and settlement monitoring period
Proceed with Stage 3 if additional fill required when 70 to 80 percent of pore pressure build up in the
soft soils has disipated
Stage 3 Fill Placement Requirements
Place fill at a rate of not more than 1 m per week up to a maximum of 11 m above the top elevation of Stage 2
Extend settlement gauges as necessary during fill placement
Establish settlement markers at the top of Stage 3 fill embankment
Allow fill to settle during settlement waiting period for Stage 3 approximately 1 to 3 months
Monitor settlement gauges settlement markers and piezometers on a weekly basis
during entire embankment construction and settlement monitoring period
Fine Grading and Pavement Construction
Proceed with fine grading when settlements reach near equilibrium
Proceed with pavement construction when settlements reach equilibrium
Areas designated as Stage Construction shall commence as early as possible to allow
the soft soils to consolidate under the fill loads

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the proposed roadway alignments for Package C
of the proposed Palau Compact Road project were explored by drilling and sampling
46 borings designated as Boring Nos B 301 through B 346 The borings extended to
depths ranging from about 2 4 to 30 9 meters below the existing ground surface using
various drill rigs equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools The field
exploration was conducted from May 5 1997 through August 8 1997 In addition a
supplemental field exploration to explore some of the realigned portions of the roadway
was conducted between and The approximate locations of
Plates 3 1 through 3 3
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by an engineer and or a geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with
the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of
the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 1 through
A 46 1
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 50 mm OD standard penetration sampler or a 75 mm OD Modified California
sampler with a 63 5 kilogram hammer falling 760 millimeters The blow counts needed to
drive the sampler the last 300 millimeters of a 450 mm drive are shown on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer and torvane shear tests were performed on selected cohesive
soil samples retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of
the unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample The torvane shear test provides
a quick estimate of the undrained shear strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
and are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the dense breccia and hard tuff rock formations encountered at
the site were obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and description

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 1200 mm of material
is recovered from a 1500 mm core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 100 mm in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 750 mm of sound material is recovered from a
1500 mm core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of
the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
vewPoor
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense rock formation with a high
RQD would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of
excavation

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Samples recovered from our field exploration program performed for Package C of
the proposed Palau Compact Road project were visually examined and classified in our
laboratories in Babeldaob Palau and Honolulu Hawaii Selected samples were tested to
evaluate the physical characteristics and engineering properties of the soils encountered
The laboratory testing program was formulated with the emphasis on evaluating the
moisture density plasticity gradation compressibility shear strength and compaction
characteristics of the soils encountered
The laboratory tests performed included determination of dry density and natural
moisture content Atterberg Limits sieve analysis hydrometer unconfined compression
triaxial compression direct shear consolidation modified consolidation collapse
potential Modified Proctor compaction California Bearing Ratio CBR and corrosion
tests Results of the tests performed are summarized in Tables B 1 1 through B 3 and are
presented on Plates B 1 through B 78 of Appendix B Detailed descriptions of the various
tests performed for this exploration are presented below
Moisture and Density
Moisture content and dry density tests were performed on selected soil samples to
aid in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and correlating
the layers between test borings The moisture content tests were performed in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 2216 The results of these tests are
summarized in Tables B 1 1 through B 3 and are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Atterberg Limits
Thirty one Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the liquid limit LL plastic limit PL and plasticity index Pl as an aid in
classifying the soils The Atterberg Limits tests were performed in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 4318 The test results are summarized on the Logs of
presentation of the test results are provided on Plates B 1 through B 4
Sieve Analysis and Hydrometer
To provide information on the particle size distribution of the soils encountered
thirteen sieve analysis tests including eight hydrometer tests were performed to evaluate
the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification The tests were performed in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation C 136 and D 422 Graphic presentation of the
test results are provided on Plates B 5 through B 7

Unconfined Compression
To evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on site clayey soils three
unconfined compression tests were performed on selected in situ samples in accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 2166 The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths and summarized in Table B 2 Individual stress strain
curves of the unconfined compression tests are presented on Plates B 8 through B 10
Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression
To evaluate the undrained shear strength of the clayey soils ten unconsolidated
undrained triaxial compression tests were performed on selected in situ and remolded soil
samples in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 2850 The approximate in situ
effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the
undisturbed samples For the remolded samples the confining pressure was varied to
reflect different fill heights up to about 20 meters thick The test results and the
stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 11 through B 20

Direct Shear
To evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the in situ soils and remolded soils
as fill materials fifteen strained controlled consolidated drained direct shear tests were
performed on the in situ and remolded soil samples in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 3080 The test results are presented on Plates B 22 through B 36
Consolidation
Nine consolidation tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the
compressibility characteristics of the on site soils These tests were performed in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 2435 and the test results are plotted on
Plates B 37 through B 45
Modified Consolidation Collapse Potential
The conventional consolidation tests were modified to evaluate the compressibility
characteristics of the remolded soil samples under different surcharge pressures A soil
sample is remolded at its natural moisture content to approximately 85 percent relative
compaction to simulate the anticipated field conditions The remolded soil sample is
allowed to settle until it has stabilized under a surcharge load Water is then added to the

Modified Proctor Test

Re Palau Compact Road Soil Analysis Report
To follow is a brief summary and recommendations for the attached soil test report no 863 011
from the Palau Compact Road Project
Results
The soil pH is acidic between 04 This is very typical of highly leached or weathered
soils In most cases a pH between is desirable At pH ranges below 5 5 Aluminum
toxicity may inhibit adequate vegetation
The phosphate P levels are extremely low This nutrient is always critical to initial grow in and
establishment of any crop Most native tropical soils are naturally low in this element In
general phosphate is a nutrient which should be incorporated in the root zone prior to planting
Potassium K could become limiting and should be applied during the initial pre plant and
maintenance periods Potassium sulfate would be a better source of this element rather than
potassium chloride The potassium chloride is much more soluble as compared to the sulfate
form and will be easily leached out of the effective root zone
The calcium Ca levels are extremely low and will severely restrict growth and establishment on
most crops The low pH of these soils are related to the low levels of calcium
The magnesium Mg levels are slightly low As the other bases K Ca are increased
moderate amounts of magnesium should be applied to prevent this element from becoming
deficient
Hydrogen H expressed as a percent of the base saturation is extremely high This is an
indication the the soil is acidic As calcium carbonate or other liming material is added the
hydrogen levels will decrease
Sodium levels are low and should not pose any problem in the establishment of most crops
The excessive quantities of aluminum Al are within the toxic ranges Aluminum toxicity is quite
common on highly leached tropical sub soils High levels of available aluminum will inhibit plant
growth and or severely reduce the rate of establishment As soil pH calcium levels and
phosphate levels are increased the amounts of available aluminum will decrease However given
the extremely poor nutritive status of these soils large amounts of both elements calcium and
phosphate must be applied
Recommendations
In most situations a pre plant amendment program will recommend the phosphate and liming
materials be thoroughly incorporated into the soil profile to a depth of 8 15 centimeters
However given the steepness of the slopes and potential increased threat of erosion and the
proximity to the roadway incorporation may not be practical Additions of large quantities of
lime could potentially cause damage i e burning of the delicate seedling roots Again most
typical liming recommendations will advise the incorporation into the root zone to reduce the
burn potential and allow for pH modification of the profile
Due to its low solubility and other interactions with the soil lime applied topically will produce
little short term effects lower in the profile Additions of large quantities of gypsum will
produce slight pH increases on severely acidic soils Being many times more soluble than lime
up to 150 times gypsum when topically applied will be able to move through the soil profile
Therefore a combination of gypsum and lime would be a practical approach to this situation
I would recommend the following pre plant amendments be applied during the hydromulching
non incorporated operation
Kilograms Hectare Finely Ground Dolomitic Limestone
Kilograms Hectare Agricultural Grade Gypsum Calcium Sulfate
Maintenance applications should consist of a high phosphate complete analysis fertilizer and
finely ground dolomitic lime stone This material should be applied approximately 6 weeks after
planting and seed germination
Kilograms Hectare Finely Ground Dolomitic Limestone
If incorporation of pre plant soil amendments is possible the dolomitic lime stone rates should
be increased to 4 480 kilograms per hectare and thoroughly incorporated along with the pre plant
fertilizer into the effective root zone of The maintenance application of dolomite
could then be removed
Other options which should be considered are
1 Topically applied pre treatment of the slopes with a hydraulically applied slurry of
hydrated lime several weeks prior to installing the non incorporated pre plant soil
amendments
2 Installation of aluminum tolerant plants
3 Application of pre plant amendments prior to sheep foot rolling tracking the soil surface
with a dozer and light scarification which will create a mixing into the soil profile
These soils if properly treated should be able to sustain adequate vegetation required for erosion
control
