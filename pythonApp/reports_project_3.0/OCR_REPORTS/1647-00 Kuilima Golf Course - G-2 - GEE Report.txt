INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed Golf Course G 2 at the Kuilima
Development at Kahuku on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location of the site and its vicinity is shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1 The exploration was performed in general accordance with
our proposal dated
This report summarizes the findings of the exploration and our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses based
on the data obtained during the exploration These recommendations are
intended for design input and are not intended for construction estimation
or bidding purposes

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the
subsurface conditions at the project site in order to develop a
generalized soil rock data set for the formulation of geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertinent to site grading and foundation
design of the project
The work was done in general accordance with our proposal dated

the project site and its vicinity
Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field
exploration to determine the engineering properties and
classification of the materials encountered
Engineering analyses of the field and laboratory data for the
purpose of developing site grading and foundation design
recommendations
Preparation of this report presenting our findings and design
recommendations

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed G 2 golf course is situated on the eastern side of the
planned Kuilima Resort Development and east of the existing Turtle Bay
Hilton Resort Hotel The golf course will be constructed adjacent to and
around the existing Punahoolapa Marsh and therefore its design is
intimately related to the ecological and hydrological aspects of the
marsh The golf course will involve the following elements of
construction
1 Site filling of generally five 5 to ten 10 feet for the golf
course
2 excavation for the golf course lakes to as much as minus five
5 feet elevation
3 excavation around the edges of the existing pond to about
elevation minus four 4 feet to create a moat
4 excavation of a lake in the southern area near Kamehameha
Highway to also serve as an effluent retention pond and
5 construction of retaining walls at various locations along the
lakes
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located on the northern tip of Oahu adjacent to
the existing Turtle Bay Hilton resort It is bounded by Kamehameha
Highway to the south the existing hotel golf course to the west Marconi
Road to the east and the Pacific Ocean to the north
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1647 00 Page 4
The topography of the site is roughly level throughout the area with
ground elevations ranging from 2 feet to 25 feet above Mean Sea Level
A major feature of the area is the presence of a large marsh in the
center of the project area
The area is moderately to heavily vegetated with tall trees large
shrubs and grasses The marsh is filled with tall reeds
The makai portion of the site is an abandoned runway while several
abandoned farming structures exist throughout the site
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built during the late Pliocene early
Pleistocene by two basaltic shielded volcanoes Waianae and Koolau
Waianae is the older of the two and forms the bulk of the western half of
the island The younger Koolau forms the eastern half of the island
extending from Kahuku to Koko Head
After the main shield building phase of the two volcanoes the island
began to subside in response to tectonic stresses imposed by the loads of
the mountain masses Concurrently reefs began to grow on the flanks of
the subsiding volcanoes forming fringing and barrier reefs of coral algal
limestone
During the middle and late Pleistocene or Ice Age sea levels
fluctuated in response to the advance and retreat of the continental
glaciers As the glaciers grew and advanced there was less water to fill
the ocean basins so sea levels fell to below the current level When the
glaciers melted and retreated more water was available and sea levels
rose often to levels above the present stand of the sea
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1647 00 Page 5
The geologic conditions at the project site are a direct result of
these glacioeustatic sea level changes during the Pleistocene
Most of the site is underlain by coral algal reef materials believed
to have been deposited during the Waimanalo 25 feet high stand of the
sea
In some areas of the site this reef material is overlain by somewhat
cemented dunes of calcareous sands believed to have been deposited during
the Mamala 350 feet low stand of the sea
In the low areas of the site the Waimanalo reef material is
generally mantled by a thin layer of recent alluvium or recent organic
marsh soils
Some amount of fill were encountered on the project site which were
probably placed during the site s former use for agricultural purposes and
as a military installation
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by the
drilling and sampling of borings utilizing a truck mounted rotary drilling
rig
A total of twenty seven 27 borings were drilled for the exploration
of the golf course
Water levels in the borings were recorded during the drilling
operations Tidal variations during the day were monitored in three 3
of the borings to evaluate the water level fluctuations at the site
Additionally tidal variations at the edges of the marsh were also
monitored Salinity measurements of the water in the borings at various
depths were also performed

Soil Conditions
The borings encountered several different subsoil conditions at the
site Generally the areas north of the proposed Beach Road are underlain
primarily by beach sand and coral deposits The areas around the fringes
of Punahoolapa Marsh were found to be underlain by soft marsh deposits
over sand or coral formation The thickness of the surface marsh layer
varied from about 3 to 11 feet Residual soils and basalt formation were
encountered in the southern mauka tip of the site
Detailed information of the conditions encountered are presented on
the boring logs in Appendix A
Groundwater Conditions
The groundwater conditions at the site were found to be complex due
to tidal influences near the ocean probable leakage of fresh water from
wells and springs and due to variable soil conditions
In the area north of the proposed Beach Frontage Road the permeable
sandy soil and proximity to the ocean result in ground water levels close
to sea level with fluctuations which correspond to tidal variations
In the marsh area it appears that the soft low permeability marsh
deposits attenuate some of the tidal influence and the water levels
reflect on a short term basis run off conditions and leakage of basal
artesian water through the cap rock Measured water levels were generally
1 to 2 feet above sea level
In the southern portion of the site slightly higher water levels of
about 1 to 3 feet above sea level were measured presumably due to fresh
water leakage from springs and or wells Significant tidal variations
were not noted probably due to the distance from the ocean and low
permeability layers in the subsoils

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration encountered various subsoil conditions at the
site In general areas away from the marsh are underlain by competent
sand and coral deposits which can support the proposed fills and cuts
However the areas adjacent to the marsh area are underlain by variable
thicknesses of soft deposits which are compressible and weak Therefore
grading near the marsh should be done with care and cuts and fills
Golf Course Site Grading
Special grading design is contemplated for the golf course fills due
to landscaping concerns Treated sewage effluent will be used for
landscape watering as well as a means of effluent disposal Therefore
there are concerns for adequate drainage in the surface soils Ponding of
the irrigation water could hinder the growth of vegetation Due to the
source of the irrigation water ponding could also pose a potential health
problem
In order to provide adequate drainage of the near surface soils the
site grading design should allow for good surface water movement and
drainage The proposed fill soils will be from designated borrow sites in
which relatively clayey materials were encountered The fill soils should
be moderately compacted to provide additional subsurface drainage of the
landscape water Since the soils in the golf course will not be required
to support structural loads it is our opinion that a lower level of
compaction effort may be used

Site Preparation
Areas to be filled should be cleared of vegetation debris and other
deleterious materials
Due to the soft wet surface soils and shallow depth to the water
table encountered around the marsh special grading techniques and
light equipment should be used for the clearing and grubbing
operations A stabilization layer consisting of a 12 inch layer of
gravel over a synthetic filter fabric see Plate 3 may be required
to stabilize localized areas with very soft soils or high water
level As a preliminary guide for cost estimating purposes it
appears that existing areas at or below elevation three 3 feet
MSL may require the stabilization layer prior to filling
Fill Material Placement
Fill material will be obtained from a designated borrow site situated
about one 1 mile southeast of the site mauka of Kamehameha
Highway
We believe that most of the borrow site soils may be suitable for use
as general fill provided that they are not contaminated with
unsuitable materials or oversized particles
Highly expansive clay soils if encountered should be used in the
deeper fill areas to allow better surface drainage
In the golf course areas the top four 4 feet of fill should be
compacted to a minimum of 75 but no greater than 85 per ASIM Test
Method D 1557 78 This lower level of compaction as opposed to the
normal 90 required for general fills will provide a slightly higher
degree of porosity in the generally low permeability borrow soils
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O

Due to the reduced level of compaction fill slope ratios should be
limited to 3H 1V or flatter
Cuts are primarily planned for the creation of lakes therefore cut
slopes would be constructed and maintained under water The cuts
Slope ratio would depend on the type of material encountered For
the sandy coralline soils encountered in the areas away from the
marsh a cut slope ratio of 3H 1V or flatter is recommended For the
soft soils encountered around the existing marsh a flatter slope
ratio of 5H 1V is recommended to maintain the stability of the
slopes Extreme care should be taken to prevent over cutting of the
soft soils which could initiate slope instability Properly designed
slope protection methods such as riprap or surface lining should be
considered for wind and wave scouring action
Irrigation Pond
The southern lake of the golf course will also serve to store treated
effluent from the treatment plant A pond depth of about 5 to 7 feet
is anticipated with a bottom elevation of plus three 3 feet above
MSL
Boring Nos 7 and 22 indicate that excavation for the lake will
expose sand coral deposits Therefore there is concern that the
treated effluent will percolate into the subsoils unless an
impervious liner is used within the lake
In order to properly place and join the sections of the liner it is
generally required to install the liner in the dry The measured
groundwater levels in the area were generally less than plus three
3 elevation therefore it appears that a liner could be
installed However due to the proximity of the groundwater level a

muddy bottom condition is anticipated Therefore a properly
graded gravel cushion approximately 12 inches thick may be required
below the liner to allow installation Care should be exercised to
avoid puncturing of the lining due to equipment and labor movements
on the lining as it is installed over the gravel cushion layer
Settlements
It is anticipated that the placement of fill in the areas of the golf
course underlain by soft wet soils will induce settlement of the
underlying compressible soils In general these settlements are
estimated to be on the order of 4 to 6 inches for about 10 feet of
fill
Moat Construction
It is planned to deepen the perimeter of the marsh to create a moat
condition Special excavation techniques will be required for this
area due to the soft soil conditions and excavation below the natural
water level Light equipment on special wide tracks or amphibious
tires may be required in addition to the placement of a stabilization
layer to establish a working platform along the marsh perimeter
provided that temporary fill stabilization placement is allowed A
relatively gentle cut slope ratio of 5H 1V or flatter is recommended
to maintain cut slope stability in the soft marsh deposits A
steeper slope ratio of up to 3H 1V may be used for the cut slope
along the interior edge of the moat if some sloughing is acceptable
Retaining Walls
Retaining walls are planned to provide grade separation along
portions of the lakes Retaining walls in the soft ground area should be
avoided or done with care The guidelines for design of retaining walls
are as follows

An allowable bearing value of 3 000 p s f may be used for

Retaining wall footings on the soft marsh deposits should be
avoided and if absolutely required each should be evaluated on
an individual basis
A passive earth resistance of 300 p s f per foot of depth may be
used for wall footings on level areas The passive pressure must
be reduced for walls located on slopes
A base friction factor of 0 3 may also be used to determine the

The wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded
a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent grade For
sloping ground the footing should extend deeper to obtain a
minimm 8 foot set back distance from the outside edge of the
footing measured horizontally to the slope face
A subsurface drainage system subdrains and or weepholes should
be utilized behind the walls to minimize the build up of
hydrostatic pressure behind the walls Subdrains should be
constructed properly to drain water away from the walls

Wetland Development Punahoolapa Marsh
It is desired to improve the conditions in the marsh to create a
refuge or habitat for wildlife Control of the water levels is important
for the management of the wetlands Therefore water level fluctuations
in the marsh were monitored and evaluated together with the subsoil
information from the recent borings drilled wells and geologic
literature to evaluate water management procedures which may be used
In addition to the water levels measured in the borings during the
drilling water level fluctuations in selected borings along the edge of
the marsh were measured during the course of one day Fluctuations of the
marsh water level were also measured at selected locations These reading
are summarized on Plate A 28
Water samples were obtained at various depths and tested in the
laboratory to determine the salinity of the water The test results are
summarized on Plate A 28 In place salinity measurements were also taken
in selected borings These test results are presented on Plates A 29
through A 33
Based on the information obtained the following general conclusions
may be drawn
1 The near surface soil conditions consist of varying thicknesses
of soft marsh deposits over permeable sand coral formation
Therefore much of the marsh area may be hydraulically connected

ji e water levels in various areas of the marsh may not be
effectively isolated from the rest of the marsh This may be
especially true after excavation of the moat around the marsh
which will probably remove the mud deposits and expose the
permeable sand coral formation
2 Tidal fluctuations are more pronounced near the ocean and are
essentially absent in the southern mauka portion of the marsh
3 The soft surface mud deposits presently covering the marsh
dampens the effect of the tidal fluctuations
4 Water levels up to about three 3 feet above Mean Sea Level were
measured during the exploration The measured water levels were
about 1 to 2 feet higher than water levels indicated on the tide
charts
5 The salinity readings indicate that there may be a surface layer
of nearly fresh water overlying the area which appears to confirm
the published geologic and hydrologic information of the area
indicating the presence of springs or leaking wells

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based
in part upon information obtained from field data points such as borings
and observation wells Variations of conditions between the field data
points may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not
become evident until construction If variations then appear evident it
will be necessary to re evaluate the recommendations given in this report
The locations of the field data points were determined by estimation
from the topographic survey map Elevations of the data points were
approximately determined by estimation from the same plan The physical
location and elevation of the field data points should be considered
accurate only to the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the
boring points depict the approximate boundaries between soil types and
as such may denote a gradual transition The strata lines shown on
profiles or cross sections are based upon interpolation between field data
points and may not represent actual subsurface conditions
