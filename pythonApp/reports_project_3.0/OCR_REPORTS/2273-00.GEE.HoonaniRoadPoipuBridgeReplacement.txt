INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Hoonani Road Poipu Bridge Replacement project in the
area of Poipu on the island of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed replacement bridge project These recommendations are
intended for design input only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project involves the construction of a new two span bridge
supporting a two lane roadway across Waikomo Stream as shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 It is our understanding that the new bridge will replace the existing wooden
bridge along Hoonani Road spanning across Waikomo Stream The new bridge will
have a concrete deck supported at the center pier and at each end on seat type
abutments The planned bridge will be approximately 92 feet long 37 feet wide and
approximately 13 feet above the lowest point of the stream We estimate the structural
loads to be approximately 850 kips for dead plus live loads at the center pier and about
half that amount for the abutments
Based on the current design concept excavation depths on the order of about
15 feet may be required to construct the abutment wall footings At the center pier
excavations on the order of approximately 8 feet are anticipated An 8 inch thick

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 5
Epoch more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this
main shield building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of
basaltic lavas of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition
of the alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic series cover most of the eastern half of the island
of Kauai and are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded from
groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various locales Associated with the
vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at the vent
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result
of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available
to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea
levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Kauai remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to present sea level on Kauai
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
In the area of the project alluvial soils have accumulated on the basalt rock
formation along Waikomo Stream The alluvial deposits generally consist of clayey
silts gravels cobbles and boulders Some fills have been placed in the area for
development of Lawai and Hoonani Roads

SITE DESCRIPTION
The proposed project site is located in the Poipu area on the island of Kauai
Hawaii The realigned new bridge will replace the existing wooden bridge spanning
across Waikomo Stream as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The existing wooden
bridge is located near the intersection of Lawai and Hoonani Roads Based on our field
observation the existing bridge is constructed of wooden framework and the deck is
supported by a masonry foundation
Due to poor sight distance a new alignment is proposed for the Hoonani Road
Poipu Bridge Replacement project In general the new bridge will span across
Waikomo Stream and connect Lawai Road to Hoonani Road The banks on both sides
of the stream slopes gently toward the stream at inclinations of five horizontal to one
vertical 5H 1V or flatter A few scattered exposures of buried cobbles and boulders
are visible at the ground surface The site is vegetated with tall grass and brush with
a few medium size trees
The lowest point of the stream is about 12 feet below the existing roads

and firm Water was observed flowing within the narrow stream channel at the location

and boulders The water flow was observed to be running rapidly and about 1 to 2 feet
in depth During our exploration in 1989 water flow was not observed
Based on our observations during the exploration the western bank off Lawai

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the site is generally underlain by alluvial deposits
consisting of clayey silts with cobbles gravels and boulders overlying basalt formation
Based on the available topographic map and our exploration data basalt formation may
be encountered at elevations of about 18 feet MSL near the center pier and about
20 to 27 feet MSL at the abutments The planned elevations at the bottom of
footing elevations will generally range from about 17 to 20 feet MSL Therefore we
believe that a shallow foundation system bearing on the basalt formation may be used
to support the abutments and center pier footings of the new bridge
Detailed discussions and recommendations for design and evaluation of the
bridge foundation earthwork and other geotechnical aspects of the project are
presented in the following sections of this report
Foundations
Based on the current design concept the footings for the abutments will be
embedded approximately 15 feet below the existing ground surface which ranges from
about 32 to 35 feet MSL Therefore the elevations at the bottom of the footings will
range from about 17 to 20 feet MSL
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2273 00 Page 9
Based on our field exploration from Boring Nos 3 through 5 medium to very
hard basalt formation was encountered from about 18 to 22 feet MSL Therefore
we believe that a shallow foundation system bearing on the basalt formation may be
used to support the abutments and center pier footings of the new bridge An allowable
bearing pressure of up to 6 000 p s f may be used to design the footings bearing on
the basalt formation The allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and
may be increased by one third for transient loads such as temporary wind and or
seismic forces
In general the footings should be embedded at or below the design scour
elevation Where footings are located adjacent to slopes or on slopes the footings
should be embedded to a sufficient depth to provide a minimum horizontal setback
distance of at least 8 feet measured from the edge of the footing to the face of the
slope
The bottom of the footing excavations should be free of all debris and loose
material All loose materials should be removed from the bottom of the footing
Contract documents should include provisions for over excavation of the footing if
basalt formation is found to be located at greater depths during construction The over
excavation should be filled with lean concrete up to the design bottom of footing
We believe that the total settlement of the new footings bearing on the basalt
formation would be on the order of less than inch with differential settlements of about
half that amount between the abutments and the center pier We believe a significant
portion of these settlements are elastic and should occur as the loads are applied

It is important that a representative from Geolabs Hawaii observe the foundation
excavations to evaluate the competency of the bearing materials and the embedment
depths
Lateral loads acting on the foundation may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the footing and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 60 may be used between the base of the footings and the

using an equivalent fluid pressure of 200 p c f for footings embedded in level ground

against the footing excavations Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches below finish grade should be neglected Passive
resistance above the design scour elevation should also be neglected

Foundation Probing
Although we did not encounter cavities in the basalt formation during our
exploration based on our experience in the area we have encountered cavities in the
basalt formation near this site Therefore to reduce the potential for loss of foundation
support resulting from collapse of a cavity in basalt formation below a footing we
recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be conducted during
construction for the footings bearing on basalt formation
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at the four
corners and center of each spread footing location and on 10 foot centers for
continuous footings The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and
should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of footing If cavities and or
voids are encountered or suspected during the probing or grouting operations
additional probes should be drilled at closer spacings to help delineate the vertical and
lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and any cavities discovered
should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures A
typical sand cement grout would consist of 1 part sand 1 part cement and 0 7 part
water
Unit prices for additional probing and grouting should be obtained during bidding
to accommodate the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids In addition
the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting operation is
complete

Construction Considerations
The project site is located adjacent to and near the bottom of Waikomo Stream
The general area is wet with frequent rains With heavy rainstorms the water level of
the Waikomo Stream rises rapidly and could flood the construction site causing delays
We recommend that some protection measures be implemented by the contractor to
reduce the potential of flooding of the construction areas due to heavy rain water runoff
Excavation
To construct the footings for the abutments and center pier excavations on the
order of 8 to 15 feet would be required An open cut excavation in the stream
may be feasible subject to practical and environmental requirements If an open
cut excavation is elected temporary side slopes of 1H 1V or flatter may be used
However excavations for the abutments may be restricted due to limited space
close to the existing roadway The side slopes should be adequately protected _
against erosion especially during heavy rains which may flood the excavation
to reduce potential instability of the side slopes The contractor should retain a
qualified geotechnical engineer to evaluate the method of excavation and or
shoring of the excavation
It should be emphasized that it is the responsibility of the contractor to provide
a safe and stable excavation for the construction workers It should be noted
that the abundant cobbles and boulders in the stream bed and in the excavated
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2273 00 Page 14
materials may pose a falling boulder hazard within the excavation Therefore
shoring of the sides of the excavation may be prudent
The excavation shoring system used must comply with all applicable safety
requirements The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be
made the sole responsibility of the contractor His representative who should
be required to be present on site during excavation and construction work will
have the best opportunity to promptly observe any changing conditions during

However it must be noted that some movement of the shoring system and the

excavation The magnitude of movement is difficult to accurately predict due to

workmanship and also on the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and
the rate of excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be
installed properly and as early as practical if necessary and that the adjacent
ground should be continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other
indications of movements with instruments such as settlement markers and
inclinometers Such monitoring is particularly critical in areas adjacent to
existing facilities and utilities

Dewatering
Groundwater was encountered in our borings at depths of approximately 3 to
7 feet below the surface corresponding to an elevation of about 22 feet MSL
If the bottom of footing is below 22 feet MSL it is likely that groundwater will
be encountered in the excavation requiring dewatering to provide a dry
excavation in which to work Therefore we recommend that dewatering
provisions be included in the contract documents for the construction of the
proposed bridge structure
It should be noted that groundwater levels may fluctuate depending on rainfall
time of year seepage conditions stream water levels and other factors that may
cause flooding of the excavation Therefore these conditions should be
anticipated by the contractor and provisions should be incorporated in the
construction plan to reduce their impacts and delays In general the method
and equipment to be used for the excavation dewatering should be determined
by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
If the excavation dewatering involves discharge of groundwater from the
dewatering operation into the adjacent drainage system a National Pollutant
Discharge Elimination System NPDES permit may be necessary The
contractor should consult their independent consultant or the State of Hawaii
Department of Health for the latest regulations and information pertaining to the
permit application
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents
should be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural
groundwater table and or to exclude the water from the excavations to allow
construction of the proposed structures under safe and dry conditions We
GEOLABS HAWAII

suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selecting a

Concrete Lining
It is our understanding that an 8 inch thick concrete lining is planned across the
width of the stream bed to reduce the effects of scouring To provide more uniform
support for the concrete lining we recommend that a 4 inch thick cushion layer of
No 3B Fine gravel be provided below the slabs
Prior to placement of the cushion material the existing ground at the stream bed
should be cleared and grubbed Upon clearing and grubbing all exposed subgrades
free of organic materials should be proof rolled to obtain a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction If organic materials are encountered during the subgrade
preparation it should be removed and replaced with fill material compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Site Grading
The extent of grading required to construct the proposed new replacement bridge
will consist of site preparation and minor grading to achieve final grades and backfill
behind abutments All backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling five
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 at the approximate locations shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of our borings ranged from about 15 to 23 feet
below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill
rig
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field geologists who monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and
by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
nia sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to
drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of
borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
