REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows from
Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau known
as Schofield Plateau
Following extrusion of the lavas in the early Pleistocene Epoch the island
underwent a long cycle of erosion and weathering forming the prominent ridgelines and
summits as we know today During the erosion period the Island of Oahu began to slowly
subside by more than 1 200 feet in elevation resulting in the drowning and sedimentation
of the valleys and the formation of the steep Koolau Pali Coral reefs continued to grow in
the surrounding shallow waters of the island
From the mid to late Pleistocene Epoch the sea level repeatedly rose and fell in
response to global glaciation and the availability of surface waters to sustain the oceans
The various sea level elevations and their representative deposits are known as stands
and include the following from oldest to youngest the Kahuku Kahipa Kaena Laie
Waialae Waipio and Waimanalo stands Geologic deposits associated with the various
sea level stands including marine sediments and coral reefs were deposited and
subsequently altered or removed by later sea level fluctuation Therefore depositional
records reflecting the changes in sea level and the occurrence of emerged coral reef
deposits are often incomplete
The site is situated on the Ewa Plain located to the southeast of the Waianae
Mountains The Ewa Plain is a gently sloping alluvial plain formed by the deposition of
alluvial clays and silts derived from weathering of the basalt rock formation further up
slope The alluvial deposits were laid down and are inter bedded with marine sediments
and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to
over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the
confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu
Basalt rock formation resides below the marine deposits at a substantial depth
The majority of the site is situated over alluvial clay soils also known as alluvium
The coralline and marine deposits are believed to underlie the site but at some depth
beneath the alluvium Toward the south coralline deposits may exist at or very near the

SITE DESCRIPTION
As indicated previously the Kahua Nursery Property is located at the intersection of
the Old and New Fort Weaver Roads The site encompasses approximately 23 5 acres in
land area and is designated by Tax Map Key 010 041 It is located adjacent to
the West Loch Golf Course located on the north
Based on our field reconnaissance the site presently consists of vacant land
covered with low brush Heavier vegetation and trees were noted along the ponds at the
northern portion of the property and at the western portions of the site In addition
portions of the site were covered with gravel fill materials From a geomorphology
perspective it appears that portions of the site consist of drainage ways the ground
elevations at the site are lower than the adjacent grounds
In general the site slopes down toward the northern portion of the site at slope
inclinations ranging up to about 15 percent or seven horizontal to one vertical 7H 1V
The steeper slopes are located near Old Fort Weaver Road The existing ground
elevations range from about 5 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the northern end to about
35 feet MSL near the southern end of the site
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our limited field exploration program at the site consisted of drilling and sampling
six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 extending to depths of about 31 5 to
41 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings
drilled at the site are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight hollow stem augers
In general our field exploration disclosed that the site is underlain by surface fills of
approximately 5 to 13 5 feet in thickness The surface fill layer generally consisted of very
stiff to very hard clays and silty clays with sands gravel cobbles and boulders and
medium dense clayey sands with gravel The fill layer is underlain by highly compressible
lagoonal deposits extending to depths of about 28 5 to 35 5 feet below the ground level
The lagoonal deposits generally are comprised of very soft sandy silts organic clays and
silts with sands In addition the lagoonal deposits also contain very loose to medium
dense silty sands
Below the lagoonal deposits alluvial soil deposits alluvium were encountered and
extended to the maximum depth explored of approximately 41 5 feet below the existing
ground surface The alluvial soil deposits alluvium consisted of stiff to hard silty clays
and clayey silts with sands and medium dense to dense silty and clayey sands It should

FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS
Based on our site reconnaissance and limited field exploration the site generally is
underlain by a surface fill layer of approximately 5 to 13 5 feet in thickness The surface fill
layer was underlain by compressible lagoonal deposits extending down to depths of
approximately 28 5 to 35 5 feet below the ground level The lagoonal deposits
encountered composed of very soft sandy silts organic clays and silts with sands The
lagoonal deposits encountered also contain very loose to medium dense silty sands
Below the compressible lagoonal deposits stiff to hard and medium dense alluvial
soil deposits alluvium were encountered extending to the maximum depth explored of
approximately 41 5 feet below the existing ground surface It should be noted that the
compressible lagoonal deposits were encountered in four of the six borings drilled
Groundwater was encountered in the borings drilled at depths of about 4 3 to 14 2 feet
below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration
As indicated previously we envision the ground elevations at the site will need to
be raised for drainage considerations by the placement of new fills Due to the presence
of the compressible lagoonal deposits we anticipate that substantial settlements will occur
due to the loading imposed on the compressible lagoonal deposits from the new fill
materials depending on the thickness of new fills Therefore a settlement monitoring
program will need to be established to monitor the ground settlements due to fill placement
for the development In order to reduce the magnitude of the post construction
settlements we recommend that a settlement waiting period of about 12 to 24 months
after placement of the fills be required for fill placement over the soft ground areas prior to
constructing the improvements such as underground utilities and structures on and over
the fills It should be noted that the settlement waiting period for the development will
depend on the type of development proposed and the project constraints imposed by the
developers of the project

Following the settlement waiting period we believe that shallow foundations
consisting of reinforced concrete slabs on grade with thickened edge slab footings may be
considered for support of the residential and or commercial structures planned at the site
Shallow foundations for the structures should bear on the recompacted on site soils
minimum 3 feet thick above the lagoonal deposits and or on the new compacted fills
minimum 3 feet thick needed to raise the site to the finished subgrades for the
development
Because of the potential for some long term settlements due to consolidation of the
soft soils and secondary compression of the lagoonal deposits we recommend that the
concrete slabs on grade be at least 5 inches thick in lieu of the traditional 4 inches thick
and reinforced with No 4 reinforcing bars spaced at 12 inches on center each way We
believe that the potential for significant distress to the concrete slabs on grade due to
ground settlements would be reduced by increasing the building slab thickness and by
incorporating steel reinforcement into the concrete slab
Due to the presence of compressible lagoonal deposits in a large portion of the site
consideration may be given to the types of developments that would require less fill
materials than that required for single family residential developments Based on our
understanding of the drainage considerations at the site other types of developments
such as commercial type developments and or multi family residential developments with
the first living floor elevated above the ground level may require less thickness of fill
above the existing ground level to achieve the design finished grades A qualified Civil
Engineer experienced in project developments in the area should be retained to evaluate
and determine the requirements of these types of developments at the site if desired
A reduction in the thickness of fills above the existing ground elevations will result in
less ground settlements and possibly a slightly reduced actual settlement waiting period
for the site after fill placement Reduced fill thickness above the existing ground elevations
also will reduce the amount of imported fill materials needed to raise the ground elevations
to the design finished grades
The presence of the compressible lagoonal deposits at the site will make
installation of gravity utility lines such as drain lines and sewer lines more costly
Drainage structures such as box culverts and major drain lines to be installed in the areas
underlain by compressible lagoonal deposits likely will need to be supported on a deep
foundation system Due to the stringent grade requirements for gravity sewer lines to be
dedicated to the City and County of Honolulu gravity sewer lines likely will need to be
supported on a deep foundation system The deep foundation system for support of the
utilities may consist of driven piles or micropiles Alternatively the use of a low pressure
sewer system may be considered for the development if acceptable to the City and
County of Honolulu For utility lines not supported on a deep foundation system we
recommend that flexible pipes such as PVC and or HDPE pipes be utilized

Fill Settlements
Based on the preliminary field exploration it appears that approximately 70 to
80 percent of the site is underlain by very soft and very loose lagoonal deposits of up to
about 30 feet in thickness The estimated lateral extent of the soft subsoil area is shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 In addition generalized subsurface profiles across the site are
presented on the Generalized Subsurface Profiles on Plates 3 and 4 It should be noted
that the approximate extents of the areas underlain by soft compressible lagoonal deposits
estimated based on our limited field exploration shown the Site Plan Plate 2 are
intended for reference and information purposes only
The results of the laboratory consolidation tests indicate that the soft to very soft
lagoonal deposits at the site appear to be normally consolidated Based on the thickness
of the soft soils the thickness of fills to be placed and our engineering analyses the range
of the probable consolidation settlements due to new embankment fills for 3 and 6 feet of
new fills was calculated The estimated consolidation settlements due to 3 feet of new
fills range from approximately 2 to 6 inches For fills up to about 6 feet in vertical height
the estimated consolidation settlements are approximately 4 to 10 inches
Based on our analyses the estimated time to achieve between 75 to 80 percent
consolidation ranges from about 12 to 24 months It should be recognized that it is difficult
to accurately predict the exact time required for the filled ground to settle because
settlement rates are affected by variations in the subsoil structure and the history of the
subsoil deposition Because the estimated settlement period could vary from the actual
settlement period the actual settlement rates should be monitored Details of the
settlement monitoring program should be established based on the actual type and extent
of development proposed and the project constraints

less in maximum dimension and should be free of deleterious materials The material
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 8 or higher and a swell
potentialof not more than 2 percent when tested in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It should be noted that the general fill requirements
presented herein are intended as guidelines only and may be modified based on additional
laboratory testing and field observations on the available fill materials conducted by

Select granular fill 12 to 24 inches thick consisting of non expansive select
granular material such as crushed coral or basalt should be provided below the
building sidewalk slabs and pavement areas The material should be well graded from
coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension The material
also should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum
swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883

commercial structures may be supported on a shallow foundation support system bearing
on the recompacted on site soils or new compacted fills placed to achieve the finished
grades at the site Based on the generally stiff surface fill soils encountered and the new
fills anticipated to raise the site we believe that shallow foundations consisting of
reinforced concrete slabs on grade with thickened edge slab footings may be used to

should not commence until the settlement waiting period has been completed or the rate
of settlement has stabilized based on the results of the settlement monitoring program
An allowable bearing pressure on the order of about 2 500 to 3 000 pounds per
square foot psf may be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the
recompacted on site soils minimum 3 feet thick above the lagoonal deposits and or on
the new compacted fills minimum 3 feet thick needed to achieve the finished subgrades
For footings bearing on the on site soils or new compacted fills the bottom of the footing
excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a
relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or
concrete
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 to 24 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grades Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping
ground conditions should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal
setback distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of
the slope Footings located adjacent to planned or existing retaining walls should be
embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations or the
planned retaining walls should be designed to resist the additional structural loads
Slabs On Grade
As indicated previously the site generally is underlain by a surface layer of slightly
to moderately expansive clayey soils To reduce the potential for structural distress
resulting from swelling of the subgrade soils we recommend that the subgrade soils be
properly prepared prior to fill placement In addition a minimum of 12 to 24 inches of
non expansive select granular fill material should be provided below the building slab
areas The non expansive select granular fill also should extend beyond the perimeter of
the building a minimum of 24 inches or extend to the edge of the concrete sidewalk
surrounding the building if applicable
For both expansive soil considerations and the potential for long term settlements
we recommend that the concrete slabs on grade be at least 5 inches thick in lieu of the
traditional 4 inches thick and reinforced with No 4 reinforcing bars spaced at 12 inches on
center each way in lieu of the standard welded wire fabric This recommendation is
provided to reduce the potential for significant structural distress resulting from differential
movements of the slabs due to the nature of the on site soils We also believe that the
potential for significant distress to the concrete slabs on grade due to ground settlements
would be reduced by increasing the building slab thickness and by incorporating steel
reinforcement into the concrete slab

Underground Utility Lines
New utility lines and utility connections such as drain lines sewer lines and water
lines will be required for development of the site Because of the presence of
compressible lagoonal deposits at the site the costs for installation of gravity utility lines
will be more costly than typical conditions Drainage structures such as box culverts and
major drain lines to be installed in the areas underlain by soft soils likely will need to be
supported on a deep foundation system Due to the stringent grade requirements for
gravity sewer lines to be dedicated to the City and County of Honolulu gravity sewer lines
likely will need to be supported on a deep foundation system
The deep foundation system may consist of driven piles or micropiles installed
through the soft lagoonal deposit and embedded within the underlying stiff alluvium The
pile foundations would derive support principally from side resistance between the sides of
the pile and the subsoil deposits encountered in the borings drilled Alternatively the use
of a low pressure sewer system may be considered for the development if acceptable to
the City and County of Honolulu Installation of the low pressure sewer system would not
require a deep foundation system for support of the sewer lines
For the other utility lines not supported on a deep foundation system we
recommend that flexible pipes such as PVC and HDPE pipes be utilized In general
granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation may be used under the pipes in firm and or dense ground conditions If soft
and or loose soils are encountered at or near the inverts of the pipes not supported on a
deep foundations system we recommend that an additional 18 to 24 inches of
open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 4 gradation or No 67 gradation wrapped in a
non woven filter fabric Mirafi 18ON or equivalent be provided below the bedding layer for
uniform support
Because of the relatively low existing ground elevations and the high groundwater
levels encountered in some of the borings drilled the utility line trenches may extend
below the groundwater table Therefore installation of the underground utilities may
require dewatering of the trenches For utility line inverts that are about 4 to 5 feet below
the existing ground surface we believe that that a standard bedding consisting of 6 inches
of open graded gravel may be used below the pipes for support For utility lines with
inverts extending deeper than 5 feet below the existing ground surface we envision that
soft and or loose soils likely will be encountered and will require special bedding
considerations
Free draining granular materials such as open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation also should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above
the pipes or groundwater level to provide adequate support around the pipes The upper
portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes or groundwater level

Pavement Design
We anticipate that flexible pavements will be used to serve the new residential
and or commercial development In general we have considered only light vehicle loading
for the roadways of this development consisting primarily of passenger vehicles and light
pick up trucks with less than 1 million equivalent single axle loads over the 40 year design
life of the pavement Based on the above assumptions we recommend the following
flexible pavement section be used for planning purposes
Flexible Pavements Low Volume Roads
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
3 0 Inch Asphalt Treated Base Course
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
11 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade
The subgrades below the aggregate subbase materials should be scarified to a
depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The aggregate base course
and aggregate subbase materials where required should consist of crushed basaltic
aggregates compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction CBR tests should
be performed during detailed design to confirm the adequacy of the above pavement
structural section
Surface Drainage

should be maintained to carry the surface water off the site Surface water ponding should
not be allowed on the site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to
isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs
extend a minimum of 2 inches into the soils below the aggregate base course and
aggregate subbase layers to reduce the potential for migration of excessive landscape
water into the pavement section

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 The borings were extended to depths
of about 31 5 to 41 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with auger equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch or 24 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
In addition relatively undisturbed samples of the soft and or loose lagoonal
deposits at the site were retrieved by hydraulically pushing a 3 inch OD thin wall Shelby
tube into the soil formation The hydraulic pressures needed to advance the sampler are
recorded as the Penetration Resistance in pounds per square inch psi per foot of
penetration on the Logs of Borings
Pocket penetrometer and torvane shear tests were performed on selected
cohesive soil samples retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an
indication of the unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample The torvane shear
test provided a quick estimate of the undrained shear strength of the soil sample
Results of the pocket penetrometer tests and the torvane shear tests are presented on
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Eight one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on selected samples of the
near surface soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils under a surcharge
pressure of 55 psf Results of the swell tests performed are presented on Plate B 1
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results
of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Two Consolidation tests ASTM D 2435 with time rates of consolidation were
performed on samples of the soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the
compressible soils encountered The results of the consolidation tests are presented on
Plates B 3 and B 4
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 5 and B 6
