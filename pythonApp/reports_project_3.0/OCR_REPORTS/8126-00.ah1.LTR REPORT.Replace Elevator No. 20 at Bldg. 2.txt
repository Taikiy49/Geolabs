PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS

to serve the existing two-story building.

specified in Unified Facilities Criteria (UFC) 3-490-01, Elevators.  In support of the new 
elevator, we understand the elevator machine room will need to be reconstructed to 
house the larger machinery.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield 
volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older volcano, Waianae, is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in Age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger 
shield volcano, Koolau, is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in 
age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Koolau shield 
reached the end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
The geologic conditions underlying the project site are the result of a complex 
series of deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio-eustatic sea level changes 
and post-erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch. Following the 
cessation of volcanic activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial soil materials were deposited 
in a deltaic or estuarine environment in the area of Joint Base Pearl Harbor-Hickam. 
The sea level fluctuated several times, receding perhaps 300 feet below and rising 
about 100 feet above the present sea level. Concurrent with a rise in sea level during the 
late Pleistocene era, the eruption of the Salt Lake-Makalapa, member of the Honolulu 
Volcanic Series, mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash, which 
consolidated to form volcanic tuff (locally known as “mudrock”) layers. Subsequent erosion 
dissected and thinned those mantles of volcanic tuff. The project site is generally located 
on the western extent of the volcanic tuff layers. The subsurface conditions of the project 
site generally consist of fill materials overlying weathered volcanic tuff materials and 
volcanic tuff formation at greater depths. 
SITE DESCRIPTION 
The project is generally located in the Southeast Loch at Joint Base Pearl 
Harbor-Hickam on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. Building 2 is generally a U-shaped, two-
story structure housing an auditorium, conference rooms, classrooms, and offices. A 
planted courtyard with wide concrete-covered walkways occupy the central to the 
northeastern portion of the lot. 
Based on our field observations, the walkways surrounding the existing elevator are 
mostly flat. Elevation of the existing ground surface was estimated at about +13 feet Mean 
Sea Level (MSL) based on Google Earth. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITION 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling one boring extending to a 
depth of approximately 20 feet below the existing ground surface.  
Our field exploration indicates that the project site is generally underlain by fill 
materials over weathered volcanic tuff, cinder sands, and volcanic tuff formation extending 
to the maximum depth drilled. In general, we encountered medium stiff sandy and silty fill 
materials extending to a depth of about 2.3 feet below the existing ground surface.

SEISMIC DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site will be subjected to seismic activity and should be evaluated for the 
potential for soil liquefaction. For seismic design considerations, UFC 3-301-01, 
Structural Engineering, references the International Building Code, 2018 Edition 
(IBC 2018) and American Society of Civil Engineers 7-16, Minimum Design Loads and 
Associated Criteria for Buildings and Other Structures, 2016 Edition (ASCE 7-16). 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground 
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess pore water 
pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes. Soils most 
susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained sands and loose silts 
with little cohesion. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, the 
geology in the area, and our engineering analyses, the potential for soil liquefaction at the 
project site is non-existent due to the presence of stiff/dense fill and weathered tuff 
materials over volcanic tuff formation encountered within the depth explored. Therefore, 
the potential for liquefaction is not a design consideration at this project site.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered stiff/dense fill and weathered volcanic 
tuff materials over hard volcanic tuff formation extending to the maximum depth explored 
of about 20 feet below the existing ground surface. We encountered groundwater in the 
drilled boring at a depth of about 9.7 feet below the existing ground surface, corresponding 
to an elevation of about +3.3 feet MSL, at the time of our field exploration. It should be 
noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to tidal fluctuations, storm surge 
conditions, rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other

structural loads, we envision that the proposed elevator tower and machine room may be

Shallow Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we 
recommend using shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous strip 
footings to support the proposed elevator tower and machine room. 
We recommend an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square 
foot (psf) for design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted fill material or 
highly weathered volcanic tuff material. The bearing pressure may be increased to 
4,000 psf for footings bearing directly on the hard volcanic tuff formation. 
This bearing value is for dead plus-live loads and may be increased by 
one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. In 
general, the bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below 
the lowest adjacent finished grade. 
Where the footing subgrade is within transition of soil and volcanic tuff formation, 
we recommend over-excavating footing subgrade in the rock formation at least 
12 inches and backfilled with select granular fill to provide uniform support. 
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below an 
imaginary 45-degree plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench, 
or the foundations should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility 
lines. 
The bottom of footing excavations in soil materials should be recompacted to at 
least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing 
surface prior to placing reinforcing steel and concrete. Soft and/or loose materials 
encountered at the bottom of footing excavations should be over-excavated until dense 
materials are exposed in the footing excavation. The over-excavation should be 
backfilled with select granular fill materials moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage 
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. Alternatively, the bottom of the footing may 
extend down to bear directly on the underlying competent material. 
If the foundations for the proposed structures are designed and constructed in 
strict accordance with our recommendations, the total settlement of the foundations is 
estimated to be approximately 1 inch or less with differential settlements on the order of 
about 0.5 inches.

Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction between the 
base of the foundation, the bearing materials, and by passive earth pressure developed 
against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of the foundation. A coefficient 
of friction of 0.30 and 0.35 may be used for foundations bearing directly on the 
recompacted soils and weathered volcanic tuff materials, respectively. Resistance to 
lateral loads due to passive earth pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid 
pressure of 350 pcf for foundations embedded in the on-site soils and/or compacted 
structural fill materials. The passive earth pressure value assumes that the concrete for 
the footings is cast neat against on-site materials. Otherwise, the soils around the 
footings should be well-compacted (minimum of 90 percent relative compaction). Unless 
covered by pavements or slabs, the passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches 
below the finished grade should be neglected. 
Geolabs representative should observe the footing excavations prior to placing 
the reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions and the 
required embedment depths. Observation of the foundation excavations should be 
designated as a “Special Inspection” item in accordance with Section 1704 of the 
International Building Code (2018).

We recommend compacting the backfill behind retaining structures to between 
90 and 95 percent relative compaction. Over-compaction of the retaining structure 
backfill should be avoided. The backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content prior to being utilized as backfill materials. 
In general, the at-rest condition should be used for retaining structures where the 
top of the structure is restrained from movement prior to backfilling of the wall. The 
active condition should be used only for gravity retaining walls and retaining 
structures that are free to deflect by as much as 0.5 percent of the wall height. 
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, line loads, and point loads within a 
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the retaining structures should be 
considered in the design. For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded 
side of the retaining structure, a rectangular distribution with a uniform pressure 
equal to 47 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of 
the structure, which is restrained, may be used in the design. For retaining 
structures that are free to deflect (cantilever), a rectangular distribution equal to 
31 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire height of the 
structure may be used for the design. 
Dynamic Lateral Earth Pressures 
Dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading (amax = 0.261g) may be 
estimated by using 5.4H2 pounds per lineal foot of wall length for level backfill 
conditions, where H is the height of the wall in feet.  The resultant force should be 
assumed to act through the mid-height of the wall.  An appropriately reduced factor 
of safety may be used when dynamic lateral earth forces are accounted for in the 
design of the retaining structures. 
Drainage 
Retaining walls should be well-drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of 
permeable material, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), 
placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of 
the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or to weep holes. As an alternative, a 
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used

Slabs-on-Grade 
We envision concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the proposed elevator 
machine room reconstructed near the existing grade. Based on our field exploration and 
laboratory testing conducted on the soil samples retrieved near the ground surface, we 
envision the near-surface soil materials at the project site may exhibit a relatively moderate 
to high potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations. To 
reduce the potential of structural distresses to the lightly loaded slabs-on-grade, we 
recommend providing a minimum of 12 inches of non-expansive select granular fill 
materials below the concrete slab cushion as the capping fill. Prior to placing the capping 
fill, the slab subgrades should be scarified to a depth of at least 12 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to 
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The select granular capping fill materials 
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to 
at least 90 percent relative compaction. 
The bottom of thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be 
embedded at least 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade. It should be emphasized 
that the areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the edges of the 
slabs with low expansion, relatively impervious soils to reduce the potential for water 
collecting below the footings and slabs. These areas should also be graded to divert water 
away from the slabs and to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs and 
foundations. 
For interior building slabs, which will not be subjected to vehicular traffic or 
machinery vibrations, we recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill 
below the slabs for uniform support. The cushion fill layer is in addition to the 12-inch 
capping layer as recommended above. The cushion fill should consist of open-graded 
gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) and would serve as a capillary moisture break. To 
reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor

Excavation 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the boring, we envision rock 
excavation for the new footing construction. Due to limited headroom space and proximity 
of adjacent existing footing/structure, use of conventional heavy excavation equipment, 
such as a large bulldozer, excavator, or similar heavy construction equipment, may not be 
feasible to achieve the excavations into the rock. Therefore, special rock excavation 
technique may be implemented to reduce excessive vibration for potential damage to the 
existing aging structure. 
Corrosion Potential 
Chemical analyses, including pH, minimum resistivity, chloride contents, and sulfate 
contents, were performed on a selected soil sample obtained during our field exploration 
for corrosivity evaluation. The test results are presented on Plate B-4 of Appendix B. 
Resistivity generally is recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. Generally, a lower 
resistivity results in a greater potential for corrosion of buried metallic objects. Conversely, 
a higher resistivity means the soil is less likely to contribute to the corrosion of metallic 
objects. Based on the test results presented on Plate B-4, the subsurface soils at the 
project site exhibit a minimum resistivity value of about 1,800 ohm-cm, indicating that the 
subsurface soils are very corrosive (Corrosion Ratings of 2) to buried metallic structures. 
Therefore, we recommend properly designing metallic substructures (such as piping) for 
protection against the potential for corrosion.

The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in the soil. 
In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), sulfate 
content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. Based on the pH, 
chloride and sulfate content tests performed on the on-site soils, the tested pH values are 
greater than 5.0 and the tested chloride and sulfate content values are relatively low. 
Therefore, we believe that either Type I or Type II cement may be used for the concrete in 
contact with the ground planned for the project. It may be appropriate to consult with a 
professional corrosion engineer to review the test results and provide detailed 
recommendations for corrosion protection.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface condition at the project site was explored by drilling and sampling 
one exploratory boring, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 20 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The boring was drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with 4-inch OD solid-stem augers and coring tools. The boring was drilled at the 
approximate location shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock materials were described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested 
Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the 
International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Log of Boring, Plate A-1. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Log of Boring indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Core samples of the harder and/or rock materials encountered at the project site 
were obtained by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with 
ASTM D2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling 
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation or harder materials. The rock 
material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and 
description.

The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. For example, if 2.5 feet of

and would be shown on the Log of Boring as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used 
to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the “Practical Handbook of Physical

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation 
of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Log of Boring at the appropriate 
sample depth. 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. A graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
One one-inch Ring Swell test was performed on a relatively undisturbed sample 
to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results are summarized 
on Plate B-2. 
Three Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were performed on 
selected intact core runs to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the 
volcanic tuff formation encountered. The test results are presented on Plate B-3. 
One set of Corrosion test, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity (ASTM 
G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), was performed 
by our office and TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on a selected soil sample obtained 
from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-4.
