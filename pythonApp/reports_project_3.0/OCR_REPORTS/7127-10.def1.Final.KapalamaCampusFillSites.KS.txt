PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located on the down slope (south) side of Reservoir Service Road 
in the upper portion of the Kamehameha Schools Kapalama Campus, on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii, as shown on the Project Location Plan, Plate 1.  Based on the information provided, 
there are three undocumented fill site areas encompassed by the study. The three fill areas 
are designated as Area Nos. 1, 2, and 3. The Reservoir Service Road is an asphalt paved 
road accessed by Waonahele Road, which provides vehicle access to two existing reservoir 
tank sites.  
Fill Area Nos. 1 and 2 adjoin each other and are situated between an active water 
reservoir tank and an inactive tank, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.1. Fill Area No. 3 is 
located near the end of Reservoir Service Road, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.2, with a 
much smaller footprint area than Fill Area Nos. 1 and 2. 
We understand that the fill areas contain undocumented soil, rock materials, and 
limited volume of construction debris, other solid waste and organic green waste. The fills 
were not placed under controlled and engineered conditions.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Koolau Volcano reached the 
end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
Natural substrates in the project area are generally composed of basaltic rock built 
by extrusion of the lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series. These rocks generally are 
characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt interbedded with thin clinker layers. 
In-situ chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million 
years. The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic soils. In 
general, saprolite is composed of mainly silty material and is typical of the tropical 
weathering of volcanic rocks. The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with 
increasing depth. 
Human activities have resulted in the placement of a mantle of fill material over the 
original residual soils at the site.  The fill materials included soils, cobbles, boulders and 
asphaltic fragments. It was reported that the fill was imported from other areas on campus 
and did not originate from sources located off-campus. In general, it appears that this fill 
was not placed as engineered fill.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The fill areas form open table-like areas with relatively level surfaces and steep 
perimeter slopes [approximately one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V)] on the Makai sides. 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
10 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 10, extending to depths ranging between 
about 16.0 and 51.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, we performed 17 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) probes, designated as P-1 through P-17, extending to 
depths of about 5 to 10 feet below the ground surface along the perimeter of the slope toe. 
The approximate boring and probe locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plate 2.1 and 
2.2. 
Based on the borings drilled and DCP probes, the project site generally is underlain 
by surface fill soils overlying residual soils and saprolites. The surface fill soils generally 
consisted of soft to stiff silty clays and clayey silts with some boulders and cobbles 
extending down to depths ranging between about 8 and 34.5 feet below the ground 
surface. The residual and saprolite materials are generally very stiff clayey and silty soils 
existing to the maximum depth drilled of approximately 51.5 feet below the ground surface. 
Solid waste and organic green waste materials were not encountered in the borings drilled. 
For illustration purposes, the Generalized Geologic Cross-Sections A-A’ through 
D-D’ depicting the interpreted subsurface conditions at the project site are provided on 
Plates 3.1 through 3.4. 
We did not encounter groundwater in our borings at the time of our field exploration. 
However, groundwater levels likely will change due to seasonal precipitation, surface water 
runoff, and other factors. 
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings and DCP probes 
conducted during our field exploration, the undocumented fill materials were placed on 
the stiff residual and saprolite soils with various thicknesses from about 8 to 34.5 feet. 
The undocumented fills consisted of soft to stiff silty clays and clayey silts with some 
boulders/cobbles and asphaltic fragments.

conducted at selected cross sections A-A’ through D-D’ as shown on the Site Plans,

Finite element and limit equilibrium slope stability analyses were performed at the 
above cross-sections using the computer programs Slide (Version 6.0) from 
RocScience, Inc.  Finite Element Method (FEM) is a numerical technique for finding 
approximate solutions to boundary value problems for partial differential equations.  The 
model for the slope stability analyses is established using the two dimensional (2D) finite 
element stress analysis method and/or Bishop Method to compute the factor of safety of 
the critical slip circle in 2D plane strain state based on Mohr-Coulomb failure criterion. 
The slope stability analyses were conducted under static and seismic conditions for 
long-term and short-term transient considerations. A surcharge load of 100 pounds per 
square feet (psf) was modeled for potential parking and/or storage in the future at the site.

Based on the results of our field exploration and slope stability analyses, we 
believe that the existing undocumented fill slopes would unlikely result in a long-term 
slope instability at the site. Provisions should be given to maintain the ground surface 
drainage pattern and divert the run-off water away from the slope face, to reduce risk of 
local slope instability at a shallow depth due to erosion and/or undermining. For 
long-term performance, we recommend placing a layer of erosion control matting on the 
slope surface. 
Based on gentle sloping ground conditions observed on the top of the fill slopes, 
a grading operation would likely be required to provide level ground condition for any 
potential reuse of the site. Grading should avoid filling operation and only consist of cut, 
to reduce surcharge load on the fill slope. 
It should be noted that soft subgrade condition may by encountered in localized 
areas. Future development may consider placing a layer of geogrid, such as Tensar 
TX140 or equivalent, on the ground surface where soft and yielding subgrades occur, to 
provide a relative stable ground condition. 
Graded areas should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to 
carry surface water off-site and away from the existing slope.  Surface water ponding 
should not be allowed on-site. 
LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from our field exploration. Variations of conditions may occur, and the 
nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until design or construction 
is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the 
discussions presented herein. 
The borings and DCP probe locations are approximate and were estimated based 
on the Topographic Survey Map transmitted by Kamehameha Schools on March 31, 2015.  
Elevations of the borings and DCP probes were estimated from the topographic plan 
provided. The physical locations and elevations should be considered accurate only to the 
degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on graphic representations of the borings depict the 
approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual transition. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration. 
However, it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in seasonal rainfall, 
and other factors. These data have been reviewed and interpretations made in the 
formulation of this report.

A P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling ten boreholes, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 10, extending to depths of 
about 16 to 51.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2.  The borings were drilled 
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary wash 
drilling/coring tools. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous basis.  These classifications were further reviewed visually and by 
testing in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made 
to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-10. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
In addition, EA Engineering, Science and Technology, Inc. PBC (EA) collected and 
analyzed environmental samples for potential chemical contamination (refer to “Site 
Investigation Report Addendum, Kamehameha Schools Kapalama Campus Fill Site,” 
prepared by EA and dated March 2016).

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Six Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Seven Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-3 through B-9. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-10 and B-11.

Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Tests 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were also explored by conducting 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) testing at seventeen locations, designated as P-1 
through P-17, extending to depths of about 5 to 10 feet below the existing ground 
surface. Four DCP tests were conducting in Area 1 (DCP-1 thru DCP-4); seven DCP 
tests were conducting in Area 2 (DCP-5 thru DCP-11), and six DCP tests were 
conducting in Area 3 (DCP-12 thru DCP-17). The approximate DCP locations are shown 
on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2. 
Portable Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) was used for probing by driving an 
8-mm diameter 60° steel cone tip with an 8-kg hammer falling 575-mm in vertical height. 
The blow counts were recorded per every or near 75-mm (3-inch) penetration and 
converted to standard penetration resistance (SPT, ASTM D1586) using correlation 
between Penetration Index (PI) and SPT, developed by Sowers and Hedges and later 
Livneh and Ishai. Both PI and SPT blow counts are shown on the Logs of DCP at the 
appropriate depths, presented on the Logs of DCP, Plates C-1 through C-17.
