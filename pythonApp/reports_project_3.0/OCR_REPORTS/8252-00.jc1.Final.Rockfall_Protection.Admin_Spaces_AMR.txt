We conducted an evaluation of the existing rockfall hazard at the project site to 
formulate a design concept and schematic plan for an appropriate rockfall protection 
program during the construction of the Repair Administrative Spaces project at Aliamanu 
Military Reservation in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  
This report is based on our geological reconnaissance of the existing site conditions, 
observation of the slope scaling operation, and our preliminary engineering and statistical 
analyses of the potential rockfall processes anticipated at the project site. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and 
Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age (2.7 to 3.4 million years ago) and forms the bulk of the western one-third of 
the island. The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene 
(Ice Age) in age (2.2 to 2.5 million years ago) and forms majority of the eastern two-thirds 
of the island. The end of volcanic activity associated with the Koolau Volcano was followed 
by a long period of landmass erosion whereby deep stream valleys were carved into the 
dome shaped Koolau Shield Volcano. 
Approximately 850,000 to 1,000,000 years ago, some limited volcanic activity 
resumed with the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series from secondary vents scattered 
across the eastern portion of the Island of Oahu. The eruptions occurred along the 
established secondary rift zones of the Koolau Volcano. Some of the most notable 
landmarks located on the Island of Oahu, such as Punchbowl and Diamond Head Craters, 
and including Aliamanu Crater, were created by the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic 
Series. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available 
to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea 
level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was correspondingly 
lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. When the sea level 
was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments filled pre-existing 
depressions and accumulated at higher elevations. 
The existing roadway connecting to the Aliamanu Tunnel is situated along the 
northern part of Aliamanu Crater in the Salt Lake area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. The slopes of Aliamanu Crater generally consist of southerly facing slopes towards 
Foster Village, which typically are composed of weathered bedded volcanic tuff rock.  The 
rock formations are mainly inclined downward towards the central volcanic vent. The 
volcanic tuff rock comprising Aliamanu Crater was created by the fall-out deposition of 
super-heated volcanic ash and cinders containing some entrained blocks of basaltic lava 
rock, which were emitted from the Aliamanu volcanic vent.

Site Reconnaissance 
Based on our field reconnaissance observation, the existing slope generally consists 
of a natural sub-vertical slope near the top of the crater rim and a sub-vertical cut slope at 
the access road.  These sub-vertical slopes are connected by a relatively steep natural tuff 
rock slope generally having an inclination of about 1.5 horizontal to 1 vertical (1.5H:1V). 
Generalized Slope Profiles (Sections ‘A-A’ through ‘J-J’) are illustrated on Plates 3.1 through 
3.5. 
The upper natural slopes generally consisted of the highly weathered friable 
formation extending from the east to the west with a near-vertical drop approximately 15 to 
30 feet high. Unstable tuff rock outcrops and loose and/or eroded tuff rock fragments were 
observed above the sub-vertical cut slope. Majority of the rock fragments ranged about 2 to 
3 feet long in dimension with a thin and slender shape. Based on our reconnaissance, 
numerous potential rockfall sources are present from the top of the crater rim down to the 
brow of the sub-vertical cut slope.  We believe the observed rockfall sources are potential 
rockfall hazards due to the relatively tall height and adverse tuff rock bedding orientation 
(sloping down towards the access road). The potential rockfall impact zone would generally 
be the entire width of the access road. 
The lower cut slope was constructed during the original construction of the facility 
access roadway. The height of the lower cut slope varies between about 20 and 30 feet. 
The lower cut slope generally exposed massive tuff formation.  However, adverse bedding, 
which generally slopes down towards the direction of the access road, was observed along 
the brow of the lower cut slope. Due to the adverse bedding along the brow of the cut slope, 
we believe that the tuff rock along the brow of the cut slope is a potential rockfall source. 
However, we believe that the rockfall impact zone would generally be limited to a narrow 
area near the toe of the cut slope.  Photographs of potentially unstable rock outcrops and 
loose boulders/cobbles are provided in Appendix A.

Although the high potential of rockfall hazard is presented throughout the steep slope 
terrain within the project limits, the thin “laminated” volcanic tuff formation generally tends to 
break down to smaller fragments from falling impact due to the brittleness characteristics 
with low potential for rolling and/or bouncing as a hard basalt rock. 
Subsequently, a slope scaling operation was conducted by the subcontractor for the 
removal of the identified unstable rock outcrops and loose boulders/cobbles during the 
rappelling site observation. The hazardous rocks and cobbles/boulders were substantially 
removed from the exposed steep crater rim slope, generally using manual scaling bars. 
Some large blocks were identified in nine locations, either along the brow of the lower 
cut slope or over-hanging near the top of the lower cut slope at the facility entrance. 
Approximate locations of unstable rock blocks are presented on the Site Plan, Plate 2. Due 
to the large sizes of the blocks, the manual scaling bar removal method was not feasible. 
Additional mitigation measures, such as shotcrete pedestal supports, may be considered 
against the sliding force and/or supporting over-hanging weight. 
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our assessment of the potential rockfall conditions included a site reconnaissance 
consisting of rappelling down the face of the crater slope to identify unstable rock outcrops 
and loose boulders/cobbles on the slope face, an extensive slope scaling to remove the 
hazardous rockfall sources and conducting a preliminary computer simulation of potential 
rockfall activity. A detailed discussion of the preliminary computer rockfall simulation 
analyses is provided in the following section. 
Based on the results of the rockfall simulation analysis, we believe the rockfall hazard 
may greatly impact the construction operation along the existing access road located at the 
bottom of the slope within the horizontal distance of 20 to 30 feet from the toe of the slope if 
the slope is left in the current condition.  In general, the entire width of the access road will 
be exposed to potential rockfall hazards.  
Considering the rockfall hazardous sources to be removed from approximately 
80 percent of the upper steep crater rim slope, the rockfall simulation analysis indicated that 
the potential impact from the rockfall would be reduced to a horizontal distance of about 
10 feet from the toe of the slope. 
Based on the results of our preliminary rockfall simulation analysis, we recommend 
implementing rock slope scaling of the crater slope from the top of the crater rim down to 
the top of the sub-vertical cut slope along the access road to remove unstable rock outcrops 
and loose boulders/cobbles. Removing the potential rockfall sources on the upper slopes 
would reduce the impact zone from rockfall to about 10 feet from the toe of the slope.  This 
zone would be considered the buffer zone, and no construction activities should be 
conducted within this buffer zone without the specific shelter protection.  We understand that 
the rockfall mitigation for the potential hazard conditions is only a temporary mitigation for

Rock slope scaling generally consists of the removal of potential rockfall hazards 
such as existing boulders/cobbles, loose rocks, and unstable rock outcroppings from the 
slope surface. Rock slope scaling is not a permanent mitigation alternative because the 
slope scaling must be repeated at future intervals to maintain the slope free of loose rock 
and keep pace with evolving slope erosion conditions. 
To further reduce the potential for rockfall hazard beyond the 10-foot buffer zone, we 
recommend placing a temporary 13-foot high protective barrier along the edge of the 10-foot 
wide buffer zone. This temporary protective barrier would retain smaller rock debris from 
rolling/bouncing into the working area. 
The installation and anchoring of wire mesh panels on sloped surfaces to control 
falling rock material beneath the net are not considered cost-effective due to the large slope 
area to be covered containing several widely scattered, small dimension, loose rock 
materials. In addition, significant disturbance of the slope is anticipated due to the cutting of 
vegetation for mesh installation and the drilling and grouting of the anchorage system. 
Where entry and egress are planned at portals, we recommend placing a hardened 
shelter, such as a steel Conex box, within the 10-foot wide buffer zone. The hardened shelter 
should also extend at least 5 feet beyond the edge of the buffer for additional protection of 
personnel. 
Large unstable rock blocks were observed and exposed near the top of the lower cut 
slope that were not able to be removed by the manual scaling bars. Photographs of the 
unstable rock blocks are shown on Plates 4.1 through 4.10.  Considering greater sliding 
force and/or over-hanging weight, it may be prudent to leave them in place supported by the 
shotcrete pedestal with the high strength rebar anchors.  The rebar anchors should consist 
of 1-inch diameter all-threaded bars with an ultimate tensile strength of 150 kips per square 
inch (ksi).  Details of the schematic mitigation of the unstable rock blocks are provided on 
Plates 5.1 through 5.9.   
Preliminary Computer Rockfall Simulation Analysis 
As part of our evaluation of the potential rockfall hazard conditions at the site, we 
performed a computer simulation of potential falling rock activity using input data collected 
during our site reconnaissance. We used the CRSP Version 4.0 for our analysis.  
The slope sections were generated based on the topographic information provided 
by Hensel Phelps Construction Co. on June 9, 2021. The slope sections were further 
modified/verified based on the visual measurements during the site reconnaissance. The 
purpose of the CRSP analysis was to model the existing slope and potential rockfall 
conditions to obtain a statistical analysis of the simulation results to assist in the

evaluation of potential rockfall encroachment and design parameters for an appropriate 
rockfall protection structure. 
The CRSP is a widely accepted engineering tool used to estimate potential rockfall 
behavior by simulating rockfall based on input parameters assigned on a site-specific 
basis. The input parameters for this project were assigned based on our field observations 
and measurements collected in the field. The program provides a statistical analysis of 
potential rockfall activity based on the slope topographic profile and other specific input 
information such as rock size, shape, and numerical parameters used to quantify the 
typical ground surface conditions. Information obtained from the CRSP analysis includes 
the predicted falling rock velocity, bouncing height, kinetic energy, and encroachment 
distance. The rock impact kinetic energy (reported in foot-ton units) can be computed 
from the rockfall simulation for any specified point on the slope. In this analysis, the 
possible rock impact energy was calculated at a designated analysis point set at the top 
of the lower cut slope. Colorado Rockfall Simulation Program calculates the impact 
energy based on the rock size and shape, rock density, slope inclination, and other input 
parameters that describe the slope surface conditions, including vegetation and ground 
surface roughness and hardness. In addition, the CRSP computes the possible rock 
bounce height at a specified analysis point based on the model input information. The 
rockfall simulation output information, when used in conjunction with a good, experienced 
understanding of the potential rockfall conditions and processes at a study site, is very 
useful to assist in the site-specific design and physical location of appropriate rockfall 
protection systems. 
Ten selected model slope profiles (identified as Generalized Slope Profiles ‘A-A’ 
through ‘J-J’) were developed from the gathered topographic information and field visual 
observation to support our preliminary CRSP analysis. The slope profile locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The graphic representation of the idealized slope profiles 
showing the typical character and general geometric condition of the slope used in our 
rockfall simulation analyses are shown on Plates 3.1 through 3.5. 
Our preliminary CRSP analysis was performed using discoidal shaped boulders 
(in respect to the general tuff material anticipated on-site) with a thickness of 1.5 feet. The 
diameter of the discoidal shaped boulders ranging from 1.0 to 3.0 feet was initiated from 
the potential rockfall source areas, generally between the top of the crater rim and the 
brow of the sub-vertical cut slope. For each rockfall simulation run, 1,000 source rocks 
were released from random points within the rockfall source region to develop a statistical 
distribution of the results. We used selected numerical input coefficients to approximate 
the typical ground surface hardness, relief, roughness, and vegetation conditions.  
Our minimum rockfall protection criterion is defined as the probable interception 
and catchment of 90 percent of possible rockfall hazards assessed by the computer 
rockfall simulation analysis (CRSP). The 90 percent catchment criterion is a target that is 
commonly used in engineering practice for evaluation criteria that can be quantified by a 
statistical and probability analysis using model data for natural occurrences such as

rockfall activity. Because the analyses are based on statistics and probability, a 
100 percent criterion is impractical to achieve. 
Based on the results of our preliminary CRSP analyses for all rock sizes used, the 
possible rockfall encroachment to the access roadway (passing top of the lower 
sub-vertical cut slope) was found to be 100 percent. The percent passing was diminished 
at the location with a horizontal distance of about 20 to 30 feet from the toe of the lower 
cut slope. 
Considering extensive rock slope scaling to remove the significant amount of the 
loose rocks and unstable rock outcroppings, we believe the remaining potential rockfall 
source may be only limited within the top of the lower sub-vertical cut slope due to the 
adverse bedding orientation of the near-vertical cut slope. Therefore, the proposed rock 
slope scaling would significantly reduce the potential rockfall impact kinetic energy 
passing top of the lower sub-vertical cut slope and limit the restricted area or buffer zone 
to a horizontal distance of about 10 feet from the toe of the lower sub-vertical cut slope. 
Unstable rock outcroppings encountered at the top of the lower sub-vertical cut slope that 
is difficult to remove by hand scaling should have low impact kinetic energy ranging 
between 0.02 and 13.3 foot-tons and would fall within the 10-foot-wide buffer zone 
measured from the toe of the lower sub-vertical cut slope. 
In the areas further away from the toe of the slope, the potential impact from the 
rockfall may be diminished and may be protected by placing a temporary protective barrier 
along the edge of the 10-foot-wide buffer zone to retain smaller rock debris from 
rolling/bouncing into the working area. The estimated bounce heights of the falling rocks 
originating near the top of the cut slopes would range between 1 and 11 feet high. We 
envision that the temporary protective barrier may consist of a 3-foot concrete jersey 
barrier with a 10-foot tall, heavy-duty chain-link fence, providing up to 13-foot tall 
temporary barrier. A summary of the CRSP results produced for this project is provided 
in Appendix B. 
Large Unstable Rock Block Mitigation 
Large unstable rock blocks were observed and exposed near the top of the lower 
cut slope at the facility entrances, either sitting on the upper 1.5H:1V natural tuff rock 
slope or over-hanging on the near-vertical cut slope. Due to the larger massive weight 
posting the greater hazardous conditions near the facility entrances and uncertainties of 
the formation bedding orientation, we recommend keeping them in-place and supporting 
with the shotcrete pedestals. The purpose of the shotcrete pedestal support is to fill and 
cover the undercut features against future raveling. 
The large unstable rock blocks were observed in five locations between 
Station 0+00 and Station 15+00. Photographs of the unstable rock blocks and the slope 
geometry are shown on Plates 4.1 through 4.10. Schematic mitigation sections at each 
unstable rock block are presented on Plates 5.1 through 5.9, showing generalized

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from visual site observations and computer simulation and statistical 
analysis of potential rockfall activity only. Variation in the surface and subsurface 
conditions between our observations and analysis points may occur, and the nature and 
extent of these variations may not become evident until additional field exploration (such 
as site reconnaissance or near-surface probing) or construction is underway. If variations 
then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the findings and recommendations 
provided herein. 
It should be noted that slopes composed of rock materials (rock slopes) deteriorate 
with the passage of time due to natural weathering processes, wet-dry and hot-cold 
cycles, and erosion conditions. Due to the inherent deterioration of rock slopes resulting 
from natural processes (weathering, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and erosion conditions), 
the potential for rockfall hazard at any site changes with the passage of time. Therefore, 
the findings and recommendations contained herein may be used only within a 
reasonable time from the date of issuance of this letter report. Land use, site conditions, 
and/or other factors may change with the passage of time. Therefore, additional work 
(such as site reconnaissance for review of the site conditions) to evaluate the applicability 
of the findings and preliminary recommendations contained in this letter report due to 
changes with the passage of time may be required.  
Finally, there are no guarantees in the professional engineering and architectural 
design fields with respect to potential rockfall hazards due in large part to the 
unpredictable nature of rockfall activity, which is affected by many external variables, 
including natural and man-induced causes. This letter report has been prepared for the
