SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
New pavements will be constructed as part of the Hana Highway Milepost 4.3 
Emergency Repair project. Based on our field exploration, we anticipate the majority of the 
pavement subgrade soils for the new roadway generally will consist of soft to stiff silty clay 
and clayey silt. Based on the laboratory data, an R-value of 28 has been adopted for the 
subgrade soils in our pavement design analyses for this project. 
Typically, the new HDOT pavements include a drainage layer to facilitate drainage 
and increase the pavement life. However, since the existing pavement section along 
Hana Highway does not have a drainage layer, only pavement structural sections without 
a permeable drainage layer were considered in our pavement design. 
Based on the traffic data provided, the R-value obtained for the subgrade soil, 
requirements by HWY-LG, and potential construction considerations, we recommend 
using a flexible pavement structural section consisting of 2 inches of Poly-Modified 
Asphalt (PMA) concrete (AC) with over 8 inches of asphaltic concrete base (ACB) over 
6 inches of aggregate subbase (ASB) for the new pavements along Hana Highway at the 
project site. 
A temporary bypass roadway will be required for the project during construction. We 
recommend using a flexible pavement structural section consisting of 6.0 inches of ACB 
over 0.5 inches of aggregate base course over 6.0 inches of aggregate base course 
confined by Geoweb on compacted subgrade for the temporary roadway. 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the subbase and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface should 
be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off the 
pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
The text of this pavement justification report should be referred to for detailed 
discussion and specific design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our analysis performed 
for the Hana Highway Milepost 4.3 Emergency Culvert project on the Island of Maui, 
Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our 
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the design of pavements 
only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations 
noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located at Milepost 4.3 on Hana Highway in the Huelo area on 
the Island of Maui. We understand that heavy rains damaged the upstream side of the 
existing culvert. It appears that the existing rock wall structure above and on the sides of 
the culvert eroded away, exposing the underlying soil. We understand that shotcrete was 
applied to the exposed soil for temporary stabilization. We understand that the existing 
culvert will be replaced with a new larger culvert structure. To avoid complete closures of 
Hana Highway during the reconstruction of the existing culvert, a temporary bypass road 
will be constructed on the downstream side of the culvert. Based on the information 
provided, the approximate height of the bypass road will be up to 26 feet in height. 
This pavement justification report was prepared in support of the design of the new 
pavements for the project. It should be noted that a separate geotechnical engineering 
report has been prepared in support of the design of the culvert and temporary bypass 
road structures, site grading, underground utilities, and other elements of the project. 
Detailed discussion and geotechnical recommendations for the design of the other 
elements of the project are presented under separate cover in our Geotechnical 
Engineering Exploration report.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes: the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala 
(Pleistocene Epoch). The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain between these 
two volcanoes. The project site is located on the northeastern portion of the island. 
The East Maui Volcano is represented by the older rocks of Honomanu Volcanic 
Series, buried beneath younger volcanic deposits. The Honomanu Series is overlain by the 
Kula Volcanic Series, which generally consists of thick lava flows with appreciable 
embedded pyroclastic (ash and cinders) materials. Rocks of the Kula Series cover much of 
the present-day surface of the East Maui Volcano. 
A long period of deep erosion and sediment deposition occurred between the end of 
the Kula Volcanic Series and the beginning of the Hana Volcanic Series. The Hana Series 
generally consists of the youngest lava flows and cinder cones presently encountered at the 
East Maui ground surface. Although the Hana lavas were generally more fluid and the lava 
flows tended to be thinner and less massive than the preceding Kula lavas, thick 
accumulation of lava did occur in the valleys and valley heads. 
The erosional process of volcanic islands is dominated by the detachment of 
weathered soil and rock materials from the interior steep mountain slopes, and the 
movement of the eroded materials downslope by gravity toward the lowland mountain 
perimeter areas. Once these eroded slope materials (colluvium) reach a valley stream, 
alluvial processes become dominant, and the sediments are further transported by flowing 
water and deposited as alluvium. 
In general, mountain stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the 
stream flows may transmit large volumes of water for a very short duration. Because of this, 
the transport of sediment is intermittent, and the bulk of a stream's hydraulic load may 
consist of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fine soil particles. 
When the erosional base level of a land mass changes through long-term changes in 
sea-level or the sinking/raising of the island mass, sedimentary deposits may either by

When unconsolidated sedimentary deposits are buried in-place for long periods of 
time, chemical processes begin to alter the materials, simultaneously causing additional 
breakdown and weathering of the materials. The continued upland erosion generates new 
eroded materials, which are transported downslope and may cover the older alluvial and 
colluvial soil deposits. Depending on the erosional base level and the rate of sediment 
transport, these newer sediments may be generally transient in terms of geologic time.  
The project site is along Hana Highway traversing steep slopes on the sides of a 
large drainage ravine. Because the culvert site is located in steep terrain containing 
excavated cut slopes, surface alluvial and colluvial soil cover is anticipated to be limited 
at the project sites. 
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located at Milepost 4.3 on Hana Highway at Hanehoi Stream in 
the Huelo area on the Island of Maui. The project location is presented on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. The existing Hana Highway consists of two traffic lanes (one lane in each 
direction) and reduces to one lane at the culvert.  Hana Highway is paved with asphaltic 
concrete pavement. Based on our field observations, the existing asphaltic concrete 
pavement was observed to be in fair to good condition. Based on the available 
information, the existing ground surface along the culvert slopes down from east to west, 
with ground surface elevations ranging from about +542 to +541 feet Mean Sea Level 
(MSL). 
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to a depth of approximately 
50.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. One bulk sample, designated as Bulk-1, 
was collected for laboratory R-value testing to evaluate the pavement support of the 
near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
New pavements will be constructed for the culvert replacement project. Typically, 
new State of Hawaii, Department of Transportation (HDOT) pavements include a 
drainage layer to facilitate drainage and increase the pavement life. However, because 
the existing pavement section along Hana Highway does not have a drainage layer, only 
pavement structural sections without a permeable drainage layer were considered in our 
pavement analyses. 
Based on the traffic data provided, the R-value obtained for the subgrade soils, 
requirements by HWY-LG, and potential construction considerations, we recommend 
using a flexible pavement structural section consisting of 2 inches of Poly-Modified 
Asphalt (PMA) concrete (AC) with over 8 inches of asphaltic concrete base (ACB) over 
6 inches of aggregate subbase (ASB) for the new pavements along Hana Highway at the 
project site. 
A temporary bypass roadway will be required for the project during construction. 
We recommend using a flexible pavement structural section consisting of 6.0 inches of 
ACB over 0.5 inches of aggregate base course over 6.0 inches of aggregate base course 
confined by Geoweb on compacted subgrade for the temporary roadway.

3.6 
Subgrade Preparation Below Pavement Section 
We anticipate the project pavements will be constructed in cut and fill conditions. 
In general, the area within the contract grading limits should be cleared and grubbed 
thoroughly at the on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and properly disposed of off-site or in a 
designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over-excavated to expose stiff

and/or dense materials. The resulting excavation should be backfilled with aggregate 
subbase or base course materials. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be 
properly disposed of off-site. 
After clearing and grubbing, the future pavement areas should be excavated, 
where necessary, to the pavement subgrade level (i.e., bottom of the aggregate subbase 
layer). The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches 
(where possible), moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to 
the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of 
the same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO T180 (ASTM D1557). Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum 
dry density. 
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade soils, we 
recommend scarifying and re-preparing the soils as recommended above. Saturation and 
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor 
drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
well-compacted fill.  
3.7 
Fill Materials 
In general, the excavated on-site near-surface soils may be used as a source of 
general fill materials, provided that deleterious materials such as vegetation are removed 
and over-sized materials greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension are screened. 
Imported fill materials, if required, should consist of non-expansive select granular 
material, such as crushed coralline or basaltic materials. The materials should be 
well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension 
and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The 
materials should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio value of 20 or more and 
should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less. Imported fill materials should be tested 
for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the 
intended use.

3.8 
Compaction Requirements 
In general, fill and backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, 
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate subbase and 
base course materials required for the new pavements should conform to the 
requirements stipulated in Subsections 703.17 and 703.06 of the Hawaii State Standards, 
respectively. The ASB and base course should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in 8-inch level loose lifts, and compacted to no less 
than 95 percent of the maximum density according to AASHTO T180 (ASTM D1557). 
Asphaltic concrete base material should consist of asphalt-treated basaltic 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to at least 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity determined 
in accordance with AASHTO T209 (ASTM D2041). 
Asphaltic concrete material should be constructed in general accordance with 
Section 401 – Hot Mix Asphalt Pavement of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications 
for Road and Bridge Construction (2005) and subsequent amendments.

3.9 
Pavement Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface 
should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off 
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures.  Surface water ponding should not be 
allowed on the site during or after construction.

different from those assumed or considered in this report, then appropriate design 
modifications should be made.

information obtained from in-house literature research and field exploration. Variations of 
subsurface conditions between and beyond the field exploration points may occur, and 
the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is

borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used.

the approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a gradual 
transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the 
graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. These data have been 
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, it must be 
noted that fluctuation may occur due to variations in rainfall, temperature, and other

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, to a depth of about 50.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. In addition, one bulk sample, designated as Bulk-1, was obtained 
near the ground surface to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the 
near-surface soils. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight auger and coring tools. The boring and bulk sample locations are 
presented on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing 
in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard 
Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification 
System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil 
classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil Classification Log 
Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on 
the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-2.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration 
sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses,” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality 
of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling, 
mechanical, and handling-induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is

Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test results 
are provided on Plate B-1. 
One one-inch Ring Swell test was performed on a relatively undisturbed sample to 
evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils. The test results are summarized 
on Plate B-2. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected soil sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plate B-3. 
Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests were 
performed on selected relatively undisturbed soil samples in accordance with 
ASTM D2850. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the 
applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” samples. The test results and the 
stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5. 
Three Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were 
performed on selected rock core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive 
strength of the basalt rock formation encountered. The test results are presented on 
Plate B-6. 
One laboratory Resistance (R) Value test (ASTM D2844) was performed by Ninyo 
& Moore on a bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-7.
