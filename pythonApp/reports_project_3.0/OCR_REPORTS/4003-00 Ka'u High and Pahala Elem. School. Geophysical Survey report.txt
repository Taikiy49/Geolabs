SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our Ground Penetrating Radar GPR survey of the school
campus detected widespread subsurface anomalies indicative
of potential below ground lava tube and or cave features within
the basalt rock underlying the site The anomalies detected by
GPR represent contrasts between the electrical conductivity of
varying subsurface materials In this case the contrasts are
between the electrical properties of basalt rock and void space
air within the rock The anomalies detected by GPR are
believed to represent potential lava tube structures
Based on the results of the geophysical survey the more
significant subsurface anomalies appear to trend generally ina
northwest to southeast direction The estimated depths to these
anomalies range from about 10 to 20 feet below the existing
ground level The estimated height and width of the anomalies
range from on the order of about 5 to 30 feet in dimension
Based on these preliminary geophysical survey findings we
believe that the potential lava tubes at the site should not pose
an immediate threat of collapse However we recommend the
implementation of a limited core drilling exploration program to
confirm the anomalies detected by geophysical methods lava
tube existence and to evaluate the quality and quantity of
overburden rock material above the potential lava tube or cave
structures In addition we recommend implementing an
on going ground surface and structure observation program for
possible signs of subsurface instability at the ground surface to
be performed by the school maintenance personnel
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed
discussion and recommendations for additional exploration and
monitoring at the project site

This report presents the results of our geophysical surveys performed for the Ka u
High and Pahala Elementary School campus located in the District of Ka u on the Island of
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and our preliminary geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our geophysical surveys The results of our geophysical
surveys and preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations are intended for the
evaluation of the lava tube and cave structures existing at the project site and for future
planning of the school campus only The findings and preliminary recommendations

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site comprises the Ka u High and Pahala Elementary School campus and
portions of the Pahala Park located in the town of Pahala in the District of Ka u on the Island
of Hawaii We understand that several below ground lava tubes were discovered by some
students and during recent construction projects at the site Previous subsurface exploration
and construction projects had also encountered lava tubes and caves at the school campus
site Most of the caves encountered during previous construction projects were left as is
without disturbance to the features with the exception of those cavities located directly below
the buildings to be constructed Portions of the lava tubes below the buildings had been
collapsed where they were encountered and backfilled with compacted fill material Based

across the site
As a result of concerns pertaining to the possible effects of the below ground caves
on the existing facilities and improvements at the campus it was desired to implement an
initial non invasive exploratory technique to evaluate the site with respect to the vertical and
lateral extent of the potential lava tube and cave features The use of Ground Penetrating
Radar GPR was proposed and selected for use at the school campus The results of the

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes including the following Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea some activity during
recent geologic times Hualalai last erupted in 1801 and is considered dormant and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4003 00 Page 5
Mauna Loa and Kilauea both still active The project site is situated on the southeast
flank of Mauna Loa
The volume of Mauna Loa extending from the ocean floor is estimated to be on the
order of about 10 000 cubic miles making it possibly the largest volcanic mountain on
earth Furthermore it is estimated from current lava production that Mauna Loa may have
been forming for the past 1 to 2 million years Mauna Loa is actually composed of two
separate shield volcanoes Mauna Loa and Ninole The latter having been covered by
Mauna Loa lavas left only a few localized surface expressions visible today The Mauna
Loa shield has been built by volcanic eruptions along two principal rift zones that extend
southwestward and east northeastward from the summit caldera
The majority of the surface rock exposures at Mauna Loa consists of lavas and
volcanic deposits belonging to the Ka u Volcanic Series Pleistocene and Holocene age
and the older Kahuku Volcanic Series Pleistocene age In addition a regional ash
deposit identified as Pahala Ash Pleistocene age forms a soil mantle overlying and
interbedded within the rocks of the Ka u Volcanic Series The ash deposits typically occur
as kipukas isolated deposits with some significant accumulations ranging up to about
30 feet in thickness
The project site is located on land that has been mapped as Pahala Ash Beneath
the surficial ash layer rocks of the Ka u Volcanic Series may be found The Ka u Volcanic
Series essentially consists of tholeiitic basalt Based on our observation and a review of
previous geotechnical data for the school campus the basalt rock underlying the project
site consists of pahoehoe lava with a high incidence of embedded lava tubes
Pahoehoe basalt rock is generally the result of thinner more fluid lava flows
The feeding river lobes of a pahoehoe flow crust over to develop a continuous roof leaving
the interior lava stream to flow through a tunnel of its own making These remnant flow
tunnels or voids are known as lava tubes The lava tubes feed the advancing lava flow
front and margins As a result the lava tube may branch or converge generally in a
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4003 00 Page 6
downhill direction to maintain the lava flow In the waning stages of an eruption the lava
may drain from the main tube system leaving open tunnels ranging from several feet to in
excess of 40 feet in diameter The open tubes resemble tunnels with arched roofs and
nearly flat floors The main lava tubes may extend downslope for several miles
occasionally reaching all the way to the coastline Some of these large tube structures are
known to exist beneath the campus site and have been explored by underground traverse
Another type of void space typically encountered in pahoehoe rock is a pocket
blister These blisters are typically isolated closed void structures formed by trapped
volcanic gas within the flow interior Some blisters may extend a short distance downhill
before pinching out
The surface of pahoehoe flows are generally smoother than their rough and irregular
counterpart the a a flow A a flows form from generally more viscous slower moving lavas
and are expressed by a solid rock core sandwiched between an upper and lower less
dense clinker layer The pahoehoe flow surface may be expressed by broad
dome shaped hillocks created by the heaving and buckling of the flow crust as it
approaches obstructions

SITE RECONNAISSANCE
A general reconnaissance of the site was performed to observe and photograph the
existing ground surface features for evidence indicative of below ground lava tubes or
caves In general evidence indicative of below ground lava tubes or caves were not
readily observed at the ground surface However some localized erosional features were
observed indicating possible surficial soil loss into small shallow subsurface voids
Soil Erosion
The localized erosional features were observed as small irregular shaped holes on
the order of about 1 to 3 inches in diameter extending into the subsurface When probed
the depth of the holes appeared to be generally less than 3 feet The most concentrated
occurrence of the erosional features was observed at the location of the eastern most
vacant teacher s cottage north of the Library Building The concentration of erosional
features was observed to be approximately 60 feet due north of the Library Building along
the chain link fence bounding the cottage southeastern lawn The ground surface was
observed to be covered by brown silty topsoil Rock fragments representing gravel and or
cobblestones were visible within the upper portion of the exposed holes erosional
features The holes appeared shallow in depth and it was not discernable whether the
individual holes are inter connected in the subsurface or reside as isolated features
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4003 00 Page 8
The overall appearance suggests that the topsoil layer may overlie rocky fill that
may have been placed with inadequate finer gravel and cobbles to serve as choking
material in between the larger rocky boulders Thus the topsoil layer may be eroding and
piping into the underlying rocky fill by the flow of water creating randomly scattered
shallow holes acting as open conduits to the underlying rock fill In support of this theory
the GPR survey did not indicate the presence of a large scale anomaly in the area of the
observed holes Some shallow anomalies were detected but were rendered as
inconclusive Maintenance personnel reported that the area had been backfilled at one
time as a result of the holes growing larger
In addition a group of small diameter holes in the ground was observed in the
northern lawn surrounding the two cottages located north and northwest of the Science
Building The condition of these holes was similar to those described previously The
holes appeared randomly distributed over a broad area of generally level ground covered
by a silty soil with underlying rocky material The GPR survey did not detect significant
anomalies in the area Therefore it appears that the holes may also be the result of topsoil
erosion into the underlying rock fill and not related to a large lava tube or cave feature
Library personnel reported another localized area of erosion and soil loss into the
subsurface A single hole in the ground surface was observed adjacent to a utility vault
located at the front entrance to the Library Building It was reported that the small hole
existing alongside the vault required repeated filling of soil It was also reported that a
septic odor was often noted in the vicinity of the vault and hole following rainfall Our
observation confirmed the existence of a hole of approximately 2 inches in diameter
alongside the vault Manual probing indicated a firm bottom at about 6 inches below the
ground surface The GPR survey showed inconclusive data signatures due to the close
proximity and interference from the utility lines surrounding the vault Based on our
observation of the location of the hole the erosional feature may be the result of soil piping
into the underlying rock fill in the vicinity of the vault However it cannot be concluded
without performing intrusive exploration that this erosional feature is not related to a below
ground lava tube or cave feature

Proposed Bus Turn Around
Based on conversations with campus maintenance personnel a large cave structure
was encountered during construction of the existing Band Building at the site Some
portions of the exposed cave were backfilled with rock while the remainder of the cave was
left intact The location of the partially backfilled cave is believed to be located beneath the
parking lot fronting the north side of the Band Building Our GPR survey was able to detect
most of the below ground cave feature however the signature of the anomaly was noted
to be locally obscured in the data record as a result of encountering the backfilled portions
of the cave structure
Based on the GPR data some lava tube branches emanating from the main cave
structure were detected in the vicinity of the once planned bus turn around area The
detected anomalies had a tendency to appear and disappear over a short distance
suggesting that portions of the lava tube had been backfilled in this area Because the
GPR survey detected some significant size anomalies on the order of about 15 to 20 feet
in diameter we recommend performing additional exploration at this location especially
if it is desired to develop the area for building or driveway use
Campus Grading

about 5 to 15 feet thick were placed at the southern portion of the campus to provide for
the level playground and soccer field surfaces The field surfaces are elevated above the
neighboring residential parcels located southerly of the campus and are separated by an
embankment Rock outcroppings on the field ground surfaces were not observed in
contrast to most other areas observed throughout the campus The GPR survey

Other areas of the campus appear to have received only slight fills mainly to provide

Pahala Park Baseball Field
The geophysical survey included the relatively level ground comprising the open
play field of Pahala Park the area surrounding the comfort station and dugouts and the
paved parking lot We did not encounter significant ground penetrating radar anomalies
in these areas from our surveys The radar signatures generally showed poor resolution

the exposed hill slopes bordering the play field indicates that a significant thickness of silty
volcanic ash soils may be present at the site

Lava Tube Observation
Our site reconnaissance included observation of a lava tube system that had been
intercepted by the construction of a drainage drywell at the site The drywell providing
access to the lava tube is located at the northwestern corner of the existing tennis court
near the driveway entrance to the campus By using an extension ladder the lava tube
may be accessed through the drywell excavation The lava tube and cave structures were
observed without the use of survey equipment Selected photographs of the lava tube
reconnaissance are provided as Plates 3 through 6 of this report
The lava tube system appeared to consist of a network of meandering tunnel
branches with the majority of the lava tubes trending approximately southeast to northwest
Some of the spur branches from the main lava tube were observed to diverge and merge
again with the main lava tube Other spur branches were observed to pinch out as dead
ends Near the drywell lava tube entrance a localized opening in the floor of the main lava
tube revealed another 6 to 8 foot diameter lava tube at a lower elevation trending generally
in a north to south direction From the drywell entrance the lava tube and spur branches
were traversed for a distance of about 400 feet in a northerly and northwesterly direction
In general the observed lava tubes had an average height of about 6 to 15 feet and
widths on the order of about 8 to 25 feet The wider cave portions generally exist at the
intersections of two or more lava tubes The shape of the lava tubes ranged from round
to elliptical The tube system exhibited both smooth sided obstruction free tunnels and
boulder strewn floors with extensive open cracks in the tube ceiling or roof Based on our
observation of the lava tube entrance at the drywell the roof of the tube system appeared
to range on the order of about 6 to 15 feet in thickness The lava tube had been filled with
rock and debris effectively closing off the access in the southerly direction

Seismic Refraction Survey
Several locations of the campus were evaluated using seismic refraction equipment
in an effort to support the findings from the GPR survey The seismic refraction method
utilizes the recording and processing of first arrival times and velocities of seismic waveforms
generated at a shot point and received by a series of geophones set at intervals at the
ground surface The results of the seismic refraction survey were marginal in that heavy
construction and excavation equipment were operating at and near the site thereby creating
significant noise interference with the signals being received from the geophones
In general the seismic data indicated that a relatively low seismic wave velocity
surface layer ash soil overlies relatively high velocity deeper layers of rock formation The
data suggests that irregularities in the seismic wave velocities of the rock layers conclude
that void space or interbedded basalt clinker layers were prevalent at the site Although not
conclusive as stand alone data the seismic data obtained serves to support the GPR survey
in that the seismic wave velocity anomalies were detected that could be interpreted as below
ground void space or varying rock densities
GPR SURVEY
Ground Penetrating Radar is an electromagnetic geophysical method that uses
high frequency radio waves which are transmitted into the subsurface and reflected back
where they are received by a recording instrument The recording instrument generates
a continuous cross sectional profile of the reflected signals Variation in the reflected signal
is caused by the radar wave reflection at subsurface interfaces of materials having
contrasting electrical properties Because of the large contrast in electrical properties
between open void space and the surrounding rock material the reflected signal indicates
an anomalous signature within the profile at the location of the below ground void space
A Ground Penetrating Radar GPR geophysical survey was performed to scan the
open areas of the campus and the adjacent sport facilities for the presence of subsurface
anomalies indicative of potentially large lava tubes The intent of the GPR survey was to
expeditiously survey the open areas of the campus by non intrusive methods The
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4003 00 Page 13
accessible open fields and paved areas of the campus were surveyed to provide
preliminary information pertaining to the location of significant anomalies that may
represent below ground lava tube systems The survey was accomplished by traversing
the transceiver transmitter receiver antenna across the ground surface to obtain the
electrical data to generate a subsurface profile ranging up to depths of about 30 feet below
the ground level
Open areas of the campus were surveyed by parallel traverses of the antenna
spaced at about 10 feet on center trending roughly perpendicular to the anticipated
alignment of the lava tubes Obstructions such as buildings sidewalks plantings and
fences restricted the survey coverage to open areas only The lines of survey were set to
take advantage of the maximum run length to reduce signal interference from repeated
stopping and restarting of the profile alignment Confined space adjacent to buildings
could not be surveyed due to excessive interference signals created by the proximity of
building walls to the transceiver antenna Photographs of the GPR equipment and field
procedures are presented as Plates 6 7 of this report
Based on the results of the GPR survey there appears to be a number of significant
GPR anomalies that were traced as meandering features trending generally northwest to
southeast beneath portions of the campus These elongated anomalous features may
represent portions of lava tube systems In some cases the anomalies may represent
distinct lobes of lava rock differing in physical properties from the surrounding rock
formation
The GPR survey disclosed three main areas showing continuous anomalies that are
believed to represent potential lava tube systems The potential lava tube alignments were
traced for significant distances effectively increasing our confidence of their existence The
three main areas include the following
e from the northwest corner of the tennis court to the east side of the
Administration Building and to the parking lot north of the Band Building

from the east side of the soccer field to the center of the Administration
Building and to the parking lot north of the Band Building and
e from the south side of the elementary school classroom buildings to the field
west of the swimming pool and to the greenhouse buildings
In general the top of the more significant anomalies detected at the site were
estimated to range in depths from about 10 to 20 feet below the existing ground level The
widths of the more significant anomalies were estimated to range from about 5 to 30 feet
The height of the anomalies could not be accurately defined from the GPR data but is
estimated to be equal to or less than the anomaly width Smaller shallow anomalies that
were traced as linear trending features were detected and found to correlate with
underground utility lines and not below ground lava tube features It was occasionally
observed that the shallow utility line signatures would interfere with deeper anomaly

Other anomalous signatures were detected in the subsurface however geographic

and other localized portions of the campus

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of the GPR survey and field reconnaissance portions of the
site appear to be underlain by a network of variable size and shape lava tubes and cave
structures Overt signs of ground failure or subsidence such as surface cracking or abrupt
depressions in the ground surface were not observed The only significant ground cracks
observed were those located in the asphalt pavement of the administration parking lot and
driveway The cracks may be related to some slight settlement of the ground overlying the
potential lava tubes mapped as crossing beneath the paved area However visible signs
of significant settlement or ground failure were not observed at this location
Based on our review of existing borehole data our reconnaissance of the accessible
lava tube and the GPR data we believe that most of the lava tubes existing beneath the
site should not pose an imminent threat of collapse or damage to existing structures due
to the estimated depth and location of the lava tubes and cave structures Based on our
preliminary findings the general thickness of the overburden material appears to be
sufficient to prevent a sudden or catastrophic collapse of the below ground lava tube or
cave structure The overburden material may be defined as the total thickness of soil and
rock materials overlying the roof of the lava tube or cave The overburden material would
include the surface volcanic ash soil and the basalt rock extending from the ground surface
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4003 00 Page 17
to the top or roof of the lava tube It should be noted that the rock material portion of the
overburden material thickness is more critical than the soil material portion of the
overburden material thickness
We also would like to stress that our conclusion pertaining to the probable stability
of the lava tubes is based largely upon our limited visual observation of portions of the lava
tubes examined during our lava tube reconnaissance As such rock conditions such as
the rock quality and overburden thickness will likely vary across the site and within discrete
sections of the lava tubes
Furthermore based on the history of the campus ground failure has not occurred
at the site as a result of the collapse of a lava tube The existing relatively lightly loaded
structures should not be adversely impacted by the existence of the lava tubes However
should future heavily loaded structures be planned at the site a detailed site specific
subsurface exploration should be conducted to evaluate the site conditions
The GPR survey provides a good preliminary evaluation of the extent of potential
lava tubes beneath the site We recommend that a follow up program of core drilling
exploration be conducted to substantiate whether the anomalies detected by the
GPR survey represent valid lava tube or cave structures The possibility exists that some
of the recorded anomalies could represent lobes of dense rock in a clinker matrix A
drilling exploration consisting borings drilled by core drilling method would serve to validate
the findings of this preliminary geophysical study The terms boring and core drilling are
essentially synonymous however core drilling is a more precise term for the type of boring
work performed Core drilling implies that a near continuous sample of intact rock material
or soil substrate may be recovered from the boring depending on the strength and integrity
of the material being cored
In addition the core drilling exploration would also serve to confirm the estimated
depth to the lava tube or cave structure Core drilling to obtain samples of the overburden
rock material would be used to evaluate the quality of the rock above the lava tube These
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4003 00 Page 18
parameters are important in evaluating the integrity of the soil and rock above the lava tube
to withstand loads from traffic or surface structures such as buildings Should the
borehole data confirm that the estimated lava tube roof thickness and quality of rock had
been under estimated by the GPR data interpretation our recommendations would need
to be amended to possibly include remedial or precautionary measures
We anticipate that a boring program consisting of core drilling about 20 to 25 borings
extending to depths of about 20 to 25 feet below the ground surface would serve to support
the preliminary evaluation provided in this report Based on recent information received
from D A G S the first priority for the boring exploration is the area of mapped lava tubes
located to the north of the Band Building and to the west of the Science Building
Approximately 8 to 10 borings may be drilled in this area in an attempt to confirm the
location of the potential cave structure s The second priority is the parking and driveway
area located to the south of the Administration Building where approximately 6 to 8 borings
would be drilled through the paved surfaces In addition the localized GPR anomalies
located to the north and northeast of the Library Building may be explored by drilling about
3 to 5 borings at these locations Depending upon the planned future use of the other
areas we believe that 1 to 2 borings each may be drilled at some of the localized
anomalies detected by the GPR survey such as the area of soil erosion located on the
playground to the south of the elementary school classrooms
Core Drilling Exploration
The borings would be drilled by truck mounted rotary drilling equipment with special
attention given to borehole logging to define the upper and lower limit of the lava tube and
the quality of the rock overburden material The drill rig is essentially a large flatbed type
truck with a self contained gasoline or diesel powered drill The drill rig would advance
4 inch diameter augers through the surficial pavement or native soil material and the
underlying weathered rock layer In general soil samples would be collected at 5 foot
intervals where appropriate by driving a 3 inch diameter sampler into the undisturbed soil
located at the bottom of the boring Once the hard rock condition is encountered below the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4003 00 Page 19
surficial soil horizon and auger penetration is stopped the drilling will progress using rock
coring tools to obtain continuous rock core samples from the boring
In general the core drilling method utilizes rotating 3 inch diameter drill rods
equipped with a diamond impregnated cutting bit that utilizes fresh water as a cooling and
lubricating fluid As the drill rods are advanced down the boring the rock which is cored
is retained in the hollow interior of the drill rods until retrieved and brought to the surface
at the end of each 5 foot core run The drilling operation should not produce undesirable
health effects such as dust or odors however some noise associated with the drill rig
engine and augering procedure would be produced In addition a source of water such
as a standard faucet supply is required during the rock coring operation to provide a
continuous flow of water The borings would be spread apart to achieve satisfactory
coverage of the area being explored We anticipate that the borings may be drilled at
about 5 to 10 feet apart to provide adequate coverage Furthermore we estimate that
each 20 foot deep boring would require about 2 to 3 hours to complete depending on the
quality of the rock formation cored
The approximately 4 inch diameter boring would be backfilled with soil compacted
and the ground surface restored to a level condition following the completion of each
boring After backfilling of the borehole the asphalt surfaces penetrated would be patched
with an asphalt cold patch or concrete plug and the work areas would be swept or
washed clear of debris Where the boring encounters a significant lava tube or void which
cannot be backfilled and compacted with soil materials a plug would be fitted in the
borehole below the ground surface and the remainder of the borehole would be sealed with
cement grout to block the migration of soil and water into the subsurface Once the
borehole is sealed with cement grout future erosion of the ground surface at the borehole
site is not anticipated to occur Due to the small diameter of the borehole the drilling of
the borings should have little or no effect on the stability of the ground or the future
development of cracks

In addition to the drilling exploration we recommend that campus personnel
implement a program of periodic semi annual field monitoring for signs of ground surface
deformation and distress A monitoring program would serve as a means to record field
observations over time to assist in the identification of potential problem areas where soil
erosion or ground deformation may be occurring as a result of subsurface ground
movement Observations may include the location and extent of soil erosion or loss
breakage of utility lines cracking of pavements sidewalks or building slabs unusual water
seepage or change in drainage pattern deformation to building frames and swimming pool
leakage among other observations
To address the concern over repeated soil erosion at the locations shown on the
Site Plans we recommend exploring the shallow subsurface by drilling at the locations of
the reported soil loss to evaluate the cause s of the soil losses Based on the GPR data
the soil loss is likely due to piping of the surface soil into shallow voids created by rock fill
or as a result of small shallow voids in the rock formation We do not recommend
excavation of the locations by backhoe at this time An excavation could encounter a
large unanticipated lava tube or cave structure for which a means of backfilling would not
be readily available or anticipated
In summary a drilling exploration is recommended to support the preliminary
findings of the geophysical survey results presented in this report The geophysical survey
was implemented as a non intrusive survey method which should be followed up with
some intrusive exploration to confirm or discount the preliminary findings In addition a
ground surface and structure deformation monitoring program conducted periodically by
school personnel would serve to provide a record of abnormal conditions that could assist
in locating potential subsurface problem areas in the future
