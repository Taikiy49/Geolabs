INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed AT T Fiberoptic Cable project in the Makaha area on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input only and are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu encompassing approximately 604 square miles of land area
may be divided into four basic geographic provinces The provinces include the Wai anae
Mountains Ko olau Mountains Leilehua Plateau and Coastal Plains The Island of Oahu
was formed by the merging of basalt lavas erupted from the two prominent volcanoes the
Waianae and Ko olau Volcanoes Of the two the is the older having
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4255 00 Page 5
breached the ocean surface nearly 4 million years ago The Ko olau Volcano rose above
sea level approximately 2 7 million years ago making it the younger of the two volcanoes
In general the two volcanoes were built by the long term eruption of thinly bedded
a a and pahoehoe lava flows which were erupted from elongated and linear trending
volcanic vents associated with the prominent rift zones of each of the volcanoes The
Ko olau lavas banked against the lava flows of the Wai anae Volcano to form the Leilehua
Plateau of the central interior portion of Oahu The Wai anae Mountains which form the
western third of the Island of Oahu contain the oldest basalt rock formations on the Island
of Oahu The Ko olau Mountains form the eastern two thirds of the island and include the
rock layers exposed along the extending from Waimanalo to Kahuku
During the final stages of eruption and continuing well after the cessation of the
Waianae and Ko olau eruptions the island mass began to subside thousands of feet under
the tremendous weight of the rock In addition to the subsidence of the island the force
of stream erosion worked in conjunction with the large scale fracture and slumping of
portions of the landmass to form the topography of today Between 850 000 and
70 000 years ago volcanic activity returned to the Island of Oahu with the eruption of ash
cinder and basalt lavas from vents located throughout the eastern and southeastern
portions of the island These most recent eruptions are collectively known as the Honolulu
Volcanic Series Subsequently erosion by streams and ocean waves have continued to
work on the exposed land surfaces to form the topography of the present day
The Island of Oahu is no longer sinking beneath the sea however the effects of the
early whole island subsidence combined with the added effect of major Pleistocene Age
sea level fluctuations worked to create drowned river valley systems with the development
of coral reefs The coral reefs and lithified sand dunes may be found exposed subaerially
on Oahu and buried in submarine deposits well offshore of the island The coral and sand
dune deposits were formed in shallow offshore seas and coastlines during periods of
higher and lower sea levels associated with the effects of the advance and retreat of
glaciers which controlled the volume of water contained in the seas Today some of the

shallow sea coralline deposits are visible on the flanks of mountains or buried beneath
terrestrial sediments evidence of the higher and lower stands of the sea in prehistoric time
GEOLOGY OF THE MAKAHA AREA
The Makaha project site lies near the boundary of the Wai anae Mountain province
and the west Oahu shoreline The proposed cable alignment traverses the coastal region
located at the mouths of several large valleys and broad areas of intermittent drainage
discharging from the mountains toward the ocean As a result much of the generally flat
land area bordering the coastline is underlain by a mix of unconsolidated coastal and
alluvial sediments sands and clays with basalt cobbles and boulders with some dense
cemented sand sandstone and beachrock and coral limestone rock formation In

underlain by potentially loose or soft soils associated with unconsolidated alluvial deposits
located at or below the groundwater table The groundwater table may be expected to
exist between about 3 feet Mean Sea Level MSL and 0 feet MSL depending upon local
influence from tidal fluctuation due to the generally close proximity of the shoreline Some
of these soft and loose soil deposits may be buried in the shallow subsurface beneath
firmer embankment fill materials placed to raise the ground elevation of Farrington
Highway
Additionally some soft or loose deposits may be encountered in the undisturbed

Horizontal Directional Drilling HDD
We believe that the use of horizontal directional drilling may be a viable alternative for
the communications cable installation at the culvert and bridge crossings within the project
limits Horizontal directional drilling is a trenchless steerable installation method using a
multi axis drilling machine to bore a small diameter pilot hole in the subsurface The pilot
hole is bored using either controlled fluid jetting or fluid assisted mechanical cutting or a
combination of the two methods The pilot hole is reamed as necessary to accommodate
the pipe to be installed The pipe is pulled back into the reamed hole by the drilling
machine Most HDD systems use a slurry to stabilize the walls of the borehole and to
reduce the frictional drag on the pipeline being installed Steering corrections are made
in the soils by rotating the slanted cutter shoe to the desired orientation and then
advancing the shoe and string without rotation
In general the HDD pilot hole would be drilled nearly horizontal with entry and exit
angles limited to a maximum of about 14 degrees measured from horizontal The drill unit
should be positioned at an appropriate setback distance such that the required depth
starting from the entry point can be achieved In general the product pipe or utility line
should be installed in one continuous operation preferably without pause and should
protrude a sufficient distance beyond the entry and exit points to allow proper termination
or connection at a later time Shallow working pits may be required at each end of the bore
to allow placement of the drill pipe and to allow transition to the conventional pipe trench
installation
The vertical and horizontal alignments of the drill hole is monitored from the surface
by a locator which tracks signals from the transmitter contained inside the drill head
housing Other methods of tracking the cutting tool such as in hole steering are generally
recommended to provide more precise line and grade information The contractor should
carefully monitor the drilling operation when approaching property boundaries and the
existing underground utilities or structures to assure proper clearance Soils containing
gravel cobbles and boulders may present difficulties in steering control due to the effect
of possible deflection or skidding around the potential hard obstructions
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4255 00 Page 11
The feasibility of the HDD technique should be established based on ground
conditions diameter and length of the pipe or duct line and the required achievable degree
of accuracy in both alignment and grade HDD is less attractive where precise line and
grade tolerances are required in soils containing cobbles and boulders in a soil matrix
especially with larger diameter pipelines The HDD method is best suited for soils with
some cohesion i e clays However the method has been successfully used in granular
soils by adding bentonite to the drilling fluid to aid in the borehole stability The HDD
method is not particularly well suited for gravelly soils greater than 50 percent gravel size
particles due to some difficulties in drilling the pilot hole and maintaining the stability of the
hole It should be noted that widely heterogeneous formations such as alluvial soils with
sands gravel cobbles and boulders such as those encountered at this project site
present some challenges to penetration and steering control Therefore use of the HDD
method in these heterogeneous ground conditions may be difficult and additional care is
required in the selection of suitable HDD equipment to maintain the precise alignment and
grade of the bore
The advantage of HDD installation on this project is that a buried installation across
the drainage channels can be achieved without the need for special stream crossing
construction permits However the primary disadvantage of HDD installation is the need
for careful control of alignment and grade in particular with regards to steering adjacent
to existing utility ducts and remaining within the highway easement boundary
Nevertheless HDD appears to be a viable and possibly cost effective alternative for the
installation of the relatively small diameter cable duct for this project In addition we
anticipate that the larger line and grade control tolerances needed due to the
heterogeneous soil formations encountered at the site would be acceptable for the
communications cable crossings

Geotechnical Conditions
The technicai feasibility of the HDD method depends on the characteristics of the
materials at the depth of the pipeline profile as well as the length of the drill path
and diameter of the pipe to be installed American Gas Association 1995 In
general gravel size material cannot be readily fluidized by the drilling fluid and may
not be stable enough to be cut and removed by the drilling fluid without caving in
Therefore subsoils containing a high percentage of gravel may present difficulties
with the utilization of the HDD method In addition it should be noted that
obstructions such as cobbles and boulders within the drill path might halt the
advance of the drill bit reamer or pipeline during pullback
Based on our field exploration clayey and sandy soils with some gravel cobbles
and boulders were generally encountered in the borings drilled at the project site
Hard coral and sandstone beachrock materials were encountered in some of the
borings at depths underlying the surficial fill and alluvial deposits In addition loose
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4255 00 Page 14
and soft alluvial sand and clay deposits were also encountered as pockets in some
of the borings drilled for the subject project
In general the HDD method should be most suitable for pipeline installation in the
cohesive alluvial soils and the hard coral formation provided that appropriate drilling
tools and experienced drilling crews are utilized during construction However the
unconsolidated alluvial and coralline deposits containing high gravel content about
50 percent or more and cobble size fragments may be problematic and are
expected to cause some difficulty in drilling and steering the pilot hole as well as
hole stability problems such as caving in of the pilot and reamed holes The
caved in materials will obstruct or divert reaming tools or the pipe string during pipe
pullback This will substantially increase the pulling stresses on the pipe string and
may impede pipe pulling or damage the pipeline In addition loose sandy soils as
encountered in some of the drilled borings may also cave into the drill hole and
obstruct and divert the pipe string during pullback Deflection of the pilot drill bit
may occur at the interface between the loose sandy soils and hard coral formation
or basalt boulders due to the abrupt change in soil conditions This may result in
difficulties in steering during the pilot hole drilling and potential change in the drill
path alignment
The cable duct line profile should be evaluated with respect to the anticipated
geotechnical considerations to be aware of some of the difficulties discussed above
Therefore Geolabs Hawaii should review the duct line profile when the alignment
and profile has been finalized

For the HDD method the most important design consideration for the pipe is the
tensile stresses imposed on the pipe when it is pulled into place through the drill
hole The magnitude of these tensile stresses is a function of the length of the
installation as well as the subsurface conditions Other important pipe design
considerations are the internal and external pressures acting on the pipe during and
after installation In addition the selected pipe material must have adequate
corrosion resistance or be protected to resist internal and external corrosion
HDPE pipes generally have low tensile strength and may be susceptible to failure
during installation This limits the length of installation that can be completed with
an HDPE pipe Therefore it is critical that the pipe be selected with an adequate
allowable pulling tensile capacity Pipe stress analyses should be performed to
evaluate if an HDPE pipe can withstand the pulling stresses during pipe installation
and the external pressures hydrostatic and soil on the installed pipeline
The HDPE pipe should be selected to provide the maximum pipe thickness and
strength for the proposed installation based on the results of the pipe stress
analyses considerations regarding HDD installation procedures probable drill hole
sizes and materials in filling the annulus between the HDD installed HDPE pipeline

Construction Staging Area
Staging areas will be required at the entry and exit locations for the HDD installed
pipelines Additional staging area is also needed for pipe storage spoil dewatering
and for laying out the pipe when the pipe string is assembled for pullback The pipe
string should be hoisted at least 20 feet above the ground surface when crossing

to the success of the proposed pipeline installation by HDD method These
underground utilities and structures should be identified located and avoided
during the project construction It is important that the HDD contractor utilize an
in hole steering tool in conjunction with a surface tracking system to provide
reliable horizontal and vertical directional control during the pipeline installation
The primary selection criteria for the HDD machine are thrust pullback and torque
capabilities However project related requirements such as length of installation
and diameter of the product pipe play an important role in the selection process
Depending on the length and size of the drill hole required for the HDD installation
the size of the drilling equipment and its capacity in rotational power as well as
pulling power should be checked by the contractor for the specific project
Type of Drilling Fluid
Depending on the soil conditions water only or water mixed with bentonite and or
polymer can be used as a drilling fluid in a directional drilling application Water
alone is normally suitable for drilling in silty or clayey soils If a highly plastic or
expansive clay is encountered polymer mixed with water will improve the hole
lubrication and reduce the swelling tendency of clay swelling tends to close off the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4255 00 Page 17
hole which increases torque and pullback requirements For sand or other poorly
consolidated formations such as those encountered at the project site a bentonite
mixture should be used to support the borehole The amount of drilling additives
bentonite or polymer depends on the soil conditions and experience of the
contractor The water used in the drilling fluid should not be hard or salty because
this will cause poor mixing or yield with the bentonite or polymer and the flocculated
bentonite may tend to settle to the bottom of the tank Hard water should be treated
with soda ash and salt water should be avoided
Muck and Slurry Disposal
Special muck and slurry handling procedures may be required for the HDD
installation method for the proposed cable crossing The properties of the various
geologic units anticipated along the drill path as indicated by the grain size and
hydrometer tests should be taken into account in selecting an appropriate slurry
separation plant Due to the low specific gravity of some of the coralline materials
these coralline materials may not settle out quickly in a cascading tank type of slurry
separation system and a slurry separation plant may be required under such
conditions
In order to aid in a clean site and to more readily separate the materials a solids
control or slurry separation plant with shakers screens de sanders de silter cones
and centrifuge may need to be considered This will facilitate a more rapid
separation and disposal process Additives may need to be included to get the
fine grained materials to flocculate so that a clean slurry can be returned to the drill
head
Muck resulting from the HDD operations should be removed from the site and
should be disposed of properly at an acceptable facility or designated area Slurry
from the HDD operations should be pumped into trucks for disposal in accordance
with the applicable governmental regulations and agencies Contaminated soils
and or groundwater if applicable should be handled tested and disposed of in

Open Trench Cut and Cover Method
We understand that the majority of the proposed cable duct line will likely be
constructed by the conventional open trench method Based on the design duct line invert
information provided we believe that the cable and utility access vaults planned at the
highway and shoulder grades may be underlain by medium dense to dense sandy and stiff
clayey materials with some gravel cobbles and boulders as encountered in the borings
drilled for the subject project Therefore attention should be given to the bedding
requirement and backfill measures as discussed in the following subsections of this report
Excavation
Based on the information provided the majority of the cable duct line inverts may be
about 4 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface and will likely be constructed by
open trench excavation Our field exploration disclosed that the site is generally
underlain by medium dense to dense sandy and stiff clayey soils with some gravel
cobbles and boulders at shallow depths along the proposed cable alignment
However hard coral formation should also be expected in localized areas along the
proposed cable alignment Groundwater levels may be encountered on the order of
about Elevation 0 to 3 feet MSL along the proposed cable alignment In general we
anticipate that the trench excavations should not encounter groundwater However
it is possible that some of the excavation bottoms will encounter the consolidated
coral and sandstone beachrock formation buried below the fill and alluvial soils
Earth Pressure Loads on Pipes
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the
pipes the unit weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance between the
backfill material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads on the buried
cable duct line pipes an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot pcf for
backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0 30 are recommended Earth forces
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4255 00 Page 19
upon the pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of the trench Therefore the
width of the trench should be kept to a minimum Traffic loads on the buried pipes
if applicable should also be considered for the portions of the pipes located in
roadway areas
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great effect upon the load
supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore pipe bedding is an important design
consideration In general a leveling cushion bed consisting of 6 inches of S4C
sand or similar free draining material is recommended under the pipes located over
stiff dense soil conditions Our field exploration indicated that dense cobbles or
boulders may be encountered near the design invert elevations of the duct line
pipes along the new cable alignment The cobbles and boulders when excavated
may produce an uneven trench bottom containing voids Thus the use of the S4C
sand bedding may not be appropriate Therefore it may be prudent to use some
free draining gravel such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation to
fill in the voids and to provide a level bed for the pipe The bedding should extend
beyond the sides of the pipe to the edge of the excavated trench width
Backfill
In general trench backfill should be performed in accordance with the Hawaii
Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994
Free draining sand materials such as S4C sand should be used for the initial
trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support
around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill thus reducing the
possibility of damaging the pipes Compaction by water ponding or jetting should
not be allowed for this project Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of
the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site granular soils with a
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4255 00 Page 20
maximum particle size of 6 inches or less in maximum dimension The backfill should
be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum
8 inch horizontal loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where
trenches are located below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill
below the pavement grade should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as apercentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Utility Vault Structures
We understand that the utility vault structures will be precast concrete structures
therefore we recommend that the structures bear on a minimum 6 inch thick cushion layer
consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation An allowable bearing
pressure of 2 000 pounds per square foot psf may be utilized for the utility vault structures
bearing on the No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation This bearing value is for
dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as
those caused by wind or seismic forces
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the vault Resistance due to friction may
be estimated using a coefficient of friction of 0 35 Resistance due to passive earth
pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square
foot per foot of depth pcf if above the water table and 175 pcf if below the water table
It should be noted that some of the utility vault structure excavations might
encounter the soft and or loose alluvial soil deposits near the bottom of the excavations
In addition groundwater may also be encountered in these conditions Where soft and or
loose soii deposits are encountered at the bottom of the excavations the soft and or loose

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentation
of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Eight sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 and ASTM D 1140 and four hydrometer
analysis tests ASTM D 422 were performed on selected samples of the soils to evaluate
the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification Graphic
