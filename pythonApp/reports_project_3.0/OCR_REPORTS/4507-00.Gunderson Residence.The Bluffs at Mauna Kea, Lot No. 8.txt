INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Gunderson Residence located at Lot No 8 of The Bluffs at Mauna Kea in the
District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed
residence These recommendations are intended for the design of house foundations
slabs on grade retaining walls site preparation and pavements only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed residential project site is located at The Bluffs at Mauna Kea within the
Mauna Kea Resort in the South Kohala area on the Island of Hawaii Based on the
information provided it is proposed to construct a new residence on Lot No 8 of the
subdivision We understand that the subject lot encompasses an area of approximately
48 750 square feet The proposed residence will be a one story structure with a swimming
pool two viewing platforms and retaining walls for grade separation Based on the
information provided we anticipate that the majority of the site grading will consist of fills of up
to about 8 feet to achieve the design finished grades for the project Along the southern
portion of the subject lot fills and retaining wall backfills of up to about 13 feet may be required
to attain the design finished grades

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity during
recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea
which are both still active
GEOLABS INC
W O 4507 00 Page 4
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Mauna Kea which has formed
the west central portion of the island The project site appears to be underlain by basaltic lava
flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea which were deposited during the
Pleistocene Epoch Stearns and Macdonald 1946
The Hamakua Volcanic Series is typically composed of basaltic lava flows which are
mantied by a relatively thin layer of palagonitized ash locally referred to as Pahala Ash The
ash soil at the project site differs from Pahala Ash found in other portions of the Island of
Hawaii in that the in situ moisture content is generally lower and the soil exists in a dry friable
state The soils at the site have been subjected to wind erosion which has resulted in the
formation of a desert pavement on the surface of the site This desert pavement is a surface
layer of cobbles and gravel which were left behind as the finer ash was blown away
The lava formation at the site appears to be of a a and pahoehoe basalt type flows
which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean A a lava is typically characterized
by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock
fragments including cobbles and boulders Typically a more dense and layered lava rock
material is contained within the lava flow core Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth
rope like or billowy surface with an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are
commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas These cavities are formed when the lavas were
still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and
pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling
flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally
along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent

Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions at the project site generally

The a a lava and clinkers encountered below the surface fill extended to the maximum depths

Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe that

House Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
that shallow spread and or continuous footings be used to support the proposed new
residence and other improvements planned at the site An allowable bearing pressure of up
to 4 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of footings bearing on basalt
formation and or compacted structural fill This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and
may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent exterior grade For footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on slopes the
footings should be embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance measured from
the outside edge at the base of the footing to the slope face is not less than 6 feet
Where foundations are located next to other foundations utility trenches or easements
the foundations should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward
from the bottom edge of the utility trench or be extended to as deep as the inverts of the utility
GEOLABS INC
W O 4507 00 Page 7
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures
with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement
The subgrade soils at the bottom of footing excavations should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the footing subgrades the materials
should be over excavated until dense materials are exposed in the footing excavation The
over excavation should be backfilled with structural fill materials moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
or the bottom of footing may be extended down to bear on the underlying competent material
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our recommendations
we estimate the total settlements to be less than 1 inch with differential settlements on the
order of about 0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 40
may be used for footings bearing on structural fill materials Resistance due to passive earth
pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot
per foot of depth pcf assuming that the soils around the footings are well compacted The
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of the soil should be neglected unless covered by
pavements or slabs

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used throughout the proposed
residence For the interior house slabs which will not be subjected to vehicular traffic we
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill be provided below the slabs for
uniform support The cushion fill should consist of open graded materials such as No 3B
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation and also would serve as a capillary break
To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor
coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is
recommended on top of the gravel cushion layer A 2 inch thick moist fine sand layer may be
considered above the plastic vapor barrier to aid in the curing of the concrete Provision of the
2 inches of moist fine sand above the vapor barrier will be left up to the discretion of the project
structural engineer Flexible floor coverings should be considered above the floor slab since
they can better mask minor slab cracking
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project In general we
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer of No 3B Fine gravel be provided
below the concrete walkway slab To reduce the potential for substantial shrinkage cracks in
the slabs crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the
walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
Garage Slab and Concrete Driveway
We envision that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the garage slab and
driveway For slabs on grade that will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the garage
slab and the driveway we recommend that a 6 inch layer of select borrow subbase be
provided below the slab in lieu of the cushion fill layer for more uniform support The vapor
barrier and the 2 inch moist fine sand layer may also be omitted for these slabs The select
GEOLABS INC
W O 4507 00 Page 9
borrow subbase should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction
The slab subgrade should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction to provide a firm and unyielding base The 6 inch thick
select borrow subbase should be placed as soon as possible after preparation of the slab
subgrade
We recommend that the concrete slab be designed for the intended loads For design

made for proper load transfer across the slab joints that will be subjected to vehicular traffic
Swimming Pool Foundations
In general we believe that the swimming pool foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the House Foundations section of this
report Since the swimming pools will be water retention structures we envision that the
foundations for the pools will likely consist of mat foundations
To provide a relatively uniform surface below the mat foundation we recommend that
the pool foundations be underlain by a layer of compacted cushion fill material The
cushion fill material may consist of either 6 inches of select borrow subbase 3 inch minus
granular materials or 12 inches of compacted structural fill 6 inch minus granular
materials The cushion fill material should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The
pool subgrade should be firm and unyielding prior to construction of the pool foundations
and or bottom slab

Retaining Structures
Based on the plans provided retaining walls of up to about 13 feet in height are
anticipated for this project In addition construction of other retaining structures such as the
swimming pool walls is also planned at this residence The following general guidelines are
provided and may be used for the design of retaining structures
Retaining Structure Foundations
In general we believe that retaining structure foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations and parameters presented in the House
Foundations section of this report However the retaining structure footings should
have a minimum width of 18 inches In addition wall foundations located on relatively
flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade For sloping ground conditions the footing should extend
deeper to obtain a minimum 6 foot setback distance measured horizontally from the
outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope Wall footings oriented parallel to
the direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings
As previously indicated foundations for retaining structures may be designed based
on the recommendations presented in the House Foundations section which
recommends that the foundations bear on basalt formation and or compacted structural
fill If additional lateral load resistance is desired the retaining wall foundation
subgrade should be over excavated and replaced with 12 inches of aggregate
subbase A coefficient of friction of 0 50 may be used for footings bearing on 12 inches
of aggregate subbase Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pcf This assumes that the soils around the
footings are well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected

Dynamic Lateral Earth Forces
Since the project site is located on the seismically active Island of Hawaii dynamic
lateral earth forces will need to be considered in the design of retaining structures
Dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading may be estimated by
using 10H pounds per lineal foot of wall length for level backfill conditions where H is
the height of the wall in feet It should be noted that the dynamic lateral earth forces
provided assume that the site wall will be allowed to move laterally by up to about 2 to
4 inches in the event of an earthquake If this amount of lateral movement is not
acceptable the retaining wall should be designed with higher dynamic lateral forces for
a restrained wall condition For a restrained wall condition dynamic lateral forces due
to seismic loading may be estimated using 20H pounds per lineal foot of wall for level
backfill conditions
The dynamic lateral earth force would generally act at the mid height of the wall The
dynamic lateral earth force presented above is in addition to the static lateral earth
pressures An appropriately reduced factor of safety may be used when dynamic
lateral earth forces due to earth pressures are accounted for in the design of the
retaining structure
Drainage
In general retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of
hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system for site retaining walls should consist
of about 1 cubic foot of permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation wrapped with non woven filter fabric at each of the weephole

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The compaction
requirement for fills or backfills below areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be

to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of rock
boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of passes
of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be conducted
at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a representative
from Geolabs On the basis of this testing program the number of passes may then
be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction

to at least 90 percent relative compaction Improper trench backfill could result in settlement
of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of granular materials generally less than
6 inches in maximum size The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Where trenches will be located in paved
areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted
to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the residential structure and retaining walls should be
sloped to shed water away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for
ponding It is also advised that gutter systems be installed around the residence and that the
discharge be diverted away from the foundation and slab areas Excessive landscape
watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations
should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for excessive
water infiltration into the subsurface

and slabs

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the residential project site were explored by drilling and
sampling four borings extending to depths of approximately 10 to 15 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
