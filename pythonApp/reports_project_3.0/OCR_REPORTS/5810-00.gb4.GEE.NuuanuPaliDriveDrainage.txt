Site Description
The project site is on Nuuanu Pali Drive at the intersection with Old Pali Road and
within the private property at Drive in the Nuuanu area of Honolulu on
the Island of Oahu Hawaii
Drain Line A begins at the Nuuanu Stream drain line outlet and traverses the
private property at Drive The drain line at this portion of Drain Line A
consists of a 30 inch reinforced concrete pipe Drain Line A crosses Nuuanu Pali Drive
and Old Pali Road as two parallel 24 inch reinforced concrete pipes
Drain Line B branches off of DMH B of Drain Line A The drain line extends
southeast along Nuuanu Pali Drive to an existing catch basin Then the drain line extends
to the east crossing Old Pali Road and terminating at an existing catch basin
Drain Line B consists of an 18 inch reinforced concrete pipe
Based on our research the anticipated surface soils underlying the project site are
described by the U S Soil Conservation Service in their publication Soil Survey of Islands
of Molokai and Lanai State of Hawaii 1972 The major soil at the
site and some of its general properties are as follows
Lolekaa LoC and LoD These are well drained soils that develop on old
gravelly colluvium and alluvium Slopes average between about 8 to 45 percent
for LoC and 15 to 25 percent for LoD The runoff is slow to medium and these
soils have a moderate erosion hazard Their engineering qualities are relatively
good and they have a moderate shrink swell potential

Discussion and Recommendations
Conventional open trench methods and horizontal directional drilling HDD were
considered for installation of the new drain line Due to the size of the proposed new drain
line and the proposed invert elevation of the new outlet at Nuuanu Stream it is not
practical to install the new drain line using HDD To install the 36 inch drain line using
HDD a radius of curvature on the order of about 3 600 feet may be required This radius
of curvature will result in the drain line going below the streambed elevation To maintain
the proposed invert elevation at the outlet into Nuuanu Stream a tighter radius of
curvature will be required however a tighter radius of curvature will result in high tensile
stresses in the pipe during installation which could damage the pipe Therefore the new
drain line will have to be installed using open trench methods Detailed discussions and
recommendations for design of retaining structure drain lines and site preparation are
presented in the following sections

Retaining Structure Foundation
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions at the project site we recommend
using an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf for
design of retaining structure foundations bearing on stiff soils This bearing value is
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In addition retaining structure foundations should be at least 18 inches wide and
should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished
grades If soft and or loose soils are encountered at the bottom of excavation the
soft and or loose soils should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
and or dense materials The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted granular fill materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a
45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
trench or the foundations should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the
utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below
grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for
appreciable foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the foundations may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundations A coefficient of
friction of 0 35 may be used for foundations bearing on the recompacted clayey
soils Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf The
passive earth pressure value assumes that the soils around the foundations are
well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive pressure
resistance in the upper 12 inches of the soils should be neglected
A Geolabs representative should monitor excavations prior to placement of
reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions

Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
placed directly around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of
the structure discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the structure
The backfill from the bottom of the structure to the bottom of the weephole should
consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for significant water

Drain Line
In general we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation under the drainpipes The initial trench
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of free draining backfills
such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for pipe damage from compaction of
the backfill It is critical to use a free draining granular material as the initial trench backfill
to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Use of clayey soils as initial trench backfill
directly around the pipes is not recommended
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to
the finished subgrade should consist of the approved on site soils 3 inch minus or select
borrow fill material The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence The upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement
grade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical
compaction equipment should be used to compact the backfill materials Compaction
efforts by water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed
To facilitate and expedite backfilling of the trench consideration should be given
to using Controlled Low Strength Material CLSM for use as backfill
Excavation
Based on our borings it is anticipated that the near surface soil materials may be
readily excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such as backhoes However
the excavations on the private property at Drive may encounter basalt
rock formation within the depths of the excavations In addition boulders were
encountered at a depth of about 4 feet below the existing ground surface at Boring No 2
Excavation encountering basalt rock formation and large boulders will require the use of
hoerams or chipping
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based
on our visual observation at the site We recommend that contractors be encouraged to
examine the site conditions to make their own interpretation

Dewatering
Since the project site is adjacent to Nuuanu Stream there is potential to encounter
groundwater within the trench excavations Therefore dewatering may be necessary if
groundwater is encountered within the excavations
Dewatering for construction is the contractor s responsibility The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and he she
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on the conditions encountered The underlying clayey soils may
have low permeability and would transmit low quantities of water into the excavation
However the basalt rock formation may have a relatively high permeability Therefore it
is anticipated that high quantities of water may be transmitted into the excavations where
basalt rock formation is encountered

The materials generated from the cut areas may be reused as a source of general
fill material provided that they are free of vegetation deleterious materials and rock
fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension If required import fill should
consist of non expansive select granular material such as crushed basalt or coral The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should
have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent Geolabs should test imported fill material for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use
Fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or
other types of acceptable compaction equipment Compaction by water tamping jetting or
ponding should not be allowed for the on site soils
A Geolabs representative should monitor site grading operations to observe
whether undesirable materials are encountered and to confirm whether the exposed
soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our field exploration
