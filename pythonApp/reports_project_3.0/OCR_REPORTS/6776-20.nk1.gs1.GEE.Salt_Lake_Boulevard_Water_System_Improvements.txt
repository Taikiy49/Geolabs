SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a thin layer of surface fill approximately 
0.5 to 1.5 feet thick overlying weathered volcanic tuff and volcanic tuff formation to the 
maximum depth explored of about 10 feet below the existing ground surface. The fill material 
generally consists of dense to hard sandy gravel and sandy silt. In general, the weathered 
volcanic tuff and volcanic tuff formation were very dense and medium hard to hard.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration. 
It should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, 
seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
We anticipate that the majority of the water line inverts will be on the order of about 
4 to 9 feet below the existing pavement or ground surface. Installation of the proposed water 
lines will involve excavation into the underlying fills, alluvium, weathered tuff and medium 
hard to hard volcanic tuff formation. In general, we anticipate that the in-situ soils along the 
proposed water line alignments may be excavated with normal heavy excavation 
equipment. Excavation into the underlying volcanic tuff formation may require chipping 
and/or the use of hoerams to remove the materials and to achieve the design invert 
elevations. Therefore, difficult excavation conditions should be anticipated for the project. 
Where deep excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary 
shoring or sloping and benching should be implemented for the trench excavations. Based 
on our site observations, we believe that temporary cut slopes for open cut excavations are 
not feasible. Therefore, trench walls would have to be cut near vertical necessitating the use 
of temporary shoring during construction. 
In general, the subsurface soils encountered during our field exploration may be used 
in the upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to the 
pavement subgrade provided that the materials are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, 
organic material, and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum 
dimension. Therefore, the excavated volcanic tuff may need to be crushed and screened to 
meet the above requirement. 
Design of metallic substructures, such as water line pipes or other metallic piping, 
should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. Based on the 
field resistivity survey results and the laboratory minimum resistivity test results, the 
subsurface soils/volcanic tuff at the project site have resistivity values ranging from about 
420 to 7,200 ohm-cm, corresponding to corrosion ratings of 1 (extremely corrosive) to 4 
(moderately corrosive), respectively. It should be noted that the resistivity values of the

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age (2.7 – 3.4 million years ago) and forms the bulk of the western 
one-third of the island. The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age (2.2 – 2.5 million years ago) and forms the majority of 
the eastern two-thirds of the island. The end of volcanic activity associated with the 
Koolau Volcano was followed by a long period of landmass erosion, whereby deep stream 
valleys were carved into the dome-shaped Koolau Shield Volcano. 
The project site is located on the south flank of the Aliamanu vent and on the 
southwestern flank of the Salt Lake vent complex of the Honolulu Volcanic Series.  As the 
molten magma ascended through these vents, it reacted with the groundwater generating 
steam.  This steam caused explosive eruptions, which deposited low cones of ash 
material that have consolidated to form tuff, locally referred to as "mudrock.” 
The Aliamanu tuff is an older water-laid ash deposit that was laid down when the 
sea level was higher than the present level.  The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its 
material over alluvium originating from the Halawa Stream. 
After this phase of volcanic activity ceased, sedimentary deposits accumulated 
over the Aliamanu tuff before the next phase of volcanic activity began.  Then, the 
Salt Lake eruptions began when the sea level was at about 40 to 60 feet below its present 
level.  The Salt Lake eruptive event deposited its material over earlier tuffs from the older 
Aliamanu events. 
The water that accumulated in the crater was believed to originate from 
depressional springs and rainwater.  During periods of little rainfall, the shallow ponded 
water was reduced by evaporation, and over the years, the mineral salt content of the 
ponded water increased resulting in the name “Salt Lake.”  Recently, man has developed 
the crater floor by filling and grading the area for residential and golf course purposes.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a thin layer of surface fill approximately 
0.5 to 1.5 feet thick overlying weathered tuff and volcanic tuff formation to the maximum 
depth explored of about 10 feet below the existing ground surface. The fill material 
generally consists of dense to hard sandy gravel and sandy silt. In general, the weathered 
tuff and volcanic tuff formation were very dense and medium hard to hard.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Approximately 5,980 linear feet of varying size water lines are planned for the 
project. We understand that construction of the water lines will generally consist of trench 
excavation, pipe placement, and trench backfill.  
Difficult excavation conditions during trench excavation should be anticipated due 
to the presence of medium hard to hard volcanic tuff formation underlying the majority of 
the water line alignments at shallow depths and may require the use of hoerams or 
chipping. 
Field resistivity survey and laboratory minimum resistivity testing were performed 
for the project. Based on the results of the resistivity testing, the subsurface soils/volcanic 
tuff range from extremely corrosive to moderately corrosive. Detailed discussions for 
design of the Salt Lake Boulevard Water System Improvements project, including trench 
excavation, trench backfilling, corrosion protection, and other geotechnical aspects of the 
project, are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Trench Excavation 
We anticipate that the majority of the pipe inverts of the new water lines will be on 
the order of about 4 to 9 feet below the existing pavement or ground surface. Installation 
of the proposed water lines will involve excavation into the underlying fills, weathered 
volcanic tuff, and volcanic tuff formation. In general, we anticipate that the in-situ soils 
along the proposed water line alignments may be excavated with normal heavy 
excavation equipment near the ground surface.

As mentioned above, our field exploration and our previous field exploration 
performed for the Salt Lake Boulevard Widening, Phase 3 project encountered medium 
hard to hard volcanic tuff material at relatively shallow depths in the borings. Therefore, 
excavations extending into the underlying volcanic tuff within the trench depths should be

tuff formation and result in a potential loss of bearing strength for foundations in its vicinity. 
The above discussion regarding the excavation and rippability of the subsurface 
materials is based on field data obtained from the borings drilled along the project 
alignment. We recommend encouraging contractors bidding on this project to examine 
the site conditions and soil/rock data to make their own interpretation. 
3.2 
Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate excavation depths of up to about 4 to 9 feet below the pavement 
surface will be required for the installation of the new water lines. Where deeper 
excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary shoring or sloping and 
benching should be implemented for the trench excavations. Based on our site 
observations, we believe that temporary cut slopes for open cut excavations may not be 
feasible. Therefore, trench walls would have to be cut near vertical necessitating the use 
of temporary shoring during construction. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and 
safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative should be on-site at all 
times during excavation and construction work for the opportunity to promptly observe 
changing or unforeseen conditions, such as high groundwater, inappropriate construction 
sequence or techniques, etc., which may affect the shoring stability.

Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for 
excessive ground movement. 
It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the 
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction. The properly braced shoring is 
essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent ground 
into the excavation, which may result in potential settlement or distress to adjacent 
structures or other improvements. 
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the 
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. Due 
to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the magnitude 
of movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship, such as how 
quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed, the subsurface 
conditions, the size of the excavation, and the rate of excavation. 
Therefore, it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed 
properly and as early as practical. The adjacent ground should be continuously monitored 
for cracks, dips and/or other indications of movements with instruments until the 
subsurface water system pipes are finally backfilled. 
3.3 
Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the stiff older alluvium or volcanic tuff formation, a 
minimum of 6 inches of granular cushion material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM 
C33, No. 67 gradation) should be provided below the pipe. Where soft and/or loose soils 
are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, a stabilization layer consisting of 
additional 18 inches of open-graded gravel wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric 
(Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below the cushion layer for uniform 
support.

The granular cushion should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of

3.4 
Trench Backfill 
In general, the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Water 
System Standards, State of Hawaii 2002. The first zone of backfill extending from the 
cushion material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes should consist of select 
free draining granular material (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) to reduce the compaction 
effort required and resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is critical to use this 
free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches 
of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench 
backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the pavement subgrade should consist of the excavated on-site soils, volcanic tuff 
material or imported fill materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and 
clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. Alluvium 
was encountered in some of the borings performed for our January 26, 2018 report for 
the Salt Lake Boulevard Widening, Phase 3 project. Based on laboratory test results, the 
alluvium has a moderate swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. 
Therefore, the alluvial material should not be used as trench backfill within the upper 
3 feet from finish subgrades. It should be noted that the fill requirements presented herein 
are intended as guidelines only and may be modified based on Geolabs additional 
laboratory testing and field observations on the available fill materials. 
Imported fill materials used for trench backfill should be well-graded from coarse 
to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material 
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell

3.5 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfill materials consisting of the excavated on-site soils or volcanic tuff material 
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. The upper 3 feet below the pavement grade should be compacted 
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course and subbase materials 
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts 
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed 
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density.

assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper 
moisture content. Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort 
should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary, to obtain the 
specified compaction. 
3.6 
Value Vault Foundations 
We envision that concrete valve structures may be required for the proposed water 
lines. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend an 
allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,500 pounds per square foot (psf) for foundations 
bearing on alluvium, weathered tuff or volcanic tuff formation. This bearing value is for 
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as 
those caused by seismic forces. 
If foundations 
are designed and constructed 
in accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the valve vault structure to be less 
than 1 inch. 
Lateral loads acting on the valve vault structure may be resisted by friction between 
the base of the foundation and on-site soils and by passive earth pressure developed 
against the near-vertical faces of the embedded valve vault.  A coefficient of friction of 
0.35 may be used for foundations bearing on the compacted on-site soils.  Resistance to 
lateral loads due to passive pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure 
of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf).  Unless covered by pavements or 
slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be 
neglected.

3.8 
Settlements 
Primary settlement of the water mains is normally caused by the difference in the 
unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the 
underlying subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our engineering analyses, 
settlement on the order of less than ½ inch is anticipated for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could occur if 
improper trench support is used.

The pH and minimum resistivity tests were performed in general conformance with 
ASTM G51 and G57, respectively, for laboratory pH and resistivity tests using a Miller 
box. The pH and minimum resistivity tests were also performed in general conformance 
with the requirements provided in the “Water System External Corrosion Control 
Standards,” Volume 3, dated 1991. 
Resistivity generally is recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regards to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower 
the resistivity is, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure will 
have. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to 
corrosion of the metallic objects. 
A total of 12 field resistivity surveys were conducted at a maximum pin interval 
spacing of about 20 feet at three locations, designated as R-1 through R-3, using the 
in-situ Wenner 4-Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured by 
Nilsson Electrical Laboratory, Inc. The method utilizes four surface probes or pins 
(electrodes) to evaluate the average resistivity (an indication of corrosivity) of the 
underlying soil mass. The field resistivity survey was used to evaluate the average 
resistivity values of the soil mass at the approximate survey locations. Results of the field 
resistivity surveys are presented on Plates F-1 and F-2 of Appendix F. It should be noted 
that the field resistivity survey may be influenced by temperature, weather conditions, soil 
moisture, existing underground utilities and other factors. 
Based on the field resistivity survey results, the subsurface soils at the project site 
have resistivity values ranging from about 2,070 to 7,200 ohm-cm, corresponding to 
corrosion ratings of 2 (very corrosive) to 4 (moderately corrosive), respectively, based on 
the Board of Water Supply, City and County of Honolulu Water System External Corrosion 
Control Standards, dated 1991. It should be noted that the resistivity values of the 
subsurface soils varied substantially with depth and location across the project area; 
therefore, special attention should be given by the corrosion engineer when using the 
resistivity results provided in this report. 
Based on the laboratory minimum resistivity test results presented in 
Appendices C and D, the subsurface soils at the project site exhibit minimum resistivity

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling three exploratory 
borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 103, extending to a depth of about 
10 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted 
drill rig equipped with 4-inch O.D. solid stem augers and PQ coring. The borings were 
drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.3. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration 
sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Please note that the efficiencies of the safety hammers used for this project were 
measured in May 2015 and the resulting energy transfer ratios are shown on the Logs of 
Borings. The soil consistencies and densities presented on the boring logs have been 
adjusted based on the energy transfer ratio, as appropriate.  
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual length 
of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For 
example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 
74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality 
of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling, 
mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is 
recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe 
the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of 
Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

Rock Log Legend
The following terms describe the resistance of a rock to indentation or scratching:
Specimen breaks with difficulty after several "pinging" hammer blows.
Example: Dense, fine grain volcanic rock
Specimen breaks with some difficulty after several hammer blows.
Example: Vesicular, vugular, coarse-grained rock
Specimen can be broked by one hammer blow. Cannot be scraped by knife. SPT may penetrate by
~25 blows per inch with bounce.
Example: Porous rock such as clinker, cinder, and coral reef
Can be indented by one hammer blow. Can be scraped or peeled by knife. SPT can penetrate by
~100 blows per foot.
Example: Weathered rock, chalk-like coral reef
Crumbles under hammer blow. Can be peeled and carved by knife. Can be indented by finger
pressure.
Example: Saprolite

Field Resistivity Survey 
Geophysical survey equipment (i.e. resistance meter and electrodes) was mobilized 
to the project site on January 17, 2018 to perform the field resistivity survey.  Twelve soil 
resistivity surveys were conducted at three locations, designated as R-1 through R-3, using 
the in-situ Wenner 4-Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured 
by Nilsson Electrical Laboratory, Inc. The method utilizes four surface probes or pins 
(electrodes) to evaluate the average resistivity of the underlying soil mass. The average 
resistivity values of the soil mass, extending from the existing ground surface to the test 
depth, was assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations shown on 
the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.3. 
The soil resistivity surveys were performed along the existing ground surface at the 
approximate survey locations shown on the site plan. The average soil resistance along the 
proposed alignment was evaluated at each survey location with the electrodes aligned at 
equal spacing of generally 2.5, 5, 10, and 20 feet. The depth of penetration of a resistivity 
sounding is a function of the electrode spacing. 
The resistance meter (Nilsson Model 400) provides a voltage between the outer 
electrodes, causing an electrical current to flow between them. The voltage drop between 
the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive, built-in voltmeter. As a result, the soil 
resistance (in ohms) is obtained from the voltmeter dial reading. The soil resistivity 
(in ohm-cm), which is directly related to both resistance and spacing, can then be computed. 
The evaluated soil resistances and computed resistivity values are presented on Plate F-1. 
In addition, the computed resistivity values with pin spacing at the test locations along the 
pipe line alignment are presented in the graph on Plate F-2. At the time of resistivity 
surveying, the air temperature was about 85º F. The existing ground surface was dry to 
damp. It should be noted that the soil resistivity may be influenced by temperature, weather 
conditions, existing underground facilities and other factors.
