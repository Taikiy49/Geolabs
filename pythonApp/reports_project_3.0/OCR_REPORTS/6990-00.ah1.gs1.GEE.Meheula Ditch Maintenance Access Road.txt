PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is adjacent to the Meheula Ditch, located on the west and east 
sides of Lanikuhana Avenue between Oheala Place and Laupalai Place in Mililani on 
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  Meheula Ditch starts near the intersection of Kamehameha 
Highway and Meheula Parkway and heads towards the southeast and outlets into the 
Waikele Stream.  The project involves repairing the maintenance access road, which 
has been narrowing due to erosion of the surface soils from stormwater surface runoff.  
Shallow gullies crossing the maintenance road and extending toward the existing 
channel structure were noted. 
We understand that the repair work will consist of widening the existing access 
road, placing a gravel pavement, and stabilizing the slopes between the access road 
and channel structure.  In addition, the evaluation of different gravel pavements 
consisting of a geocell system, rigid pavers, and gravel with geogrid is desired.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger Koolau 
Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and forms 
the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Waianae Volcano became extinct 
while the Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below 
the Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank. These banked or ponded lavas formed 
a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau.

The project site is located within the Schofield Plateau. The Schofield Plateau was 
formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against the already eroded slopes of 
the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch. The dips of the lava beds are near 
horizontal (between 3 to 5 degrees from horizontal). The lava flows on the plateau have 
undergone in-situ weathering to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the 
reddish color of the soil. 
This in-situ weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils that grade 
to saprolite with increasing depth. In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty material 
while residual soils are more clayey. Both residual and saprolitic soils are typical of the 
tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The residual and saprolitic soils grade to basaltic 
rock formation with increased depth. Spherically weathered boulders within the residual 
and saprolitic soils are typical in the Schofield Plateau.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on observations made during our site visit, the project site is generally 
covered with residual soil consisting of stiff to hard reddish brown silty clays.  Based on the 
shallow erosion gullies observed near the crest of the slopes adjacent to the access road, 
the probable cause of the narrowing of the access road is due to erosion from surface 
stormwater runoff.  Therefore, the maintenance access road pavement should be 
well-drained and erosion protection should be provided on the existing slopes to prevent 
erosion of the new access road and adjacent slopes. Detailed discussions and 
recommendations for the remedial design of the maintenance access road and other 
geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following sections. 
Maintenance Access Road Pavement Design 
We understand that the use of a geocell system, rigid pavers, and gravel with 
geogrid pavements are being considered.  In general, we anticipate vehicle loading will 
consist primarily of passenger vehicles, light pick-up trucks, and utility trucks which will 
result in less than 50,000 equivalent single axle loads (ESALs) during the roadway’s 
design life of 40 years.

The existing subgrade soils under the planned pavement structural section should 
be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 
percent above the optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
The following recommended pavement sections assume that adequate drainage 
will be provided for the subgrade, which should be sloped and drainage gradients 
maintained to collect and carry subsurface water away from the roadway structure. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed on-site during or after construction.  
Geocell System Pavement 
A geocell system typically consists of a series of polyethylene cells that are filled 
with soil or aggregates. The geocells increase the strength and stability of fills in the 
geocells by providing lateral confinement of the in-filled soil or aggregates. 
Based on the anticipated design traffic and the design CBR value for the subgrade 
soils, we recommend using the following preliminary geocell system pavement 
section for design purposes:

To protect the geocells from damage from direct tire loading, a minimum of 2 inches 
of No. 2 Coarse aggregate should be used above the top of the cells.  The 
overfilled No. 2 Coarse aggregates above the top of the cells should be tamped to 
create a smooth driving surface.  The aggregates along the downslope vertical face 
of the geocell roadway should be tamped and graded down to the ground level at 
an inclination of four horizontal to one vertical (4H:1V) or flatter. 
It should be noted that there is a potential for raveling and rutting of the gravel 
surface over time.  Therefore, periodic maintenance will be required for the gravel 
surfaced road. We believe the majority of the maintenance will consist of periodic 
grading to remove ruts and depressions caused by the environment and traffic. 
Rigid Paver Pavement 
We understand that a rigid paver pavement is also being considered for the 
planned maintenance access road.  Similar to geocells, rigid pavers increase the 
strength and stability of fills in the paver system by providing lateral confinement of 
the in-filled soils or aggregates.  Rigid pavers are typically shallower in height 
compared to the geocell system, and are rectangularly shaped with rigid walls that 
distribute vehicular loading and withstand torsional loading.  The rigidity of the 
material allows for faster placement of the paver along straight sections of 
roadways but requires cutting of the pavers to match contours along curved 
roadways.   
Due to the significant cost of rigid pavers, such as Presto GEOPAVE system, and 
the associated construction costs, we anticipate a rigid paver pavement will cost 
about twice as much as geocell system pavement.  Therefore, we recommend that 
this type of pavement section not be considered for the project. 
Gravel with Geogrid Pavement 
A gravel with geogrid pavement consists of aggregates reinforced with a geogrid 
layer to provide vertical and lateral stability. In general, the geogrids will interlock 
with the aggregate material resulting in two benefits consisting of increasing the 
modulus of the aggregate base course by providing lateral confinement and 
enhancing the subgrade bearing capacity.

We recommend not using 1/2-inch aggregate base course based on the geogrid 
openings. We also recommend utilizing aggregate base course because the higher 
fines content will provide a less permeable surface for runoff water to infiltrate the 
roadway section.  It should be noted that the aggregate base course will be more 
susceptible to weeds growing than clean gravel because of the higher fines content 
and susceptible to raveling due to lack of confinement near the surface. Therefore, 
long-term periodic maintenance will be required for this pavement section. 
The material costs of geogrids are considerably lower than geocell and rigid paver 
products, and geogrids are relatively faster to construct.  Therefore, we anticipate 
the gravel with geogrid pavement will cost less than half as much compared to the 
geocell system pavement.  
Slope Stability & Erosion Protection 
Based on the available site plans and cross-sections available at the time of this 
report, the slopes from the planned access road down to the concrete channel are at an 
inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Based on the limited site reconnaissance and the results of 
the laboratory testing, design soil parameters were developed and slope stability analyses 
were performed on the existing slopes. Based on our slope stability analyses, finished 
slope inclination of about 2H:1V or flatter should be provided for long-term stability of the 
slopes.   
To reduce the potential for future erosion of the slope face, we recommend 
installing a turf reinforcing matting (North American Green Vmax C350 or equal) on the 
slope face. Because of the sloping conditions, the matting should be properly anchored 
per manufacturer’s recommendations.

encroach towards existing retaining structures, such as drainage channel walls. It is 
unclear if the existing retaining structures were originally designed for vehicular surcharge 
loading from the maintenance access road.  Therefore, the retaining structures should be 
evaluated to resist the lateral earth pressures due to the surcharge effects. Based on the

Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, line loads, and point loads, including 
but not limited to tire loads, within a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the retaining

pressure acting over the entire height of the structures may be used for the design.

point loads and line loads.  
In general, an active condition may be used for gravity retaining walls, retaining 
structures, and channel walls that are free to deflect by as much as 0.5 percent of the 
structure height. If the tops of the structures are not free to deflect beyond this degree or 
are restrained, the retaining structures should be designed for the at-rest condition. These 
lateral earth pressures do not include hydrostatic pressures that might be caused by 
groundwater trapped behind the structures.

Soft and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm material, and the 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills.  
After clearing and grubbing, finished subgrades and areas designated to receive 
fills should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at 
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, and recompacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. The compaction requirements for finished 
subgrades subjected to vehicular traffic, such as the pavement subgrade, should be 
increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum 
dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
Where shrinkage cracks are observed at the ground surface after compaction of 
the subgrade, we recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended 
above. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to 
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas 
and replacing these areas with well-compacted fill. A Geolabs representative in the 
field should evaluate the need for over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil 
conditions. 
Fills and Backfills 
In general, the excavated on-site materials may be reused as a source of general 
fill materials provided that the materials are free of deleterious materials. Imported 
fill materials, if required, should consist of non-expansive select granular materials, 
such as crushed coral or basalt gravel. The materials should be well-graded from 
coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The 
materials should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a 
maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these 
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use.  
Aggregate base course should meet the material requirements for Aggregate Base 
Course as specified in Section 31 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works 
Construction, City and County of Honolulu, September 1986. The No. 2 Coarse 
aggregate should meet the material requirements for ASTM C33 No. 4 gradation.

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture 
content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction 
requirements for finished subgrades subjected to vehicular traffic, such as the 
pavement subgrade, should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction.  
Aggregate base course material should be moisture-conditioned to near the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  
Compaction should be accomplished by using sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, 
or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. However, it should be noted 
that caution should be used when vibratory equipment is used adjacent to the 
existing houses. Measures may need to be implemented to reduce the potential of 
inducing vibrations which may result in damage to the adjacent houses. If vibrations 
are anticipated during construction, we recommend the contractor develop and 
implement a vibration monitoring program to detect ground movement and/or 
subsidence adjacent to the nearby structures. 
As an additional check for stability and uniform compaction, we recommend 
proof-rolling the finished subgrade using a pneumatic-tired vehicle with a gross 
vehicle weight of at least 30,000 pounds, such as a fully loaded water truck. The 
equipment used for proof-rolling should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet 
per minute and make at least two passes over each area designated for 
proof-rolling.

Cut and Fill Slopes 
We envision that the cut slopes planned at the project site will generally expose the 
stiff silty clays observed during our site reconnaissance. In general, cut and fill 
slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills placed on 
slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to 
provide stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations should start at 
the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance 
with the above fill placement recommendations. 
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. In the event that overcutting of a slope 
occurs, keying and benching requirements should be implemented instead of 
backfilling the slope to the design grade with sliver fills. Water should be diverted

Excavations 
We understand that some excavations for the curbs may be required. Based on  
our site reconnaissance, the project site is generally underlain by stiff to hard silty 
clays. We anticipate that the near-surface materials may be excavated with normal 
heavy excavation equipment.  
However, it should be noted that saprolitic soils are commonly encountered at 
deeper depths beneath residual soils present at the site.  Within the residual and 
saprolitic soils, remnants of weathered rock materials and boulders are commonly

difficult excavation conditions.
