REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was
still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its
eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the
Schofield Plateau
The Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded
against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch
The lava flows on the plateau have undergone in situ weathering to depths of 50 to 100 feet
and are characterized by the red colors of the soil The deep narrow canyons such as
Waikakalaua Kipapa and Kaukonahua are the result of this in situ weathering
This in situ chemical weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils that
grade to saprolite with depth In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and
is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks Spheroidally weathered boulders are
typical in the Schofield Plateau The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with depth

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our exploration we believe that the proposed depressed
concrete paved parking area may consist of an 8 5 inch thick concrete slab bearing on the
underlying tannish white silty sand material or reddish brown clayey silt material The
subgrade material should be scarified moisture conditioned to above its optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report
Site Preparation
Site preparation operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Inc
It is important that a representative from our office observe the site preparation operations
to evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation process
GEOLABS INC
W O 4182 00 A Page 6
and to confirm that the exposed soil conditions are similar to those encountered in our field
exploration
At the on set of earthwork the project area within the asphalt concrete pavement
should be saw cut and removed The asphalt and other unsuitable materials i e vegetation
debris or rubbish should be removed and disposed of properly off site or stockpiled in a
designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered should be over excavated to expose firm natural
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with fill placed in 12 inch
maximum loose lifts moisture conditioned to at least the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction ASTM D 1557 91 The excavated
soft soils should be disposed of properly off site and or used in landscape areas where
appropriate
At the finish subgrade the existing ground should be scarified to a depth of 10 inches
and moisture conditioned to at least the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Where shrinkage cracks are noted after
compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close
cracks or prepared again as recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of
the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with fill placed in 12 inch maximum loose
lifts moisture conditioned to at least optimum moisture content and compacted to at least
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
The fill material should consist of well graded low expansive materials less than
3 inches in largest dimension The fill material should also be free of vegetation and
GEOLABS INC
W O 4182 00 A Page 7
deleterious materials In general the on site clayey silt material should be suitable for fill
material
Imported fill should consist of non expansive granular material such as crushed coral
mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with
no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR
value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 Imported fill materials should be tested
by Geolabs Inc for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project
site for its intended use
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils are
critical elements of earthwork observations and soil density tests should be performed by
Geolabs Inc during the site preparation operation to assist the contractor in obtaining the
required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content Where compaction is less
than required additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture
content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction It should be noted that the
moisture requirement of the subgrade is an important requirement for the use of the on site
clayey soils
Slabs On Grade
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by
near surface clayey soils with low swelling potential However to reduce the potential for
structural distress resulting from the shrinking and swelling of the subgrade soils we
recommend that the subgrade soils be kept at or above the optimum moisture content prior
to final placement of concrete
The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab and graded
to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential of water ponding around the slab

Utilities
It is anticipated that some utilities may be constructed for the proposed project The
methods and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the contractor
subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavation should comply with all
applicable local state and federal safety requirements Trench shoring design and
installation should be the responsibility of the contractor Trench shoring and bracing should
conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under
the pipes The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the
pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material be
used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use of on site soils as backfill
immediately around utility pipes is not recommended
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
finish subgrade should consist of approved on site soils or select granular material The
backfill material should be moisture conditioned to at least the optimum moisture content

of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence The

less than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical compaction equipment should be used
to compact the materials at the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding should not
be used to compact the backfill material
