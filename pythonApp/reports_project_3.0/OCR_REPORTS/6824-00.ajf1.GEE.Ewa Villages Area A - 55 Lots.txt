SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered about 8 to 10 feet of stiff/dense fill 
materials overlying stiff to hard alluvial soils to the maximum depth explored of about 
21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The fill materials consisted of stiff to very 
stiff silty clay and very dense coral sand and gravel, while the alluvial soils consisted of 
stiff to hard silty clay. 
Our laboratory test results and previous experience indicate these clayey soils 
exhibit moderate to high shrink/swell potential characteristics when subjected to 
fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. Proper site preparation, earthwork procedures, 
and foundation design are necessary to provide adequate support for the building 
structures. 
Due to the moderately to highly expansive soil conditions at the site, we 
recommend over-excavating the clayey soil a minimum depth of 3 feet below finished 
grade and replacing with non-expansive, select granular fill material beneath the 
building slab area. Additionally, we recommend a proprietary slab system, the 
Wafflemat, to be used for the building foundations to resist the movements of the 
expansive soil.  
For foundation design of the anticipated lightly loaded residential structures, an 
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for footings bearing on the 
compacted fill placed in accordance with the recommendations presented herein. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL

performed for the Ewa Villages Area A – 55 Lots project in the Ewa area on the Island 
of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project

findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended to assist in the design of site 
preparation, infrastructure installation, building foundations, and driveway pavements. 
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted 
at the end of this report.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be 
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. 
The project site is situated on the Ewa Coastal Plain, which extends seaward off 
the southeastern flank of the Waianae Volcano and the southwestern flank of the 
Koolau Volcano. This plain was formed by the accumulation of a thick wedge of 
sediments and coral reef formations on the flanks of the two volcanoes.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to 
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. 
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous 
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials. Grading operations for the Ewa Villages project have brought 
the site to its present form.

Seismic Design Considerations 
The project site may be subject to seismic activity, and seismic design 
considerations will need to be addressed. The following sections provide discussions on 
the seismicity of the Island of Oahu and the soil profile (site class) for seismic design. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to 
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or 
during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the 
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not 
erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but 
most are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. 
However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause 
minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes directly associated with the movement of magma are 
concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island 
of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) are 
related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking 
diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii 
has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, 
earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii.  
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui also has experienced earthquakes greater 
than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have occurred in the 
County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands of Hawaii and 
Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, small landslides occurred on

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered about 8 to 10 feet of stiff/dense fill 
materials overlying stiff to hard alluvial soils to a maximum depth explored of about 
21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The fill materials consisted of stiff to very 
stiff silty clay and very dense coral sand and gravel, while the alluvial soils consisted of 
stiff to hard silty clay. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the 
time of our field exploration. 
Our laboratory test results and previous experience indicate these clayey soils 
exhibit moderate to high shrink/swell potential characteristics when subjected to 
fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. Proper site preparation, earthwork procedures, 
and foundation design are necessary to provide adequate support for the building 
structures. 
Due to the moderately to highly expansive soil conditions at the site, we 
recommend over-excavating the clayey soil a minimum depth of 3 feet below finished 
grade and replacing with non-expansive, select granular fill material beneath the 
building slab area. Additionally, we recommend a proprietary slab system, the 
Wafflemat, to be used for the building foundations to resist the movements of the 
expansive soil.  
Prior to placement of the select granular fill, the soil subgrades should be 
scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction. The subgrade soils should be kept moist until covered with the select 
granular fill and/or improvements. 
Shrinkage cracks have been known to occur in expansive soils in the Ewa area 
during prolonged dry weather conditions and were observed at the existing ground at 
the time of our field exploration. Shrinkage cracks adjacent to the building foundation 
are potentially detrimental because water can readily infiltrate into the subsurface 
through the crack openings and soften the subsurface soils around the building 
foundations, thus affecting their performance. Therefore, we recommend scarifying the

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other 
unsuitable materials, should be removed and disposed properly off-site or 
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the 
excavated materials. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing, grubbing, and over-excavation 
work should be over-excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting 
excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted engineered fill. The excavated 
soft soils may be re-used as fill, provided that fill material requirements are met.

3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
Generally, we anticipate the earthwork will likely involve two primary types of 
materials: select granular fill and general fill. Select granular fill should be used for 
the top 3 feet beneath the building slab area and consist of non-expansive granular 
material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The material should 
be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest 
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the 
No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and 
should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D 1883. Geolabs should test imported fill materials for conformance with 
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
General fills below 3 feet from finished grade may consist of the excavated on-site 
soils with a maximum particle size of 6 inches and should be free of deleterious 
materials. Excavated on-site materials with high organic content and/or deleterious 
materials should be disposed off-site properly or used in landscape areas 
(if appropriate).

3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fills and backfills using on-site clayey soils should be moisture-conditioned to 
about 5 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 
8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to between 85 and 90 percent relative 
compaction. Fills and backfills consisting of select granular fill material should be 
moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in 
level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum 
of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry 
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same 
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.  
It should be noted that our recommendations provide elevated moisture contents 
and reduced compaction requirements for fill and backfill work to limit the potential 
swell capacity of the clayey soils encountered at the site. Therefore, we wish to 
emphasize that moisture conditioning of the fill materials is an integral and critical 
part of the fill and backfill placement recommendations provided herein. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the subgrade soils are critical 
elements of earthwork, particularly for the reduced compaction requirements, 
Geolabs should provide observations and perform soil density tests during site 
grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the appropriate degree of compaction 
and the proper ranges of moisture content. It should be noted that the moisture and 
reduced compaction requirements for fills and subgrades are important criteria for 
use of the on-site clayey soils. Where compaction is less than required, additional 
compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as 
necessary, to obtain the specified compaction. Similarly, in situations where the 
reduced compaction requirements are applicable, it may be necessary to scarify the 
soil and re-compacted it to the proper range of relative compaction, if the required 
rang of compaction is exceeded.

3.1.4 Excavation 
It is anticipated that the on-site alluvial clayey soils may be excavated with 
conventional heavy excavation equipment. However, our past experience in the 
Ewa Villages area indicates that coral formation could be encountered and the 
surface of the underlying coral formation is karstic in nature with large elevation 
variations in the top of the formation. Therefore, excavation for subsurface 
infrastructure construction may encounter hard coral, which may require the use of 
hoerams or trenching machines.  
The above discussion regarding the rippability of the surface materials is based on 
data obtained from the field borings. Contractors bidding on this project should 
examine the site conditions and soil data to make their own interpretation. 
3.1.5 Cut and Fill Slopes 
It was assumed that elevation changes to reach design finish grade will be minimal 
and that few cut or fill slopes will be involved for the overall site grading. However, 
this section gives guidance for design and construction of slopes in the event that 
they may be required. Based on the encountered subsurface conditions, it appears 
that cut slopes required for this project would expose soils consisting of stiff to very 
stiff clayey soil or dense coral sand and gravel. In general, we believe that cut 
slopes into the soil material may be at an inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. 
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. To provide 
stability of the new fill against sliding, fills should be keyed and benched into the 
existing slope. The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up 
in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the fill placement 
recommendations. 
To obtain a well-compacted slope face, fill slopes should be constructed by 
overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio. Track-rolling of slopes should 
not be accepted. In the event that over-cutting of a slope occurs, keying and 
benching requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling with sliver fills 
to the design grade. To reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes, water

3.2 
House Foundations 
For foundation design of the anticipated lightly loaded residential structures, an 
allowable bearing pressure of 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be utilized for 
footings bearing on the compacted fill or backfill placed in accordance with the 
recommendations presented herein. This bearing value is for supporting dead-plus-live 
loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) when considering transient loads, such as 
those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Footings adjacent to or on slopes should be embedded to a sufficient depth to 
provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of at least 8 feet measured from the 
outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope. Foundations next to utility trenches 
or easements should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending 
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench, or the footings should be extended to 
a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. 
We estimate that settlements for footings bearing on properly compacted fill, as 
recommended herein, to be less than 1 inch total. Differential settlement between 
footings supported on similar materials is estimated to be less than 0.5 inch. 
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure 
acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system and by friction developed 
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil. Resistance due to passive 
earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per 
square foot per foot of depth (pcf). This assumes that the soils around the footings are 
well-compacted. Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the 
upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected. For footings bearing on properly 
compacted select granular fill, a coefficient of friction of 0.40 may be used.

resist the movements of the expansive soil. This proprietary system utilizes a series of 
recycled plastic boxes, “Waffleboxes”, which are placed with the open side down on the 
soil. The boxes are arranged to form deep ribs or footings throughout the floor system, 
creating a very stiff foundation. A post-tensioned concrete slab is monolithically poured 
over and between the boxes. The empty space beneath the boxes allow for soil 
expansion, reducing the uplift on the slab. 
Generally, minimum subgrade preparation is required for the Waffelmat. 
However, we recommend that prior to placing the Waffleboxes, the subgrade soil be 
moistened to above optimum moisture content and covered with a plastic membrane to 
maintain the soil moisture.

3.4 
Sidewalks 
To help reduce the effects of the expansive soils, sidewalks should be underlain 
by a minimum of 12 inches of select granular fill. The select granular fill should consist 
of well graded granular material with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest 
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 
sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR of 20 or more and should have a 
maximum swell of less than 1 percent. The select granular fill should be compacted to 
at least 90 percent relative compaction. Control joints should be provided at intervals 
equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right-angle intersections. 
The thickened edges of walkways adjacent to unpaved areas should be 
embedded at least 8 inches below the lowest adjacent grade. It should be emphasized 
that the areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the edges of the 
slabs with relatively impervious soils. These areas also should be graded to divert water 
away from the walkways and to reduce the potential for water ponding around the 
walkway edges.

Retaining Structures 
For the construction of the proposed residential development, retaining walls may 
be required for grade separation. In general, the following guidelines may be used for 
preliminary design of the retaining structures. 
3.5.1 Wall Foundations 
In general, we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed in 
accordance with the "Foundations" section presented in this report. The 
foundations should have a minimum width of 18 inches and should be embedded a 
minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade. For sloping ground 
conditions, the footings should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 8-foot horizontal 
setback distance measured from the outside edge of the footing to the slope face. 
Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in 
stepped footings.

3.5.3 Drainage 
To reduce the build-up for hydrostatic pressures, retaining structures should be well 
drained. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of 
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation),

retaining structure discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an 
alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may 
be used in lieu of the drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product should 
also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of select granular fill

The subgrades below the pavement section should be scarified to a depth of 
about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, 
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to 
carry the surface water off-site. Surface water ponding should not be allowed on-site 
during or after construction. To reduce the potential for migration of landscape water 
into the pavement section, we recommend using thickened edges for the pavement 
slabs adjacent to unpaved areas. These thickened edges should be embedded at least 
12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade. Alternatively, a subdrain system could be 
constructed to collect the excess water from landscaping irrigation. For long-term 
performance, we recommend constructing a subdrain system adjacent to the 
paved/landscaped areas.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, each extending to a depth of about 
21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-4. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) 
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and 
evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Six Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & ASTM C 136) including six hydrometer tests 
(ASTM D 422) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation 
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Graphic presentation of the 
test results are provided on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Four one-inch ring swell tests were performed on remolded samples to evaluate 
the swelling potential of the near-surface soils. The test results are summarized on 
Plate B-4. 
Five Expansion Index of Soil tests were performed on samples of the 
near-surface soils at selected locations in general accordance with ASTM D 4829. The 
test is a standardized test used to determine the expansive characteristics of the soil. 
The Expansion Index of Soil test is the same test (UBC Standard 18-2) described in the 
Uniform Building Code (UBC) to determine the expansive characteristics of soils. The 
test results are summarized on Plate B-5.
