Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we envision the new garages
at the Heleloa MCBH site will be underlain by stiff to very stiff clayey soils that were
encountered in the shallow subsurface Therefore we recommend supporting the new

may consist of thickened edge slab footings An allowable bearing pressure on order of

on the recompacted on site soils This bearing value is for sizing footings based on
loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces

Garage Slabs and Concrete Driveways
We envision concrete slabs on grade will be used for the new garage structures
and driveways constructed at or near the existing grades Based on the laboratory test
results the clayey fill soils encountered exhibit variable potential for shrinking and
swelling ranging from slight to moderate when subjected to moisture fluctuations
To reduce the potential for appreciable future distress to the lightly loaded
slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the surface silty and clayey
soils we recommend preparing the subgrade properly and capping the subgrade with a
12 inch thick layer of non expansive select granular fill materials or aggregate subbase
which should extend a minimum of 2 feet beyond the edges of the structures

subbase we recommend scarifying the clayey subgrade soils to a depth of about
8 inches moisture conditioning the soils to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and compacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The

performed for the Regional Housing Privatization Increment III
Marine Sites project located within the Marine Corps Base Hawaii MCBH at

and installation of underground utility lines only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

The Hawaii Regional Housing Privatization project consists of reconstructing and
renovating military housing at various locations across the Island of Oahu Hawaii This

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano became
extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried
below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank The Koolau Volcano reached the
end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
The project is located on Mokapu Peninsula which was built by three volcanic
vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series These vents are believed to be among the
oldest of the series The pyroclastic deposits and lava flows from vents formed salients
permitting the growth of a coral reef that merged with the main body of the Island of
Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age many sea level changes occurred as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level of Oahu During the times of low stands of the sea
emerged coral reefs were blown inland by the prevailing winds to form sand dunes

Fills and Backfills
In general the excavated on site clayey soils may be re used as a source of
general fill materials provided that the materials are free of vegetation deleterious
materials and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum
dimension The on site clayey soils if accepted for use as general fills may be
used as part of the trench backfill or in landscape areas
Imported general fill materials should consist of soil materials with a maximum
particle size of 3 inches or less with sufficient fines between 10 and 60 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve to prevent the occurrence of voids in the
compacted mass In addition general fill materials should have a CBR value of 8 or
greater and a swell of 2 percent or less when tested in accordance with
ASTM D 1883
Select granular fills if needed should consist of select granular materials such as
crushed coralline or basaltic materials The materials should be well graded from
coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The
materials should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of 1 percent or less Imported fill materials should be tested for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use

3 4 Corrosion Potential
A total of 12 soil resistivity surveys were conducted at three selected locations
designated as R 1 through R 3 on the Site Plan Plate 2 using the in situ
Wenner 4 Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured by
Nilsson Electrical Laboratory Inc The method utilizes four surface probes or pins
electrodes to evaluate the average resistivity of the underlying soil mass The average
resistivity values of the soil mass extending from the existing ground surface to the test
depth was assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 Results of the field soil resistivity surveys are presented on
Plate C 1 of Appendix C
In addition we conducted laboratory tests including pH minimum resistivity
chloride contents and sulfate contents to evaluate the corrosivity of the underlying
subsurface soils encountered at the project site Based on the test results presented on
Plate B 4 the subsurface soils generally exhibit a minimum resistivity value of about
4 520 and 7 710 ohm cm indicating that the subsurface soils are corrosive to
moderately corrosive to buried metallic structures Therefore we recommend properly
designing substructures especially metallic substructures to be protected against
potential corrosion A corrosion engineer should be consulted for detailed
recommendations on corrosion protection

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the housing project site were explored by drilling
and sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths
of about 19 5 to 20 2 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring

examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on the Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests performed on selected samples are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX C
Field Resistivity Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Resistivity Testing
Resistivity generally is recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects In general the lower the
resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure
Conversely the higher the resistivity the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of
metallic objects
Geophysical survey equipment i e resistance meter was mobilized to the project
site on for the field survey Twelve soil resistivity surveys were
conducted at three locations designated as R 1 through R 3 on the Site Plan Plate 2
using the in Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter
manufactured by Nilsson Electrical Laboratory Inc Our field resistivity work was
performed under cloudy conditions with generally damp surface conditions
The method utilizes four surface probes or pins electrodes to evaluate the
average resistivity an indication of corrosivity of the underlying soil mass The average
resistivity values of the soil mass extending from the existing ground surface to the test
depth was assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations
The average soil resistance was evaluated at each survey location with the
electrodes aligned at equal spacings of various distances based on the anticipated utility
depths The depth of penetration of a resistivity sounding is a function of the electrode
spacing
The resistance meter Nilsson Model 400 provides a voltage between the outer
electrodes causing an electrical current to flow between them The voltage drop between
the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive built in voltmeter As a result the soil
resistance in ohms is obtained from the voltmeter dial reading The average soil
resistivity in ohms cm which is directly related to both resistance and spacing can then
be computed Results of the field resistivity surveys are presented on Plate C 1 It should
be noted that the soil resistivity may be influenced by temperature weather conditions soil
moisture existing underground utilities and other factors
