PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
It is desired to repair sections of shotcrete placed at Diamond Head tunnel that 
were reported as “hazardous falling objects” for an emergency procurement. Based on the 
information provided, we understand that a section of the shotcrete above and adjacent to 
the Tunnel M-3 doorway was reported to have been cracking and/or spalling off, resulting 
in an imminent safety hazard. Tunnel M-3 is currently being used by the Department of 
Health to store emergency preparedness equipment for use in the event of an emergency. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that eight tunnels were 
constructed during World II along the side of Diamond Head Crater. The tunnel entrances 
were shotcreted to prevent fractured rock raveling. As-built drawings for the tunnels were 
not available at the time of our study. Locations of these eight tunnel entrances are shown 
on the General Site Plan, Plate 2.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of the 
western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of the 
eastern two-thirds of the islands. It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau 
was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central 
Oahu. 
Volcanic activity from Koolau Volcano ceased for nearly 2 million years. Volcanic 
activity once again resumed from mid-Pleistocene Epoch and has lasted through recent 
geologic time. These volcanic events, known as the Honolulu Volcanic Series, have 
formed cinder, spatter, tuff and ash cones throughout the southern portion of the Island of 
Oahu. 
One of the most prominent vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series is Diamond 
Head. Diamond Head is a tuff cone and was generally formed by hydromagmatic 
explosions. Materials within the tuff cone consist of magmatic debris, blocks of Koolau

at Tunnel M-3, which is used by the Department of Health for emergency management 
and supplies. However, our observations extended to all tunnel entrances due to 
additional potential falling objects observed and identified in other locations during the 
initial site reconnaissance.

The project site is underlain by highly to moderately fractured weathered tuff 
formation with either horizontal or adverse bedding. The fractured near-vertical rock slope 
is covered by concrete shotcrete. Based on our observation, the existing shotcrete facing 
is about ½ to 2-inch thick with no visible wire-mesh reinforcing. No drainage weephole 
was observed at the bottom of the shotcreted near-vertical cut slope. 
Our site reconnaissance consisted of visual observation and physical sounding 
examination (with a hammer) in spall/crack areas to determine approximate limits of the 
emergency shotcrete repairs identified with photographic documentations. The shotcrete 
distresses observed may be categorized as follows.

Surface drainage swales were observed above the tunnel crown, which serves to 
reduce water from sheet flow directly flowing over the entrance. However, the drainage 
swales above Tunnels M-2 and M-3 were observed to have extensive damage due to tree 
root lifting and/or impact from previous rockfall events. Some sections of the drainage 
swales were undermined due to erosion from surface water. The damaged drainage 
swales resulted in water infiltration behind the shotcrete facing, which may have 
introduced excessive hydrostatic pressure. 
We also observed over-grown vegetation above the tunnel crown. Large tree roots 
were exposed in spalled sections through the fractured tuff rock. The tree roots push the 
shotcrete causing cracks and spalls. 
SHOTCRETE REPAIR RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our observations, it appeared that an imminent falling object safety 
hazard may occur not only near the entrance of Tunnel M-3, but also at other tunnel 
entrances. This assessment was based on the sizes of spall/crack, locations of potential 
falling objects, and dimensions of the tunnel entrances. It is our opinion that the 
emergency shotcrete repairs should be implemented near the entrances of Tunnels M-0.1, 
M-1 through M-3, and M-5. Photos of these tunnel entrances are presented on Plates 3.1 
through 3.5. Details of existing shotcrete facing conditions are documented on Plates 4.1 
through 4.6. 
As previously mentioned, reinforcement was not observed in the existing 1 to 2-inch 
thick shotcrete facing. We recommend using fiber reinforced shotcrete with a minimum 
7-day unconfined compressive strength of 3,000 pounds per square inch (psi) for the 
shotcrete repairs. The fiber reinforcement should consist of synthetic macro fiber 
reinforcement in accordance with ASTM C1116-03, Strux 85/50, or accepted equal. The 
shotcrete should contain 128 ounces per cubic yard of shrinkage reducing admixture. 
water to cement ratio should be limited to 0.45. We recommend a thorough cure of the 
shotcrete by use of a curing agent. The repaired shotcrete thickness should match the 
existing adjacent shotcrete facing.  
It should be noted that potential hazard was also observed at other tunnel 
entrances, but with less severity. We understand that a regular maintenance contract will 
be in process for repairing deteriorated shotcrete sections. As an interim precaution 
measure, we recommend installing warning signs, such as “DANGER! ENTER WITH 
CAUTION!” at each location. 
Based on our observation of the existing shotcrete, we identified the sections to be 
included for repair as part of the emergency procurement on Plates 3.1 through 3.5. It

Pre-Construction 
Prior to starting the repair construction, the contractor should verify and delineate 
the repair sections by visually examining and sounding the concrete surface with a 
hammer or by other approved method. The repair sections should be clearly marked on 
the shotcrete facing with spray paint for the engineer’s inspection and concurrence. 
The contractor should remove concrete in the identified repair sections using 
shallow saw cut, pneumatic hammer, and/or chisels as appropriate. The exposed concrete 
surface and/or fractured rock surface should be abrasive blasted to remove loose or 
weakened material/debris that could prevent or reduce shotcrete bond, prior to applying 
shotcrete. The edges of the patch should be square to the thickness of the patch with no 
feathering of edges. Long, narrow patching areas or sections of a patch should be 
avoided. The repair areas should be saturated with clean water the day before applying 
shotcrete, and kept moist until the shotcrete placement. 
The time between removal of shotcrete section and applying shotcrete should not 
exceed 5 days. If the time allowance exceeds 5 days, the contractor should prepare the 
surface as the above, before applying shotcrete. 
The shotcrete contractor’s nozzleman should have a minimum of 3 years shotcrete 
experience on similar size and type projects. The nozzleman should be certified by the 
American Concrete Institute (ACI). The experience description and certification should be 
submitted, including the name, address and telephone number of referenced projects, for 
the engineer’s review and approval. The nozzleman should maintain certification at all 
times while work is being performed for the emergency shotcrete repairs. 
The contractor should submit the concrete mix design with fiber reinforcement, 
associated with compressive strength testing results meeting the specified strength, for the 
engineer’s review and approval.

Shotcrete 
The shotcrete should be placed from the lower part of the repair area upwards to 
prevent accumulation of rebound.  The nozzleman should maintain a position to orient the 
nozzle at a distance and approximately perpendicular to the working face, so that rebound 
will be minimal and compaction will be maximized. The rebound should be removed by 
blowing and raking off, to avoid sand pockets in the shotcrete. 
The nozzleman should maintain the water amount at a practicable minimum, so the 
mix properly adheres to the repair area. Water content should not become high enough to 
cause the mix to sag or fall from vertical or inclined surfaces, or to separate in horizontal 
layers. 
The shotcrete placement should be suspended in the event of high winds or heavy 
rain that may prevent proper application or cause cement washing out or mortar sloughing. 
The surface finish of the repair section should be the natural finish as applied, 
similar to the existing adjacent shotcrete. The joint between the repair section and the 
existing shotcrete should be finished with proper floats and steel trowels. The contractor 
should check for sags, bridging, and other deficiencies as soon as the shotcrete is placed. 
Quality Control and Testing 
A test panel with dimensions of 18” x 18” x 6” should be prepared prior to 
production shotcrete placement for the engineer’s review and acceptance. The test panel 
should be prepared in a similar position as the emergency shotcrete repairs, to 
demonstrate the shotcrete is being applied properly and matching the existing shotcrete 
facing. The test panel should be stored on-site for reference. 
Three core samples with 3-inch diameter should be retrieved from the test panel 
7 days after of placement. The cores should be inspected for delamination, sand pockets, 
and tested for unconfined compressive strength. If the unconfined compressive strength is 
less than 3,000 psi, the contractor should revise the concrete mix design and prepare 
another test panel for approval and testing at no additional cost to the State. 
Approximately 72 hours after completing the shotcrete placement, the contractor 
should thoroughly test the surface with a hammer, to verify the repair section soundness, 
in the presence of the engineer. Unsound portions should be removed and replaced prior 
to the final inspection of the work at no additional compensation from the State. 
The engineer may require additional core samples be taken from the repair section, 
to verify the compressive strength. If the results of the compressive strength exceed 
3,000 psi, the State will pay the cost of coring and testing, otherwise, the contractor should 
pay the cost. The contractor should submit a correction plan for approval and 
implementation to meet the design requirement at no additional cost to the State. The core 
holes should be patched using the same concrete mix as the repair section.

The clearing and grubbing includes removal of vegetation and debris above the top 
tunnel crown within 5 feet from the top edge. After clearing and grubbing, all exposed tree 
stems and roots should be cut flush to ground surface and poisoned to reduce the

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted in this letter report are based, in part, 
upon information obtained from our review of the available information and our field 
reconnaissance conducted for the emergency shotcrete repairs project. Variations of 
subsurface conditions from those assumed in our evaluation may occur, and the nature 
and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is underway.  If 
variations then appear evident, Geolabs should be contacted to re-evaluate the 
recommendations presented herein.
