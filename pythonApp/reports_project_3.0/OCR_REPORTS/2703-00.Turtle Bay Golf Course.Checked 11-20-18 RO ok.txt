INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed modifications to the existing Turtle Bay Golf
Course at Kahuku on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location of the site and
its vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The exploration was performed
in general accordance with our proposal dated
This report summarizes the findings of the exploration and our geotechnical
engineering recommendations derived from our analyses based on the data obtained
during the exploration These recommendations are intended for design input and are
not intended for construction estimation or bidding purposes
GEOLABS HAWAII

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface
conditions at the project site in order to develop a generalized soil data set for the
formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to site grading and
foundation design of the project
The work was done in general accordance with our proposal dated November 2
1990 The scope of work consisted of the following
1 Reconnaissance of the project site and vicinity to make visual observations
of the surfical soil conditions
2 The installation of three 3 automatic reading electrical piezometers in
drilled holes to measure the groundwater level fluctuations
3 Review of available soils and geologic information pertinent to the project
site and its vicinity
4 Engineering analyses of the field and laboratory data for the purpose of
developing site grading and foundation design recommendations
5 Preparation of this report presenting our findings and design
recommendations
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration and piezometer readings are
presented in the appendices of this report

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The existing Turtle Bay Golf Course is situated on the western and southern sides
of the Turtle Bay Hilton Resort Hotel The golf course modifications will general consist
of
1 Site filling of up to five 5 feet
2 Excavation for the golf course lakes to as much as minus five 5 feet
elevation
3 Construction of an underpass structure
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located on the northern tip of Oahu adjacent to the existing
Turtle Bay Hilton Resort It is bounded by Kamehameha Highway to the south the new
hotel golf course to the east and the Pacific Ocean to the north
The topography of the existing golf course is gently sloping with ground elevations
ranging from 2 to 10 feet above Mean Sea Level
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built during the late Pliocene early Pleistocene by two
basaltic shielded volcanoes Waianae and Koolau Waianae is the older of the two and
forms the bulk of the western half of the island The younger Koolau forms the eastern
half of the island extending from Kahuku to Koko Head
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2703 00 Page 4
After the main shield building phase of the two volcanoes the island began to
subside in response to tectonic stresses imposed by the loads of the mountain masses
Concurrently reefs began to grow on the flanks of the subsiding volcanoes forming
fringing and barrier reefs of coral algal limestone
During the middle and late Pleistocene or Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response
to the advance and retreat of the continental glaciers As the glaciers grew and
advanced there was less water to fill the ocean basins so sea levels fell to below the
current level When the glaciers melted and retreated more water was available and sea
levels rose often to levels above the present stand of the sea
The geologic conditions at the project site are a direct result of these glacioeustatic
sea level changes during the Pleistocene
Most of the site is underlain by coral algal reef materials believed to have been
deposited during the Waimanalo 25 feet high stand of the sea
In some areas of the site this reef material is overlain by somewhat cemented
dunes of calcareous sands believed to have been deposited during the Mamala 350
feet low stand of the sea
In the low areas of the site the Waimanalo reef material is generally mantled by a
thin layer of recent alluvium or recent organic soils

Soil Conditions
The borings indicate that most of the existing golf course is underlain by coralline
sand and gravel ranging from loose to dense
Hard coral formation may be encountered near the existing grade particularly near
the existing tennis courts and in thin ledges at various depths throughout the site
Cavities are common in coral environments and have been encountered during
construction activities at the resort development
Boring logs from previous explorations are presented in Appendix A
Groundwater Conditions
The three 3 piezometers provided continuous groundwater level readings for an
extended period of time and indicated that the permeable sandy soil and proximity to the
ocean result in groundwater levels close to sea level with fluctuations which correspond
to tidal variations
All three stations show tidal influence to some degree Station 3 I is closest to the
shore about 400 feet away The other two 4 l and 5 l are nearly twice as far nearly 850
feet from shore The data show that Station 5 I has the highest tidal influence with nearly
one 1 foot of variation On the other hand 3 1 the closest station to the shore and 4 1
show a smaller amplitude of tidal fluctuation only about 0 3 feet This may indicate strata
allowing more freely flowing circulation between 5 1 and the sea The other stations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2703 00 Page 6
appear to show more restrictive strata such as a silty or clayey material between them
and the sea
A much greater influence on groundwater elevation seems to be the weather The
two stations closest to the shore recorded a groundwater level one 1 foot higher than
sea level after the rainstorm on November 20th After approximately 8 days the water
gradually assumed a level of 0 5 feet above sea level at both stations This implies a thin
fresh water lens floating on the sea water at these locations Station 5 recorded the
flooding of the surrounding ground surface and subsequent inundation of the test well
At the time of data retrieval during the storm on November 20th there were approximately
0 5 feet of water ponded on the ground over the well As in the two other stations the
water eventually returned to an average level approximately 0 5 feet above sea level This
indicates the occurrence of a thin fresh water lens floating on the sea water just as the
other stations had

in Appendix A

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration encountered generally competent subsoil conditions at the
site consisting of coralline sand and gravel deposits which can support the proposed fills
and cuts

Golf Course Site Grading
Special grading design is contemplated for the golf course fills due to landscaping
concerns Treated sewage effluent will be used for landscape watering as well as a
means of effluent disposal Therefore there are concerns for adequate drainage in the
surface soils Ponding of the irrigation water could hinder the growth of vegetation
In order to provide adequate drainage of the near surface soils the site grading
design should allow for good surface water movement and drainage The proposed fill
soils may be from designated borrow sites in which relatively clayey materials were
encountered The fill soils should be moderately compacted to provide additional
subsurface drainage of the landscape water Since the soils in the golf course will not be
required to support structural loads it is our opinion that a lower level of compaction
effort may be used
Site Preparation
Areas to be filled should be cleared of vegetation debris and other deleterious
materials
Fill Material Placement
Fill material may be obtained from a designated borrow site situated about one 1
mile southeast of the site mauka of Kamehameha Highway
We believe that most of the borrow site soils may be suitable for use as general
fill provided that they are not contaminated with unsuitable materials or oversized
particles
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2703 00 Page 8
Highly expansive clay soils if encountered should be used in the deeper fill areas
to allow better surface drainage
In the golf course areas the top four 4 feet of fill should be compacted to a
minimum of 75 but no greater than 85 per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
This lower level of compaction as opposed to the normal 90 required for general
fills will provide a slightly higher degree of porosity in the generally low
permeability borrow soils
Due to the reduced level of compaction fill slope ratios should be limited to 3H 1V
or flatter
Cuts are primarily planned for the creation of lakes therefore cut slopes would be
constructed and maintained under water The cutsslope ratio would depend on
the type of material encountered For the sandy coralline soils encountered a cut
slope ratio of 3H 1V or flatter is recommended Properly designed slope protection
methods such as riprap or surface lining should be considered for wind and wave
scouring action
Settlements
It is anticipated that the placement of fill in the areas of the golf course underlain
by loose soils will induce settlements on the order of 1 to 3 inches for about 10
feet of fill

Cavities
Cavities are common in coralline and limestone formations They are caused by
the presence of fresh water which inhibits the growth of the coral and or dissolves
channels in the formation Many cavities have been encountered in the general area at
various depths below the surface
The conventional method to locate and fill cavities is to drill probe holes throughout
the site at close spacings and fill the cavities encountered by pumping cement grout into
the holes Due to the very large areal extent of the site this does not seem practical nor
economical Therefore it appears that the cavities should be dealt with on an individual
basis when encountered

2 An allowable bearing value of 3 000 p s f may be used for retaining wall
foundations bearing on sand or coral soils

3 A passive earth resistance of 300 p s f per foot of depth may be used for
wall footings on level areas The passive pressure must be reduced for
walls located on slopes
4 A base friction factor of 0 4 may also be used to determine the resistance
of the wall against sliding
5 The wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a
minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent grade For sloping
ground the footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 8 foot
set back distance from the outside edge of the footing measured
horizontally to the slope face
6 A subsurface drainage system subdrains and or weepholes should be
utilized behind the walls to minimize the build up of hydrostatic pressure
behind the walls Subdrains should be constructed properly to drain water
away from the walls
7 Select granular material e g select borrow or coral is recommended as
backfill behind the retaining walls to reduce the amount of compactive effort
required The upper two 2 feet of backfill should consist of an impervious
non expansive material to reduce the infiltration of surface water behind the
walls
8 Care should be taken in the siting of proposed walls and proposed
foundations for structural elements above behind the walls so that the
foundation loads do not impose an excessive surcharge on the walls

Design Review
Plans and specifications for the proposed construction should be forwarded to the
geotechnical engineer for review and written comment prior to construction This review
is needed to determine adherence to the earthwork and foundation recommendations
given herein If this review is not made the geotechnical engineer can not assume
responsibility for misinterpretation of the recommendations
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the geotechnical engineer be retained to provide
geotechnical engineering services during construction of the earthwork and foundation
phases of the work This is to observe compliance with the design concepts
specifications or recommendations and to allow design changes in the event that
subsurface conditions differ from that anticipated prior to start of construction The
recommendations given in this report are contingent upon such observations
Any import material required should be tested and approved by the geotechnical
engineer prior to hauling to the site
If actual exposed rock soil conditions encountered during construction are different
from those assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made
LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon
information obtained from field data points such as borings and observation wells
Variations of conditions between the field data points may occur and the nature and
extent of these variations may not become evident until construction If variations then
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2703 00 Page 12
appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the recommendations given in this
report
The locations of the field data points were determined by estimation from the
topographic survey map Elevations of the data points were approximately determined
by estimation from the same plan The physical location and elevation of the field data
points should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the boring points depict
the approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote a gradual
transition The strata lines shown on profiles or cross sections are based upon
interpolation between field data points and may not represent actual subsurface
conditions
Water level data from the data points were collected at the times given on the
graphic representations and or in the text of this report These data have been reviewed
and interpretations made in the formulation of this report However it must be noted that
fluctuation may occur due to variations in rainfall tides temperature and other factors
