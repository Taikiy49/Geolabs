PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration was to obtain an overview
of the surface and subsurface conditions at the project site The subsurface information
obtained was utilized to develop a soil rock data set for the formulation of geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of pavement light pole
foundations and other geotechnical aspects only To accomplish this we conducted an
exploration program consisting of the following tasks and work efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and
two operators to and from the project site
2 Drilling and sampling of five borings extending to depths of about 16 5 to
20 8 feet below the existing ground surface
3 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by a field
engineer from our firm
4 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field exploration
as an aid in classifying the materials encountered and evaluating their
engineering properties
5 Analyses of field and laboratory data for the formulation of geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertaining to the proposed asphaltic concrete
pavements and light pole foundations
6 Preparation of six copies of this letter report summarizing our work on the
project and presenting our findings and recommendations
7 Coordination of our overall work on the project by a project engineer from
our firm
8 Quality assurance of our work and client design team consultation by a
principal engineer from our firm
9 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing and clerical
support
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration and Logs of Borings are presented on

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located at Fort Shafter in the Moanalua area on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The project site is located on the vicinity of Wisser Road and
Macomb Road The location of the project site is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The project site is triangular in shape At the time of our exploration the project site
was paved with asphaltic concrete and served as a parking lot It is bounded by
Wisser Road to the north Building B 525 to the west and a ditch to the southeast Trees
were observed at the northeastern corner and along the southern edge of the parking lot
Based on our observations and the topographic map provided the site gently slopes
downward from northeast to southwest with existing ground surface elevations ranging
from about 92 to 103 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
Based on the field observation the current pavements have experienced some
weathering and raveling In general block cracking was observed on the majority of the
parking lot and minor to severe alligator cracking appeared on some localized areas
Detailed descriptions of the pavement conditions observed are presented on Plate 3
Pavement Survey Plan
FIELD EXPLORATION
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling five borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 extending to depths of about 16 5 to 20 8 feet
below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers The approximate locations of the
borings drilled at the site are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Boring Log Legend Plate 4 Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings
Plates 5 through 9
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths

LABORATORY TESTING
Selected soil samples from the field exploration were tested in the laboratory with
an emphasis on evaluating the moisture density plasticity and strength characteristics
The following laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples obtained from the
field exploration
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on one selected soil
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentations of the test results
are provided on Plate 10
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of in situ soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils
The test results are presented on Plates 11 through 13
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered fill consisting of
medium to stiff silty clay extending to depths up to 16 feet below the existing ground
surface Alternating medium stiff to hard silts and clays and highly weathered tuff were
encountered below the fill extending to the maximum depths drilled of 16 5 to 20 8 feet
below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field exploration
However groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall seasonal precipitation
surface water runoff and other factors

Pavement Design
Based on the field survey results and the pavement analysis we believe that the
existing pavements may be reconstructed or rehabilitated with asphalt overlay for the
Eastside Parking Lot Building B 525 at Fort Shafter project Engineering
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this letter report pertaining to
these two construction options
The procedures described in Chapter 8 of the Pavement Design for Roads
Streets Walks and Open Storage Areas prepared by the Departments of the Army and
the Air Force dated June 1992 were referred to in determining the new pavement sections
We have made our preliminary pavement designs assuming the pavement subgrade soil
will consist of the clayey near surface soils encountered during our field exploration with a
CBR value of 5 The laboratory CBR tests were performed on samples remolded at
moistures close to the optimum moisture content This resulted in high swells and very
low CBR values ranging between 1 5 and 2 8 However it is our opinion that the in situ
subgrade soil is presently at a higher content and can provide a higher CBR resistance In
addition we have assumed that the loading on the pavements will consist of light vehicular
traffic as the project site serves as a parking lot If site grading exposes soil other than
those assumed additional tests should be performed to confirm or revise the
recommended pavement sections for actual field conditions
Pavement Reconstruction Design
Reconstructed pavement is recommended for areas with severe alligator cracking
G J K and N as shown on the Pavement Survey Plan Based on the above
assumptions we recommend the following pavement section be used for
reconstructed pavement areas
4 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
10 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness over Compacted Moist Subgrade
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density The
aggregate base course should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction

Asphalt Overlay Design
Based on the subsurface information obtained from our exploration the existing
pavement consists of 4 inches of asphaltic concrete over aggregate base course
materials with thickness ranging from 8 to 26 inches Based on our logs of borings
a pavement section consisting of 4 inch asphaltic concrete and 12 inch base
course was assumed in our engineering analyses
Based on the same assumptions used in the pavement reconstruction design and
the assumed existing pavement section we recommend the existing asphaltic
pavement be cold planed for a depth of 2 inches and repaved We noted that in
Boring No 5 base course was not encountered below the surface asphalt concrete
layer We recommend that this area be exposed to verify the presence or absence
of the base course layer A minimum of 6 inches of base course should be present
It should be noted that the recommended pavement section and overlay
thickness assumes that good drainage will be provided for areas adjacent to the
pavements To reduce the potential for infiltration of water into the pavement structural
section the pavement surface and the subgrade should be sloped and drainage gradients
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures
This is critical to the long term performance of the pavement structure considering the
nature of the on site soils Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during
or after construction Ponding of water adjacent to the edge of the pavement structural
section will allow infiltration and accumulation of subsurface water which will likely soften
the subgrade soils and cause premature failure of the pavement structure Extensive
landscaping should be avoided adjacent to the pavement areas otherwise we recommend
that a subdrain system be constructed to collect the excess water from landscaping
irrigation and to reduce the potential for migration of landscape water into the pavement
section

Light Pole Shaft Foundations
We understand that the light poles will be subjected to ground line overturning
moment and lateral forces at the foundation level Based on the loading conditions
provided and the subsurface conditions encountered we recommend that the light
poles be supported by shaft foundations
Based on the preliminary design of the light pole foundations 24 inch diameter
drilled shafts were considered in our analyses The major considerations in our
foundation analyses included the lateral loads and ground line overturning
moments induced in the shaft foundations
In general lateral load resistance for shafts is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of the
shaft and induced moment in the shaft Based on a maximum horizontal deflection
of inch at the top of the shaft subjected to ground line overturning moments and
lateral forces we evaluated the required shaft lengths using the computer software
program LPILE Plus for Windows This program is a microcomputer adaptation of
a finite difference laterally loaded pile program originally developed at the

Light Pole Construction Considerations
The performance of the shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures As a result we recommend
that a representative from Geolabs be present to observe the installation of the
drilled shafts during construction In our opinion the following may have a
significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the shaft foundations
The load carrying capacity of shafts depends to a large extent on the contact
between the shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper construction
techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling or
excavating the shaft holes and in placing concrete in the holes The bottom of
shafts should be cleaned of loose or disturbed material prior to constructing the
foundation The bottom should also be fairly level The concrete should be poured
neat against the excavation sidewalls Backfill against the shaft foundations should
not be allowed
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch slump range should be used
to provide close contact between the shafts and the surrounding soils The
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce segregation of the
aggregates from the concrete mix and to displace disturbed material from the
bottom of the drilled shaft

Soft loose and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated

Utility Trench
We envision that new electrical duct lines will be required to service the proposed
new lighting system In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or
S4C is recommended under the duct lines Free draining granular materials such as
clean sand or S4C should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 6 inches
above the duct lines to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the
compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is
critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper backfill material and placement procedures could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the duct lines
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site soils or imported select
granular fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 6 inch level loose lifts
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of
the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
corresponding to the maximum dry density
