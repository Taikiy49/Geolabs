INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Fort Shafter Building T 200 in the district of Honolulu on the island
of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Building T 200 is an old stone masonry building currently used by the Army
Corp of Engineers A complex pattern of cracks has formed in all four walls of the
building as well as in concrete pavements adjacent to the building Cracks over 1 inch
wide were observed and some of the cracks have been repaired with mortar Since
continued use of the structure is planned evaluation of the observed distress to the
structure was initiated
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration has been to characterize subsurface conditions
in the area of Building T 200 and to develop preliminary findings regarding possible
causes for the apparent distress to the structure Our services were perform in general
accordance with our proposal dated Our exploration program
consisted of the following tasks and work efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and operators
to and from the project site

SITE DESCRIPTION
Building 200 is located on the west side of Kahauiki Stream The building
appears to be situated within the alluvial plain of the stream An embankment to the
east of the building suggests that the eastern portion of the building pad was formed
by placement of fill within the stream valley The western portion of the building is
recessed about 1 to 2 feet below the adjacent ground surface indicating that the
western portion of the building pad may have been formed by cutting Detailed
topographic mapping of the Building T 200 site is not available
We understand that Building T 200 is over 50 years old Building T 200 is
constructed with stone masonry walls and based on visual estimates approximately 16
inches thick We understand that the original structure was rectangular with dimensions
of roughly 43 feet by 118 feet and it appears that subsequently two small additions
were built on the north side front of the structure The western two thirds of the
building has a concrete slab floor while the eastern third has a raised wood floor We
do not know if the raised wood floor is part of the original building design and
construction
Records or plans showing construction details of the building foundation are
apparently not available Considering the age of the building and standard construction
practices of that time period we would expect that the building probably has a relatively
shallow spread footing foundation The wall footing if any could be constructed of
concrete or rock masonry
As noted above a complex pattern of cracks has formed in all four walls of the
building as well as in concrete pavements adjacent to the building The most severe
cracks were observed at the junction between the main building and the two additions
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3177 00 A Page 4
on the north side of the building Cracks at this junction were over 1 inch wide near the
roof line and taper smaller toward the foundation More than one repair effort to patch
these cracks with mortar appears to have been performed
Numerous smaller cracks were observed in the four walls of the main structure
Cracks were observed at the corners of windows and door and in many orientations
The cracks appear to have opened subsequent to previous patching efforts Along the
south wall a long horizontal crack was noted about 3 feet above the adjacent ground
surface
The interior slab is covered with carpeting which precluded observation of the
slab conditions No gross unevenness of the interior flooring was noted during our site
visit However we do not know if repairs had been made to the slab We also are not
sure if the eastern raised wood floor was installed subsequent to excessive slab
settlement at the eastern section
The concrete side wall along the west side of the building was observed to be
severely cracked and exhibited signs of heaving Surface drainage in this area
appeared to be poor which may have caused localized ponding of water during heavy
storms
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
Regional geologic maps indicate that the site is underlain by recent and older
alluvium associated with Kahauiki Stream The alluvium has been deposited within the
eroded stream course by flowing water The alluvium overlies basaltic volcanic rock
of the Koolau Volcanic Series

Soil Conditions
Subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling four borings
adjacent to Building T 200 to depths of about 20 feet at the approximate iocations
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Detailed iogs of borings are presented in Appendix
A Our laboratory testing program and test results are provided in Appendix B The
conditions encountered in each of the borings are briefly summarized as follows
Boring B 1 About 7 feet of fill was encountered consisting of brown clayey silt
with rock fragments The fill was dry and varied from medium stiff to hard Laboratory
tests indicate that this fill has moderate expansion potential Underlying the fill
alluvium was encountered to a depth of about 15 feet consisting predominately of dark
brown and gray brown clayey silt The alluvium varied from dry to moist and stiff to
very hard Basalt rock was encountered at a depth of 15 feet The basalt encountered
was moderately weathered and hard
Boring B 2 About 8 feet of fill was encountered consisting of red brown and
gray brown clayey silt and sandy clay with rock fragments The fill was dry and varied
from stiff to hard Laboratory tests indicate that this fill has moderate to high expansion
potential Underlying the fill alluvium was encountered to a depth of about 18 feet
consisting predominately of dark brown clayey silt with some zone containing rock
fragments The alluvium was dry to moist and very stiff to very hard Basalt rock was
encountered at a depth of 18 feet The basalt encountered was moderately to highly
weathered and hard
Boring B 3 About 2 feet of fill was encountered consisting of red brown and
light brown silty sand and gravel The fill was damp and stiff Underlying the fill
alluvium was encountered to a depth of about 19 feet consisting predominately of
brown clayey silt and sandy silt with some zone containing rock fragments The

alluvium was damp and ranged from medium stiff at about 5 feet deep to very hard at
about 18 feet deep
Boring B 4 About 2 feet of fill was encountered consisting of red brown clayey

was encountered to a depth of about 20 feet From about 2 to 4 feet deep the

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration and engineering analyses we believe that
foundation soil conditions may have caused the distress observed at Building T 200
This may be due to settlement of fill and subsurface soils and shrink swell of expansive
foundation soils
Fill material in thickness of 7 feet with relatively low blow counts were
encountered in Boring B 1 It is our opinion that the relatively thick fill under the
eastern portions of the building may not have been adequately compacted and the fill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3177 00 A Page 7
material may have experienced settlement under the loads induced by the building
and or water infiltration The western portion of building would experience less
settiement because of thin fill layer
Our laboratory test results indicate that subsurface soils at the project site have
moderate to high shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the moisture
content Smaller cracks in the walls of the building may be the result of areal
settlement and or shrink swell of expansive foundation soils Highly expansive clayey
soils encountered in Boring B 4 and heaving of the sidewalk on the west side of the
building lead us to believe that the observed movements could likely have resulted from
the shrink swell of expansive soils particularly on the western portion of the building
We believe that the cracks and openings in Building T 200 are caused by a
combination of both differential settlement and expansion shrinkage of subsurface soil
Wide tapering cracks observed at the junction between the main structure and the
additions on the front of the building appear to be the result of different settlement rates
between the additions and the main building Furthermore we believe that continued
opening of previously repaired cracks as well as repeated repair of some cracks
indicate that continued distress to the structure is likely to occur in the future It is our
opinion at this moment that reconstruction of the existing foundation system would be
very costly and impractical Therefore we recommend that the existing cracks be
patched and an additional investigation be carried out The additional investigation
program as outlined below will be used to monitor the possible future distress of the
structure i e the pattern of the cracks the rate cracks develop and to evaluate
possible future remedial measures such as underpinning and compaction grouting

Additional Investigation
Additional investigation would provide information needed to evaluate the
feasibility of remedial measures and could also provide additional data to support
preliminary findings regarding the type of movement that appears to be occurring
Additional exploration and monitoring that should be considered are as follows
1 Examine Existing Foundation Test pits could be excavated to expose the
existing foundation and determine the foundation type depth of embed
ment and the nature of the soils directly under the foundation At least
one test pit should be excavated to expose the foundation of the main
building and at least one test pit should expose the foundation for the
additions at the front of the building
2 Floor Level Survey A floor level survey of the building could provided
information regarding the pattern of settlement or heave For example if
the floor level survey shows a general downward slope of the floor to the
east differential settlement of the existing fill would be indicated
3 Level Survey The elevation of selected points on the exterior of the
structure could be compared with a stable bench mark This kind of
survey could determine where settlement versus heaving type movements
may be occurring over a period of time and the settlement rate of the
foundation soils
Remedial Measures
Initially the remedial measure recommended is to patch the existing cracks and
monitor the settlement heave on a periodic basis as recommended above This would
allow additional information relating to the settlement and or heaving to be obtained to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3177 00 A Page 9
further evaluate other remedial measures The feasibility of other remedial measures
depend in a large part on the existing foundation The remedial measures of the entire
foundation system will be considered only if the additional investigation program
indicate that the foundation soils have almost completed consolidation and or expan
sion shrinkage
Underpinning of the walls of the structure may be initiated to transfer the
structural loads to a more competent bearing stratum If the existing foundation
consists of rock masonry use of an underpinning system would likely require
construction of a reinforced concrete beam under the existing foundation Construction
of an underpinning and beam system would be very costly and difficult to construct
Compaction grouting could be an effective alternative to mitigate settlement of
the existing fill however it is our opinion that no improvement of expansive soil
conditions would be achieved effectively at this moment The structural integrity of the
existing foundation would also be a concern using this grouting technique since uneven
uplift forces would be exerted on the footings Compaction grouting is also expected
to be a very costly remedial measure
Another alternative would be to construct new footings inside the building
structure to transfer the roof loads to the new footings In this alternative the rock
masonry walls will then be non bearing walls
It appears that the rock masonry walls sustained the most distress Therefore
an alternative consisting of shoring the roof and demolishing the walls followed by
reconstruction of new footings and walls may be considered This alternative would be
very costly to implement

Therefore we recommend that these remedial measures be evaluated after the
additional investigation has completed Considering the age and type of construction
of the existing structure it is possible that the remedial measures presented may be
determined to be impractical or not cost effective
LIMITATIONS
The recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon information
obtained from field data points such as borings drilled outside the structure Variations
of conditions between the field data points and inside of the building area may occur
and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until remedial
construction is undertaken If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to
reevaluate the recommendations provided in this report
The locations of the field data points were estimated by taping from the existing
building The physical location of the field data points should be considered accurate
only to the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the field data points
depict the approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote a
gradual transition The strata lines shown on profiles or cross section are based upon
interpolation between field data points and may not represent actual subsurface
conditions under the building footprint
Groundwater was not encountered during our field exploration however it must
be noted that fluctuation in water level may occur due to variations in rainfall
temperature and other factors

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration Alaboratory testing program was developed to aid in determining pertinent
engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials sampled
Moisture Content and Dry Density Determinations are used as an aid to soil
classification and to aid in evaluating certain properties such as relative saturation The
moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight of the tested sample
and the dry density in pounds per cubic foot are shown at the appropriate sample
depths on the Logs of Borings
A Proctor ASTM D 1557 91 test was performed on a composite sample of the
existing fill for comparison with the dry density test results of the existing fill The
results of this test are summarized on Plate B 1
Three expansion tests were performed to evaluate the swelling potential of soils
under structural loads Relatively undisturbed samples in a 2 4 inch diameter by 1 inch
high rings were tested The samples were air dried overnight then saturated for 24
hours under a surcharge load of 55 p s f These test results are summarized on Plate
B 2
One Atterberg Limits test was performed to determine the plasticity of the fine
grained portion of the soils The Liquid Limit and Plasticity Index of the materials are
used to classify the soil under the Unified Soil Classification System The results of this
test are shown on Plate B 3
