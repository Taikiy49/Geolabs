INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed replacement of the foundations supporting an existing flow
tube structure located at the Kaneohe Wastewater Treatment Plant at Kaneohe on the
island of Oahu Hawaii The general location of the project site and its vicinity is shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1

geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from analyses of the data obtained
during the field exploration These recommendations are intended for design input and
may not be appropriate to form the basis for preparation of construction cost estimates
or for construction bidding purposes
This report confirms and supplements the information and recommendations
presented in our design memorandum dated and the addendum thereto

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to replace a 30 foot long deteriorated section of the foundations
supporting a portion of an aging 42 inch diameter flow tube structure located to the south
of the existing Effluent Pump Station at the Kaneohe Wastewater Treatment Plant
Our field observation indicated that the affected portion of the aging flow tube is
elevated above the existing ground on three 3 concrete cradles The bolts originally
fastening the anchor straps which secured the tube to the cradles have pulled out of the
concrete on the west sides of the cradles due to the excessive settlement of the
foundations It appears that the existing pile foundations supporting the cradles and the
base slabs had subsided substantially over a long period of time causing the cradles to
settle

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Our field observation at the subject site indicated that the existing pile foundations
supporting the cradles and the base slabs may have subsided substantially over a long
period of time causing the cradles to settle and the bolts fastening the anchor straps to
pull out from the concrete at the west side of the cradles
Currently we believe the main reason for the failure of the existing pile foundation
is that the piles used to support the foundation were not driven down to sufficient depth
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2914 00 Page 7
or sufficiently embedded in the underlying boulder layer We are unable to confirm our
belief because the pile driving records for the existing pile foundations are not available
for our review
Based on our previous experience with the surrounding site area it appears that
the highly compressible lagoonal deposits encountered below the surficial fill layer could
still be undergoing the process of consolidation under the influence of the existing high
fill It appears that the downdrag effect thus transmitted to the piles may have initiated
the current unknown pile length foundation system to settle
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our current
exploration to obtain additional information on the consolidation characteristics of the
subsoils under the proposed flow tube foundation replacement area Based on the
consolidation test results it appears that the soft lagoonal deposits are still in a process
of consolidation under the influence of the existing fill
Foundations
Based on the above discussion it is our opinion that a new foundation system
should be installed to replace the existing one which is no longer adequate to provide
foundation support for the existing flow tube structure The new pile system should
extend through the thick soft compressible deposits and be driven down to an end
bearing layer to support both the downdrag loading and the proposed vertical pile

Pile Capacities
Based on our pile foundation analysis we believe that 12 inch square precast
prestressed concrete piles may be used The piles should be driven through the
soft lagoonal clays to end bearing into the underlying boulder layer
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2914 00 Page 8
The piles may be designed for a total allowable vertical capacity of 60 tons per pile
However due to the on going consolidation of the soft subsoils downdrag loads
of as much as 30 tons per pile are estimated These loads should be deducted
from the total allowable pile capacity Therefore a net allowable vertical capacity
of up to 30 tons per pile may be used for the pile foundation design
An allowable lateral pile capacity of up to five 5 kips per pile may be used in the
design Since predrilling is recommended as stated under the section Predrilling
the predrilled holes should be properly backfilled with dry sand after the pile driving
process in order to develop the above recommended lateral pile capacity
Pile Lengths
For initial design and construction cost estimating purposes a pile length of about
75 feet may be used The actual production pile lengths should be determined
after the completion of a test pile program
However the limited number of piles to be installed for the new foundation system
for the cited project may preclude the feasibility of a test pile program for the
proposed pile foundation construction Therefore it is our opinion that a longer
pile length of about 85 feet be used should the test pile program be not included
in the scope of the proposed construction contract
Pile Driving Criteria
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a minimum rated
energy of about 24 000 foot pounds The hammer should be equipped with an
energy level control so that driving within the loose deposits may be performed
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2914 00 Page 9
at a reduced energy level to minimize the development of tensile stresses within
the piles which could cause damage cracking of the piles
Additional pile driving criteria are as follows
1 The driving process should be continuous without interruption for each pile
A pile may be rejected or its design capacity modified when the driving
process is interrupted for more than four 4 hours
2 During easy driving when the pile tip is still within the loose sandy soil the
hammer stroke should be carefully controlled with reduced energy levels so
that excessive tensile stresses are not induced in the pile
3 The pile driving may be terminated when the blow counts steadily increase
with depth and reach six 6 blows per inch for the last three inches or
when aggregated blow counts reach seventy two 72 blows for the last
foot

design assumptions and to develop an economical design for the proposed pile
foundation system replacement to support the flow tube
The test pile program should be undertaken to satisfy the following objectives
1 To examine the driveability of piles with the use of the selected hammer

A number of splice cans should be made available at the site during the test pile

Settlement Monitoring Program
It was originally proposed to evaluate the existing conditions along the entire length
of the flow tube structure The scope of work for this current investigation was reduced

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of two
2 borings to depths of 86 0 and 100 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted equipment
The material encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer who continuously monitored the drilling
operations These classifications were further reviewed by visual inspection and testing
in the laboratory Al soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered are presented
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a standard penetration sampler or 3 O D Modified California sampler with a
140 pound hammer free falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the
last 12 inches are shown on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
In soft cohesive soils samples were obtained using a thin walled tube sampler
Shelby Tube The sampler was hydraulically pushed The gauge pressure required
to advance the sampler is shown on the Boring Logs at the appropriate depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a five foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting
any drilling

run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as RQD 50
