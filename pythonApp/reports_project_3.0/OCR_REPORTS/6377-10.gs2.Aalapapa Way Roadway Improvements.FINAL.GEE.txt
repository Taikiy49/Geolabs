SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our exploratory borings generally encountered a surface fill layer about 6 feet thick
underlain by very stiff colluvial deposit to 9 to 13 feet below the existing ground surface
Below the colluvial deposit a very stiff to hard alluvial deposit was encountered extending
to the maximum explored depth of 21 5 feet The fill layer consisted of asphaltic concrete
about 2 inches thick loose to medium dense sandy gravel stiff to very stiff clay and clayey
silt The colluvial and alluvial deposits were comprised of clay and silty clay with some
gravel We did not encountered groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration
Roadway distress consisting of longitudinal cracks at the pavement surface near
the top of the slope area was observed along Aalapapa Way The roadway in the distress
area is being supported on the downslope side by guardrail posts with rail beams between
the posts acting as walers supporting the roadway Based on our observations it appears
that the existing guardrail and waler system is not adequate to support the lateral loading
from the roadway fill and vehicular traffic
A permanent engineered retaining wall system was recommended for the project
We understand that the use of soldier piles and plastic lumber facing wall is desired for the
replacement wall The replacement wall will also consist of a reinforced fill zone behind
the plastic lumber The reinforced fill material should consist of select granular fill
materials and Tensar Biaxial geogrid BX1200 or equivalent Based on our stability
analyses the reinforced fill zone should extend at least 8 feet behind the back of the
plastic lumber facing A minimum of five layers of geogrids at a maximum spacing of
10 inches should be used The geogrid should wrap around the granular fill material at the
plastic lumber facing for each lift and extend at least 24 inches beyond the face of the wall
The guardrail post and foundation should be designed for TL 1 vehicle impact
loading We understand that W8 x 28 galvanized steel beam is being considered for the
post We envision that the post will be installed within an excavated hole about 12 inches
in diameter In addition the area between the post and the sides of the hole will be filled
with cement grout Two downslope conditions were used in the analysis Areas 1 and 2
Area 1 extends from the existing CRM to about Sta No 0 95 and Area 2 extends from
Sta No 0 95 to the end of the new wall Based on the above a minimum beam length of
20 and 18 feet should be used for Areas 1 and 2 respectively The recommended length
includes both the top steel beam section and the bottom steel beam with cement grout
backfill section

Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Aalapapa Way Roadway Improvements project in the Lanikai area on
the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing field
observations and engineering analyses for the proposed project The recommendations
presented herein are intended for retaining wall and pavement design purposes only
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted
at the end of this report

Project Considerations
The project site is along Aalapapa Way in the Lanikai area on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii The Aalapapa Way roadway consists of an asphaltic concrete paved roadway
about 10 to 11 feet wide for two way traffic The project location and general vicinity are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 and the Site Plan Plate 2

private residence at 008 061 Numerous longitudinal cracks were observed

guardrail between the roadway and the adjacent grade is about 2 feet and increases up
to about 5 5 feet towards the end of the road
Based on our site observations we had previously recommended replacing the
existing guardrail and waler system with an engineered wall system such as a

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes beginning in the mid Pliocene Epoch The older
Waianae Volcano was the first to develop closely followed by the development of
Koolau Volcano For a period of time Oahu was comprised of two islands formed by
each of the volcanic centers The volcanoes continued to erupt and banked against the
slopes of each other with Waianae terminating its eruptive activity prior to the cessation
of Koolau The main shield building activities ceased approximately 3 5 to 2 5 million
years ago
The rocks of the Koolau Volcano are divided into the Kailua Volcanic Series and
the Koolau Volcanic Series The lava of the Kailua Volcanic Series has been altered by
hydrodynamic action of rising steam within the ancient Koolau caldera The lava of the
Koolau Volcanic Series lies outside the caldera
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of
lavas of the Kailua Volcanic Series In situ chemical weathering of the lava flows has
occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The weathering process has formed a mantle
of residual and saprolitic soils Since the project is located on a hillside colluvial
deposits are present at the project site In addition recent fills have been placed from
residential developments in the area

Subsurface Conditions
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway improvement area by
drilling and sampling two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 each extending to
a depth of about 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

colluvial deposit residual soils extending to the maximum drilled depth of 21 5 feet were
encountered

dense sandy gravel stiff to very stiff clay and clayey silt with some sand and gravel
The colluvial deposit was comprised of very stiff clay The residual soils consisted of
very stiff to hard silty clay with some gravel
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration It should be noted that groundwater levels may vary with seasonal rainfall
time of year and other factors

up to about 5 5 feet towards the end of the road Based on our observations it appears

Soldier Piles With Plastic Lumber Wall
The soldier piles and plastic lumber facing wall generally consists of metal
guardrail posts with plastic lumber facing attached between the posts Behind the
plastic lumber facing a reinforced fill zone consisting of compacted granular fill material
and geogrids will be installed The new pavement section will be constructed over the
reinforced fill
The reinforced fill material should consist of select granular fill materials with
maximum particle size of 3 inches compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Within 3 feet from the wall face the compaction requirement may be
reduced to 90 percent relative compaction The reinforced fill material should have an
angle of internal friction of at least 34 degrees when compacted to at least 95 percent
relative compaction In addition the reinforced fill material should have a minimum
CBR value of 25 We recommend using Tensar Biaxial geogrid BX1200 or equivalent
within the reinforced fill
The reinforced fill zone should extend from the plastic lumber facing to at least
8 feet behind the back of the plastic lumber facing Based on our analysis we
recommend using five layers of geogrid at a maximum vertical spacing of 10 inches for
the reinforced fill wall system In addition the geogrid should wrap around the granular
fill material at the plastic lumber facing for each fill lift and extend at least 24 inches
beyond the face of the wall
Prior to constructing the reinforced fill zone the finished subgrade should be
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction To reduce the potential for
erosion of the slope in front of the new wall from surface runoff water installation of a

Guardrail Post Foundation
Guardrail posts will be installed at the face of the soldier piles with plastic lumber
wall system We understand that the guardrail post and foundation needs to be
designed for TL 1 vehicle impact loading The structural loading consists of a vertical
load of 4 5 kips and a horizontal load of 13 5 kips acting 32 inches above the ground
surface We understand that W8 x 28 galvanized steel beam are being considered to
be used for the post
A lateral load analysis was performed to determine the required depth of post
embedment and the induced structural loading in the post The LPILE program was
used in the lateral load analysis We envision that the steel guardrail post will be
installed within an excavated hole about 12 inches in diameter In addition the area
between the post and the sides of the hole will be filled with cement grout from the
bottom of the hole up to the bottom of the reinforced fill The cement grout should be
placed by tremie from the bottom of the hole
Two downslope conditions were used in the analysis The conditions consisted
of a downslope of one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V inclination from the end of the
existing CRM wall to about Sta No 0 95 designated as Area 1 and downslope of
4H 1V inclination from Sta No 0 95 to the south end of the wall designated as
Area 2 Areas 1 and 2 are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Based on our analysis
we recommend using a steel beam with minimum lengths of 20 and 18 feet for
Areas 1 and 2 respectively The recommended length includes both the top steel beam

Pavement Design
New pavements will be required for the roadway improvements The pavement
design methodology and parameters and the recommended pavement structural
section are provided below
Pavement Design Methodology and Parameters
The design pavement section was generally determined based on the
1993 AASHTO Guide Procedure for the Design of Pavement Structures The
pavement was designed for a pavement life of 30 years
We understand that the design traffic parameters for this project were not available
at the time this report was prepared Research was conducted to estimate the
truck traffic quantity and distribution Based on TheBus schedule no bus travels
along Aalapapa Way Refuse recyclables and green waste collection along
Aalapapa Way occurs twice a week on Tuesday and Friday Occasional additional
truck traffic along Aalapapa Way is anticipated Based on the above an average
monthly truck traffic of 14 trucks per direction was used in the analysis with
100 percent of the truck traffic consisting of 2 axle trucks

The clayey subgrade materials below the aggregate base course layer should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction prior to placing the new pavement section If wet soft subgrade soils
are encountered at the finished subgrade level the wet soft soils should be
removed to stiff natural ground and the excavation should be replaced with
compacted aggregate base course material

September 1986 The aggregate base course material should be compacted to a

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement surface
should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface
water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding
should not be allowed during or after construction Development of good shoulder
drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage structures is
strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or
premature failure of the pavements

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the roadway improvement site by
drilling and sampling two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 each extending to
a depth of about 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface using a truck mounted drill
rig equipped with continuous flight augers The approximate boring locations are shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on the
Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Two Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on selected
in situ cohesive soil samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the
soils The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depth and graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B 2 and B 3
Four Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plates B 4
through B 7
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed
on a bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The sample was remolded to near the optimum moisture
content of the soils Compaction of the sample was performed by dropping a 10 pound
hammer from a height of 18 inches The sample was compacted in five equal layers with
each layer receiving 56 blows of energy The sample was soaked in water prior to the
penetration test The test results are presented on Plate B 8
