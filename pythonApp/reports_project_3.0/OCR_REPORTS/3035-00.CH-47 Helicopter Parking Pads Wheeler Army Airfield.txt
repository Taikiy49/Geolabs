INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed CH 47 Helicopter Parking Pads at Wheeler Army Airfield on
the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings resulting from our field exploration and
laboratory testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived
from our analyses based on the data obtained through the field exploration and laboratory
testing These recommendations are intended for the design input of pavements and may
not be appropriate to form the basis for construction cost estimates or for construction
bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to reconstruct sixteen 30 feet by 50 feet helicopter parking pads to
accommodate the parking and operation of the larger CH 47 helicopters in the future
The approximate locations of the proposed and existing parking pads are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
According to the information provided to our office by the engineering consultant
the CH 47 helicopter has a maximum gross weight of 44 000 pounds with wheel gear
tread of 10 feet and wheelbase of about 22 feet We understand that the anticipated
traffic loading of these proposed parking pads are up to 1 400 cycles of departures and
arrivals annually at each parking pad Asphalt concrete pavement areas are proposed
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3035 00 Page 3
around the parking pads In addition the CH 47 helicopters are expected to travel along
the asphalt concrete pavement areas We understand that the proposed parking pads
and pavement areas will remain close to the existing grades therefore we anticipate that
the site grading for this project will be minimal

The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration was to obtain an overview
of the subsurface conditions at the project site to develop an idealized soil data set for
the formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the evaluation
of the existing pavements and the design of new pavements for the CH 47 helicopters

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located at the Wheeler Army Airfield in the Wahiawa District on
the island of Oahu Hawaii The site is currently used for the parking of rotary wing
aircraft such as UH 1 and AH 1 helicopters During our field exploration we noted that
the existing parking apron consists of asphalt concrete pavements with concrete parking
pads of approximately 20 feet by 20 feet in plan dimensions Cracks were noted at most
of the parking pads
The project site occupies an area of approximately 560 feet by 1180 feet The
project site is bounded by an approximately four horizontal to one vertical 4H 1V
grassed slope extending up to Santos Dumont Avenue to the north and by the existing
airfield runway to the south To the east it is bounded by Parking Taxiway A and to
the west it is bounded by a grassy area In general the project site is relatively flat and
slopes gently from the north to the south with vertical relief of less than about ten feet

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four 4 borings to
depths of 6 to 11 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations
of the four 4 borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our exploration generally encountered a five inch 5 to six inch 6 layer of fill
material consisting of crushed basalt gravel underlying the existing helicopter parking
pads which consist generally of five 5 to six inch 6 thick concrete One of our
borings drilled at the parking apron area indicates that the parking apron may be
underlain by 1 inches of asphalt concrete pavement over approximately six inches 6
of crushed basalt gravel fill In general below the crushed basalt gravel fill a thin layer
of very stiff to hard clayey silt with some gravel appeared to overlie saprolite soil which
extends to depths of about 8 to 11 feet below the existing ground
Groundwater was encountered only in Boring No 1 at a depth of approximately
six feet 6 below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration
Groundwater was not encountered in the remaining three 3 borings that extend beyond
six feet 6 below the existing ground The groundwater elevation measurement in Boring
No 1 was much higher than anticipated for the geologic and hydrologic conditions at the
site and in its vicinity It is our opinion that the water level encountered was probably due
to transient perched water condition and therefore does not reflect the static
groundwater conditions
Detailed descriptions of the subsurface materials encountered are presented on
the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 4 of Appendix A Laboratory tests were performed
on selected samples obtained from our field exploration A summary of the results of
laboratory tests are presented on Plates B 1 and B 2 of Appendix B

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
On the basis of our geotechnical exploration the existing concrete parking pads
at the project site consist of up to six inches 6 of concrete overlying crushed basait
gravel fill of approximately five 5 to six 6 inches thick Steel reinforcements were
noted in the concrete core samples obtained The parking apron area adjacent to the
existing parking pads consists of about 1 inches of asphalt concrete pavement overlying
about six inches 6 of crushed basalt gravel fill based on one boring drilled in this area
Cracks were noted on the concrete parking pads during our field exploration
Based on our exploration and laboratory test results our evaluation indicates that
the existing concrete parking pads and the asphalt parking apron area would not be able
to accommodate the anticipated traffic loadings from the CH 47 helicopters without
causing distress to the existing pavements As a result new pavements or overlays
above the existing pavements will be necessary to accommodate the anticipated traffic
loadings from the CH 47 helicopters
For construction of new pavements we recommend that new flexible pavements
consisting of four inches 4 of asphalt concrete over eight inches 8 of base course be
constructed to replace the existing pavements Alternatively rigid pavements consisting
of eight inches 8 of Portland Cement Concrete over six inches 6 of base course may
be used A combination of flexible and rigid pavements may also be considered
If removal of the existing pavements and construction of new pavements are not
desired overlay of the existing pavements may be considered Because of the relatively
small areas of the existing concrete parking pads asphalt concrete overlay should be
considered If asphalt concrete overlay is selected special attention should be given to

Design Aircraft
It is our understanding that the new pavements will be subjected primarily to the
loadings of CH 47 helicopters Based on the information provided to our office the
CH 47 helicopter has a maximum gross weight of 44 000 pounds with a tread of 10 feet
and a wheelbase of about 22 feet The helicopter is anticipated to travel along the
adjacent parking aprons onto the parking pads with estimated arrivals and departures of
up to 1 400 trips per year

Design Subgrade California Bearing Ratio CBR
Laboratory CBR tests were performed in accordance with ASTM D 1883 test
method on bulk samples obtained from the subgrade soils of the existing concrete
parking pads during our field exploration The test results indicated CBR values between
12 and 21 which are summarized on Plate B 1 of Appendix B Graphic presentations of
the individual CBR tests are presented on Plates B 3 through B 5 The lowest CBR test
value of 12 was selected for use in our asphalt concrete pavement analysis to account
for some localized soil variability
From the approximate relationship between CBR and modulus of subgrade
reaction Figure 1 of MIL April 1987 a CBR value of 12 correlates
approximately to a modulus of subgrade reaction of 210 pounds per cubic inch pci
Considering the contribution from a well compacted base course an effective modulus
of subgrade reaction of 250 pci was used at top of the base course for design of Portland
Cement Concrete pavement

traversed the existing concrete parking pads At some parking pads the cracks extended
throughout the entire length of the concrete parking pads However significant cracks
were not observed on the existing asphalt concrete pavement parking aprons
We understand that the CH 47 helicopters would travel along the parking aprons
and onto the new parking pads Therefore the entire parking areas including the parking
aprons would have to be designed for the anticipated loading from the departures and
arrivals of the CH 47 helicopter

Base and Subgrade Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared All existing surface pavements debris and unsuitable materials
should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce possible contamination
of the excavated materials The excavated soil below the pavements may be used as fill
material for the pavement subgrade provided it meets the requirements of pavement
subgrade soils
Based on our laboratory tests the subgrade soil was found to have a minimum
design CBR of 12 If fill material for the pavement subgrade is needed they should meet
the minimum CBR of 12
The rigid and flexible pavement sections planned for this project will consist of
either Portland Cement Concrete or asphalt concrete pavements overlying aggregate
base course materials The aggregate base course material should conform to the
requirements of base course as specified in Section 6 of the Navy Design Manual DM
21 3 August 1978 and is presented in Appendix C The aggregate base course should

by ASTM D 1557 test procedures
Aggregate base course should be placed in thin lifts not exceeding eight inches

scarified to a minimum depth of six inches 6 moisture conditioned to above optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent of its maximum dry density
Any loose or soft spots encountered should be over excavated to expose firm subsoils

The scarified crushed basalt gravel should not be mixed or compacted together with the

Pavement Joints
Joints in concrete pavements should be provided to reduce potential curling and
warping stresses in the pavement caused by temperature and moisture gradients across
the pavement slabs to reduce and control cracking due to volume changes in the
concrete to reduce damage to immovable structures and to facilitate construction
revision Joints Reinforcements and Mooring Eyes in Concrete Pavement should be
referred to for joint details and construction requirements
Joint sealants should be used to provide a seal to reduce potential infiltration of
water and to provide a compressible joint Joint sealing compounds used should be jet
fuel resistant and blast resistant and should follow the recommendations of Federal
Specification SS S 200 Type M Two Compound Cold Applied Sealing Compound
Pavement Overlay
As previously indicated pavement overlays may be considered if removal of the
existing pavements are not desirable In general we believe that asphalt concrete
overlays would be more cost effective and more practical when compared to concrete
overlays
The minimum concrete overlay thickness generally varies from six 6 to eight
inches 8 In some concrete overlays such as bonded concrete overlay joints in the
overlay must be constructed such that the expansion joints match with existing expansion
joints and contraction joints match with the existing contraction joints In addition all
cracks greater than inch in width should be filled prior to placing the overlay The
smaller cracks should be filled with hot liquid crack filler while the larger cracks be filled
with asphalt hot mix or cold patch Due to the minimum concrete overlay thickness and
GEOLABS HAWAII

cost effective nor practical

inches 4 Prior to asphalt concrete overlay the existing asphalt concrete surface

Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in evaluating pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in evaluating certain properties such
as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight
of the tested sample is shown at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of the soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining
to the performance or suitability of a soil
California Bearing Ratio ASTM D 1883 tests were performed on selected samples
as an aid to evaluating their pavement support characteristics The individual test results
are presented on Plates B 3 through B 5 The results of these tests are also summarized
on Plate B 1
Splitting Tensile ASTM C 496 90 tests were performed on selected core samples
of the existing Portland Cement Concrete PCC Pavement The results of these tests are
summarized on Plate B 2
Soil Compaction ASTM D 1557 78 tests were performed on bulk sample of the
subgrade of the pavement to evaluate the degree of compaction of the existing subgrade
material The results of the compaction tests are shown on Plate B 1
