2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island As the
volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked
against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the Schofield
Plateau
As the main shield building on the Island of Oahu drew to a close during the
Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of continental
glaciation As the sea levels fluctuated fringing coral algal reefs began to grow on the
seaward margins of the island After long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcanic Shield volcanic activity returned
with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations The series are
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked the Schofield Plateau generating
sediments which were transported to the coast In the project vicinity and further south
these alluvial sediments were interbedded with marine sediments coral algal reef
formations and volcanic deposits of the Honolulu Volcanic Series to form a sedimentary
wedge ranging in thickness from zero meters in the area of the Interstate Route H 1
Highway to over 300 meters at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves
as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern
Oahu Deposition of these sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through
the present

2 4 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic activity
therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during volcanic
eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground movement of
magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of Hawaii
experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so small that only
sensitive instruments can detect them However some of the earthquakes are strong
enough to be felt and a few earthquakes cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the
Island of Hawaii Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent are
related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking
diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has
experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 M5 however
earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii
Hawaii California

greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have occurred in

Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of Some houses on the

3 1 Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the structural loads
anticipated for the project we recommend utilizing shallow foundations consisting of
spread and or continuous footings to support the proposed building structures An
allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used to design the
foundations bearing on the recompacted near surface soils or new compacted fills The
bearing pressure provided above is for supporting dead plus live loads and may be
increased by up to 50 percent for supporting transient loads such as those caused by
wind or seismic forces
In general the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 460 mm
below the lowest adjacent finished grade Footings adjacent to planned retaining walls
or other below grade structures should be embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging
the retaining wall foundations or below grade structure Foundations next to utility
trenches or easements should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or the footing should extend
to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and
to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
If the footings are designed and constructed in accordance with the
recommendations presented herein we estimate total settlements of footings to be on
the order of about 25 mm or less with differential settlements between adjacent footings
supported on similar materials to be on the order of about 13 mm
Due to the potential for presence of cavities as commonly encountered in
coralline deposits we recommend implementing a program of probing and grouting of
the foundation locations during construction Probing and grouting requirements are

3 3 Slab On Grade
Based on the anticipated design finished floor elevation of the building structures
the building slabs may be underlain by the existing soils or new compacted fills placed
to raise the existing ground to the finished subgrades
Laboratory test results indicate the surface clayey fill soils exhibit slight to low
swelling potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations Therefore we recommend
scarifying the clayey subgrade soils if exposed at the subgrade level to a minimum
depth of about 200 millimeters moisture conditioning to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture and recompacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
The slab subgrades should be kept moist prior to the final placement of concrete
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks Saturation and subsequent yielding
of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill
Where hard coral formation is exposed and scarification of the subgrades is not
practical the subgrade soils should be proof rolled with a minimum 9 metric ton
vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes to help detect and collapse
near surface cavities and or voids

weakly cemented coralline detritus deposit We believe the cut slopes planned may
be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or
flatter Permanent fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or
flatter This assumes that the fill slopes will be constructed using the on site
materials
In addition fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be benched The fill
slope face should be finished to a relatively smooth and well compacted surface
The fill slope face should be free of voids which would allow erosion and migration
of fines to occur In addition slope planting or other means of slope protection
should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for significant
erosion of the finished slopes

3 7 Utility Trenches
We envision new utility line connections will be required to serve the proposed
new construction In general good construction practices should be utilized for the
installation and backfilling of trenches for the new utilities at the site The contractor
should determine the method and equipment to be used for trench excavation subject
to practical limits and safety considerations In addition the excavations should comply
with the applicable local safety requirements The contractor should be responsible for
trench shoring design and installation
In general we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 150 millimeters
of free draining gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation for support under the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation also should be
used for the initial trench backfill up to about 300 millimeters above the pipes or
300 millimeters above the groundwater level to provide adequate support around the
pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation
of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of
the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 300 millimeters above the
pipes or groundwater level to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist
of approved on site soils The backfill should be placed in maximum 250 mm level loose
lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction to
reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are
below pavement areas the upper 1 meter of the trench backfill below the pavement
grade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
One 25 mm Ring Swell test was performed on a remolded sample to evaluate
the swelling potential of the near surface clayey soils The test results are summarized
on Plate B 1
Three sets of and minimum resistivity ASTM G57 tests were
performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration to evaluate the
corrosivity of the in situ soils to buried metallic substructures The test results are
summarized on Plate B 2
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths and
graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B 3
Three Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B 4
One Direct Shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested The direct shear test
results are presented on Plate B 5
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 6 and B 7
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 8 and B 9

APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
The percolation characteristics of the on site soils were evaluated by performing
field percolation tests at two test locations designated as P 1 and P 2 extending to
depths of 2 7 and 2 2 meters below the existing ground surface The percolation tests
were performed in general accordance with the State of Hawaii Department of Health s
Administrative Rules Chapter 2 Wastewater Systems and Chapter 10 of the
Ten State s Standards to evaluate the permeability of the subsurface soils for storm
water disposal The field percolation test locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
A total of four field percolation tests designated as T 1 through T 4 were
conducted at various depths ranging from about 1 4 to 2 7 meters below the existing
ground surface at the two percolation test locations The tests were performed in a
100 mm diameter borehole Approximately 25 millimeters of gravel was placed at the
bottom of the borehole to protect the bottom of the hole from scouring and sediments
During testing the hole was carefully filled with water to a depth of about
150 millimeters above the gravel and refilled as necessary The time intervals and water
drops were then recorded to provide data upon which the calculation of the percolation
test was based The test results are presented on Plates C 1 through C 4
