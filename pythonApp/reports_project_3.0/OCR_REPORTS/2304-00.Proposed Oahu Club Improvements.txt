INTRODUCTION
This report represents the results of our geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the preliminary design of the site grading
foundations and pavements of the proposed Oahu Club Improvements in Hawaii
The general location of the site is shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1 Our work was performed in general
accordance with our proposal dated March 30 1989
This report summarizes our findings and recommendations
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the
subsurface conditions at the project site to develop a generalized
soils rock data set for the formulation of geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertinent to the design and construction of the proposed
improvements
The scope of our work included
1 The mobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and operators to and
from the project site
2 The drilling and sampling of six 6 borings to depths ranging
between 6 3 and 25 0 feet below the existing ground surface and
the drilling of six 6 shallow hand auger probes at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
3 Logging of the borings and hand auger probes general site
reconnaissance and coordination of the field work by a Field
Engineer Geologist from our firm

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The project site is located on the southeastern end of the Koolau
Range which is the eroded remnant of a basaltic shield volcano After the
cessation of eruptive activity the Koolau dome became deeply eroded
During the Pleistocene epoch sea levels throughout the world
fluctuated in response to the advances and retreats of the great
continental glaciers As the glaciers advanced and spread less water was
available to fill the ocean basins and sea levels fell below the present
level As the glaciers melted and retreated more water was available and
sea levels rose to stands above the present These fluctuations resulted
in changes in the local erosional and depositional base levels
Our exploration encountered what appear to be sediments which were
deposited in response to the fluctuating sea levels
During recent years as the Hawaii Kai area was developed the area
was graded Previously the site had been a portion of Kuapa Pond a
shallow estuary at the confluence of Hahaione Kamilonui and Kamiloiki
Valleys This area has been reclaimed and filled to create developable
land

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
It is our opinion that the proposed project is feasible from a
geotechnical engineering standpoint provided that our recommendations are
followed Our exploration encountered soft subsoils which could result in
ground and or foundation settlements unless special design considerations
and or construction methodologies are implemented on the project
Site Grading
The details of the proposed site grading were not available at the
time this report was prepared However it is anticipated that site
grading consisting of up to as much as twelve 12 feet of fill placement
within the existing pond area will be necessary to provide a level pad for
the construction of the proposed clubhouse building parking and law
area
The following recommendations are specific to the anticipated general
site grading
1 Prior to the commencement of any earthwork all areas within the
grading limits should be cleared and grubbed of all vegetation
demolition debris rubbish and deleterious materials The
resulting grub and spoil material should be disposed of properly
off site Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during the
clearing operations should be over excavated to firm ground and
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted
engineered fill to the proposed building subgrade level
2 Cut and fill slopes of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V may
be utilized for the design of the project site grading In order
to achieve properly compacted slope faces fill slopes should be
constructed by over filling and cutting back to the design grade

Foundation Support
For the design of foundations bearing on compacted fill and or as per
above over excavation of soft soils and replacement with compacted
fills procedures an allowable bearing capacity of 2 000 p s f may be
utilized This value may be increased by one third when temporary wind or
seismic loads are considered
Foundations should be embedded a minimum of eighteen 18 inches below
the lowest adjacent outside grade
Settlements
Estimated settlements for foundations bearing on compacted fill are on
the order of one inch total and one half inch differential
Slabs On Grade
It is anticipated that the lower floor s of the proposed structure
will be concrete slabs on grade The slabs and their cushions may be
supported by the compacted fill A 6 inch layer of 3B Fine crushed rock
should be provided below the slabs as cushion fill The slab subgrade
should be kept moist prior to concrete placement
In order to prevent future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage
to floor coverings an impervious moisture membrane is recommended below
the slabs

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based
in part upon information obtained from field data points such as
borings probes test pits and or observation wells Variations of
conditions between the field data points may occur and the nature and
extent of these variations may not become evident until construction If
variations then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the
recommendations given in this report
The location of the field data points were approximately determined
by taping from reference points shown on the Topographic Survey by
Towill Shigeoka and Associates Inc Elevations of the data points were
approximately determined by interpolation and estimation from the same
survey The physical location and elevation of the field data points
should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method
used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the
field data points depict the approximate boundaries between soil types
and as such may denote a gradual transition The strata lines shown on
profiles or cross sections are based upon interpolation between field
data points and may not represent actual subsurface conditions
Water level data from the field data points was collected at the
times given on the graphic representations and or in the text of this
report This data has been reviewed and interpretations made in the
formulation of this report However it must be noted that fluctuation
may occur due to variations in rainfall tides temperature and other
factors
