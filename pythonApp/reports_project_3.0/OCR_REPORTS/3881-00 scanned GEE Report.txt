INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Kurisu Residence at 1925 Judd Hillside Road in the
Punahou Manoa area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of ine project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located at 1925 Judd Hillside Road in the Punahou Manoa area
of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The subject parcel is approximately square in
shape with a total land area of about 49 285 square feet
It is preposed to demolish the existing building and swimming pool and to develop
the site with the construction of a two story main dwelling a detached single story ohana
structure and a new swimming pool The new buildings will be of concrete and masonry
construction with steel framing and concrete slabs on grade Based on the information
provided the finish floor elevations of the main dwelling and the ohana structure will be at
approximately 261 5 and 270 feet Mean Sea Level MSL respectively Detailed

prepared In addition a concrete access driveway is also planned for the project
At the time of our field exploration the existing structures at the site had been
demolished Based on the current design concept we anticipate that only minor site
grading work will be required to achieve the design finish grades for the proposed
construction Special attention was given to the conditions of the near surface materials

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built up during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene
Epoch by the activities of two basaltic shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau Koolau is
the younger of the two and reached the end of its main shield building phase about
2 million years ago
Koolau was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from northwest to southeast
trending rift zones These lava flows are interbedded pahoehoe and a a flows with
pahoehoe being the dominant form After the cessation of the main shield building phase
of Koolau Volcano there was a long period of erosion The project site is situated in
Manoa Valley which is an amphitheater headed valley deeply eroded into the Koolau
shield
During the Pleistocene Epoch there was a renewal of volcanic activity with the
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series Along the crest of the ridge on the
westerly side of Manoa Valley there are the Tantalus Roundtop and Sugarloaf vents
When these vents were active one or more lava flows from these vents spilled down the
westerly wall of the valley and moved across the valley floor These lava ponded and
filled the lower portion of the valley The ponding allowed the lava to densify as it cooled
forming dense massive rock
Other eruptive events of the Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled these lava flows
with pyroclastic material particularly sand sized cinder The project site is underlain by
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3881 00 Page 5
cinder deposits from the Roundtop vent Subsequent fluvial action has transported minor
amounts of alluvial soils on the site During the development of the project site
man made fills were placed to grade the site
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located at 1925 Judd Hillside Road in the Punahou Manoa area
of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The subject parcel is approximately square in
shape with a total land area of about 49 285 square feet
The project site was previously occupied by a two story L shaped wood residential
structure and an outdoor swimming pool These structures have been recently
demolished and the project site was vacant at the time of our field exploration Based on
our field observations and hand probing with a steel rod the project site is generally
blanketed with loose cinder sands at the existing ground surface
Based on our field observations and the topographic survey maps provided the
project site is bounded by existing CRM walls to the north which provide grade separation
between the subject parcel and the adjacent Judd Hillside Road The northern portion of
the project site is relatively level and could be accessed through an existing driveway
from the northeastern corner of the site Except for the northern portion of the project
site the remaining area of the project site is gently sloping from the northwest to the
southeast with existing ground elevations ranging from about 290 to 230 feet MSL

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the subject site generally encountered medium dense to
dense cinder sands and very stiff to hard sandy silts extending to the maximum depth
explored of 30 2 feet below the existing ground surface Based on the relatively dense
sandy subsoil conditions encountered in our borings we believe that a shallow foundation
system consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the recompacted
near surface soils and or new engineered fills may be used to support the proposed
building We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3881 00 Page 7
Geolabs Hawaii to verify that the exposed footing subgrade conditions are similar to those
encountered in our field exploration Soft and or loose soils encountered at the footing
subgrade elevation should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials with
the resulting over excavation backfilled with compacted select granular fill material or the
footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying competent material
Our laboratory tests indicate that the in situ sandy silts exhibit moderately high
swelling potential when subjected to moisture fluctuation To reduce the potential for
structural distress to the future slabs on grade resulting from the potential swelling of the
in situ sandy silts we recommend that concrete slabs on grade be supported on at least
12 inches of non expansive select granular fill materials Because of the presence of
loose surficial fills and overcast we recommend that all building and pavement slabs be
proof rolled with a vibratory drum roller prior to placement of the non expansive capping
fill layer Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report
Building Foundations
Based on the current design concept and the generally competent subsoil
conditions encountered at the site we believe that shallow spread and or continuous
footings may be used for support of the proposed residential structures An allowable
bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for the
design of foundations bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new engineered fills
needed to achieve the final design grades This bearing value is for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 and 24 inches below
the lowest adjacent finish grades for the one and two story structures respectively
Footings located along sloping grounds should be constructed in stepped footings For
footings constructed near top of slopes or on sloping ground the footings should be
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3881 00 Page 8
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back distance of 8 feet
measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face Footings located
adjacent to planned retaining walls should be embedded deep enough to avoid
surcharging the retaining wall foundations or the retaining walls should be designed to
resist the additional structural loads
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement
For footings bearing on the in situ soils or new engineered fills the bottom of the
footing excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test
procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations
they should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The
over excavation should be backfilled with select granular material compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down
to the underlying competent material
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3881 00 Page 9
coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted in situ
soils or new engineered fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f
This assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted The passive pressure
should be reduced for foundations located on slopes Unless covered by pavements or
slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new engineered fills as
recommended herein to be less than one inch total with differential settlements on the
order of one half inch
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the ground floor level
of the proposed structures Our laboratory tests indicate that the sandy silts encountered
in our borings exhibit moderately high potential for swelling when subjected to moisture
fluctuations To reduce the potential for structural distress of the slabs on grade resulting
from the swelling of the sandy silt soils we recommend that the subgrade soils
underneath the slab areas be properly prepared and capped with at least 12 inches of
non expansive select granular fill material below the slab cushion The capping fill should
also extend beyond the building perimeter a minimum of 24 inches
The non expansive select capping fill should be placed as soon as possible after
the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in
the Site Preparation section of this report The prepared capping fill and the underlying
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to the final placement of concrete
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3881 00 Page 10
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that
the soils be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks Saturation and subsequent yielding
of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill at
no additional cost to the Owner
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer consisting of
free draining materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be
placed below the slab to provide more uniform support of the slab To reduce the
potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on
top of the cushion layer Two inches of moist fine sand should be placed above the
plastic membrane to provide protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab
concrete Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered
since they can better mask minor slab cracking The thickened edges of slab adjacent
to any unpaved area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent
finish grade Areas adjacent to the slab should be backfilled tightly against the slab with
non expansive impervious soil and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce
the potential for water ponding around the foundations
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that concrete walkways be supported on 12 inches of granular soils
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Contraction joints should be
provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at
right angle intersections

Garage Slab and Concrete Driveway
For slabs on grade that will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the garage
slabs and the driveway a 6 inch layer of base course over the 12 inches of
non expansive select granular capping fill is recommended for more uniform support
The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction We recommend that the concrete slabs be designed
by a structural engineer for the intended loads and should be reinforced as a structural
slab to accommodate some amount of differential movements For design of structural
slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per cubic inch p c i may be
used for the compacted base course Provisions should be made for proper load
transfer across the slab joints which are subjected to vehicular traffic The subgrade
soils beneath the garage and driveway slabs should be prepared in accordance with
the recommendations presented in the Site Preparation section of this report
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere on
the site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate
landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be

used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate
support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus
reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining
material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result
in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site granular soils or
select fill material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not
less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground
subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet of the trench
backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction

Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive landscape watering
near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations
should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into
the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain surface run off away from the slab and footing
foundations
