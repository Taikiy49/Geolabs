SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
New pavements will be constructed as part of the Farrington Highway Ramp
Widening project Based on our field exploration we anticipate that the majority of the
pavement subgrade soils will consist of medium dense to dense gravel and sand and
hard sandy silts with a minimum R value of about 62 Typically new HDOT pavements
include a permeable base layer to facilitate drainage and increase the pavement life
However we anticipate the existing roadway does not have a drainage system to collect
and discharge the infiltrated water in the permeable base layer Therefore incorporating a
permeable base layer in the new pavement would not be feasible Based on the above
we have considered only pavement structural sections without a permeable base layer in
our analyses
Because the existing on ramp is constructed of flexible pavements and lengthy
roadway closures would be required to construct rigid pavements we recommend using a
flexible pavement section for this project Based on the traffic data provided and the design
R value of the subgrade soils we recommend using the following structural pavement
section for the new pavements for the Farrington Highway Ramp Widening project
4 5 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
9 0 Inch Asphalt Concrete Base
6 0 Inch Aggregate Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
19 5 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement surface
should be sloped adequately and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry
surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific
recommendations for the design of the project

SECTION 1 GENERAL
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Kapolei Interchange Complex Phase 2 Farrington Highway
Widening project located in Kapolei Oahu Hawaii The project location and general
site vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of
pavements traffic signal foundations slabs on grade and earthwork only The findings
and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of
this report
1 1
Project Considerations
As part of the Kapolei Interchange Complex Phase 2 project it is proposed to
widen the existing Kamehameha Highway on ramp at the intersection of Farrington
Highway and Kalaeloa Boulevard Improvements include
Widen on ramp to provide two lanes which will be tapered to one lane
before the highway entrance
Shrink the existing island to provide a shoulder and add sidewalk
Signalize intersection

SECTION 2 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the
island The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the
island As volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows from Koolau
Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau known as
Schofield Plateau
Following the extrusion of lavas in the early Pleistocene Epoch the island
underwent a long cycle of erosion and weathering forming the prominent ridgelines and
summits as we know today During the erosion period the Island of Oahu began to
slowly subside by more than 1 200 feet in elevation resulting in the drowning and
sedimentation of the valleys and the formation of the steep Koolau Pali Coral reefs
continued to grow in the surrounding shallow waters of the island
From the mid to late Pleistocene Epoch the sea level rose and fell repeatedly in
response to global glaciation and the availability of surface waters to sustain the
oceans The various sea level elevations and their representative deposits are known
as stands and include from the oldest to the youngest the Kahuku Kahipa Kaena
Laie Waialae Waipio and Waimanalo stands Geologic deposits associated with the
various sea level stands including marine sediments and coral reefs were deposited
and subsequently altered or removed by later sea level fluctuation Therefore
depositional records reflecting the changes in sea level and the occurrence of emerged
coral reef deposits are often incomplete
The project site is situated on the Ewa Plain to the southeast of the Waianae
Mountains The Ewa Plain is a gently sloping alluvial plain formed by the deposition of
alluvial clays and silts derived from the weathering of basalt rock further up slope The
alluvial deposits accumulated and the materials interbedded with marine sediments and

Existing Site Conditions
The project site is in the general vicinity of the intersection between Farrington
Highway and Kalaeloa Boulevard in the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
Elevations along the roadway surface range from about 92 to 100 feet Mean Sea
Level MSL However the roadway is elevated up to about 17 feet above the
surrounding area
2 2
Subsurface Conditions
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about 22 5
to 25 feet below the existing ground surface In addition a bulk sample of the
near surface soils designated as Bulk 1 was obtained to evaluate the pavement
support characteristics of the near surface soils The approximate boring and bulk
sample locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our borings generally encountered surface fills overlying alluvium volcanic
cinder clinker and basalt rock formation The surface fills were encountered to depths
of about 15 feet below existing grades and consisted of medium dense to very dense
gravel and sand and hard sandy silt The surface fills were underlain by alluvium
consisting of very stiff to hard sandy silty clay and extended to depths of about 14 to 20
feet below existing grades In Borings 1 and 2 the alluvium was underlain by volcanic
cinder clinker consisting of very dense silty sand Soft to medium hard basalt rock
formation was encountered below 20 feet in Borings 2 and 3 and extended to the
maximum depth explored of about 25 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field exploration
However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to
rainfall time of year seasonal precipitation surface water runoff and other factors

Methodology of Pavement Design
Because the existing Farrington Highway on ramp is constructed of flexible
pavements and lengthy roadway closures would be required to construct rigid
pavements we recommend using a flexible pavement section for the widening project
Therefore only flexible pavement was considered in the pavement analyses for
this project The flexible pavement section presented in this report was generally
determined based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the revised Pavement
Design Manual dated March 2002 The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the
State of Hawaii Department of Transportation Highways Division Material Testing and
Research Branch The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem
Stabilometer method developed and used by the California Department of
Transportation Caltrans

Subgrade Preparation Below Pavement Section
We anticipate that the majority of the new roadway pavements will be
constructed at or near the existing grades or in a fill condition In general the area
within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed at the
on set of earthwork Vegetation debris deleterious material and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and properly disposed of off site or disposed of in a
designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose
stiff and or dense materials The resulting excavation should be backfilled with
aggregate subbase materials The excavated soft and or organic soils should be
properly disposed of off site

After clearing and grubbing the future pavement areas should be excavated
where necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the aggregate
subbase course layer The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of
at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with D1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend scarifying and re preparing the soils as recommended above Saturation
and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor
drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted fill Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit
prices for over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the
over excavation quantities

Fill Materials
In general the excavated on site materials with particles no larger than 3 inches
may be re used as a source of general fill material Imported fill materials if required
should consist of non expansive select granular material such as crushed coralline or
basaltic materials The materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with
particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The materials should have a
laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 22 or more and should have a
maximum swell of 1 percent or less Imported fill materials should be tested for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore special attention
should be given to the surface drainage of the pavements
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS

As mentioned previously an existing drainage system nor a drainage layer is
anticipated in the existing pavements at the site Additional infrastructure work such as
new drain lines and inlets would be necessary in order to incorporate a drainage layer
into the new pavements We believe this would not be feasible nor practical and that it
would result in a build up of subsurface water within the drainage layer if proper
drainage outlets are not provided The subsurface water likely would cause saturated
soil conditions which would cause the pavement subgrade soils to soften and result in
premature pavement problems
Therefore it is essential to slope the pavement surface adequately and to
maintain positive drainage gradients at the pavement surface such that surface water
may be carried off the pavement quickly for proper discharge Surface water ponding
should not be allowed on site during or after construction

Sidewalks and Gutters
We anticipate that concrete sidewalks and gutters may be required The
near surface soils encountered in the borings generally consist of gravel and sand and
sandy silt
The soil subgrades below the sidewalks and concrete gutters should be scarified
to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture and compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction
Control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the sidewalks
and with expansion joints at right angle intersections to reduce the potential for
substantial shrinkage cracks in the slabs

Utility Trenches
Based on our understanding of the project new utility lines will be installed In
general good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling
of the trenches for the proposed utilities The contractor should determine the method
and equipment to be used for excavation subject to practical limits and safety
considerations The excavation should comply with the applicable local state and
federal safety requirements The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring
design and installation Trench shoring and bracing also should conform to the
appropriate health and safety requirements
Granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open graded gravel ASTM C33
No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials
such as open graded gravel ASTM C33 No 67 Gradation should also be used for the
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support
around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort on the backfill thus reducing the
potential for pipe damage It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the
potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill material and placement could
result in backfill settlement and pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site soils maximum
particle size of 6 inches or general fill material The backfill should be placed in
maximum 8 inch level loose lifts moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
three exploratory borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending down to
depths of about 22 5 to 25 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled
with truck mounted drill rigs equipped with 4 inch O D solid stem augers and coring tools
In addition a bulk sample of the near surface soils designated as Bulk 1 was obtained to
evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the near surface soils The approximate
boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described
in the Soil Classification Log Key Plate A 0 2 Rock materials were described in general
accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock Log Legend
Plate B 0 3 The Rock Description System is based on the publication Suggested
Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses by the
International Society for Rock Mechanics March 1977 Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM D1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
O D standard penetration sampler using a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance
with ASTM D3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch O D modified
California sampler with the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The penetration
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the
specific sampler type used The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the
Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
Please note that the efficiencies of the safety hammers used for this project were
measured in May 2015 and the resulting energy transfer ratios are shown on the Logs of
Borings The soil consistencies and densities presented on the boring logs have been
adjusted based on the energy transfer ratio as appropriate

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D7263 determinations
were performed on selected soil and samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation
of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
One Atterberg Limit test ASTM D4318 was performed on a selected soil sample
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth and graphic
presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B 1
Three Sieve Analysis tests were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification The fines content of the samples tested percent particles passing the No
200 sieve are indicated on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B 2
Two Direct Shear tests ASTM D3080 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested The test results
are presented on Plates B 3 and B 4
One laboratory soaked California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D1883 was
performed on a bulk soil sample to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soils The test results are presented on Plate B 5
One laboratory Resistance R Value test ASTM D2844 was performed by
Ninyo Moore on a selected bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the
pavement support characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on
