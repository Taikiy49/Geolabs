performed for the proposed Kea au Pahoa Bypass Road project in the District of Puna

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes which include the following Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna Kea
which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801
and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the eastern flank of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano
which has been built up by basaltic lava flows These flows are of the Pleistocene Epoch
Ice Age and of the Recent Age and belong to the Ka u volcanic series The lava flows
were subsequently covered by a surface layer of palagonitized ash locally referred to as
Pahala Ash The volcanic ash soil at the project site differs from the Pahala Ash found in
other locations on the island of Hawaii in that the in situ moisture content is generally
lower
The lava formation at the site appears to be of pahoehoe flow which is
characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava These
cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes
are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like
structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow
cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The proposed Kea au Pahoa Bypass Road is located about 7 miles southeast
of Hilo in the vicinity of Kea au Town in the District of Puna on the island of Hawaii
The proposed alignment is bounded by Highway 130 Kea au Pahoa Road to the
southwest Highway 11 Volcano Road to the west and the 8 Mile Camp to the east
as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The alignment traverses both undeveloped and partially developed land which
exists as abandoned sugar cane fields and an existing macadamia nut orchard The
ground surface is generally composed of lava flows of the Mauna Loa Volcano located
at a distance to the southwest The centerline of the proposed alignment had been
surveyed and staked out in the field prior to our field exploration and was utilized to
locate the borings
A reconnaissance of the proposed roadway alignment indicates that the existing
ground surface is generally comprised of very moist to wet volcanic ash deposits from
about Station 0 00 to about Station 30 00 The volcanic ash deposits generally
consist of medium stiff to stiff clayey silt soils intermixed with some basalt sands and
gravel Depending on the topography the soil horizon was observed to exist as a
relatively thin surface cover on the order of about 1 to 3 feet below the existing ground
level In general areas of lower elevations relative to the surrounding terrain were
observed to contain a thicker soil deposit possibly due to erosion and deposition of
sediments At the time of our site reconnaissance the surface soils were observed to
be damp to very moist and yielded substantially to foot traffic indicating soft ground
conditions may exist when the subsoils are subjected to water infiltration

W O 3681 00 Page 7
According to the field surveyor a Caterpillar D 6 bulldozer or similar equipment
had been stuck in the soft wet soils during the clearing operations near Station 28 00
of the proposed bypass road alignment Based on our field observations at this
location some bulldozer ruts extending to a depth of about 3 to 4 feet below the
adjacent ground level were evident At the time of our field reconnaissance the
location was observed to be relatively dry and the surficial silty soils existed as friable
clumps of soil Since the location exists in a slight topographic depression where
thicker soil deposits might occur it is believed that the area may convey some seepage
flow of groundwater during periods of wet weather However it should be noted that
evidence of groundwater was not observed in any of the drilled borings at the time of
our field exploration Based on the field observations topographic low areas where
thicker soil deposits may exist should be treated as suspect areas for seepage of
groundwater and soft yielding soil conditions
The surficial volcanic ash deposits consisting of silty soils generally grade to
sandy basalt gravel with some silt at shallow depths This layer is believed to represent
the depositional surface of the basalt lava flows at the site The layer indicates that the
tops of the lava flows consist of severely fractured clinker material comprised of a loose
to dense mix of basalt fragments with some soil In general the upper clinker layer
grades to competent basalt rock at depths of about 5 to 8 feet below ground level
Some locations may not contain this surficial clinker layer and may encounter dense
basalt rock at relatively shallow depths just beneath the surface soil horizon
Our field reconnaissance indicates that the surficial volcanic ash deposits
become drier as the alignment progresses toward the south between about
Station 30 00 and Station 65 00 Beyond Station 65 00 the volcanic ash soil horizon
was no longer apparent and the existing ground generally consisted of clinker
materials overlying basalt rock formation at shallow depths

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration along the northern portion up to about
Station 30 00 of the proposed bypass road alignment a relatively thin about 1 to 3 feet
thick surficial deposit of volcanic ash soils consisting of medium stiff to stiff clayey silts
existing in a very moist to wet state was generally encountered It is our opinion that the
wet volcanic ash deposits encountered at the northern portion of the proposed alignment
should be removed prior to site filling and or pavement construction
We believe that the drier near surface volcanic ash deposit encountered between
about Station 30 00 and Station 65 00 may be left in place provided that the poor
pavement support characteristics of the volcanic ash soils are taken into consideration for
the design of the pavement structural section along this portion of the roadway alignment
In addition special attention should be given to proper surface drainage of the roadway
pavements to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the underlying volcanic ash
soils which may lose substantial strength when saturated
Based on the available road profile plans for the subject project earthwork for the
proposed roadway construction may involve excavations up to about 15 feet and
embankments on the order of up to about 20 feet Our field exploration indicates that the
excavated materials along the proposed alignment would range from volcanic ash soils to
dense clinker materials and hard basalt rock formation In general we believe that the
excavated materials excluding the soft and or wet volcanic ash soils encountered up to
about Station 30 00 may be re used as a source of fill materials for embankment
construction provided that the materials are properly processed and placed in accordance
with the recommendations presented in the Site Grading section of this report

Based on the existing terrain at the subject site and the road profile plans provided
we anticipate that site grading work for the proposed roadway construction will generally
consist of cuts and fills of less than about 5 to 10 feet in height However substantial cuts
and fills on the order of up to about 15 to 20 feet thick will be required in localized areas to
attain the design finished grades for the proposed roadway project Items of grading that
are addressed in the following subsections include 1 Site Preparation 2 Fills and
Backfills 3 Boulder Disposal 4 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
5 Excavation 6 Cut and Fill Slopes and 7 Drainage
Site grading operations should be observed by a _ representative of

those encountered in our field exploration
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site or in a
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3681 00 Page 12
designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated
materials Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below
any areas designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm
natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well
compacted engineered fill
Based on our field exploration we anticipate that a majority of the near surface
volcanic ash deposits encountered up to about Station 30 00 and in other
localized areas especially in the vicinity of the drainage features observed at the
subject site may consist of very moist to wet soils Therefore these wet volcanic
ash deposits should be completely removed and disposed of properly off site prior
to site filling and or pavement construction The drier volcanic ash deposits
encountered between about Station 30 00 and Station 65 00 may be left in place
provided that the design pavement structural section to be determined by the
State of Hawaii Department of Transportation takes into consideration the poor
pavement support characteristics of the volcanic ash soils
In areas where the drier volcanic ash soils are exposed and designated to receive
fill the subgrades should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
AASHTO T 180 ASTM D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content
corresponding to the maximum dry density Due to the generally thixotropic nature
of the near surface volcanic ash soils use of the vibratory action of the compactor
should be carefully controlled to avoid disturbing the volcanic ash soils In
addition moisture conditioning of the volcanic ash soils should be carefully
controlled to avoid over wetting of the soils It should be noted that the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3681 00 Page 13
near surface volcanic ash soils may be sensitive to the moisture content and
difficult to achieve the specified compaction Therefore additional compaction
effort with proper moisture control may be necessary to meet the specified
compaction criteria
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the subgrade
should be proof rolled and a woven reinforcing fabric such as Mirafi 500X or
equivalent should be placed on the subgrade prior to placement of fill material
Guide specifications for the woven reinforcing fabric are presented on Plate 3
Alternately the pumping areas may be over excavated to expose firm natural
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted
engineered fill Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the
subgrade we recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks
or the subgrade should be re scarified moisture conditioned and recompacted
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement
weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted engineered fill
In areas where the subgrade exposes clinker or dense basalt rock formation the
subgrade should be scarified to a depth of about 12 inches where possible and
proof rolled with a minimum 20 ton vibratory roller a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or
similar heavy equipment for a minimum of six passes to aid in detecting and
collapsing near surface cavities Any loose areas or cavities disclosed during
clearing and proof rolling operations should be backfilled with well compacted
engineered fill materials The proof rolling operation should also be performed at
the finish subgrades in cut areas

Fills and Backfills
Fill and backfill materials up to the bottom of the design pavement section should
consist of well graded materials less than 6 inches in largest dimension The soft
and or wet volcanic ash soils encountered should not be used as fill materials and
should be properly disposed of off site The drier volcanic ash soils encountered in
cut areas may be stockpiled and reused as a source of general fill material
provided that they are blended with the clinker materials and or basalt rock
fragments to provide a relatively well graded material prior to placement as fills
This serves to increase the workability of the volcanic ash soils and reduces the
potential for erosion The contractor should be made aware that additional time
and effort may be involved with the fill placement operations utilizing volcanic ash
soils
Excavated clinker materials may be used as a source of fill material or blended
with the drier volcanic ash soils and basalt rock fragments to provide a relatively
well graded fill material Cut materials generated from excavations into the
underlying basalt formation may be used as general fill or backfill materials
provided that they are screened of the over sized materials greater than 6 inches
in largest dimension and processed to meet the above gradation requirements
This requirement is to facilitate future utility trench excavations

Over sized rock fragments greater than 6 inches in dimension may be disposed
of off site used in deep fills or broken down to smaller sized materials and
incorporated into the fill material If the excavated materials do not contain
sufficient fines to produce a well graded material off site borrow or on site rock
crushing of large sized rock fragments or boulders should be considered to provide
the required gradation and particle size to develop a well graded material
On site materials consisting of boulders less than 5 feet in diameter may be used
as rock fill provided that the top of the rock fill is placed at least 3 feet below the
finished pavement subgrades In general placement of boulders in fills should be
in accordance with the procedures recommended in the Boulder Disposal
subsection of this report
If imported material is required the material should be non expansive granular
material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 6 inches in
largest dimension The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or
more and should have a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less Imported fill
materials should be tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to
delivery to the project site for its intended use
Boulder Disposal
Over sized materials such as cobbles greater than 6 inches in dimension and
boulders rock fragments larger than 12 inches in maximum size but less than
5 feet in largest dimension are anticipated from cuts into the basalt formation
These over sized materials may be used in general and rock fills provided that the
following recommendations are followed

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fills and backfills up to 3 feet below the finish grade of the pavement areas
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts
not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness and compacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction Fill select borrow and base course materials within 3 feet of
the finish grade of pavement areas should be compacted to at least 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with AASHTO T 180 ASTM D 1557 91 Optimum
moisture is the water content corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed to compact
the fills
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3681 00 Page 17
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
rocks or 5 feet whichever is less Placement should be by haul trucks traversing
previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the currently placed lift
Spreading of the rock fill should be by a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy
equipment to facilitate seating of the rock The rock fill should be watered heavily
during placement by water trucks traversing in front of the current rock lift face and
spraying water continuously during placement Compaction equipment such as a
Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or other similar compaction equipment providing suitable
energy to achieve the required compaction should be utilized The number of
passes to be made to achieve proper compaction should be determined at the
on set of site grading by the performance of a trial compaction test and by plate
bearing tests
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rocks boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the
number of passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of
compaction should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project
under the observation of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii On the basis of
this testing program the number of passes may then be used as the field criterion
for adequate compaction

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings it appears that
most of the proposed cut slopes would expose clinker materials and hard basalt
rock formation with some portions of the cut slopes exposing the volcanic ash
soils In general we believe that cut slopes composed of the in situ drier volcanic
ash soils or the underlying dense clinker materials may be designed with a slope
inclination of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter Where the cut slopes
expose basalt formation at the slope face the cut slope may be steepened to a
slope inclination of 1H 1V if desired Cavities that may be exposed on the cut
slope face should be backfilled and grouted We recommend that cut slopes
exposing the volcanic ash soils be immediately protected by appropriate slope
planting or other means to reduce the potential for erosion of the exposed soils
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the granular fills may be designed with a slope
inclination of or flatter Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should
be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill
against sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue
up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement

mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to
at least 95 percent relative compaction

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the proposed roadway alignment were explored
by drilling and sampling ten borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10 to depths of
about 15 to 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools The approximate locations of
the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through
A 10
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
O D standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a
140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as

APPENDIX B
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the near surface soils to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil
classification The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depth Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
One sieve analysis ASTM C 136 was performed on a selected sample to
determine the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on Plate B 2
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 3 and B 4
Two Modified Proctor tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk samples of the
near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content relationships The
test results are provided on Plates B 5 and B 6
