INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Saddle Road Well A Pump Control Building and Well Pump Pad project
located in the Hilo area on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings based on the field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the
proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of building
foundations headwall structures site grading pavements and utility lines only and are subject
to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These include the following Kohala which has been long extinct Mauna Kea
which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801
and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the northeastern flank of Mauna Loa which has been
built up by successive layers of basaltic lava flows These flows are of the Pliocene Epoch
and belong to the Ka u Volcanic Series The lava formation encountered at the project site
appears to be of pahoehoe flow which is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy
surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly
encountered in pahoehoe lavas These cavities were formed when the lava was still in a
molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such
as blisters and pockets Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling
flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance
longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent

Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
that concrete slabs on grade with thickened edge footings be used to support the generally
light structures such as the pump control building and well pump pad To provide for uniform
support of the slab we recommend that a 6 inch layer of compacted aggregate subbase
course be placed below the slabs and footings The 6 inch layer of aggregate subbase course
should extend laterally a minimum of 2 feet beyond the perimeter of the structures The 6 inch
layer of aggregate subbase course should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may
be used for the design of footings bearing on the compacted aggregate subbase course This
loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Foundations should be embedded a minimum of 8 inches below the lowest adjacent
finished grade Where foundations are located next to utility trenches and or easements they
should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom
edge of the utility trench or embedded as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement

level the volcanic ash or loose materials should be over excavated to expose the
underlying dense materials The over excavation should be backfilled with fill materials
with a maximum particle size of 3 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
The soils encountered at the slab and footing subgrade level below the aggregate
subbase course should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with

content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

course to be on the order of about 0 5 inches

Retaining Structures
We understand that some drain lines are planned for the project Therefore we

following general guidelines may be used for preliminary design of the headwall structures
Retaining Structure Foundations
In general we believe that retaining structure foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the Building Foundations
section of this report However the aggregate subbase course may be omitted
below the headwall structure footings The footings should have a minimum width
of 18 inches In addition foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded
a minimum depth of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade It should
be noted that the soils encountered at the footing subgrade level should be
moisture conditioned and compacted to provide a firm and unyielding subgrade For
sloping ground conditions the footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum
6 foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing
to the face of the slope Footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope
should be constructed in stepped footings

Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the
retaining structures discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an
alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may
be used instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product
should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the
retaining structure
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of granular fill materials
less than 3 inches in maximum dimension Unless covered by concrete slabs the
upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce
the potential for significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure
Dynamic Lateral Earth Pressures
Since the project site is located on the seismically active Island of Hawaii dynamic
lateral earth pressures will need to be considered in the design of retaining
structures Dynamic lateral earth pressures due to seismic loading
may be estimated by using 10H psf rectangular pressure distribution for level
backfill conditions where H is the height of the wall in feet For a 2H 1V sloping
backfill condition dynamic lateral earth pressures of 15H psf rectangular pressure
distribution should be used in design The dynamic lateral earth pressures
presented above are in addition to the static lateral earth pressures An
appropriately reduced factor of safety may be used when dynamic lateral earth
pressures are accounted for in the design of the retaining structure

Site Grading
Based on the existing topography we anticipate that minimal site grading work will be
required to achieve the design finished grades In general areas within the contract grading
limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed at the on set of earthwork Vegetation
debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly

over excavated to expose firm dense material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted fill
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation
should be properly backfilled with fill material placed in 8 inch loose lifts moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative

APPENDIX A

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to a depth of approximately 15 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
solid stem continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2 of Appendix A
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
