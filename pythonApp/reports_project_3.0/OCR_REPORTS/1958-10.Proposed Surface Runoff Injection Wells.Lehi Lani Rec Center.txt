This report presents the results of our preliminary percolation
testing for the design of surface runoff disposal wells in the lowest
portion of the proposed Lehi Lani Recreational Community development
site This project site is located at the base of Pupukea Bluffs on the

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed Lehi Lani Recreational Community development is a
planned recreational and residential development consisting of golf
courses and residential lots to be constructed on the top of Pupukea
Bluffs An access road will be constructed on the the face of the bluffs
to provide access to the community
It is anticipated that the proposed site improvements will result in
an increase in the amount of surface runoff generated within the project
site during rainfall events It is desired to maintain a status quo of or
to decrease the amount of runoff leaving the project boundary along
Kamehameha Highway To this end it is proposed to construct a series of
detention structures and or runoff disposal wells in the lower portion of
the site

Regional Geology
The island of Oahu is composed of two 2 shield volcanoes Waianae
and Koolau built up by the extrusion of basaltic lavas during the late
Pliocene and Pleistocene epoches
Koolau basalts are typically thin bedded primitive lavas with a large
percentage of pahoehoe flows Cavities or voids such as lava tubes
are frequently encountered Generally Aa flows which are
characterized by a clinkery surface with a dense inner core comprise the
remainder of the volcanic mass Generally Koolau basalts are noted for
having high permeabilities and are capable of transmitting large volumes
of water The voids and clinker strata are the major permeability
features
During the Pleistocene or Ice Age global sea levels rose and fell
in response to the great continental glaciers As the glaciers grew the
global sea level fell below the existing level and as the glaciers melted
and retreated the sea level rose to heights above the current stand of
the sea
The Pupukea Bluffs appear to be relict sea cliffs formed as the
result of marine erosion during the high stands of the sea ILocally the
basalt bench at the base of these cliffs have been mantled by alluvial
deposits and coral algal reef formations which were laid down during
higher stands of the sea The permeability of the coral algal formations
is also frequently very high The permeability of the alluvial materials
is usually very low and they often behave as confining layers

Subsurface Conditions
Based on our exploration the subsurface conditions at the project
site appear to be divided into three major sets In most of the area
explored the surface soils consist of stiff alluvial silty clays ranging
from about five to fifteen feet in thickness mantling coral algal reef
deposits hereinafter referred to as coral In the easternmost
portion of the site the alluvial clay soils are absent and in the
westernmost area they thicken and the coral deposits are absent
Underlying the alluvial soils throughout most of the site are
deposits of coral and related detrital deposits These deposits generally
range from ten to fifteen feet in thickness and overlie in situ basalt
formation
The basalt formation is generally a a lava flows consisting of
interbedded dense rock and clinker The weathering of the formation
ranges from decomposed basalt saprolites at the top to slightly
weathered and decreases with depth The basalt was encountered to the
maximum depths 23 0 to 48 5 feet drilled in each of the eight borings
Percolation Testing
Percolation testing was performed in the drilled borings by the
constant head method i e water is injected into the boring at a rate
which will maintain a constant water level in the hole The rate of
injection is limited either by the capacity of the hole to accept the
water or the maximum flow rate of the available water source The test is
continued until steady state conditions are attained i e until a
constant rate of injection will sustain a stable water level
The tests are set up to minimize the entrainment of air in the
injected fluid and to reduce the amount of turbulence in the borehole
This is accomplished by maintaining the feed point of the injected fluid
below the surface of the fluid in the borehole Since the tests used

for the transmission of clear fresh water through the subsurface materials
encountered and tested
The data obtained from these tests are reduced and analyzed for

confined aquifers as appropriate
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
In general our exploration and field permeability testing indicates
that there are permeable formations underlying most of the project site at
relatively shallow depths These formations are capable of accepting and
transmitting moderate amounts of clear fresh water
The calculated permeability values of the coral formation range from
1 000 to 12 000 gallons per day per square foot gpd ft and are higher
than those determined for the portion of the underlying basalt formation
which was tested 13 to ft However the thickness of the
coral limits the amount of water which may be transmitted through it
On the other hand the thickness of the basalt below the coral is by
comparison very great It is generally known that the basalt formation
becomes less weathered and more permeable with depth Published

Based on our exploration it is our opinion that a typical drywell
excavated at the site to a depth of about thirty feet below the existing
ground surface would penetrate an average of 10 feet of coral formation
and an average of 10 feet of basalt formation Using an average
permeability value of ft for the coral formation and an

average of for the basalt we estimate that a three foot
diameter drywell could accept about 850 gallons per minute This is based
on average values for clear fresh water and allows no factor of safety
Increasing the diameter to six feet would increase the capacity to about
900 gallons per minute However increasing the diameter of the drywells
will increase their storage capacity
Based on the boring data we do not recommend the construction or
installation of such drywells in the westernmost portion of the project
site in the vicinity of Boring 8 The thick alluvial clay soils
encountered and the absence of coral formation in this area preclude the
effective operation of drywells
We recommend that the walls of the drywells be lined with either
unmortared masonry or precast liners to reduce the potential of erosion or
sloughing of the sides of the drywells However the use of precast
liners may reduce the efficiency of the drywells
Based upon a review of published permeability data for Koolau Basalt
and the Underground Injection Control permit records for the nearby City
and County of Honolulu drainage wells it is our opinion that it may be
possible to construct higher capacity drywells by penetrating deeper into
the basalt formation below the project site However our current
exploration did not provide sufficient data for design of such wells In
addition it is not clear whether or not government agencies such as the
Hawaii State Department of Health and the City and County Board of Water
Supply would permit deep disposal of surface runoff water in this area
If higher capacities are needed we recommend that the current regulatory
positions be clarified and that additional exploration and testing be
performed for the design of such deeper wells

Maintenance and Replacement
Over long periods of time the capacity of injection wells
decreases This decrease in the case of drywells is generally the
result of clogging of the formation by suspended solids carried by the
fluid being injected To reduce formation clogging the injected water
should be as clear as possible such as by desilting in a detention
basin In addition the drywells should be periodically inspected and
cleaned of accumulated silt trash and other materials
Even with a regular maintenance program injection wells may fail
Provisions should be made to allow for the replacement of failed wells

changes in the event that subsurface conditions differ from that
anticipated prior to start of construction The recommendations given in
this report are contingent upon such observations
If actual exposed rock soil conditions encountered during
construction are different from those assumed or considered in this
report then appropriate modifications to the design should be made

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based
in part upon information obtained from field data points such as
borings probes and or observation wells Variations of conditions
between the field data points may occur and the nature and extent of
these variations may not become evident until construction Tf
variations then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the
recommendations given in this report
The location of the field data points were approximately determined
by taping from reference points shown on the Layout Plan prepared by
Engineering Concepts Incorporated dated July 1988 Elevations of the
data points were approximately determined by estimation from the same
plan The physical location and elevation of the field data points
should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method
used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the
field data points depict the approximate boundaries between soil types
and as such may denote a gradual transition The strata lines shown on
profiles or cross sections are based upon interpolation between field
data points and may not represent actual subsurface conditions
Water level data from the field data points was collected at the
times given on the graphic representations and or in the text of this
report This data has been reviewed and interpretations made in the
formulation of this report However it must be noted that fluctuation
may occur due to variations in rainfall tides temperature and other
factors
