The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and
Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age 4 million years ago and forms the bulk of the western one third of
the island The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age 5 million years ago and forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Waianae and Koolau shields volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations mainly at
the southeastern portion of the Island of Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
filled pre existing depressions and accumulated at higher elevations
In the steep mountainous regions of Hawaii and at the heads of deeply incised
valleys erosional processes are commonly dominated by detachment of soil and rock
materials from the steep valley walls and transportation down slope toward a valley axis
primarily by gravity as colluvial earth materials Once these materials reach the stream in
the central portion of a valley stream flow processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvial earth materials
The project site is on the southerly facing side slope of a tributary valley associated
with the larger adjacent Waikakalaua Gulch in the Ewa District of the Island of Oahu
Hawaii The tributary valley and gulch is incised within deeply weathered and decomposed
basaltic rock belonging to the Tertiary Age Koolau Volcanic Series Tkb
The near surface basaltic rock underlying the project site has been completely
weathered to saprolitic materials generally represented by stiff silty and clayey soils that
possess some remnant rock texture and embedded soft decomposed rock like fragments
Common in some saprolitic materials are erosional remnant hard rock boulders that are
embedded within the completely weathered matrix of saprolitic soil material Based on our
review of the regional surface mapping by the United States Department of Agriculture Soil
Conservation Service the land at the project site is classified as Helemano silty clay
HLMG comprising slopes ranging between about 30 to 90 percent in gradient

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Falling rocks are a special category of natural hazard within the large family of
gravitationally driven phenomena called landslides Rockfall events may involve various
modes of detachment and movement including detachment from the parent rock outcrop
individual boulder movement raveling of loose rock material from steep slope faces
toppling of rock outcrops and rock slides Rockfall is the fastest type of landslide and is
primarily caused by the loss of support and detachment of individual components of the
rock mass under the influence of gravity Complex interactions between the physical
parameters of both the rock and the slope may cause the falling rocks to move down the
slope in a high velocity seemingly random and erratic manner Rockfall is a natural and
on going process that occurs in steep terrain Rockfall source areas may be defined by
topographically high regions where the rock mass may contain discontinuities such as
open fractures undercuts or inclined bedding surfaces capable of producing sliding
action In addition source areas may include loose surface boulder deposits where
reactivation of movement occurs due to loss of support on steep slopes or the impartation
of an external force to initiate movement
Destabilization of a rock mass may occur by mechanical weathering root pry and
expansion contraction along rock joints chemical weathering decomposition of the rock
mineralogy and erosional undercutting loss of support Physical triggers for rockfall
events may include heavy precipitation high groundwater pressure decreased normal
rock friction extreme temperature fluctuations seismic shaking erosion by concentrated
surface runoff and prying and levering of tree roots within rock joints Human activities
such as excavation vibration traffic on the slope surface and development of
over steepened cut slopes may also trigger rockfall activity
