INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the new Wahiawa Fire Station in the Wahiawa area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses The
recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of foundations
slabs on grade site preparation and pavements only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project site is located at 640 California Avenue in the Wahiawa area on
the Island of Oahu Hawaii It is proposed to construct a new fire station to replace the existing
fire station The new fire station building will be built at the same location as the existing
building Based on the information provided the project site encompasses an area of
approximately 20 000 square feet as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The new fire station
will consist of a one story building of approximately 76 by 76 feet in plan dimension with
concrete masonry walls supporting a wood framed roof The new building will comprise of the
fire truck parking area rooms and quarters and other related facilities The existing carport
and fuel tank will be relocated to along the northern property line of the site A new fuel
dispenser will be constructed on the north side of the new building In addition a new
asphaltic concrete paved open courtyard with 11 parking stalls will be constructed to the rear
of the new fire station
Structural details of the new building were not available at the time this report was
prepared We anticipate that the finished floor of the new building will be near the existing
grades Therefore we envision that only minor site grading consisting of cuts and fills on the
order of about 1 foot or less would be needed to achieve the design finished grades

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the western
third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern two thirds
of the island Itis believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and its
eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in Central Oahu
Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against the
already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch The dips of the
lava beds generally range from 3 to 5 degrees The lava flows on the plateau have weathered
to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors of the soil The deep
narrow canyons such as Waikakalaua Kipapa and Kaukonahua are a result of this
weathering The brown and reddish brown soils generally consist of ancient alluvial deposits
overlying the weathered rock The new fire station project site is located near the center of the
Schofield Plateau
The project site is located in an area composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion
of the lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows
of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The
weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils which grade to saprolite with depth
In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical
weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades with depth to basaltic rock formation

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As discussed above our field exploration at the project site generally encountered
medium stiff silty clay fills underlain by residual soils consisting of very stiff to hard silty clays
Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered in our borings and the
anticipated light structural loads we believe that a shallow foundation system consisting of
spread and or continuous footings bearing on the recompacted on site soils may be used to
support the new fire station building
Our laboratory tests indicate that the in situ silty clay soils exhibit a slight potential for
shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations In order to reduce the
potential for structural distress to future slabs on grade resulting from the shrinking swelling
of the in situ silty clays we recommend that concrete slabs on grade be supported on at least
6 inches of non expansive select granular fill materials In addition the subgrade soils below
the non expansive select granular fill materials should be properly prepared and kept moist
prior to placement of the cushion fill and slab concrete Detailed discussion of these items and
our geotechnical engineering recommendations are presented in the following sections of this
report
Foundations
Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the site we
believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the
proposed building and related facilities An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 500 pounds
GEOLABS INC
W O 4317 00 Page 7
per square foot psf may be used for design of foundations bearing on the recompacted
on site soils This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should
be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility tench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
The bottom of the footing excavations should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
prior to placement of reinforcing steel Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Foundation subgrades
should not be allowed to dry before placing concrete If shrinkage cracks appear in the
foundation excavations the soil should be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks and
recompacted to provide a firm base prior to concrete placement
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations they
should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation
should either be backfilled with approved on site soils compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction or the bottom of footing may be extended down to the underlying
competent material
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate that the total foundation settlements under the anticipated
design loads to be on the order of less than 1 inch with differential settlements on the order of
about 0 5 inches
GEOLABS INC
W O 4317 00 Page 8
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 40
may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted on site soils Resistance due to passive
earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square
foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils around the footings are well
compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive pressure resistance in the
upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
We recommend that foundation excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the competency
of the bearing materials and embedment depths
Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the new fire station building
the gas tank and the fuel dispenser As previously indicated our laboratory tests indicate that
the near surface soils encountered at the site consisting of medium stiff silty clay exhibit a
slight expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations Therefore we recommend
fill and the subgrade should be properly prepared and kept moist prior to placement of
cushion fill and slab concrete
The non expansive select granular fill should be placed as soon as possible after the
subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the
Site Preparation section of this report The capping fill and the underlying subgrade soils
should be wetted and kept moist until final placement of concrete Where shrinkage cracks
are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils be thoroughly
moistened to close the cracks or the subgrade soils be prepared again Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage
may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill
GEOLABS INC
W O 4317 00 Page 9
In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer consisting of
free draining materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed
below the slab above the capping fill to provide more uniform support of the slab for the
building To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to
floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier is recommended on top of the cushion
layer Two inches of moist fine sand may be considered above the plastic membrane to
provide protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Provision of the
2 inches of fine sand above the vapor barrier will be left up to the discretion of the project
structural engineer
Concrete slabs for the fuel tank and fuel dispenser may be supported directly on the
6 inch thick select granular fill material The select granular fill materials should be compacted
to not less than 95 percent relative compaction The subgrade below the select granular fill
should be scarified a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction For design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 200 pounds per
square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted select granular fill
material
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at least
12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab should be
backfilled tightly against the slab with relatively impervious soils on site soils and graded to
divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the
foundations
Site Preparation
We envision that minimal site grading work will be required for the proposed project
construction In general we recommend that the following guidelines be used for the minor
site grading anticipated At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits
GEOLABS INC
W O 4317 00 Page 10
should be thoroughly cleared Vegetation debris rubbish asphalt concrete pavements
landscape lawn grass and other unsuitable materials within the grading limits should be
removed and disposed of properly off site
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive
fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or dense material and the
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The excavated soft and or
organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
Over excavation resulting from the clearing and demolition should be backfilled with
compacted general fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the
resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with general fill material placed in 6 to 8 inch
level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum 90 percent relative compaction Utilities to be
abandoned in place under the proposed structure should be backfilled by pumping lean
concrete under low pressure
Our laboratory tests indicate that the near surface clayey soils exhibit a slight
shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in moisture content To reduce the
potential for distress to the future structures resulting from swelling of the clayey soils we
recommend that the clayey subgrade soils be capped with a minimum of 6 inches of
non expansive select granular fill material below the building slab areas The non expansive
select granular fill should extend a minimum of 2 feet beyond the perimeter of the proposed
building structure
Prior to placement of the capping fill the subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth
of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Where shrinkage
cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soil be thoroughly
moistened to close the cracks or scarified and prepared again as recommended above
GEOLABS INC
W O 4317 00 Page 11
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and
poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted fill Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for
over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variation in the over excavation
quantities
The select granular fill recommended below slabs and imported fill materials should
consist of non expansive granular material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder
sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing
the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should
have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less Imported fill material should be tested for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended
use
Site preparation should be observed by a representative of Geolabs It is important that
a representative from our office observe the site preparation operations to evaluate whether
undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and scarification process and
whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our field
exploration
Pavement Design
Based on the information provided we understand that the driveway leading to the fire
station will be paved with concrete The driveway pavement will be subjected to the relatively
heavy fire truck traffic We understand that the open courtyard and parking areas in the rear
will be paved with asphaltic concrete In general we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the
asphaltic concrete pavements will consist primarily of pick up trucks and passenger vehicles
We have made our preliminary pavement designs assuming the pavement subgrade soils will
generally consist of the near surface clayey soils as encountered in the borings If site
GEOLABS INC

grading exposes soils other than those assumed additional tests should be performed to
confirm or revise the recommended pavement sections for actual field conditions Based on
the above assumptions we recommend that the following pavement sections be used for
preliminary design and budgeting purposes
Rigid Pavements areas subjected to the fire trucks
8 0 Inch Portland Cement Concrete 600 psi flexural strength
6 0 Inch Aggregate Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
14 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Moist Subgrade
Flexible Pavements for the driveway and parking areas
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
6 0 Inch Aggregate Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
14 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Moist Subgrade
Based on the results of our laboratory tests the near surface clayey soils exhibit a
slight expansion potential when subjected to changes in the moisture content Therefore we
recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and kept moist until placement of
the aggregate subbase The pavement subgrade should be scarified to a depth of at least
8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction Subgrades should be thoroughly moistened and kept moist
until covered by the aggregate subbase If shrinkage cracks appear on the subgrade soils
prior to placement of the aggregate subbase we recommend that the soil be thoroughly
moistened to close the cracks or scarified and prepared again as recommended above
Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials should meet the material
requirements in Section 703 06 and Section 703 17 respectively of the Hawaii Standard
Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 The aggregate base
course and aggregate subbase should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction CBR and field density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade
materials encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above section

Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines and utility connections may be required for the
proposed project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes for support Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also
be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes It is critical to use
this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches
of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill
could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils The backfill
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in about 8 inch
loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to
reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement
areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted
to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from the
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems be
installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation and
GEOLABS INC
W O 4317 00 Page 14
pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also
be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce
the potential for water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations
and slabs

fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall tides temperature and other factors

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling two
borings extending to a depth of about 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations ASTM D 2937 were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of this test
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected soil sample to
evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plate B 2
Two 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are presented on Plate B 3
