REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 70 Page 5
be of middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau known as Schofield
Plateau
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of this plateau generated
sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and to
the south these sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and
coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to
over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the
confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present
Agricultural development within the last 100 years and recent mass grading work have
brought the Ewa Villages area to its present form

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by very stiff
to hard silty clays extending to the maximum depth explored of about 11 5 feet below
the existing ground surface Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the project
site it is our opinion that a shallow foundation system may be used to support the
proposed residential structures However the near surface silty clay soils exhibit
moderate to high shrink swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil
moisture contents As a result we recommend that the expansive clayey soils be
capped with a minimum of 3 feet of non expansive select granular capping fill material
below all building slab areas prior to constructing the lightly loaded residential
structures and improvements The expansive clayey soils below the driveway areas
should be capped with a minimum of 2 feet of non expansive select granular fill
material
As an alternate the non expansive select granular capping fill below the building
slab areas may be reduced to a minimum of 2 feet provided that a 5 foot deep
perimeter cut off wall is incorporated around the house structure to reduce the potential
for water infiltration into the underlying foundation soils In addition special attention
should be given to the recommended procedures for subgrade soil preparation below
slabs and driveways in order to reduce the potential for shrink swell effects of the
near surface clayey soils

Shrinkage Cracks
Our field observations indicated that a substantial amount of shrinkage cracks is
present at the existing ground surface across the subject site In general shrinkage
cracks will start to develop in the ground due to the lack of maintenance of the soil
moisture content as the soils start to dry out under the sun and dry weather conditions
The development of shrinkage cracks in the ground was apparently intensified by the
prolonged dry climatic conditions in the Ewa area resulting in large fluctuations of the soil
moisture content Detailed discussions of the shrink swell behavior of the on site soils
and the mechanism of shrinkage crack development are presented below
Shrink Swell Behavior of On Site Soils
Based on the laboratory test results performed for the subject project the in situ
clayey soils generally have a high shrink swell potential when subjected to
fluctuations in the moisture content and may be classified as expansive soils
In addition it will lose substantial strength when saturated and will shrink
appreciably when subjected to drying resulting in shrinkage cracks In general the
mechanism of shrink swell in expansive soils is a complex phenomenon and is
influenced by a number of factors including soil characteristics and environmental
factors
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 70 Page 13
Expansive soil characteristics can generally be measured by engineering
properties such as soil plasticity and density to evaluate the shrink swell potential
of soils In general the soil plasticity reflects the capacity of the clay minerals in
the soil to retain large amounts of water while maintaining a coherent structure
Therefore a highly plastic soil such as those found at the subject site will have a
greater potential for volume change shrink and swell due to the loss or absorption
of moisture under cycles of drying and wetting In addition increased soil density
through compaction or natural depositional history such as desiccation generally
have greater shrink swell potential
Environmental factors comprise of the initial moisture content of the soil and
moisture variations over time The initial moisture content generally impact the
potential of a soil to absorb or expel water from the soil system In general a dry
soil will have a higher capacity to absorb water and swell while a wet soil will lose
water more readily upon exposure to dry conditions and will shrink more Moisture
variations can generally be caused by climate surface drainage vegetation
permeability etc
Shrinkage Crack Development Mechanism
We believe that the moisture content of the on site soils was kept moist and
relatively constant during the mass grading phase As a result the development of
large shrinkage cracks might not be evident during the site grading operations
Due to the high plasticity of the on site clayey soils shrinking of the near surface
soils started to develop as soil moisture was evaporated from the soil system
under the prolonged dry climatic conditions characteristic of the Ewa area
These shrinkage cracks may close up to a certain degree upon wetting as the soils
expand during periods of wet weather or heavy rain storms However shrinkage
of the on site soils and subsequent cracking of the ground surface will recur when
the dry season returns Therefore the development of shrinkage cracks will recur
when the on site clayey soils are subjected to prolonged dry climatic conditions
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2819 70 Page 14
which causes large fluctuations in the moisture content unless some measures
are taken to maintain the soii moisture content relatively constant
In areas where water ponding occurs the wet ground will have a higher potential
for shrinkage cracks to develop since the wet soils will lose water more readily
upon exposure to dry conditions and will shrink more Therefore it should be
recognized that this phenomenon will recur and continue unless measures are
taken to maintain the soil moisture content relatively constant in order to reduce
the shrink swell behavior of the on site clayey soils due to moisture fluctuations
Recommendations During Site Preparation
If the existing ground cracks are left unattended for an extended period of time
and are allowed to penetrate deeper below the surface the ground cracks could
adversely affect the performance of the future house foundations The ground
cracks could serve as conduits for water to percolate into the foundation soils
and cause loss of strength of the foundation soils resulting in potential settlement
of the house structure In addition migration of the foundation soils into the
openings of the shrinkage cracks could undermine the foundation system and
result in potential settlement of the house structure Therefore remedial work to
correct or mitigate the observed ground cracks should be implemented prior to
construction of the proposed houses
Based on our field measurements shrinkage cracks up to about 6 inches wide
and 3 to 4 feet deep were observed across the project site Therefore we
recommend that the proposed building and driveway footprints be
over excavated a minimum of 2 feet below the existing ground surface to remove
the zone with shrinkage cracks The over excavation should also extend
laterally a minimum of 3 and 2 feet beyond the building and driveway limits
respectively If walkways are planned around the house structures the 2 foot
over excavation should extend laterally to the outside edge of the walkways

10 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum

Long Term Maintenance Measures
As discussed above the phenomenon of shrinkage crack development will recur
depending on climatic conditions unless measures are taken to maintain the soil
moisture content relatively constant thereby reducing the shrink swell behavior of
the on site clayey soils due to moisture content fluctuations These long term
solutions may include landscaping installation of concrete walkways around the
house structure and or placement of non expansive fills in the yard areas
Based on our previous experience with similar soils house lots without
landscaping have experienced varying degrees of soil erosion during heavy rains
It is our opinion that landscaping of the house lots should be given serious
GEOLABS HAWAIi
W O 2819 70 Page 16
consideration by the homeowners since it would generally achieve the following
objectives
1 Landscaping along with proper irrigation will serve to maintain the soil
moisture content relatively constant due to controlled regular irrigation
which reduces the shrink swell behavior of the soils and consequently
reduces the potential for development of shrinkage cracks
2 Development of a root system from landscaping would reduce the potential
for erosion and for altering the drainage patterns
However the future homeowners should be advised or cautioned about the
following conditions which may occur or would be potentially detrimental to their
property
1 Ground cracks may develop in the landscaped areas if there was a ration
on the use of irrigation water due to prolonged dry seasons As the root
system is deprived of the irrigation water it would start to draw water out
from the surrounding soil system thus accelerating the drying out of the
soils and resulting in faster development of shrinkage cracks
2 To ensure proper performance of the foundation system the homeowners
should be strongly cautioned against over irrigation or landscaping within
3 feet around the house structures as this may introduce irrigation water
into the subsoils around the thickened edge footings which can soften the
subsoils and affect the foundation performance In lieu of landscaping we
recommend that concrete walkways be constructed within this zone to
reduce the potential for direct water infiltration into the subsoils along the
perimeter of the house foundation Where provided the concrete walkways
should be sloped to shed water away from the house foundations and
garage slab In addition the concrete walkways should be supported on
non expansive select granular fill material
Based on the above discussion it is our opinion that the yard area may be

After clearing and grubbing the areas outside the building or walkways and
driveway limits should be scarified to a depth of about 12 inches and recompacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction This serves to close up some of the large
shrinkage cracks which may become a potential tripping hazard to the future
homeowners As previously recommended the yard area may be over excavated to
provide an 8 inch layer of non expansive or low expansion capping soil
Areas within the building and driveway limits will need to be over excavated a
minimum of 2 feet below the existing ground surface as recommended in the Shrinkage
Cracks section of this report The over excavation should extend laterally a minimum of
3 feet beyond the perimeter of the proposed building structure or to the outside edge of
the walkways The 3 foot lateral over excavation requirement may be reduced to 2 feet
for the driveway slab areas
The over excavated subgrades should be scarified to a minimum depth of
10 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and
recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Where shrinkage cracks are
noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soil be prepared again
as recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade

General fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above

compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
The 2 or 3 foot non expansive select granular capping fill required under and

Driveway Slabs
We envision that concrete driveways at the house lots will be required for the
project development We anticipate that the vehicle loading for the proposed driveways
will consist primarily of passenger vehicles and light pick up trucks We have made our
preliminary design assuming the subgrade soil will generally consist of the near surface
highly expansive clayey soils as encountered in our soil borings If site grading exposes
soils other than those assumed additional tests should be performed to confirm or revise
the recommended driveway section for actual field conditions
Based on the above assumptions we recommend the concrete driveway slabs be
supported on a 2 foot thick non expansive select granular capping fill compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction as recommended earlier The final lift
8 inches of the non expansive select granular capping fill should be compacted to not
less than 95 percent relative compaction We recommend that the concrete slab be
designed by a structural engineer for the intended loads and should be reinforced as a
structural slab to accommodate some amount of differential movement
For design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 200 pounds per
cubic inch pci may be used for the compacted select granular capping fill Provisions
should be made for proper load transfer across the slab joints which are subjected to
vehicular traffic The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any unpaved area should be
embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
Based on our field observations abundant shrinkage cracks were observed across
the project site In addition our laboratory tests indicated that the near surface clayey
soils exhibit a high expansion potential when subjected to changes in the moisture
content Therefore we recommend that the soil subgrades be properly prepared and
kept moist prior to placement of the non expansive select capping fill materials Detailed
discussions were presented in the Shrinkage Cracks and Site Preparation sections of

Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Surface water ponding should
not be allowed anywhere on the site during or after construction It is highly
recommended that gutter systems be installed around the buildings and that the
discharge be diverted away from the foundation and driveway areas
Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs within 3 feet of the
building perimeter should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided
as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface run off away from the slab
and foundations

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Seventeen one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results
of these tests are summarized on Plates B 1 through B 3
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 4
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 5 and B 6
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 91 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 7 and B 8
