INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the slopes along Mokapu Saddle Road located between Mikiola Drive and 400 feet east
of the centerline of Kahinani Way in the District of Kaneohe on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
GEOLABS INC
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the design of slope stabilization measures which include
the design of retaining wall foundations rip rap slopes excavation and backfill placement
only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to stabilize three cut slopes along Mokapu Saddle Road from Mikiola
Drive to 400 feet east of the centerline of Kahinani Way The approximate slope lengths to
receive remedial work listed from west to east are 370 feet 290 feet and 400 feet
respectively The slopes are generally at about a three fourths horizontal to one vertical
4H 1V to 1H 1V slope inclination with slope heights ranging from 0 to about 37 feet The
base of slope elevations range from about 24 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the slope on
the west end to about 97 feet MSL at the slope on the east end
After heavy rains in early 1996 a shallow slope failure was noted at the corner of
Mikiola Drive and Kaneohe Bay Drive The slope failure was of a toe failure type with the
maximum failure thickness of about 2 feet and affecting nearly the entire slope height of
about 11 feet
Also after heavy rains in early 1996 a shallow slope failure was noted above the
existing rip rap at about 250 feet east from the centerline of Mikiola Drive The rip rap is
about 12 feet in height leaving the remaining upper slope of about 12 feet in height
unsupported The slope failure was of a toe failure type with the maximum failure thickness
of about 2 feet affecting about 9 feet above the rip rap
These above mentioned failures are generally due to water intrusion into the clayey
silt material through shrinkage cracks resulting in lowered shear strengths and subsequent
slope failure In the case of the slope failure at the corner of Mikiola Drive and Kaneohe Bay
GEOLABS INC
Drive the water could have originated from spillover from the concrete lined trapezoidal
cutoff ditch of about 18 inches deep as the water approached the 60 degree bend
At Station 19 65 about 213 feet east from the centerline of Kahinani Place a
washout of soil material was noted during a recent site reconnaissance The washout was
generally due to a channelized flow of water forcing the displacement of soil beneath the
existing concrete lined trapezoidal cutoff ditch The channelized flow may have been caused
by the grading of the upper adjacent residential backyard It appears that the grades to the
sides of the washout are sloping toward this channel It also appears that soil material was
built up against the existing chain link fence
At various locations along the three slopes small isolated slumping was observed
near the toes of slopes This occurrence may also be attributed to lower shear strengths due
to softening from waier intrusion

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was
still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava
During the later Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the continental glaciers advanced less water was available io fill
the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below its present stand When the glaciers
melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose to
above its present level
GEOLABS INC
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans
of terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials
caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
In some of the mountainous regions of Hawaii chemical weathering has altered the
basaltic flows pyroclastic ejecta to a soil like condition Erosional processes consisting of
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and transport downslope toward
the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium have also altered the natural condition
of the volcanic flows Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of valley
alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as
alluvium
When alluvial colluvial deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical
processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering
of the material Erosion continually deposits newer material covering the older
alluvial colluvial deposits Depending on the rate of transport the newer sediments are
generally transient in terms of geologic time
The project area is generally underlain by these older and more recent alluvial
deposits or by the soil like weathered basaltic flows pyroclastic ejecta Urban development
has since altered the natural formation of the area

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The slopes generally consist of steep cut slopes about H 1V to 1H 1V with
concrete lined trapezoidal cutoff ditches at the top of the slope The cutoff ditches are likely
designed to catch sheet flow surface water from the uphill adjacent houselots The cutoff
ditches then transport the water by gravity flow either to the concrete lined ditches at the
base of the slope or to the inlets that transport the water through large diameter drainlines
which both lead to catch basins and another network of drainlines AC lined swales at the
edges of the roadway also drain to the concrete lined ditches at the base of the slopes
The concrete lined trapezoidal cutoff ditches are designed to drain in a westerly
direction except for Slope No 2 which drains in both directions away from the middle The
right of way line is generally about 3 feet from the edge of the cutoff ditch A chain link fence
is located about 1 foot within the right of way line At Slope No 1 a 5 to 12 foot high rip rap
facing covers the middle of the slope
Slope Nos 1 and 2 are generally concave down in front view with the greatest slope
height near the middle of the slope length Slope No 3 is generally of a constant slope
height up until about the location of the second catch basin east of Kahinani Way The
slopes were heavily vegetated with grass weeds and large trees

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our exploration we believe that a retaining wall with a reverse
footing and a may be used to stabilize the eroded slope
Due to the existence of two catch basins at Slope No 3 transitions to a grouted
rip rap lining will be necessary at the catch basins
Based on the current retaining wall design and our field exploration excavation for
the keyways at the eastern end of the site may involve cuts into the underlying soft to
medium hard basalt rock It is anticipated that the soft basalt rock may be excavated with
normal heavy excavation equipment such as ripping with large bulldozers where the rock
is near the existing ground surface However excavations into the deeper formations may
require the use of hoerams The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface
materials are based on field data obtained from the borings performed at the subject site
We recommend that contractors bidding on this project be encouraged to examine the site

Slope Stabilization Alternatives
A tip rap lining on the slope face and up against the cutoff ditch was considered
However there was a concern that water may still undermine the cutoff ditch and
GEOLABS INC
therefore pop out failures within the rip rap slopes may occur Also due to the steep
slope water infiltration may lead to deeper slope failures
Other stabilization methods such as a Keystone retaining wall gravity retaining wall
or conventional cantilever retaining wall would require large excavation into the steep
slope which could further destabilize the slopes and the houses above the slope Other
alternatives such as placing a buttress or filling or cutting the slope to creaie a flatter
slope are not possible due to the area constraints Based on the above a cantilever
retaining wall with a reverse footing is recommended
Due to the existence of two catch basins which would conflict with the retaining wall
footings at Slope No 3 transitions to a grouted rip rap lining is recommended at the catch
basins Since the areas proposed for the rip rap are at the ends of this slope are of limited
length and are currently in fair to good condition we feel that construction of a rip rap is a
viable solution
Due to the need for transitions from rip rap to retaining wall use of veneer rock facings
on the retaining wall may be considered for aesthetic reasons It should be noted that the
thickness of the veneer facing will be about 2 to 2 inches
Retaining Wall Recommendations
The retaining walls should extend to the ends of the slope In the case of the Slope
No 3 the retaining wall should reside between the two catch basin structures The eastern
rip rap should be extended to about 400 feet east of the centerline of Kahinani Way Note
that at 400 feet east of the centerline of Kahinani Way weathered rock material is exposed
at the bottom of the cut slope
An ultimate bearing pressure of up to 12 000 pounds per square foot psf may be
utilized to evaluate the extreme event limit state for the design of retaining wall foundations
bearing on the underlying stiff clayey silt material To evaluate the strength limit state of
GEOLABS INC
foundations a resistance factor of 0 6 or a bearing pressure of up to may be
utilized
An allowable bearing pressure of 4 300 pounds per square foot psf may be utilized
to evaluate the service limit state for the design of retaining wall foundations bearing on the
underlying stiff clayey silt material This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
The tops of footings should be embedded a minimum of 4 feet below the existing grade to
allow for future development of the shoulder into an additional travel lane
To minimize excavation into the slope the heel of the wall should not extend past the
stem by more than 2 feet However for instances where the slope height dictates walls with
toe lengths in excess of 8 feet beyond the stem the heel of the wall may be increased to
4 feet beyond the stem If the additional cut is limited in width the slope stability due to the
additional cut should not be significantly reduced
If foundations are located next to utility ttenches or easements it should be embedded
below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench
or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce
the potential for foundation settlement
We estimate that footing settlements bearing on the underlying stiff clayey silt
material as recommended herein to be less than one half inch total Differential settlement
over a 20 foot length of wall is estimated to be less than one fourth inch If soft and or loose
materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations the footings should be
deepened to expose the underlying firm materials
The service and strength limit states of the retaining wall should be designed for the
situation where the material over the toe of the wall is removed during construction of the wall
for an additional travel lane Therefore the wall should be designed for the retaining wall
GEOLABS INC
condition for the full height of the wall The extreme event limit state of the retaining wall may
be designed for the fully constructed roadway condition
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using equivalent fluid
pressures of 203 408 and 815 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf for the service
limit state the strength limit state and the extreme limit state respectively Lateral loads
acting on the abutment may be resisted by frictional resistance developed between the
bottom of the foundation and the stiff clayey silt Resistance due to friction may be estimated
using coefficients of friction of 6 and 0 72 for the service limit state the strength limit
state and the extreme limit state respectively In addition a cohesion value of 400 psf may
be used for resistance These values assume that the concrete for footings is placed directly
against the footing excavations with undisturbed soil Backfilling on the sides of the footing
and key should not be permitted

Retaining Wall Backfill
Backfill behind the retaining wall should consist of on site soils or select granular fill
material compacted to a minimum 90 percent relative compaction ASTM D 1557 91
Over compaction of the backfill should be avoided
Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should
start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance
with the above fill placement recommendations
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope
ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face In the event that over cutting of a slope occurs
keying and benching requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope
to the design grade with sliver fills Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes
and slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion
of the finished slopes
GEOLABS INC
Imported fill should consist of non expansive select granular maierial such as crushed
coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to
fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory
CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883
Backfill behind the retaining wall should be constructed in such a manner to allow for
a minimum 2 foot relatively flat area at the top of the slope next to the cutoff ditch and at the
bottom of the slope next to the retaining wall
Retaining Wall Drainage
To reduce the potential for future undermining of the cutoff ditches we recommend
that a minimum 6 inch thick concrete slab with standard reinforcement of 6 also
known as x W29 galvanized welded wire fabric be constructed flush with the
top of the uphill side of the cutoff ditch
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
The drainage system should consist of a zone of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation equal in width to the wall heel length The No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No
67 gradation should be immediately around the perforated pipe perforations down at the
base of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes The backside of the
weephole should be covered with a geotextile AMOCO 4545 or similar to prevent loss of
gravel material The fill area between the existing slope and the required No 3B Fine gravel
may be filled with either select granular fill or No 3B Fine gravel The fill area above the
required No 3B Fine gravel should be filled with select granular fill The 2 feet directly above
the heel should be filled with on site clayey soil to reduce water infiltration below the wall and
to reduce the potential for ponding of water below the weephole The upper 24 inches of
GEOLABS INC
backfill surface should consist of the on site clayey soil to reduce water infiltration behind the
walls At the backside of the wall a concrete lined ditch should be constructed to carry water
along the length of the wall to appropriate discharge locations To accommodate the ditch
and to prevent spillovers the top of wall should be a minimum 12 inches above the backfill
A typical retaining wall backfill detail is provided on Plate 5
Based on the bottom of slope elevations being about 174 feet below the existing
pavement elevation the weepholes should be placed at about 2 feet above the existing
bottom of slope elevation to allow for an additional travel lane in the future
Rip Rap Recommendations
The rip rap should have a minimum thickness of 12 inches with a 24 inch deep and
24 inch wide socket at the bottom and at mid slope A typical rip rap detail is provided on
Plate 6
At the top of the rip rap the facing should be constructed flush with the top of the
cutoff ditch and should be embedded a minimum of 36 inches and outside of a 2H 1V
plane projected downward from the cutoff ditch top
Transitions from retaining wall to rip rap should be constructed by varying the slope
inclination of the lower slope and varying the length of the upper 2H 1V slope of the rip rap
Transitions from retaining wall to rip rap should occur within a 30 foot length prior to the
catch basins to minimize surcharge loading on the catch basins
Due to the potential for spillover at the 60 degree bend we recommend that a rip rap
lining be constructed on the backfill slope above the retaining wall The rip rap should cover
an area of about 25 feet from the 60 degree turn in both directions Therefore the rip rap
should be about 50 feet in width
GEOLABS INC
To reduce the potential for future undermining of the cutoff ditches we recommend
that a minimum 6 inch thick concrete slab with standard reinforcement of 6 also
known as x W29 galvanized welded wire fabric be constructed flush with the
top of the uphill side of the cutoff ditch
Washout Location Recommendations
At the washout location at Station 19 65 we recommend that PVC pipes be laid over
backfilled or concrete material to allow for water drainage from the existing ground elevation
of the channel on the adjacent houselot side to the concrete lined ditch next to the top of the
retaining wall
After placement of the PVC pipes we recommend that concrete be used to backfill
beneath the undermined cutoff ditch Backfill material in accordance with the Retaining Wall
Backfill section of this report may be used for the sloped material beyond the undermined
cutoff ditch
At the water entrance end of the PVC pipe we recommend a strip of No 3B Fine
gravel wrapped with woven geotextile AMOCO 2016 or similar with minimum cross section
dimension of 4 inches in width and 12 inches in height be installed to cover the pipes The
woven geotextile should also be mechanically attached to the concrete A typical washout
area detail is provided on Plate 7
Drainage
The finished grades adjacent to the retaining wall should be sloped to shed water
along the length of the walls to appropriate discharge locations to reduce the potential for
ponding
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause hydrostatic pressures
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain
all surface run off away from walls and foundations

Shoring and Other Precautionary Measures During Construction
In order to reduce the possibility of slope movements during construction we
recommend that the following be included in the construction contract for retaining wall and
rip rap construction
1 Work should be performed in horizontal increments less than 100 feet
2 Shoring of the excavated areas should be provided by the Contractor The
design for the shoring should be done by a licensed structural engineer
3 During construction the Contractor should survey locations along the top of the
slope at 50 foot intervals to monitor possible movement at the top of the cut
slope between Stations 17 00 and Station 20 00 The survey should be done
daily by a licensed land surveyor and the results transmitted to the Engineer
within 24 hours The survey should be stamped and submitted to the State
upon completion of the work

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring and 4 to a depth of about 21 feet below the
existing ground elevation at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger equipment
The subsurface conditions at the site were also explored by hand augering and
sampling one boring designated at Boring No 2 to a depth of about 3 4 feet below the
existing ground elevation at the appropriate location shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 to 24 inch
drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed on
selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The
results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
One Atterberg Limits Test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of this
test is summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
representations of the test results are provided on Plate B 1
Two 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on remolded samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the soils under surcharge loads These tests were run on air dried
samples Results of these swell tests are presented on Plate B 2
One Triaxial Unconsolidated Unconfined Compression test TXUU was performed
on a relatively undisturbed sample to evaluate the strength characteristics for retaining wall
design parameters Results of this TXUU test are presented on Plate B 3
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate their peak and residual shear strength characteristics The test results and shear
stress versus normal stress plots are presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
