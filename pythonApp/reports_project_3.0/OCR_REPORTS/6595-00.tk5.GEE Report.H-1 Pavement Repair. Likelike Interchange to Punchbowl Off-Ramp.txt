SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement areas 
would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing pavements should 
be performed.  Existing pavements showing minor distresses and severity are generally 
rehabilitated with cold-planing and an AC overlay. Reconstruction of the existing 
pavements is usually performed for those areas where the observed distresses are likely 
caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials. 
Pavement condition survey conducted at the project site indicated that the majority 
of the pavements within the project limits appear to exist in fair to good condition. 
Pavement areas with moderate to severe distresses have been identified and delineated 
by Parsons Brinckerhoff, Inc.  Based on our evaluation, it is our opinion that pavement 
areas with minor distresses may be rehabilitated with an AC overlay consisting of 
cold-planing 4 inches of the existing AC surface and replacing with 2 inches of new SMA 
over 2 inches of Mix IV AC overlay. 
Pavement areas exhibiting moderate to severe distresses within the project limits 
should be reconstructed with new pavement section designed based on a 10-year 
pavement life in accordance with the guidelines provided in the pavement design manual 
prepared by HDOT. We believe that the majority of the pavement distresses may be 
attributed to the softening of the clayey subsurface soils anticipated at the pavement 
subgrade level resulting from moisture infiltration.  Ideally, the distressed pavements 
should be completely removed (including the clayey subsurface soils) and should be 
replaced with select granular fill materials. However, complete removal and replacement 
would significantly increase the construction time required and may not be feasible nor 
practical due to the high traffic volumes of the Interstate Route H-1 Freeway at this 
location. Therefore, we recommend replacing the existing weakened pavements with a 
new pavement structural section consisting of 4 inches of SMA/AC over 6 to 8 inches of 
ACB. A new pavement structural section consisting of 4 inches of SMA/AC over 6 to 
12 inches of ACB may be used for the reconstruction of the shoulder pavements 
(median and right shoulder) to carry the anticipated traffic loads as a result of the lane 
reconfiguration.  The recommended new pavement sections have taken into account the 
contribution from the remaining existing pavement sections as well as constructability 
issues due to the limited time frame within a single work shift. 
We recommend allocating a contingency fund for additional distressed areas 
requiring reconstruction that may develop between the time construction plans are 
prepared and the actual commencement of the project construction.

Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering evaluation and 
analyses for the Interstate Route H-1 Pavement Rehabilitation project from 
Likelike Interchange to Punchbowl Street in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1.

literature and document review, and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our analyses for the design of pavement rehabilitation 
and/or reconstruction, new retaining wall and light pole foundations.  The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The volcanic 
materials erupted from the Waianae Volcano are referred to as the Waianae Volcanic 
Series. 
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the 
island. Volcanic materials erupted from the Koolau Volcano are referred to as the 
Koolau Volcanic Series. Waianae Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano 
remained active; therefore, the Waianae Volcano’s eastern flank was partially buried 
below the younger Koolau lavas that banked against its eastern flank. These banked or 
ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau of Central 
Oahu. The present day form of the Island of Oahu is a result of stream dissection and 
mass erosion of the steep valley walls. 
During the history of the evolution of the Island of Oahu, multiple global 
fluctuations in sea-level occurred as a result of the advance and retreat of the great 
continental glaciers. These sea-level changes occurred more substantially in the 
Pleistocene Epoch and had some significant effect on the geologic evolution of the 
Island of Oahu. The changes in worldwide sea-level affected the erosional baseline of 
streams and caused local submergence and emergence of the coastal landforms with 
respect to the level of the sea. 
The end of volcanic activity associated with the Koolau Volcano was followed by 
a long period of erosion whereby deep valleys were carved into the Koolau Mountain 
Range. The valleys were filled with thick accumulations of alluvial sediments as the 
island slowly sank under the weight of the new landmass. 
The project site is along the southern flank of the Koolau Mountain Range. The 
geomorphology and subsurface conditions at the site are related directly to the

glacio-eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the 
genesis of Pearl Harbor and the Honolulu Coastal Plain. These glacio-eustatic 
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to the 
present sea level of Oahu. The subsurface conditions at the project site are dominated 
by fossil Pleistocene age alluvial soil deposits and related deposits laid during high 
stands of the sea that are intercalated with terrigenous sediments. The base of the 
stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt. 
The higher sea level stands resulted in the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal 
sediments may have also resulted in deposits of coral-algal limestone and marls within 
the predominantly lagoonal sedimentary units. 
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef 
deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused 
desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials. In addition, renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of 
the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high-energy 
conditions. 
During periods of insignificant sea level changes, continued stream action 
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal 
sediments. About 15,000 years ago, a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred. During 
that rise, the deep valleys in the project area were submerged. Within the last 
10,000 years, the sea level has adjusted to its present stand. Terrigenous and marine 
sediments have continued to accumulate in low-energy estuarine or lagoonal 
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft harbor sediments along the coast in 
areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways.

2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The Interstate Route H-1 Freeway is a major thoroughfare extending from about 
the Kahala area in the east to about the Kapolei area in the west on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii.  The approximate limits for this pavement rehabilitation project generally extend 
from about the Likelike Interchange in the west (Sta. 49+22 or Mile Post 19.62) to about 
Punchbowl Street in the east (Sta. 173+06 or Mile Post 21.80) as shown on the 
General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
This portion of the Interstate Route H-1 Freeway was constructed in the 1960s 
and has gone through various phases of upgrading and expansion through the years. 
The existing highway within the project limits is generally a six-lane divided highway 
except near the on-ramp and off-ramp areas.  The topography along the highway 
alignment is undulating with highs near the Likelike Interchange, Palama Settlement, 
and Punchbowl Street and lows at the Kapalama Canal and Nuuanu Stream.  The 
existing ground surface elevations along the highway alignment generally range from 
about +10 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) near the stream channels to about +60 feet MSL 
near the Likelike Interchange.  Based on our field observations and the information 
provided, the traffic volume at the highway may be considered as heavy with the 
average daily traffic count (both directions) on the order of about 185,000 to 
195,000 vehicles in the Year 2012.  
The following guidelines and criteria are used in describing various types of 
pavement distresses and failures in this report.  It should be noted that the term 
“Structural Defects” refers to areas where the pavement deterioration is believed to be 
caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials. The 
term “Ride Characteristic” refers to the response of a vehicle when passing at normal 
speed over the highway. 
2.2.1 Areas with Minor Distresses 
The existing pavements under this category are generally good with no structural 
defects observed.  Overall pavement deterioration is restricted to light alligator 
cracking of low severity.  The ride characteristic of the pavement is generally fair to 
good.

2.2.2 Areas with Moderate to Severe Distresses 
The existing pavements under this category are generally in poor condition with 
poor-quality, moderately to highly distressed areas.  Alligator and alligator block 
cracking are widespread and generally moderate to high in severity. The poor areas 
may consist of elongated, severely distressed zones of alligator block cracking with 
depressions, rutting and some shoving of the pavement. The ride characteristic of 
the highway is generally fair to slightly irregular and bumpy in the areas of poor 
pavements. 
A pavement condition survey was conducted by Parsons Brinckerhoff, Inc. in 
October 2011 to collect general information for the existing pavement conditions along 
the portion of the highway under consideration. Pavement areas exhibiting moderate to 
severe distresses requiring repairs were delineated in the field. Graphic presentations of 
these distressed pavement areas are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.11 
in this report for reference. In addition, photographs taken at these distressed pavement 
areas were transmitted to our office for review. 
2.3 
Anticipated Subsurface Conditions 
Based on our experience and the soil boring data provided in the as-built 
construction drawings for Interstate Route H-1 Freeway, we envision that the existing 
pavement structure along the highway alignment is generally underlain by a surface 
layer of clayey soils of varying thickness and consistency.  The surface clay layer could 
be underlain by cinder sands, boulders, and/or basalt rock formation. 
Groundwater is not anticipated within the shallow subsurface based on the 
topography along the highway alignment.  However, it should be noted that the 
groundwater level may be influenced by seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, 
storm surge conditions, and other factors.

3.9.3 Drainage 
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of 
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), 
placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of 
the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, a 
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used 
instead of the drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product also should be 
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of compacted on-site 
materials or free-draining compacted fills, where specified by the designer. Unless 
covered by concrete slabs, the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of 
low-expansion, relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for excessive 
water infiltration behind the walls.

3.10.4 Construction Considerations 
Drilled shafts are desirable for the light pole foundations because of the significant 
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations.  
However, the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the 
contractor's method of construction and construction procedures.  In our opinion, 
the following conditions would have an impact on the effectiveness and cost of the 
drilled shaft foundations. 
Based on our field exploration, the proposed new light pole locations are generally 
underlain by soft/loose and inadequately compacted soils at shallow depths. In 
order to reduce the potential for caving-in of the drilled holes, a temporary steel 
casing with a ½-inch thickness may be required for the drilled shaft foundation 
construction work. 
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend, to a large extent, on the 
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils.  Therefore, proper 
construction techniques are important.  The contractor should exercise care in 
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes. 
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is not recommended.  Placement of 
concrete using a tremie pipe will be required due to the depth of the drilled shaft 
holes.  The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner by starting at the 
bottom and continue in an upward fashion to the top of the shaft. 
A low-shrink concrete mix with high slump (6 to 9-inch range) should be used for 
the concreting operation.  The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to 
reduce the potential for segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix.  If 
pre-cast concrete pole bases are used, the gap between the pole base and the 
surrounding soils should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete.

3.11 Subterranean Seepage 
Based on the information provided and our field observations, we understand 
that the right lanes (both eastbound and westbound) along Interstate Route H-1 
Freeway between about Nuuanu Stream and Nuuanu Avenue have exhibited pavement 
distresses and failures that are likely attributed to the presence of subterranean 
seepage water.  A field exploration was conducted by our office to evaluate the 
subsurface conditions at these distressed pavement areas.  Based on our findings, we 
believe that the pavement distresses may be attributed to the presence of hard basalt 
rock formation and an existing concrete box culvert at shallow depths, impeding the 
dissipation of subterranean seepage water, and resulting in water ponding and 
saturation of the pavement structure and underlying subgrade soils.  Therefore, we 
recommend reconstructing these distressed pavements in accordance with the 
pavement design section provided in this report. In addition, a subdrain system should 
be installed alongside the new pavement structural section to collect the subterranean 
seepage water and daylight for proper discharge.  A schematic detail of the proposed 
subdrain system is presented on Plate 5. Detailed discussions and recommendations 
will be presented in a letter report transmitted under separate cover. 
3.12 Shallow Slope Movement 
Based on the information provided and our field observations, we understand 
that the right lane and shoulder area (westbound) along Interstate Route H-1 Freeway 
adjacent to Peter Buck Mini Park have exhibited pavement distresses and failures that 
are likely caused by the movement of the adjacent embankment slope, as evident by 
the tilting of guardrails and light poles in the proximity.  A field exploration was 
conducted by our office to evaluate the subsurface conditions at the distressed 
pavement areas.  Based on our findings, it appears that the upper portion of the 
embankment fills, particularly along the sloping ground, consisted of soft/loose and 
inadequately compacted soils.  We believe shallow movement of these upper soils have 
resulted in the depressions, separation, and cracking of the pavements observed in the 
area.  Based on our evaluation, we recommend reconstructing the upper portion of the 
embankment fills within the pavement areas with Geosynthetic Reinforced Soil (GRS) 
with a Concrete Masonry Unit (CMU) facing.  A schematic detail of the proposed

3.13 Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils.  Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures.  
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction.  Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.
