SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration along the proposed East Welakahao Road Pavement 
Rehabilitation project generally encountered loose to very dense sands and very stiff to 
hard clayey silts extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 5.5 feet 
below the existing pavement surface. We encountered groundwater at a depth of about 
2.3 feet below the existing pavement surface in Boring No. 4 drilled towards South Kihei 
Road at the time of our field exploration. 
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement areas 
would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing pavements should 
be performed. Existing pavements showing minor distresses and severity are generally 
rehabilitated with cold-planing and an AC overlay. Reconstruction of the existing 
pavements is usually performed for those areas where the observed distresses are likely 
caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials. 
Our pavement condition survey at the project site indicated that the majority of the 
pavements under consideration for this project appear to exist in fair to good condition. 
Based on our engineering evaluation and construction considerations, we recommend 
rehabilitating these areas with a regular AC overlay consisting of cold-planing 2.5 inches of 
the existing pavements and replacing with 2.5 inches of new AC overlay. 
Since the areas requiring reconstruction due to structural distress amount to more 
than 15 percent of the total pavement area proposed for rehabilitation, the pavement 
reconstruction should be designed based on a pavement life of 20 years in accordance 
with the guidelines presented in the pavement design manual prepared by the State of 
Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing and Research 
Branch. In general, the distressed pavements may be reconstructed with a new pavement 
structural section consisting of 2.5 inches of AC on 4.5 inches of ACB over 6 inches of AB. 
It should be noted that CBR tests and/or field observations should be performed on the 
actual subgrade soils during construction to confirm that the above pavement design 
section is adequate. 
A layer of reinforcing geogrid (such as Tensar TriAx Grid, TX7 or equivalent) on a 
layer of permeable separator (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below the 
new pavement structural section. The reinforcing geogrid would serve to enhance the 
R-value of the subgrade materials by providing stabilization and reinforcement to the 
underlying base/subbase/subgrade materials.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering analyses performed for the East Welakahao Road Pavement Rehabilitation 
(South Kihei Road to Piilani Highway) project in the Kihei area on the Island of Maui, 
Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location 
Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the rehabilitation and reconstruction of the existing pavements only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The roadway rehabilitation project is along East Welakahao Road between 
South Kihei Road and Piilani Highway for a total distance of approximately 0.6 miles in 
the Kihei area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The approximate limits of the roadway 
rehabilitation project are shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the existing pavements showing minor distresses and severity will be 
rehabilitated with cold-planing and an AC overlay. Reconstruction of the existing 
pavements will be performed for those areas where the observed distresses are likely 
caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials.  We 
understand that the proposed construction will be a federally funded project. Therefore, 
a pavement justification report conforming to the requirements and guidelines of the 
State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation will be required. The new pavements for 
the project will be designed based on the methodology described in the Pavement 
Design Manual dated March 2002 prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department of 
Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research Branch. 
Special attention was given to the potential presence of near-surface soft and/or 
loose soils, which may be a contributing factor to the pavement failures observed along

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala 
(Pleistocene Epoch).  The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located 
between these two volcanoes.  The project site is situated on the isthmus connecting 
the two volcanic domes. 
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts 
of the two volcanoes.  During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fell to stands lower than 
present sea level exposing large areas of coralline sand on what was the ocean bottom. 
Wind action transported these coralline sands inland forming extensive areas of sand 
dunes.  In some areas, these dunes have been mantled by man-made fill which was 
apparently placed during the period that the area was used for the cultivation of sugar 
cane. 
2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The existing East Welakahao Road within the project limits is generally a 
two-lane undivided roadway except for the additional turn lanes at the intersection areas 
and parking lanes along the shoulders.  The approximate limits of the roadway are 
shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The shoulder areas beyond the edge of pavement generally consist of short-cut 
grass, sidewalks, traffic signs and utility posts. The surface soils along the shoulder 
areas below the vegetation generally consist of red-brown sands and silts. The existing 
East Welakahao Road alignment generally slopes down from the east to west, between 
the limits of Liloa Drive and South Kihei Road. 
Our office conducted a site reconnaissance in June 2016 to collect general 
information for the existing pavement conditions along the portion of the roadway under 
consideration. Detailed descriptions of our field surveys are presented in the following 
subsections. It should be noted that the term “Structural Defects” refers to areas where 
the pavement deterioration is believed to be caused by failures in the underlying base,

2.2.1 Areas with Minor to Moderate Distresses and Severity 
Based on our site reconnaissance, the majority of the existing pavements along 
East Welakahao Road within the limits under consideration falls into this category 
and appear to be generally fair with minor signs of structural defects observed. 
Overall pavement deterioration is restricted to light alligator cracking of low to 
moderate severity, as well as both transverse and longitudinal cracking. It was 
observed that a crack sealant has recently been placed over these cracks. The ride 
characteristic of the pavement was generally fair.  
2.2.2 Areas with Base/Subgrade Failures 
In general, various localized areas along the roadway alignment from South Kihei 
Road to approximately 200 feet west of Akai Street intersection fall under this 
category and was observed to be in poor condition with moderately to highly 
distressed areas. Alligator and alligator block cracking were observed to be 
widespread and generally moderate to high in severity. The poor areas consist of 
elongated, severely distressed zones of alligator block cracking with depressions of 
1/8 to 1/4 inches, rutting and some shoving of the pavement within the tire lanes. In 
addition, separation of the pavement from the curb and gutter were observed in 
various locations. Potholes and subsurface utility trenches patched with AC 
material were observed at various locations along the alignment. The ride 
characteristics of the roadway within these subject areas were observed to be 
generally fair to irregular and bumpy in the areas of poor pavements. The 
approximate limits of these poor areas requiring pavement reconstruction are 
shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 and 3.2.

3.5 
Design Pavement Section 
As discussed in the “Existing Site/Pavement Conditions” section herein, some of 
the observed distresses along the roadway are likely caused by failures in the 
underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials. In general, these areas should be 
reconstructed with a new pavement section in lieu of an AC overlay section. In addition, 
these distressed areas should be repaired prior to the AC overlay work for the entire 
roadway under consideration. 
Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicate that some portion of the 
roadway alignment under consideration for this project is generally underlain by sandy 
soils with little or no cohesion.  As a result, the subgrade may not be able to compact 
into a dense mass to receive placement of the asphalt concrete base (ACB) layer.  In 
addition, other areas of the roadway alignment may be underlain by clayey silts with a 
significant amount of fines content.  We understand that placement of ACB directly on 
these clayey subgrade is not recommended.  Based on our evaluation and these 
construction considerations, we recommend providing a layer of aggregate subbase 
between the upper ACB layer and the underlying subgrade soils.  The aggregate 
subbase will serve to provide confinement of the sandy soils as well as a granular 
surface for the placement of the ACB. 
Based on the assumptions and the construction considerations described above, 
we have determined the following flexible pavement design section for the proposed 
East Welakahao Road Pavement Rehabilitation project.  Detailed calculations and 
analyses of the pavement design are presented on Plate C-3.1 in Appendix C.

The thickness of the flexible pavement section, especially the aggregate subbase 
layer, for East Welakahao Road may be reduced by incorporating a layer of triaxial 
geogrid between the upper aggregate subbase and underlying sandy and/or clayey silt 
subgrade soils. To maximize the benefits of the triaxial geogrid, we understand that 
aggregate base course with nominal maximum size of 1.5 inches should be used in lieu 
of the aggregate subbase. In general, the triaxial geogrid will interlock with the 
aggregate base course, resulting in two benefits during initial construction and for the 
life of the project: 1) lateral confinement – increasing the modulus of the aggregate base 
course, and 2) subgrade bearing capacity enhancement.  Based on the full scale 
performance test results by Tensar as shown on Plate 4, we believe that the R-value of 
the subgrade soils may be enhanced from a value of 8 to 35 (conservative side) with the 
incorporation of a layer of triaxial geogrid.  Based on this enhanced R-value of the 
subgrade soils, we recommend using the following flexible pavement design section for 
the East Welakahao Road Pavement Rehabilitation project. Detailed calculations are 
presented on Plate C-3.2 in Appendix C.

3.6 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

3.7 
Sinkhole Repair 
Based on the information provided, we understand that an old sinkhole appeared 
in the upper section of East Welakahao Road towards Piilani Highway in 2012.  The old 
sinkhole was located approximately 100 feet makai of Piilani Highway and about 4 feet 
off the north shoulder as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 3.2.  The sinkhole has been 
temporarily backfilled by the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways 
Division to reduce the potential for adverse pavement distresses and/or collapse of the 
roadway pavements.  We understand that it is desired to address the sinkhole situation 
and provide a permanent remedial solution as part of this pavement rehabilitation 
project. 
We recommend identifying the exact location of the old sinkhole in the field by 
the appropriate parties that are familiar with the situation.  The existing AC pavements 
should then be saw-cut to expose the underlying temporary backfill materials.  Since the 
temporary backfill materials might not have been placed properly, we recommend 
removing them down to the underlying native ground.  The lateral extent of the sinkhole 
may then be chased (with additional removal of the existing AC pavements and cavern 
roof, if needed) to a minimum of 5 feet beyond the pavement limits.  The resulting 
excavation should be backfilled with non-expansive select granular materials up to the 
bottom of the pavement section recommended above for the project.  In lieu of 
removing additional AC pavements and cavern roof beyond the temporary backfill area, 
consideration may be given to backfill the sinkhole beyond the temporary backfill area 
by pumping lean concrete or flowable fill under low pressure. 
Non-expansive, select granular fill materials should consist of imported materials, 
such as crushed coral or basalt. The material should be well graded from coarse to fine 
with particles no greater than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should contain 
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. In addition, the material 
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of not more 
than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. The non-expansive select 
granular fill materials should be placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to at least 
95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignment by drilling and 
sampling four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths of 
about 5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate boring locations are 
shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 and 3.2. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentation of the test results is 
provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B-2. 
Three laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D2844) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-3 through B-5.
