performed for the Hawaii Air National Guard F 22 Apron and Taxiways project within
Hickam Air Force Base on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project location and general

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity
during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of
depositional and erosional events due to glacio eustatic sea level changes and
post erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch The Koolau Volcanic
Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows
Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano alluvial soil materials
were deposited in an estuarine and deltaic environment in the area of Pearl Harbor The
sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 300 feet below and rising about
100 feet above the present sea level
During the Recent Epoch as the sea level rose to its current stand the area was
covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of soft and loose lagoonal marine
deposits Land development and reclamation projects in the past 40 to 60 years have
added man made fills bringing the site to its present configuration
Based on our research the anticipated surface soils underlying the project site
are described by the U S Soil Conservation Service in their publication Soil Survey of
Islands of Molokai and Lanai State of Hawaii 1972 A soil

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic

Liquefaction
Based on the International Building Code the project site may be subjected to
seismic activity and should be evaluated for soil liquefaction potential
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In
this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration
such as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume
causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly
enough to dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective stress

Page 9

internal strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the pore water
pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially reducing the
soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid During
liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and
vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that
liquefaction will not occur if the deposit is greater than 40 to 50 feet below the
ground surface In deeper deposits the greater overburden pressure is generally
sufficient to prevent liquefaction from occurring The major factors affecting the
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows
1
Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2
Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible
to liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to
relative density
3
Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is
directly proportional to the magnitude and duration of the
earthquake
In general the subsurface information obtained from the borings indicates that the
site is underlain by very soft and very loose lagoonal deposits The lagoonal
deposits consisted of silty sand and gravel and silt with little cohesion The
lagoonal deposits are potentially liquefiable during a seismic event of Magnitude 6
with an associated peak ground acceleration of 0 13g Based on the information
provided by the United States Geologic Survey USGS the probability of this event
occurring is once every 475 years
Based on our analyses it appears that the soft and or loose lagoonal deposits have
a factor of safety of 0 6 to 1 0 against liquefaction Therefore it is our opinion that
the apron and taxiway site could be subject to appreciable ground settlements
on the order of 3 to 6 inches in the event of liquefaction during a moderate
earthquake M6 Based on our analyses the Hush House site could be subject to

Ground Settlement
As previously mentioned the apron and taxiway areas will be raised on the order
of about 5 feet to achieve the finished pavement grades an average of about 3 feet of
fill will be required to achieve pavement subgrade and about 3 feet at the Hush House
site Because of the very loose very soft lagoonal deposits encountered at the site
ground settlements are anticipated when new fills are placed over the compressible
lagoonal deposits Based on our analyses we estimate ground settlements due to the
new fills at the apron and taxiway and Hush House sites to be on the order of about 2 to
3 inches Since the new fills would induce ground settlements at the apron and taxiway
and Hush House sites we recommend placing the new fills as soon as practical to allow
the anticipated ground settlements to occur prior to pavement construction

Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects In
general the lower the resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried
metallic structure Conversely the higher the resistivity the less likely the soil will
contribute to corrosion to metallic objects Based on our experience in the area the
subsurface materials may be classified as very corrosive for buried metallic structures
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and
structures is dependent on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in the

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths In addition the results are presented on Plates C 1 1
and C 1 2
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples obtained from the test pits to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plate C 2
Sixteen Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 including eight Hydrometer
tests ASTM D 422 were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification Graphic presentations of the
grain size distribution are provided on Plates C 3 through C 6
One Direct Shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested The test results are
presented on Plate C 7
Four Standard Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 698 were performed on bulk
samples obtained from the test pits to evaluate the relationship between the moisture
content and the dry density of the near surface soils The test results are presented on
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples obtained from the tests pit to evaluate the relationship between the moisture
content and the dry density of the near surface soils The test results are presented on
Twenty four laboratory soaked California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883
were performed on bulk soil samples obtained from the test pits to evaluate the
pavement support characteristics of the soils The bulk soil samples collected from the
test pits were remolded to obtain a relative compaction of 90 95 and 100 percent
in accordance with ASTM D 1557 at optimum moisture and at 2 percent above the
optimum moisture The test results are presented on Plates C 16 through C 39
Four Chloride Content tests were performed on selected samples
obtained from our field exploration The chloride content tests were performed by Test
America and the test results are summarized on Plate C 40
