SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration along the proposed roadway reconstruction project alignment 
generally encountered dense sands extending to the maximum depth explored of 
approximately 5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. We did not encounter 
groundwater in the shallow borings drilled at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the relatively poor conditions of the existing pavements, we recommend 
reconstructing the entire Kea Street within the limits under consideration for this project 
with a new pavement section.  Because the project will involve the reconstruction of the 
existing roadway, we have performed a detailed economic comparison involving both 
flexible and rigid pavement sections in accordance with the latest guidelines provided by 
the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing 
and Research Branch. 
Based on our field exploration program and the current design concept, we envision 
that the new pavements will be constructed over the in-situ sandy soils in the shallow 
subsurface.  Based on the anticipated subgrade conditions, the traffic data provided for our 
pavement analyses, the economic analysis performed, and construction considerations, it 
is our opinion that new pavements for the roadway reconstruction project may consist of 
2.5 inches of AC over 4 inches of ACB on 6 inches of ASB.  It should be noted that CBR 
tests and/or field observations should be performed on the actual subgrade soils during 
construction to confirm that the above pavement design section is adequate. 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface 
should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off 
the pavement to the appropriate drainage structures. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
pavement design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering analyses performed for the Kea Street Pavement Reconstruction (South 
Papa Avenue to South Wakea Avenue) project in Kahului on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. 
The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the design of new pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The roadway reconstruction project is along Kea Street from South Papa Avenue 
to South Wakea Avenue for a distance of approximately 0.6 miles. The approximate 
limits of the roadway reconstruction project are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
We understand that the proposed pavement project will be federally funded. 
Therefore, a pavement justification report conforming to the requirements and 
guidelines of the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation will be required. The 
new pavements will be designed based on the methodology described in the Pavement 
Design Manual dated March 2002, prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department of 
Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research Branch. 
Special attention was given to the potential presence of near-surface soft and/or 
loose soils, which may be a contributing factor to the pavement failures observed along 
the roadway under consideration. The findings obtained from our field exploration and 
laboratory testing programs will be used as a basis to formulate geotechnical 
engineering recommendations for the pavement reconstruction of the project roadway.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene 
Epoch).  The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located between these 
two volcanoes.  The project site is situated on the isthmus connecting the two volcanic 
domes. 
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts 
of the two volcanoes.  During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fell to stands lower than 
present sea level exposing large areas of coralline sand on what was the ocean bottom. 
Wind action transported these coralline sands inland forming extensive areas of sand 
dunes.  In some areas, these dunes have been mantled by man-made fill which was 
apparently placed during the period that the area was used for the cultivation of sugar 
cane. 
2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The existing Kea Street within the project limits, between South Papa Avenue 
and South Wakea Avenue, is generally a two-lane undivided roadway with additional 
turn lanes at the intersection areas and shoulder parking lanes. The approximate project 
limits of the roadway are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The shoulder areas beyond the edge of pavement generally consist of short cut 
grass, paved and unpaved road shoulders and emergency parking, traffic signs and 
utility posts. The surface soils along the shoulder areas generally consist of brown to 
gray soil materials such as sand, gravel, silt, clay, cinders, and cobbles in various 
mixtures. The existing Kea Street roadway alignment gently slopes down from west to 
east within the limits of the project. 
Our office conducted a site reconnaissance in February 2018 to collect general 
information for the existing pavement conditions along the portion of the roadway under 
consideration. Detailed descriptions of our field surveys are presented below. It should 
be noted that the term “Structural Defects” refers to areas where the pavement

Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, at selected locations along the roadway 
reconstruction project extending to a depth of about 5.5 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. In addition, four bulk samples of the subgrade soils, designated as 
Bulk-1 through Bulk-4, were collected for laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests.  The 
approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, our borings drilled for the roadway reconstruction project encountered 
a pavement section consisting of approximately 3 inches of AC over base course 
material consisting of dense silty basaltic gravel.  Below the base course material, the 
borings generally encountered dense sands with a little silt along the roadway alignment 
under consideration for this project. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow drilled borings at the time of our 
field exploration.  It should be noted that water level may vary with seasonal rainfall, 
time of the year, and other factors.

3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
The pavement structural section presented herein was generally determined 
based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual 
dated March 2002. The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii 
- Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research 
Branch. The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer 
method developed and used by the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans). 
The design procedures for rigid pavements are based on the design procedures of the 
Portland Cement Association “Thickness Design for Concrete Highway and Street 
Pavements.”

Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicate that the roadway under 
consideration is underlain by sandy soils with little or no cohesion.  As a result, the 
subgrade may not be able to compact into a dense mass to receive placement of the 
asphalt concrete base (ACB) or PCC layer.  Therefore, the pavement section should 
consist of an aggregate subbase (ASB) layer between the upper ACB or PCC and the 
underlying sandy subgrade soils.  The aggregate subbase will serve to provide 
confinement of the sandy soils and a compacted surface for the placement of the ACB 
or PCC.   
Due to the long length of the roadway alignment, we envision that new 
pavements for the project would need to be constructed in sections.  Because of the 
poorly-graded nature of the permeable base material, we envision that the material 
could not be compacted into a dense and stable mass.  Our experience suggested that 
the permeable base material would tend to unravel or spill out of the completed 
pavement section when excavation is made for the adjacent section.  In addition, it is 
our opinion that the granular nature of the in-situ sandy soils could be considered 
relatively permeable and would readily allow the drainage of infiltrated water without 
creating a ponding condition. Therefore, incorporation of a permeable base layer for 
drainage purpose would not be practical nor necessary from a construction and design 
standpoint and was not included in our pavement analyses.

The exposed pavement subgrade materials should be proof-rolled with a large 
vibratory drum roller (minimum 10 tons static weight) a minimum of eight passes. The 
vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and 
scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar 
to those encountered in our field exploration. 
Aggregate subbase materials required for the project should consist of crushed 
basaltic aggregates and should conform to Subsection 703.17 of the State of Hawaii, 
Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction (2005).  Aggregate subbase 
material should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers 
to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T-180 or 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity

3.5 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the test borings, bulk samples, and laboratory test data. 
Variations of conditions between and beyond the test borings and bulk samples may 
occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until 
construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to 
re-evaluate the recommendations presented herein. 
The test boring locations are approximate, having been estimated by taping from 
reference points and visible features shown on the Topographic Survey Map provided 
by the County of Maui - Department of Public Works, Engineering Division. Elevations 
of the field borings were estimated by interpolation from the spot elevations shown on 
the same plan. The physical locations and elevations of the borings should be 
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on the graphic representations of the borings 
depict the approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. These data 
have been reviewed and interpretations made to formulate this report. However, it must 
be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall, temperature, and other 
factors.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignment by drilling and 
sampling four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to a depth of 
about 5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate boring locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.
