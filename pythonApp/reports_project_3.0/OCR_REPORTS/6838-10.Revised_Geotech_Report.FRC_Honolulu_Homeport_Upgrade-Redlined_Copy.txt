REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of the 
Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of 
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of 
the eastern two-thirds of the island. It is believed that Waianae Volcano became extinct 
while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below 
Koolau lavas in Central Oahu. 
The Sand Island project site is on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano, and the 
geomorphology and subsurface conditions of the area are directly related to 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the 
genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain and its fringing reefs. The subsurface conditions

in the project vicinity are dominated by fossil Pleistocene age coral/algal reef deposits 
and related deposits laid during high stands of sea. The coral/algal reef deposits and 
related deposits are inter-layered with terrigenous sediments and late series volcanic 
deposits. The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt. 
The Waianae and Koolau Volcanic Shields were built during the late Pliocene 
and early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly-bedded basaltic lava flows. The main 
shield-building activities ceased approximately 2.5 to 3.5 million years ago. Evidence 
from drilled wells indicates that the Island of Oahu has subsided by as much as 
1,200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity (Macdonald and Abbott, 
1970). 
During that period of subsidence, coral-algal reefs began to grow on the southern 
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth. The growth of the 
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence. A series of lagoons formed 
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the 
lagoons (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred 
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers advanced, the level of the ocean fell because less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global 
sea levels rose because more water was available. The land mass of Oahu remained 
essentially stable during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. 
These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher 
and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal 
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials. The lower sea stands caused 
streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial erosion of the 
upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively 
high energy conditions within and along the streams. During periods of no significant 
sea level changes, the continued stream action extended the alluvial deltas and fans 
seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal sediments. 
The southern coast of the Island of Oahu forms a broad embayment referred to 
as Mamala Bay. At the head of this bay, the mouths of Kalihi and Nuuanu Streams 
formed an open lagoon behind the extensive fringing reefs on the seaward side of the 
island. This lagoon served as an anchorage for vessels coming to Oahu and lead to the 
settlement of Honolulu (Hawaiian: “sheltered bay”).

The area now occupied by Sand Island was formerly an extensive area of reef 
flats between the seaward margin of the fringing reef and the lagoon. The flats were 
characterized by an accumulation of detrital material broken from the hard reef front and 
softer organic material from the lagoonal area. 
Around the beginning of the 20th century, a small island was built out on the reef 
flats by using dredge spoils to expand an existing islet. This island was named 
Quarantine Island and was used to quarantine incoming passengers, livestock and 
cargo prior to debarkation onto Oahu. Later, the general area of the present US Coast 
Guard Base was filled, again with dredged material from Honolulu Harbor, to create 
additional fast land to support the quarantine operations. 
In the 1930s and 1940s, dredging of the seaplane runways in Keehi Lagoon 
created additional dredge spoils, which were used to fill the reef flat area enclosing 
Quarantine Island and other previously filled areas to form the majority of what is now 
Sand Island. The quality of the surface fill materials in the area may be considered 
variable and of poor quality in terms of supporting heavy structural loads. Throughout 
much of the area, soft compressible organic silt was entrapped between the coralline 
reef table and the overlying man-made fill. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Based on the information provided in the RFP documents, it appears the project 
site generally is underlain by surface fill materials consisting of medium dense to dense 
sands and silty sands extending to depths of about 5 to 7 feet below the existing 
ground/pavement surface. The surface fill materials generally were underlain by 
lagoonal deposits consisting of loose silty and gravelly sands extending to depths of 
about 13 to 14 feet below the existing ground/pavement surface. It should be noted that 
the lagoonal deposits encountered at the project site are compressible when subjected 
to structural loads. 
Below the compressible lagoonal deposits, coral reef formation and coralline 
detritus generally were encountered and extended to depths of about 50 feet below the 
existing ground/pavement surface. 
Groundwater was encountered at depths of about 6 to 7.3 feet below the existing 
ground/pavement surface, corresponding to about Elevation +0 feet Mean Sea Level 
(MSL). Due to the proximity of the project site to Honolulu Harbor and the Pacific 
Ocean, water levels are expected to vary with tidal fluctuations. It should be noted that 
water levels also may vary with seasonal precipitation, storm surge conditions, 
groundwater withdrawal, and other factors.

MICROPILE FOUNDATIONS 
Based on the information provided in the RFP documents, we understand 
approximately 55 micropiles will be used to support the one-story office and shop 
structure for the home porting of three vessels at the USCG facility on Sand Island. In 
accordance with Section 1810.8.4.1 of the International Building Code, 2006 Edition, 
micropiles should be considered as an alternative system where a structure is assigned 
to Seismic Design Category D. Therefore, the Building Official (United States Coast 
Guard) should be consulted for the approval in the use of micropiles as an alternative 
foundation support system for this project. 
A micropile consists of a small diameter (usually less than 12 inches) drilled and 
grouted pile with steel reinforcing. The micropile foundation typically is constructed by 
drilling a borehole, placing reinforcing steel in the hole, and grouting the borehole. 
Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed readily in access restrictive 
environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions. In addition, installation 
of the micropiles generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent structures, the 
adjacent soils, and the environment. 
Based on the availability of equipment, we envision a micropile system with a 
minimum grout bulb diameter of 7 inches should be used. We recommend designing 
each micropile based on an allowable compressive load capacity ranging from 20 to 
40 kips. The load supporting capacity of the micropile foundation would be derived 
primarily from skin friction between the micropile bonded zone and the coralline 
materials anticipated at depths starting from about 15 feet below the ground surface 
The allowable compressive load capacity for the micropiles is for supporting dead-plus-
live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as wind or 
seismic forces. 
The permanent casing should have an outside diameter (OD) of at least 7 inches 
(same as the grout bulb size), and the permanent casing should extend 3 feet into the 
coral and coralline materials. The center reinforcing steel bar should consist of a No. 9 
(Grade 75) reinforcing bar with a cross sectional area of 1.0 square inch. The following 
table provides a summary of the micropile recommendations for ease of reference.

Downdrag Loads 
In the event of seismically induced liquefaction, settlement of the compressible 
deposits will occur at the project site. When the loose soil deposits consolidate, or 
move downward relative to the micropiles, the consolidation or downward 
movement will create negative skin friction forces, generally known as downdrag 
forces, acting on the micropile shafts. The magnitude of the downdrag force 
depends on the thickness of the liquefiable layer, relative density of the potentially 
liquefiable deposits, and the micropile size. 
In addition, placement of fill materials required to achieve design grades also may 
induce settlement of loose compressible deposits. The ground settlement would 
result in downdrag forces acting on the micropile shaft supporting the new 
structures. 
Therefore, the allowable compressive load capacities of the micropiles have been 
reduced by the expected downdrag loads in the pile foundation analyses. Based on 
our foundation analyses, we estimate the downdrag load imposed on the micropiles 
due to settlement of the lagoonal deposits may be on the order of about 15 kips for 
the 7-inch diameter micropiles. 
Lateral Load Resistance 
It should be noted that the micropile foundation system provides low lateral load 
resistance due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile. Therefore, the 
lateral load resistance contributed by the vertical micropiles should be neglected. 
Lateral loads imposed on the new structure foundations may be resisted primarily 
by passive pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the micropile cap and 
grade beams and by shear acting along the sides of the micropile caps and grade 
beams. 
Passive pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the micropile cap and 
grade beams may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 and 
150 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf) for above and below 
groundwater conditions, respectively. The static groundwater level at the project 
site may be assumed at about Elevation +3 feet MSL for design purposes. 
Additional resistance to lateral loads may be provided by shear resistance between 
the sides of the micropile caps and grade beams and the adjacent soils. A side 
shear resistance of up to 150 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for 
transient loads such as wind or seismic forces only.

adequate time to cure. Two additional micropiles may be used for reaction during 
the compressive load testing of the pre-production load test micropile. The reaction 
micropiles may be installed to depths as deep as the load test micropile to provide 
adequate reaction in uplift (to be determined by the contractor). 
The load test micropile should be loaded gradually to at least 250 percent of the 
allowable design load in compression. We recommend holding the maximum test 
load (250 percent of the design load) for a minimum of 4 to 8 hours depending on 
the recorded movements of the load test micropile. The pre-production load test is 
an integral part of the design of the micropile foundation system. Therefore, we 
recommend a Geolabs representative observe the pre-production load test. 
In addition to the pre-production load test, we also recommend performing pullout 
tests (proof tests) on selected micropiles during construction to confirm the load 
carrying capacity of the installed micropiles. We recommend testing a minimum 
10 percent of the total number of micropiles (or minimum of two micropiles) for 
pullout. The pullout tests should consist of subjecting the micropile to at least 
150 percent of the design load. The micropile should be loaded in 12.5% design 
load increments, and each load should be held for at least 5 minutes. The 
maximum test load should be held for a minimum of 10 or 60 minutes. Pullout test 
on the selected micropiles is an integral part of the design of the micropile 
foundation system. Therefore, we recommend conducting the pullout tests under 
the observation of a Geolabs representative.

We anticipate the floor slabs for the proposed MAT building will consist of 
reinforced concrete slabs-on-grade, which is common for similar type construction in the 
area. Based on the existing topography and the anticipated finished floor elevation, we 
envision that the slab-on-grade for the proposed structure MAT Building will be

appreciable ground settlements in the event of liquefaction caused by a moderate 
seismic event (M6+). Seismically induced ground settlements generally on the order of

Based on the dense surface fill soils encountered at the project site, we believe 
shallow continuous strip footings may be used for support of low retaining walls 
planned for the project. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 psf may be

one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches. In addition, wall footings 
on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below 
the lowest adjacent finished grade. In addition, embedment of the shallow footings 
should be limited to a maximum of 30 inches below the lowest adjacent grade due 
to the relatively thin surface fill crust. In addition, the bottom of footing elevation for 
the shallow foundations should be placed at or above +3 feet MSL. 
The bottom of footing excavations should be re-compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing 
surface prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete. Soft and/or loose 
materials encountered at the bottom of footing excavations should be 
over-excavated to expose the underlying firm and/or dense materials. The 
over-excavation may be backfilled with on-site soils compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction, or the footing bottom may be extended down to 
bear directly on the underlying competent materials. 
Lateral loads acting on the retaining structure may be resisted by friction developed 
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth 
pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A 
coefficient of friction of 0.40 may be used for footings bearing on the compacted 
on-site fill materials. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated 
using equivalent fluid pressures of 350 and 150 pcf for above and below 
groundwater conditions, respectively. This assumes that the soils around the 
footings are well compacted. The passive pressure resistance should be reduced 
for foundations located on slopes. Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the 
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected.

In general, we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes for uniform support.

gradation), also should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above

critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids 
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes.

DRAINAGE 
Finished grades outside the new building should be sloped to shed water away 
from the slabs and foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding around the 
structure. In addition, it is advised to install roof gutter systems around the building and 
to divert the discharge away from the slab and foundation areas. Excessive landscape 
watering near the slabs and foundations also should be avoided. Planters next to 
foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the 
potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
above foundation recommendations because ponded water could cause subsurface soil 
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength. The foundation excavations 
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of 
the concrete to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface. 
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to 
drain surface water runoff away from the slabs and foundations.
