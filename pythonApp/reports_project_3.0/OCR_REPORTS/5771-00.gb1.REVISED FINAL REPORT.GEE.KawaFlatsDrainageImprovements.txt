PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project involves drainage improvements to allow under road drainage for
storm runoff and prevent flow over the roadway at a portion of Hawaii State Road 11
near Milepost 58 7 within Ka u on the Island of Hawaii The overall project is shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
Work will involve vertical realignment along approximately 1 500 feet of the
roadway which may involve raising the highway at least 5 to 10 feet We understand
that it is proposed to construct two new concrete culvert structures along the roadway

Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and

Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and

Site Preparation

cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris pavements deleterious

disposed properly off site to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated
materials to be used as embankment fill materials The contractor should exercise
caution while clearing and grading near collapsed lava tube features and or
cavities which may represent potential lava tubes or cavities that may further
collapse under the load of heavy construction equipment

Fills and Backfills
In general we recommend capping the fill areas of the project site with a 2 foot
thick layer of structural fill materials Structural fill materials should consist of
well graded granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension
with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass In
addition structural fill materials should contain less than 30 percent particles
passing the No 200 sieve by weight In general the excavated on site materials
may be reused as a source of structural fill materials provided that they are
processed to meet the maximum particle size and gradation requirements
presented above
Fill and backfill materials below the 2 foot thick structural fill layer may consist of
general fill materials General fill and backfill materials should consist of well graded
granular materials the majority of which are less than 12 inches with an absolute
maximum dimension of 18 inches Materials between 12 and 18 inches in particle
size should be limited to about 15 percent or less of the total volume of general fill
materials The excavated on site materials if less than 18 inches in maximum
dimension may be used as a source of general fill or backfill materials except for
trench backfill provided that they are processed to meet the above gradation
requirements for general fill If the excavated materials do not contain sufficient
fines to produce a well graded material off site borrow or on site rock crushing of
larger rock fragments or boulders should be considered to provide the required
gradation and particle sizes to develop a well graded material
The maximum particle size of the fills and backfills should be limited to a maximum
of 3 inches for fills and backfills placed in confined locations Confined locations are
defined as areas where the compaction equipment is limited in size to less than
2 tons in static weight In addition if the contractor elects to use smaller sized
compaction equipment less than 2 tons in static weight the maximum particle size
of the fill and backfill materials should be limited to 3 inches in dimension

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills
should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 18 inches in loose thickness
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or
other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of
boulders and cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of passes
by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a
Geolabs representative Based on this testing program the number of passes then
may be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction For preliminary
estimating purposes compaction may be accomplished by using a large vibratory
drum roller having a static weight of at least 10 tons traveling at a speed of about
300 feet per minute 3½ miles per hour The roller should pass over the fill surface
at least eight times for structural and general fills
Cut and Fill Slopes
In general we envision cut slopes would expose dense basalt formations In
general we believe the cut slopes planned at the site may be designed with a slope
inclination of one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V or flatter In areas where
volcanic ash or highly fractured basalt are exposed at the cut slope faces the cut
slopes may need to be flattened to inclinations of 2H 1V or flatter Therefore
provisions to flatten the cut slopes should be considered in the project design A
Geolabs representative should observe and map the cut slopes during construction
to evaluate whether the exposed cut slope materials are consistent with our
assumptions
In general permanent fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V
or flatter This assumes that the fill slopes will be constructed using well graded

Excavation
The project site is generally underlain by a thin layer of fill and volcanic ash over
basalt formation It is anticipated that the fill volcanic ash and fractured basalt
formation may be readily excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such
as excavators and also by ripping with large bulldozers However some of the
deeper excavations in the basalt rock likely will require the use of hoerams or
chipping
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based
on our visual observation of the existing basalt formation and field data from the
borings Contractors should be encouraged to examine the site conditions and the
subsurface data to make their own reasonable and prudent interpretation
Box Culverts

hard to very hard basalt rock formation were encountered in the borings drilled at the site

encountered at the bottom of footing elevations to expose the underlying basalt rock
formation

Foundations
It is generally difficult to provide a smooth level bearing surface when the bearing
surface is composed of basalt formation Therefore we anticipate that the
excavated surfaces on the basalt formation will likely be irregular and
over excavated in localized areas Due to concerns regarding the potential for
scour we recommend avoiding placement of fill materials to fill in the irregular and
over excavated surfaces When over excavation of the basalt formation occurs the
subgrade surfaces should be leveled and or backfilled to the bottom of the box
culvert structure using concrete or lean concrete
We recommend using foundation bearing values of at least 13 500 and
30 000 pounds per square foot psf to evaluate the foundation bearing conditions
under the strength limit and extreme event limit states respectively
In general foundations should be embedded a minimum of 2 feet below the lowest
adjacent finished grades Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should
be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench or they should extend to a depth as deep as the
inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the
potential for appreciable foundation settlement
Based on a service limit state bearing pressure of we estimate that
foundation settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings bearing
directly on the basalt rock formation as recommended herein to be less than ½ inch
with differential settlements on the order of ¼ inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system
Coefficients of friction of 0 60 and 0 48 may be used for footings bearing on the
basalt rock for the extreme event and strength limit states respectively
Resistance due to passive pressure for footings embedded in the basalt rock
formation may be estimated using a rectangular pressure distribution of
for the extreme event limit state Passive pressure with a rectangular pressure
distribution of may be used for the strength limit state of footings
embedded in the basalt rock formation
Additional resistance to lateral loads may be provided by shear resistance

resistance of the footings should not be used for the strength and or service limit

Foundation Probing
Based on our exploration and experience in the area voids and or cavities appear
to be present in the basaltic lava flows at the project site To reduce the potential
for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities below foundations
we recommend implementing a program of cavity probing and grouting of the
foundations during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend drilling a probe hole for every 100 square
feet of foundation area or at 10 feet on center along the foundations The probe
holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend at least 10 feet
below the planned bottom of footing elevation If cavities and or voids are
encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probes should
be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the vertical and lateral extent of the
cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled
with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures We recommend
utilizing a low strength sand cement grout mixture with a slump range of 6 to
9 inches for grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that the probe drill be available on site until the probing and grouting
operations are complete It should be noted that a longer lag time between
probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in the
construction schedule
A Geolabs representative should observe the probing and grouting program This
would allow our firm to monitor the presence of cavities and or voids and to allow
for additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or changed
conditions are observed

Dynamic Lateral Earth Forces
Because the project site is on the seismically active Island of Hawaii forces due
to dynamic lateral earth pressures will need to be considered in the design of
retaining structures The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures associated
with seismic loading may be estimated using 56H2 pounds per foot of
wall length for level backfill conditions where H is the height of the wall in feet
The dynamic lateral earth force would generally act at the mid height of the wall
The dynamic lateral earth forces provided assume that the wall will be allowed to
move laterally by about 14 inches in the event of an earthquake The force due to
the dynamic lateral earth pressures presented above is in addition to the static
lateral earth pressures An appropriately reduced factor of safety may be used
when dynamic lateral earth pressures are accounted for in the design of the
retaining structure
Drainage
In general retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the potential for
build up of hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a
12 inch wide zone of permeable material such as open graded gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation placed directly around a perforated pipe
perforations down at the base of the retaining wall The perforated pipe should
discharge to an appropriate outlet or weepholes
As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or
EnkaDrain may be used instead of the permeable drainage material The
prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a
perforated pipe at the base of the retaining wall Unless covered by concrete or
asphaltic concrete the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively
impervious materials such as well graded soils to reduce the potential for
significant water infiltration behind the walls
Alternate Retaining Wall System
Because of the high seismic loading conventional retaining structures may not be
feasible An alternate wall system consisting of a reinforced concrete wall with Controlled
Low Strength Material CLSM backfill may be considered We believe that CLSM backfill
is self supporting materials Therefore lateral earth pressure static and dynamic will not
act on the wing wall structure
The embankment fill behind the wing walls should be sloped back an inclination of
two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V and the area between the new wall and slope
should be backfilled with CLSM To reduce the hydrostatic pressure exerted on the wing
wall from wet CLSM during placement we recommend placing the CLSM backfill in
maximum lifts of 4 feet The CLSM should be allowed to cure prior to placing the
subsequent lift of CLSM In addition roughening of the CLSM surface should be
