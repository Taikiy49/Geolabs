INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Replacement of Waimanalo Homes HA 1 25 project located
along Huminiki Street in the Waimanalo area on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended for the design of house lot grading foundations
and pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this report

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project site is located along Humuniki Street in the Waimanalo area
on the island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the information provided it is proposed to
demolish the 50 existing homes and construct 50 new housing units in the existing lots
The proposed project also includes construction of a new drainage system sewer
laterals and new driveways for each lot
We understand that the new housing units will consist of one to two story homes
with slab on grade construction Therefore we envision that the new housing units will
generally be lightly loaded structures In addition since the site has been previously
graded we anticipate that only minor grading work to provide positive drainage would be
needed for the proposed construction
Based on our field observations it was noted that the yard areas of each individual
lot at the site were generally covered with grass at the time of our field exploration Some
ground cracks up to about 1 inch wide were noted in the yards during our field
exploration In addition minor cracks were also noted on the asphalt driveways and
roadways Based on conversations with some residents the ground cracks in the yards
were reported to be as wide as 4 to 5 inches in the dry season especially if sufficient
landscape water was not provided The cracks generally penetrate relatively deep into
the ground Usually the ground cracks would close up or get smaller if the near surface
soils were wetted
Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project site we believe that the
proposed project site is underlain by highly expansive clayey soils Therefore special
attention was given to the shrink swell characteristics of the near surface soils and their
effects on the proposed new construction

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which erosion incised
deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows
followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu
Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
GEOLABS HAWAIi
W O 3605 00 Page 6
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of
the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy
conditions The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments at correspondingly higher elevations in areas protected by fringing
reefs and beach strands During periods of no significant sea level changes continued
stream action extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward
The project site is underlain by alluvial soil deposits Agricultural land development
and other projects within the last century have brought the project site to its present form

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the subject site were explored by drilling and
sampling four borings to depths of about 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface
The borings were drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general alluvial soils consisting of stiff to very stiff silty clays and clayey silts
were encountered from the ground surface to depths of about 18 to 22 5 feet below the
existing ground surface in Boring Nos 1 and 2 The silty clays and clayey silts were
underlain by medium dense silty gravels extending to the maximum depth drilled of about
26 5 feet below the ground surface In Boring Nos 3 and 4 alluvial soils consisting of stiff
to very stiff silty clays and clayey silts were encountered from the surface to the maximum
depth explored of about 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface The stiff to very stiff
silty clays and clayey silts may be classified as highly expansive soils when subjected to
moisture fluctuations
Groundwater was encountered in the borings at depths of about 7 to 12 feet below
the ground surface at the time of our field exploration However it should be noted that
groundwater levels measured in the borings during our field exploration will likely be
affected by seasonal rainfall storm surge conditions groundwater withdrawal and or
injection tidal fluctuations and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our laboratory tests performed on the near surface clayey
soils the near surface materials may be classified as highly plastic clayey soils and
exhibit a very high potential for volume change shrink or swell when subjected to
moisture fluctuations As a result of the highly expansive nature of the on site soils
shrinking of the near surface soils occurred as soil moisture evaporated from the soil
system under the relatively dry climatic conditions The shrinkage cracks would close up
to certain degree upon wetting as the soils expand during periods of wet weather or
heavy rain storms However shrinkage of the on site soils and subsequent cracking of
the ground surface will recur when the dry season returns This phenomenon is seasonal
and is dependent on the weather conditions such as prolonged wet weather followed by
prolonged dry weather
Large ground cracks are potential hazards to the residents and building structures
Therefore proper measures should be taken to reduce the potential effects of the
recurring ground cracks at the site In order to reduce the potential for the ground cracks
from evolving into a detrimental problem we recommend that proper grading
recommendations followed by maintenance of the yard by irrigation be maintained to
keep the soil moisture content relatively constant thereby reducing the shrink swell
behavior of the on site clayey soils due to moisture fluctuations In addition positive
drainage should be provided to avoid ponding of surface water which would accentuate
the shrink swell behavior of the near surface soils
Due to the highly expansive soils encountered at the site we recommend that
select granular fill to reduce the effects of shrink swell of the near surface expansive soils

House Lot Grading
As previously discussed the near surface soils at the site exhibit a high
shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations and would result in large
ground cracks if left unattended no irrigation In order to reduce the potential for ground
cracks to occur or develop the following recommendations may be considered in an
attempt to maintain the soil moisture content relatively constant thereby reducing the
shrink swell behavior of the on site clayey soils due to moisture fluctuations
1 Based on our experience landscaping along with proper irrigation would
serve to maintain the soil moisture content relatively constant due to the
controlled regular irrigation Therefore we believe that the yard areas of
the house lots may be graded with on site soils followed by landscaping
The top 12 inches below the finish grade should be moisture conditioned to
near the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3605 00 Page 10
In addition irrigation of the landscaped areas should be maintained on a
daily basis to keep the near surface soil moisture content relatively
constant The amount of irrigation water should be adjusted depending on
the climatic conditions In the dry season additional efforts in watering the
yard may be necessary to keep the soil consistently moist
2 Alternatively the yard area may be capped with a minimum 8 inch layer of
non expansive or low expansion capping soils The 8 inch capping fill
would serve as a moisture barrier to maintain the moisture content of the
underlying highly expansive soils relatively constant The capping fill should
consist of low expansion silty or clayey soils with low permeability It should
be noted that the recommended capping fill for the yard areas should not
consist of relatively permeable materials such as the select granular fill
below the slabs The capping fill should be moisture conditioned to near
the optinum moisture content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative
compaction Prior to placement of the capping fill the on site soil subgrade
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The yard area
should also be landscaped as soon as the grading work is
completed Even with the use of an 8 inch capping fill layer constant
watering of the yard areas would be needed to maintain the soil moisture
content relatively constant
3 A third alternative to reduce the effects of shrink swell behavior of the near
surface soils is to mix the near surface soils with fly ash moisture condition
and recompact There has been a relatively substantial amount of research
done on the use of fly ash to reduce the shrink swell behavior of highly
plastic clayey soils and the results of the research has been favorable on
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3605 00 Page 11
reducing the shrink swell effects on plastic clays The normal proportions of
fly ash to soil has been between 15 to 25 percent by weight If this
alternative is desired additional laboratory tests to evaluate the proportions
and workability of the fly ash mixture will be necessary to determine the
proportions and specifications for construction
It should be noted that it will not be possible to completely eliminate the ground
cracks due to the nature of the highly expansive soils encountered at the site The
recommendations provided above are intended only to reduce the potential for recurring
of large ground cracks
In general all subgrades in the yard areas to receive fill should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction The finish grades should
be sloped with a minimum of 1 5 to 2 percent grade to carry all surface water off the site
and to reduce the potential for ponding of water
Generally the on site soils may be used as a source of general fill and backfill
However the on site clayey soils should not be used as non expansive capping fill due to
the expansive nature of the material Where used as general fill and backfill material the
on site soils should be properly moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to 90 percent relative compaction
We envision that import fill material will be needed for the proposed project The
imported material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for its intended use prior to
delivery to the project site Imported fills should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction

Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site we believe that a
shallow foundation system consisting of shallow spread and or continuous footings
bearing on the near surface stiff to very stiff clays and or compacted fills may be used to
support the proposed buildings An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per
square foot p s f may be used for the design of footings bearing on the near surface stiff
to very stiff soils and or compacted fills This allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus
live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as temporary
wind and or seismic forces Footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches
below the lowest adjacent finished grade for the building foundations
Footing subgrades should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction If
loose and or soft soils are observed at the footing subgrade level they should be over
excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting over excavation should be backfilled
with compacted engineered fill The footing subgrade should be kept moist prior to
concrete placement If shrinkage cracks are noted at the footing subgrade the footing
subgrade should be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks Otherwise over
excavation of the footing subgrade and replacement with compacted fills may be
necessary
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3605 00 Page 13
Where footings are to be located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities the
footings should extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane projected upward
from the bottom of the below grade structure or utility This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
If the foundation is designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total footing settlements under the anticipated design loads are estimated to be on
the order of less than 1 inch with differential settlements of approximately inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the footing and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure
developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings A
coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used between the base of the footings and the near
surface clayey soils and or compacted fills Resistance due to passive pressure may be
calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of
depth p c f for footings embedded in the clayey soils These values assume that the
concrete for footings is placed directly against the footing excavations Unless covered
by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished
grade should be neglected
We recommend that footing excavations and subgrade preparation be observed
by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or
concrete to confirm the bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Slabs On Grade
We understand that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed
buildings The near surface soils encountered at the site exhibit high expansion potential
when subjected to fluctuations in the moisture content Therefore to reduce the potential
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3605 00 Page 14
of structural distress to lightly loaded slab on grade construction resulting from swelling of
the on site expansive soils we recommend that a minimum of 24 inches of
non expansive select granular fill material be provided below slabs on grade The
non expansive select fill should extend a minimum of 2 feet laterally beyond the limits of
the building structures
Prior to the filling operation the subgrade to receive the 24 inch non expansive
select fill should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
The 24 inch non expansive select fill recommended below the floor slabs should
consist of imported granular material such as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder sand
The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles
passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory California Bearing
Ratio CBR value of 15 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 All select fill materials should be
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction The slab subgrade should be kept moist until covered by cushion layer and
concrete slabs

For more uniform support of interior slabs we recommend that concrete slabs on
grade be supported on a minimum 4 inch cushion fill The cushion fill layer should consist
of open graded gravels such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
which will also serve as a capillary moisture break The slab cushion fill layer should be
placed as soon as possible after preparation of the subgrade soils
For slabs on grade with moisture sensitive surfacing we recommend that an
impermeable membrane be placed over the cushion fill to reduce potential migration of
moisture vapor through the concrete slab In order to promote more uniform curing of the
slab concrete and to provide protection of the vapor membrane we recommend that a
2 inch layer of moist fine sand be placed above the membrane prior to placing the slab
concrete The sand should be moistened slightly prior to placing concrete
Where slabs on grade will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the garage
slab a 6 inch layer of compacted base course is recommended for more uniform support
in lieu of the 4 inch cushion fill and 2 inch sand The base course should consist of
crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
For design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per square
inch per inch of deflection p c i may be used for the compacted base course Provi
sions should be made for proper load transfer across the slab joints which are subjected
to vehicular traffic The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any unpaved area should
be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
lf exterior concrete walkways are planned for the proposed project we recommend
that a minimum 12 inch thick layer of select granular fill compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction be provided below the concrete walkway slab for more
uniform support and to reduce the potential effects of the highly expansive near surface

Pavements
Improvements to the existing roadways are not planned for this project However
we understand that new flexible pavements are planned for the driveways leading to the
residences at the proposed project site We anticipate the vehicle loading for this project
will consist primarily of passenger vehicles and light trucks Based on the results of our
laboratory CBR tests the near surface soils at the site have CBR values ranging from
about 4 to 13 6 with swells of about 1 8 to 3 3 percent Therefore we have made our
preliminary pavement design assuming the pavement subgrade will consist of the in situ
stiff to very stiff clayey soils with a minimum CBR value of 4 and a swell of 3 3 percent or
less Based on the above assumptions we recommend that the following pavement
section be used for preliminary design purposes
Asphaltic Concrete Pavement
2 0 Inch Asphalt Concrete
6 0 Inch Basalt Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
12 0 Inch Basalt Select Borrow 95 Percent Relative Compaction
20 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on a Moist Compacted Subgrade
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The base course and select borrow
material should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction

top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site clayey soils or select
granular material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in maximum 68 inch level loose lifts and mechanically

Drainage
As previously indicated positive drainage should be provided to avoid ponding of
surface water which will accentuate the shrink swell behavior of the on site near surface
clayey soils The finished grades at each lot should be sloped at a minimum of 1 5 to
2 percent to effectively carry away all surface water and to reduce the potential for
ponding Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to
drain all surface run off away from the site Excessive landscape watering near the
foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation and pavement recommendations since ponded water could cause
shrink swell of the near surface clayey soils and foundation saturation In addition the
foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately
after setting of the concrete to reduce water infiltration

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling four
borings to depth of about 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate
locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck
mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Eight one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded soil samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The
test results are summarized on Plate B 1
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the near surface soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits Graphic presentation of
the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Four California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics and
swelling behavior of the near surface soils Results of the CBR tests are presented on
Plates B 3 through B 6
Two Modified Proctor tests ASTM D 1557 91 were performed on bulk samples of
the near surface soils to evaluate the maximum dry density and optimum moisture
content The test results are presented on Plates B 7 and B 8
