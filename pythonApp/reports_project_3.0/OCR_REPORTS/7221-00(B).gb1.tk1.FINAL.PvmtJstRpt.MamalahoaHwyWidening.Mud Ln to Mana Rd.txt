In general, the borings drilled between Mud Lane and Hohola Drive encountered 
about 7 to 11 inches of asphaltic concrete over 12 to 34 inches of dense to very dense 
sand and gravel fill material underlain by volcanic ash consisting of medium stiff to stiff 
clayey silt extending to the maximum depths explored.  Several borings encountered 
medium dense to dense clinker at depths of about 2.5 to 5 feet below the existing 
pavement surface.  
The borings drilled between Puu Nani Drive and Mana Road generally 
encountered asphaltic concrete ranging in thickness from about 9 to 16 inches over fill 
material consisting of dense to very dense sandy gravel about 3 to 18 inches thick.  The 
fill material was underlain by volcanic ash consisting of medium stiff to stiff clayey silt 
extending to the maximum depths explored.  We did not encounter groundwater in the 
shallow drilled borings at the time of our field exploration.  
Since the project will involve mainly widening of the existing roadway around the 
intersection areas and the existing pavement consists of a flexible pavement section, 
we recommend using a flexible pavement structural section for the new pavement. 
Since the existing pavement does not have a drainage layer, incorporating a drainage 
layer below the new pavement at the intersection areas would not be feasible nor 
practical.  Therefore, we recommend using a flexible pavement section without a 
drainage layer where the new pavement is being constructed for widening the roadway 
only. 
Due to the poor condition of the existing pavement between the western project 
limit and Mokuloa Drive, we understand that it is planned to reconstruct the pavement in 
this area.  This area is generally located in a low area; therefore, the potential for 
ponding of surface water runoff is relatively high.  In addition, there appears to be 
inadequate drainage to dispose of surface water runoff in this area.  To facilitate 
drainage in this area, we recommend incorporating a drainage layer into the new 
flexible pavement section.  In addition, a drainage system should be considered to 
dispose of surface water runoff that collects in this area.  
We anticipate that volcanic ash with relatively high moisture content will be 
encountered at or near the pavement subgrade.  It may be difficult to achieve the 
normal 95 percent compaction requirement of the subgrade if volcanic ash is 
encountered at or near the subgrade.  Therefore, we recommend placing a reinforcing 
geogrid (such as Tensar TriAx Grid, TX7 or equivalent) on a layer of permeable

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago with an area 
of approximately 4,000 square miles.  This island was formed by the activity of five shield 
volcanoes:  Kohala, which has long been extinct; Mauna Kea, which has had activity 
during recent geologic time; Hualalai, which last erupted in 1801; and Mauna Loa and 
Kilauea, both of which are still active. 
The project site is located on the northern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield 
Volcano, which has been built up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and 
pyroclastic materials.  These lava flows are believed to be late Pleistocene in age and 
belong to the Hamakua Volcanic Series.  Cavities are commonly encountered in these 
lava flows.  Cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and represent 
both lava tubes (intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities).  Lava 
tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow 
tube-like structure, which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow.  
Blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities) are generally smaller in horizontal extent. 
2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The proposed project involves improvements to Mamalahoa Highway between 
Mud Lane and Mana Road in South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii.  The project limits are 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Mamalahoa Highway is generally a two-lane roadway consisting of flexible 
pavement.  The terrain along the project limits varies from fairly level along the existing 
roadway to sloping along the roadway embankment slopes. The existing pavement 
generally appeared to be in relatively fair to good condition at the time of our field 
exploration. However, the existing pavement between the western project limit and 
Mokuloa Drive appeared to be in relatively poor condition.  Distresses such as rutting and 
alligator cracking were observed at the pavement surface. The severity of the alligator 
cracking appeared to be moderate to high.  Small potholes were forming in areas with 
alligator cracking of high severity.  This area is super elevated and appeared to be lower in

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions along the roadway were explored by drilling and 
sampling twenty-eight borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 28, extending to 
depths of about 4.5 to 6.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. In addition, five 
bulk samples of the anticipated subgrade soils, designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-5, 
were collected for laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests.  The approximate boring and 
bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.11. 
In general, the borings drilled between Mud Lane and Hohola Drive encountered 
about 7 to 11 inches of asphaltic concrete over about 12 to 34 inches of fill material 
consisting of dense to very dense sand and gravel.   The fill material was underlain by 
medium stiff to stiff volcanic ash consisting of clayey silt extending to the maximum 
depths explored.  Medium dense to dense clinker was encountered in several of the 
borings at depths of about 2.5 to 5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  
The borings drilled between Puu Nani Drive and Mana Road, where moderately 
to highly distressed pavements were observed, generally encountered asphaltic 
concrete ranging in thickness from about 9 to 16 inches.  Below the asphaltic concrete 
layer, fill material consisting of dense to very dense sandy gravel about 3 to 18 inches 
thick was encountered in the borings.  The fill material was underlain by volcanic ash 
consisting of medium stiff to stiff clayey silt extending to the maximum depths explored.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the current design concept and guidelines, new pavements will be 
constructed for the road widening project.  Because the project will involve mainly 
widening of the existing roadway around the intersection areas and the existing 
pavement consists of a flexible pavement section, we recommend using a flexible 
pavement structural section for the new pavements; therefore, a detailed economic 
comparison involving a flexible pavement section and a rigid pavement section will not 
be required for the proposed widening project.   
The existing pavement between the western project limit and Mokuloa Drive is in 
relatively poor condition.  This area is generally located in a low area; therefore, the 
potential for ponding of surface water runoff is relatively high.  In addition, there appears 
to be inadequate drainage to dispose of surface water runoff in this area.  The water 
ponding in this area likely infiltrates into the subsurface and softens the soils below the 
pavement structure, resulting in the various pavement distresses observed.  We 
recommend reconstructing the pavement in this area with new flexible pavement with a 
drainage layer to facilitate drainage.  In addition, a drainage system should be 
considered to dispose of surface water runoff that collects in this area. In general, the 
following sections provide discussion on the areas necessary for pavement analyses 
and design.

To maximize the benefits of the triaxial geogrid, we understand that aggregate 
base course with nominal maximum size of 1.5 inches should be used in lieu of the 
aggregate subbase. In general, the triaxial geogrid will interlock with the aggregate base 
course, resulting in two benefits during initial construction and for the life of the project: 
1) lateral confinement – increasing the modulus of the aggregate base course, and 
2) subgrade bearing capacity enhancement.  Aggregate base course should conform to 
Subsection 703.06 of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge 
Construction, 2005 (HSS).  Aggregate base material should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with AASHTO T-180 or ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D2041. Asphaltic concrete 
(AC) material should be constructed in accordance with Section 401 Asphalt Concrete 
Pavement of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge 
Construction (2005) and its amendments.

confirm whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to those encountered in our field

to saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. It should be noted that the

clayey subgrade soils are susceptible to softening and loss of strength upon saturation. 
Therefore, special attention should be given to the surface and subsurface drainage of 
the pavements. As a minimum, the pavement surface should be sloped and drainage

drainage structures. Surface water ponding should not be allowed on site during or after

separator (non-woven filter fabric) conforming to Section 648 of the HSS should be

fines into the drainage material, as shown on Plate 4. In addition, a permeable 
separator should also be placed between the untreated permeable base and aggregate 
base. Care should be exercised when placing the base and/or constructing the surface 
course to avoid damaging the edge drain collector pipe. A typical edge drain detail is 
provided on Plate 4 for reference. The edge drain should be daylighted into appropriate 
drainage structures for proper discharge of infiltrated water.

The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the test borings, bulk samples, and laboratory test data. 
Variations of conditions between and beyond the test borings and bulk samples may

December 22, 2015. The physical locations and elevations of the borings should be 
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used.

gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. These data 
have been reviewed and interpretations made to formulate this report. However, it must 
be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall, temperature, and other 
factors.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignment by drilling and 
sampling twenty-eight borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 28. The borings 
extended to depths of about 4.5 to 6.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.11. The 
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-28. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are presented on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136), including two Hydrometer 
tests (ASTM D422), were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation 
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification.  Graphic presentations of the 
grain size distribution are provided on Plate B-2. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the shear strength parameters. The test results are presented on Plates B-3 
and B-4. 
Five laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D2844) by Ninyo & Moore 
were performed on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement 
support characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-5 through 
B-9.
