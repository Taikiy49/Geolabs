REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
of middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield volcano Koolau is estimated to be of late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Ice Age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became
extinct while Koolau was still active After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series
7 fall by cind tuft i Tk
the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are then transported down
slope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3777 00 Page 5
reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant
and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream
flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this transport
of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a
poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional
base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the information obtained from our field exploration and laboratory
testing the proposed new residence site is underlain by undocumented fills from the
surface to a depth of approximately 2 to 3 feet below the existing ground surface The
near surface undocumented fills consist of a silty clay soil which contains some debris
that can cause variability in the strength characteristics and compressibility of the soil
Therefore we recommend that the footprint of the proposed new residential structure and
other improvements such as the concrete driveway and walkways be over excavated to
expose stiff native ground and be backfilled with engineered fill In addition we
recommend that the lateral limits of the over excavation and backfilling extend a minimum
of 5 feet beyond the limits of the structure and a minimum of 2 feet beyond the limits of
any improvements

Site Grading section of this report
Our laboratory test results indicate that the near surface soils exhibit a slight to

The over excavated subgrade should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the
subgrade we recommend that the soil be re scarified moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum and recompacted to the specified requirements
The on site fill material generated from the over excavation may be reused as
backfill and fill materials up to the bottom of the non expansive select granular fill layer
provided that it be screened to remove all debris deleterious material and particles
moderate expansion potential of the on site soils the on site soils should be thoroughly
moisture conditioned prior to use as engineered fill Engineered fills on site or imported
should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction

Foundations
The proposed new residence will consist of a 2 story residential structure Based
on the subsurface conditions encountered in our exploration we believe that a shallow
foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used to
support the proposed structure An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per
square foot p s f may be used for the design of footings bearing on the new engineered
fills recommended in the Site Grading section of this report or on undisturbed native
ground This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third
for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
GEOLABS HAWAII

For footings bearing on the new engineered fills the bottom of the footing
excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior
to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete If soft and or loose materials are
encountered at the bottom of footing excavations they should be over excavated to
expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation should be backfilled with
select material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing
bottom may be extended down to the underlying competent material
All footing excavations should be thoroughly moistened and kept moist until
placement of concrete If shrinkage cracks appear on the subgrade soils prior to
placement of concrete the subgrade soils should be moistened to close all cracks and
recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction

For more uniform support of interior slabs we recommend that the concrete

Utility Trench
New utility connections may be required for the proposed new residence A
granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the trench
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the
pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of
for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for
the sides of the pipes Improper backfill material and placement could result in settlement
of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site excavated materials The
backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in
maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches
are below areas subjected to vehicular traffic the upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below
the subgrade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the new structure should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that
gutter systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away

from the foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs
should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete
