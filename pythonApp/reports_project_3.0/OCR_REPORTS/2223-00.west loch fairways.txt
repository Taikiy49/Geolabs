INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the subject project Previously we had performed a subsurface exploration
for the West Loch Estates Subdivision some of which encompassed the areas of the
Proposed West Loch Fairways development The results of these findings are presented
in our Geotechnical Engineering Report Proposed Increment Housing and Proposed
Golf Course West Loch Estates Development W O 1889 10 report dated December
18 1987 Where findings from our previous exploration are relevant to the current
project they are incorporated into this report
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2223 00 Page 2
The purpose of our exploration was to provide subsurface information within the
proposed development site to aid in the formulation of design recommendations for
foundations site grading infrastructure and preliminary pavement design
The scope of our exploration included but was not limited to
1 review and compilation of the available soil and geologic data pertinent to
the general project area and its vicinity
2 field reconnaissance to develop an overview of site surface conditions as
they relate to future proposed grading as well as determining locations for
selective borings and soils sampling
3 drilling and sampling of 16 borings to depths between 10 5 and 22 0 feet
below the existing ground surface
4 drilling and probing of 3 borings to depths between 31 5 and 37 5 feet to
further define the extent of soft subsoils
5 laboratory testing of selected soil samples obtained and
6 engineering evaluation of the laboratory and field data

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae ended lava flows from Koolau banked
against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the Schofield
Plateau or Central Oahu
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating sediments
which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and further south
these sediments accumulated along with and interbedded with marine sediments and
coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from zero
feet in the area of H 1 Freeway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the
Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal
aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic
time through the present
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2223 00 Page 5
During the Pleistocene or Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
advance and retreat of the continental glaciers As the glaciers grew sea levels fell below
the present level Conversely as the glaciers melted the sea rose to levels higher than
the present These fluctuations in sea level resulted in fluctuations in sedimentary
deposition as the base level changed During higher stands of the sea sediments
accumulated at elevations above present sea level During lower stands these sediments
were exposed and eroded or hardened by exposure These Pleistocene sediments are
referred to in this report and our previous report as Older Alluvium
Following the Pleistocene sea level stabilized at its current elevation and sediments
began to accumulate in areas which had been eroded during the lower stands of the sea
Most of these sediments have been deposited in estuaries marshes and similar
low energy environments Therefore they are less consolidated than the previously
exposed Pleistocene sediments These younger sediments are referred to in this report
and our previous report as Recent Alluvium
Subsurface Soil Conditions
Based on our current and previous field explorations the soil types within the
development site may be generalized into two distinct groups as follows
Recent Alluvium
Recent alluvial deposits were encountered mainly at the lower elevations in the
Honouliuli Stream flood plain and near the West Loch Golf Course The recent
alluvium encountered consisted primarily of medium stiff to very soft brown to dark
gray clayey silt These soft soils are generally capped with a thin layer of fill At
higher elevations stiff silty sand and clay deposits were encountered at shallow
depths approximately 6 feet below existing ground surface In most of the lower
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2223 00 Page 6
portion of the proposed housing area the Recent Alluvium encountered was
characterized by very soft soils near the ground surface with water levels
encountered at very shallow depths
Older Alluvium
Older alluvial deposits were encountered in the upper portion of the area which
is presently in sugar cane cultivation The alluvium sampled consisted primarily of
very stiff to hard brown silty clay to clay
In general most of the stiff to hard clays may be classified as having low to
moderately high swelling expansion potential Although in some boring locations
clays with higher shrink swell potential were also encountered at various depths
There was considerable variation in the swell potential of the soils encountered
with the results of our laboratory swell testing ranging from negligible to over 10
Visually the clayey soils appear to be similar and the difference in swelling
potential when wetted is found mainly by laboratory swell testing

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
In general it is our opinion that the proposed residential development is feasible
from a geotechnical engineering point of view provided that our recommendations are
followed
The underlying soft compressible soil conditions in the lower northern areas may
result in ground settlements which will require special consideration in the earthwork
construction schedule Additionally the soft wet soils may become unstable when
influenced by loads imposed by fills This potential instability will affect the grading design
by limiting the steepness and or height of fill slopes
As previously discussed the soils encountered in the proposed development area
generally consist of silty clay soils with a low to moderately high swelling expansion
potential As discussed elsewhere in this report it is not readily possible to distinguish
between the low expansion potential soils and the moderately high expansion potential
soils Therefore it is preferable to design for the higher expansion potential and give
special consideration to the preparation of the subgrade and the construction of the
building foundations
Special considerations must also be given for the utilities roadways and structures
which will be built in the portions of the lowlying area underlain by compressible subsoils

Site Grading
At the time of this report only a conceptual grading scheme had been developed
for the project It appears that the upper elevation areas 25 MSL may be cut about
5 feet deep to generate fill material for the lower areas Significant quantities of import fill
will be required to raise the grades in the low areas
Some of the proposed housing area is underlain at some depth by soft wet
compressible soils In the lowlying area at the north end of the project site these soft
soils are generally encountered at depths of one to thirty feet below the relatively
competent upper fill or alluvium In this area normal site grading techniques may be
utilized where the competent upper fill or alluvium is of sufficient thickness to allow for
placement and compaction of fill materials i e in excess of three feet However if soft
soil conditions are encountered at or near the surface during the earthwork activities the
site preparation and fill placement methodology should be modified as described below
in the discussion Soft Areas
Upper Firm Ground Areas
The following recommendations present general guidelines for grading in areas of
relatively competent ground
1 Areas to be graded should be thoroughly cleared of vegetation boulders
debris and other deleterious materials Topsoil should be removed and
stockpiled for later usage and to avoid contamination of fill materials
2 In areas to receive fill the ground should be scarified to a depth of six 6
inches brought to near optimum moisture content and recompacted to a
minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density as established by ASTM
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2223 00 Page 9
D 1557 78 Soft yielding or pumping areas should be over excavated and
stabilized by backfilling with select material placed in 10 to 12 inch loose lifts
compacted to a minimum of 90 maximum dry density
3 Fill material should be placed in 10 to 12 inch thick loose horizontal lifts and
compacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density as established
by ASTM D 1557 78
4 If additional off site borrow is required it should be tested by the
geotechnical engineer to verify its suitability as fill
5 Imported select material for any non expansive capping layer required
should be low expansion potential material such as crushed coral mudrock
or cinder The material should be less than 3 inches in size with a
laboratory CBR swell value not exceeding 1 and a CBR value of 20 or
more It should not contain large percentages of boulders organics or
other deleterious material should contain sufficient fines so that there
is a minimum of 25 passing the No 200 sieve and should be capable of
forming a competent stable mass when compacted
6 Where the existing ground is steeper than 5 horizontal to 1 vertical keying
and benching are required to properly bond the new fill to the slope The
filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level
compacted layers as recommended above For construction details refer
to Plate 3
7 Cut and fill slopes of 2 horizontal to 1 vertical are recommended for the
upper firm ground portion of the development site Fill slopes if required

back
8 Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting
utilized to reduce erosion
9 Generally slope drainage benches should be provided for slopes having a

Lower Elevation Areas
The soils encountered near the groundwater elevation in the lower northern
portion of the proposed development area present a stability problem for rapid
and high fill embankment slope construction over such low strength soils In areas
underlain by near surface soft wet soils fill slope ratios should be no steeper than
three horizontal to one vertical 3H 1V to lessen the possibility of slope failure or
slippage
In the event grading is to be performed in close proximity to the groundwater
elevation in the project site special grading techniques should be used to avoid
bogging down of the earth moving equipment and or loss of stability in the
subsoils In general light equipment should be used for the clearing and grubbing
operations and earthwork operations should progress slowly to lessen the
possibility of disruption of the subsoils which could result in failure or slippage of
the fill mass
1 After the clearing and grubbing of a section of existing ground a working
platform or stabilization layer should be established prior to the placement
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2223 00 Page 11
of major fills The working platform should consist of a minimum 18 inch
thick layer of gravel Where surface conditions warrant a synthetic filter
fabric should be placed prior to the gravel layer The gravel should consist
of material generally between one fourth and three fourths of an inch in size
The gravel layer should also extend a minimum of 12 inches above the
water level
2 After the initial recompaction or a working platform is established fill
material should be placed in maximum 10 to 12 inch thick loose horizontal
lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density as
established by ASTM D 1557 78
Settlements
It is anticipated that the placement of fill in the lower areas underlain by soft wet
soils will induce settlement of the underlying compressible soils In general these
settlements are estimated to be on the order of two to four inches
It is anticipated that the settlement will occur within one to three months of the
completion of fill placement We recommend that during and after the placement of fills
in areas underlain by soft compressible subsoils that settlement gauges be placed and
monitored to measure the extent and the durations of settlements
Foundations
Our exploration has indicated that most of the proposed development site is
underlain by alluvial soils These soils were deposited in a complex depositional
environment The complexities of the environment have resulted in a wide range of
variation in soil types and characteristics in both the horizontal and vertical dimensions
Our laboratory testing indicates that the soils are primarily silty clays but range from low
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2223 00 Page 12
to high plasticity Visually it is not readily possible to distinguish between the low and
high plasticity clays Such critical differentiation may be verified by further laboratory
testing
In general low plasticity CL soils have low shrink swell potential and high
plasticity CH soils have high potential Laboratory swell testing performed for the native
soils of the proposed development generally confirms that this situation exists at the
project site Laboratory swell test values on one inch thick air dried soil samples range
from negligible to in excess of 15 when saturated under a surcharge load of 55 p s f
loading conditions which are similar to a conventional slab on grade residence
Unless it is possible to test a sufficient number of soil samples in the subgrade of
each lot during construction so that individualized recommendations may be formulated
it may be preferable to design for the higher potential swell conditions For building
construction in areas underlain by soils having potential swell values greater than about
4 conventional foundation systems such as slabs on grade or on grade post footings
may result in distresses to the proposed structures
Our exploration has indicated that most of the lowlying area in the northern section
of the site is underlain by moderately expansive soils If fill material is imported from the
West Loch Estates housing development or the upper portions of the Fairways site it is
anticipated that most of this soil will exhibit low to moderate expansion potential
Therefore alternate foundations systems should be considered to reduce the
effects of the moderately expansive soils at the site The following discussion presents
examples of alternate foundation systems which may be feasible for the proposed
residential development

Slab on Grade
Alternate No 1 The expansion potential of the on site subgrade soils may be
reduced by capping it with a minimum of 24 inches of non expansive off site select

Prior to placement of the capping fill the bottom of the excavation should be

scarification and recompaction of the existing soil This will create a cap of moist
soil above the potentially expansive soils and thereby reduce the drying of the
underlying clayey subgrade soils The reduction in drying will reduce the potential
swelling of the expansive silty clay soils

Infrastructure
It is anticipated that the infrastructure for the development will consist primarily of
water drainage and sewerage for the housing area
In the areas underlain by Older Alluvium it is anticipated that normal construction
methods may be utilized for the installation of the proposed infrastructure However in
the areas underlain by Recent Alluvium special construction techniques and designs
may be required
It is anticipated that some of the infrastructure system will be installed in areas
underlain by the soft wet Recent Alluvium soils The placement of fill for grading may
induce settlements in these soils which could cause damage to the utilities Therefore
it is recommended that in areas receiving in excess of two feet of fill construction of the
infrastructure be delayed until the settlement has occurred

specifications or recommendations and to allow design changes in the event that

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results
of these tests are presented on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Nine 9 one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected near surface soil
samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils The test results are
summarized on Plate B 4
Three 8 Atterberg Limit tests and nine 9 sieve analyses were performed on
selected samples to aid in the classification of the subsoils The test results are
summarized on Plates B 1 through B 3
Three 8 consolidation tests were performed on the soft deposits to evaluate
the compressibility of the soils The consolidation curves are presented on Plates B 5
through B 7
