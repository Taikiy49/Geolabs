2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes which include the following Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which
has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the
site appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe type flows which spread and ponded as they
approached the ocean Aja lava is typically characterized by a porous rough and
irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including
cobbles and boulders Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy
surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe lava
These cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and they represent both
lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes
are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like
structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and
pockets are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and
along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they
serve as the primary water transmission features

2 2 Existing Site Conditions
The project site is located to the southwest of the adjacent Hualalai Resort in the
Kaupulehu area of the District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii The 1545 1345 and
1145 Reservoirs project site is located on the mauka side of Queen Kaahumanu Highway
as shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2
The terrain at the project site is generally rugged which reflects the original surface
of thick basaltic lava flows of the a a form Based on the topographic map provided the
project site is gently sloping downward from south to north The majority of the project site
is underlain by a a and pahoehoe lavas at the ground surface Portions of the project site
were roughly leveled during the trail clearing operations to provide access to our drill rig
Vegetation at the project site generally consists of some wild grasses growing through the
crevices of the basaltic lava flows An existing unpaved access road off of Queen
Kaahumanu Highway leads to the project sites
It should be noted that a large collapsed lava tube of about 8 feet in diameter was
observed near the ground surface adjacent to the 1345 Reservoir site Based on our
observations the lava tube appears to dive deeper into the ground and a portion of this
lava tube may continue across the project site In addition relatively small lava tube
features and cavities were observed at numerous locations near the three reservoir sites
Therefore the presence of lava tubes and cavities should be anticipated at the proposed
reservoir sites

3 1 Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we
recommend that shallow spread and or continuous footings be used to support the pump
control building and other minor structures planned at the project site In addition
slabs on grade with thickened edge footings may be used to support the relatively light
pump control building and other minor structures An allowable bearing pressure of up to
4 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of footings bearing on
either the basalt formation and or compacted structural fill This bearing value is for
dead plus live loads and may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as
those caused by wind or seismic forces In general footings should be embedded a
minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent exterior grade Footings constructed
near the tops of slopes or on slopes should be embedded deep enough such that the
horizontal distance measured from the outside edge at the base of the footing to the
face of the slope is not less than 6 feet
Foundations located next to other foundations utility trenches or easements
should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility tench Alternatively the footing should be extended to a depth
as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce
the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the footing subgrades the soft
and or loose materials should be over excavated until dense materials are exposed in the
footing excavation The over excavation should be backfilled with structural fill materials
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is
the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

3 4 Foundation Probing
As mentioned previously a large collapsed lava tube feature was observed
adjacent to the 1345 Reservoir site In addition based on our exploration and experience
in the area voids and or cavities appear to be present in the basaltic lava flows at the
project site To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse
of the cavities below foundations we recommend that a program of cavity probing and
grouting of the foundation locations for the three reservoirs be implemented during
construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probe holes be drilled at 10 foot on
centers for the perimeter ring foundation In addition probe holes should be drilled within
the tank floor area at about 10 foot on centers starting at the center of the tank and
radiating out at 10 foot radial increments The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in
diameter and should extend to a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of
foundation If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing
operation additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacings to help delineate the
vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities
discovered should be backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material CLSM with a
slump of about 6 to 9 inches The CLSM should be injected at low to moderate pressures
As an alternate to CLSM sand cement grout with a slump of about 6 to 9 inches may also
be used
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during bidding
In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting
operations are completed The contractor should also be made aware that a longer lag
time between probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in
the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of cavities

with a large vibratory drum roller 15 ton minimum static weight for a minimum of
eight passes to help detect and collapse near surface cavities and or voids
The finished subgrades in cut areas should also be proof rolled with a minimum
15 ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of eight passes to assist in detecting and
possibly collapsing near surface cavities The proof rolling operations should be
conducted under the observation of a representative from Geolabs Soft loose

should be over excavated to expose firm and or dense ground The resulting

voids in the compacted mass In general the excavated basalt rock materials
may be re used as a source of structural fill materials provided that the materials

In general structural fills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in

expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Fill compaction
should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of
acceptable compaction equipment
Where fills are composed of rock and or cobbles conventional compaction testing

be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on our borings it appears that most of the cut slopes at the reservoir sites
would expose the basalt formation encountered in the borings In general we
believe that the cut slopes planned at the reservoir sites may be designed with a
slope inclination of 1H 1V or flatter In areas where highly fractured basalt or clinker
layers are encountered cut slopes should be flattened to 2H 1V or flatter
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the structural fill materials may be designed
with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills to be placed on slopes with
inclinations steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing
slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The fill slope face should be
free of large boulders greater than 18 inches in largest dimension and should
also be free of voids which would allow erosion and migration of fines to occur
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement
recommendations Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back
to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face Surface water
should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be
provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the
finished slopes
It is generally difficult to provide a smooth cut slope face when excavating in basalt
formations If over cutting of the cut slopes occurs consideration should be given
to flattening the slope or leaving the slope as is Patching of the over cut areas with
sliver fills is not recommended due to the potential for erosion of the fill materials in
the patched areas
In general slope benches may be omitted for cut and fill slopes up to 30 feet in
vertical height on this project Appropriate measures for cut and fill slopes with a
vertical height of 30 feet or greater should be considered to reduce the velocities of
the surface water runoff and potential erosion of the slope face One method to

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about 36 feet
below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools The locations of the three
borings are shown on the Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 3
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD value would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
