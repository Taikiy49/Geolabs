Dear Mr. Lind: 
In accordance with the project specifications, a static tensile load test was 
conducted on a sacrificial micropile for the Matson Terminals, Inc., Sand Island Terminal 
Wharf and Yard Modifications, Sand Island, Oahu, Hawaii project. The project site is 
located at the Matson Terminal along Piers 51 through 53 on Sand Island in the Honolulu 
Harbor on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. This letter report presents the results of our field 
observations, the test data collected during the load testing conducted on April 24, 2018, 
and our engineering analyses and recommendations. 
The sacrificial tensile load test micropile (TP-1) is situated along the west side of 
Pier 52 near project Station 2+75 within the coralline and clay material soil profile area. 
The load test micropile was installed by Hawaiian Dredging Construction Company, Inc. 
(HDCC) from April 12 through 17, 2018, utilizing a Klemm 806D track-mounted drill rig. 
The tensile load test micropile (TP-1) extended to a depth of about 84 feet below 
the ground surface with about 26 feet of 7.625-inch outside diameter (OD) steel casing left 
in-place (about 2-foot of the steel casing stick up above the ground surface). Records of 
the micropile installation are provided in this letter report as reference, as shown on 
Plates 1.1 through 1.4. 
Based on our field observations, the pre-production sacrificial load test micropile 
was installed by advancing the steel casing to a design depth of about 84 feet below the 
ground surface. The load test micropile generally encountered loose to dense sandy 
gravel materials and bouldery deposits to a depth of about 26 feet below the ground 
surface. Loose to medium dense coralline deposits were then encountered to a depth of 
about 62 feet below the ground surface. This was underlain by stiff alluvial clays extending 
to a depth of about 71 feet below the ground surface. A loose coralline deposit was

encountered below the clay layer and extended to the final depth drilled to about 84 feet 
below the ground surface. Upon reaching the design pile tip elevation, a No. 14 Grade 150 
all-thread center bar with centralizers, spaced at about every 10 feet, was installed to the 
full depth of the borehole. A tremie pipe was then inserted for the full depth of the borehole 
and cement grout was pumped via the tremie pipe from the bottom to the top of casing, 
displacing the groundwater inside the steel casing. 
The steel casings were extracted from the bottom 60 feet of the load test micropile, 
providing a bond zone within the coralline materials and alluvial clays while the remaining 
steel casings (about 26 feet) were left in-place at the top of the micropile (unbonded zone) 
as permanent casings. The permanent steel casings were then further extracted and 
plunged 3 feet back into the bouldery materials. It should be noted that the pre-production 
sacrificial micropile was extended about 1 foot above the adjacent ground surface for load 
testing access. 
The pre-production static tensile load test was conducted in general accordance 
with ASTM D3689. An allowable tensile load capacity of 150 kips was specified for the 
load test micropile. A hydraulic jack system against a reaction beam was used to apply the 
testing load to the tensile load test micropile. In general, the reaction beam was supported 
on a cribbing system placed at two ends of the beam. 
A load cell was used to measure the loads applied to the micropile. The micropile 
elongation was measured with two dial gages attached to a reference beam. The dial 
gages were placed on the left and right sides of the load test micropile. In addition, 
two 1/100-inch division rulers were set on a remote reference structure and on a reference 
beam attached to the micropile center bar to monitor the potential ground settlement that 
could affect the dial gages readings. The surveyor’s level was used to observe the rulers 
and the remote reference for movement. 
In general, the load test micropile was loaded to a maximum test load of 300 kips in 
15-kip load increments. Each load increment was held for at least 4 minutes. The test load 
was held for 240 minutes at the 300-kip load increment, about 200 percent of the design 
tensile load capacity, to evaluate the effects of creep. The gross rates of settlement at the 
300-kip load increment were less than 0.01 inches per hour. Subsequently, the micropile 
was unloaded in approximately nine equal decrements and held for 4 minutes each. The 
results of the micropile load test are summarized in the following table. Detailed 
information and data collected during the tensile load test are presented on Plates 2.1 
through 2.8

Total micropile head displacement at the 300-kip load increment (200 percent of the 
design tensile load capacity) was about 0.320 inches, as illustrated on Plate 3. 
Based on the above load test results, it is our opinon that the 7.625-inch OD load 
test micropile installed at the project site with a bond length of about 60 feet in coralline 
deposits and alluvial clays is capable of supporting an allowable compressive/tensile load 
capacity of 150 kips with a minimum factor of safety of 2 with reasonable settlement 
characteristics compatible with the project design. 
Based on our evaluation, we recommend that the production micropile lengths for 
the project remain at about 64 to 84 feet below the existing grades, consisting of 24-foot 
unbonded length and 40 to 60-foot of bonded lengths.
