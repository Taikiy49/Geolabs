2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes which include the following Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea
which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801
and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the
site appears to consist of both a a and pahoehoe type flows which spread and ponded
as they approached the ocean A a lava typically is characterized by a porous rough
and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including
cobbles and boulders Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy
surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are encountered commonly in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe type
lava These cavities are formed when the fava is still in a molten state and they
represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow
cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving
a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along
the flow Blisters and pockets are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock formation and can transmit
water quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is
transmitted through cavities along clinker layers through the porous rock matrix and
along joints fractures and inter flow contacts The permeability of the cavities and
clinker layers is high therefore they serve as the primary water transmission features

2 4 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by
shifts in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily
to volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most of the earthquakes
are so small that only sensitive instruments could detect the small tremors However
some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to
moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the
Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent of
earthquakes are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and degree of
ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the southern portion of the
Island of Hawaii

Fills and Backfills
In general we recommend the fill areas be capped with a minimum 2 foot thick
layer of structural fill materials to serve as a choking layer for the larger sized
general fill materials below Structural fill materials should consist of well graded
granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with
sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of large voids in the compacted mass In
general the excavated materials a a and pahoehoe basalt may be re used as a
source of structural fill materials provided they are processed to meet the
maximum particle size and gradation requirements presented Where
appropriate such as within utility corridors the thickness of the structural fill layer
may be increased to a minimum of 1 foot below the invert elevation of the utilities to
facilitate future utility construction
Fill and backfill materials below the minimum 2 foot thick structural fill layer may
consist of general fill materials General fill and backfill materials should consist of
well graded granular materials the majority of which are less than 12 inches in size
with an absolute maximum dimension of 18 inches Materials between 12 and
18 inches in particle size should be limited to about 15 percent or less of the total
volume of general fill materials The excavated on site materials if less than
18 inches in maximum dimension may be used as a source of general fill or backfill
materials except for trench backfill provided that they are processed to meet the
above gradation requirements for general fill If the excavated materials do not
contain sufficient fines to produce a well graded material off site borrow or on site
rock crushing of larger rock fragments or boulders should be considered to provide
the required gradation and particle sizes to develop a well graded material
Where over cutting of the finished cut subgrades occur during the mass grading

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills
should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 18 inches in loose thickness
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction of fill materials should be accomplished by utilizing sheepsfoot rollers
vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lift sizes not exceeding the maximum dimension of
the majority of the rocks or 5 feet whichever is less Placement of rock fills
should be accomplished with haul trucks traversing previously placed lifts and
dumping at the edge of the currently placed lift Spreading of the rock fill should
be accomplished with a large bulldozer to facilitate seating of the rocks During
placement the rock fill should be heavily watered with water from trucks traversing
in front of the current rock lift face while continuously spraying water Compaction
equipment such as a Caterpillar D 10 bulldozer or similar heavy compaction
equipment providing suitable energy to achieve the required compaction should be
utilized The number of passes usually six to eight to be made in achieving proper
compaction should be determined at the on set of site grading by performing a trial
compaction test or by plate bearing tests Subsequent layers of rock fill above the
previously placed rock fill should provide a choking layer of smaller sized boulder
materials to fill in the voids between the boulders of the preceding rock fill layer
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of
rocks boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number
of passes needed by a compactor to achieve the desired level of compaction
should be conducted at the start of the project grading phase under the observation

In general we believe continuous strip footings may be used to support the new

based on dead plus live loads and may be increased by 50 percent when

In general footings for retaining walls should have a minimum width of 18 inches
In addition wall foundations located on relatively flat areas should be embedded a

bearing on dense basalt formation pahoehoe basalt the minimum embedment
depth may be reduced to 12 inches For sloping ground conditions the footing
should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 6 foot setback distance measured

reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement

be estimated using 16H pounds per foot of wall length for level backfill conditions

Drainage
In general retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the potential for build up
of hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide
zone of permeable material such as open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation placed immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the
base of the retaining wall The perforated pipe should discharge to an appropriate
outlet or weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as
MiraDrain 6200 or EnkaDrain 9010 may be used instead of the permeable
drainage material The prefabricated drainage product also should be hydraulically
connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the retaining wall
For site retaining walls the typical drainage system may consist of 1 cubic foot of
permeable material such as open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
wrapped with non woven filter fabric placed at each of the weephole locations The
weepholes should be spaced not more than 6 feet apart
Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of
relatively impervious material well graded materials to reduce the potential for
significant water infiltration behind the retaining wall

Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained

drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material
left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and
description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as

APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
As part of our field exploration program we conducted eight percolation tests at
selected depths within some of the borings drilled Seven percolation tests were
performed using the constant head method and one test was performed using the
falling head method In general the borings drilled had a diameter of about 4 inches
and extended to depths of about 11 to 21 5 feet below the ground surface at the time of
the tests A water truck was used to pump clear water into the boreholes for the
constant head test method The flow rate was recorded by timing and calculating the
volume as a relatively constant flow rate was achieved
For the falling head method water was filled in the borehole and was allowed to
percolate into the ground The water levels and corresponding times were recorded for
the calculations of subsurface permeability Results of the percolation tests conducted
are presented on Plates C 1 through C 8
