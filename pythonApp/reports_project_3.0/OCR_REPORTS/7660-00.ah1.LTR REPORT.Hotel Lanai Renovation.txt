This letter report presents our assessment of the proposed Hotel Lanai Renovation 
project on the Island of Lanai, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown

This letter report summarizes our geotechnical engineering recommendations

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The proposed project consists of renovation of Hotel Lanai, including 
repairs/retrofits and/or underpinning some of the existing footings. Based on the 
information provided, we understand that some of the existing footings were placed on 
the concrete pads about 4 inches by 4 inches in dimension and have experienced 
settlement resulting in wood sagging. 
The current renovation project consists of replacing some of the concrete pads 
with concrete footings and retrofitting some of the long span floor beams. Details of the 
retrofits and underpinning were not available at the time this report was prepared. 
Due to limited access around the footprint of the existing hotel, we have tested 
the subsurface soils using a portable Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) at six 
locations.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Lanai is a shield volcano built by eruptions at the summit and along 
three rift zones for more than 1.20 to 1.46 million years ago. The principal rift zone trends 
northwestward as a broad ridge and is responsible for the conspicuous elongation of the 
island in that direction. A less conspicuous bulge on the southern side of the island is the 
result of volcanic extrusion on the southwest rift zone. The rift zones intersect at the island 
summit. The summit of the shield volcano collapsed to form a caldera from which a 
shallow graben, bordered by an echelon of step faults, extends south-southeast toward 
Manele Bay. Numerous dikes exposed in the sea cliff indicate that this Manele Graben lies 
along another rift zone. 
The caldera was largely, but not completely, filled by lava flows and the present 
Palawai Basin is a remnant of the caldera. Just to the west of it, Miki Basin (with an 
average diameter of about 0.9 miles) is a nearly filled pit crater. The top of the ridge 
between these basins is about 140 feet above the floor of the Palawai Basin. On the 
south, the floor of Palawai Basin merges with the floor of the Manele Graben, where the 
most recent lava flows in the caldera overflowed onto the outer slope of the volcano. The 
project site is near the northern flank of the Palawai Basin.

The Island of Lanai was built by the extrusion of thin-bedded a’a and pahoehoe 
tholeiitic basaltic flows that are generally inclined at about 6 to 15 degrees from horizontal 
(where not disturbed by faulting). Volcanic rocks on the Island of Lanai are grouped as the 
Lanai Volcanic Series. The near-surface soils generally consist of well-drained, 
fine-textured and moderately fine-textured soils derived from volcanic ash and the in-situ 
weathering of the igneous rocks. The formation of the Island of Lanai is also the result of 
large changes in sea-level that caused submergence and re-emergence of the land mass 
through geologic time. Faulting and stream erosion in conjunction with changes in 
sea-level are also responsible for the high sea cliffs along the southern and western 
coastlines. 
SITE DESCRIPTION 
The project site is located at the Hotel Lanai in the central portion of Lanai City, 
Lanai, Hawaii. It is approximately bounded by Lanai Community Hospital to the north, 
Lanai Avenue to the west and 9th Street to the south, as shown on the Project Location 
Map, Plate 1.  
The existing hotel building is supported on a post-on-pier system with raised floor 
level about 3.5 feet above the ground surface. The hotel is situated on a relatively level 
ground condition and surrounded by other buildings, asphalt pavement and concrete 
paved outdoor dining area along the uphill side. Surface storm water run-off from the uphill 
may adversely cause erosion and ponding in the open space beneath the hotel building. 
Erosion and ponding water would cause soften subsurface soil and subsequently loss of 
strength and/or heaving. 
On the northern side of the hotel, a concrete sidewalk extends beneath the hotel 
structure near DCP No. P-2, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The wooden lattice 
appears to be buckling between the building structure and the sidewalk, as shown in 
Photograph No. 1. The buckling may indicate concrete sidewalk heaving or building 
settling.  
The structure is supported by timber beams transferring the load to concrete pads 
about 4 inches by 4 inches in dimension. Photograph No. 2 was taken of the hotel’s 
substructure near DCP No. P-2, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2, indicating that one of 
the vertical posts is misaligned to the concrete pad foundation. 
Based on Google Earth™, the project site generally slopes down from east to west 
with ground surface elevations ranging from about 1,640 to 1,675 feet above Sea Level.

SUBSURFACE CONDITION 
Our field exploration at the project site consisted of conducting six DCP tests, 
designated as P-1 through P-6. The DCP tests extended to a depth of about 10 feet below 
the ground surface. The approximate DCP locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on our field exploration and our experience within the vicinity of the project 
site, the subsurface conditions at the project site consist of stiff to very stiff residual and 
saprolitic materials extending to the maximum depth explored of about 10 feet below the 
existing ground surface.  
Detailed descriptions of DCP testing are presented on the Logs of DCP, Plates 1.1 
through 6.5 of the Appendix. 
SEISMIC DESIGN CONSIDERATION 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition), the project site may be 
subject to seismic activity that can impact the planned improvements, and seismic design 
considerations may need to be addressed. 
The geotechnical data disclosed by our limited field exploration and review of the 
geologic data of the project site were used to classify the subsurface conditions for seismic 
design considerations.  Generally, the DCP testing encountered stiff to very stiff residual 
and saprolite soils.  We anticipate basalt formation at greater depths, underlying the stiff 
saprolite soils.  Because the depth of the basalt formation is unknown, we assume the 
upper 100 feet of the soil profile consists of stiff saprolite soils.  Based on these subsurface 
site conditions, we classified the site as “Stiff Soil Profile” corresponding to a Site Class “D” 
in accordance with the International Building Code (Table No. 1613.5.2), 2006 Edition. 
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using 
slab-on-grade with thickened-edge and/or spread footings to support the current 
renovation project planned at the site. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 
3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design of foundations. The 
footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished 
grade. 
We recommend grading improvements to be included in the renovation project, to 
divert the storm run-off water away from building footprint and reduce the risk of foundation 
subgrade softening. 
Detailed discussions and recommendations for foundation design and site grading 
are presented in the following sections.

Foundations 
Based on the competent subsurface soil condition at the site, we recommend 
using slab-on-grade with thickened-edge and/or spread footings to support the 
proposed renovation. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of 
foundations. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by 
one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
We anticipate that the subsurface clayey and/or silty soil may exhibit slight to 
moderate potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations. 
To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slab-on-grade resulting 
from shrinking and swelling of the near-surface clayey and/or silty soils, we recommend 
embedding the thickened-edge footings a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest 
adjacent grade. 
Where footings are located adjacent to other below-grade structures, utility 
trenches or easements, the footings should extend to a depth below a 45-degree 
imaginary plane projected upward from the bottom edge of the structure or utility trench, or 
the footings should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This 
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with 
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation 
settlement. Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground should be 
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of 6 feet 
measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of the slope. 
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction between the bottom 
of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure acting against the 
near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of 0.3 may be used for 
foundations bearing on the re-compacted on-site soils or new compacted fills. Resistance 
due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). This assumes soils around the 
foundations are well-compacted. Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive 
resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected. 
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with the 
recommendations presented herein, total settlement of foundations is estimated to be 
less than 1 inch with differential settlements of less than about 0.5 inches. 
We recommend properly preparing the slab-on-grade subgrades by scarifying to 
a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioning to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture, and compacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of 
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established 
in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage

by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. Where shrinkage cracks are 
noted after compaction of the subgrade, we recommend moistening the soils thoroughly 
to close the cracks or preparing the subgrades again. Saturation and subsequent 
yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may 
require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well-compacted fill. 
Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of footing excavations 
should be over-excavated until stiff/dense materials or to a maximum of 2 feet deep in 
the footing excavation. The over-excavation should be backfilled with non-expansive 
well-graded 3-inch minus fill materials moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
For the interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic, we recommend 
providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill below the slabs for uniform support. 
The cushion fill should consist of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) and 
would also serve as a capillary moisture break. Where slabs are subjected to vehicular 
traffic or sustained vibrations, a minimum of 6 inches of compacted aggregate base course 
should be provided below the slabs in lieu of the 4-inch thick cushion fill layer. The 
aggregate base course layer should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
To reduce the potential for excess moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to 
floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel 
cushion layer or compacted aggregate base course layer. Flexible floor coverings should 
be considered above the floor slab because they can better mask minor slab cracking. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor slab-on-grade foundation excavations prior 
to the placement of reinforcing steel and concrete, to confirm the foundation bearing 
conditions and the required embedment depths, and observe backfill placement to evaluate 
the compaction quality of the fill material. 
Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce the 
potential for contamination of the excavated materials to be used as embankment fill 
materials. 
In general, the on-site materials generated from the excavations may be reused as 
a source of general fill and backfill materials, provided that the materials are screened of 
over-sized materials (greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension) and are free of 
deleterious materials. Where used as fill and backfill materials, the on-site soils should be 
properly moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts 
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction.

Areas to receive fill should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to at 
least 90 percent relative compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry 
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
established in accordance with ASTM D1557.  Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Excavation 
Based on our field exploration and our experience within the vicinity, the project site 
is generally underlain by stiff to very stiff residual and saprolite materials with localized 
cobbles and boulders.  It is anticipated that the soil material encountered in the DCP tests 
near the ground surface may be readily excavated with normal heavy excavation 
equipment, such as excavators, and ripped with large bulldozers.  However, some of the 
deeper excavations in the hard residual/saprolite materials may encounter large boulders 
requiring the use of hoerams or chipping. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based 
on our field exploration data from the DCP tests at the project site. We recommend 
encouraging contractors to examine the site conditions and the subsurface data to make 
their own interpretation. 
Drainage 
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from 
the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding.  It is also advised to install gutter 
systems around the building and divert the discharge away from the foundation and 
pavement areas.  Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should 
also be avoided considering the slight to moderate shrink/swell potential of the on-site 
soils.  Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and 
drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above 
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation 
and subsequent loss of strength and/or heaving of the near-surface soils.  The foundation 
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of 
the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration.  In addition, drainage swales 
should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface water runoff away 
from the foundations and slabs.
