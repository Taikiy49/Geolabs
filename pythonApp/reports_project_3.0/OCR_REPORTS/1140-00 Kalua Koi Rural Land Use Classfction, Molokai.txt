This report presents the results of our preliminary percolation
testing at the above referenced site in the Kalua Koi area of
West Molokai Hawaii
The purpose of our investigation is to obtain preliminary sub
surface conditions at the site and to evaluate the in situ
seepage characteristics for possible future private sewage
disposal design
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located adjacent to and north of Kakaako
Gulch in Hawaii The site until recently
has been used as pasture land and is vegetated with a thin to
moderate cover of grasses and isolated thickets of keawe trees
W O 1140 00 Page 2
The site is bounded approximately by Kakaako Gulch to the
south the Kalua Koi resort residential development to the
west Kawakiu Gulch to the north and open range to the east
The potential development area consists basically of two
ridge top areas between Kawakiu and Kakaako Gulches which are
separated by Pohakumauliuli Gulch
These ridge tops slope gently to the west at an average slope
of about 3 degrees At the edges of the gulches the slopes
steepen to 35 degrees or more The ground surface elevations
vary from about 50 feet at the western boundary to about 480
feet in the extreme eastern corner
FIELD EXPLORATION
Four test pits were excavated utilizing a John Deere backhoe
provided by Goodfellow Brothers contracting company The test
pits were excavated to depths between 4 0 and 9 0 feet at the
approximate locations shown on the Percolation Test Location
Plan Plate 1
The excavating operation was monitored by our geologist who
also logged the test pits and performed the percolation tests
The logs of the test pits are presented in the appendix

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The project site is located on the western flank of the West
Molokai Volcano This shield volcano was built during the late
Tertiary and early Quaternary periods by a rapid succession of
thin bedded basalt lava flows In situ chemical weathering has
resulted in the formation of a deep residual soil mantling the
parent basalt rocks
There is evidence that high stands of the sea during the Pleis
tocene epoch which were the result of glacioeustatic sea level
fluctuations have caused a stripping of a significant portion
of the residual soil mantle
Soil Conditions
The test pits encountered relatively uniform soil conditions at
the site
The test pits in the upper areas of the site 1 and 3 encoun
8 feet of residual silty soils overlying
weathered basalt The test pits in the lower areas of the site
2 and 4 encountered basalt materials at shallower depths of
about 4 feet underlying residual silty soils

PERCOLATION TESTS
Falling head percolation tests were performed in the four test
pits
After completion of the test pit excavation each pit was
filled with approximately 3 feet of water which was the appro
ximate capacity of the available water truck The water was
allowed to percolate into the subsoil The drop of water
level with respect to time was monitored and recorded The
test data is presented in the appendix
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The excavated test pits encountered basalt formation at shallow
depths Visual observations of West Molokai basalts in other
areas indicate that the formation is composed of interbedded
pahoehoe and a a flows and is generally quite permeable It is
our opinion that strata sufficiently permeable for cesspool
disposal of effluent will probably be encountered at relatively
shallow depths below the project site This conclusion is
supported by Test No 2 where a basalt clinker layer was
encountered under the surface soil The percolation rate in
the clinker material was about sq ft or 2 inches per
Minute which in our opinion is quite good

The percolatin tests were limited by the fact that the backhoe
used for the excavation of the test pits could not penetrate
the underlying basalt material Therefore with the exception
of Test No 2 the remaining tests were performed in the silty
residual soils overlying the basalt rock
As anticipated the observed percolation rates in the upper
residual soils overlying the basalt rock were low The rates
in three of the tests average about 0 075 gallons per minute
per square foot or in terms of drop per time 20 to 30 minutes
per inch It is our opinion that cesspools extended into the
underlying basalt rock formation could achieve adequate
percolation rates for sewage disposal
The percolation tests performed in the shallow test pits were
of a preliminary nature thus the percolation test results
obtained are insufficient for the final design of cesspools
We recommend that additional percolation testing be performed
either in borings or a full size cesspool extending into the
underlying rock formation prior to the final design phase to
obtain more accurate cost data for the proposed cesspool con
struction The additional drilling and percolation testing
could provide more pertinent information for a more economical
cesspool design depending on the location and seepage charac
teristics of the permeable zones which may possibly be encoun
tered in the lower portions of the future cesspool locations
