REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was
still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava
During the later Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the continental glaciers advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below the present stand When the
GEOLABS INC
W O 4438 10 Page 6
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above the present level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans
of terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys
in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials

Beach Deposit
Beach deposits generally consist of tan white or gray colored very fine to
coarse grained and poorly graded well sorted sands with traces of silt and coralline gravel
The deposits may be unconsolidated loose or mildly cemented medium dense to dense
with increasing depth The sands are typically calcareous coralline and possibly include
GEOLABS INC
W O 4438 10 Page 9
some basaltic materials The materials are typically deposited in medium to high energy
environments such as active shorelines and shallow bays
The beach deposits resemble beach sands and are essentially derived primarily from
wave erosion and hydraulic action working on the reduction of coral and shells which are
further abraded and sorted by the activity of waves The sand is usually deposited in layers
along shorelines and in bays and lagoons open to currents and the influx of coralline
materials The grain size and sorting of the sand is controlled by the wave energy of the
depositional environment In general the greater the wave energy the coarser the grains
Some beach deposits are deposited on land by wind eolian activity to form dune
deposits These deposits may be classified as eolian deposits and are typically stratified with
cross beds representative of dune deposition Dunes are known to migrate with the
passage of time
Lagoonal Deposits
Lagoonal deposits generally consist of light gray to dark gray silty and clayey soils with
sands and gravel ranging from very soft to very stiff in consistency The soils are typically
deposited in lagoons where the wave energy is low and where marine and terrestrial
fine grained sediments may accumulate The deposits may be layered stratified as grayish
marine sediments with embedded coralline fragments and brownish terrestrial sediments
The more deeply buried thick lagoonal deposits may become indurated to form siltstone and
claystone with the passage of time
Coralline Detritus
Coralline detritus generally consist of tan brown or grayish white colored well graded
poorly sorted calcareous sands and gravel The materials are usually intermixed with
terrestrial silt and clay and form a matrix around coralline fragments The coralline fragments
within the detrital matrix may consist of cobble or boulder sized materials It should be noted
that cobbles and boulder sized materials are commonly encountered in coralline detritus In
addition coralline detritus may be unconsolidated loose or moderately cemented dense
GEOLABS INC
W O 4438 10 Page 10
in localized areas Therefore significant variation of engineering properties ranging from
loose sands to hard boulders should be anticipated in the coralline detritus materials
The deposits are associated with coastal environments where terrestrial stream flow
and ocean marine environments coalesce The deposits are typical of low to medium energy
depositional environments where periodic influxes of marine and terrestrial materials intermix
such as shorelines bays estuaries and lagoons
Due to the complex nature of coralline detritus extreme localized variations in the
subsurface materials should be anticipated in areas underlain by coralline detritus It should
be noted that boulder sized coralline fragments and cemented materials may be present
within zones of loose sandy gravel in the coralline detritus In short the character of the
coralline detritus materials is highly variable and quite unpredictable in short distances
Coral Formation
Coral formations generally consist of tan white or grayish white colored organic rock
consisting of cemented coral and algal reef materials with a fine grained calcareous
crystalline rock ground mass The rock may contain varying degrees of vugs and may be
non porous to very porous by interconnected vug structures Depending on the degree of
cementation and crystallization the coral formation may range in harness from soft to very
hard
The coral rock mass is essentially formed by live marine organisms that consist of
coral and calcareous algae plants that deposit calcium carbonate In general the corals
build the rock framework with lime secretions derived from dissolved calcium carbonate from
seawater The algae growth then in fills and coats the coral surface to armor the coral
against surf action The hardened algae growth is what provides much of the cementing
agent to form hard limestone
The rock may be core sampled with variable recovery due to the presence of
interconnected voids and vug space present in the coral rock mass The rock may be
GEOLABS INC
W O 4438 10 Page 11
sampled by split barrel samplers with density ranging from medium dense to refusal
however split barrel sampling is generally limited to the soft to medium dense very porous
rock type containing a high volume of void space in the rock framework The open vugs and
voids commonly encountered within the porous rock framework may be partially or wholly
filled with fine sediments making it difficult to differentiate between mildly cemented coralline
detritus and strongly vugular coral formation
Large voids with or without sediment in filling may be encountered in the coral
formations The voids are believed to have formed either by dissolution of the buried
limestone by groundwater or due to the original depositional character of the reef building
organisms
Alluvium
Alluvium generally consists of red brown gray or black unconsolidated soils
consisting of silts sands gravel and clays with some weathered cobbles boulders The soils
may have a mottled appearance and range from very soft very loose to very stiff dense
Some shells or organic material may be present in the alluvium near the coastline
The materials are typically deposited along streams and marshes and mantling hills
and pre existing topographic low features The materials are derived from the erosion of
terrestrial rock and soils transported by stream or stormwater runoff activity from a source
region and deposited elsewhere Older more deeply buried alluvium may be transformed
to form an indurated rock identified as siltstone sandstone or conglomerate depending upon
the predominant material grain size The indurated deposits may be identified locally as
older alluvium

Soft Loose Soil Deposits
Ground behavior for soft ground tunneling may be described based on six categories
as summarized on Plate 6 Categories of Ground Conditions for Soft Ground Tunnels
Loose sandy soils can be characterized as firm running or flowing ground depending
on the degree of cementation and on the groundwater conditions Above the
groundwater level these soils will not stand unsupported but will tend to ravel or run
until a stable slope is formed that buttresses the tunnel face If there are sufficient
fines present to give the soil some apparent cohesion these soils may be able to
stand unsupported in the heading for short periods of time Below the groundwater
level these soils may behave as flowing ground unless the cohesion or cementation
of the soil is high enough to resist the seepage forces Both running or flowing ground
behavior indicates potentially unstable ground conditions with low stand up time
Squeezing ground conditions may occur where the soil is observed to advance slowly
into the tunnel excavation without any signs of fracturing Squeezing occurs without
an increase in the water content or a volume change in the soil and is governed by
the soil strength in comparison to the overburden pressure Squeezing ground may
include soft to medium stiff or stiff clays depending on the overburden pressure at the
tunnel depth
Our borings indicate that loose beach and lagoonal deposits are present along the
new sewer line alignment The approximate limits of the beach and lagoonal deposits
based on our borings are shown on the Generalized Subsurface Profile Plate 4
please also refer to the Detailed Subsurface Profile on Plates 5 1 and 5 2 In
addition the inverts and crowns of the new sewer line are generally below the
groundwater elevations measured at the site For microtunneling under such
conditions significant loss of ground due to running or flowing ground behavior may
occur at the heading resulting in potential surface settlement unless an appropriate
pressure balance system is used for microtunneling in these subsurface conditions

Potential Surface Settlement
Surface settlement is usually considered to be the primary source of damage to
adjacent streets utilities and other surface facilities during tunneling operations For
the microtunneling technique loss of ground is generally associated with soil
squeezing or running into the heading losses due to over cutting by the shield shield
steering adjustments and from construction dewatering
Minor ground losses during the advance of the heading are unavoidable with normal
construction techniques The actual magnitudes of these losses are largely
dependent on the type of ground groundwater conditions overload factor
in cohesive soils size and depth of the tunnel and the skill of the operator in
steering the shield or machine As indicated previously we recommend that
interlocking steel sheet pile be used for the shoring support system with a tremie
concrete plug for groundwater control Use of an interlocking steel sheet pile shoring
support system with a tremie concrete plug is relatively watertight which should allow
the groundwater levels outside the jacking and receiving pits to be maintained close
to the original pre construction levels Therefore we believe that only minor
dewatering will be required at the jacking and receiving pit locations The minor
dewatering at the jacking and receiving pits should not cause significant areal ground
settlement at the site
Empirical methods have been developed for estimating surface settlement due to soft
ground tunneling by study of observed settlements on past projects The typical
settlement pattern that develops above a soft ground tunnel is a trough shaped
depression resembling an inverted normal probability curve with the maximum
settlement occurring above the tunnel centerline The behavior of the ground is also
influenced by the cover above the tunnel When the cover is greater than about
two tunnel diameters arching develops in the ground above the tunnel reducing the
potential for significant ground movement and surface settlement

Cobbles and Boulders
Ground that contains cobbles and boulders can be problematic for microtunneling
equipment A boulder or cluster of cobbles encountered in the heading of the tunnel
boring machine may severely hinder or even halt progress of the microtunneling
operations Boulders are likely present in the coralline deposits encountered at the
site Based on the geologic setting at the project site it is probable that the coralline
detritus layer may consist of cobbles and boulders embedded in the sandy gravel soil
matrix
In general microtunneling is expected to be suitable for pipe installation in the
coralline detritus underlying the site However zones of cemented detritus and
embedded boulders may be problematic and might cause difficulty in drilling and
steering the heading However we believe that the microtunneling machine should
be able to advance through these hard zones with careful steering and proper cutter
head selection
Mixed Face Conditions
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the proposed depths of the new

out of beach deposits lagoonal deposits and coralline detritus as shown on the
Generalized Subsurface Profile Plate 4 and the Detailed Subsurface Profile
Plates 5 1 and 5 2 Due to the difference in the relative density of these materials
deflection of the disk cutting head causing difficulties in steering may be encountered

at the interface between the beach deposits lagoonal deposits and coralline detritus
The disk cutting head may skid along the coralline detritus and loose beach and

In order to reduce the potential for significant deviation of the microtunneling boring

Jacking and Receiving Pits
As mentioned previously the coral formations and the presence of boulders and
cemented zones in the coralline detritus may pose difficulties during construction of the
jacking and receiving pits We understand that concrete caissons have been contemplated
for use at the jacking and receiving pits However the subsurface conditions at the site have
posed significant challenges in order to advance the concrete caissons to the required
penetration depths Therefore we believe that the use of interlocking steel sheet piles for
shoring of the jacking and receiving pits would be more appropriate for this site However
it should be noted that the loose granular soils at the site are susceptible to liquefaction
resulting in ground surface settlements when subjected to vibrations generated from the
sheet pile installation
Due to the subsurface conditions at the site we recommend that the sheet pile
locations for excavation support be predrilled in order to achieve the required penetration
below the bottom of excavation and to reduce the potential for significant vibrations
generated due to sheet pile installations In addition consideration should be given to
predrilling the areas within the perimeter sheet piles of the jacking and receiving pits to help
break down potential coral layers large coralline boulders and other obstacles for ease of
GEOLABS INC
W O 4438 10 Page 20
removal of these materials Considering the permeable nature of the granular subsurface
materials and the predrilling recommended for the pit excavations we also recommend that
a tremie concrete plug be used at the bottom of the pit excavations to provide for
groundwater control at the jacking and receiving pits
Excavation Method
It is anticipated that conventional earthmoving equipment may be used to excavate
the materials from within the jacking and receiving pits based on the subsurface
conditions encountered However it should be noted that coralline detritus materials
containing cobbles and boulder sized materials and cemented zones are likely
present at the jacking and receiving pit locations In addition the deeper excavations
or sheet pile embedments may encounter the hard coral formations underlying the
coralline detritus at the site Therefore some areas of the pit excavation may require
predrilling or heavy equipment for excavation In addition it should be emphasized
that the excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance
equal to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation in order to
reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement toward the excavation
Excavation Support
We understand that concrete caissons were previously considered for construction
of the jacking and receiving pits It should be noted that excavation for the jacking
and receiving pits will likely encounter the coralline detritus materials As mentioned
previously cobble and boulder sized materials are typically embedded within the
sandy gravel matrix in the coralline detritus materials In addition coralline detritus
materials may be slightly to moderately cemented in localized areas Based on our
experience it is generally difficult to sink the precast concrete caissons plumb in
areas where the subsurface materials contain cobble and boulder sized rocks and or
cemented materials Therefore we believe that temporary shoring using sheet piles
would be more appropriate for this project site

Thrust Block Resistance
The thrust block at the jacking pit provides the reaction for the main jacks when
pushing the pipe forward The allowable passive pressure resistance of the soil
which is a function of the strength of the soil will control the thrust resistance that can
be developed It may seem reasonable to allow the thrust block to develop the full
passive pressure resistance because this is a temporary construction loading
GEOLABS INC
W O 4438 10 Page 22
condition However it must be recognized that relatively large strains will be required
in order to develop the maximum passive pressure resistance At the jacking loads
that impose stresses approaching the maximum passive pressure resistance there
may be large movements of the thrust block in response to the strains required to
develop full passive pressure resistance
In addition large movements of the thrust block are not desirable for pipe jacking
because the movements are usually not uniform and it becomes difficult to maintain
the thrust block perpendicular to the pipe alignment If the thrust block does not
remain square the force applied to the pipe may not be uniform resulting in eccentric
loading that could damage the pipe Design of the thrust block for this project should
take into consideration the presence of loose sands adjacent to the shaft excavations
These loose beach and lagoonal deposits may limit the jacking forces that may be
mobilized unless a rigid reinforced concrete thrust block is constructed to distribute
the jacking forces over a large enough area to avoid overstressing these soils
Movements of the thrust block can also be controlled by limiting the allowable passive
pressure resistance to about one half of the maximum passive pressure resistance
This corresponds to a much lower level of strain and smaller soil movements strain
compatible design If the project requires higher jacking forces than can be safely
transferred to the soil mass behind the thrust block then the soil mass should be
improved or stabilized

Ground Categories
Description
Firm Ground
A heading may be advanced several feet or more without
immediate support Hard clays and cemented sand or gravel
generally fall into this category
Raveling Ground
After excavation material above the tunnel or in the upper part of
the working face tends to flake off and fall into the heading
Slightly cohesive sands silts and fine sands gaining their strength
from apparent cohesion typically exhibit this type of behavior Very
stiff fissured clays may be raveling materials also
Running Ground
Cohesionless dry soils run from any unsupported vertical face until
a stable slope forms at the natural angle of repose i e
approximately 3 degrees to 35 degrees Running ground consists
of dry cohesionless materials such as clean loose sands or
gravel
Flowing Ground
4
If seepage develops at the working face raveling or running
ground is transformed to flowing ground which advances like a
viscous fluid into the heading Silt sand or gravel below the water
table without a high enough clay content to develop significant
cohesion will be flowing type soils
Swelling Ground
A condition where the ground absorbs water increases in volume
and expands slowly into the tunnel This may occur in highly
over consolidated clays that exhibit high volume change
characteristics upon wetting
Squeezing Ground
hn
Squeezing ground conditions are analogous to plastic flow and the
soil is observed to advance slowly into the tunnel excavation
without any signs of fracturing Squeezing occurs without an
increase in the water content or a volume change in the soil and is
governed by the soil strength in comparison to the overburden
pressure Squeezing ground may include soft to medium stiff or
stiff clays depending on the overburden pressure at the tunnel
depth

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at a portion of the sewer line alignment Station 52 70
through Station 153 70 were further explored by drilling and sampling 27 borings
designated as Boring Nos 101 through 123 including Boring 114A and
119A The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plans
Plates 3 1 through 3 6 The borings extended to depths of about 25 to 52 feet below the
existing ground surface The borings were drilled with a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 27
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition samples were also obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the hard coralline materials encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral formation The coral material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of coral masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Eight unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
cores of the coral formation to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the coral
formation encountered The results of the unconfined compression tests are presented on
Plate B 1
Sixteen sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 D 1140 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are presented on
Plates B 2 through B 5
Five direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials sampled The results of
the direct shear tests are presented on Plates B 6 through B 10
