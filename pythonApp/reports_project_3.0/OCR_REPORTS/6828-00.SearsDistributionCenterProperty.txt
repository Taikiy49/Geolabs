REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two main shield 
volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau.  The older shield volcano (Waianae) is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The 
younger shield volcano (Koolau) is estimated to be late Pliocene through early Pleistocene 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct 
while Koolau remained active resulting in the partial burial of Waianae’s older eastern flank 
below younger Koolau lavas that banked and ponded against Waianae’s eastern slopes.  
These banked and ponded lava flows formed a broad upland plateau referred to as the 
Schofield Plateau in central Oahu.

During the Pleistocene Epoch (commonly referred to as the “Ice Age”), fluctuation 
of the ocean sea level occurred as a result of the worldwide advance and retreat of the 
great continental glaciers.  These sea-level changes had some effect on the geologic 
evolution of the Island of Oahu.  The changes in worldwide sea levels affected the 
erosional baseline of terrestrial streams and caused local submergence and emergence 
of the coastal island landforms with respect to the level of the sea. 
The project site is along the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano.  It is situated 
at the head of Pearl Harbor which coalesces an extensive region of southerly 
discharging streams that drain from the Koolau summit.  The site is adjacent to a 
widened area of stream confluence located at Waimalu Gulch, which was formed by 
stream incision on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano and mass wasting erosion 
of the valley walls.  The portion of Waimalu Gulch adjacent to the project site appears to 
have been affected by Pleistocene Epoch sea level changes, which alternatively 
submerged and emerged the gulch floor, changing the base level condition of stream 
erosion.  Therefore, the gulch floor contains some geologic deposits that are 
representative of both shallow marine and terrestrial origin as evidenced by the 
presence of some organic-rich alluvial sediment that contain sea-shell fragments.  
The project site is situated on the edge of the base of an interfluvial ridge 
between Waimalu and Waimano Gulches.  The ridge consists of hilly terrain that is 
underlain by weathered volcanic deposits, which were erupted from Koolau.  The 
volcanic deposits have been mapped previously and are referred to as the Tertiary 
Period Koolau Volcanic Series (H.T. Stearns).  The thick volcanic deposits consist of 
mainly thinly-bedded, sequential layers of basaltic lava flows consisting of hard basalt 
rock and clinker.  Much of the near-surface basaltic rock is extremely weathered and 
has been reduced to clayey residual soils underlain by silty saprolitic materials 
containing decomposed rock fragments. 
Based on the subsurface exploration conducted in the vicinity of the project site 
and available foundation plans for the existing structure on the site, it appears that there 
is a localized alluvial channel transecting the site.  It is believed that this channel may 
have been carved into the interfluvial ridge during a period of rapid erosion during a low 
stand of sea level and subsequently refilled when sea level rose. 
Anecdotal information indicates that, during construction of the existing 
warehouse structure, after construction of the floor slab, there was a storm event that 
resulted in significant erosion of the alluvial material under the slab.  The same 
anecdotal information indicates that the erosion was repaired.

Subsurface Soil Conditions 
As mentioned above, field exploration consisting of drilling and sampling borings 
was not conducted for this study.  Instead, research and review of available in-house soil 
and boring data from the project vicinity were performed in order to evaluate the 
subsurface conditions at the property.   
The primary surface soils in the area, as shown on the Soil Unit Map, Plate 3, are 
classified in the “Soil Survey of Islands of Kauai, Oahu, Maui, Molokai, and Lanai, State 
of Hawaii” issued August 1972, by the U.S. Department of Agriculture Soil Conservation 
Service, and is described as follows: 
Eastern Portion of Site – Molokai silty clay loam, 3 to 7 percent slopes (MuB) 
On this soil, runoff is slow to medium and the erosion hazard is slight to 
moderate.  Included in mapping were a few small areas that are eroded to soft, 
weathered rock.  Also included in mapping on Oahu were small areas of dark

Based on the available information and our experience in the area, we envision 
the project site is generally underlain by residual soils overlying saprolites and basalt rock 
formation at greater depths.  In addition, it appears that there is a localized alluvial channel 
transecting the site.  Based on our experience in the area and the structural drawings 
provided, we anticipate that the southern portion of the property may be underlain by 
soft and/or loose soils at shallow depths.  We envision that the subsurface soils will 
generally increase in stiffness towards the northern portion of the property.  In addition, 
our experience suggest that the near-surface soils may exhibit slight to moderate 
shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. 
Groundwater is not anticipated at shallow depths below the existing ground 
surface.  In general, the project site is at an elevation several tens of feet above the 
highest basal groundwater table in the area.  However, groundwater levels can vary 
significantly depending on seasonal rainfall, groundwater withdrawal and/or injection, 
and other factors.  In addition, it should be noted that surface runoff towards the site 
should be expected due to the lower elevation of the property relative to the adjacent 
grounds. 
Soil Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the 2006 International Building Code (IBC 2006) (code adopted by the 
City and County of Honolulu), the project site may be subjected to seismic activity and the 
potential for soil liquefaction at the project site will need to be evaluated.  In general, we 
envision the project site is underlain by silty clays and clayey silts overlying basalt 
formation at greater depths.  Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions and our 
engineering evaluation, we believe the potential for soil liquefaction at the project site is 
negligible.

Development of Sloping Ground 
We understand that it is desired to evaluate the feasibility of developing the sloping 
grounds along the eastern and western boundaries of the property.  Based on the 
anticipated subsurface conditions as described above, we believe that development of the 
sloping areas is feasible from a geotechnical engineering point-of-view.  We envision that 
the existing toe of slope areas would need to be pushed back in order to create additional 
level area for development.  Based on our evaluation, it is our opinion that the following 
options may be considered to create additional level areas for development at the sloping 
grounds.  A schematic illustration of these design options is provided on Plate 4. 
Concrete Retaining Wall 
We believe that conventional concrete retaining wall may be used to provide the 
necessary grade separation between the new level area and the sloping ground 
behind the wall.  The new wall will need to be designed to take into account the 
lateral earth pressure exerted by the sloping ground consisting of clayey soils.  A 
properly designed and constructed subdrain system would need to be installed to 
relieve the hydrostatic pressure that might be built up behind the new retaining wall. 
Depending on the proximity of the retaining wall alignment to the boundary line, 
extensive shoring may be required along the upslope area of the excavation to 
reduce the potential for earth movement during construction. Alternatively, if the 
retaining wall is set back a sufficient distance from the property line, consideration 
may be given to stabilizing the open excavation face with shotcrete or gunite to 
reduce the potential for sloughing or unraveling of the exposed soils.   
Soil Nail Retaining Wall 
In lieu of constructing a conventional retaining wall for grade separation, 
consideration may be given to the construction of a soil nail retaining wall system. 
In general, the soil nail retaining wall system consist of a series of individual 
reinforcing bars grouted into drilled holes to stabilize the soil mass behind the wall 
system.  A structural shotcrete layer is applied to the face of the reinforced wall and 
an architectural facing is then placed on the wall face for aesthetic appearance, if 
desired.  This approach is generally performed in a top-down construction and will 
eliminate the need for shoring requirement as compared to the conventional 
retaining wall option.   
Steepened Slope Stabilized with Soil Nails over Tecco Mesh 
In order to create additional level areas for development, consideration may also be 
given to steepen the existing slopes to an inclination of up to about 0.75H:1V to 
1H:1V.  The steepened slope would be stabilized by installing a grid pattern of soil 
nails anchored into a layer of Tecco mesh over the steepened slope face.  This

Additional Exploration 
A design-level field exploration program should be conducted for future planned 
development and/or improvements. The geotechnical engineering exploration should be 
directed at evaluating the presence of soft and/or loose soils, which will affect the 
foundation design of the proposed development or improvements.  In addition, relatively 
“undisturbed” samples should be obtained for laboratory ring swell tests to evaluate the 
swelling potential of the near-surface soils for slabs-on-grade design.
