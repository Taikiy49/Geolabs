Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age
and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct
while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below
Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed
a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau of Central Oahu
The project site is located along the distal southern flank of the Koolau Volcano
therefore the project site traverses terrain that is mostly underlain by deeply weathered
volcanic deposits erupted from the Koolau Volcano The volcanic deposits have been
mapped and are referred to as the Tertiary Period of the Koolau Volcanic Series The
thick volcanic deposits consist of mainly thin individual layers of basaltic lava flows The
volcanic rock appears to be extremely weathered to form clayey and silty saprolitic soils

Seismic Design Considerations
Based on the International Building Code 2006 Edition IBC 2006 the project
site may be subjected to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to
be addressed for the project The following sections provide discussions on the
seismicity of the Island of Oahu and the soil profile for seismic design
Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most of the earthquakes are so small that only sensitive instruments could detect
them However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few
cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii
over 90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of high seismic activity and
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of
Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field explorations for the proposed residential development at the Kam Drive In
Property project generally encountered stiff to hard residual and saprolite soils overlying
weathered basalt rock to the maximum depth drilled of about 102 5 feet below the existing
ground surface Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site and large
column loads anticipated we recommend utilizing drilled shaft foundations to support the
proposed high rise residential buildings As an alternative mat foundations bearing on the

spread and or continuous footings bearing on the near surface stiff to very stiff soils may
be used Soft and or loose soils encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations
should be over excavated to expose firm materials The over excavated areas should
be backfilled with granular material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction or the footing may extend down to bear on the underlying firm material
To reduce the potential for structural distress to the lightly loaded slabs resulting
from shrinking and swelling of the near surface silty and clayey soils due to changes in
the moisture content we recommend capping the on site silty and clayey soils below
the slab cushion with a minimum of 12 inches of non expansive select granular fill
material
Relatively deep excavation is anticipated for the basement parking structures
and or mat foundation construction in proximity of existing structures and or roadway
Therefore it will be necessary to temporarily shore the excavation to reduce the potential
for undermining the existing foundations and to facilitate the foundation construction

Uplift Load Resistance
In general uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead weight of the
drilled shaft and by shear along the shaft surface and the adjacent soils
Considering that the drilled shafts are designed based on adhesion between the
shaft and the surrounding soils the ultimate uplift capacities shown in the above
table may be used in the design
The uplift load capacities for the drilled shafts are based on the lengths of drilled
shafts recommended and designed for the compressive load capacity The uplift

The ultimate uplift load capacities shown in the above table were computed

Lateral loads imposed on the building structures may be resisted by a combination

In general lateral load resistance of the drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of
the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of
shaft and induced moment in the shaft In general we recommend spacing the
drilled shafts at a minimum of 2 5 times the diameter of the shaft from
center to center Therefore the effect of group action was considered in our lateral
load analyses by including an efficiency factor in the direction of loading
The lateral load analyses were performed using the program LPILE plus for
Windows which is a microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference laterally
loaded drilled shaft program originally developed at the University of Texas at
Austin The program solves for deflection and bending moment along a drilled
shaft under lateral loads as a function of depth The analysis was carried out with
the use of non linear p y curves to represent soil moduli The lateral deflection
was then computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths
The lateral loads were assumed ranging from 50 to 150 kips acting at the top of the
drilled shaft The lateral deflection the maximum induced moments and the
depths at which the maximum moments occur in the drilled shafts are presented on
the Summary of Lateral Load Analysis for Drilled Shafts Plates 3 1 through 3 6
The lateral load analyses were conducted based on two boundary conditions
where the tops of drilled shafts are either free or fixed against rotation

3 10 Drainage
It should be noted that surface and subsurface drainage are especially critical
considering the moderately expansive nature of the soils Therefore the finished grades
outside the new buildings should be sloped to shed water away from the slabs and
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding around the structure Gutter
systems should be installed around the building and discharge should be diverted away
from the slab and foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the slabs and
foundations also should be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration
into the subsurface
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations because ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to
drain surface water runoff away from the slabs and foundations

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling eleven boreholes designated as Boring Nos 101 through 111 extending to
depths of about 50 5 to 102 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary wash
drilling coring tools
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1
Rock cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as
shown on the Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2 Graphic representations of the materials
encountered and other observations such as groundwater levels are presented on the
Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 11
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond Core
Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow
bit to cut into the soil materials The soil material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and description

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content and Unit Weight tests were performed on selected soil samples
to aid in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and
correlating the layers between the borings The moisture content tests were performed
in general accordance with ASTM D 2216 The unit weight tests were performed in
general accordance with ASTM D 2937 The test results are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths in Appendix A
Thirteen Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B 1 and B 2
Two Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Direct shear test
results are presented on Plates B 3 and B 4

ASTM D 2850 were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained
shear strength of the silty and clayey soils encountered The approximate in situ
effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the
relatively undisturbed soil sample The test results and the stress strain curves are
presented on Plates B 5 through B 14
Six Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 7012 were performed on selected
rock core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength characteristics of
the basalt formation encountered The test results are summarized on Plate B 15
A one inch Ring Swell test was performed on a relatively undisturbed sample of
the near surface clayey soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the subsurface soils
The test results are summarized on Plate B 16
