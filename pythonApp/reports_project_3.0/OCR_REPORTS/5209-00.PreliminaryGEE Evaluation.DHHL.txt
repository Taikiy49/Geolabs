2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two separate
submarine shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is
estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in age 2 7 to 3 4 million years ago and Koolau
is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age 2 2 to 2 5 million years
ago After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep
valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows
followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These more recent series are referred to
as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the worldwide glaciers grew and advanced less
water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present
stands of the sea When the glaciers melted and receded a larger volume of water
became available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level The
larger glacioeustatic sea level fluctuations ranged between about 250 feet above the
present sea level to about 400 feet below the present sea level Thus the island
shorelines have transgressed and regressed in response to the fluctuations
The processes of terrestrial erosion and the associated depositional
environments were affected by these glacioeustatic sea level fluctuations When the
sea level was low the erosional base level was correspondingly lower and valleys were
carved to depths below the present sea level As a result terrestrial sediments were
carried further seaward and deposited in what had been marine environments When
the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations In addition it allowed for the deposition of marine
deposits such as coral and limestone in environments that had been covered with
terrestrial soils and rock

In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock mass from the slopes and movement of the material
downslope toward the axis of the valley by gravity to form colluvial deposits Once the
colluvium reaches streams in the valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium Large areas of alluvium can be
found in the valley floors lowland areas and bordering streams
When alluvial and colluvial deposits are left in place or buried for long periods of
time chemical processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a
breakdown or weathering of the material The chemical processes also cause
induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion of the sediment resulting in a
poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate Simultaneously erosion
continues in areas above the valley floors and upstream in headwaters This continued
erosion generates new alluvial material which is transported downslope covering the
older alluvial deposits
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer sediments
are generally transient in terms of geologic time and may be referred to as recent
alluvium In addition their consistency and density are generally less than those of the
older partially consolidated alluvial and colluvial deposits
The project site is situated on a broad gently sloping sedimentary alluvial plain
located at the foot of the northeasterly facing Pali of the Koolau Mountain Range Asa
result of the geographic location and past fluctuation of sea levels the site is believed to
be underlain by unconsolidated and semi consolidated alluvial and colluvial deposits
Weathered basalt rock formation is believed to exist at significantly greater depths
beneath these deposits

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our preliminary evaluation in general it appears that the proposed
residential development is feasible from a geotechnical engineering point of view
Based on the results of our laboratory tests performed on the near surface clay soils
the near surface materials may be classified as highly plastic silty clay soils that exhibit
a high potential for volume change shrink swell when subjected to moisture
fluctuations Due to the highly expansive soils encountered at the project site we
anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will require a minimum 36 inch thick layer of
non expansive select granular fill to reduce the effects of shrink swell of the
near surface expansive soils For design of the foundations of the new building
structures we believe that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or
continuous footings bearing on the stiff to very stiff in situ soils and or compacted fills
may be used
Based on our site reconnaissance we believe that portions of the project site
may contain some localized near surface deposits of very soft sediment associated with
the existing drainage ditch that traverses the site Where these soft near surface soils
are encountered the materials should be overexcavated and backfilled in accordance
with the recommendations provided herein Furthermore the upper 1 to 2 feet of the
existing ground surfaces located within the agricultural fields appear to be very soft and
compressible due to the effects of plowing and tilling for agicultural cultivation
Therefore we believe that the upper 24 inches of the ground surfaces should be
scarified and re compacted in accordance with the recommendations presented later in

3 1 House Lot Grading
As previously discussed the near surface soils at the site exhibit a high
shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations and would result in large
ground cracks if left unattended no irrigation The large ground cracks are primarily a
concern from an aesthetic standpoint however the cracks can also allow deeper
penetration of water into the underlying expansive soil which could be detrimental to
structures In order to reduce the potential for ground cracks to occur or develop the
following recommendations may be considered in an attempt to maintain the soil
moisture content relatively constant thereby reducing the shrink swell behavior of the
on site clayey soils due to moisture fluctuations
1 Based on our experience landscaping along with proper irrigation would
serve to maintain the soil moisture content relatively constant due to the
controlled regular irrigation Therefore we believe that the yard areas of the
house lots may be graded with on site soils followed by landscaping The
top 12 inches below the finish grade should be moisture conditioned to near
the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557
Optinum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density In addition irrigation of the
landscaped areas should be maintained on a daily basis to keep the
near surface soil moisture content relatively constant The amount of
irrigation water should be adjusted depending on the climatic conditions In
the dry season additional efforts in watering the yard may be necessary to
keep the soil consistently moist
2 Alternatively the yard area may be capped with a minimum 8 inch layer of
non expansive or low expansion capping soils The 8 inch capping fill
would serve as a moisture barrier to maintain the moisture content of the
underlying highly expansive soils relatively constant The capping fill should
consist of low expansion silty or clayey soils with low permeability It should
be noted that the recommended capping fill for the yard areas should not
consist of relatively permeable materials such as the select granular fill
below the slabs The capping fill should be moisture conditioned to near the
optinum moisture content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative

3 2 Foundations
Based on the near surface conditions encountered at the site we believe that a
shallow foundation system consisting of shallow spread and or continuous footings
bearing on the near surface stiff to very stiff clays and or compacted fills may be used to
support the proposed buildings For preliminary design an allowable bearing pressure
of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of footings
bearing on the near surface stiff to very stiff soils and or compacted fills This allowable
transient loads such as temporary wind and or seismic forces Footings should be
embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade for the
building foundations
Footing subgrades should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above
the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
If loose and or soft soils are observed at the footing subgrade level they should be
over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting over excavation should be
backfilled with compacted engineered fill The footing subgrade should be kept moist
prior to concrete placement If shrinkage cracks are noted at the footing subgrade the
footing subgrade should be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks Otherwise
over excavation of the footing subgrade and replacement with compacted fills may be
necessary

Where footings are to be located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities
the footings should extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane projected
upward from the bottom of the below grade structure or utility This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
If the foundation is designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total footing settlements under the anticipated design loads are estimated to be
on the order of less than 1 inch with differential settlements of approximately 1 inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the footing and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 8 may be used between the base of the footings and the
near surface clayey soils and or compacted fills Resistance due to passive pressure
may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per
foot of depth pcf for footings embedded in the clayey soils These values assume that
the concrete for footings is placed directly against the footing excavations Unless
covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below
finished grade should be neglected

3 6 Drainage
As previously indicated positive drainage should be provided to avoid ponding of
surface water which will accentuate the shrink swell behavior of the on site
near surface clayey soils The finished grades at each lot should be sloped at a
minimum of 1 5 to 2 percent to effectively carry away surface water and to reduce the
potential for ponding Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and
maintained to drain surface run off away from the site Excessive landscape watering
near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations
should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into
the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation and pavement recommendations since ponded water could cause
shrink swell of the near surface clayey soils and foundation saturation In addition the
foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately
after setting of the concrete to reduce water infiltration
