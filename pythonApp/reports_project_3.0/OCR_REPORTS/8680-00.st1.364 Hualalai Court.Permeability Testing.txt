SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Hualalai Court residential development at 364 Hualalai Court in the 
District of South Hilo on the Island of Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. This report summarizes the findings from 
our field exploration and presents our findings for the design of the stormwater disposal 
system for the proposed project. 
The recommendations provided herein are intended for the design of a stormwater 
disposal system only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject 
to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located at 364 Hualalai Court in the District of South Hilo on the 
Island of Hawaii. The project site encompasses an area of about 3.3 acres.  We 
understand that it is desired to develop residential buildings at the site.  In general, the 
project consists of constructing five two-story residential buildings, parking spaces, and 
associated infrastructures. As part of the project, a stormwater disposal system consisting 
of seepage pits and/or drywells will be required. 
We understand that a geotechnical engineering report was prepared by others for 
the project.  Therefore, this report covers the performance of permeability testing to assist 
in the design of a stormwater disposal system only.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers 
an area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during the 
recent geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea 
(both still active). 
The project site is on the northeastern flank of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano, 
which had been built up by the successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and 
pyroclastic materials. The lava formation encountered at the site appears to consist of 
both a'a and pahoehoe type flows of Kau Basalt Series, which spread and ponded as 
they approached the ocean (Stearns and Macdonald, 1946). A’a lava is typically 
characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged 
accumulation of rock fragments, including cobbles and boulders. Pahoehoe is 
characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal structure of 
vesicular (porous) rock. 
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas, especially pahoehoe type 
lava. Cavities form when the lava is still in a molten state, and they represent both lava 
tubes (intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form 
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow, leaving a hollow tube-like structure, which 
may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets are 
generally smaller in horizontal extent.

Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is at 364 Hualalai Court in the Hilo area of the District of South Hilo 
on the Island of Hawaii. An existing residence is present at the project site. 
Parts of the project site are generally open lawn areas. Some areas are heavily 
vegetated with thick shrubs and large trees. The surface materials generally consist of 
silty volcanic ash materials with varying amounts of sand and gravel. The ground surface 
is generally sloping down from west to east. Based on the topographic survey map

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling seven borings, designated 
as Boring Nos. 1 through 7, extending to depths of about 10 to 30 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on our borings, the subsurface conditions at the site generally consist of 
volcanic ash materials underlain by interbedded layers of clinker and basalt formation.  In 
general, the thickness of the volcanic ash materials is less than about 2 feet.  Volcanic 
ash materials generally consist of clayey silts with some gravel and cobbles.  Volcanic 
ash materials are generally underlain by interbedded layers of clinker and basalt 
formation extending down to the maximum depth explored of about 30 feet below the 
ground surface.  Basalt formation at the site is generally hard to very hard and relatively 
unweathered to slightly weathered. Clinker materials generally consist of medium dense 
gravel and cobbles with some volcanic ash.  It should be noted that cavities and/or voids 
are commonly present in the basaltic lava flows of the vicinity. Therefore, the presence of 
cavities and/or voids should be expected during construction. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field 
exploration. However, groundwater levels likely will change in response to seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.  
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered during our field exploration are 
presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-7 of Appendix A.  Results of the 
field permeability tests are presented in Appendix B. Photographs of the core samples 
retrieved during our field exploration are presented in Appendix C.

Based on our field permeability test, the subsurface materials generally have 
coefficients of permeability (k) of about 1.6 x 10-1 to 4.0 x 10-1 feet per minute at the 
locations tested. The hydraulic conductivity value roughly corresponds to an estimated 
capacity of about 1.0 to 2.7 cubic feet per second (cfs) for an 8-foot diameter shallow 
seepage pit.  The hydraulic conductivity value roughly corresponds to an estimated 
capacity of about 2.3 to 4.4 cfs for a 5-foot diameter, 20-foot deep drywell. 
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the on-site materials is 
highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids/fractures in the lava or 
the presence of volcanic ash soils and embedded clinker seams, which greatly affect the 
disposal capacity of the seepage pits and drywells. In general, the greater the number of 
clinker seams and/or voids/fractures encountered at the seepage pits and drywells, the 
higher the overall permeability and disposal capacity.  In addition, the greater the amount 
of volcanic ash encountered at the seepage pits and drywells, the lower the overall 
permeability and disposal capacity.  We recommend that the stormwater disposal system 
be tested during construction to confirm design capacities.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 
seven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 7, extending to depths of about 10 to 
30 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted 
drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on 
the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-7. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond 
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow 
bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is 
mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3.  The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material is 
recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown 
on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality 
of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling-induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot 
core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings 
as RQD = 50%.  Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock 
based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals."

Permeability Tests 
As part of our field exploration program, we conducted seven permeability tests in 
selected borings using the constant head test method. In general, the borings had a 
diameter of about 4 inches and extended to depths of about 10 to 30 feet below the ground 
level at the time of the tests. 
The constant head tests were performed by pumping water into the boreholes until 
the water surface stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time. The 
water height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which to 
base the calculation of the subsoil permeability. Results of the permeability tests conducted 
in selected borings are presented on Plates B-1 through B-7.
