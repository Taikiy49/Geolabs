GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions, our engineering analyses, and 
construction considerations, we envision that the new generators will be supported on a 
deep foundation system consisting of cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts. The rigid 
pavement at the fueling slab will be designed based on the heaviest container handler, the 
Taylor Model TXLC-975 Container Handler Forklift Truck. Detailed discussions and 
recommendations for the foundation design of the new generators and pavement design 
of the fueling slab are presented in the following sections. 
Drilled Shaft Foundations 
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances, the proposed 
new generators may be supported by a foundation system consisting of cast-in-place 
concrete drilled shafts.  Based on the information provided and the subsurface soil 
conditions anticipated at the project site, we recommend that the proposed drilled shaft 
foundations be at least 18 inches in diameter.  Based on the structural loads provided and 
the subsoil conditions anticipated at the project site, we recommend using drilled shaft 
foundations with a minimum drilled shaft length of 16 feet below the ground surface 
elevation. 
The cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally 
from skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils.  A net allowable

Lateral Load Resistance 
The lateral load resistance of drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of the 
surrounding soil, the stiffness of the drilled shaft, allowable deflection at the top of 
the drilled shaft, and the induced moment in the drilled shaft. The lateral load 
analyses were performed using the program LPILE-plus for Windows, which is a 
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference, laterally loaded deep foundation 
program originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin. The program 
solves for deflection and bending moment along a deep foundation under lateral 
loads as a function of depth. The analysis was carried out with the use of non-linear 
“p-y” curves to represent soil moduli. The lateral deflection was then computed 
using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths.

Foundation Settlement 
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundation will result from elastic compression of the 
shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the soils encountered 
at the site. We anticipate that the total settlements of the drilled shafts are 
estimated to be less than 0.5 inches. We believe that a significant portion of the 
settlement will be elastic and should occur as the loads are applied.

the contractor's method of construction and construction procedures.  In our opinion, the

Soft/Loose Lagoonal Deposits 
Based on our review of available soil boring data, the proposed drilled shaft 
foundations are likely to be embedded in soft and/loose compressible lagoonal 
deposits anticipated at depths between about 8 and 15 feet below the ground 
surface.  Therefore, caving-in and/or sloughing of these materials could occur 
during the drilling operations. To reduce the potential for caving-in of the drilled 
holes, temporary casing may be required during the drilled shaft installation to 
maintain the integrity of the drilled hole during drilled shaft installation. 
Care should be exercised during removal of the temporary casing to reduce the 
potential for "necking" of the drilled shaft. Therefore, a minimum 5-foot head of 
concrete above the bottom of the casing should be maintained during removal of 
the casing.  
Hard Coral Formation 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions and our experience in the project 
vicinity, the proposed new drilled shaft locations could be underlain by hard coral 
formation within the recommended drilled shaft depth.  Therefore, coring into 
potentially hard formation should be anticipated during the drilled shaft construction.  
Appropriate drilling equipment (and coring tools) should be utilized by the drilled 
shaft contractor to install the drilled shafts to the depth and dimension 
recommended herein. 
Workmanship 
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend, to a large extent, on the 
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils.  Therefore, proper 
construction techniques are important.  The contractor should exercise care in 
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes. 
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is not recommended.  Placement of 
concrete by wet construction methods using a tremie pipe will be required due to 
the shallow groundwater table and proximity of Honolulu Harbor.  The concrete 
should be placed in a suitable manner by displacing the water in an upward fashion 
from the bottom of the drilled hole. 
A low-shrink concrete mix with high slump (6 to 9-inch range) should be used for 
the concreting operation.  The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to 
reduce the potential for segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix.  In 
addition, the concrete should be placed promptly after drilling (within 24 hours after 
drilling of the holes) to reduce the potential for softening of the sides of the drilled 
holes.

Design Subgrade Conditions 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions at the fueling slab, we believe the 
pavement subgrade soils will likely consist of the dense silty sands overlying 
medium stiff silty clays and medium dense to loose silty sands at shallow depths. 
To provide a stable subgrade for the concrete pavement, we recommend providing 
a minimum of 6 inches of aggregate subbase between the concrete pavement and 
the underlying subgrade soils. Based on these assumptions and recommendations, 
an effective subgrade k-value of 250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection 
(pci) was adopted for our pavement analyses. Additional subgrade preparation 
requirements are presented in the “Subgrade Preparation and Aggregate Subbase 
Placement” subsection. 
Design Pavement Sections 
Based on the above assumptions and a properly prepared subgrade, it is our 
opinion that the fueling slab may consist of the following unreinforced rigid 
pavement section.

Subgrade Preparation and Aggregate Subbase Placement 
The subgrade soils under the new pavement structural section should be scarified 
to a minimum depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. The aggregate subbase should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 95 percent relative 
compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.  Compaction should be 
accomplished by using sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other types of 
acceptable compaction equipment. 
Pavement Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface should be 
sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off the 
pavement to appropriate drainage structures. Surface water ponding should not be 
allowed on the site during or after construction. The recommended pavement 
section assumes that good drainage will be provided for the paved areas.

LIMITATIONS 
The findings, discussions, and recommendations submitted herein are based, in 
part, upon information obtained from site reconnaissance, literature research, and 
experience in the vicinity of the project site. It should be noted that it is not possible to 
observe and/or anticipate all the site conditions based on a brief reconnaissance of the 
project site and review of literature. Variations of the subsurface conditions from those 
described in this letter report may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations 
may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, 
it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations presented herein.
