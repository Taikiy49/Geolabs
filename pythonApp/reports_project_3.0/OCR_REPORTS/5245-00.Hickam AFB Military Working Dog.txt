SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered near-surface fill
materials underlain by lagoonal and alluvial deposits consisting of very soft to very loose
clayey and sandy soils extending to depths of about 1.1 to 3.5 meters below the existing
ground surface. Coralline detritus and coral formation were encountered below the
lagoonal deposits extending to the maximum depths explored. The coralline detritus
consists of medium dense to very dense sand and gravel with varying amounts of silt. The
coral formation was generally medium hard or dense. Groundwater was encountered in
the drilled borings at depths ranging from about 1.2 to 1.7 meters below the existing
ground surface.
Based on the anticipated light structural loads, we recommend that the new building
structures be supported on a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and/or
continuous footings bearing on the recompacted in-situ soils or compacted fills. An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 120 kPa may be used for design of new footings
bearing on the in-situ soils or compacted fills. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live
loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads such as those caused by
wind and/or seismic forces.
Soft and loose lagoonal deposits were encountered in the borings. The new fills
placed at the project site will cause the soft and loose lagoonal deposits to consolidate,
which will result in ground surface settlements at the project site. Due to the variable
thickness of the soft and loose soil deposits and the variable thickness of the fills planned
at the project site, some differential ground settlements should be anticipated.
The new fills should be placed as soon as practical to allow the anticipated ground
settlements to occur prior to installation of the underground pipelines and structures at the
project site. After placement of the new fills, we recommend that a settlement-monitoring
program be implemented at the site to evaluate the magnitude and rates of settlement.
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and special design
recommendations.

SECTION 1.0 - GENERAL
1.1. Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Military Working Dog (MWD) Kennel project located within Hickam Air
Force Base on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and
engineering analyses for the project. These recommendations are intended for the
design of foundations, slabs-on-grade, site grading, and pavements only. The findings
and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of
this report.
1.2 Project Considerations
The project site is located within Hickam Air Force Base on the Island of Oahu,
Hawaii. The project site is located along the south side of Moffet Street near
Building 4027. Based on the information provided, the project involves the construction
of a Military Working Dog (MWD) Kennel facility.
We understand that the MWD Kennel facility will consist of two building
structures connected by a covered walkway. The building structures include an
administration area and a kennel area. Based on the available plans, the planned
finished floor elevations for the two building structures are +2.7 and +2.9 meters
(+9.0 and +9.5 feet) Mean Sea Level (MSL). Details of the two new structures were not
available at the time this report was prepared.
In addition, we understand that new driveways, parking areas, exterior walkways
and slabs, and a fenced obedience training ground are planned. The new driveways
and parking areas will consist of asphaltic concrete pavements. Based on the available
grading plans, we understand that fills on the order of about 1 meter are planned at the
project site.

2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older volcano, Waianae, is estimated
to be middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during
which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series.
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of
depositional and erosional events due to glacio-eustatic sea level changes and
post-erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch. The Koolau Volcanic
Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows.
Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial soil materials
were deposited in an estuarine and deltaic environment in the area of Pearl Harbor.
The sea level fluctuated several times, receding perhaps 90 meters below and rising
about 30 meters above the present sea level.
During the Recent Epoch, as the sea level rose to its current stand, the area was
covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of soft and loose lagoonal marine
deposits. Land development and reclamation projects in the past 40 to 60 years have
added man-made fills, bringing the site to its present configuration.

3.1 Foundations
Based on the anticipated light structural loads, we recommend that a shallow
foundation system consisting of spread and/or continuous footings may be used to
support the new building structures. An allowable bearing pressure of up to
120 kilopascals (kPa) may be used for new footings bearing on the recompacted in-situ
soils and compacted fills. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be
increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads such as those caused by wind and/or
seismic forces.
In general, footings should extend a minimum of 450 mm below the lowest
adjacent finished grade. The soils encountered at the bottom of footing elevations
should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. If loose and/or
soft soils are encountered at the bottom of footing elevation, the loose and/or soft soils
should be over-excavated to dense and/or firm material and the resulting
over-excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted granular fill materials.
Where new foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements,
footings should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward
from the bottom edge of the utility trench, or the footing should be extended to a depth
as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement.

If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with the
recommendations presented herein, total footing settlements are estimated to be on the

Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance

compacted. Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper
300 mm of soil should be neglected.

3.2  Slabs-On-Grade
We envision that concrete slabs-on-grade will be constructed as part of the
project. Our field exploration generally encountered sandy and clayey soils near the
surface. Our laboratory tests indicate that the in-situ clayey soils have a low potential
for shrinking and swelling due to moisture fluctuation. To reduce the potential for future
distress to the slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils,
we recommend that the slab subgrade be scarified to a depth of about 200 mm,
moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture, and compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Where shrinkage cracks are noted
after compaction of the subgrade, we recommend that the soils be thoroughly
moistened to close the cracks or prepared again.
For interior building slabs, we recommend that a minimum 100-mm thick layer of
cushion fill consisting of open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) be

3.3 Settlement Monitoring
As indicated previously, fills on the order of about 1 meter thick are planned at
the project site. Because of the soft and loose lagoonal deposits encountered in the
borings, ground surface settlements will occur as a result of the new fills placed at the
project site. Due to the variable thickness of the soft and loose soil deposits and the
variable thickness of the fills planned at the project site, some differential ground
settlements should be anticipated. We estimate ground settlements on the order of
about 75 mm may occur as a result of 1 meter of new fills placed at the project site.
We recommend that the new fills be placed as soon as practical to allow the
anticipated ground settlements to occur prior to installation of the underground pipelines
and structures at the project site. After placement of the new fills, we recommend that a
settlement monitoring program be implemented at the site to evaluate the magnitude
and rates of settlement.

We believe that a settlement waiting period of about 8 weeks should be
incorporated into the construction schedule for the project. The settlement waiting
period should commence after completion of the fill placement. It should be recognized
that it is difficult to accurately predict the exact time required for the filled ground to
settle, because the settlement rates will be affected by variations of the subsoil structure
and the history of the subsoil deposition.
For the loose and/or soft subsoils anticipated at the project site, it is possible that
the estimated settlement period could vary by as much as 50 to 100 percent from the
actual settlement period. Therefore, the actual settlement rates should be monitored,
and a settlement monitoring program should be established to evaluate the magnitude
and rate of the estimated settlements during the settlement waiting period prior to
construction of the underground structures. In addition, provisions should be made for
potential delays in the construction schedule if a longer settlement waiting period is
required.
To monitor the actual settlement rate, we recommend that a minimum of
six settlement gauges be installed at the locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. A
typical settlement gauge detail is presented on Plate 3 of this report. The settlement
gauges should be read optically by a qualified professional surveyor, and the readings
should be transmitted to Geolabs for review in a timely manner. We recommend that
two readings (minimum 24 hours apart) for each settlement gauge be taken prior to site
filling to establish a baseline. Subsequent readings of the settlement gauges should be
taken on a weekly basis for the entire settlement waiting period.
To determine whether ground settlements at the project site have reached an
acceptable level, a plot of the settlement gauge readings versus logarithm of time is
performed. Based on this plot, the ground settlement at 100 percent consolidation may
be determined by the Log-Time Method.

minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. If soft and/or loose soils are encountered
at the pavement subgrade, the soft and/or loose soils should be over-excavated to
expose the underlying firm or dense materials. The resulting over-excavation should be
backfilled with well-compacted fill.
Aggregate base course materials should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates
and should conform to TM 5-822-5/AFM 88-7, “Pavement Design for Road, Streets,
Walks, and Open Storage Areas.” In addition, the aggregate base course materials
should be compacted to a minimum of 100 percent relative compaction. CBR and field
density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during
construction to confirm the adequacy of the above sections. The recommended sections
consider only light traffic conditions and assume that good drainage will be provided
adjacent to paved areas.
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry
surface water off the site. Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site
during or after construction. Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or
adjacent to the pavement areas, we recommend that the curbs be extended a minimum
of 50 mm into the subgrade soil below the base course layer to reduce the potential for
migration of landscape water into the pavement section. Alternatively, a subdrain
system could be constructed to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation. For
long-term performance, we recommend that a subdrain system be constructed adjacent
to the paved/landscaped areas.

SECTION 3 — DICUSSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
3.6 Underground Utilities
We envision that new underground utilities will be required for this project. In
general, for support of the utility lines, we recommend that granular bedding consisting
of 150 mm of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) be used under the
pipes. The initial backfill up to about 300 mm above the pipes should consist of
free-draining backfill material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel, to reduce the potential for
damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill. It is critical that a free draining
granular material be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes.
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 300 mm above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on-site soils having
particles less than 75 mm in largest dimension or select granular material. The backfill
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in about
200-mm loose lifts, and mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction. Where trenches are located in paved areas, the upper 1 meter of the
trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction.
3.7 Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding. Excessive
landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided.
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength. The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting
of the concrete io reduce waier infiltration. in addition, drainage swales should be
provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run-off away
from slabs and foundations.

APPENDIX A

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings extending to depths ranging from about 3.1 to 6.6 meters below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings
were drilled using truck-mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who observed the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils, by driving a 75-mm OD Modified California sampler with a 63.5-kilogram
hammer falling 760 millimeters. In addition, some samples were obtained from the
drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586, Penetration
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 50-mm OD standard penetration
sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the second and third 150 mm of an 450-mm drive are shown as the
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths.
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths.

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties. The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths.
Two one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
of the surface soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils. The test
results are presented on Plate B-1.
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D 4318) was performed on a selected sample
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification.
Results of the test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depth. Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-2.
Two Sieve Analysis tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification. The tests were
performed in accordance with ASTM Test Designation C 117 and C 136. Graphic
presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B-3.
Twp consolidation tests (ASTM D 2435) with time rates of consolidation were
performed on samples of the soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the
materials encountered. The results of the consolidation tests are presented on
Plates B-4 and B-5.
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D 1883) were
performed on the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils. Results of the tests are presented on Plates B-6 and B-7.
