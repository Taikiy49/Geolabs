Localized Pavement Cracking at Access Road
Regional Relay Facility Opana

Dear Mr Whitmore
In accordance with our fee proposal dated we have conducted
a limited geotechnical engineering exploration for the pavement cracking along the existing
access road at the Regional Relay Facility Opana on the Island of Oahu Hawaii This
report summarizes our work on the project and presents our findings and geotechnical
engineering recommendations derived from our analyses
Project Considerations
The existing access road consists of newly re constructed asphaltic concrete
pavement We understand that several areas along the new pavement experienced
distresses shortly after construction The contractor then repaved these areas as a repair
measure However new distresses have developed within some of the newly repaved
areas It is desired to evaluate the possible cause s for these distressed pavement areas
Four borings were drilled along the existing access road For comparison our
borings were drilled both adjacent to areas experiencing pavement distresses and areas
where no pavement distress was observed The thickness and density of the existing
pavement was verified by coring the AC layer and the consistency of the underlying
ground surface was evaluated by Dynamic Cone Penetrometer in conformance with
ASTM D6951 Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Testing for Subgrade Stability The
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 1 Photographs of the
distressed areas are included in Appendix C
Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration was to obtain information
pertaining to the subsurface condition to formulate a summary of the soil rock conditions
and to evaluate possible cause s and remedial measures for the pavement settlement In
order to accomplish these objectives we conducted an exploration program generally
consisting of the following tasks and work efforts

Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling four borings designated as
Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths of about 2 to 3 5 feet below the existing
ground surface One Dynamic Cone Penetrometer DCP Test was conducted in
Boring No 2 The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 1
The DCP test results are shown on Plate 3
Our field exploration generally encountered asphalt concrete ranging from
4 to 5 thick overlying base course consisting of dense sandy gravel with silt
extending to depths of about 1 5 to 2 feet below the existing ground surface This
pavement section was underlain by medium stiff to stiff silty clay extending to the
maximum depths drilled of about 2 to 3 5 feet
Groundwater was not encountered in our borings at the time of our field
exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change
due to rainfall tides seasonal precipitation surface water runoff and other factors

Based on our field observation the newly constructed roadway exhibited signs of
low to moderate weathering and raveling Majority of the new cracks were observed at
new locations along the roadway It appeared that cracks did not continue to develop at
most of repaved areas However cracks were observed at two repaved areas in the
vicinity of Station Nos 4017 and 6040
In general majority of the pavement cracks appeared to be longitudinal hairline
cracks However the cracks located at the edges of two asphalt concrete patches near
Station No 4017 were longitudinal cracks approximately 1 8 to inches wide
An 1 8inch pavement elevation difference was observed between two sides of the
cracks The lower pavement elevation was located on the down slope side In addition
surface erosion was also observed at this location
Possible Causes of Pavement Distress
The field exploration disclosed that subsurface conditions along the existing
access road generally consist of asphalt concrete base course materials and subgrade
materials of silty clay
Based on our laboratory test results the asphalt concrete density ranges from
133 to 149 pounds per cubic foot Our boring encountered various base course
thickness ranging from 6 to 30 inches In addition the subgrade material stiffness
varies from soft to medium stiff to stiff
In general pavement distress may be caused by traffic loads and materials
including asphalt base course and subgrade During our site visits and field
exploration we observed minimal traffic along the access road only one vehicle
passing during a five hour period Therefore it is unlikely that pavement distress is
caused by traffic loads
Based on the field exploration and field observation possible causes of the
pavement distress have been identified and are discussed below
Repaved Areas without New Cracks
New cracks did not develop in majority of the repaved areas as shown on
Plates C 1 and C 2 However low to moderate weathering and raveling were observed
in these areas which may be indicative of poor AC quality In addition because traffic
pattern and subsurface condition did not change before and after repaving we believe
that the previous cracks might have been caused primarily by the asphalt concrete
quality factors such as asphalt temperature and construction methods

Repaved Area near Station 4017
This area is located at about 5 feet south of a relatively steep down slope as
shown on Plate C 2
Significant cracks developed after repaving Two longitudinal cracks about 10 to
30 feet long were observed at the edges of two asphalt concrete patches as shown on
Plate C 3 The cracks were approximately 1 8 to inch wide with an elevation
difference of 1 8 inch between the two sides of the cracks as shown on Plate C 4 In
addition new longitudinal hairline cracks were observed beyond the two AC patches as
shown on Plate C 5
Boring No 2 was drilled in this area Based on our field exploration the boring
encountered 4 inches of AC and 18 inches of base course overlying soft to medium
stiff silty clay subgrade The thickness and strength of the pavement section is more
than adequate at this location However as compared to the materials encountered in
other borings the subgrade materials silty clay were less stiff in this area as indicated
by blow counts shown on our logs of borings
Considering the significance pattern and location of the cracks near steep down
slope we believe the cracks are not cause by inadequate pavement section The
cracks may be caused by slope creep failure on the north side of the pavement
Repaved Area near Station 6040 and New Areas with Cracks
New hairline cracks were observed in the repaved area near Station No 6040
as shown on Plates C 6 and C 7 and in new areas where cracks were not observed
prior to repaving operation There is insufficient information to determine the cause s
of pavement distress in these areas

from the field borings Variations of subsoil conditions between and beyond the field
borings may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident
until construction is underway If variations then appear evident Geolabs should be
contacted to re evaluate the recommendations presented in this report
The boring locations in the field were determined by taping from the Site Plan by
Hankins and Anderson Consulting Engineers dated The physical
locations of the field borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by
the method used
