INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our soil investigation for the
proposed new runway at the Lihue Airport Lihue Kauai The purpose of our
study was to obtain and evaluate the subsurface soil conditions along the
proposed new taxiway and runway alignments to be located east of the
existing runway
Based on the laboratory test data engineering analysis was performed
to evaluate the pavement subgrade support and to develop recommendations
for pavement design drainage grading and general site development

W
Preliminary results of our study were transmitted to Muroda
Associates Inc as the data became available

research and review of available soil and geologic literature and boring

exploratory borings laboratory testing analysis of the field and lab
oratory data engineering analysis and report preparation The scope of
our study is performed generally in accordance with our proposal dated

SITE DESCRIPTION
The proposed new runway site is located east of the existing Lihue
Airport runway in the Kalapaki and Hanamaulu districts on the island of
Kauai State of Hawaii
Most of the project area is situated in sugar cane land presently
cultivated by the Lihue Sugar Company Access roads and irrigation
flumes lace the cane field area
The existing cane field area is generally level and slopes gently
east towards the ocean There are no extreme changes in the site
topography except for a natural guily located approximately between Station
20 and Station 23 50 This natural gully is shown on Plate 1 and is
designated Gully Area
Page 4
W
The project area south of the gully is about 10 to 15 feet lower
than the area north of the gully indicating this was previously a flood
plain area during ancient times and the gully was formed when the rain
water was concentrated in the lowest area
North of the Gully Area there is a very gentle slope up towards
the existing runway which slopes from elevation 149 to 80 in 6000 feet
The proposed pavement elevation is 92 at each end and 99 in the center of
6500 feet long runway There is another gully which is shallower south of
Taxiway aGt near Station 60 00 This smaller gully is dry and planted
with sugar cane
Surface boulder piles were found in many locations along the cane haul
roads The rock piles probably were taken from the adjacent cane field
areas during previous cane field preparation and cultivation The sizes
of boulders range from 6 inches to 6 feet in diameter but are mainly in
the 2 to 4 feet range
The ditches used to irrigate the cane vary in size The unlined
irrigation ditches are about 10 feet wide and 3 feet deep About 1 to 2
feet of soft silty material has deposited on the bottom of these ditches
due to ponding of water and constant accumulation of fines washed in with
the irrigation water Those ditches running along the cane haul roads are
about 3 feet wide and about 2 to 4 feet deep
Cane is generally planted along furrows making the upper 2 feet of the
surface soils loose from plowing Cane planting areas are generally sloped
away from the watering ditches

Existing Gully Area
The gully near Boring and 10 is about 25 to 35 feet deep
and ranges from 150 to 300 feet across its upper slope banks It runs
east toward the ocean almost perpendicular to the runway and taxiway
alignment The gully has about a 1 to 1 horizontal to 1 vertical
slope ratio Vegetation is thickest along the bottom where irrigation
runoff from adjacent cane fields flows The vegetation on gully slopes
consists of trees and bushes but with little ground cover leaving the
natural soil exposed Boulders line the stream edges
At the time of our investigation the stream in the gully consisted
of about 6 inches of surface water 6 to 12 inches of soft organic silt
and from 1 to 3 feet of soft saturated natural soil The soft organic
silt deposit ranges from about 5 feet wide at its narrow point to a maximum
width of 25 feet in the broadened gully portion
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The geology of the region began about 5 million years ago in the
Tertiary period with the Waimea Volcanic Series The Waimea shield volcano
formed the main mass of the Island of Kauai
On the eastern flank of Kauai a shield caldera formed and subsequently
collapsed The collapsed caldera is what is now the Lihue Basin or depression
The Lihue Airport site lies on the eastern edge of this well known geologic
feature
Page 6
W
Stratigraphically overlying the basin and also of Tertiary age is
the Haupu volcanic series The Haupu caldera lavas flowed over what is
now the Lihue Airport area For about 1 million years there was no
volcanism only erosion In the Pleistocene the lavas from the Kilohana
vent of the Koloa volcanic series were deposited over the eroded lavas
of the Haupu caldera
The local geology of this site consists of rocks of Koloa volcanic
series some intercalated sedimentary breccias and conglomerates along
the Hanamaulu stream to the north and also along the Nawiliwili stream
to the couthe The Koloa volcanic rocks are composed of basalt melilite
nepheline basalt and basanite They were possibly interrupted by periods
of weathering and erosion which results in layers of soil intermixed
between flows
These paleo soils are very hard to distinguish from the highly
decomposed residual lavas The Koloa lavas when subjected to warm climate
and abundant rainfall decomposed through chemical and mechanical
weathering and erosion into a dark reddish brown compact silty clay that
has subangular blocky structures at the surface The substratum is soft
mottled color weathered rocks

Soil Conditions
According to the U S Soil Conservation Service Soil Survey of
the Islands of Molokai and Lanai State of Hawati
August 1972 four soil series were noted in this area
LhB Lihue silty clay MH 0 to 8 slopes
LhC Lihue silty clay MH 8 to 15 slopes
LIB Lihue gravelly silty clay MH 0 to 8 slopes
KvD Koloa stony silty clay to 25 slopes
The exploration disclosed essentially uniform conditions within the
proposed project area
Generally a 4 to 10 foot crust of stiff clayey soils covers the
entire site Surface soils include a reddish brown silty clay in most of
Page 8
W
the areas except near boring 20 and 24 where a dark red
silty clay was noted Boulders were found occasionally in the surface
crust layer However in the gully area surface boulders ranging in
size from 4 to 3 feet in diameter were found frequently scattered along
the stream banks
Below the surface layer stiff reddish brown to gray colored sandy
and clayey silts were encountered in all of the borings
The surface soils within the cane field areas were loose to a maximum
depth of about 2 feet The moisture content below the loose layer was at
or near the optimum moisture content at the time of our field exploration
Ground water levels were encountered in some of the bore holes and
they varied from elevation 67 to 87 feet at the time of the subsurface
exploration
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The subsurface conditions at the project area indicated that the
proposed runway construction is feasible from the soils and foundation
engineering viewpoint The recommendations presented herein should be
incorporated in the design and construction considerations project plans
and specifications
Over a majority of the project area the upper 2 feet of surface soils
is loose and moist due to continual sugar cane cultivation These loose
Page 9
W
surface soils without special preparation will not be adequate for pavement
support Special treatment will be required to process the upper loose
soils in order to provide a uniform subgrade support
Based on the laboratory test results the on site reddish brown clayey
surface soil can be used as fill material up to subgrade elevation A large
quantity of fill will be required within the gully area as shown on Plate 1
Areal settlements will occur in the gully due to the loading of the fill
material The fill operation within this area should be scheduled as far
in advance as possible prior to pavement construction Due to the low
water table in this area the majority of the settlement should occur over
a period of about 45 days
In general the project site slopes down toward the makai end As
presently planned the new runway at the makai side will have lower elevation
than the proposed mauka taxiway A drainage system should be provided along
the taxiways and runway to keep the surface run off water away from the
pavement Due to the low permeability of the clayey soils positive
drainage should be established in the granular base and subbase material
to prevent an underground bathtub condition beneath the pavement To
minimize this condition subdrains should be installed in the north south
alignment areas where 0 slope grades are planned
Treatment of Existing Ground
The site should be cleared of all surface vegetation and debris Any
local soft spots existing structures and irrigation ditches encountered
within the construction area should be removed and replaced with compacted

Prior to placement of fill the existing loose surface soils should be
excavated to a depth of about 18 inches The exposed surface after the
recommended excavation should be scarified down to a depth of 6 inches
and recompacted to a minimum of 90 relative compaction as determined by
the FAA compaction control test no T 611 The excavated areas should be
inspected and approved by the soils engineer prior to placing controlled
compacted fill
At the subgrade elevation in the cut area the ground surface should
be scarified down to a minimum of six inches brought to near optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 relative compaction
Where cut is less than 2 feet below the existing ground surface the loose
soil should be excavated as outlined above

Shrinkage Factor
The shrinkage factor for the upper 1 foot of loose cane soils is
estimated at 40 to 50 That is it will take 140 to 150 cubic yards
of the loose soils to obtain 100 cubic yards of properly compacted fill
Minimum shrinkage is expected on the very stiff to hard upper soils
from 1 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Also because of boulders anticipated in the subsoil formations some
allowance should be provided for boulder removal A shrinkage factor of
about 5 is expected for the soil materials from 5 to 10 feet below the
existing ground surface
Settlements
Settlement analysis was performed to estimate the ground settlement due
to the loading from the proposed new fill over the low lying area For an
average fill height of 10 feet the calculated settlement would be about
1 to 3 inches For the proposed deep fill up to 35 feet high over the
Gully Area the calculated settlement is about 5 inches
The above settlements are estimated to occur within about 40 days The
occurance of settlement within a short period of time is due to the sandy
and silty formations found immediately under the very stiff to hard surface
clayey layer Therefore for the gully area it is recommended that the fill
operation be scheduled as soon as possible and that the pavement construction
be scheduled toward the end of the project development

and ranges from 150 to 300 feet across its upper slope banks It runs
east toward the ocean almost perpendicular to the runway and taxiway
alignment The gully has about a 1 to 1 horizontal to 1 vertical
slope ratio Vegetation is thickest along the bottom where irrigation
runoff from adjacent cane fields flows The vegetation on gully slopes
consists of trees and bushes but with little ground cover leaving the
natural soil exposed Boulders line the stream edges
At the time of our investigation the stream in the gully consisted
of about 6 inches of surface water 6 to 12 inches of soft organic silt
and from 1 to 3 feet of soft saturated natural soil The soft organic
silt deposit ranges from about 5 feet wide at its narrow point to a maximum
width of 25 feet in the broadened gully portion
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The geology of the region began about 5 million years ago in the
Tertiary period with the Waimea Volcanic Series The Waimea shield volcano
formed the main mass of the Island of Kauai
On the eastern flank of Kauai a shield caldera formed and subsequently
collapsed The collapsed caldera is what is now the Lihue Basin or depression
The Lihue Airport site lies on the eastern edge of this well known geologic
feature
Page 6
W O 651 00
Stratigraphically overlying the basin and also of Tertiary age is
the Haupu volcanic series The Haupu caldera lavas flowed over what is
now the Lihue Airport area For about 1 million years there was no
volcanism only erosion In the Pletstocene the lavas from the Kilohana
vent of the Koloa volcanic series were deposited over the eroded lavas
of the Haupu caldera
The local geology of this site consists of rocks of Koloa volcanic
series some intercalated sedimentary breccias and conglomerates along
the Hanamaulu stream to the north and also along the Nawiliwili stream
to the couch The Koloa volcanic rocks are composed of basalt melilite
nepheline basalt and basanite They were possibly interrupted by periods
of weathering and erosion which results in layers of soil intermixed
between flows
These paleo soils are very hard to distinguish from the highly
decomposed residual lavas The Koloa lavas when subjected to warm climate
and abundant rainfall decomposed through chemical and mechanical
weathering and erosion into a dark reddish brown compact silty clay that
has subangular blocky structures at the surface The substratum is soft
mottled color weathered rocks

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The subsurface conditions at the project area indicated that the
proposed runway construction is feasible from the soils and foundation
engineering viewpoint The recommendations presented herein should be
incorporated in the design and construction considerations project plans
and specifications
Over a majority of the project area the upper 2 feet of surface soils
is loose and moist due to continual sugar cane cultivation These loose
Page 9
W
surface soils without special preparation will not be adequate for pavement
support Special treatment will be required to process the upper loose
soils in order to provide a uniform subgrade support
Based on the laboratory test results the on site reddish brown clayey
surface soil can be used as fill material up to subgrade elevation A large
quantity of fill will be required within the gully area as shown on Plate 1
Areal settlements will occur in the gully due to the loading of the fill
material The fill operation within this area should be scheduled as far
in advance as possible prior to pavement construction Due to the low
water table in this area the majority of the settlement should occur over
a period of about 45 days
In general the project site slopes down toward the makai end As
presently planned the new runway at the makai side will have lower elevation
than the proposed mauka taxiway A drainage system should be provided along
the taxiways and runway to keep the surface run off water away from the
pavement Due to the low permeability of the clayey soils positive
drainage should be established in the granular base and subbase material
to prevent an underground bathtub condition beneath the pavement To
minimize this condition subdrains should be installed in the north south
alignment areas where 0 slope grades are planned
Treatment of Existing Ground
The site should be cleared of all surface vegetation and debris Any
local soft spots existing structures and irrigation ditches encountered
within the construction area should be removed and replaced with compacted
fill

Shrinkage Factor
The shrinkage factor for the upper 1 foot of loose cane soils is
estimated at 40 to 50 That is it will take 140 to 150 cubic yards
of the loose soils to obtain 100 cubic yards of properly compacted fill
Minimum shrinkage is expected on the very stiff to hard upper soils
from 1 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Also because of boulders anticipated in the subsoil formations some
allowance should be provided for boulder removal A shrinkage factor of
about 5 is expected for the soil materials from 5 to 10 feet below the
existing ground surface
Settlements
Settlement analysis was performed to estimate the ground settlement due
to the loading from the proposed new fill over the low lying area For an
average fill height of 10 feet the calculated settlement would be about
1 to 3 inches For the proposed deep fill up to 35 feet high over the
Gully Area the calculated settlement is about 5 inches
The above settlements are estimated to occur within about 40 days The
occurance of settlement within a short period of time is due to the sandy
and silty formations found immediately under the very stiff to hard surface
clayey layer Therefore for the gully area it is recommended that the fill
operation be scheduled as soon as possible and that the pavement construction
be scheduled toward the end of the project development
