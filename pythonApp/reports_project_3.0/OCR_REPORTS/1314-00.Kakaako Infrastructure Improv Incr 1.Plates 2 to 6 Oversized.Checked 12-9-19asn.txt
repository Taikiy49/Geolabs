PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of this soil engineering investigation was to explore
the subsurface soil and groundwater conditions along the proposed
utility corridor in order to provide soil data for use in the design and
construction of the proposed infrastructure improvements
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1314 00 Page 2
The results of our field exploration and laboratory tests which we
used in the engineering analysis to formulate our conclusions and
recommendations are presented in the appendices of this report
This report summarizes our findings and recommendations
GENERAL PROJECT DESCRIPTION
The project includes infrastructure improvements consisting of the
construction of curbs gutters sidewalks roads drain lines sewer
lines electrical lines telephone lines and water lines within the
Kakaako Community Development District Increment I Increment I
includes an area of approximately 138 acres bounded by Punchbowl King
and Cooke Streets and Ala Moana Boulevard A portion of the proposed
new drain line extends shoreward beyond Ala Moana Boulevard along
Cooke Coral Ilalo and Keawe Streets to an existing open channel below
Kelikoi Street The general project location is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate l
GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The general project area is presently occupied mostly by commercial
buildings and warehouses The significant streets crossing through the
site are South Coral Halekauwila Queen and
Kawaihao Streets and Kapiolani and Ala Moana Boulevards Smaller
streets include Streets and
Mission Lane
The existing road pavements are generally in fair to poor condition
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1314 00 Page 3
Many of the existing buildings are situated close to the street
right of way
The existing ground topography slopes down from Kapiolani Boulevard
toward the southwest Ala Moana Boulevard at an overall gradient of
less than 1 percent The ground surface elevations vary from about 6 to
14 feet along Kapiolani Boulevard to about 4 feet along Ala Moana
Boulevard

Geology
After formation of the Koolau Volcano that built up the eastern
side of Oahu ceased about 2 million years ago Macdonald Abbott
1970 the coastal plain developed along the southern shores of Oahu
during the Pleistocene epoch from a complex series of erosional and
depositional processes
Numerous glacial eustatic sea level variations caused the emergence
and submergence of the land mass that produced coral reef deposits at
various levels that can be identified at both above and below the
present sea level The coral surface slopes seaward from 0 to 5 feet
above sea level near King Street to 25 to 30 feet below sea level on the
seaward side of Ala Moana Boulevard
The lowering of the sea level during periods of Ice Age stages
permitted the mountain streams to cut channels through the predominantly
coral reef deposits
The next rise in the sea level resulted in the redeposition of
alluvial deposits in these channels Two such buried channels were
identified in the project area in this investigation The extent of
these buried channels are sometimes difficult to identify accurately
from the data that is available The approximate locations of these
channels are shown on Plate 2
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1314 00 Page 5
Less than one million years ago the
post erosional Honolulu Volcanic Tantalus and Roundtop eruptions
deposited cinder and ash deposits over portions of the site These
deposits were probably contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level
changes
As the sea level continued to rise the off shore fringing reef
continued to build and created a lagoon behind it which was later
filled with loose sediments This probably occurred less than 4 000
years ago when the sea reached its present level As the lagoon became
shallower softer sediments were deposited in a swampy environment
In more recent years the lowlying swampy areas were reclaimed by
man filling with dredged coral and other materials to the present
elevations The former shoreline shown on Plate 2 was taken from a
report titled Engineering Feasibility Studies Comprehensive Plan for
Ala Moana Reef by HAR Austin Associates Ltd and Law Wilson Ltd
December 1960

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The invert elevations of the various utility lines were not
available at the time of our investigation However it is anticipated
that they would be about 6 to 15 feet below the existing ground
elevation and in most cases would be at or below the ground water
levels
Fair to poor soil conditions were encountered below the site
Relatively high water levels and loose sand deposits were encountered
throughout the area Therefore proper dewatering trench shoring and
bracing would be needed to minimize loss of ground and resulting damage
to existing facilities structures

Earth Pressure Loads on Pipe
Loads on buried conduits are influenced by the width of the trench
the size of the pipe the unit weight of backfill material and the
frictional resistance between the backfill material and the trench side
walls To compute the vertical loads on the pipes the unit weights
presented on Plate 7 and a coefficient of friction of 0 25 is
recommended Earth forces upon the pipe increase rapidly with the width
of the trench Therefore the width of the trench should be kept to a
minimum
Bearing Capacity and Settlement
The underlying coral has good bearing capacity Therefore
minimal settlements are anticipated for structures supported on this
layer
The upper loose sandy soils encountered have fair to poor bearing
capacity
The new utility line construction in general will not materially
increase the vertical loading upon these underlying soils if no
additional fills are placed upon the existing ground surface The net
difference in loading between the weights of the excavated material and
backfill will be essentially small Therefore significant settlements
are not anticipated if proper construction techniques particularly for
shoring and dewatering are utilized
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 1314 00 Page 9
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great
effect upon the load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the
pipe bedding is an important design consideration
For pipes bearing on coral at the invert level a minimum of six
inches of compacted granular bedding material should be placed below the
pipe The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a
minimum width of one fourth the outside pipe diameter and should extend
half way up the pipe sides The bedding material should consist of
clean granular material such as 3B Fine gravel
For pipes on the loose sands an additional one foot layer of
granular material such as 3B Fine gravel should be provided below the
utility pipe and bedding layer to provide more uniform support for the
pipe
Trench Excavation
The anticipated depth of excavation is 6 to 15 feet The method
and equipment used for excavation should be the choice of the
contractor within practical limits
Relatively easy excavation is anticipated for the soils above the
coral Hard excavation is anticipated in the coral layer
GEOLABS HAWAII

Trench Wall Support
It is our opinion that the trench walls in the loose sand deposits
will require sheet piling It is important that adequate sheeting work
be installed prior to the excavation and maintained tight against the
trench walls with proper bracing to minimize lateral movements of the
adjacent ground into the trench excavation and resulting settlements of
adjacent structures This is particularly critical since many of the
existing structures may be supported on spread footings bearing on the
upper sandy soils
The excavation should be provided with proper bracing in accordance
with the anticipated loading conditions traffic and construction
materials stockpile and equipment and to satisfy the applicable safety
requirements The design and safety of the trench supports should be
the responsibility of the contractor The apparent earth pressure
diagram presented on Plate 8 may be used as a guide for construction
cost estimate purpose and for preliminary design of the trench support
system
Where practicable the trench sheeting should extend down to the
coral layer to increase the stability of the trench bottom and to
minimize the possibility of bottom heaving or quick conditions Where
the coral is too deep or not present the sheeting should extend down a
sufficient depth to minimize up lift seepage pressures
GEOLABS HAWAII

Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be in accordance with the
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction May 1975
Granular material such as sand should be used for the backfill up
to about one foot above the pipe to reduce the compactive effort
required and possible damage to the pipe
Except for the organic clays and silts most of the excavated
on site sandy and gravelly soils can be re used as the upper portion of
the trench backfill from above one foot over the pipe to within 2 feet
of the finished grade Backfill should be mechanically compacted to a
minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density established by ASTM D 1557
Below roadway areas the backfill within 2 feet of the finished grade
should consist of select borrow and be compacted to 95 of its maximum
dry density
If practicable the trenches should be kept free of water during
backfilling Should backfilling under the water be necessary granular
material such as base course rock should be utilized to minimize
excessive ground settlement of the trench backfill in the future
Typical sections of the pipe trench are presented on Plates 9 and
10

Joints and Connections
Due to the various soil conditions which will be crossed by the
pipe flexible pipe joints are recommended to allow for some amount of
differential settlement These flexible connections are particularly
important in the loose sand areas
Manhole Structures
Where possible the manhole structures should bear on the dense
coral Structures supported on the underlying dense coral may be
designed using an allowable bearing value up to 5000 p s f Minimal
settlements are anticipated for these structures
If it is necessary to place the manhole structures on the loose
sandy soils an 18 inch layer of gravel 3B Fine should be provided
below the structure to provide uniform support An allowable bearing
value of 1500 p s f may be used on the 18 inch gravel layer The
increase in load on the subsoils due to the manhole structures is
small Therefore large settlements are not anticipated if proper
shoring and dewatering techniques are utilized
The lateral earth pressure acting on the underground structures
will depend on the type of backfill used the extent of the backfill
material and the compactive effort required on the material behind the
structure walls Granular material such as the select on site sand is
recommended for the backfill adjacent to the structures to reduce the
compactive effort required and to minimize the build up of additional
lateral earth pressures

Backfill below water 25 p c f equivalent fluid pressure

Corrosion Tests
To examine the corrosive characteristics of the natural soil
against the utility pipes soil resistivity and pH tests were conducted
on selected soil samples The test results are summarized on Plate B 16
in the appendix
Generally the soils tested have pH values from 7 5 to 9 0 and
resistivity values from 115 to 4150 ohms cm sq therefore the soils
can generally be classified as having moderate to high corrosion
potential
Roadway Pavements
The borings indicate that the existing pavement sections consist of
2 to 3 inches of asphalt concrete over 4 to 8 inches of basalt base
course Most of the borings encountered silty sand below the base
course layer Laboratory CBR tests indicate that the silty sand can
provide good subgrade support
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1314 00 Page 15
Most of the existing pavements in the area are in generally poor
condition This is probably due to age erratic subgrade condition and
inadequate pavement thickness for the present traffic Under these
circumstances a very thick overlay would be required on the existing
pavement to strengthen the section and to meet the projected traffic
volumes
The construction of the new infrastructure improvements will result
in considerable excavations and require substantial repaving work In
addition due to the existing flat gradient the road grades will
probably have to be adjusted to improve surface drainage conditions
Therefore it may be desirable to repave the entire roadway sections
Prior to the road reconstruction the existing asphalt and concrete
pavements should be removed It is our opinion that the existing base
course will not be suitable for reuse as base course in the new
pavement However if it is carefully segregated during removal it may
be suitable for reuse as select borrow under pavements and in the trench
backfill The contractor should be encouraged to evaluate the
feasibility of recycling the existing asphalt concrete pavement
The underlying silty sand subgrade soil should be scarified for a
depth of 6 inches moisture conditioned to about optimum moisture
content and recompacted to a minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density
ASTM D 1557 78 Soft pockets detected during the above operation
should be removed and replaced with select borrow

Plans and specifications for site grading and utility construction
should be forwarded to the soils engineer for review and written
comments prior to construction to see that the intent of our
recommendations is included

Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the site grading excavation and backfill
operations be observed by a soils engineer during construction to see
that adequate compaction has been obtained and that the subsurface
conditions are consistent with the findings of our investigation
Any imported material required should be tested and approved by
the soils engineer prior to hauling to the site
If conditions encountered during construction are different from
those presented in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made

Field Exploration

borings at the approximate locations shown on the Geologic and Site
Plan Plate 2 Due to the change in the proposed drain line alignment

drill the borings

examination in the field by one of our engineers who continuously
monitored the drilling operations The classifications were further
reviewed by visual inspection and testing in the laboratory The soils
encountered in the borings are presented in the Boring Logs Plates A 1
through A 45 1 All soils were classified in accordance with the
Unified Soil Classification System
Soil samples were obtained by driving either a 2 4 inch I D ring
sampler or a standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer free
falling a distance of 30 inches or a 3 inch 0 D piston sampler
hydraulically pushed The blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12
inches or the hydraulic pressure required are shown on the Boring Logs
at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
LABORATORY TESTING
APPENDIX _ B
Laboratory Testing
ee eee
Moisture content Atterberg Limit and unit weight determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the
boring logs at the appropriate sample depths
Twenty three sieve analyses were performed on samples of the
underlying sandy soils to determine their grain size distributions to
evaluate the general permeability of the soils The test results are
summarized on Plates B 1 through B 6
Four CBR tests were performed on bulk samples of the soils under
the existing pavement sections to evaluate its subgrade support
characteristics The test results are summarized on Plates B 1 through
B 6
Six direct shear tests were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength of the underlying soil The test results
are presented on Plates B 7 through B 12
One consolidated undrained and one unconsolidated undrained
triaxial test were performed on the underlying soils to evaluate its
shear strength parameters The test results are plotted on Plates B 13
and B 14
Five constant head permeability tests were performed on undisturbed
samples of the underlying sandy soils The test results are summarized
on Plate B 15
Nine pH and resistivity tests were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the corrosion potential of the soils along the utility
alignments The test results are summarized on Plate B 16
