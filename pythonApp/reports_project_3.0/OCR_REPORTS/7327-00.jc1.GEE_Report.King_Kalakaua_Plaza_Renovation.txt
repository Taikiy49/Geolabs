PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The existing King Kalakaua Plaza Renovation project site is located at 
2080 Kalakaua Avenue between Olohana and Kalaimoku Streets in the Waikiki district of 
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. It was proposed to renovate the existing 
four-story building into a six-story hotel structure. 
We understand that the existing building has four levels of retail space 
(approximately 20 feet floor to floor) and two basement levels for parking, and is 
supported on a mat foundation. The renovation concept consists of demolishing the upper 
three levels and renovating the building into a new six-story hotel structure (approximately 
9 feet floor to floor). Based on the information provided, we understand that the renovation 
will keep the total load from the new taller structure approximately equal to the total load 
from the current shorter structure. Therefore, no additional structural load will be added 
on the existing mat foundation. 
We anticipate that the site is underlain by potentially liquefiable soils. As a result, 
the project site would be classified as Site Class F based on the International Building

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which 
time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred 
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano, and its geomorphology 
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio-eustatic fluctuations of the sea 
level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain. The 
subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age 
coral/algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea that are 
intercalated with terrigenous sediments. The base of the stratigraphic section in this area 
is Koolau Basalt. 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred 
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. These 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower 
relative to the present sea level on the Island of Oahu. About 15,000 years ago, a 
relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred. During that sea level rise, the valleys in the 
project area were drowned. In the last 10,000 years or so, the sea level has adjusted to 
its present stand. Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in 
low-energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits of soft and/or 
loose sediments along the coast in the areas that were formerly valleys and 
drainageways. 
In the early part of this century, the Waikiki area consisted of low, marshy wetlands. 
As the City of Honolulu grew and the Waikiki area was urbanized, man-made fills were 
placed to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons for development. The fill materials placed 
on the lagoonal deposits consisted of silty sands with coral fragments. The lower clayey 
materials were deposited during periodic influxes of non-marine alluvial debris, which

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling two boring, designated as 
Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 124 to 126.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The project site is generally underlain by surface fill materials and lagoonal 
deposits over recent alluvium and coralline reef formation. In general, surface fills 
consisting of medium dense to dense silty and sandy soils extended down to about 4 to 
8 feet below the ground surface. The surface fill layer is generally underlain by loose/soft 
lagoonal deposits. The lagoonal deposits extended to about 34 to 35 feet deep. It should 
be noted that the lagoonal deposits encountered at the project site are compressible when 
subjected to structural loads. 
Below the compressible lagoonal deposits, stiff clayey alluvial soils and coralline 
reef formation were generally encountered. The recent alluvium deposit was extended to 
about 49 to 50 feet deep. The coralline reef formation generally consisted of interbedded 
soft to medium hard coral formation and medium dense to dense sandy and gravelly 
coralline detritus, and extended to the maximum depth explored of about 126.5 feet below 
the ground surface. It should be noted that loose silty sand and silty gravel pockets were 
encountered in the coralline detritus. 
We encountered groundwater at depths of about 4 to 6.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface, corresponding to about Elevations +1 to +0.5 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). 
Due to the proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean, water levels are expected to 
vary with tidal fluctuations. It should be noted that water levels also may vary with 
seasonal precipitation, storm surge conditions, groundwater withdrawal, and other 
factors.

SEISMIC DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition) (IBC 2006), the project site 
would be subjected to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to be 
addressed. The following sections provide discussions on the seismicity, soil profile for 
seismic design, and the potential for liquefaction at the project site. 
Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts 
in the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during volcanic 
eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground movement of magma 
that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences 
thousands of earthquakes each year, but most of the earthquakes are so small that they 
can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of the earthquakes are 
strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of magma are 
concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island of Hawaii. 
Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent of earthquakes) are 
related to volcanic activity, the risk of high seismic activity and degree of ground shaking 
diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has 
experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, 
earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have occurred in the 
County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands of Hawaii and Maui 
may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, several small landslides occurred on the 
Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of 1938 (M6.8). In addition, some houses 
on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 
(M7+). 
Due to the relatively short period of documented earthquake monitoring in the 
State of Hawaii, information pertaining to earthquakes that were felt on the Island of Oahu 
may not be complete. In general, over the last 150 years of recorded history, we are not 
aware of reported earthquakes greater than Magnitude 6 occurring on the Island of Oahu. 
Based on available information, we understand an earthquake of about Magnitude 5.6 
occurred on June 28, 1948 in the vicinity of the Island of Oahu, possibly along the 
hypothesized and controversial Diamond Head Fault feature.

Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition), the project site should be 
evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction. Based on the subsurface conditions 
encountered and a design peak ground acceleration of 0.20g, liquefaction may occur 
resulting in appreciable settlements at the ground surface. Therefore, the effects of potential 
liquefaction should be taken into consideration in the design of the proposed development. 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the 
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess pore 
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes. In this 
process, when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration (such as during an 
earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in pore 
water pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of 
pore water pressure, the effective stress (internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under 
sustained vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could equal the overburden pressure, 
essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous 
fluid. During liquefaction, the soil acquires mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and 
vertical movements, and if not confined, will result in significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained sands 
and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics 
of a soil deposit are as follows:

Our field exploration generally encountered a surface fill over loose/soft lagoonal

the highly compressible lagoonal deposits, we generally encountered stiff clayey alluvial 
soils and coralline reef formation with interbedded soft to medium hard coral formation and

Based on our geotechnical analysis, the loose granular lagoonal deposits (about 27 
to 30 feet thick) encountered at the site may appear to have a factor of safety of less than 
1.0 with regard to potential soil liquefaction. Therefore, we believe the project site could be 
subjected to appreciable ground settlements (on the order of up to about 6 to 8 inches) in

lateral spreading associated with liquefaction would not be a significant design consideration 
based on the relatively flat site topography. 
Soil Profile 
Based on the subsurface materials encountered and our engineering analyses, the 
project site is underlain by potentially liquefiable soils. Therefore, the project site would need 
to be classified as Site Class F based on IBC 2006 (Table No. 1613.5.2). Per Section 
1613.5.5.1 of the IBC 2006, a site-specific ground motion study will need to be performed to 
determine the appropriate values for seismic design considerations. 
Seismic Shear Wave Velocity Testing 
Due to the presence of loose lagoonal deposits near the ground surface, shear wave 
velocity profiling was performed in an effort to more closely analyze the subsurface 
conditions for seismic design considerations. We performed shear wave velocity profiling 
using seismic cone penetration testing (SCPT) equipment at discrete depths extending to a 
depth of approximately 126.5 feet below the existing ground surface at Boring No. 2 location. 
Based on the subsurface conditions, the weighted average shear wave velocity for the 
materials within the upper 100 feet of the soil profile is on the order of about 1,079 feet per 
second at the test location. As mentioned above, the project site would need to be classified 
as Site Class F based on IBC 2006 due to the presence of potentially liquefiable soils. 
Site-Specific Response Spectrum Analysis 
As required by IBC 2006, a site-specific elastic response spectrum (site-specific 
design ground motion) was developed using the Shake 2000 computer program for the 
one-dimensional wave propagation analysis. In general, the site-specific elastic response 
spectrum (site-specific design ground motion) was generated in general accordance with 
Chapter 21, Site-Specific Ground Motion Procedures for Seismic Design contained in ASCE 
7-05 (Minimum Design Loads for Buildings and Other Structures). 
A site response model was established based on the subsurface conditions 
encountered in our borings and shear wave velocity testing at the project site. Non-linear 
shear stress-strain relationships were selected for each subsurface geo-material, as 
appropriate.

Seven pairs of horizontal ground motion acceleration time histories were selected 
from seven earthquake events with earthquake Magnitudes of 5.99, 6.0, 6.19, 6.6, 6.61, 6.7 
and 6.93.  The seven pairs of horizontal ground motion acceleration time histories were from 
earthquake events recorded on the Island of Hawaii and California.  Each selected time 
history was scaled to approximate the maximum considered earthquake acceleration of 
0.27g expected at the project site. The ground motion time histories were input at the base 
of the soil profile and propagated through the soil profile to generate the site-specific ground 
motion time history and response spectrum at the ground surface. The elastic response 
spectrum was calculated based on materials with 5% of critical damping. If a damping ratio 
other than 5% is desired, please contact our office for additional information on modifying 
the site-specific elastic response spectrum. 
It should be noted that the existing building has four levels of retail space and 
two basement levels for parking and is supported on a mat foundation. Based on the 
information provided, we understand that the bottom of the mat foundation is located at 
about 28 feet below the ground surface. Because the existing building is in regular shape 
with rigid mat foundation at the bottom of the basement level, we envision that the ground 
motion would be transmitted at the bottom of the mat foundation level. Therefore, the 
site- specific elastic response spectrum at the bottom of the mat foundation level was 
generated for the structural analyses. For comparison, the site-specific response spectrum 
at the ground surface is also generated. 
Site-specific response spectra are dependent on the magnitude of the earthquake 
and the distance of the seismic event to the project site in addition to subsurface conditions. 
Based on our review of the geologic conditions, there are no known significant mapped fault 
zones located on the Island of Oahu; therefore, we do not anticipate seismic events to occur 
within 5 miles of the project site. As a result, we do not expect the site-specific elastic 
response spectrum to be affected by near-source effects from seismic events. Results of 
the site-specific elastic response spectrum along with some of the more critical seismic 
design parameters are summarized below.

BASEMENT WALL DESIGN PARAMETERS 
Based on the information provided, we understand that the existing two-level 
basement supported on the mat foundation system will remain. Bottom of the basement 
floor is at an elevation of about -18 feet MSL with a 4-foot thick mat foundation slab. It is 
desired to mobilize the lateral resistance of the mat foundation to support additional lateral 
structural demands. 
Lateral loads acting on the basement mat foundation may be resisted by frictional 
resistance developed between the bottom of the mat foundation and the bearing material 
and by passive pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the basement wall.  For

resistance and friction for passive resistance) of lateral load resistance may be used in 
combination.  It should be noted that the lateral load resistance of mat foundation is 
primarily from friction between the base of the mat foundation and the supporting 
subgrade materials.  The passive pressure resistance components should be secondary 
lateral load resistance mechanisms.  It is important to recognize that the component of 
passive pressure resistance should be reduced to account for strain compatibility due to 
the depth of the mat foundation. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our drilled borings, we 
recommend using a coefficient of friction of 0.3 to evaluate the sliding resistance of 
foundations bearing on the loose sandy and/or silty soils. We believe that lateral load 
resistance due to passive pressure may be estimated using a triangular pressure 
distribution of 250 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (above ground water table) 
and 150 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (below ground water table). 
It should be noted that the amount of deflection needed to mobilize the full passive 
pressure is at least 12 inches.  For deflections less than that needed to mobilize full 
passive pressure, a linearly interpolated reduced passive pressure may be used in 
evaluating the lateral load resistance of the footing.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling two boreholes, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 
124 and 126.5 feet below the ground surface.  The approximate boring locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary wash drilling/coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  Soils, where encountered, were classified in general 
conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.1.  Symbols and abbreviations used in the boring logs are also 
described on the Soil Log Legend.  Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance 
with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1.1 through A-2.4. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD 
Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In addition, we 
obtained some samples from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation.  Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation.  The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description.  Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3.  The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate.  Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.  
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality 
of rock masses.  RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling, 
mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks.  If 2.5 feet of sound material is 
recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%.  Generally, the following is used to describe 
the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of 
Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

Seismic Shear Wave Velocity Test 
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling of the subsurface materials at the project site 
was performed using Seismic Cone Penetration Testing (SCPT) equipment. The purpose 
of the seismic shear wave velocity profiling of the subsurface materials is to more closely 
analyze the seismic design considerations for the project. Shear wave velocity testing 
was performed at a boring location, designated as B-2 on the Site Plan (Plate 2). The 
seismic shear wave velocity profiling was performed at various depths extending to a 
maximum depth of about 126.5 feet below the ground surface. 
In order to conduct the shear wave velocity test in the boring, the test boring was 
advanced utilizing rotary coring methods to the maximum depth of the boring. A log of the 
materials encountered in the boring is presented on the Logs of Boring in Appendix A. 
After the boring was advanced to the maximum depth of the borehole, the bored hole was 
backfilled with 0.25-inch diameter coated bentonite pellets. The temporary casing from 
the coring operations was used as a tremie pipe to place the bentonite pellets starting 
from the bottom and advancing upward. When the bentonite pellets are in contact with 
the groundwater in the borehole, the pellets start to hydrate slowly. As the bentonite 
pellets hydrate, they swell and soften. The probes were then pushed through the softened 
bentonite extending to a depth of about 126.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10-ton 
steel electronic subtraction cone with an apex angle of 60 degrees and a projected 
surface area of 1.55 square inches (10 square centimeters) into the ground at a rate of 
approximately 20 mm/second (+5 mm/second). The depth of the cone penetration was 
recorded at about 2-inch (50-mm) intervals using an electronic depth sensor attached to 
the cone rod. The values of the tip resistance, friction ratio (sleeve friction over tip 
resistance), and pore pressure measurements in this instance are irrelevant. 
The cone carries a uniaxial horizontal accelerometer geophone to detect the arrival 
of a shear wave generated and propagated from the ground surface. The seismic 
measurements were made when the SCPT had stopped and a shear wave was sent into 
the subsurface. A shear wave was generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with 
a switched hammer. The propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone 
was measured at each discrete depth interval. The vector difference of these depths divided 
by the time difference for the shear wave to arrive at the various depths provided the 
average shear wave velocity over the depth interval. 
The shear wave velocities measured in the boring are presented on Plate C-1. The 
weighted average shear wave velocity calculated for the top 100 feet of the soil profile is 
1,079 feet per second. The weighted average shear wave velocity was calculated based 
on the average shear wave velocity method described in Section 1613.5.5 of the 
International Building Code (2006 Edition).
