SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered residual and/or saprolite soils extending 
to the maximum depths explored of about 26.5 to 36.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to occur or 
change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other 
factors. 
We recommend using a shallow foundation system, consisting of spread and/or 
continuous strip footings, bearing on recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills 
to support the proposed new buildings and reservoir structures, with an allowable bearing 
pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf). Footings should be embedded a 
minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grades. Footings constructed near the tops 
of slopes or on the sloping ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum 
horizontal setback distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the 
face of the slope. 
Slope stability analyses were performed on the new fill slope planned for the three 
reservoir orientation options. To achieve factors of safety of 1.5 and 1.1 for long-term slope 
stability under static and seismic conditions, respectively, we recommend modifying the 
design slope inclination to 3H:1V. As an alternative, the new fill slopes with 2H:1V inclination 
may be reinforced using geogrids and/or geotextiles to strengthen the fill materials. 
Based on the provided plans and our field exploration, we anticipate the site grading 
construction may encounter clayey residual and saprolite soils with abundant basaltic 
cobbles and boulders similar to what was encountered in the borings drilled. The anticipated 
on-site residual/saprolite soils may be excavated with conventional heavy excavation 
equipment. However, the hard cobbles and boulders may require the use of hoerams. 
Based on the anticipated vehicular traffic loads and subgrade conditions at the 
project site, a pavement section consisting of 5.5 inches of asphalt treated base course may 
be considered for the driveways. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
geotechnical recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Ka’ala Ranch Electrical Improvements project located in the Waialua 
area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown 
on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of building 
foundations, stability of the new fill slope, retaining structures, site grading, and 
pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
Based on the information provided, we understand that the proposed improvement 
project involves constructing a non-regulated reservoir structure. The proposed reservoir 
structure will be about 400 feet by 275 feet with concrete lining. The project also includes 
the design and construction of a barn and control building with driveways using recycled 
asphalt concrete material. 
Details of the structural design were not available at the time this report was 
prepared. We understand three options of the proposed reservoir structure are 
considered with various orientations. 
Based on the preliminary grading plans provided, we anticipate site grading 
consisting of cuts up to about 30 feet deep and fills up to about 50 feet thick may be 
required to achieve the design finished grades for the project.

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. 
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice 
Age) in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. 
The project site is situated on a narrow coastal plain and pediment, which was 
formed by the accumulation of a relatively thick wedge of colluvium, alluvium, and marine 
sediments along the base of an extensive wave-cut cliff on the northern flank of the 
Waianae Volcano. Geological conditions at the site and in its immediate vicinity can 
generally be delineated by the location and slope of the various portions of the site. In the 
area close to Farrington Highway, where the slope is relatively level, the shallow 
subsurface conditions generally consist of marine sediments, dune sands, and recent 
alluvium. The soils in this area generally have poor engineering characteristics (e.g., soft 
soil conditions and high groundwater table). 
As the slope steepens to the inland side of the site, the subsurface conditions 
become dominated by older alluvial materials that have accumulated in alluvial fans 
originating from major drainageways draining to the north from the Waianae Range. 
These alluvial materials consist of interbedded clays, silts, gravels, and some areas of 
boulder conglomerate. In some areas, the alluvium has weathered to a residual-type soil 
and the underlying conglomerates have weathered to a saprolitic state. The soils in these 
areas are generally more competent; however, they exhibit moderate to high shrink-swell 
potentials. 
Further inland, near the base of the Waianae Range, the geology grades to an 
accumulation of talus and colluvium at the base of the wave-cut cliff. This interbedded 
talus and colluvium generally consist of angular boulders and cobbles with a matrix of 
highly expansive clay. A large number of boulders and cobbles create grading difficulties, 
and the highly expansive clay is poorly suited for most engineering uses.

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is generally located at Ka’ala Ranch in the Waialua area on the 
Island of Oahu. The project site is bounded by undeveloped land near the south end of 
Waialua Ranch Road. 
At the time of our field exploration, the project site was mostly covered by grass 
with some trees. Several basaltic cobble/boulder outcrops were observed near the stream 
traversing the southeast end of the project site.  
The topography of the site was generally downward from southwest to northeast 
with steeper downward slopes from northwest to southeast surrounding the stream areas.  
The ground surface elevations of the site ranged from about +120 to +217 feet Mean Sea 
Level (MSL).  
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling six borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, in the vicinity of the planned reservoir structure 
extending to the maximum depths drilled of about 26.5 to 36.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. In addition, two test pits, designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 and 2, were 
excavated to a depth of about 7 feet below the existing ground surface. Two bulk samples 
of the near-surface soils were obtained to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2.  
Based on our field exploration, the subsurface conditions at the site consisted of 
residual and/or saprolite soils extending to the maximum depths explored of about 26.5 
to 36.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In general, the residual and saprolite 
materials consisted of stiff to hard clayey/silty soils.  
Dense older alluvium consisting of gravelly boulders was encountered in our test 
pits from a depth as shallow as 1 foot below the existing ground surface. It should be 
noted that Boring Nos. B-3 and B-6 also encountered very dense weathered basaltic 
boulders at about 19.5 to 23.5 feet below the existing ground surface.

SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The project site is generally underlain by residual and/or saprolite soils extending 
to the maximum depths explored of about 26.5 to 36.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to occur or 
change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other 
factors. 
We recommend using a shallow foundation system, such as spread and/or 
continuous strip footings, bearing on recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted 
fills to support the proposed new buildings and reservoir structure, with an allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf). Footings should be 
embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grades. 
Based on the results of the slope stability analyses performed on the new fill slope 
planned for the three reservoir orientation options, we recommend modifying the design 
slope inclination to three horizontal to one vertical (3H:1V) to achieve the factors of safety 
of 1.5 and 1.1 against the global slope instability under static and seismic conditions, 
respectively. As an alternative, the new fill slopes with 2H:1V inclination may be reinforced 
with geogrids and/or geotextiles to strengthen the fill materials. 
Detailed discussions and recommendations for these items and other geotechnical 
aspects of the project are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Building Foundations 
In general, we believe the new proposed buildings may be supported on a shallow 
foundation system consisting of spread and/or continuous strip footings. We recommend 
an allowable bearing pressure of up to about 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used for the design of the shallow foundations bearing on recompacted on-site soils 
and/or compacted fill material needed to raise to the design finished grades. 
This allowable bearing pressure is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased 
by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. In

Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on the sloping ground should be

foundation settlement. 
The bottom of footing excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent 
relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placing 
reinforcing steel and/or concrete. Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of 
footing excavations should be over-excavated until dense/stiff materials are exposed in the 
footing excavation. The over-excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill 
materials moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Alternatively, the bottom of the footing may 
extend down to bear directly on the underlying competent material. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed as recommended herein, we 
estimate total settlements of footings supported on the recompacted on-site soils and/or 
compacted fills to be on the order of about 1 inch or less. Differential settlements between 
adjacent footings supported on similar materials should be on the order of about 
0.5 inches or less.

Bishop’s simplified method of limit equilibrium was used to evaluate the balance of 
forces and moments acting within the slope sections to determine the factors of safety 
against the global slope instability based on the generalized subsurface profile and shear 
strength parameters. The Bishop’s simplified method of slope stability analysis assumes 
a circular slip surface, which is divided into slices with finite mass. The following 
parameters were used in our global slope stability analyses to evaluate the rotational 
slope failure mechanism: 
1. 
Slope geometry. 
2. 
Density and shear strength of the in-situ soils and new fill materials based 
on the borings drilled at the project site and our experience in the area. 
To evaluate the long-term stability of the proposed site grading schemes for the 
three reservoir orientation options, effective stress soil parameters were determined and 
utilized in our analyses. Generally, minimum factors of safety of 1.5 and 1.1 are typically 
required for long-term slope stability under static and seismic conditions, respectively. 
The slope stability analyses were conducted on four cross-sections provided, designated 
as East, East Southeast, South (Section 1) and South (Section 2). 
To evaluate the potential for slope instability due to seismic ground motions, a 
conservative horizontal seismic coefficient of 0.125g was used for the seismic conditions 
at the project site. It should be noted that the slope stability analysis during a seismic 
event assumes that the slope will be allowed to move laterally by up to about 1 to 2 inches 
in the event of an earthquake.

3.4.3 Dynamic Lateral Earth Forces 
Dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading will need to be considered in 
the design of retaining structures, such as the retaining walls, reservoir walls, and 
manhole walls. For design in accordance with the 2012 IBC, the force due to 
dynamic lateral earth pressures associated with seismic loading (PBA = 0.249g) 
may be estimated using 5.0H2 pounds per linear foot of wall length for level backfill 
conditions, where H is the height of the wall in feet. It should be noted that the 
dynamic lateral earth forces provided assume that the wall will be allowed to move 
laterally by up to about 2 inches in the event of an earthquake.  
If the estimated amount of lateral movement is not attainable or the retaining 
structure is restrained, the retaining structure should be designed with higher 
dynamic lateral forces for a restrained condition. For a restrained condition (less 
than 0.5 inches of lateral movement), dynamic lateral forces due to seismic loading 
may be estimated using 12.5H2 pounds per linear foot of wall (H measured in feet) 
for level backfill conditions. 
The resultant force should be assumed to act through the mid-height of the wall. 
The above dynamic lateral earth forces are in addition to the static lateral earth 
pressures provided previously. An appropriately reduced factor of safety may be 
used when dynamic lateral earth pressures are accounted for in the design of the 
retaining structure

3.4.4 Drainage 
The retaining walls should be well-drained to reduce the potential for build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide 
zone of permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the 
base of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, 
a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used 
instead of the drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product also should be 
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
The backfill from the bottom of the wall to the bottom of the perforated pipe or 
weephole should consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential 
for significant water infiltration into the subsurface. In addition, the upper 12 inches 
of the retaining structure backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials 
to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure 
unless covered by concrete slabs at the surface.

3.6.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be cleared 
and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other

Soft and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm material, and the 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills. 
After clearing and grubbing, finished subgrades and areas designated to receive fills 
should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least

3.6.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the excavated on-site materials may be reused as a source of general fill 
materials provided that the materials are free of deleterious materials and the 
material has particles with a maximum size of 3 inches. Imported fill materials, if 
required, should consist of non-expansive select granular materials, such as crush 
coralline or basaltic materials. The materials should be well graded from coarse to 
fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain 
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should 
have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 
percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
General fill materials used for the new fill slope should have an angle of internal 
friction of at least 34 degrees when tested by the standard direct shear test (ASTM 
D3080). The sample to be tested should be compacted to 90 percent relative 
compaction at a moisture content of at least 2 percent above the optimum.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling six 
borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending to the maximum depth drilled of 
about 26.5 to 36.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, two test pits, 
designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 and 2, were excavated to depths of about 7 feet below the 
existing ground surface and lengths of about 10 feet. Two bulk samples of the near-surface 
soils were obtained to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the near-surface 
soils. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings 
were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock samples were described in general accordance 
with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-8.  
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
