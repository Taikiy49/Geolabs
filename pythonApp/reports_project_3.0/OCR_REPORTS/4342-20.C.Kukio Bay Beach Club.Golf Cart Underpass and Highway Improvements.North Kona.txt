INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering reconnaissance
performed for the proposed Golf Cart Underpass and Highway Improvements for the Kukio
Bay Beach Club located in the District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our background research and presents our
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed Golf Cart
Underpass and Highway Improvements project These recommendations are intended for the
design of foundations retaining structures and grading only A Pavement Justification Report
for the highway improvements portion of the project has been issued separately The findings
and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes including the following Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had
activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the site
appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe type flows which spread and ponded as they
approached the ocean A a lava is typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular
flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and
boulders Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal
structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe lava These
cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and they represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may
extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and pockets inter flow
cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted through
the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as the
major water transmission features

SITE DESCRIPTION
As previously mentioned the project site is located to the southwest of the adjacent
Hualalai Resort in the Kaupulehu area of the District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii
The proposed Golf Cart Underpass and Highway Improvements project site is located along
a portion of the Queen Kaahumanu Highway as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The terrain at the project site is generally rugged which reflects the original surface
of thick basaltic lava flows of the a a and pahoehoe forms The majority of the project site is
underlain by a a and pahoehoe lavas at the ground surface and embankment fills in some
areas of the existing highway The pahoehoe lava flows are generally located toward the
northern portion of the site Some collapsed lava tube features were observed in the project
vicinity during our reconnaissance of the project site
Based on the topographic map provided the project site is gently sloping downward
from southeast to northwest The existing ground elevations at the project site generally
range from approximately 180 to 220 feet MSL Vegetation at the project site is
generally sparse with some wild grasses growing through the crevices of the lava flows

Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions described above we believe that the on site
basalt rock formation would provide suitable foundation support for the proposed Con Span
bridge An ultimate bearing capacity of up to 20 000 pounds per square foot psf may be
used to evaluate the extreme event limit state of the foundations bearing on basalt rock
formation To evaluate the strength limit state of the foundations a resistance factor of
0 60 or a bearing pressure of up to may be used
It should be noted that some embankment fills associated with the highway construction
should be anticipated at the site Therefore footings for the proposed structure should be
embedded deep enough to bear directly on the basalt rock formation or a minimum of
2 feet below the lowest adjacent finished grade whichever is deeper Where foundations
are located next to utility trenches or easements the foundations should be embedded below
a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or
extended to as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the
potential for appreciable foundation settlement
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the footing subgrades the materials
should be over excavated and replaced with structural fill The over excavation should be
extended until the underlying dense materials are exposed The over excavation should be
backfilled with structural fill materials that are moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction As an
alternative the bottom of footing may be extended down to bear on the underlying competent
material

Foundation Probing
Based on our exploration and experience in the area voids and or cavities appear to
be present in the basaltic lava flows at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of
foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities below foundations we recommend
that a program of cavity probing and grouting of the foundation locations for the Con Span
bridge structure be implemented during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probe holes be drilled at 10 foot on
centers for the continuous strip footings In addition probe holes should be drilled at each
isolated spread footing The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and
should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation If cavities and or
voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probe holes
should be drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the
cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with
controlled low strength material CLSM with a slump of about 6 to 9 inches The CLSM
should be injected at low to moderate pressures As an alternate to CLSM sand cement
grout with a slump of about 6 to 9 inches may also be used
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during bidding In
addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting operations
are completed The contractor should also be made aware that a longer lag time between
probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in the construction
schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of cavities and
to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or changed
conditions are observed

Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 foot wide zone of permeable
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation placed
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the retaining
structures discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes Where provided the
weepholes should be spaced not more than 6 feet apart As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used instead
of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the retaining structure
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of granular fill material
less than 3 inches in maximum dimension Unless covered by concrete slabs the
upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious material
well graded materials to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration behind
the retaining structure

Requirements subsection It should be noted that disposal of boulders in the general
fill material will require that the entire fill mass be compacted and properly choked off
at the surface to reduce the potential for migration of fines through the voids between
boulders
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to
at least 95 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills should be placed
in level lifts not exceeding 18 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other
types of acceptable compaction equipment
GEOLABS INC
W O 4342 20 C Page 17
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
majority of the rocks or 5 feet whichever is less Placement should be by haul trucks
traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the currently placed lift
Spreading of the rock fill should be by a large bulldozer to facilitate seating of the
rocks The rock fill should be watered heavily during placement by water trucks
traversing in front of the current rock lift face and spraying water continuously during
placement Compaction equipment such as a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar
heavy compaction equipment providing suitable energy to achieve the required
compaction should be utilized The number of passes usually six to eight passes to
be made to achieve proper compaction should be determined at the on set of site
grading by the performance of a trial compaction test or by plate bearing tests
Subsequent layers of rock fill above the previously placed rock fill should provide a
choking layer of smaller sized boulder materials to fill in the voids between the
boulders of the preceding rock fill layer
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of rocks
boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of passes
by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Based on this testing program the number of passes
may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction For preliminary
estimating purposes compaction may be accomplished by using a large vibratory
drum roller having a static weight of at least 15 tons traveling at a speed of about
300 feet per minute The roller should pass over the fill surface at least eight times
for structural fill and six times for general fill For general fill a Caterpillar D 9
bulldozer or similar size equipment may be used in lieu of the vibratory drum roller

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on available subsurface information it appears that most of the cut slopes
would expose the pahoehoe basalt formation In general we believe that cut slopes
planned at the site may be designed with a slope inclination of one and one half
horizontal to one vertical or flatter
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings clinker zones and or
voids may be encountered at the cut slope face In the event that clinker zones are
exposed at the cut slope face the cut slope may need to be flattened Large cavities
that may be exposed on the cut slope face should be backfilled and or grouted if
necessary A representative from Geolabs should observe and map the cut slopes
during construction to evaluate whether the exposed cut slope materials are
consistent with our assumptions
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the general fill and structural fill materials
recommended herein may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter In
addition fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be benched A keyway
should be provided for fill slopes greater than 10 feet in vertical height placed on
existing ground steeper than 5H 1V The fill slope face should be finished to a
relatively smooth and well compacted surface The fill slope face should be free of
