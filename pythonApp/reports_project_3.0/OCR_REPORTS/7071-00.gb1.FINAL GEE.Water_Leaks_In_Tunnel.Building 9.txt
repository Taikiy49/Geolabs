SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our observations, several construction/expansion joints were observed in 
the tunnel lining.  The construction/expansion joints at the tunnel walls were sealed except 
for the upper 2 feet.  The unsealed construction/expansion joints show evidence of past 
water leaks.  The past water leaks were determined from the staining around the 
construction/expansion joint area.  We observed staining at the lower portion of the tunnel 
walls, which is likely from water dripping down the tunnel walls from the unsealed portion 
of the construction/expansion joint.  Staining was also observed at the edges of asphaltic 
concrete pavement in the areas near the construction/expansion joints.   
We believe that the leakage in the tunnel is primarily through the unsealed portion 
of the construction/expansion joints (ceiling and upper 2 feet of walls).  The source of the 
water appears to be rainfall from the surface that infiltrates through the soil.  It is not 
economically practical to reduce soil permeability in an attempt to reduce leakage in the 
tunnel, since this would require consolidation and cut-off grouting on a massive scale from 
the surface or from within the tunnel. Collecting water from the construction/expansion 
joints and routing the water into pipes and discharging into drain inlets outside of the 
tunnel may not be practical since it may be costly to run drainage pipes down the length of 
the tunnel.     
A cost effective way to reduce leakage in the access tunnel would be to seal the 
construction/expansion joints in the tunnel lining.  The text of this report should be referred 
to for detailed discussion and preliminary recommendations.

SECTION 1. GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering evaluation 
performed in support of the Eliminate Water Leaks in Tunnel, Building 9 project within 
the NSA Kunia Facility in the Kunia area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the data and results from our inspection of the access 
tunnel to Building 9 and presents our preliminary recommendations to provide 
improvements to the leakage problems in the access tunnel to Building 9. The data, 
results, and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at 
the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is at the NSA Kunia Facility in the Kunia area on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii.  Access to Building 9 at the facility is via a concrete tunnel that is about 
1,184 feet long.  We understand that water leaks have been observed in the access 
tunnel occasionally.  This results in small ponds of water in some locations. Since the 
leak locations were not documented previously, visual signs of ponding or water stains 
were used to identify potential leak zones. 
The leak locations were identified and possible means to eliminate/reduce the 
leaks were evaluated. The results of this study may be used to assist in preparation of 
future remedial construction to eliminate/reduce the leaks and identify specific 
exploration or testing that may be required for final design. The limits for the project 
begin from the access tunnel north portal to the entrance to Building 9.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are 
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against 
the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch. The dips 
of the lava beds generally range from 3 to 5 degrees. The lava flows on the plateau have 
weathered to depths of about 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors of the 
soil. The deep narrow canyons, such as Waikakalaua, Kipapa, and Kaukonahua, are the 
result of this weathering.  
The central operations area of Schofield Barracks lies at the mouth of a broad 
amphitheater headed valley where colluvial and alluvial materials intercalate with and 
sometimes overlie the Koolau lavas. These colluvial and alluvial materials weather to 
darker brown and reddish brown soils and generally have higher clay fractions than the 
more silty residual soils.  The brown and reddish brown soils generally consist of ancient 
alluvial deposits overlying weathered volcanic rock.

SECTION 3.  TUNNEL HISTORY 
3.1 
Tunnel Design and Construction 
There is limited information regarding the design and construction of the access 
tunnel to Building 9.  The tunnel was constructed between 1942 and 1944.  Drawings 
dated July 1942 indicate that the access tunnel was constructed using the 
cut-and-cover method.  This method consists of excavating a large trench down to the 
tunnel invert and foundation elevations.  The tunnel structure is constructed in the 
trench.  After construction of the tunnel structure, the trench is backfilled with soil 
typically consisting of the excavated soils from the trench. 
The available drawings indicate that the tunnel trench excavation may have 
ranged between 40 and 50 feet below the existing ground surface.  It is likely that the 
tunnel trench excavation was backfilled with the excavated on-site soils which may have 
consisted of silty and clayey soils. 
Based on the available drawings and our observations, the access tunnel 
consists of three separate tunnel linings, which are described below.  The three different 
tunnel lining sections are shown on Plates 3.1 through 3.3.

Building 9 and extends about 120 feet north along the tunnel alignment.  
The tunnel width is about 42.3 feet with the tunnel height ranging from 
about 17.8 feet at the center line to about 15.2 feet at the tunnel edges.  
The lining section is supported on shallow continuous footings about 
16.5 feet wide.   
Tunnel Lining Section B:  This tunnel lining section begins at the end of

SECTION 4.  TUNNEL INSPECTION 
4.1 
Inspection Methodology 
Detailed tunnel inspections for the access tunnel walls and ceiling were 
conducted on September 3, 9, and 11, 2014.  The inspection of the air intake and 
exhaust shafts was conducted on September 11, 2014.  
The access tunnel was inspected following the methodology in the Federal 
Highway Administration, Highway and Rail Transit Tunnel Inspection Manual (2005).  In 
general, map sheets for each tunnel lining section were prepared.  Inspection 
observations were recorded on these sheets, which are located in Appendix A. 
Observations that were recorded for the tunnel lining are listed and discussed below: 
1. 
Cracking (CR):  A crack is linear, but often irregular, fracture in the 
concrete caused by tensile forces exceeding the tensile strength of the 
concrete.  Cracks can occur during curing (non-structural shrinkage 
cracks) or thereafter from external load (structural cracks).  They may 
extend partially or completely through the concrete section.  The following 
are the ratings used to describe the cracking: 
Rating 1:  Up to 0.03 inch wide. 
Rating 2:  From 0.03 inch to 1/8 inch wide. 
Rating 3:  Wider than 1/8 inch. 
2. 
Leakage:  Groundwater penetrating through the concrete tunnel via 
defects such as cracks or construction related features such as 
construction joints.  The following are the ratings used to describe the 
leakage: 
Rating 1:  Concrete surface is wet, but there are no drips. 
Rating 2:  Active flow of less than 30 drips per minute. 
Rating 3:  Active flow of more than 30 drips per minute.

SECTION 5.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
We believe that the leakage in the tunnel is primarily through the unsealed 
portion of the construction/expansion joints.  The source of the water appears to be 
rainfall from the surface that infiltrates through the soil.  It is not economically practical 
to reduce soil permeability in an attempt to reduce leakage in the tunnel, since this 
would require consolidation and cut-off grouting on a massive scale from the surface or 
from within the tunnel. Additionally, the magnitude of the leakage is considered small 
and only occurs intermittently, after heavy rains. 
We did not observe an existing drainage system within the tunnel structure; 
therefore, leak management such as collecting water from the construction/expansion 
joints and routing the water into pipes and discharging into a drainage system in the 
tunnel may not be practical since it would be costly to install drain inlets and pipes down 
the length of the tunnel.     
A cost effective way to reduce leakage in the access tunnel would be to seal the 
construction/expansion joints in the tunnel lining.  Detailed discussions are presented in 
the following sections. 
5.1 
Leak Management 
One option to mitigate the tunnel leakage would be implement a leak 
management system, which would consist of collecting water leaking from the cracks 
and directing into a drainage system.  Water leaking from the joints would be collected 
in panning material consisting of synthetic sheets.  The panning sheets would be placed 
over the unsealed portion of the joint.  The sheets would be secured to the tunnel 
surface by masonry nails.  The masonry nail penetrations are sealed with caulk, and the 
edges of the sheets (except the bottom) are sealed with batten strips and caulk.  The 
bottom edge of the panning would be left unsealed to allow water to discharge into a 
drainage system.  We did not observe an existing drainage system in the tunnel 
structure.  Therefore, a drainage trough or drain pipes would have to be installed along 
the edges of the tunnel.  Installing a drainage trough would consist of excavating 
through the asphaltic concrete pavement and possibly into the tunnel foundations of

5.2 
Leak Sealing 
Many different types of materials are available for sealing leaking cracks or 
construction/expansion joints.  Sealant options can generally be divided into two 
categories.  One option is to use a continuous membrane that forms a barrier to water 
flow.  The second option consists of injecting grout or resin to seal water pathways such 
as cracks and joints. 
Membranes used to seal tunnels post-construction are typically applied as spray.  
Since most of the leakage are occurring at the construction/expansion joints, we believe 
that the spray-on membrane is not applicable for this project. 
Grouts used for sealing cracks and joints can be made up of very fine particles 
such as cement or microfine cement held in suspension during injection, or by 
chemicals in solution.  The primary characteristics used in selecting a grout for 
waterproofing are its viscosity, setting time, strength, permeability, durability, toxicity, 
flammability, and cost. 
The types of cement grouts for leak sealing are summarized as follows: 
1. 
Sand-Cement Grouts - Commonly used to fill large voids or for contact 
grouting of a concrete lining.   
2. 
Neat Cement Grouts - Commonly used to seal joints in rock or cracks in 
concrete with a significant aperture. Requires sufficient confinement of the 
grout until the grout has set, otherwise the grout will flow uncontrollably 
with incomplete results.   
3. 
Microfine Cement Grouts - Used to seal joints in rock or cracks in 
concrete.  Requires sufficient confinement of the grout until the grout has 
set.

4. 
Cement-Chemical Grouts - These types of grouts use cement in 
combination with an epoxy or other chemical.  They can be very effective 
at sealing leaks, but often require a great deal of joint preparation before 
application. In addition, cement-chemical grouts are not particularly 
effective on cracks subject to continued expansion, as the material is 
relatively brittle.  Also, most products are rated for only limited hydrostatic 
pressures.  Some products require application of two or more components 
to effectively seal the crack.  Occasionally, in the area surrounding a crack 
that is sealed, new cracks that were dry will start to produce water.  These 
cracks must then be grouted if inflows are unacceptable.  It is difficult to 
predict quantities of “secondary” leaks as a result of chasing water inflows. 
An example of a cement-chemical grout is Krystol T1 & T2 by Kryton.  
Chemical grouts generally have lower viscosity than cement grouts.  In the fluid 
state, chemical grouts can penetrate into very fine cracks where cement grouts cannot 
enter.  They are generally more costly than cement-based grouts.  Types of chemical 
grouts for leak sealing are summarized as follows: 
1. 
Silicate-Based Grouts - These grouts consist of sodium silicate gel and a 
reactant, usually calcium chloride.  Of the chemical grouts, silicates are 
usually the least costly and least toxic, although they are a severe skin 
irritant.  Silicate grouts have good durability but do suffer from leaching 
and syneresis (an exudation of water from the gel, producing shrinkage in 
the gel). 
2. 
Plant-Product-Based Grouts - Usually petrochemical by-products, these 
grouts include lignin-based grouts.  Most plant-product-based grouts have 
fallen out of favor, as acrylamides and polyurethane grouts have become 
more popular in underground applications. 
3. 
Acrylamides - These grouts are resins that absorb water and penetrate 
into tighter cracks compared to microfine cement.  They usually consist of 
two organic monomers as a two-part catalyst system.  Gel time can be 
controlled by varying the amount of catalyst used.  Viscosity of these 
grouts is very close to that of water.  Although water within the gel 
evaporates in low humidity situations causing shrinkage of the gel, the gel 
will reabsorb water in wet conditions, returning approximately to the 
permeability of the original gel.  Ingredients of the acrylamide grouts are 
highly toxic and must be handled with complete protection, although the 
set gel is nontoxic.  Acrylamide grout was used extensively to seal leaks in 
the Detroit-Windsor vehicular tunnel (Hebert et al., 1996). 
4. 
Phenoplast Resins - These grouts are obtained through the reaction of 
phenol and an aldehyde.  These grouts have viscosities approaching that

5. 
Polyurethane - This type of grout has become one of the most popular

HA-Flex LV by De Neef and Sikafix HH by Sika.   
Since polyurethane grout is flexible and are available in variations of viscosity, 
we believe that polyurethane grout may be used to seal the construction/expansion 
joints. The basic procedure for polyurethane grouting of the joints in tunnel is as follows: 
1. 
The joint is cleaned (remove debris and efflorescence). 
2. 
Small holes (less than ½ inch) are drilled directly into the joint at an angle 
to intersect the joint approximately 6 inches behind the face of the 
concrete.  The holes are approximately 12 inches apart and alternating 
from the left to the right side of the crack in a “stitching” pattern.  If rebar is 
encountered, the hole is re-drilled as close as possible to the planned 
hole. 
3. 
A simple rubber-sleeved mechanical packer is used. 
4. 
Each hole is flushed with water to clean the hole and verify adequacy of 
the connection between the holes and return through the crack to the 
concrete surface. 
5. 
A hole is grouted until return is seen in the joint or the adjacent hole. 
Grouting continues along the joint in the adjacent holes until the entire 
joint is grouted.  If an excessive volume of grout would flow out from one 
small area of the joint, grouting should stop and time allowed for grout to 
set.  Once this grout sets, the joint grouting procedure begins again.  New 
holes are drilled, if required. 
6. 
After the injection resin has cured, the packers are removed and the area 
cleaned.

lining, there could be dry hairline cracks that extend through the thickness of the tunnel

leaking.  Since leaking in the tunnel does not occur regularly, new leakage through dry 
cracks may not be evident during construction. Therefore, provisions should be made in 
the contract documents to have the contractor seal cracks that began leaking (within a 
one year period) after the initial sealing of the joints mentioned herein.
