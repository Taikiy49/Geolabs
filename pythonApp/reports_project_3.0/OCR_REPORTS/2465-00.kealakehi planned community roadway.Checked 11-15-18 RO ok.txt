INTRODUCTION
In accordance with your request we have performed a geotechnical engineering
exploration for the subject portion of the Kealakehe Planned Community Roadway project
This report presents our findings and recommendations
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface soils
and rock conditions along the proposed road alignment in order to provide data for the
formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the design of the
proposed road grading and infrastructure installation

GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The terrain is generally rugged with some steep slopes locally approaching a ratio
of one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V The terrain typically reflects the original surface
of thick basaltic lava flows in the A a form The ground surface elevations range from
approximately 48 feet above sea level to approximately 690 feet above sea level with
drainage trending towards the west
The lower half of the site is generally barren rocky land with a vegetation cover that
grades from thin grasses with some haole koa christmas berry and other dry land
vegetation in the central portion to a dense cover of low haole koa in the upper portion
of the site
Most of the ground surface is covered with basaltic cobbles and gravel Clinker
with localized exposures of in situ basalt rock formation
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Kealakehe Planned Community Roadway is a planned development of
residential and recreational facilities As part of the development it is proposed to
construct a major road connecting Queen Kaahumanu Highway with the existing
development at Kealakehe adjacent to Palani Road This proposed major road will be
about 8700 feet in length and will connect to a 2200 foot long extension of the existing
Kealakehe Street It is anticipated that the road construction will also involve the
installation of underground utilities along the road alignments

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The project site is underlain by prehistoric basaltic lava flows of the Hualalai
Volcanic Series which were most likely deposited during the late Pleistocene Stearns and
Macdonald 1946
At and in the area of the project site the lava flows are in the A a form which is
characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly surface of fragmental clinker underlain by a
dense core of massive basalt which frequently has a thinner less continuous layer of
clinker below the massive core
Visual observations of exposures of existing road cuts indicate that the clinker
layers may be as much as ten feet thick with discontinuous ledges of hard basalt
interbedded within the clinker

Site Preparation
Prior to commencement of grading all areas within the roadway and right of way
grading limits should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting

Soft or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below any areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to firm natural material and the
resulting depression backfilled with well compacted engineered fill

Excavation
The field exploration and visual observations made at the site and in its vicinity
indicate that the site is most likely underlain by clinker with ledges of dense basalt at
relatively shallow depths
It is anticipated that some hard excavation and or ripping may be encountered in
localized cemented areas or when ledges are encountered
Cemented clinker and ledge basalt formation will most likely require some hard
excavation such as heavy ripping hoerams or blasting
Generally the excavation method should be left to the contractor Blasting should
be considered to expedite the excavation process Generally consideration should be
GEOLABS HAWAII

given to the economics of blasting to initially reduce the size of the excavated rock
material and to facilitate excavation as opposed to extended periods of heavy ripping at
high equipment rates However care should be exercised to avoid overshooting which
could disrupt the clinker or rock formation resulting in a loss of strength
Blasting Considerations
It is our opinion that much of the rock at the site could be excavated without the
use of explosives However the progress of deeper excavations may be slow and
inefficient The following discussions are presented to aid in the selection of an
excavation method
Hazards or objectionable impacts from blasting operations generally originate from one
or more of the following
1 Flyrock
2 Ground Vibration
3 Air Blast
Flyrock
Flyrock is the blaster s term for flying rock or other debris which is launched into
the air during open blasting by the force of the detonating explosives These
projectiles often have tremendous kinetic energy and can crush or destroy objects
in their flight path or at their point of impact
Flyrock generated during the earthwork for the proposed road grading could
potentially strike nearby features If the flyrock has sufficient kinetic energy it
could create significant damage
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2465 00 Page 10
The occurrence of flyrock may be reduced and possibly eliminated by increasing
the confinement or depth of the explosive charges and or by using safety mats
Generally flyrock would be a consideration only in the portions of the project near
populated or developed areas such as the existing residential area near Palani
Road Queen Kaahumanu Highway and Honokahau Harbor
Ground Vibration
With respect to the subject project ground vibration is probably the most critical
potential impact When an explosive detonates below ground it generates intense
stress waves in the surrounding material These waves crushes the rock
immediately adjacent to the explosion and permanently distorts and cracks the
rock for some distance
When the intensity of the stress waves attenuates to the point that there is no
longer any permanent deformation of the rock the stress is propagated through
the rock as elastic waves These elastic waves create ground vibration
The potential of the ground vibration to damage a structure is a function of the
intensity of the vibration and the natural frequency of the structure According to
the Du Pont Blaster s Handbook a formula has been developed to relate the
intensity of ground vibration at a given point in terms of particle displacement
velocity and acceleration to the weight of the explosive charge and the distance
between the explosion and the point of interest
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2465 00 Page 11
The equation to determine the peak particle velocity is in the form of
V KW R where
V is the the peak particle velocity how fast the ground moves
K is the ground transmission constant which is empirically determined based on
the rock surrounding the explosives and at the point of interest
W is the weight of explosives used
m and n are geologically controlled empirical constants and
R is the distance between the explosion and the point of interest
Work by the U S Bureau of Mines and others indicate that the equation may
be reduced to
V 160 R W 7 where
W is the maximum charge weight in pounds per delay period of eight milliseconds
or more
Blasters use this equation to estimate the peak particle velocity for a given
scaled distance
Vibration would also be a consideration in the vicinity of developed areas
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2465 00 Page 12
Air Blast
The air blast from an explosion is a compressional wave in air Noise is the
portion of the air blast with frequencies between 20 and and
concussion is the portion with frequencies below 20 Hz The Du Pont Blaster s
Handbook also sets forth a scaled distance relationship for air blast
The effects of air blast can be a consideration for much greater distances
depending upon atmospheric and or other conditions
lf blasting is to be used for the earthwork we recommend that the scaled
distances of the explosive charges be limited to those that will generate peak
particle velocities of 1 5 inches per second or less at any nearby structure
Blasting should be performed in accordance with the applicable statutes rules and
regulations and only by a qualified operator with a proven record We recommend
the prequalification of contractors and blasting operators bidding on the project
If blasting is to be utilized for the earthwork we recommend a program of test
shots with instrumentation by seismographs or other appropriate means The
program should start with large scaled distances and gradually decrease to
determine the lowest allowable scaled distance

the rock materials at the project site will experience a volume change when excavated
processed and used as fill material In general the net change will depend upon whether
the material is loose clinker versus dense basalt the average particle size of the fill
material and the amount of void space in the fill

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the drilling and sampling
of three 3 borings to depths ranging between 14 0 and 17 0 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Truck
mounted rotary drilling equipment was utilized to drill the borings
Samples of the rock material encountered at the project site were obtained by
using diamond core drilling techniques Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the bit was
mechanically recovered for examination classification and testing The recovered rock
cores were logged by an Engineer or Geologist from our firm Descriptions of the
recovered rock cores are presented on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 3
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material that is in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities
discounting any drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 and would be shown on the
Boring Logs as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative
quality based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD
would be very difficult to rip and would probably require blasting or splitting for
excavation

The subsurface conditions at the site were also explored by the performance of
four 4 seismic refraction surveys at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The seismic refraction surveys were conducted using a single channel signal
enhancement seismograph with a 12 pound sledge hammer as a seismic energy source
Refraction seismography is based on the transmission of elastic energy
compressional waves through geologic materials Generally the denser or more
consolidated a material is the faster the transmission of the compressional waves These
waves are refracted at interfaces between materials
The refracted waves are received by geophones on the surface and recorded by
the seismograph operator
This technique permits mathematical calculations which will yield approximate
depths or thicknesses of materials below the survey and give their approximate
compressional wave velocities The calculated velocities may be empirically related to the
excavation characteristics of the materials

of the attached Appendix

Il Special Considerations
A
Cut slopes shall be excavated to the lines and grades indicated on the
contract drawings and where necessary adjusted in accordance with the
recommendations of the Geotechnical Engineer during construction
All trench backfill placed above 12 inches of the top of the pipes shall be
compacted in maximum lift thickness of 9 inches unless specifically
authorized by the Geotechnical Engineer The Geotechnical Engineer may
require thinner lifts should compaction operations consistently fail to produce
the specified compaction requirements In any event the required levels of
compaction shall be obtained throughout the full depth and width of each
layer of backfill

A Clearing and grubbing shall be performed and debris shall be disposed of
in accordance with Section 10 CLEARING AND GRUBBING of the Standard
Public Works Specifications
All vegetation and deleterious materials encountered within the Grading Limits
shall be cleared and disposed of off site This removal shall be completed

B Organically contaminated topsoil shall be stripped prior to actual grading
Materials determined as being unsuitable for placement in compacted
structural fill or backfill shall be used in landscaping or shall be removed and

removed and the resulting depression backfilled and compacted in

D Loose surface soils encountered in areas to receive fill shall be removed to

and backfilled with compacted select materials
E All surfaces shall be observed and approved by the Geotechnical Engineer
prior to the placement of any fill

Fill Materials
Material for fill shall consist of on site soils or approved import material Fill
material shall contain less than 5 percent organic matter and other deleterious
material and shall not contain rocks or lumps in excess of twelve inches 12 in
diameter

Representative samples of imported materials to be used as compacted fill shall

Placement and Compaction of Fill
A
The fill shall be placed in horizontal layers not exceeding eighteen inches
18 in loose thickness Each layer shall be spread uniformly and
blade mixed to attain uniformity of material and water content within each
layer
Additional fill material shall not be placed on any fill layer which has not been
properly compacted Subsequent removal of any unacceptable layer shall
be done at the Contractor s expense
Rocks greater than twelve inches 12 in diameter shall be removed from the
fill unless specifically approved for placement in the fill by the Geotechnical
Engineer
Rock Fragments or boulders larger than twelve inches 12 in diameter may
be utilized provided they are not concentrated in any one area and that there
is sufficient fine grained material to fill the voids between the rocks
GEOLABS HAWAII
Standard Earthwork and Grading Specifications Continued Plate B 8
W O 2465 00
D Boulders ranging in size from 18 to 24 inches encountered in excavations
may be used in the deeper fills provided that the following instructions are
followed
1 Boulders shall be spread out and must not be nested together They should
be placed so that compaction equipment will be able to compact around
them
2 No part of the boulder should encroach any closer to finished subgrade
than the boulder s largest dimension and in no case be closer than 4
feet to finished subgrade
3 No boulder larger than 5 feet in its largest dimension should be placed
in the fill It may be necessary to reduce the size of larger boulders or
dispose of them off site
4 Care must be exercised to avoid placement of boulders in the proposed
utility alignments to prevent difficulty in the later excavation of the utility
trenches
E Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in excavated
volcanic rock material due to the large particle size Instead a testing
program consisting of visual observations and test strips to determine the
lift thickness and number of passes of a compactor required to achieve the
desired compaction should be conducted at the start of the project The
number of passes can then be used as the criteria for compaction
GEOLABS HAWAII

F The compaction equipment may be sheepsfoot vibratory or pneumatic tire
rollers and capable of producing the specified results in an expedient and
efficient manner The equipment shall be in good working condition fully
ballasted and self cleaning
G The compaction shall be accomplished in a systematic and uniform manner
over the entire fill area to produce a uniformly compacted fill
H The Contractor shall be required to obtain the above specified levels of
compaction out to the finish slope of fill slopes This may be achieved by
either over building the slope and cutting back to the compacted core or by
direct compaction of the slope face with suitable equipment
I Fill placed against previously graded cut or fill slopes shall be properly keyed
through topsoil or loose slope material into firm material and the transition
stripped of loose material prior to fill placement Fill shall be placed and
compacted from the low side up
J Fill slopes should be planted or protected from erosion by methods indicated
on the plans
K _ Fill material placed in an unsatisfactory condition and not in accordance with
these specifications shall not be approved and shall be removed or reworked
until the required density is obtained
