2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows from
Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau known
as Schofield Plateau
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of this plateau generated
sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and to the
south these sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and
coraValgal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to
over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the
confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present
Development within the last 100 years has brought the project area to its present form

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling six borings designated as
Boring Nos 1 through 6 extending to depths ranging from about 5 to 21 5 feet below the
existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by surface
fills on the order of about 1 to 5 feet thick consisting of dense silty coralline gravel and stiff
silty clays Below the surface fills coral formation was encountered and extended to the
maximum depth explored of approximately 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface
The coral formation generally appeared to be moderately weathered and medium hard in
nature It should be noted that the strength characteristics of coral formation are highly
variable The depositional nature of the formation may create localized areas of hard
crystalline coral intermixed with soft severely fractured detritus It should be noted that
coral formation is frequently cavernous or contain pockets of voids Although cavities were
not encountered in our drilled borings the potential for the presence of cavities and or
voids exists at the project site
Groundwater was encountered at depths of about 14 5 to 16 feet below the existing
ground surface in the borings drilled at the time of our field exploration It should be noted
that groundwater levels are subject to change due to seasonal precipitation surface water
runoff and other factors In addition it should be noted that water seepage is commonly
encountered in coral formations due to infiltration of surface water

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered surface fills overlying
coral formation extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 21 5 feet
below the existing ground surface Since cavities and or voids are commonly
encountered in coral formations we believe that the primary geotechnical considerations
for the proposed construction will include the following 1 probing and grouting for
potential cavities and or sinkholes beneath the building foundations and 2 proof rolling of
future slab and pavement subgrades to detect the presence of potential near surface
cavities sinkholes or loose zones
Based on the relatively competent subsoil conditions encountered in our borings
and the anticipated design structural loads it is our opinion that spread and or continuous
footings may be used for support of the proposed buildings planned at the project site An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for
design of footing foundations bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new compacted
fills
To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the
cavities and or sinkholes below foundations we recommend that a program of cavity
probing and grouting at the foundation locations be implemented during construction In
addition we recommend that the excavated subgrades underneath future slabs and
pavements be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller to detect the
presence of near surface cavities sinkholes or loose zones Special attention should be
given to the subgrade preparation requirements presented in the Site Preparation section
of this report to reduce the potential for future distress on the floor slabs and pavements

3 1 Building Foundations
Based on the design concept and the subsoil conditions encountered at the site we
believe that spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the proposed
buildings planned at the site An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be
used for design of footing foundations bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new
compacted fills This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
For footings bearing on the in situ stiff dense soils the bottom of the footing
excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a
relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing steel or
concrete If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing
excavations they should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The
over excavation may be backfilled with on site soils compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down to the
underlying competent materials For footings bearing on new compacted fills the bottom
of the footing excavations should also be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum
dry density
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades Footings located adjacent to the planned retaining walls should be
embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations or the
retaining walls should be designed to resist the additional structural loads
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of
the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary

3 3 Slabs On Grade
We understand that concrete slabs on grade will be utilized for the construction of
the new retail development Based on our field and laboratory test results we believe that
the building slabs may be constructed on grade provided that the slab subgrade is
properly prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented below and in the
Site Preparation section of this report
In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer consisting of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below the slabs to provide
more uniform support of the slabs and to serve as a capillary moisture break To reduce
the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel cushion layer Flexible
floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better
mask minor slab cracking

3 4 Site Preparation
Based on the grading plan provided we anticipate that the majority of the site
preparation work for the proposed project will consist of fills on the order of about 2 to
4 feet thick to achieve the design finish grades As previously mentioned the project site
may be underlain by cavities and or sinkholes near the existing ground surface
Therefore special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation for the project
development
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation organic debris construction debris
deleterious material and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of
properly off site or in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials with the cleared and grubbed spoils Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing should be over excavated to expose firm
natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill
The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site Contract
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and
engineered fill placement to account for variations in the over excavation quantities
The excavated subgrades underneath future slab and pavement areas should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller to a
minimum of 90 and 95 percent relative compaction respectively to detect the presence of
potential near surface cavities sinkholes or loose zones If the proof rolling operations
detect a near surface cavity or loose zone the area should be ripped open cleaned of
debris and then backfilled with compacted fill material or lean concrete

3 6 Surface Water Runoff Disposal System
One in situ falling head permeability test was conducted to evaluate the infiltration
characteristics of the subsoils to provide data for design of the facilities to be used for
disposal of storm water runoff The permeability test was conducted in Boring No 2
extending to a depth of about 15 feet below the existing ground surface
During the falling head test the borehole was carefully filled with water and the time
intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which to base
calculation of the permeability value of the subsurface soils Based on the permeability
test conducted the calculated permeability of the tested subsoils is on the order of about
2 5 x 10 feet per minute The results of our permeability test are presented on Plate C 1
Due to the variability of the subsoil conditions the absorption capacity of the disposal
system should be confirmed by additional percolation tests during construction
The planned storm water runoff disposal facilities snould be located away from the
building foundations since water infiltration into the ground from the disposal facilities
would cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent loss of bearing strength of the
foundation soils resulting in foundation settlement and building distress

These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the concrete to
reduce the potential for water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain surface runoff away from the slab and footing
foundations
