In accordance with the scope of services outlined in our revised fee proposal dated 
March 19, 2020, we performed a geotechnical engineering exploration for the Old 
Haleakala Highway Sidewalk Improvements, Pukalani Street to Makawao Avenue project

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located along Old Haleakala Highway between Pukalani Street 
and Makawao Avenue in the Makawao area on the Island of Maui. The project location 
and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. Based on the 
information provided, we understand that a new sidewalk is planned along the southern 
side of the highway. In addition, we understand retaining walls with heights up to 5.5 feet 
for grade separation will be designed for the project. 
Due to the presence of low overhead electrical lines and underground utilities, we 
proposed a field exploration program consisting of Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) 
testing to evaluate the subsurface soil conditions at the project site.  Our experience in the 
area suggested that the project area is generally underlain by surface fills overlying 
residual soils, saprolite, and basalt rock formation at shallow depths.  Special attention 
was given to the bearing capacity and lateral earth pressures of the subsoils at the project 
site for the design of the new retaining structures.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions by performing DCP testing at ten selected 
locations, designated as DCP-1 through DCP-10, to provide data and information for our 
evaluation and analyses.  The approximate DCP test locations are shown on the Overall 
Site Plan, Plate 2, and Site Plan, Plates 3.1 through 3.5. 
The DCP test provides a measure of a material’s in-situ resistance to penetration. 
The test is performed by driving a metal cone into the ground by repeatedly striking it with 
a 17.6-lb (8-kg) weight dropped from a distance of 22.6 inches (575 mm). The penetration 
of the cone is measured after each blow and is recorded to provide a continuous measure 
of shear resistance. Test results can be correlated to Standard Penetration Test (SPT) 
blow count, California Bearing Ratio (CBR), in-situ density, resilient modulus, and bearing 
capacity.

The DCP tests extended to depths of about 8.4 inches (0.7 feet) and 102 inches

Based on our literature review, field observations, and DCP testing, it appears that 
the project site is likely underlain by medium stiff to hard clayey silt soils and basaltic rock 
formation at deeper depths. 
FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the current plans, we understand a new sidewalk is planned along the 
southern side of the Old Haleakala Highway. In addition, we understand retaining walls 
with heights up to 5.5 feet for grade separation will be designed for the project. We 
envision that the retaining walls will be supported on shallow foundations consisting of 
continuous strip footings bearing on the medium stiff to hard clayey silt soils, or basalt rock 
formation anticipated at the project site. Detailed discussions and recommendations for the 
project are presented in the following sections. 
Retaining Structures 
Based on the information provided, retaining structures less than about 5.5 feet 
high are planned for grade separation between the proposed sidewalk and adjacent 
properties. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, the following guidelines may 
be used to design the retaining structures. 
Wall Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we believe that 
the retaining walls may be supported on a shallow foundation system bearing on 
the medium stiff to hard clayey silt soils anticipated at the project site. An allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the 
design of foundations bearing on medium stiff to hard clayey soils.  This bearing 
value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient 
loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Bottom of wall footings should be embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below 
the lowest adjacent finished grade.  Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of 
the slope should be constructed in stepped footings.  Foundations located next to 
utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary 
plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench, or the bottom of 
footing should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines.  
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade 
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable 
foundation settlement.

Lateral loads acting on the retaining walls may be resisted by frictional resistance 
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by 
passive earth pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation 
system.  A coefficient of friction of 0.35 may be used for footings bearing on the 
medium stiff to hard clayey silt soils.  Resistance due to passive earth pressure 
may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot 
per foot of depth (pcf).  The passive earth pressure value assumes that the soils 
around footings are well-compacted.  Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the 
passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of the soils should be 
neglected.

Dynamic Earth Pressures 
Dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading will need to be considered in 
the design of retaining structures, such as the site retaining walls. For design in 
accordance with the 2006 IBC, the force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures 
associated with seismic loading (PGA = 0.296g) may be estimated using 6.1H2 
pounds per linear foot of wall length for level backfill conditions, or 25.1H2 
pounds per linear foot of wall length for maximum two horizontal to one vertical 
(2H:1V) sloping backfill conditions where H is the height of the wall in feet. It 
should be noted that the dynamic lateral earth forces provided assume that the 
wall will be allowed to move laterally by up to about 1 to 2 inches in the event of 
an earthquake.  
If the estimated amount of lateral movement is not attainable or the retaining 
structure is restrained, the retaining structure should be designed with higher 
dynamic lateral forces for a restrained condition. For a restrained condition (less 
than 0.5 inches of lateral movement), dynamic lateral forces due to seismic 
loading may be estimated using 12.2H2 pounds per linear foot of wall 
(H measured in feet) for level backfill conditions. 
The resultant force should be assumed to act through the mid-height of the wall. 
The above dynamic lateral earth forces are in addition to the static lateral earth 
pressures provided above. An appropriately reduce factor of safety may be used 
when dynamic lateral earth pressures are accounted for in the design of the 
retaining structure. 
Drainage 
Retaining walls should be well-drained to reduce the potential for the build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures.  A typical drainage system should consist of permeable 
material, such as ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation material, placed directly around a 
perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of the wall discharging to an 
appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, a prefabricated drainage 
product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used instead of the drainage 
material. The prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically 
connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall.

Unless covered by concrete slabs or pavements, the upper 12 inches of backfill 
should consist of relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for water 
infiltration behind the walls.  In addition, the backfill below the drainage outlet (or 
weepholes) should consist of the relatively impervious material to reduce the 
potential for water infiltration into the footing subgrade.  The relatively impervious 
material should be compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction. 
Concrete Slabs-on-Grade 
Exterior concrete slabs, such as walkways, will be required for the project. In 
general, we recommend providing a minimum 6-inch thick cushion fill layer consisting of 
aggregate subbase below exterior concrete slabs that will not be subjected to vehicular 
traffic. The aggregate subbase supporting the exterior concrete slabs should be 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The subgrade soils below the 
select granular fill should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned 
to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum 
of 90 percent relative compaction. To reduce the potential for significant slab cracking 
due to concrete shrinkage, crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the 
width of the walkways with expansion joints at right-angle intersections. 
Concrete Driveways 
We envision concrete driveway aprons will be required at the entrances of existing 
residential driveways. In addition, we anticipate the driveway aprons will be constructed 
near the existing grades. In general, we recommend supporting the concrete driveway 
aprons directly on a minimum 6-inch thick layer of aggregate subbase material. 
The compaction requirement for the finished subgrade of the 6-inch thick layer of 
aggregate subbase supporting the driveway apron slabs should be compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  The aggregate subbase should be placed as 
soon as possible after preparation of the slab subgrade. To reduce the potential for 
substantial shrinkage cracks in the slabs, crack control joints should be provided at 
intervals equal to the width of the slabs with expansion joints at right-angle intersections.  
We recommend designing the concrete apron slabs for the intended loads. For 
design of structural slabs subject to vehicular traffic, a modulus of subgrade reaction of 
200 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection (pci) may be used for the compacted 
aggregate subbase. In addition, provisions should be made for proper load transfer across 
slab joints that will be subject to vehicular traffic. 
Differential movement and cracking of the driveway/walkway slabs may be 
attributed to the lack of maintenance of the sidewalk subgrade moisture content after the 
initial subgrade preparation. It should be noted that the moisture content requirement of 
the clayey silt subgrades (at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture) is an important 
requirement considering the potential for expansion of the on-site clayey soils. Therefore, 
the subgrade soils underneath the driveway/sidewalk slabs should be properly

are similar to those encountered in our field exploration. 
Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, 
existing structures, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and 
disposed of properly off-site. 
Loose and/or soft soils, where encountered, should be over-excavated to expose 
firm and/or dense materials, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled 
with well-compacted fills. The excavated loose and/or soft soils should be 
disposed of properly off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, the cut subgrades and areas to be filled should be 
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Where shrinkage cracks develop in the subgrade soil after compaction, we 
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and 
replacement with well-compacted fill. The need for over-excavation due to soft

Fills and Backfills 
Based on the anticipated grading concept, we envision the excavated on-site 
soils may be reused as a source of general fill materials provided that the 
materials are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and clay lumps and rock 
fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. Imported fill materials 
should consist of non-expansive select granular materials, such as crushed coral 
or basalt. The material should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles 
no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material also should contain 
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. In addition, the 
material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or 
more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in 
accordance with ASTM D1883. 
Aggregate subbase materials required for the project should consist of crushed 
basaltic aggregates and should conform to Section 30 of the County of Maui 
Standard Specifications (September 1986). Geolabs should test the imported fill 
materials for conformance with these recommendations 
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in 
loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The 
compaction requirements for finished subgrades subjected to vehicular traffic, 
such as the pavement subgrade, should be increased to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. 
The non-expansive select granular fill materials should be placed in level lifts of 
about 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The 
compaction requirement for the non-expansive select granular fill below the 
pavement section should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. Aggregate subbase course material should be moisture-conditioned 
to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 
8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. Compaction 
should be accomplished by using sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other 
types of acceptable compaction equipment.

In the event of over-ripping of basalt rock formation below the design finish 
grades occurs during the site grading operations, the over-ripped areas should 
be backfilled with select granular fill materials and compacted to a minimum of 
90 or 95 percent relative compaction, as appropriate. 
Because moisture-conditioning and compaction of the clayey silt subgrade soils 
and general fill materials are critical elements of the earthwork, Geolabs should 
perform observations and soil density tests during the site grading operations to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the 
proper moisture content on each fill lift. Where compaction is less than required, 
additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment in the moisture 
content as necessary, to obtain the specified compaction.  
Excavations 
The project site generally is underlain by medium stiff to hard clayey silts and/or 
basalt formation. It is anticipated that the medium stiff to hard clayey silts and 
basalt formation near the ground surface may be readily excavated with normal 
heavy excavation equipment, such as excavators, and also ripped with large 
bulldozers. However, some of the deeper excavations in the basalt rock 
formations may require the use of hoerams or chipping. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are 
based on our visual observation of the basalt formation observed in the existing 
road cuts and our experience in the area. Contractors should be encouraged to 
examine the site conditions and the subsurface data to make their own 
reasonable and prudent interpretation. 
Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, we believe the cut slopes planned at the site exposing medium stiff to 
hard clayey silts may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. In 
areas where dense basalt formation is exposed at the cut slope faces, the cut 
slopes may be steepened to inclinations of 1H:1V or flatter. 
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the general fill and structural fill materials 
may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills placed on 
slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope 
to provide stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations should start 
at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in 
accordance with the fill placement recommendations presented in the “Fill 
Placement and Compaction Requirements” subsection herein. Fill slopes should 
be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a 
well-compacted slope face. In the event over-cutting of a slope occurs, keying 
and benching requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the 
slope to the design grade with sliver fills.  The fill slope face should be free of 
voids, which would allow erosion and migration of fines to occur. In addition,

Underground Utility Lines 
New underground utility lines, such as water, drain lines, and electrical lines, will be 
required for the project. We anticipate most of the trenches for the utility lines will be 
excavated in the medium stiff to hard clayey silts and/or basalt formation anticipated at the 
project site. 
In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) should be provided below the pipes for uniform bearing 
support. Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the 
pipes. It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for the formation 
of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the 
pipes. Improper backfill material around the pipes and improper placement of the backfill 
could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular fill materials 
less than 3 inches in maximum particle size. The backfill should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture, placed in about 8-inch level loose lifts, and mechanically 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Where trenches are below 
pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should 
be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Because basalt rock formation may be encountered at relatively shallow depths, it 
appears that some of the trench work for utility installation may involve excavation in the 
basalt rock formation and may require hard ripping and/or the use of hoerams. The 
contractor must exercise care to avoid over-ripping, which would disrupt the structure of 
the rock formation and result in a potential loss of bearing strength for footings in its 
vicinity.

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and discussions submitted herein are based, in part, upon information 
obtained from the DCP testing in the field. Variations of subsurface conditions between 
and beyond the DCP test locations may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear 
evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the findings and discussions provided herein. 
The field DCP test locations indicated herein are approximate, having been 
estimated by having been obtained using a hand-held Global Positioning System (GPS) 
device. Elevations noted on the DCP test field logs were obtained from Google Earth Pro. 
The physical locations of the DCP test locations should be considered accurate only to the 
degree implied by the methods used.

Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the transformer pad area by performing 
ten Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) tests, designated as DCP-1 through DCP-10, 
extending to depths of about 0.7 to 8.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The 
approximate locations where DCP tests were performed are shown on the Overall Site 
Plan, Plate 2, and Site Plan, Plates 3.1 through 3.5. The test results are shown on the 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Test Field Logs, Plates A-1.1 through A-10.2. 
The procedures used in our DCP tests are in general accordance with 
ASTM D6951, Standard Test Method for Use of the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer in 
Shallow Pavement Applications.  The DCP apparatus consists of a 5/8-inch diameter steel 
rod with a 60-degree conical tip. The rod is topped with an anvil that is connected to a 
second steel rod. This rod is used as a guide to allow a 17.6-lb hammer to be repeatedly 
raised and dropped from a height of 22.6 inches. The connection between the two rods 
consists of an anvil to allow for quick connections between the rods and for efficient 
energy transfer from the falling weight to the penetrating rod. 
Once the test apparatus is assembled, the DCP is placed at the test location and 
the initial penetration of the rod is recorded to provide a zeroing scale. While holding the 
rod vertically, the weight is raised to the top of the rod (22.6 inches above the anvil) and 
dropped. The penetration of the rod is measured after each drop as shown in the field logs 
provided in Appendix A.
