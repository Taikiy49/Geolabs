2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in
age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was partially
buried below Koolau lava
The project site is on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano and _ its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio eustatic
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the
Honolulu Coastal Plain
During the later Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the continental glaciers advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below its present
stand When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to above its present level
Glacio eustatic sea level fluctuations affected the processes of erosion and
deposition The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal and or marine sediments
in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to
carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments
and calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial
erosion of the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils
under relatively high energy conditions

During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial delta and fans seaward by deposition of alluvium over the
previously deposited lagoonal sediments
The geology of the project area has been further complicated by the deposition of
recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of vents of the Honolulu
Volcanic Series which included the nearby Aliamanu Salt Lake Makalapa vent These
eruptions were contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations
Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy
estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits of soft harbor sediments
along the coast in areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways Urban
development and reclamation projects have since altered the natural formation of the
area

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling six borings
designated as Boring Nos 101 through 106 extending to depths of about 51 to
101 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring locations are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on the results of the field exploration the subsurface conditions consisted
of fill and coralline detritus over volcanic tuff and dense coral formation The fill
materials generally comprised of a mixture of medium dense sandy silt and medium stiff
clayey soil extended to depths of about 5 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface
Isolated soft clay pocket was encountered in Boring No 101 below the surface fill
materials
The coralline detritus below the surface fill generally consisted of medium dense
to dense coralline sands and gravel with some zones of dense coral formation The
coralline detritus extended to depths of about 28 to 31 feet below the existing ground
surface followed by volcanic tuff formation about 4 to 10 feet thick Localized loose silty
sand pockets were encountered within the coralline detritus in Boring Nos 105 and 106
The coral formation coral ledge was encountered below the volcanic tuff
formation extending to the maximum depth drilled of about 101 5 feet below the ground
surface It should be noted that the coral ledge was generally highly variable in terms of
thickness and quality The quality of the coral ledge encountered in the borings was
variable ranging from soft in some locations to hard A thin volcanic tuff pocket was
encountered within the coral ledge at the depth of about 93 feet below the ground
surface in Boring No 102
We encountered groundwater in the drilled borings at depths ranging from about
8 to 11 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration The
groundwater levels measured corresponded to about Elevations 1 to 2 feet Mean Sea
Level MSL It should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to
seasonal precipitation runoff and other factors

2 4 Seismic Design Considerations
Based on the International Building Code IBC 2006 the project site may be
subjected to seismic activity Therefore seismic design considerations will need to be
addressed for the project Based on the IBC 2006 the maximum considered earthquake
MCE peak ground acceleration corresponds to about 0 282g The following sections
provide discussions on the seismicity of the Island of Hawaii and the Site Class for
seismic design at the site
Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are solely caused by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most are so small that only sensitive instruments can detect the small tremors
However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause
minor to moderate damage

Liquefaction Potential
In accordance with the 2006 IBC the project site may be subjected to seismic
activity and should be evaluated for soil liquefaction potential
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In
this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibrations such
W O INC Page 7
Hawaii e California
SECTION 2 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing
an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to
dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective stress internal strength
of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the pore water pressure build up
could equal the overburden pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to
zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil
acquires mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements and if
not confined will result in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion It generally is acknowledged that
liquefaction would not occur if the deposit is greater than 40 to 50 feet below the
ground surface In deeper deposits the greater overburden pressure is generally
sufficient to prevent liquefaction from occurring The major factors affecting the
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows
1 Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
3 Magnitude_and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake
Our analysis indicates that the medium dense silty sand and coralline deposits
below the surface fill generally has a factor of safety against liquefaction of greater
than 1 5 However the loose silty sand and coralline deposit has a factor of safety
against liquefaction of less than 1 0 Therefore the project site might be subjected
to appreciable ground settlements in the event of liquefaction during a moderate
earthquake M6 especially within the vicinity of Boring No 105
Based on the review of the site geologic setting and idealized subsurface profiles
this marginal liquefaction potential may be isolated In addition the new air cargo

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
This geotechnical engineering technical memorandum presents preliminary
foundation design for the new air cargo building and maintenance facility
Based on the results of the shear wave velocity profiling testing the average
shear wave velocity for the subsurface materials within the upper 100 feet of the profile
is on the order of about 2 120 feet per second at the test location We believe the
project site may be classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as being a Very
Dense Soil and Soft Rock site corresponding to Site Class C based on IBC 2006
Table No The effects of potential liquefaction and lateral spreading may be
neglected in the design of the proposed air cargo building
We recommend using shallow foundations consisting of spread footings to
support the new air cargo building An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds
per square foot psf may be used to design shallow foundation bearing on the
re compacted fills We recommend embedding foundations a minimum of 36 inches
below the lowest adjacent finished grades with a minimum of 1 foot scarification below
the bottom of the footing and 90 percent recompaction
We recommend a rigid pavement section consisting of 7 inches of Portland
Cement Concrete and 6 inches of aggregate subbase course for the design of the
loading dock subjected to WB 50 vehicle similar to 5 axle semi trailer We also
recommend a flexible pavement section consisting of 3 5 inches of hot mixed asphalt
concrete and 9 inches of aggregate base course for design of the service areas
subjected to heavy forklift traffic
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical recommendations for the

3 1 Shallow Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings and preliminary
structural column loads provided by the structural engineer we believe that the shallow
foundations consisting of spread footings may be used to support the new air cargo
building The following sections provide recommendations for the spread footings
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may
be used to design shallow foundation bearing on the re compacted fills The bearing
loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
It should be noted that soft to medium stiff and or loose to medium dense fill
materials were encountered in the borings To provide relatively firm bearing surface
and reduce future settlement we recommend embedding foundations a minimum of
36 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades We recommend scarifying the
bottom of the footing excavations to a minimum of 1 foot below the bottom of the footing
and recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction prior to placing reinforcing
steel and or concrete
Soft and or loose materials encountered at the footing subgrade level should be
over excavated to expose the underlying firm or dense materials The over excavation
should be backfilled with select granular fill material compacted to no less than
90 percent relative compaction or the footings may extend down to bear on the firm or
dense materials
Foundations next to utility trenches and or easements should be embedded
below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
trench or embedded to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement

Fill and Backfill Materials
The fill and backfill materials may consist of the excavated on site soils provided
that they are screened of vegetation deleterious materials and particles greater
than 6 inches in maximum dimension Imported general fill materials should
consist of materials with particle sizes of 3 inches or less in maximum dimension
The material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 12
or higher and a swell potential of no more than 1 percent when tested in
accordance with ASTM D 1883
