PRESENT CONDITION OF BRIDGE FOOTINGS
During our exploration we noted that generally the footings
of the first four bridges Bridge Nos 1 through 4 are in
need of large repair work while the footings of the last
three bridges may require only minor repairs
Two to three feet of loose material was observed under both
footings of Bridge No 1 and the north footing of Bridge No 4
The footings of the remaining bridges were found to have only
eight to fifteen inches of loose material
The soil under the footing of Bridge 4 only south
footing 6 and 7 has been eroded as much as two feet in some
places and would require repairs The center footing along
with center wall of the double arch Bridge No 1 has been

RECOMMENDATIONS FOR BRIDGE FOOTING REPAIRS
SSPE EVN FOR BRIDGE FE
Based on the above bridge footing conditions we present
the following general recommendations which could be used
for the bridge footing repairs
1 Remove all the loose material under and around all
the bridge footings The loose material should be
removed in small sections to avoid undermining of the
footings The loose excavated material should be
replaced either by compacted granular fill or by
placing concrete rubble masonry CRM in the
excavated areas
Although CRM would be Slightly more costly as com
pared to compacted granular material yet it would
provide long range maintenance cost Savings
2 Similarly eroded material under the bridge footings
should be replaced either with compacted granular
fill or concrete rubble masonry as described above
3 The center footing and wall of the double arch
Bridge No 1 have been completely washed away
A new center footing would be required to replace
the washed away footing

At the location of this footing about 2 feet of
loose material was encountered This footing
being the center support of the double arch bridge
would be subject to large lateral stream flow
pressures which are usually greater at the center
of the stream flow as compared to the two end
footings We recommend that the footing should
be placed at least 5 feet below the loose existing
stream bed To reduce future erosion the stream
bed lining should extend all around this footing
as discussed under Item No 6
4 Boulderly fill behind the bridge walls should be
replaced where boulders have eroded and the
slopes grouted to reduce hydrostatic pressure
build up behind the bridge walls
5 The existing asphaltic concrete pavement is two to
three feet higher than the stream bed and provides
obstruction to the normal stream flow After the
stream beds are formed as described below drainage
pipes should be installed beneath the pavement to
enable the stream water to flow freely Two to
three pieces of pipe minimum 36 inches in diamter
are recommended under the A C pavement for each bridge

6 The drainage under the stream beds should be improved
to provide future protection against erosion of soil
under the bridge footings This could be accomplished
by cleaning the stream beds under all the bridges and
cutting the stream bed to form bed Slopes away from
the bridge footings as shown on the attached Plate 1
The sloping stream bed should be lined with boulders

We would like to point out that the above recommendations
are presented based on the consideration that the future
maintenance costs would be minimal after the repairs of
the bridge footings are completed The first three
recommendations presented above should be considered
most essential for the preservation of the structural
integrity of the historic bridges while the last three
deal mainly with reducing the future maintenance costs
and prolonging the bridge footing stability

CONSTRUCTION MONITORING DURING BRIDGE FOOTING REPAIRS
VURTNG DRIDGE
It is recommended that the soils engineer be present during
remedial construction to monitor the contractor s operations
It is also recommended that the footing excavations be
observed by a soils engineer during construction to see
that adequate bearing has been obtained and that the sub
surface conditions are consistent with the findings of
our subsurface exploration
Any imported material required should be non expansive
and should be tested and approved by the soils engineer
prior to hauling to the site
The recommendations given in this report are contingent
upon the above inspections
