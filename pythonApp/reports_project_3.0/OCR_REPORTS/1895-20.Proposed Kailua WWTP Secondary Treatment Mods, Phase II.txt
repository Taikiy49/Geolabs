INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed Phase II secondary treatment
modifications to the existing Kailua Wastewater Treatment Plant in Kailua
on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location of the project site
and its vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our exploration and our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses of the
data obtained during the exploration These recommendations are intended
for design input and not intended for construction estimate or bidding
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The existing Kailua Wastewater Treatment Plant is being expanded and
modified in phases to meet the growing needs of the Kailua sewer system
and also to comply with the federal environmental standards
The Phase I modifications are currently under construction at the
plant site The proposed Phase II modifications will include the
following major structures
1 Four 4 anaerobic digester tanks Tank Nos 1 through 4
2 One 1 anaerobic digester tank control building
3 One 1 dewatering building
4 One 1 dewatering area scrubber and
5 One 1 anaerobic digesters backup fuel oil system
Several other proposed structures previously planned for the Phase I
modifications are also included in the current phase However the
geotechnical evaluation for these structures had been completed in our
Phase I study and will not be included here

The four 4 proposed digester tanks include two tanks relocated from
the original Phase I locations The proposed tanks will be cylindrical
concrete structures approximately 66 feet in diameter 30 feet high above
the adjacent finish ground and embedded about 17 feet below the adjacent
finish grade at the lowest point
The proposed digester tank control building will be a one story
concrete masonry building approximately 50 by 65 in plan with a basement
level embedded at least 12 feet below the adjacent finish ground
The proposed dewatering building will be a two story concrete masonry

The proposed dewatering area scrubber will be a light weight

The proposed digesters backup fuel oil system consists of a 9 foot

the connecting fuel lines The containment box will be embedded about 2
feet below the adjacent finish grade
New asphalt driveways and underground sewer lines transporting sewage
loads among the proposed structures are also anticipated for the proposed

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located in the existing 25 acre Kailua Wastewater
Treatment Plant to the south of Kaneohe Bay Drive in Kailua on the island
of Oahu Hawaii The treatment plant lies south of a cluster of ponds
connecting Kaneohe Bay and Kailua Bay
The plant is occupied by various existing structures including a
sewage pump station a screening chamber clarifiers a trickling filter
sludge tanks a digester control building a chlorination building an
effluent pump station a centrifuge building sludge drying beds and a
maintenance building The on going Phase I modifications construction
will add a new primary treatment facility and a new administration
building to the plant
The topography of the plant site slopes gently down from about
elevation 36 feet above Mean Sea Level MSL at the south end adjacent to
Kaneohe Bay Drive to about elevation 2 feet MSL towards the north end
near the existing chlorination building Adjacent ground elevations of
the existing buildings and structures range from about 4 to 25 feet MSL
The proposed Phase II structures will be located on vacant lands at
about elevation 6 to 11 feet MSL to the west and south of the
chlorination building
The treatment plant site is extensively grassed and also paved with
asphaltic concrete driveways
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The project site is located at the base of the northeastern Slope of
Puu Papoa which is essentially an erosional remnant of caldera filling
lava flows of the Koolau Volcano
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1895 20 Page 6
After the Koolau Volcano ceased its activity it was followed by a
period of erosion and subsequent submergence during which most of the
eastern flank of the volcano was removed Tater glacio eustatic
sea level changes during the Pleistocene resulted in the accumulation of
several hundred feet of marine and non marine sediments
Our previous Phase I exploration at the plant indicate that the
southwestern upper portion of the site is underlain by highly weathered
basalt and saprolite soils derived from the Koolau Volcano flows while
the central and the northeastern lower portions of the site are
underlain by interbedded sediments of both detrital marine and alluvial
origins The current Phase II exploration generally verified the lowland
subsoil conditions at the central and the northern portions of the site
During the past century human activities have shaped the land to the
present form by fill placement
Subsoil Conditions
The subsurface soil conditions at the proposed Phase II structure
locations were explored by drilling and sampling seven 7 borings to
depths of 21 5 to 111 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
borings are numbered 21 to 24 and 26 to 28 following the number sequence
used for the previous Phase I borings Boring Nos 1 through 20
In addition information from Boring Nos 1 and 15 which were
drilled during our previous Phase I exploraticn were also used
The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
The drilled borings encountered complex subsoil conditions
characterized by a thin fill crust and a thick deposit of soft to hard
alluvial clay locally interbedded with loose lagoonal deposits hard coral
ledges and medium dense reef detritus

Based on our laboratory tests and visual classification it appears
that a majority of the fill crust materials are highly expansive clay

The thickness of the underlying alluvial clay varies widely with
location ranging from approximately 5 feet in Boring No 1 drilled at
the proposed location of Tank No 4 to more than 75 feet in Boring No
22 Grilled at the proposed location of Tank No 3
The alluvial clay also varies in strength or consistency with depth

stiff to hard below the depths of about 30 to 50 feet below existing
ground surface
Interbedded hard coral ledges were encountered in the alluvial clay

proposed location of Digester Tank No 4
In three 3 of the deeper borings Boring and 27

DISCUSSTON AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Since the proposed anaerobic digester tanks are embedded relatively
deep below ground mat and footing foundations had been considered for
supporting the tank structures to take advantage of the removed earth
overburden from the deep excavation However due to the soft subsoils
and heavy tank loads we believe that the settlements would be excessive
and therefore the proposed digester tanks should be supported by pile
foundations
Similarly the proposed on grade dewatering building which is
underlain by similar soft subsoil conditions should also be supported by
pile foundations
The pile foundations should penetrate into the lower stiff to hard
alluvial clay and or medium dense reef detritus layers to derive their
frictional capacity
The proposed one story digester tank control building will have a
basement level embedded at least 7 feet below groundwater level We
believe that a mat foundation may be used since the total building weight
would be fully compensated by the removed earth overburden from the
excavation However the mat foundation should be thick enough to add
sufficient weight to the building to resist the uplift buoyant force due
to groundwater
Mat foundations may also be considered for the proposed dewatering
area scrubber and digester backup fuel oil system which are light weight
structures
Due to the expansive potential of the surface fill materials
subgrade soils below the on grade dewatering building dewatering area
scrubber and digester backup fuel oil system should be over excavated and
properly backfilled with non expansive gravel

Deep excavations are anticipated for the proposed construction and

Pile Foundation
Anaerobic Digester Tanks
Based on the design finish grade the bottoms of the tanks except
Tank No 4 would be bearing directly on the underlying soft clay if
mat or footing foundations are used for the proposed structures The
bottom of Tank No 4 would bear partly on soft clay and partly on the
hard coral ledges as encountered in Boring Nos 1 and 21
Based on the settlement analysis results it appears that total
mat footing foundation settlement in excess of 3 inches and up to 9
inches may occur due to compression of the underlying soft to medium
stiff clay layers under the heavy tank loads For Tank No 4
significant differential settlement may also occur since the tank
foundation will straddle different subsoils
Therefore we recommend that friction pile foundations be used to
support the proposed tank structures The piles would derive their
vertical compressional capacity primarily from adhesion or friction
between the pile shafts and the underlying medium stiff to very stiff
clays and or the medium dense reef detritus deposits and partially
from end bearing on the underlying subsoils below the pile tips
are recommended Different pile lengths should be considered for the
proposed tanks due to the variability in depth and thickness of the
underlying clay and gravel layers that will provide the required
frictional resistance

Pile Driving
The friction piles should be driven either to their full lengths
below the existing ground surface and or to a minimum resistance of
30 blows per foot or 3 blows per inch for the last 6 inches of pile
penetration
The pile driving hammer used should be capable of delivering a
minimum of 24 000 foot pounds of energy during final driving
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption A pile
may be rejected or its design capacity reduced when the driving
process is interrupted for more than 4 hours
During easy driving when the pile tip is still within soft soil
the hammer stroke or energy should be carefully controlled so that
excessive tensile stresses are not developed in the pile
The pile driving should be monitored by a qualified geotechnical
engineer to see that the piles are driven to the sufficient depth or
a competent bearing formation

Excavation Support
A eS ae i i ae en
Based on the soft subsoil and high groundwater conditions we
recommend that braced continuous steel sheetpile walls be used to
support the deep excavations
Depending on the width of the excavations either horizontal struts
or other forms of bracing support such as ring wales and rakers may
be used at the choice of the contractor within practical limits It
is recommended a qualified geotechnical engineer should be consulted
for the adequacy of the bracing support system
Generally it is essential that properly interlocked steel sheeting
be installed prior to excavation and be maintained tight against the
excavation faces with proper bracing to reduce the potential of
adjacent ground loss or movement which may jeopardize the adjacent
existing structures
The steel sheeting should be driven properly with a suitable
sheetpile hammer to sufficient depth to improve the stability of the
excavation bottom and to reduce the possibility of bottom heaving or
quick conditions which may be created by upward seepage pressure
during dewatering of the excavations
Based on our estimate the braced sheeting should be driven to a
minimum embedment of 10 feet below the excavation bottom where
granular subsoils such as sand or gravel layers are encountered The
required embedment depth of the braced sheetpiles may be reduced to 5
feet below the excavation bottom where the alluvial subgrade is
anticipated A factor of safety of 1 5 was assumed for determining
the preliminary sheetpile penetration

Construction Precautions and Monitoring
The sheeting and bracing system used must comply with all applicable
safety requirements The adequacy and safety of the sheeting and
bracing installation operations should be made the responsibility of
the contractor since his representative s on site will have the best
opportunity to observe any changing conditions during construction
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1895 20 Page 19
such as unforeseen subsoils unexpected high groundwater level
unsuitable construction sequence or techniques etc which might
affect the excavation support stability
Even with good construction procedure some movement of the sheetpile
walls might still occur because of changes in earth stresses during
excavation Movements depend greatly on workmanship for example
how quickly and tightly the sheetpiles and the bracing support are
installed It also depends on the types of subsoils the depth and
width of excavation and the type of support used In view of the
complexity of the problem it is difficult to accurately predict
movement of a braced retaining wall system and its adjacent ground
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation support
should be installed as early as practicable and that measurements of
vertical and horizontal ground movements behind the sheetpile walls
are useful especially in areas where ground movements may result in
damage to the existing adjacent structures
The ground adjacent to the excavations should be continuously
monitored for cracks or dips and other indications of movement and
possible sloughing of the excavation faces Such monitoring is
particularly critical in areas adjacent to the existing structures
and utilities
Dewatering
Dewatering of the excavations will be necessary since the groundwater
level is generally above the bottom elevations of the proposed embedded
structures and the subsoils immediately below the excavations may locally
consist of loose sand and gravel
The granular subsoils are relatively permeable and where encountered
at the excavation bottom may be susceptible to piping and quick
conditions Therefore dewatering operations should be done without
pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and should be
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1895 20 Page 20
coordinated with the excavation support so that the excavations are not
jeopardized The operations should be carried out without creating a
quick condition or softening at the bottom of the excavations
Dewatering Method
The method and equipment to be used for the dewatering operations
should be the choice of the contractor within practical limits and
safety considerations It should be realized that the dewatering
system chosen will have an effect on the penetration depth of the
steel sheeting used and also on the resultant areal depression of the
groundwater level
We suggest that the following three 3 basic selection criteria be
considered in determining a suitable method of dewatering
a The method should impose the least disturbance or damage to the
adjacent existing development
b The method should maintain adequate excavation stability and
provide safe working conditions
c The method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modification
to accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of the
excavations could be used This method could reduce the required
sheetpile penetration However the resultant areal depression of the
groundwater table could induce consolidation of subsoils or
settlements below the adjacent existing structures Therefore
recharging injecting water back into the subsoils to balance the
natural groundwater levels and minimize areal drawdown in areas near
the existing adjacent structures may be required to reduce the risk
of settlement to these buildings

Construction Precautions and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system used on depressing the
areal groundwater table must be carefully evaluated The impact and
safety of the dewatering system operations should be made the
responsibility of the contractor since his representative on site
will have the best opportunity to cbserve promptly the effect of
dewatering operations during construction and to implement as soon
as possible necessary precaution or remedial measures including but
not limited to stopping or slowing down the dewatering operations
Caution should also be taken during the dewatering operations not to
dewater the gravel bedding backfill of the utility trenches open to
into the excavations The resultant areal groundwater depression may
extend along the granular bedding and or backfill due to the
relatively high permeability and cause significant ground settlements
and damages to the utility lines Therefore it is also recommended
that the new utility installation should be postponed until after the
building construction dewatering program is completed

Backfill
The trench excavations should be backfilled as soon as the proposed
utility pipes are properly bedded and installed In general the
trench backfill requirements should follow the Standard
Specifications for Public Works Construction City and County of
Honolulu September 1986
The first zone of backfill extends from the bedding material to at
least 12 inches above the top of the pipes Select free draining
well graded granular material is recommended for the first zone of
backfill to minimize the compaction effort required and the resultant
stresses in the pipes The granular material should be well graded
less than three 3 inches in size and have no more than 10 passing
No 200 sieve Compaction by water ponding or jetting may be allowed
provided the granular material is self draining
The trench backfill from 12 inches above the top of the pipes to
within two 2 feet of the finished road grade may consist of the
excavated on site soils except organic clays and silts The backfill
should be mechanically compacted to a minimum 90 of its maximum dry
density per ASIM Test Method D 1557 78
The trench backfill from within two 2 feet of the finished road
grade to subgrade level should consist of select borrow compacted to
a minimum 95 of its maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method
D 1557 78

Site Grading
Besides the deep excavations required for constructing the new
embedded structures and utility lines minor site grading is contemplated
for the proposed construction due to the existing gentle topography at the
project site
At the on set of earthwork the project area within the contract
grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed All existing
vegetation demolished pavements and structures debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
in order to prevent contamination of the excavated materials
After clearing the area to receive fill cut should be scarified at
least 6 inches below the cleared finished grades and recompacted to a
minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
The excavated foundation areas above groundwater level and the new
pavement areas should also be scarified at least 6 inches below the
foundation subgrade level and recompacted to a minimum of 95 of its
maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Loose spots encountered at the foundation or pavement subgrade level
should be removed and replaced with select on site capping fills compacted
to the specifications mentioned above
If required new imported fill materials should consist of
non expansive select granular materials less than 3 inches in maximm size
with less than 20 passing the 200 sieve

other foundation construction phases of the project This is to observe

report then appropriate modifications to the design should be made

to evaluate their compressibility The test results are plotted on Plates

characteristics The test results are presented on Plates B 6 through
