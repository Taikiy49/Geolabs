for the proposed Pave Additional Barge Terminal Area project located within Kawaihae
Harbor Job H C 5313 in the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The general

the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of pavements
light pole foundations retaining structures and site grading only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
GEOLABS INC
W O 4086 00 Page 5
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the southwestern flank of Kohala Mountain which is the
oldest of the five shield volcanoes forming the Island of Hawaii Kohala Mountain was built
during the Pleistocene Epoch by basaltic lava flows from the Polou and Hawi Volcanic
Series Typically the lava flows of the Polou Volcanic Series are composed of tholeitic
olivine basaltic lava flows grading to oceanite In general such flows are characterized as
thinly bedded with a high degree of porosity
Basalt is considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions n general water is transmitted through
the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as
the major water transmission features
Lagoonal sediments and beach sand deposits typically overlie the basalt formations
at the project site A surface layer of man made fill was placed over these deposits for the
development of Kawaihae Harbor within the last century The filling activities have brought
the land area around Kawaihae Harbor to its present form

Pavement Drainage

Pavement Joints
Due to the rigidity of the Portland cement concrete pavements significant stresses
may develop in the concrete from variations in temperature and moisture content In order
to relieve the high level of stress and to reduce the potential for cracking adequate joints
should be provided in the Portland cement concrete pavement The following describes the
various types of joints and general guidelines that should be implemented in the design of
concrete pavements
Crack Control Joints Contraction Joints
In general decrease in temperature or moisture content such as that during concrete
curing will cause the concrete to contract and possibly crack Crack control joints are
used to provide controlled cracking due to volume changes and relieve the stresses
caused by curling or warping Crack control joints such as saw cut joints or formed
grooves are provided to a depth of generally one fourth 7 the slab thickness
These saw cut joints or formed grooves crack control joints provide a weakened
plane which will likely crack through the full depth as the concrete shrinks during the
curing process
In general the spacing of crack control joints should be limited to a maximum of
20 feet Smaller joint spacing should be used if the concrete pavement will be
subjected to extreme temperature gradients during the placement and or life span of
the pavement The crack control joints should be provided in both longitudinal and
transverse directions The transverse joint spacing should not vary from the
longitudinal joint spacing by more than 25 percent
Expansion Joints Isolation Joints
Expansion joints are typically used to relieve compressive stresses developed at
critical locations due to fluctuations in temperature Expansion joints are cut to the full
GEOLABS INC
W O 4086 00 Page 13
depth of the concrete slab and compressible filler materials are placed in the cut
These joints allow for isolated lateral movement of the slabs and reduce the potential
for damage to the adjoining concrete spalling of the edges of the concrete slabs
Expansion joints should be used at pavement intersections and at intersections of
pavements with fixed structures such as the light pole structures Because
expansion joints generally do not provide for load transfer across the joint placement
of dowels with lubrication on one end of the dowel should be considered for load
transfer across the expansion joint where required
Construction Joints
Construction joints are generally required when two abutting slabs are placed at
different times Therefore construction joints usually form the edges of each day s
work For the relatively thick concrete slab 15 inches planned for this project the
construction joints should consist of a keyed or butted joint In general construction
joints should be aligned with the crack control or expansion joints to reduce the
number of pavement joints required
Light Pole Foundations
Due to the significant height of the light poles the light pole foundations will be
subjected to substantial structural loading In order to develop the required bearing and
lateral load capacities the proposed new light pole structures may be supported by a
foundation system consisting of cast in place concrete drilled shafts Based on the
anticipated structural loads and the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we
recommend that drilled shafts with a minimum diameter of 60 inches be used to support the
proposed 39 foot high light pole structures Based on the subsurface conditions encountered
at the project site a drilled shaft embedment length of not less than 15 feet below the design
finished grades may be used for the light pole foundations at the subject site
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally from
skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable compressive load
GEOLABS INC
W O 4086 00 Page 14
capacity of up to 15 kips per shaft may be used for the 39 foot light poles The compressive
load capacity is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient
loads such as wind or seismic forces The end bearing component of the drilled shafts
should be discounted due to difficulties in obtaining a clean bottom during the drilled shaft
installation
Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of shaft and
induced moment in the shaft Our lateral load analyses based on the structural loads
provided indicated that the estimated deflection at the top of the drilled shaft should be less
than about 0 5 inches
The anticipated settlements of the drilled shafts are estimated to be on the order of
less than about 0 25 inches The estimated settlements are generally elastic and should
occur as the loads are applied
Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the light pole foundations because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations
However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the following conditions
would have an impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations
Based on our field exploration medium dense to dense granular fills may be
anticipated at shallow depths Although the surface fill materials generally are not considered
hard difficult drilling conditions such as boulders in the fill may be encountered at localized
areas at the project site Appropriate drilling equipment should be utilized by the drilled shaft
contractor to install the drilled shafts to the depths and dimensions recommended herein
GEOLABS INC
W O 4086 00 Page 15
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the contact
between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper construction
techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft holes
keeping the shaft holes open and clean and in placing concrete into the holes This is
especially important where soft lagoonal deposits may be encountered below the surface
fills In addition groundwater will likely be encountered in the drilled holes therefore
concrete placement in the drilled holes will need to be by tremie methods
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch slump range should be used
to provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soil The concrete
should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce segregation of the aggregates from the
concrete mix If pre cast concrete pole bases are used the gap between the pole base and
the surrounding soils should be backfilled with lean concrete A representative of Geolabs
should be present to observe the installation of drilled shafts during construction

Where foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom
edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement
is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the footing subgrades they should
be over excavated and replaced with well compacted structural fill The
over excavation should be extended until the underlying dense materials are
exposed The over excavation should be backfilled with structural fill materials
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the bottom of the footing may be
extended down to the underlying competent material
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate total settlements to be less than 1 inch with differential
settlements on the order of about 0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the retaining structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 40 may be used for footings bearing on the compacted
on site granular soils Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth
pcf This assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted Unless
covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil
should be neglected

Our field exploration indicated that the upper 10 to 12 feet of subsoils at the proposed
modified drywell locations consist of medium dense to dense granular soils overlying loose
materials In addition groundwater was encountered at a depth of about 11 feet below the
ground surface in this area at the time of our field exploration Based on these subsurface
conditions we recommend that the modified drywells be limited to about 10 feet in depth to

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of the
tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B 1
Six sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 ASTM D 1140 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are presented on
Plates B 2 and B 3
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics Results of the direct shear tests are
presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
Six California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement
subgrade support Results of the CBR tests are presented on Plates B 6 through B 11
Six Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on the bulk
samples obtained to evaluate the relationship between the moisture content and the dry
density of the compacted near surface soils The test results are presented on Plates B 12
through B 17
Two sets of corrosivity tests consisting of pH ASTM D 2976 and minimum
resistivity ASTM G 57 tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the
corrosivity of the on site materials to buried metallic substructures The test results are
summarized on Plate B 18
