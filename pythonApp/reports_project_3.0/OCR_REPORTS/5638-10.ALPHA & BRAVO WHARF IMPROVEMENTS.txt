Dear Mr. Bates:
As requested, we performed wave equation analyses to evaluate the proposed pile
driving operations for the 16.5-inch octagonal precast and prestressed concrete piles
supporting the two fender backboard structures for the Alpha & Bravo Wharf
Improvements project in the Polaris Point area on the Island of Guam. The wave equation
analyses were conducted based on the information provided and the subsurface
conditions encountered in the borings drilled for the project.
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is at the Polaris Point area on the Island of Guam. Based on the
information provided, we understand that 16.5-inch octagonal precast and prestressed
concrete piles will be used to support the two fender backboard structures at the
eastern end of the Alpha Wharf improvements.
We understand the current design requires two concrete piles to support each of
the concrete fender backboards. Based on the size of the fender backboards, we
estimate the backboard structure weighs a little less than 250 kips per backboard
(based on 63.5 cubic yards of concrete at 3.915 kips per cubic yard of concrete). It is
desired to evaluate the pile driving behavior to address the estimated driving resistance
and induced compressive/tensile stresses in the pile.
Because there are only two concrete piles supporting each of the two fender
backboard structures resulting in a total of four concrete piles for this project, we believe
that the test pile driving program and associated dynamic load testing utilizing a pile
driving analyzer may be waived for this situation. To provide a higher degree of
assurance that the concrete pile foundations for the two fender backboard structures will

Subsurface Conditions
In general, the subsurface conditions consist of loose coralline detritus deposits
overlying limestone formation. Mudline was encountered at about Elevation -40 feet Mean
Lower Low Water (MLLW). The coralline detritus deposits in the borings generally consist
of loose silty sands and gravel grading to loose clayey sands with gravel. The depth to the
limestone formation in the borings is generally at about Elevation -55 feet MLLW at the
eastern end of Alpha Wharf.

Pile Details
The specified concrete piles will consist of 16.5-inch octagonal precast and
prestressed concrete piles. The pile cast length will be 80 feet. As previously discussed,
each concrete pile will support 82.8 kips (41.4 tons) of structural weight from the fender
backboard structure based on utilizing three concrete piles instead of two concrete piles
to support each fender backboard structure. Utilizing a higher factor of safety to
determine the termination criteria, we have adopted a factor of safety of 3.0 resulting in
an ultimate pile capacity of about 250 kips.
We understand the concrete piles will be cast from concrete with a minimum
28-day unconfined compressive strength of 41 MegaPascals [about 5,947 pounds per
square inch [(psi)]. We also anticipate that the effective prestress in the 16.5-inch
octagonal concrete pile is approximately 775 psi based on utilizing seven 0.5-inch
strands for the concrete piles.
Based on the above information, the maximum allowable compressive stress in
the 16.5-inch octagonal precast, prestressed concrete pile during pile driving is about
2,793 psi. The maximum allowable tensile stress developed in the 16.5-inch octagonal
precast, prestressed concrete pile during pile driving is approximately 775 psi.
WAVE EQUATION ANALYSES
Wave equation analyses were conducted on the concrete piles based on the pile
driving system proposed and the two idealized soil profiles previously described. The wave
equation analyses were performed using the computer program, GRLWEAP 2005 (Wave
Equation Analysis of Pile Driving), which is an extension of the WEAP87 program
developed under the sponsorship of the Federal Highway Administration.
The primary purpose of the analyses is to estimate the relationship between the pile
driving stresses and driving resistance for the proposed pile driving system. The results of
the analyses may be utilized as a guide during pile installation to establish termination
criteria for the piles in order to reduce the risk of over-stressing the piles.

Driving Stresses
A graph showing the computed maximum compression and tensile stresses
induced in the pile during pile driving is provided on Plates 3 and 4 for the two idealized
soil profiles analyzed. Please note that the graph was prepared based on stress
measurements in kips per square inch (ksi), which is equivalent to 1,000 psi.
Based on our analyses, the compressive stresses during the pile driving using
strokes of 4 and 5.5 feet for both soil profiles are less than the allowable compressive
stress of 2,793 psi. The compressive stresses developed during pile driving did not
exceed the allowable compressive stresses for the full range of driving resistance at the
two lower pump settings. However, the compressive stresses during the pile driving
using strokes of 7 or 8.4 feet (the two upper pump settings) may exceed the allowable
compressive stress when driving resistance is greater than 40 or 25 blows per
foot (bpf), respectively.
The tensile stresses induced in the pile during the pile driving for both idealized
soil profiles are generally less than the allowable tensile stress of 775 psi for the
16.5-inch octagonal pile at Pump Settings 1 through 4. However, the tensile stresses
may potentially exceed the allowable tensile stresses during pile driving using pump
settings 3 or 4 when the driving resistance is less than 10 blows per foot.
Therefore, care should be exercised if the lower driving resistance (less than
10 blows per foot) or higher driving resistance (greater than 25 bpf) is encountered
during pile driving using pump settings 3 or 4. This may be achieved by reducing the
pile driving energy to pump setting 2 (or lower) with stroke height of about 5.5 feet (or
less). To reduce the risk of over-stressing during the pile driving, we recommend using
hammer stroke of 5.5 feet for the project. We also recommend the following pile driving
termination criteria as a guide during pile driving operation for 16.5-inch octagonal
concrete pile.
° Pile is installed to minimum tip elevation of -70 feet (-21.336 meters) MLLW
using 5.5-foot stroke achieving a minimum driving resistance of 41 blows per
foot.
e Before pile tip elevation reaches minimum tip elevation of -70 feet MLLW, pile
driving using 5.5-foot stroke may be terminated when the driving resistance
exceeds 100 blows per foot, or 10 blows per inch lasting for 4 inches.

ABOUT THE WAVE EQUATION ANALYSIS RESULTS
The GRLWEAP program simulates the behavior of a preformed pile driven by
either an impact hammer or a vibratory hammer. The program is based on
mathematical models, which describe motion and forces of hammer, driving
system, pile and soil under the hammer action. Under certain conditions,
the models only crudely approximate, often complex, dynamic situations.
A wave equation analysis generally relies on input data, which represents
normal situations. In particular, the hammer data file supplied with the
program assumes that the hammer is in good working order. All of the input
data selected by the user may be the best available information at the time
when the analysis is performed. However, input data and therefore results
may significantly differ from actual field conditions.
Therefore, the program authors recommend prudent use of the GRLWEAP results.
Soil response and hammer performance should be verified by static and/or
dynamic testing and measurements. Estimates of bending or other local
non-axial stresses and prestress effects must also be accounted for by the
user.
The calculated capacity - blow count relationship, i.e. the bearing graph,
should be used in conjunction with observed blow counts for the capacity
assessment of a driven pile. Soil setup occurring after pile installation
May produce bearing capacity values that differ substantially from those
expected from a wave equation analysis due to soil setup or relaxation. This
is particularly true for pile driven with vibratory hammers. The GRLWEAP user
must estimate such effects and should also use proper care when applying blow
counts from restrike because of the variability of hammer energy, soil
resistance and blow count during early restriking.
Finally, the GRLWEAP capacities are ultimate values. They MUST be reduced by
means of an appropriate factor of safety to yield a design or working load.
The selection of a factor of safety should consider the quality of the
construction control, the variability of the site conditions, uncertainties
in the loads, the importance of building and other factors.

ABOUT THE WAVE EQUATION ANALYSIS RESULTS
The GRLWEAP program simulates the behavior of a preformed pile driven by
either an impact hammer or a vibratory hammer. The program is based on
mathematical models, which describe motion and forces of hammer, driving
system, pile and soil under the hammer action. Under certain conditions,
the models only crudely approximate, often complex, dynamic situations.
A wave equation analysis generally relies on input data, which represents
normal situations. In particular, the hammer data file supplied with the
program assumes that the hammer is in good working order. All of the input
data selected by the user may be the best available information at the time
when the analysis is performed. However, input data and therefore results
may significantly differ from actual field conditions.
Therefore, the program authors recommend prudent use of the GRLWEAP results.
Soil response and hammer performance should be verified by static and/or
dynamic testing and measurements. Estimates of bending or other local
non-axial stresses and prestress effects must also be accounted for by the
user.
The calculated capacity - blow count relationship, i.e. the bearing graph,
should be used in conjunction with observed blow counts for the capacity
assessment of a driven pile. Soil setup occurring after pile installation
may produce bearing capacity values that differ substantially from those
expected from a wave equation analysis due to soil setup or relaxation. This
is particularly true for pile driven with vibratory hammers. The GRLWEAP user
must estimate such effects and should also use proper care when applying blow
counts from restrike because of the variability of hammer energy, soil
resistance and blow count during early restriking.
Finally, the GRLWEAP capacities are ultimate values. They MUST be reduced by
means of an appropriate factor of safety to yield a design or working load.
The selection of a factor of safety should consider the quality of the
construction control, the variability of the site conditions, uncertainties
in the loads, the importance of building and other factors.
