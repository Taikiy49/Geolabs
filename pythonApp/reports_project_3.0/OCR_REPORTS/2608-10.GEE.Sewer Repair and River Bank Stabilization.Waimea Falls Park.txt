INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed sewer repair and river bank stabilization work in Waimea Falis
Park in Waimea on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location of the project site
is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from analyses of the data obtained
for the project These recommendations are intended for design input and are not
intended for construction bidding or estimation purposes
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface
conditions at the project site in order to develop a generalized soil rock data set for the
formulation of our geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the proposed
work
Our expioration was performed in accordance with our fee proposal to Sea Life
Park dated The scope of our work consisted of the following

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
During a recent rain storm an eight 8 inch diameter ductile iron sewer main
crossing a stream in the park was destroyed due to the loss of foundation support during
flooding It is proposed to replace the broken sewer crossing the stream with an inverted
siphon constructed of the same ductile iron pipes but encased in an approximately 2 0
feet by 2 7 feet concrete jacket embedded below the existing stream bed
The river banks at both ends of the broken sewer were also eroded during the
heavy flood The extent and degree of erosion was particularly severe at the southern
bank resulting in an almost vertical bank exposing fine grained soil prone to further
erosion during future flood flows it is proposed to restore and stabilize the eroded river
banks adjacent to the sewer against future erosion by utilizing boulder buttresses The
stabilization of other river banks in the park is not included in our scope of work
GENERAL SITE CONDITIONS
Waimea Falls Park is a major tourist attraction located in a valley on the eastern
side of Kamehameha Highway opposite Waimea Beach Park in Waimea at the north
shore of Oahu Hawaii During a recent heavy rain storm the park was flooded and
damaged extensively
The park is divided by a stream originating at Waimea Falls and flowing through
the valley into the sea The existing broken sewer main was constructed of eight 8 inch
diameter ductile iron pipe supported on spread footing foundations bearing on the stream
bed The foundation support was undermined by scouring action and the sewer was
destroyed during the flooding
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2608 10 Page 5
During the heavy flood the stream bed and the river banks were also eroded
resulting in deposits of eroded bank soils on the stream bed and an almost vertical bank
face composed of fine grained clayey silts at the southern bank of the stream adjacent
to the broken sewer
Scattered boulders were observed in the stream and also upstream of the project
site

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Inverted Siphon
Based on the subsoil conditions we believe that the proposed inverted siphon may
be constructed across the stream bed by bearing within the underlying subsoils We
recommend that the concrete jacket be embedded in the underlying cemented sand layer
to provide erosion protection
As a result the concrete jacket may bear either on cemented tan sand and or
medium dense dark gray to light black silty sand depending on the actual invert of the
jacket in the stream Due to this potential for bearing on different subsoils we
recommend that a minimum six 6 inch thick granular bedding of 3B Fine or ASTM C
33 No 67 gravel be used below the concrete jacket to provide uniform subgrade support
and also to serve as a working platform on the saturated trench subgrade
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2608 10 Page 7
The gravel bedding material is self compacting under water and may be loosely
deposited on to the trench bottom in the stream bed Where the gravel bedding is placed
above water level such as at the ends of the siphon in the river banks it should be placed
on a layer of filter fabric such as MIRAFI 140N or its equivalent and compacted by a
vibratory plate with a minimum of three 3 passes to obtain a more uniform surface The
purpose of the filter fabric layer is to reduce the potential of significant outwash of the
underlying fine grained clayey silt subgrade from the river banks
The constructed siphon and concrete jacket may then be covered with a blanket
of boulders embedded below the original stream bed to a minimum depth of two 2 feet
across the stream to provide further erosion protection The boulder blanket should
extend laterally for a minimum distance of four 4 feet on both sides of the siphon trench
Boulders between one 1 to two 2 feet in size are recommended for use in the blanket
Relatively easy excavation is anticipated for trenching in the stream bed and also
at both ends of the siphon at the river banks Some boulder removal work is anticipated
However continuous dewatering of large quantities of water may be necessary to
keep the excavated siphon trench in a safe and dry working condition In addition
adequate shoring support should be provided in the trench during construction since the
uppermost clayey or boulderly subsoils and the bottom excavation in medium dense silty
sand may have a high potential of caving in under hydraulic pressure especially during
dewatering
The method and equipment to be used for excavation dewatering and shoring
support should be left to the choice of the contractor within practical limits and safety
considerations

River Bank Protection
It is proposed to restore and protect the river banks from future erosion by
constructing a buttress along each bank near the proposed siphon by using the abundant
supply of boulders in the park
Based on the subsoil conditions we recommend that the rock buttresses be
placed directly on the underlying cemented sand layer As a result the toe of the
buttresses should be embedded below the lowest adjacent grades of the existing stream
bed a minimum of 2 0 to 2 5 feet depending on the actual depth of the cemented sand
layer
Properly interlocking boulders a minimum of two 2 feet in diameter may be used
as shown on the Typical Rock Buttress Details provided as Plate 3
Ideally the minimum boulder size to be used should be determined by considering
design factors such as the maximum flow rate and velocity of the stream during a future
flood However such information is not readily available in consideration of the urgent
project deadline Informal estimates indicate that flow rates of 10 000 cubic feet per
second had been measured This value could be considered by the designer
We also recommend that the existing eroded bank faces be cut back adequately
and trimmed smooth then protected with a layer of filter fabric such as MIRAFI 140N or
its equivalent prior to the placement of the boulder buttresses The purpose of the filter
fabric layer is to reduce outwash of the fine grained clayey silt subsoils from the banks
due to stream fluctuation
The restored river banks may be graded to a stable slope at a maximum ratio of
one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V with a minimum horizontal cover of five 5 feet of

Design Review
Plans and specifications for the proposed construction should be forwarded to the
project geotechnical engineer for review and written comments prior to construction This
review is needed to determine adherence to the recommendations given herein If this
review is not made the geotechnical engineer cannot assume responsibility for
misinterpretation of the recommendations
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the project geotechnical engineer be retained to provide
geotechnical engineering services during construction of the foundation phases of the
work This is to observe compliance with the design concepts specifications or
recommendations and to allow design changes in the event that subsurface conditions
differ from that anticipated prior to start of construction The recommendations given in
this report are contingent upon such observations
If actual exposed rock soil conditions encountered during construction are different
from those assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made
