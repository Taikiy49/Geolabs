Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Waikiki Sewer Rehabilitation project in the Waikiki area of Honolulu
on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for
the sewer rehabilitation project In addition discussions on the excavation and
dewatering requirements are included for information purposes only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

Project Considerations
The project involves the rehabilitation of approximately 6 174 lineal feet of
existing 10 inch to 30 inch diameter sewer lines along Kuhio Avenue extending from
Namahana Street to Kapuni Street in the Waikiki area of Honolulu on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii Based on the information provided the existing sewer lines consisted of
both cast iron and vitrified clay pipes with invert elevations ranging between about
6 and 12 feet Mean Sea Level MSL The project scope will also include the
installation of two new sewer manholes to improve maintenance access and the
rehabilitation of 31 existing sewer manholes at various locations throughout Waikiki A
layout of the proposed sewer rehabilitation project is shown on the Site Plans
Plates 2 1 and 2 2

thermosetting resin saturated material into the sewer line and heating the liner under
pressure until it cures into a rigid new pipe within a pipe finished product In general
the CIPP method will require the temporary installation of insertion points or manholes
at selected locations along the existing sewer line alignment We understand that the

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau Volcano forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae Volcano
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank is partially
buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano and the
geomorphology and subsurface conditions of the area are directly related to
glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the
genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions in the project vicinity
are dominated by fossil Pleistocene age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits
laid during high stands of sea The coral algal reef deposits and related deposits are
inter layered with terrigenous sediments and late series volcanic deposits The base of
the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late Pliocene and
early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building
activities ceased approximately 2 5 to 3 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells
indicates that the Island of Oahu has subsided by as much as 1 200 feet since the
cessation of this early volcanic activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age many sea level changes occurred
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell because less water was

available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted
global sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu
remained essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in
nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both
higher and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials The lower sea stands caused
streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial erosion of the
upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively
high energy conditions within and along the streams During periods of no significant
sea level changes the continued stream action extended the alluvial deltas and fans
seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal sediments

Fills
The fill materials consist of variable soils containing some rock fragments which
were placed by man during the development of the Kakaako coastal areas The fill
materials generally consist of both calcareous and non calcareous soils containing
various mixtures of gravel sands silts clays and potentially some embedded rock
fragments such as hard coralline or basaltic cobbles and boulders The fills were
typically placed to achieve the desired finished grades and to provide reclaimed
land for development The fill materials may be encountered as both
unconsolidated and semi consolidated deposits having variable density resulting

from man induced compaction of the fill materials In general the fills may extend
to depths of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Lagoonal Deposits
Lagoonal materials generally consist of variable light gray calcareous sediments
clay silt and sand mixed with some consolidated coral reef formation and broken
granular fragments of coralline reef materials gravelly coralline detritus The
materials are generally deposited in low wave energy shallow marine
environments such as bays and lagoons which are commonly protected by an
outer fringing coralline reef and an inner shoreline environment The deposits are
typically soft and or loose and provide infill among localized in situ hard coral reef
structures The deposits are variable in type and consistency as a result of
changing depositional environments affected by short term storm wave action and
long term global fluctuations in sea level Lagoonal deposits may be encountered
interbedded within coralline detritus deposits or as layers or void infilling within
porous and cavernous coral reef formation materials The lagoonal deposits may
extend to depths of about 30 to 36 feet below the existing ground surface
Alluvial Deposits
Alluvial deposits will be encountered within the Launiu Channel and the
Kaiulani Channel Alluvial deposits generally consist of unconsolidated to
consolidated terrestrial sediments of silts clays sands and some gravel which are
deposited by flowing water Alluvial deposits are mainly encountered adjacent to
stream and estuary environments but they also may be encountered where
sediment laden runoff emanating from higher elevation terrain is deposited on
adjacent lower elevation flatter terrain The deposits may be encountered as wide
spread valley fill deposits or as elongated channel filling deposits The consistency
of the alluvial deposits at the site may generally be very stiff to hard
Coral Algal Reef Formation
Tan or grayish white medium hard to very hard organic rock consisting of
moderately to strongly cemented coral and algal reef materials with a fine grain
calcareous crystalline rock groundmass The rock may contain varying degrees of

vugs and may be non porous to very porous by interconnected vug structures The
material densities logged by SPT sampling may vary widely over a short interval of
depth with very dense coral algal limestone immediately underlying overlying a
seam of medium dense material
The rock is essentially formed by live marine organisms that consist of coral and
calcareous algae plants that deposit calcium carbonate In general the corals
build the rock framework with lime secretions derived from dissolved calcium
carbonate from seawater The algae growth then in fills and coats the coral surface
to armor the coral against surf action The hardened algae growth is what provides
much of the cementing agent to form hard limestone
The rock may be cored with variable recovery due to the presence of
interconnected voids and vug space present in the rock mass The rock may be
sampled by SPT with density ranging from medium dense to SPT refusal however
SPT penetration is generally limited to the medium dense very porous rock type
containing a high volume of void space in the rock framework The open vugs and
voids commonly encountered within the porous rock framework may be partially or
wholly filled with fine sediments making it difficult to differentiate upon cursory
inspection of an SPT sample between mildly cemented coral detritus and strongly
vugular coral algal limestone rock It is best to retrieve cored samples of the
material to evaluate the true cemented hardness and volume of vugs within the
rock
Large voids with or without sediment in filling may be encountered in this material
The voids are believed to have been formed either by dissolution of the buried
limestone by groundwater or due to the original depositional character of the reef
building organisms

Tan or white or grayish white rock medium hard generally porous organic rock
consisting or coral and some algal reef material The rock typically consists of an
open framework of coral growth with abundant small voids and vugs The rock is

Cured In Place Pipe CIPP
The Cured In Place Pipe CIPP system can be used to eliminate or reduce the
inflow of storm water infiltration of groundwater exfiltration of pollutants surface
settlement caused by soil migration into the pipe corrosion attack and pipe
irregularities defects joints in the sewer collection systems The CIPP technique
normally is preferred over other alternatives to rehabilitate the existing pipelines under
the following conditions
The pipeline has adequate flow capacity during the expected life of the
liner
The pipe diameter ranging from 4 to 108 inches and length
up to 1 000 to 3 000 feet of the rehabilitation run are within the general
envelope of capabilities of the CIPP system
The host pipe is not severely damaged broken or deformed There are
no flat surfaces or corners significant changes in pipe diameter from
manhole to manhole and sharp bends or tees
Flow bypassing is feasible or unnecessary due to low flows
CIPP is a liquid thermosetting resin saturated material that is inserted into the
existing pipeline through the existing manholes by hydrostatic inversion air inversion or
by mechanically pulling with a winch and cable The water inversion process utilizes
water pressure to expand the lining against the walls of the host pipe The air inversion
process employs air pressure to invert the lining With the mechanical winching process
the lining is winched into place and expanded using a calibration hose inverted into the
lining under water pressure After insertion the resin saturated material cures by
application of hot water or steam to form a new pipe of slightly smaller inside diameter
but of the same general shape as the original pipe The liner is designed to fit snugly
against the walls of the host pipe
There are several advantages of the CIPP method over other methods of
trenchless pipeline rehabilitation Grouting is not normally required due to the tight fit of
the CIPP against the existing pipe The CIPP has no joints and has a very smooth
interior surface which typically improves flow capacity despite the slight decrease in

diameter Non circular shapes can be accommodated often without a decrease in flow
capacity The lining is capable of accommodating bends and some existing pipe

Compared with other rehabilitation techniques CIPP liners require expertise to

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the proposed manhole locations by
drilling and sampling two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths
of about 43 5 and 43 feet below the existing pavement surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 1 The borings were drilled using a
track mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows
required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need to be factored to
obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

The permeability of the in situ soils at the project site was evaluated by performing
two constant head and two falling head permeability tests designated as P 1 through P 4
within the two drilled borings shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 1 The tests were performed
at depths of about 10 and 16 5 feet below the existing pavement surface in Boring Nos 1
and 2 respectively
The constant head tests were performed by pumping water into the boreholes until
the water surface has stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time
The water height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which
to base calculation of the subsoil permeability Results of the constant head permeability
tests are presented on Plates C 1 and C 3
During the falling head testing the boreholes were carefully filled with water to the
top of the boreholes The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide
data upon which to base calculation of the permeability values of the subsoils The test
results are presented on Plates C 2 and C 4
