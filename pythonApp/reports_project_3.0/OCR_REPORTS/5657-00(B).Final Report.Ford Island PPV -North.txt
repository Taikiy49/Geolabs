Dear Mr Patel
As requested we have conducted a supplemental field exploration and additional
laboratory testing to evaluate the engineering properties of the on site clayey soils at the
Ford Island PPV North site on Ford Island and to determine their suitability as a
potential source of general fill materials
Our supplemental field exploration consisted of excavating 14 test pits
designated as Test Pit Nos 1 through 14 extending to depths ranging from about
4 feet below the existing ground surface The test pit excavations were
generally terminated upon encountering hard coral formation resulting in excavation
refusal The approximate test pit locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 1
The materials encountered in the test pits were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the excavation operations on a
near continuous basis We further reviewed these classifications by visual observation
and laboratory testing We classified the soils in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on the Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered in the test pits are presented on the Logs
of Test Pits Plates A 1 through A 14 We performed pocket penetrometer tests in the
field on selected cohesive soils The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of
the unconfined compressive strength of the soils Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are summarized on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate depths
In addition to the field exploration work we collected selected samples of the
clayey soils encountered in the test pit excavations and transported them to our office
for laboratory testing We performed laboratory moisture content ring swell Atterberg
Limits and California Bearing Ratio CBR tests on the soil samples and the test
results are presented on the Logs of Test Pits and Plates B 1 through B 19 The
laboratory test results also are summarized on Plate 2

Based on our supplemental exploration program it appears that the on site
clayey soils at the Ford Island PPV North site may be delineated into two areas as
shown on the Site Plan Plate 1 The clayey soils within the western portion of the site
are generally classified as Sandy Clay CL with a plasticity index ranging from about
19 to 29 Laboratory tests performed on the on site sandy clay soils yielded a ring swell
generally on the order of about 2 to 3 percent indicating that the on site clayey soils
exhibit a slight potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to soil moisture
fluctuations We believe that the clayey soils within this area may be re used as a
source of general fills below building structures provided that the clayey soils are
capped with a minimum of 12 inches of non expansive select granular materials below
the slab cushion
In contrast the clayey soils within the eastern portion of the site are generally
classified as Silty Clay CH with a plasticity index ranging from about 38 to 45
Laboratory tests performed on the on site silty clay soils yielded a ring swell generally
on the order of about 9 to 16 percent indicating that the on site clayey soils exhibit a
high potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to soil moisture fluctuations
We believe that the clayey soils within this area may be re used as a source of general
fills below building structures provided that the clayey soils are capped with a minimum
of 24 inches of non expansive select granular materials below the slab cushion
The in situ moisture contents of the majority of the clayey soils encountered at
the site generally range between about 20 and 30 percent Therefore we believe that
the clayey materials may be re used without additional handling such as aeration prior
to their use as fill and or backfill material in order to meet the normal 90 percent
minimum relative compaction requirement
Based on the above discussions and the grading plans provided it is our opinion
that the non expansive select granular capping fill underneath some of the house pads
will need to be increased from 12 inches to 24 inches due to the presence of the highly
expansive silty clay soils encountered in the supplemental field exploration The
approximate limits of these house lots are shown on the Site Plan Plate 1
The findings and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon
information obtained from the soil samples collected at selected locations and depths
across the project site Variations of subsurface conditions between and beyond the
bulk samples collected may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may
not become evident until construction is underway If variations then appear evident
Geolabs should be contacted to re evaluate the findings and supplemental
recommendations presented herein

SECTION 2 0 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2 1 Regional Geology
The project site is on the northeastern side of Ford Island in the Pearl Harbor
area on the south central coastal plain of Oahu Hawaii A complex series of erosional
and depositional events resulting from glacio eustatic sea level changes and
post erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch resulted in the
accumulation of alluvial deposits in a lagoonal deltaic environment in Pearl Harbor
during higher stands of the sea
Ford Island is a remnant of the hard reef rock that once covered a larger portion
of the embayment and was subsequently carved away in many places by streams
during lower stands of the sea During the Recent Epoch the sea has adjusted to its
present level and additional sediments have continued to accumulate along the lochs
and channels of the harbor Land developments in the past 40 to 60 years have added
man made fills along the shoreline to shape the island as it presently exists
2 2 Site Description
The Ford Island PPV North project site is at the northeast side of Ford Island in
the Pearl Harbor area on the Island Oahu Hawaii The site is an approximately
rectangular shaped parcel of land encompassing approximately 19 acres It is bounded
approximately by Ford Island Boulevard to the north Saratoga Boulevard to the east
Lexington Boulevard to the south and open land to the west as shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2

demolition of the old turnaround The southern portion is generally occupied by an
asphalt concrete paved parking lot Based on the topographic survey map the project
site slopes down slightly from the north toward the south with existing ground surface
elevations ranging from about 22 feet Mean Sea Level MSL along the northern

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the housing project site were explored by drilling
and sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to a
depth of about 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations
of the borings drilled for the project are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings
were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in drilled
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests performed on selected samples are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX C
Field Resistivity Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Resistivity Testing
Resistivity generally is recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects In general the lower the
resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure
Conversely the higher the resistivity the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of
metallic objects
Geophysical survey equipment i e resistance meter was mobilized to the project
site on for the field survey Twenty soil resistivity surveys were
conducted at five locations designated as R 1 through R 5 on the Site Plan Plate 2
using the in Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter
manufactured by Nilsson Electrical Laboratory Inc Our field resistivity work was
performed under cloudy conditions with generally damp surface conditions
The method utilizes four surface probes or pins electrodes to evaluate the
average resistivity an indication of corrosivity of the underlying soil mass The average
resistivity values of the soil mass extending from the existing ground surface to the test
depth was assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations
The average soil resistance was evaluated at each survey location with the
electrodes aligned at equal spacings of various distances based on the anticipated utility
depths The depth of penetration of a resistivity sounding is a function of the electrode
spacing
The resistance meter Nilsson Model 400 provides a voltage between the outer
electrodes causing an electrical current to flow between them The voltage drop between
the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive built in voltmeter As a result the soil
resistance in ohms is obtained from the voltmeter dial reading The average soil
resistivity in ohms cm which is directly related to both resistance and spacing can then
be computed Results of the field resistivity surveys are presented on Plate C 1 It should
be noted that the soil resistivity may be influenced by temperature weather conditions soil
moisture existing underground utilities and other factors
