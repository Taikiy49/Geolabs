INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Makaha 242 Reservoir No 2 in the Waianae District on the
island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project site is located in Makaha Valley on the island of Oahu
Hawaii It is proposed to construct a 2 0 Million Gallon MG reservoir next to the existing
tank
We understand that the new concrete reservoir will be 140 feet in diameter by
20 feet high with a bottom tank elevation at 222 feet Mean Sea Level MSL to match
the existing tank floor elevation Column loads of about 80 kips and wall loads of about
3 kips per foot are anticipated
Based on the topographic map provided the existing paved road at the south side
of the site has an elevation of approximately 220 feet MSL The valley slopes toward
the north at an inclination of about to 2H 1V Extensive site grading including
cuts of over 100 feet is anticipated due to the steep topography

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration for the subject project
was to obtain information pertaining to the subsurface conditions at the project site The
subsurface information was used to formulate a summary of the soil rock conditions for
the design of the reservoir foundation and site grading In order to accomplish these
objectives we conducted an exploration program consisting of the following tasks and
work efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a skid mounted drill rig and operators to
and from the site Due to the steep topography and rough terrain a
helicopter was used to move the drill rig to each boring site
2 Drilling and sampling of two borings to depths of about 60 and 80 feet
below the existing ground surface for a total of about 140 lineal feet of
exploration
3 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by a Field
Engineer Geologist from our firm
4 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field exploration
as an aid in classifying the materials encountered and evaluating their
engineering properties
5 Analyses of the field and laboratory data to develop geotechnical design
recommendations pertinent to the proposed reservoir project
6 Preparation of this report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
7 Coordination of our work on the project by a Project Engineer of our firm
8 Quality assurance of our overall work on the project and client design team
consultation by a Principal Engineer of our firm
9 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing clerical
support and reproductions

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age Ice Age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank
The project site is located on the lower southeast facing flank of Puu Keaau which
forms the northwestern ridge of Makaha Puu Keaau is an eroded ridge expression of a
portion of the Waianae Shield Volcano
The area is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and pyroclastic material
which are grouped and classified as the Waianae Volcanic Series Typically surficial
deposits of unconsolidated alluvium and colluvium are found overlying the basalt
formation

Reservoir Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe
that the underlying basalt rock formation would provide suitable foundation support for the
proposed water reservoir structure It is our opinion that spread and continuous footing
foundations may be used to support the proposed reservoir structure
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 10 000 p s f may be used for design of
spread and continuous wall footings bearing on the hard basalt formation This bearing
value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces For design of structural slabs a
modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection p c i
may be used for the compacted base course
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 16 inches below the lowest adjacent
finished grade If foundations are located next to utility trenches they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
GEOLABS HAWAII

settlement
Due to the potential presence of cavities as commonly encountered in basaltic lava
flows we recommend that a program of probing and grouting of the footing locations be
conducted during construction A representative of Geolabs Hawaii should observe the
probing and grouting operation to evaluate the extent of cavities if encountered Probing
and grouting requirements are further discussed in the Foundation Probing section of
this report
It is generally not practical to produce a smooth level surface in basalt rock
formation for placement of the reservoir footings and slab Localized irregularities in the
rock surface may be filled with concrete monolithically with the foundation
lf the footings are designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total settlement of footings is estimated to be on the order of inch with
differential settlements on the order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 56 may be used for footings bearing on the basalt rock
Resistance to lateral loads due to passive pressure may be calculated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 500 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f for
footings embedded in the basalt formation These values assume that the concrete for
footings is cast neat against the basalt rock Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be neglected
Foundation Probing
Based on our previous experience in the area voids and or cavities may be
present in the basaltic lava flows encountered at the project site To reduce the potential
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3639 00 Page 8
for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities below a footing we
recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be conducted during
construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each spread
footing location and 10 foot on centers for continuous footings The probe holes should
be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned
bottom of footing If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the
probing operation additional probes should be drilled at closer spacings to help delineate
the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and any cavities
discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate
pressures A typical sand cement grout would consist of 1 part sand 1 part cement and
0 7 part water
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and
grouting operation is complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer
lag time between probing grouting operations and foundation construction may be
required in the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of
cavities and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or
changed conditions are observed

Rockfall Hazard
Structures situated below a hillside slope have an inherent potential hazard from
boulders rolling down the hillside Since the natural slopes are generally flatter than
1H 1V the boulders tend to roll along the ground surface and most are stopped by trees
other boulders or come to rest on flatter areas Manmade structures such as fences or
ditches may be installed to stop the occasional boulders that may continue down slope
Steep rock cuts increase the hazard from rockfalls Pieces of the rock formation
may break loose from the cut slope face and may roll or bounce downslope depending
on the steepness of the slope Extensive research by the U S Department of
Transportation Federal Highway Administration FHWA indicates that boulders tend to
roll down slopes flatter than 1H 1V bounce on the slope face for ratios of about
to 1H 1V and fall straight to the bottom for steeper slopes
Due to the high cut slope planned and the proximity of the reservoir structure the
potential rockfall hazard is a significant design issue for the project The rockfall hazard
in basalt formations is generally more severe in weathered basalt and formations with
thick clinker layers which are susceptible to erosion and loosening of the boulders over
time The borings drilled at the site encountered hard moderately weathered basalt
without significant layers of clinker indicating that future erosion may be very limited
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3639 00 Page 12
Several measures may be taken to reduce the potential hazard from rockfalls at
the site
e The finish slope face should be carefully cut to avoid loosening of the
intact rock and undercutting portions of the slope which may create future
instability Loose pieces of rock should be removed from the slope as the
cut progresses If the contractor is unable to cut a relatively smooth
surface the use of chemical expanders installed in predrilled holes may
be considered
e Mid slope benches should be avoided as recent research by FHWA has
indicated that the benches tend to accumulate debris and form launching
ramps for falling rocks increasing the hazard
e A rockfall catchment ditch should be placed at the toe of the cut slope to
collect falling rocks and debris Guidelines from FHWA indicate that the
ditch should be about 25 feet wide for a slope height over 100 feet
Structures should be set back from the slope as much as practicable to
provide an additional safety zone

The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 to depths of about 60 and
80 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a skid mounted
drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with
the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
