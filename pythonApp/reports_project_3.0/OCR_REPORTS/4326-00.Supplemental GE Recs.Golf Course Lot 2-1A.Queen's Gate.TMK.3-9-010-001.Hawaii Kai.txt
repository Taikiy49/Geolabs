REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The project site is located at the base of the eastern flank of Koko Crater Koko
Crater is a compound tuff cone which is part of the Honolulu Volcanic Series Potassium
argon dating indicates that Koko Crater is about 32 000 years old Koko Crater is believed
to have been formed by violent explosions that occurred when rising lava came in contact
with sea water Fragments of lava as well as fragments of older volcanic rock and coral were
ejected and deposited as tuff The gap at the northeast side of the crater probably resulted
from trade winds blowing most of the ash towards the southwest
Following the period of eruption the crater has been modified by processes of
weathering and erosion to create the current landform Volcanic fragments in the tuff have
been altered to palagonite through the weathering processes Tuff has eroded from the side
slopes of the crater creating gullies and steep scarps The eroded materials have been
deposited as talus and alluvium on the interior and exterior of the crater

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other
types of acceptable compaction equipment General fill materials should be placed
in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
In general the boulders placed within the general fill mass should be spread
out and should not be nested together The boulders should be spread out such
that compaction equipment may be able to compact finer general fill materials
between and around the boulders The general fill materials between and around
the boulders should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
In addition the boulder fill mass should be heavily watered by water trucks during
placement to facilitate seating of the boulder in the fill mass
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of rock
boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of
passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs On the basis of this testing program the number of
passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction Therefore
it is critical that a representative from Geolabs be present during the earthwork
operations to observe and document that the fill placement procedures are consistent
with the recommendations provided herein

The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based on
our visual observation of the existing site and field data from the borings drilled at the
site We recommend that contractors proposing to bid on this project be encouraged
to examine the site conditions and the boring data to make their own interpretation
Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the available information cut and fill slopes will be constructed as part of
this project Where cut slopes are planned it appears that most of the cut slopes
would expose the silty sands and gravels encountered In general we believe that
the minor cut slopes planned at the site may be designed with a slope inclination of
2H 1V or flatter
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the general fill materials recommended herein
may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed on the slopes
steeper than 5H 1V should be benched A keyway should be provided for fill slopes
greater than 10 feet in vertical height placed on existing ground steeper than 5H 1V
The fill slope face should be finished to a relatively smooth and well compacted
surface The fill slope face should be free of boulders rock fragments greater than
12 inches in largest dimension and should also be free of voids which would allow
erosion and migration of fines to occur In addition slope planting or other means of
slope protection should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for
significant erosion of the finished slopes

Abandoned Sewer Line
As previously mentioned an abandoned 30 inch sewer line traverses the project site
with the pipe inverts located approximately 12 to 17 feet below the existing ground surface
Because of the large diameter of the pipe relative cover depth and the location of the pipe
below future residential structures we recommend that the pipe be filled with Controlled Low
Strength Material CLSM The CLSM should have a minimum unconfined compression
strength of 50 pounds per square inch psi The CLSM should be flowable with a slump of
at least 7 inches We envision that filling of the pipe will be performed by blocking the pipe
at the lower manhole location and placing CLSM from the upper manhole location
Underground Utility Lines
We envision that new underground utility lines such as water sewer and drain
lines will be required for development of this project It is anticipated that most of the
trenches for underground utility lines will be excavated in the fill materials and or granular
soils encountered at the site As previously mentioned we recommend that consideration
be given to avoid the placement of boulders within the major utility line corridors such as
the roadways and within the depths of the proposed utility lines This recommendation is
provided to reduce the potential for encountering the large boulders during excavation for
the utility trenches
In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or 1 inch minus well graded granular material is
recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials No 3B Fine gravel or
1 inch minus well graded granular materials should be used for the initial trench backfill
up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes It is
critical that free draining materials be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Where the 1 inch minus well graded granular materials are used as bedding and initial
trench backfill the bedding and initial trench backfill should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative
GEOLABS INC
W O 4326 00 Page 14
compaction Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage
to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site materials generally less
than 6 inches in maximum size The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to at
least 95 percent relative compaction
It should be noted that the excavated trench spoils may contain some over sized
materials greater than 6 inches in largest dimension Therefore some processing of the
excavated trench spoils to remove the over sized materials may be necessary prior to
re using the spoils as trench backfill
