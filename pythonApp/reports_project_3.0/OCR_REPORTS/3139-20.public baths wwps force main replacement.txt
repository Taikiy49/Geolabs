INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our additional geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed Public Baths Wastewater Pump Station WWPS
Force Main Replacement project in the Waikiki area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field explorations and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of the Koolau shield Its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3139 20 Page 7
Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil
Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits which are intercalated with
terrigenous sediments laid during high stands of the sea The base of the stratigraphic
section in this area is Koolau Basalt
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly
bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased approximately 2
million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the island of Oahu has
subsided as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity
Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global
sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to present sea level on Oahu
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3139 20 Page 8
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of
upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high
energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments During these stable periods the growth of fringing coral algal reefs formed
distinct ledges of reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea
during that period About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred
During that rise the valleys in the project area were submerged In the last 10 000 years
or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments
have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting
in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in the areas which were formerly
valleys and drainageways
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought
the Waikiki area to its present form Many of the resulting fills are of poor quality in terms
of supporting heavy structural loads

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field explorations it
appears that the proposed force main replacement alignment is generally underlain by
saturated sands with silts and gravels and occasional thin layers of hard coral ledges
The relative density of the sandy beach deposits underlying the site is generally medium
dense but the sands grade to loose to very loose lagoonal deposits at about and below
the proposed force main invert elevations along a large portion of the alignment These
subsurface conditions which consist of relatively clean granular soils with small amount of
fines and little cohesion under a loose and saturated state indicate the potential for
difficult tunneling conditions The contractor should carefully consider the expected
subsurface conditions when selecting appropriate equipment and methods for tunnel and
shaft construction
Assuming that appropriate construction equipment and methods are employed to
minimize the loss of ground the maximum ground surface settlement due to the tunneling
excavation is estimated to be on the order of about to inch for the case where
pressurized close face tunneling equipment is used In addition we believe that some
amount of force main pipe settlement may occur during installation due to the relatively
loose state of the sandy soil deposits underlying the force main pipe invert Considering
that the force main will be a pressurized pipe system some minor settlement of the force
main pipes during pipe installation should not adversely affect the performance of the
force main and may be considered acceptable However if the potential for minor
settlements of the force main pipe due to installation is not acceptable then some type of
ground stabilization measure such as cement or chemical grouting jet grouting should
be considered in order to reduce the potential for settlement of the installed force main
pipes

We understand that precast concrete caissons are considered for construction of
the jacking receiving pits In installing these precast concrete caissons care should be

Microtunneling
Microtunneling pipe jacking is a trenchless process for installing underground
pipelines ducts culverts and utilities The technique involves the use of powerful
hydraulic jacks that push or jack successive pipe sections through the ground behind a
remotely controlled excavating unit or cutting disk head at the same time as the
excavation is taking place within the excavating unit The following subsections briefly
describe the advantages and methods of construction for Microtunneling Pipe Jacking
Major Advantages or Benefits
In general use of microtunneling pipe jacking techniques may offer the following
major advantages or benefits as compared to the conventional open trench
construction method
e Reduced disturbance and potential damage to adjacent utilities existing roads
and surrounding structures
e Improved public and job site safety
e Reduced surface disruption to vehicular and pedestrian traffic
e Reduced disturbance to local residents and businesses
e Reduced air and noise pollution and vibration
e Greater flexibility in alignment selection and reduced need for utility relocations
e Reduced extensive shoring system
e Reduced need for significant dewatering
e Smaller volumes of spoils that require transportation and disposal
e Reduced potential for treatment of contaminated soil and groundwater

Construction Methodology
In general tunneling methods would depend on the stability of the ground Where
unstable conditions are likely to be encountered the face of the tunnel must be
made stable to prevent ground loss and to enable mining to take place safely
This may be achieved by the following methods
dewatering by well points or deep wells
grouting by the use of cement cement bentonite or chemicals
ground freezing and
FON
using specialized tunneling methods such as
a compressed air
b full face earth pressure balancing machines or
c slurry soft ground tunnel boring machines
The choice of process is a function of the nature of the ground the water content
and in particular the particle size analysis of the subsoils encountered In soft
clays or saturated sand deposits such as those encountered at the site
pressurized face microtunneling pipe jacking equipment can be used to install the
pipeline without excessive loss of ground and significant surface settlement
In order to install a pipeline using this technique jacking and receiving pits will
need to be constructed A concrete thrust block constructed behind the jacking
frame within the jacking pit provides the reaction to the hydraulic jacks In
weak poor ground conditions piling or other arrangements may need to be
employed to increase the reaction capability of the thrust block The hydraulic
jacks pressing against the back of the pipeline advance the disk cutting head into
the soil formation To ensure that the jacking forces are distributed around the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3139 20 Page 16
circumference of a pipe being jacked a thrust ring is normally provided with the
design depending on the number of jacks being used The jacks are
interconnected hydraulically to ensure that the thrust from each jack is the same
The number of jacks used may vary depending on the pipe size the strength of
the jacking pipes the length to be installed and the anticipated frictional
resistance
The disk cutting head of the tunnel boring machine is controlled via cables from a
cabin on the surface where the operator monitors speed pressure and tunneling
accuracy At the face of the tunnel a rotating cutter head digs into the soil and
rock and mixes it with groundwater and water pumped from the surface Rocky
material is reduced to maximum inch size in a crushing chamber located directly
behind the cutter disk The excavated material is pumped out of the tunnel as
slurry The water introduced into the machine is matched in pressure and volume
to the material being displaced This prevents over excavation of the tunnel face
thereby reducing the potential for cave in of the soil surrounding the new casing
The cutting disk speed and direction of rotation the hydraulic jacking system as
well as the slurry system operations can be controlled from one control panel
located remotely at the launch pit The hydraulic steering cylinders located on the
disk cutting head can be extended to steer the head back on course should any
deviation be detected A laser guidance system provides information such as
length of drive vertical and horizontal heading vertical horizontal and _ roll
deviation from the programmed course and steering cylinder status data on a
near continuous basis so that adjustments can be made continuously to keep the
disk cutting head on course

Anticipated Tunneling Conditions
Site exploration in evaluating the subsurface conditions are important in assessing
the feasibility of microtunneling pipe jacking techniques and in determining appropriate
construction methods For the design and cost estimation pertaining to
microtunneling pipe jacking special attention should be given to the characteristics of the
soils likely to be encountered together with details of the water table such as its rate of
inflow and any tidal fluctuation or seasonal changes There are several important factors
relating to the soil conditions that may significantly influence project feasibility
construction planning and the associated construction costs These generally include
face or heading stability settlement potential and magnitudes friction along the pipeline
allowable passive pressures for providing thrust block resistance groundwater control
muck characteristics and any special disposal requirements For microtunneling pipe
jacking in non cohesive soils field and laboratory tests should be performed to evaluate
the following 1 gradation characteristics 2 permeability 3 density and 4 standard
penetration of the subsoils

Ground Behavior
Ground behavior for soft ground tunneling may be described according to six
categories as summarized on Plate 5 Categories of Ground Conditions for Soft Ground
Tunnels Coarse grained granular cohesionless or predominantly sandy soils can be
characterized as firm running or flowing ground depending on the degree of cementation
and on the groundwater conditions Above the groundwater level these soils will not
stand unsupported but tend to ravel or run until a stable slope is formed that buttresses
the tunnel face If there are sufficient fines present to give the soil some apparent
cohesion these soils may be able to stand unsupported in the heading for short periods
of time Below the groundwater level these soils may be flowing ground unless the
cohesion or cementation of the soil is high enough to resist the seepage forces Both
running or flowing ground behavior indicates potentially unstable ground conditions with
low stand up time

excavations and to provide information on the selection of appropriate equipment and
methods for the tunnel and shaft construction Based on our review of the subsurface

sands at about the pipe invert elevations along a large portion of the proposed force main

Groundwater was encountered at depths ranging from about 3 8 to 10 3 feet below

approximately 2 to 6 feet of water above the top of the proposed force main pipes Based
on the groundwater levels anticipated and the nature of the soils to be encountered

for the excavation work The applicability of several methods of groundwater control is
discussed below
Available methods of groundwater control may include the following
1 predrainage or dewatering using pumping well system 2 ground freezing methods
3 cement or chemical grouting and 4 balancing the hydrostatic pressure using
compressed air or pressurized face tunneling equipment Predrainage methods utilizing
dewatering systems may cause consolidation of the sand layers leading to significant
settlement of the ground surface and is not recommended Surface constraints such as
traffic flow would also make it difficult to install the wells at the required close spacing
Ground freezing although technically feasible would probably be very expensive and
less effective in the State of Hawaii Compressed air tunneling techniques would be more
expensive than other options due to the high labor costs associated with compressed air
working environment Cement or chemical grouting methods may be feasible because
the percentage of fines in the sand layers are considered relatively low and may be
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3139 20 Page 21
adequate for grout permeation Pressurized face tunneling equipment such as slurry
shields can provide comparable face support to compressed air but more safely and less
costly Therefore the use of a pressurized close face tunneling shield should be
considered for the subject project based on the anticipated subsurface conditions along
the proposed force main alignment A tunneling shield with a pressurized face capability
is well suited for this project because it can control both flowing and squeezing ground
without the need for dewatering along the tunnel alignment Face stability can be

tunneling operations

Settlement Due to Tunneling Operations
Surface settlement is usually considered to be the primary source of damage to
adjacent streets utilities and other surface facilities during tunneling operations For
microtunneling pipe jacking techniques loss of ground is generally associated with soil
squeezing or running into the heading losses due to overcutting by the shield shield
steering adjustments and from construction dewatering
Minor ground losses during the advance of the heading are unavoidable with
normal construction techniques and the actual magnitudes of these losses are largely
dependent on the type of ground groundwater conditions overload factor in cohesive
soils size and depth of the tunnel and the skill of the operator in steering the shield or
machine Use of an interlocking sheet piling shoring support system with dewatering or
precast concrete caissons with tremie concrete plugs are relatively watertight which
should allow the groundwater levels outside the shafts to be maintained close to the
original preconstruction levels Therefore dewatering at the jacking and receiving pit
locations should not cause significant settlement
Empirical methods have been developed for estimating surface settlement due to
soft ground tunneling by study of observed settlements on past projects The typical
settlement pattern that develops above a soft ground tunnel is a trough shaped
depression resembling an inverted normal probability curve with the maximum settlement
occurring above the tunnel centerline The behavior of the ground is also influenced by
the cover above the tunnel When the cover is greater than about two tunnel diameters
arching develops in the ground above the tunnel reducing the potential for significant
ground movement and surface settlement For the proposed microtunnel located at
depths of about 8 to 12 feet below the existing ground surface the maximum ground
surface settlement is estimated to be on the order of to inch with the width of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3139 20 Page 23
settlement trough estimated to be approximately 20 feet or about 10 feet on each side of
the tunnel centerline This estimate assumes that pressurized close face tunneling
equipment is utilized that such equipment is operated properly and that appropriate
construction procedures are used to minimize loss of ground Depending on the actual
construction procedures and workmanship of the Contractor larger surface settlement
may occur particularly if loss of ground is not monitored and carefully controlled as the
tunnel is excavated We believe that the estimated settlement magnitudes discussed
above are not likely to result in significant damage to the roadways or utilities above the
tunnel or to the surrounding structures

Thrust Block Resistance
The thrust block provides the reaction for the main jacks when pushing the pipe
forward The allowable passive resistance of the soil which is a function of the
strength of the soil will control the thrust resistance that can be developed It may
seem reasonable to allow the thrust block to develop the full passive resistance
because this is a temporary construction loading condition However it must be
recognized that relatively large strains are required to develop the full maximum
passive resistance At jacking loads that impose stresses approaching the
maximum passive resistance there will be large movements of the thrust block in
response to the strains required to develop full passive resistance These
movements may result in damage to buried utilities or other facilities behind the
thrust block
In addition large movements of the thrust block are not desirable for pipe jacking
because the movements are usually not uniform and it becomes difficult to
maintain the thrust block perpendicular to the pipe alignment If the thrust block
does not remain square the force applied to the pipe may not be uniform resulting
in eccentric loading which could damage the pipe Design of the thrust block for
this project should take into consideration the presence of loose sands adjacent to
the shaft excavations These loose sand deposits may limit the jacking forces that
can be mobilized unless a rigid reinforced concrete thrust block is constructed to
distribute the jacking forces over a large enough area to avoid overstressing these
soils
Movements of the thrust block can also be controlled by limiting the allowable
passive resistance to about one half of the maximum passive resistance This
corresponds to a much lower level of strain and smaller soil movements If the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3139 20 Page 27
project requires higher jacking forces than can be safely transferred to the soil
mass behind the thrust block then the soil should be improved or stabilized

Categories of Ground Conditions for Soft Ground Tunnels
After Terzaghi 1950
Firm Ground A heading may be advanced several feet or more without immediate
support Hard clays and cemented sand or gravel generally fall into this category
Raveling Ground After excavation material above the tunnel or in the upper part of
the working face tends to flake off and fall into the heading Slightly cohesive sands
silts and fine sands gaining their strength from apparent cohesion typically exhibit this
type of behavior Very stiff fissured clays may be ravelling materials also
Running Ground Cohesionless dry soils run from any unsupported vertical face until a
stable slope forms at the natural angle of repose i e approximately 3 degrees to
35 degrees Running ground consists of dry cohesionless materials such as clean
loose sand or gravel
Flowing Ground If seepage develops at the working face raveling or running ground is
transformed to flowing ground which advances like a viscous fluid into the heading
Silt sand or gravel below the water table without a high enough clay content to
develop significant cohesion will be flowing type soils
Swelling Ground A condition where the ground absorbs water increases in volume
and expands slowly into the tunnel This may occur in highly overconsolidated clays
that exhibit high volume change characteristics upon wetting
Squeezing Ground Squeezing ground conditions are analogous to plastic flow and
the soil is observed to advance slowly into the tunnel excavation without any signs of
fracturing Squeezing occurs without an increase in the water content or a volume
change in the soil and is governed by the soil strength in comparison to the overburden
pressure Squeezing ground may include soft to medium stiff or stiff clays depending
on the overburden pressure at the tunnel depth

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling a total
of eighteen borings to depths ranging from about 16 5 to 36 5 feet below the existing
ground surface using a truck mounted drill rig Boring Nos 1 through 8 were conducted
during our previous exploration in 1993 for the preliminary engineering phase and Boring
Nos 9 through 18 were conducted during this current exploration The approximate
locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineers who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials

APPENDIX B
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D2216 and unit weight determinations are used as an
aid to soil classification and to aid in evaluating certain properties such as relative
saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight of the
tested sample and dry unit weight are shown at the appropriate sample depths on the
Logs of Borings
Twenty two sieve analyses ASTM D422 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plates B 1 through
B 7
Four direct shear tests were conducted on selected samples to estimate the angle
of internal friction and cohesion of the sandy soils Results of the direct shear tests are
presented on Plates B 8 through B 11
