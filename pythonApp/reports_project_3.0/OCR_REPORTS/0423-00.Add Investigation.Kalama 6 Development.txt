The property as shown on Plate 1 covers approximately 35 acres
of sloping ground in the upper end of Kalama Valley Ehukai

GEOLOGY AND SOIL CONDITIONS
A Geology
Kalama Valley was formed by stream erosion of Tertiary Age
Koolau Range basalt flows The initial period of erosion
and valley carving was followed by several periods of re
lative rise and fall of sea level which permitted valley
filling with alluvium followed by subsequent periods of
erosion
Page 4
W O 423 00
During a late stage in the valley s development submarine
and subaerial explosions of Koko Head volcano blanketed
the valley with volcanic ash breccia and coral fragments
Much of this erupted debris was subsequently washed into
the valley and mixed with the clay and boulders being
eroded from the valley walls
Finally after the ash deposits had been washed from the
valley walls a1 to at least 9 foot thick layer of black
organic expansive boulder clay accumulated on the lower
valley slopes Due to the asymmetrical shape of the valley
in cross section this Adobe layer is more thickly
developed on the east side of the valley
A large boulder train cohesionless talus was formed in
the last stages of the valley s development at the head
of the valley

CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
A General
The exploration programs disclosed highly variable subsur
face conditions within the site From a geologic and
engineering viewpoint site development appears feasible
but in areas will be very expensive
Page 7
W O 423 00
The valley slopes in their present condition are generally
stable however the upper layer of black clay material is
in a continual state of downhill creep Development will
alter the balance of the present system
Removal of existing vegetation site grading structures
underground utilities irrigation etc must be carefully
engineered to minimize their adverse affect on the present
balance The recommendations presented in this report are
made with that purpose
The presently proposed development extends past the limits
of development outlined for our initial report Preliminary
Soils and Geologic Investigation Upper Kalama Valley
The recommendations herein therefore encompass a much
larger area than previously considered
Due to the variability we have divided the site into four
areas see Plate 1 which are Central Lower Area West Slope
Boulder Area and East Slope

The central lower area of the property has somewhat flatter

1 Grading
Site grading in this area is acceptable however
grading should be kept to a minimum
Areas to receive compacted fills should be cleared
of all deleterious material such as vegetation
topsoil trash etc which should be wasted from the
site
After clearing and grubbing the fill areas should
be scarified to remove uneven features and should be
recompacted according to the requirements for struc
tural fills
In structural areas the surface layer of black
highly expansive CLAY and any local soft spots must
Page 9
W O 423 00
be removed and replaced with compacted structural fill
Fill material should have no rocks larger than 12 inches
Larger rocks may be disposed of on site by placing in
staggered windrows as detailed in Appendix C Plates C 1 2
or they may be removed from the site
The onsite highly expansive black CLAY should not be
used in the upper 10 feet of fill areas Imported fill
material should be relatively non expansive material
and must be approved by the soils engineer prior to
utilization
Approved fill material should be placed in 12 inch lifts
and compacted to 90 relative compaction using ASTM
D 1557 70 as a standard
Fills placed on existing slopes steeper than 5H 1V must
be keyed at the toe and benched into the slope All
lifts must be placed horizontally or dipping slightly
into the hillside see Plate C 3
Fill slopes should not be steeper than 2H 1V If
steeper slopes are desired each case must be evaluated
Page 10
W O 423 00
separately based on necessary testing and stability
analyses using acquired data
Compaction of the fill slope face should be done pro
gressively after each three foot increment of fill
has been placed It should be accomplished by back
rolling with the compaction equipment Slope planting
immediately after completion is recommended to mini
mize erosion
All backfill for footings and utilities must be im
pervious clay material compacted to a minimum of 90
relative compaction
Provisions for site drainage both during and after
completion of the filling operation must be considered
If springs are encountered during excavation or where
fill is placed over intermittent streams subdrains
may be required

Lateral Earth Pressure
Retaining walls less than 10 feet high should be designed
for an equivalent fluid pressure of 35 pounds per cubic
foot provided only moderate compaction of backfill is
required If a high degree of backfill compaction is
required or the walls cannot yield even slightly an
equivalent fluid pressure of 60 pounds per cubic foot
should be used These values assume free draining
granular backfill for at least 3 feet from the wall
The top 12 inches of backfill must be an impervious clay
material to prevent runoff from entering the backfill
Surcharges due to adjacent footings hydrostatic pressure
construction equipment etc must be added to the above
values

Boulder Train Area
Approximately in the center of this area a boulder train
was observed This area is stable in its present condi
tion Any steepening or surcharging of this area would
result in reducing the factor of safety against sliding
below an acceptable value
The angle of deposition 6 is the inclination of a
Slope formed by the deposition of cohesionless material
The angle of repose is the maximum possible inclination
of a slope of a given mass of cohesionless material
The factor of safety FS for cohesionless material has
been defined as
tan p repose
FS
tan 6 deposition
The angle of repose for hard angular igneous rock has
been found to range from 37 to 42 degrees Hence the
factor of safety for the angular basalt comprising the
talus slope at Kalama Valley is approximately
tan 37 753
tan 25 466
Page 17
W O 423 00
The talus slope is therefore considered to be relatively
stable in its present configuration However since the
material is cohesionless it cannot be cut reworked
or surcharged without reducing its factor of safety with
regard to stability below an acceptable value
Natural phenomena such as earthquakes or floods could
markedly reduce its present factor of safety It is
therefore recommended that this cohesionless mass of
boulders be left in its natural state and loading con
dition
If this is not an acceptable solution and development
of the area is essential we recommend that the entire
boulder train and all the secondary feeder trains be
removed prior to development

Utilities
All underground utilities should be provided with flexible
connections so that dislocations due to creep movement
will not cause leaks and or exfiltration These utilities
must be specifically designed to eliminate leakage and
exfiltration
The most desirable design would be to have these utilities
in a utility duct so that movements associated with creep
would not directly affect the actual utilities
Backfill for utility trenches must be relatively impermeable
clay material with no rocks
All runoff including house roof and gutter runoff must be
collected and transfered in a closed system to the storm
drains
Landscaping
Landscaping should be planned to prevent any disturbances
to existing vegetation
Since the addition of water to sloping ground would tend
Page 22
W O 423 00
to accelerate the rate of creep we recommend that land
scaping be done with vegetation that requires little
preferably no irrigation

LABORATORY TESTING
A laboratory testing program was developed to establish the
a engineering properties of the soils and rocks encountered at
the project site The test where applicable to the subject
project are included in this Appendix
1 Classification Tests
a
c
Visual Classification All soil samples obtained from
the borings were brought to our laboratory where they
were visually reclassified to confirm or modify the
field classification prior to finalizing the Logs of
Borings
Water Content Determinations In addition to visual
classification typical samples were tested for natural
water content as an aid in soil classification and in
evaluating soil properties The water content values
are based upon the dry weight of the soil
Atterberg Limits Atterberg limit tests were performed
on selected clay and silt samples obtained from the
borings for the purposes of identification and correla
tion of soils Standard procedures ASTM D 424 for the
plastic limit and ASTM D 423 for the liquid limit were
used in the performance of these tests
1
2
d Grain Size Analysis Sieve analysis were performed on
granular soils For fine grained soils the hydrometer
test was performed to determine the distribution of
the grain sizes beyond the No 200 Sieve
Shear Strength Tests
a Unconfined Compression Tests Intact core and split
spoon soil samples which apparently exhibited high
plasticity were tested for unconfined strength Length
to diameter ratio of 2 was maintained throughout the
tests to avoid scatter of test results and to facilitate
evaluation of shear strength values for design purposes
b Direct Shear Test In general soil samples were tested
for shear strength using a 2 41 I D direct shear box
Each set consisted of at least two tests each test run
under different normal loads The results are plotted
as Normal Stress vs Shear Strength with a failure envelope
drawn to determine values of cohesion c and angle of
frictional resistance
Consolidation Test Consolidation tests were performed on
selected undisturbed ring samples of the compressible
soils to provide basic data for making settlement calculations
Porous stones were placed on the top and bottom of the samples
to allow drainage Vertical loads were applied in increments
with each load increment being allowed to consolidate prior
2
to adding the next increment Measurements of the time
and consolidation were obtained during each load increment
and rebound was measured during the unloading portion
Consolidation test resujts are plotted in terms of percent
settlement versus applied loads
Swell Test The ring sample was placed between porous
stones and a 60 psf load was applied to the sample for a
24 hour period The difference between the initial and
final sample heights is the amount of swell which is expressed
as a percentage of the initial height of the sample
Maximum Density Test Samples of soils are compacted in a
mold of given size with a 10 pound rammer dropped 18 inches
to determine the relationship between moisture content and
density of the soil At least three trial points at dif
ferent moisture content are run to determine the maximum
density and optimum moisture content The test is run in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 70

Seasonal requirements
1 Clearing
All areas within contract limit lines shall be cleared

In areas where the bottom of footings are designed on

scarified to a depth as determined by the soils engineer

shall be precompacted as outlined in the following
Section 4b

All areas upon which fill is to be placed after clearing
as outlined in Section 1 of these specifications shall
be scarified until free of uneven features to a depth
as determined by the soils engineer and watered and
compacted according to Section 4 of these specifications
Placement of Fill
a
Material for fill shall consist of onsite soils
Fill material shall be free of all organic matter
and other deleterious material and shall not contain
rocks or lumps in excess of four inches 4 in
diameter
Compaction of Fill
After the base for the fill has been prepared as
described above it shall be brought to the proper
moisture content and compacted to not less than
90 of maximum density in accordance with Test
ASTM D 1557 70
Depth of Fill
Fill shall be placed in horizontal layers which
when compacted will not exceed six inches 6

t

The soils engineer shall determine the type of compacting
equipment which will attain the specified results in the
_most efficient manner Sheepsfoot vibratory or pneumatic
tire rollers may be used in the test section and the equip
ment which produces the specified results in the most
expedient manner as determined by the soils engineer shall
be employed by the contractor The equipment used in roll
ing shall be in good working condition fully ballasted
and self cleaning Fill material placed in an unsatis
factory condition and not within the enclosed specifications
shall be rejected by the soils engineer and the contractor
shall rework the fill placed such that the specifications
are followed

cations

the required density is obtained

approve previously placed fill before resumption of earth
moving operations

achieve 90 compaction
