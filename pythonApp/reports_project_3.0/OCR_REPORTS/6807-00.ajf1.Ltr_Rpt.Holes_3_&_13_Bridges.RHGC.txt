REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the 
sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such 
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea. The processes of erosion and 
deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea level fluctuations. When the sea 
level was low, the erosional base level was correspondingly lower, and valleys were 
carved to depths below the present sea level. When the sea level was high, the erosional 
base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosional processes are dominated by 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope 
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once these materials reach 
the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become dominant, and the 
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. In general, stream flows in Hawaii 
are intermittent and flashy, such that the stream flows transmit large volumes of water for 
very short durations. Because of this, transport of sediments is intermittent, and the bulk 
of the stream's hydraulic load consists of a poorly-sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, 
gravel, sands, and fines. When the erosional base levels change, these sediment loads 
are left as deposits.

FIELD EXPLORATION 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the proposed bridge locations by drilling 
and sampling two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 4 and 5, extending to depths 
ranging from about 25.4 to 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were 
drilled using a track-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2 and 3. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field. He monitored the drilling operations on a near-continuous 
basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by laboratory testing. Soils 
were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System, as 
shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate 4. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings, Plates 5 and 6. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration 
sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our boring drilled at the Hole #3 Bridge generally encountered fill materials 
consisting of stiff clayey silts overlying alluvial soil deposits consisting of soft to medium 
stiff silty clays and stiff to hard clayey silts extending to the maximum depth explored of 
about 26.5 feet.  
Our field exploration at the Hole #13 Bridge generally encountered fill materials 
consisting of medium stiff to stiff clayey silts overlying alluvial soil deposits consisting of 
medium stiff silty clays and stiff to hard clayey silts. Highly weathered basalt rock 
formation was encountered below the alluvial soil deposits at a depth of about 13 feet and 
extended to the maximum depth explored of about 25.4 feet below the existing ground 
surface. 
Groundwater was encountered in Boring No. 4 at a depth of about 6.2 feet below 
the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. Groundwater was not 
encountered in Boring No. 5 within the depths drilled at the time of our field exploration. 
Considering the bridge structures are located adjacent to a waterway, groundwater levels 
likely will vary in response to the water level in the waterway. In addition, it should be 
noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.

SEISMIC DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS 
We understand the bridge structures will be designed based on Load and 
Resistance Factor Design (LRFD) methods. Based on AASHTO LRFD Bridge Design 
Specifications, 2010 Edition (2010 AASHTO LRFD), seismic design of the bridge 
structures will need to be based on a 1,000-year return period. Based on the subsurface 
materials encountered at the project site and the geologic setting of the area, we 
anticipate the project site may be classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as a “Stiff 
Soil Profile,” corresponding to Site Class D based on Table 3.10.3.1-1 of the 2010 
AASHTO LRFD.  
When seismic waves propagate through a soil profile, most of the times the 
seismic shear waves get intensified and amplified (or attenuated) depending on the 
thickness and properties of the soils. Based on a Site Class D soil profile, the following 
seismic design parameters were estimated and may be used for seismic analysis of the 
new bridge structures.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered relatively stiff ground conditions at both 
bridge locations. Therefore, we recommend using shallow spread and/or continuous strip 
footings to support the bridge abutments and wing walls.  
Based on our field exploration, the excavated on-site clayey soils may be re-used 
as a source of fill provided they are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock 
fragments greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension. It should be noted that the 
project site is located in a high rainfall environment throughout the year; therefore, the 
on-site soils will be constantly in a very moist to wet condition. We believe it may be

BRIDGE FOUNDATIONS 
Based on discussions with the project ownership, the bridge foundation design will 
consider the potential for scour at the bridge abutment locations. Based on this direction, 
the bridge foundations should be embedded to a depth of at least 24 inches below the 
estimated scour depth at the bridge abutment locations. It should be noted that estimated 
scour depths at the bridge abutment locations have not been provided at this time. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, we recommend supporting the 
bridge abutments and wing walls on a shallow foundation system consisting of spread 
and/or continuous strip footings bearing on the underlying stiff clayey soils. However, we 
do not recommend supporting the structures on the soft clayey soil encountered in 
Boring No. 4. Based on 2010 AASHTO LRFD methods, an ultimate bearing capacity of 
up to 7,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used to evaluate the extreme event limit 
state of the footings bearing on the underlying stiff clayey soils. To evaluate the strength 
limit state of the bridge structure foundations, a bearing value of up to 3,400 psf may be 
used.  
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the 
estimated scour depth and bear on the underlying stiff clayey soils. The footing subgrades 
should be compacted to a firm and unyielding surface prior to placement of foundation 
concrete. Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the footing 
excavations, such as the soft clayey soil encountered in Boring No. 4, should be 
over-excavated to expose the underlying stiff soils. The over-excavation should be 
backfilled with lean concrete, or the bottom of footing may be extended deeper to bear on 
the more competent subgrade materials. We wish to emphasize that soil-type backfills, 
such as granular fill or leveling course, should not be used below the bridge structure 
foundations to avoid the potential for undermining and scour due to the anticipated 
flowing water conditions at the bridge locations. 
In the process of excavating to provide a level footing subgrade with the 
recommended embedment, we anticipate the footing excavation likely will be larger than 
the concrete footings. Therefore, we recommend backfilling the footing excavations with 
lean concrete or mortar (sand-cement grout) around the concrete foundations to reduce 
the potential for ponding water to accumulate around the poorly backfilled footing 
excavations. 
Foundations constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground should be 
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set-back distance of 6 feet 
measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face. Footings located 
adjacent to planned (or existing) retaining walls should be embedded deep enough to 
avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations. In addition, foundations next to utility

Drainage 
The abutment walls should be well drained to reduce the potential for build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of 
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), placed 
directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of the wall 
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, a prefabricated 
drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used instead of the drainage 
material. The prefabricated drainage product also should be hydraulically connected to a 
perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
The backfill from the bottom of the wall to the bottom of the perforated pipe or 
weephole should consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for 
significant water infiltration into the subsurface. In addition, the upper 12 inches of the 
retaining structure backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the 
potential for significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure unless covered by 
concrete slabs at the surface. 
SITE GRADING 
We anticipate the site grading work for the bridge structures generally will consist 
of excavations for foundations and the placement of backfill material behind the abutment 
and wing walls. At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the excavation limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site or in a designated 
area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials with the spoils.  
The footing subgrades should be compacted to a firm and unyielding surface prior 
to placement of foundation concrete. Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the 
bottom of the footing excavations, such as the soft clayey soil encountered in Boring 
No. 4, should be over-excavated to expose the underlying stiff soils. The over-excavation 
should be backfilled with lean concrete, or the bottom of footing may be extended deeper 
to bear on the more competent subgrade materials. The excavated soft and/or organic 
soils should be properly disposed of off-site or used in landscaping areas, if appropriate. 
As mentioned previously, soil-type backfills such as granular fill or leveling course should 
not be used below the bridge structure foundations to avoid the potential for undermining 
and scour due to the anticipated flowing water conditions at the bridge locations.  
The excavated on-site soils may be re-used as a source of backfill material 
provided they are free of deleterious materials and contain rock fragments no larger than 
6 inches in maximum dimension. It should be noted that the project site is located in a 
high rainfall environment throughout the year; therefore, the on-site soils will be constantly 
in a very moist to wet condition. We believe it may be difficult to achieve adequate 
compaction without significant aeration of the on-site soils. However, it may not be 
feasible and practical to aerate the on-site soils, and it may be advantageous to replace 
the on-site soils with select granular fill materials.

Imported backfill materials, if required, should consist of non-expansive select 
granular material, such as crushed coral or basalt. The material should be well graded 
from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. In addition, 
the material should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 
sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a 
maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883. 
Backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to 
between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Based on the anticipated grading work and our field exploration, excavations may 
encounter boulders and involve cuts into the underlying basalt formation. It is anticipated 
that most of the material may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. 
However, deep excavations and excavations into basalt formations may require the use of 
hoerams or trenching machines.  
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based 
on field data obtained from the borings performed at the project site. We recommend 
encouraging contractors bidding on this project to examine the site conditions and soil data 
to make their own interpretation. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor site grading operations to observe 
whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation process and to 
confirm whether the exposed soil and/or rock conditions are similar to those encountered 
in our field exploration.
