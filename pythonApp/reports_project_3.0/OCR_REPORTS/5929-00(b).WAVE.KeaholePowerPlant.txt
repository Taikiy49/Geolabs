Project Considerations
The project site is within the existing Keahole Power Plant Unit ST 7 at 73 4249
Queen Kaahumanu Highway in the Kailua Kona area on the Island of Hawaii The project
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The project
site is near the southeast corner of the intersection of Queen Kaahumanu Highway and
Keahole Airport Road as shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2
As an alternative to seismic crosshole downhole testing as specified in the
Request For Quotation RFQ we proposed to perform seismic shear wave velocity
profiling of the subsurface materials at two of the probe hole locations using Seismic
Cone Penetration Testing SCPT equipment After the borehole was advanced to the
desired depth the borehole was backfilled with 0 25 inch diameter coated bentonite
pellets The temporary casing from the coring operations was used as a tremie pipe to
place the bentonite pellets starting from the bottom and advancing upward When the
bentonite pellets are in contact with water in the borehole from the drilling operations
the pellets slowly hydrated As the bentonite pellets hydrated they swelled and
softened The probes are then pushed through the softened bentonite to the bottom of
the borehole using Seismic Cone Penetration Testing equipment

Field Exploration
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling using SCPT equipment was performed in an
effort to more closely analyze the seismic design considerations for the project site Shear
wave velocity testing using seismic cone penetration testing equipment was performed at
two selected probe holes designated as BVP 111 and BVP 113 as shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 The tests were performed to a depth of about 50 feet below the existing
ground surface using seismic cone penetration testing SCPT equipment After drilling at
the locations the boreholes were backfilled with 0 25 inch coated bentonite pellets using
the casing as a tremie pipe When the bentonite pellets encountered water from the drilling
core operations they swelled and softened as a coupling medium from the cone
penetrometer to the surrounding ground

The seismic shear wave velocity test consisted of hydraulically pushing a 10 ton
steel electronic subtraction cone with an apex angle of 60 degrees and an area of
1 55 square inches 10 square centimeters into the ground The depth of penetration was
recorded at about 2 inch 5 centimeter intervals using a depth sensor attached to the
cone rod
The cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the arrival of a shear
wave propagated from the ground surface The seismic measurements are made when
the SCPT has stopped and a shear wave is sent into the subsurface A shear wave is
generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a switched hammer The
propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone was measured at each
depth interval The vector difference of these depths divided by the time difference
provided the average shear wave velocity over the depth interval
Soil Profile Type
The shear wave velocities measured and the weighted average shear wave
velocity for the top 50 feet of the soil profile at the probe hole locations are presented on
Plates 3 and 4 The weighted average shear wave velocity was calculated based on the
average shear wave velocity method of the Uniform Building Code Section
1997 Edition
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration the
weighted average shear wave velocity was measured at about 3 340 and 2 550 feet per
second at the two probe holes designated as BVP 111 and BVP 113 respectively Based
on the shear wave velocity test results we believe the project site may be classified from a
seismic analysis standpoint as a Rock Profile corresponding to a Soil Profile Type Sz in
accordance with Table 16 J of the Uniform Building Code 1997
Limitations
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon
information obtained from the seismic cone penetration test Variations of subsurface
conditions and shear wave velocities between and beyond the test probes may occur and
the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until further testing or
construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to
re evaluate the recommendations provided in this letter report
