performed for the proposed expansion of the existing Honouliuli Wastewater Treatment

its vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The project site is located on the Ewa Coastal Plain on the southern coast of the
island of Oahu This coastal plain is composed of a thick wedge of interbedded marine
deposits and terrestrial sediments which have accumulated on the southern flanks of
Waianae and Koolau the two extinct volcanoes that built the island
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
growth and retreat of the great continental glaciers As the glaciers advanced less water
was available to fill the ocean basins and sea levels stood below the current sea level
As the glaciers melted and retreated more water became available and sea levels rose
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 2951 00 Page 7
to and often above the current level When the stand of the sea was high and prolonged
marine deposits accumulated at elevations above the current sea level
At the project site the near surface materials are primarily coral algal reef deposits
which are composed of medium hard limestone with an open lattice like structure It is
believed that these coral deposits were laid during the Kaena 95 feet MSL stand of
the sea and subsequently planed to near their current elevation during the Waimanalo
25 feet MSL stand The coral formation has also been subjected to subaerial
weathering
Coral algal reef materials are chemically composed of calcium carbonate During
subaerial weathering slightly acidic rain and groundwaier can slowly attack the carbonate
dissolving the weaker portions of the formation This creates solution cavities ranging
from small pores to large caverns
Subsoil Conditions
The subsurface soil conditions at the proposed plant expansion site were explored
by drilling and sampling twenty five 25 borings ranging in depths between 10 to 3814 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown
on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3
Our field exploration indicates that the project site is generally underlain by coral
algal reef formation and coral detritus deposits of varying degrees of hardness This
coral was generally hard very competent and had few apparent voids to a depth of
about 15 feet below the existing ground surface At greater depths the coral became
softer in most of the borings It appears to be more friable and contain more voids Most
of the voids encountered during our exploration appeared to be infilled at least partially
with loose to medium dense sand and gravel

a one 1 to two feet 2 thick surficial layer of imported topsoil consisting of slightly

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our field exploration the project site appears to be underlain primarily
by dense coral algal reef formation interbedded with severely jointed coral detritus
deposits The major geotechnical considerations for the expansion project are the quality
and uniformity of the coral algal reef formation at the locations of the proposed
structures
We believe that the dense coral algal reef formation encountered would provide
good foundation support for the new construction We recommend that the large
diameter below grade tank structures be supported by ring footing foundations bearing
directly on the coral algal reef formation For the above grade tank structures we
recommend ring footing foundations bearing on the compacted structural fill needed to
raise the finish grade elevation The foundation slabs for the above and below grade
tank structures may be supported on grade Shallow spread footing foundations may be
used for support of the proposed one story building structures with slabs on grade
construction
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2951 00 Page 9
Because of the potential variable quality of the coral aigal reef formation at the site
deep construction excavations for the below grade structures may require some
temporary shoring of the excavations The type of shoring depends on the quality of the
coral algal reef formation encountered during construction In lieu of temporary shoring
sloping back of the excavations may be a feasible alternative
Details of our geotechnical engineering recommendations are further discussed in

Excavation Support
Based on the existing topography at the project site and the anticipated design
bottom elevations of the future structures it appears that deep excavation will be
required for some of the structures Our field exploration indicates that the site is
generally underlain by competent coral algal reef formation and coral deirital
deposits However the quality of the coral algal reef formation and coral detrital
deposits can be highly variable
It is our opinion that excavations in competent coral formation may be able to
stand unsupported at relatively steep angles for temporary construction purposes
However if poor quality coral formation is encountered during construction
temporary shoring of the excavation may be required As an alternative because
of the large area of the project site sloping the sides of the excavations should be
considered
Since relatively hard coral formation was encountered at the project site the use
of sheet pile shoring does not appear to be feasible It is our opinion that a
support system consisting of soldier piles and lagging with temporary tiebacks or
rakers could be adopted to provide support of the excavation The use of
horizontal bracing or struts inside the excavation does not appear practical
because of the anticipated wide excavation For preliminary design of the

excavation The magnitude of movement is difficult to accurately estimate due to

rate of excavation

properly and as early as practical if necessary and that the adjacent ground
continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of movements

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of
twenty five 25 borings to depths ranging between 10 to 38 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3
The borings were drilled using truck mounted drill rig
The material encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who continuously monitored the drilling
operations These classifications were further reviewed by visual inspection and testing
in the laboratory All soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered are presented
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a standard penetration sampler or 3 O D Modified California sampler with a
140 pound hammer free falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the
last 12 inches are shown on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 and would be shown on the Boring Logs as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
