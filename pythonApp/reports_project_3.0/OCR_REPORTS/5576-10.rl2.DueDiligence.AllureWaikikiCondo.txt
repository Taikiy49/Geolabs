REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The Waianae Volcano older forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the Koolau Volcano younger shield forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae Volcano
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank is partially
buried below Koolau Volcano lavas in Central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano and its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu
Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil
Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of
the sea that are intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic
section in this area is Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level on the Island of Oahu About 15 000 years ago a
relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that sea level rise the valleys in the
project area were drowned In the last 10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its
present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in
low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits of soft and or
loose sediments along the coast in the areas that were formerly valleys and drainage
ways

In the early part of this century the Waikiki area consisted of low marshy wetlands
As the City of Honolulu grew and the Waikiki area was urbanized man made fills were
placed to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons for development The fill materials placed
on the lagoonal deposits consisted of silty sands with coral fragments The lower clayey
materials were deposited during periodic influxes of non marine alluvial debris which
apparently smothered the coral reef development Land development and reclamation
projects within the last 100 years have brought the Waikiki area to its present form It
should be noted that much of the resulting fill materials placed are of poor quality in terms
of supporting heavy structural loads

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Based on our experience in the area and a review of the general geology of the
Waikiki area the upper coral ledge typically encountered at depths of about 25 to 50 feet
below the existing ground surface in the Waikiki area is likely present at the project site
The upper coral ledge is generally underlain by coralline detritus materials interbedded
with alluvium coral formation and dense sandstone ledges extending to depths of over
100 feet below the ground level
Based on the preliminary geotechnical engineering exploration conducted at the
site the subsurface conditions at the project site are typical of the conditions found
elsewhere in the Waikiki area The project site is underlain by 3 feet of surface fills placed
over highly compressible lagoonal deposits The surface fills encountered in the

Liquefaction Potential
Based on the 1997 Uniform Building Code UBC current code adopted by the
City and County of Honolulu the project site may be subjected to seismic activity and the
potential for soil liquefaction at the project site will need to be evaluated Based on the
anticipated subsurface conditions and a design peak ground acceleration of 0 15g
liquefaction of the loose lagoonal deposits may occur resulting in appreciable settlements
at the ground surface Therefore the effects of potential liquefaction at the site will need to
be taken into consideration in the design of the development

In general the project site is underlain by soft and or loose lagoonal deposits
overlying thin coral ledges and medium dense coralline detritus deposits at greater depths
The coral ledges and medium dense coralline detritus generally are not susceptible to
liquefaction However the lagoonal deposits consisting of loose silty coralline sands and
gravel with little cohesion are potentially liquefiable during a seismic event of Magnitude 6
with an associated peak ground acceleration of 0 15g
Therefore the project site could be subjected to appreciable ground settlements in
the event of liquefaction during a moderate earthquake M6 with a peak ground
acceleration of 0 15g Based on the relatively flat site topography we believe the potential
for lateral spreading associated with liquefaction would not be a significant design
consideration for the project These seismic related issues should be further evaluated
and potential mitigation measures should be presented for implementation during the
design and construction of the project
Soil Profile Type for Seismic Design
As indicated previously the subsurface conditions consist of surface fills about
3 feet thick over loose lagoonal deposits about 25 feet thick overlying coral ledges and
medium dense to dense coralline detritus extending to over 100 feet below the existing
ground surface Based on the geology of the area and the subsurface materials
encountered within the upper 100 feet of depth the project site is classified as a Soft Soil
Profile Therefore the seismic design of the building structures at the project site would
likely need to be designed based on a Soil Profile Type Se in accordance with Table 16 J
of the Uniform Building Code 1997
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we believe that the site may be
upgraded to a Soil Profile Type Sc in accordance with Table 16 J of the Uniform Building
Code 1997 if a site specific seismic shear wave velocity profiling is performed during
detailed design Therefore we strongly recommend that seismic shear wave velocity
profiling using Seismic Cone Penetrometer Testing SCPTu equipment be conducted as
part of the design level geotechnical engineering exploration

Seismic Design Considerations
As mentioned previously the project site is underlain by potentially liquefiable soils
Therefore the effects of a potential seismic event will need to take into consideration the
design of the new condominium project planned at the site We recommend a site specific
seismic response evaluation including 1 seismic shear wave velocity profiling using
SCPTu equipment and 2 a site specific response spectrum analysis be conducted for
the seismic design of the high rise structure We anticipate deep foundations may be
utilized to mitigate the liquefaction concerns
Foundation Considerations
Based on the compressible lagoonal deposits anticipated and significant structural
demands on the high rise condominium foundations we believe a deep foundation
support system such as pile foundations or drilled shaft foundations would be required for
support of the proposed structure We envision these deep foundation support systems
on the order of about 80 to 120 feet deep would be similar to the designs for high rise
buildings in the Waikiki Ala Moana areas Evaluation of the feasibility of pile and drilled
shaft foundation alternatives to support the proposed building structure should be
conducted for the development Based on our recent experiences on high rise building
developments we believe that drilled shaft foundations would likely be more efficient when
compared to piles Detailed discussion pertaining to the preliminary design of the building
foundations utilizing drilled shaft foundations is provided below
In general we believe each column location for the building structure may be
supported by one drilled shaft to reduce the size of the footing at each column location
Based on the anticipated structural loads on the order of about 1 000 to 3 000 kips per
column the proposed building structure would likely be supported by a combination of
30 and 42 inch diameter cast in place concrete drilled shafts depending on the vertical
and lateral load demands on the foundations The drilled shafts would extend through the
loose and or soft lagoonal deposits and would embed into the coral ledges and medium
dense coralline detritus deposits The drilled shafts would derive vertical support primarily
from adhesion between the shaft and surrounding soils

Based on preliminary analyses the 42 inch diameter drilled shafts on the order of
about 100 to 120 feet deep would be required to develop allowable compressive loads of
about 2 000 to 3 000 kips per drilled shaft to support the structural loads anticipated at the
column locations of the proposed building structure For the lighter structural loads less
than 1 000 kips per column we recommend that drilled shafts with a smaller diameter
such as 30 inches be utilized to support the columns with lighter structural loads
It should be noted some difficult drilling conditions may be encountered during
installation of the drilled shafts due to the presence of loose granular soils and shallow
groundwater levels anticipated To reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes
temporary casing and or use of drilling fluids will likely be necessary to maintain the
integrity of the drilled holes during drilled shaft installation Furthermore placement of
concrete by tremie methods will be required for the drilled shafts because groundwater will
be encountered within the depths of the drilled shafts
It should be noted that cavities and voids are commonly encountered in the
coralline materials in the project vicinity Therefore the actual volume of concrete required
to fill the drilled shaft foundation may be appreciably more than the theoretical concrete
volume Based on our experience at the Waikiki Ala Moana areas we envision that
average concrete overpour on the order up to about 50 to 60 percent over the theoretical
concrete volume may be required for the drilled shaft foundations
Ground Settlement
Based on the existing topography we anticipate minimal site grading work would
be required to achieve the design finished grades at the site Due to the presence of loose
and or soft lagoonal deposits underlying the site at shallow depths we believe new fills if
required should be placed as soon as practical to induce the anticipated ground
settlements to occur prior to construction of structures slabs on grade and pavements
Settlement monitoring should be established to evaluate the magnitude and rate of the
estimated settlements during the waiting period prior to foundation construction over the
fills
Ground Floor Slabs
We anticipate that the ground floor slabs for the new building construction will
consist of reinforced concrete slabs on grade Based on the existing topography and the
anticipated finished floor elevation we envision that the slabs on grade for the proposed
building structure will be supported either on new compacted fills placed to raise the
existing ground surface to the finished subgrades or on the existing ground conditions
As mentioned previously the project site could be subjected to ground settlements
in the event of liquefaction caused by a moderate seismic event M6 Seismically
induced ground settlements on the order of about 3 to 4 inches may be anticipated and
the building slabs on grade may sustain some damage Therefore there exists the

Dewatering of Below Grade Excavations
Dewatering is not anticipated because a basement level is not planned However
some dewatering may be required during excavation and construction of the foundations
caps elevator pits and other below grade structures Special attention should be given to
the effects of dewatering on the adjacent properties and beyond
Based on our initial evaluation of the dewatering requirements of projects in the
Waikiki area we believe soil stabilization using jet grouting or similar methods may need
to be implemented for groundwater control of deeper excavations if planned In addition
the excavation support system for the below grade construction will have a significant
effect on the dewatering considerations The selected shoring system for the below grade
excavations will need to be carefully evaluated to reduce the potential impacts of the
shoring on the adjacent properties and travelways
Due to the shallow groundwater levels anticipated dewatering of planned
excavations such as the elevator pits and other below grade structures will be required
for the project construction Therefore we recommend that groundwater monitoring wells
be installed during the field exploration program for the performance of field permeability
tests collection of groundwater samples for National Pollutant Discharge Elimination
System NPDES permit application and groundwater level monitoring purposes

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
Our preliminary field exploration at the project site consisted of drilling and sampling
two test borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of about
132 5 and 133 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the
borings drilled for the project are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled
using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring
tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer or geologist who observed the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations
of the materials encountered in the test borings drilled for the project are presented on the
Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition samples also were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of coral formation and or hard materials encountered at the project
site were obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock and or hard material
left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of soil rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD also is a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses and or coral formation RQD is defined as the percentage of the
core run that is sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities
discounting drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties Theresults of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Two Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 and C 136 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification In addition a hydrometer test ASTM D 422 was conducted on one of the
two samples subjected to sieve analysis tests to further evaluate the finer grain materials
of the soils Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution of the soils are provided on
Plate B 2
Four Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
cores to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the coral formation encountered
at the project site Results of the unconfined compression tests are presented on
Plate B 3
