recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The proposed Ho`opili Subdivision Development project is located south of the 
Interstate Route H-1 Freeway and west of Fort Weaver Road in the Ewa area on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The proposed development will encompass approximately 
1,500 acres. The subdivision development site is gently sloping and is currently used for 
agricultural cultivation. 
The Phases 10A and 13 areas are located at the southeastern portion of the 
residential development. The majority of the eastern portion of the areas is situated 
adjacent to a near-vertical cliff on the order of about 30 feet high. We understand that it is 
desired to evaluate the stability of the existing steep slope and to formulate geotechnical 
engineering recommendations for the slope stabilization measures, if appropriate.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased, lava 
flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad 
plateau, now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the southeastern flank 
of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering, followed by erosion of the Schofield Plateau, 
generated sediments which were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to the 
south, these sediments have accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments 
and coral/algal reef formations, forming a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of the 
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to 
over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu. 
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When the 
glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. The higher sea level stands caused the 
formation of deltas and fans of accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old 
bays, accumulated reef deposits at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited 
lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea 
stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial 
exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal 
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials.  
The project is situated on a broad alluvial apron that flanks the southern margin of 
the Schofield Plateau. Agricultural development within the last 100 years has brought the 
Kapolei/Ewa area to its present form.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field observations, the subsurface conditions encountered in our field 
exploration, and our engineering analyses, it is our opinion that the additional loads from 
the planned residential development in Phases 10A and 13 may pose a slope stability 
hazard to the existing near-vertical slope.  In addition, it should be noted that the integrity 
of the existing near-vertical slope could deteriorate with the passage of time due to natural 
weathering processes, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and erosion conditions.  To improve 
the long-term stability of the existing near-vertical slope, we recommend constructing a 
new fill slope in front of the existing near-vertical slope.  The new fill slope may be 
designed with a gradient of either one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) or 2H:1V using 
general or screened fill materials that are reinforced with geotextile fabric. Detailed 
discussions and recommendations for the design of the slope stabilization measures are 
presented in the following subsections. 
New Fill Slope 
Based on our evaluation, we believe that the existing near-vertical slope may be 
stabilized with the following three options: (1) construction of a new 2H:1V fill slope using 
general fill materials reinforced with geotextile fabric in the upper 6 feet of the slope; 
(2) construction of a new 1H:1V fill slope using general fill materials reinforced with 
geotextile fabric in the entire slope; or (3) construction of a new 2H:1V fill slope using 
screened fill materials reinforced with geotextile fabric in the entire slope, as shown in the 
following  table.

Reinforced Soil Slope 
Due to the surcharge loading from the planned residential development, steepness 
of the new fill slope proposed in Option No. 2 and the relatively poor engineering 
properties of the screened fill materials used in Option No. 3, we recommend reinforcing 
the earth materials used in the construction of the new fill slope with adequate slope 
geogrids and/or geotextiles to strengthen the fill soils, thus improving the long-term stability 
of the new fill slope. 
Reinforced slope geogrids are generally polymer grid structures with high tensile 
strength.  It generally provides a cost-effective solution to slope stability problems and 
unfavorable soil/subgrade conditions, such as insufficient right-of-way, high surcharge 
loads, poor-quality fills, high seismic forces, steep slopes, or difficult landslide repairs.  
When reinforced slope geogrids are placed in soil, the grid geometry interlocks with 
adjacent soil, creating a soil-geogrid composite with greatly enhanced engineering 
properties.  Different grid configurations are available to provide optimum soil-grid 
interaction for a range of soil types and slope reinforcement applications.  Reinforced 
slope geotextiles work in a similar manner to reinforced slope geogrids. 
As the slope is constructed, horizontal layers of reinforced slope geogrids or 
geotextiles are placed in the compacted fill at predetermined levels.  The lengths of the 
reinforced slope geogrid or geotextile layers are designed to anchor potential failure zones 
into stable interior sections of the embankment or hillside.  As forces develop within a soil 
mass, the high-modulus reinforced slope geogrids or geotextiles are immediately pulled 
into tension.  The reinforced slope geogrids or geotextiles transfer this tensile force from 
the unstable soil back into the less-stressed portions of the slope, and stability is thus 
maintained. 
Based on our evaluation, we recommend using woven geotextile fabric, such as 
Mirafi HP570 or equivalent, for the reinforcement of the new fill slope planned for the 
project.  The length and vertical spacing of the geotextile fabric recommended for the three 
options are shown on Plates 3 through 5.  In addition, the geotextile fabric should extend 
up the slope face and wrap around for a minimum distance of 8 feet as shown on Plates 3 
through 5.

Fill Materials 
Fill materials required for the new fill slope construction will depend on the slope 
stabilization option selected for the project.  In general, we envision three types of fill 
materials may be required for the project construction, including general fill materials, 
screened fill materials, and select granular fill materials. 
General fill materials used in Option Nos. 1 and 2 should be free of vegetation, 
debris, deleterious materials and other unsuitable materials and should have a maximum 
particle size of 6 inches. In general, the on-site soils generated from excavations below the 
upper 12 inches of cultivated soils may be used as a source of general fill materials.

The screened fill materials used in Option No. 3 should consist of the upper 
12 inches cultivated soils after the clearing and grubbing operations. The materials 
should be screened of the remaining roots, debris, and deleterious materials and should 
have a maximum particle size of 3 inches. 
Select granular fill materials used in the upper 6 feet of the new fill slope should 
consist of non-expansive select granular materials, such as crushed coral or basalt. The 
material should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches 
in largest dimension. The material also should contain between 10 and 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve. In addition, the material should have a laboratory 
CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when 
tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
Screened fill and general fill materials used for the new fill slope should have an 
angle of internal friction of at least 28 and 32 degrees, respectively, when tested by the 
standard direct shear test (ASTM D3080).  The sample to be tested should be 
compacted to 90 percent relative compaction at a moisture content of at least 2 to 
4 percent above the optimum. 
Select granular fill materials used for the top 6 feet of the new fill slope should 
have an angle of internal friction of at least 35 degrees when tested by the standard 
direct shear test (ASTM D3080).  The sample to be tested should be compacted to 
90 percent relative compaction at a moisture content of above the optimum. 
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fill materials and screened fill materials should be placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent 
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction. The non-expansive select granular fill materials should be placed in level 
lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. Compaction should be accomplished by 
using sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction 
equipment. 
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the general and screened fill 
materials are critical elements of the earthwork, Geolabs should perform observations 
and soil density tests during the site grading operations to assist the contractor in 
obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on each 
fill lift. Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort should be

Erosion Control 
Excessive water should be diverted away from the top of slope, and slope planting 
and erosion control mattting should be provided as soon as the fill slope is completed to 
reduce potential erosion of the finished slope.  Construction of earth berms, interceptor 
ditches, and the use of geotextile fabrics over the fill slope face should be considered to 
reduce the potential for erosion, thus enhancing the long-term stability of the planned fill 
slope. 
We recommend using erosion control matting for erosion control of the new slope 
face.  The erosion control matting may consist of multi-layered geosynthetic netting.  The 
matting should allow grass or other natural ground cover to grow and take root through the 
matting.  In general, the slope face should be properly graded and compacted.  Materials 
such as rocks and vegetation that would interfere with the soil and the erosion control 
matting should be removed.  The erosion control matting should be placed in accordance 
with the manufacturer’s recommendations and supervision.  Supervision by the 
manufacturer should be provided at the start up and initial installation.  In general, the 
matting roll ends should be overlapped a minimum of 18 inches.  The adjacent edges of 
the matting should be overlapped a minimum of 3 inches.   
We also recommend installing anchor trenches prior to placing the erosion control 
matting.  The anchor trenches should be a minimum of 8 inches deep and 8 inches wide.  
The anchor trenches should be properly backfilled and compacted to 90 percent relative 
compaction at a moisture content above the optimum moisture.  The erosion control 
matting may be anchored at the overlaps by metal staples.  The distribution of the staples 
should be a minimum of two per square yard (follow manufacturer’s requirements).  Wood 
anchors, such as pegs or stakes of any kind, which extend above the ground surface, 
should not be allowed.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by the excavation and sampling of 18 test 
pits, designated as Test Pit (TP) Nos. 1 through 18, extending to depths ranging between 
about 5 and 8 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate test pit locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The test pits were excavated using a Hitachi 
Zaxis 450 Excavator. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the test pits by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the excavation 
operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further 
reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general 
accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for 
Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with 
ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock was 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are 
presented on the Logs of Test Pits, Plates A-1 through A-18. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soils encountered 
in the test pits. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the soil sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are presented 
on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) determinations were performed on selected 
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties. The test results 
are presented on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths. 
Nine Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits.  The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths.  Graphic presentation of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Ten Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on remolded samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. Direct shear test results 
are presented on Plates B-3 through B-12.
