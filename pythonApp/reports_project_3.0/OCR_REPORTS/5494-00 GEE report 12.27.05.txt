SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed House at Manele Lot 61 project on the Island of Lanai
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and
engineering analyses These recommendations are intended to assist in design of lot
grading construction residential structure foundations pavements utilities excavation
and fill placement only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The proposed project involves the construction of a luxury garden single family
residence on Manele Lot 61 on the Island of Lanai Hawaii Based on the available
conceptual site plan provided for the preparation of this report the residence will be a
three bedroom home with a detached garage and guest quarters It is anticipated the
home will enclose about 4 000 square feet of floor space The conceptual site plans
also indicate use of low retaining walls at various locations to provide grade separation
The project will involve site grading and infrastructure installation including a
driveway sidewalks and utilities Since the site is relatively level we anticipate site
grading work will generally consist of shallow fills less than 4 to 5 feet in thickness and
cuts of up to about 10 feet in height to provide level pads for the home garage and
guest quarters Field observations indicate the lot pad is about 8 to 12 feet above the
road level and may require a retaining wall or slope cutting for the steep slope adjacent
to the road

SECTION 2 0 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Lanai was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from a
single shield volcano The volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
in age As the volcanic activity ceased the central portion subsided forming a low basin
now known as the Palawai Basin Block faulting on the southern flank of the island
formed a broad graben leading from Palawai Basin to the south to Manele Bay The
project site is located within the Manele Graben
2 2 Site Description
The project site is located on Lot 61 in Manele on the Island of Lanai Hawaii
The site consists of a single parcel of land with an irregular polygonal shape It appears
the site was previously graded by the placement of rock fill materials At present the
site is an open area surrounded by low grasses and dryland vegetation such as keawe
trees A shallow ditch separates the site from the adjacent road
In general the site slopes down gently towards the south Based on the
topographic information of the conceptual site plan the existing ground surface
elevation ranges from approximately 70 to 92 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
2 3 Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling three borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 to depths of about 20 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
Our field exploration generally encountered a surface layer of rock fill material
ranging in thickness from about 1 to 4 feet overlying slightly weathered basalt rock
formation which was penetrated to the maximum depth explored of about 20 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The basalt rock formation was moderately to
severely fractured Occasional outcroppings of basalt formation through the rock fill
were observed at some locations at the site and in the vicinity

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris deleterious material and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site or stockpiled
in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated
materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should
be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft soils may be
used as fill provided it meets fill material requirements
Portions of the proposed residential structures will span from cut to fill which may
have the potential to induce adverse differential loading on the structures To
reduce the potential for distress to the structures from such differential loading we
recommend that cut portions of the building site be scarified down to a minimum of
18 inches below subgrade level and then recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction This scarification and recompaction may be eliminated for
individual structures resting entirely on cut or entirely on fill
Based on the site conditions encountered we recommend prior to the placement
of fill in areas designated to be filled the existing ground surface be scarified
moisture conditioned to optimum moisture content and then proof rolled with a
vibratory sheepsfoot compactor or similar heavy equipment for a minimum of
six passes to help verify whether adequate support for the fill has been achieved
The proof rolling operations should be performed in the presence of a Geolabs
representative Yielding areas loose areas or cavities disclosed during clearing
and proof rolling operations should be backfilled with fill materials compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The excavated soft soils may be used
as fill provided it meets fill material requirements Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Fills and Backfills
General fill material should consist of low expansive materials less than 3 inches in
largest dimension with a CBR swell of less than 1 percent The fill material should
also be free of vegetation and deleterious materials
Base course and select borrow subbase material should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate and should conform to the City and County of Honolulu Department of
Public Works Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction dated
September 1986
Structural fill should consist of non expansive granular material such as crushed
coral basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine
with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum
swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883
Geolabs should test imported fill materials for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to optinum moisture
placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness and compacted to at
least 90 percent relative compaction
Base course and structural fill materials should be moisture conditioned to optimum
moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum
dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test
procedures Optimum moisture is water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to maximum dry density

3 2 Building Foundations
Shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the proposed
residential structures provided they are embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grade
An allowable bearing pressure of 3 500 pounds per square foot psf may be
utilized for the design of residential building foundations bearing on properly compacted fill
on site rock fill or basalt formation This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may
be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces Footings should have a minimum width of 12 inches and should be
embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade For footings
constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground the footings should be embedded
deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back distance of 5 feet measured from
the outside edge of the footings to the slope face
We estimate settlements for footings bearing on properly compacted fill on site
rock fill or in situ basalt formation to be less than one 1 inch total Differential settlement
is estimated to be less than one half 7 inch
lf soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations
they should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The

over excavation should be backfilled with select granular material compacted to a

If in situ basalt rock formation protrudes into the foundation excavation the rock
formation should be removed to foundation subgrade level to reduce the potential of

Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded

Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure

resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 psf This assumes the soil around the footings is relatively undisturbed
or well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in
the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected For footings bearing on properly
compacted fill or on site stiff silty clay a coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used

3 4 Retaining Structures
Retaining walls may be required to provide grade separation in the proposed
residential development In general the following guidelines may be used for
preliminary design of retaining structures
Wall Foundations
In general we believe retaining wall foundations may be designed in accordance
with the Foundations section presented in this report The foundations should
have a minimum width of 18 inches and should be embedded a minimum of 18
inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade on relatively flat areas For sloping
ground conditions the footings should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 8 foot

face of the slope The horizontal setback distance may be reduced to a minimum of
3 feet if the footings are embedded at least 12 inches into in situ basalt rock
formation Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be
constructed in stepped footings

Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
placed immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the
retaining structure discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an
alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may
be used in lieu of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should
also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of granular fill or
excavated on site soils less than 3 inches in maximum dimension Unless covered
by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively
impervious materials to reduce the potential for water infiltration behind the
retaining structures

3 6 Utilities
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used
under the pipes The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging
the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular
material be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 1 foot above the pipes to
the finish subgrade should consist of the approved on site rock fill less than 3 inches in
size or select granular material The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to
at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence The upper
2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to not
less than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical compaction equipment should be
used to compact the materials at the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding
should not be used to compact the backfill material

landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Gravel
strips adjacent to slabs and foundations should be avoided Planters next to foundations
should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into
the subsoils

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the drilling and sampling
three borings to depths of approximately 20 5 feet below the existing ground surface
The borings were drilled with a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger and diamond
coring equipment at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the field exploration operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System Logs of the materials encountered are
presented on Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTMs D 3550 and D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 to 24 inch drive are shown on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
