Dias report presents the results of oun soil investigation
at the above referenced project The purpose of this study
was to investigate possible causes of the site damage and
to recommend possible remedial measures
The soil investigation consisted of a review of the grading
plans for the lift station site and foundation plans for the
lift station structure visual observations at the site
surveyed cross sections test borings around the site and
laboratory testing of selected subsurface soil samples

Visual observations at the site indicated the following
areas of distress

4 Some erosion of the surface soils on the slopes was noted

CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration indicates that expansive adobe clay
soil was used to fill the lift station area and natural
adobe clay soil was also encountered below the fill
These adobe clay soils typically have high shrink swell
potential tremendous slope creep tendency and low internal
friction angles which made them stable only on relatively
flat slopes
The soil samples of the fill and natural Clay recovered
in the borings were generally of stiff consistency How
ever natural density determinations on undisturbed samples
yielded relatively low dry densities when compared to the
maximum density determination performed on a surface sample
of the on site soil Part of the discrepancy is due to the
larger percentage of sand and gravel in the bulk surface
sample recovered for laboratory testing Based on experience
with similar adobe clay soil it appears that the fill
material tested has an average percentage of compaction of
about 75 to 85 This lower compaction probably did not
significantly increase the stability problems at the site

Based on the field exploration and laboratory test results
it is our opinion that most of the damages noted at the
Site are a result of the adobe clay fill material used
to build up the site and the relatively steep slopes
14H 1V to 3H 1V utilized for these soils
It appears that the adobe clay soils are unstable at
these slope ratios and are experiencing surface downhill
creep and deeper seated sliding failure These slope
movements start with the shrinking and cracking of the
surface adobe soil which allows surface water to enter
the subsoils and decrease the soil strength This triggers
surface soil creep near the top of slope and deeper seated
Sliding which in turn increases the soil cracking and allows
further saturation of the underlying soils increasing the
instability It is our opinion that the pavement cracks
and transformer pad and chain link fence movements are a
result of slope movements These soil movements may
continue until eventually a stable slope close to the
angle of internal friction of the soil is achieved
Laboratory direct shear tests performed on the clay soils
indicated friction angles of about 18to 21 degrees which
correspond fairly well with the observed failure planes
at the site

Based on our investigation the following alternatives for
repair of the lift station site are presented and discussed
Alternate 1 No Remedial Work
If no remedial work is performed at the site
ground movements will probably continue The
ground and structures near the top of slopes
particularly the chain link fence and transformer
pad will be pulled farther downhill Additional
cracking in the pavement should be expected
The amount and time of soil movement are dependent
on weather conditions and therefore it is very
difficult to predict However based on the
laboratory tests it is probable that much of
the soil movements may continue until a stable
slope of about 18 to 20 degrees is reached

Alternate 2 Construct Buttress Fill
A buttress fill could be constructed to resist
the sliding movement of the soils See Plate
4 Various configurations for the buttress
fill have been studied and the one presented
on Plate 4 appears to be adequate and cost efficient
Granular material such as crusher waste or
coral compacted to a minimum of 95 of its
maximum density determined by ASTM D 1557
should be used to construct the buttress
The buttress fill should be keyed and benched
into the existing slope A 4 inch perforated
subdrain should be placed near the bottom of
the buttress fill to drain seepage water
The subdrain should be daylighted to drain
into the existing drainage ditch along the
western side of the site
