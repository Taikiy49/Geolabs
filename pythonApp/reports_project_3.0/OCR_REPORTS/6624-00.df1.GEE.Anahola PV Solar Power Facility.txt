the driven steel piles should have a minimum center-to center spacing of 8.0 times the

1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is presently about 54 acres of fallow land and pasture that was 
formerly used for the cultivation of sugar cane.  The site gently slopes down towards the 
east. 
It is proposed to construct a photovoltaic generating facility on the site.  The 
generating facility will consist of arrays of solar collectors supported on posts.  Gravel 
access roads will be provided between banks of arrays and through main corridors 
throughout the site.  It is anticipated that grading for the new power generation facility 
will consist of shallow cuts and fills to construct pads for various equipment that will be 
located on-site, and for the gravel access roads.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from a 
single shield volcano.  Following the extrusion of shield building lavas, the main Kauai 
dome underwent a long cycle of erosion and weathering deeply dissecting the island. 
After this erosional period, there was a renewal of volcanic activity with the Koloa 
Volcanic Series covering large portions of the island, including the Lihue Basin, a large 
caldera-like structure on the southeastern portion of the island, extending from Lihue to 
north of Anahola.  In many areas, the Koloa lavas have been subjected to deep 
chemical weathering, forming a mantle of residual soil and saprolite which grade with 
depth to weathered basalt rock formation. 
The project site is situated on the northeast portion of the Lihue Basin.  The 
project site is underlain by residual clayey silt soils derived from the Koloa Volcanic 
Series. 
2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is situated in the Anahola area immediately to the west of Kuhio 
Highway on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii, as shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
The project site is presently about 54 acres of fallow land and pasture that was formerly 
used for the cultivation of sugar cane.  The site gently slopes down towards the east. 
The site is roughly rectangular in plan view.  The site is bounded to the north, 
south and west by vacant land that was formerly used for the cultivation of sugar cane; 
and, to the east by Kuhio Highway. 
The terrain within the site generally slopes down gently to the east towards the 
highway.  Based on the topographic map provided by Esaki Surveying and Mapping, 
the ground surface elevations at the site range from about +256 feet Mean Sea Level 
(MSL) at the northwestern corner of the site to about +178 feet MSL near the 
southeastern corner.  The site is covered with light brush and grass.

Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling 25 borings,

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered competent residual soils underlying 
most of the site.  Based on our laboratory tests, these soils exhibit a low to moderate 
expansion potential.  Our exploration indicated that there is a small potential for 
encountering cobbles or boulders in the subsurface soils.  These are remnants, or 
corestones, from the in-situ chemical weathering of the original lava flows.  These 
corestones may present refusal conditions to driven H-beam or pipe foundation 
supports. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, we recommend using an 
ultimate skin friction of 1.0 kip per square foot (ksf) for the stiff to hard clayey silt 
encountered at the site to determine the ultimate compressive load capacity. 
An ultimate uplift skin friction of 0.65 ksf for the stiff to hard clay and silt may be 
used to achieve the uplift load capacity for transient loading.  The uplift load capacities 
should not be increased for transient load applications.  The lateral resistance due to 
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds 
per square foot per foot of depth (pcf).  For flexible shafts or piles, a coefficient of lateral 
subgrade reaction of 200 pounds per square inch per inch (pci) of deflection may be used.  
In general, the driven steel piles should have a minimum center-to center spacing of 
8.0 times the shaft/pile diameter to avoid reduction in the lateral load capacity of the 
shaft/pile. 
Minor cuts and fills are anticipated for the site grading.  We believe that cuts and 
fills will range from about 2 to 4 feet to achieve the finished grades for the proposed 
project.  In general, cut slopes and permanent fill slopes constructed of the on-site 
native and imported fill soils may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal 
to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may 
be used for design of foundations bearing on the compacted base course placed over 
re-compacted on-site materials.  This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may

3.1 
Foundations 
3.1.1 Solar Panel Foundations 
It is our understanding that the vertical loads for solar panel systems are minimal 
and the primary concerns are uplift and overturning due to wind loads on the 
panels.  Based on similar projects, we envision that vertical loads from the 
proposed solar panels will be relatively light with wind uplift anticipated on the order 
of about 4.5 kips per post.  A lateral load due to wind is also anticipated and is 
estimated to be on the order of about 1 kip acting at about 5 feet above ground. 
In general, we envision that the solar panel arrays would be supported by H-beam 
or pipe posts driven about 10 feet below the existing ground surface.   
Our exploration indicated that there is a small potential for encountering cobbles or 
boulders in the subsurface soils.  These are remnants, or corestones, from the in-
situ chemical weathering of the original lava flows.  These corestones may present 
refusal conditions to driven H-beams or pipes. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, we recommend using an 
ultimate skin friction of 1.0 ksf for the stiff to hard clayey silt encountered at the 
site to determine the ultimate compressive load capacity.  A factor of safety of 2.0 
should be used to determine the allowable compressive load capacity of the 
driven steel piles.  The allowable compressive load capacity is for supporting 
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, 
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.  
Uplift loads imposed on the driven steel piles may be resisted by a combination of 
the dead weight of the pile and shear along the shaft or pile surface and the

general, the driven steel piles should have a minimum center-to center spacing of 
8.0 times the shaft/pile diameter to avoid reduction in the lateral load capacity of the

3.1.1.a Construction Considerations 
In general, corrosion protection is one of the concerns for the foundation 
system.  Hawaii’s volcanic soils tend to be somewhat aggressive to 
uncoated steel.  Therefore, it is recommended to galvanize the posts. 
A specialty subcontractor experienced in the installation of solar posts 
should install the foundation posts.  Due to the specialized nature of the 
foundation construction, observation of the foundation installation should 
be designated as a “Special Inspection” item.  Therefore, observation of 
the installation operations by Geolabs (Special Inspector) is necessary to 
confirm our design assumptions and should be designated as a “Special 
Inspection” item in accordance with Section 1704 of the International 
Building Code (2006).

3.1.2 Shallow Spread and Continuous Footings 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we envision 
that shallow footings, such as continuous strip foundations and spread footings, 
may be used to support new structures and pads planned for the project site. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for design of 
foundations bearing on the compacted base course placed over re-compacted 
on-site materials.  This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be 
increased by 50 percent for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or 
seismic forces. 
In general, the foundations should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below 
the lowest adjacent finished grade.  Footings constructed near tops of slopes or 
on sloping ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum 
horizontal setback distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the 
footings to the face of the slope. 
In order to reduce the potential for appreciable differential settlement, the new 
structures should be situated entirely over cut or entirely over fill.  They should 
not straddle across cut and fill transitions.  If foundations are design and 
constructed in accordance with our recommendations, we estimate that the total 
settlement of foundations to be less than 1 inch. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed 
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth 
pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system.  A 
coefficient of friction of 0.40 may be used for footings bearing on the base 
course.  Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an 
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pcf.  This assumes that the soils around the 
footings are well compacted.  Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the 
passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected.

3.2.1 Site Preparation 
In general, the areas within the contract grading limits should be cleared and 
grubbed thoroughly at the on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious 
material, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly 
off-site or disposed in a designated area to reduce the potential for contaminating 
the excavated materials. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during the clearing and grubbing should be 
over-excavated to expose firm natural material, and the resulting excavation should 
be backfilled with well-compacted engineered fill. In general, the excavated soft and 
wet soils may not be re-used as a source of fill and backfill materials. 
After clearing and grubbing, the existing ground surface in areas designated to 
receive fill or to remain at grade should be scarified to a depth of 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum

3.2.2 Fill and Backfill Materials 
In general, the excavated on-site materials may be used as a source of general fill 
materials, provided that they are free of deleterious materials.  
Imported fill materials required for the project construction should consist of 
non-expansive select granular material, such as crushed coral and basalt. The 
material should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR 
value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when 
tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883. 
3.2.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fill materials (on-site soils and imported materials) should be placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. Compaction should be

3.2.4 Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, cut slopes and permanent fill slopes constructed of the on-site soils may 
be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills placed on slopes 
steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide 
stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations should start at the 
lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance 
with the above fill placement recommendations. 
When the existing ground slope is steeper than 5H:1V in an area designated to 
receive fill, keys and benches should be provided to bond the fill to the existing 
slope.  These benches should be cut horizontally into the existing ground with a 
minimum width of about 5 feet and a height not exceeding about 3 feet.  The bench 
shelf should, as much as practicable, be founded in firm undisturbed natural 
ground.  
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. In the event that over-cutting of a slope 
occurs, keying and benching requirements should be implemented instead of 
backfilling the slope to the design grade with sliver fills. Water should be diverted 
away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as 
possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes. 
3.2.5 Excavation 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by stiff to very 
hard clayey silts. We anticipate that the near-surface materials may be excavated 
with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as a backhoe excavator, or ripped 
with a large bulldozer.  However, based on our understanding of the subsurface 
conditions at the site, there is a potential for encountering remnant boulders in 
excavations at the site.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from in-house literature research and our field exploration. 
Variations of subsurface conditions between and beyond the field exploration points 
may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until 
construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to 
re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The boring locations indicated in this report were located in the field by taping 
from reference points shown on the topographic plan transmitted by Esaki Surveying 
and Mapping on February 12, 2011.  Elevations of the borings were estimated based on 
interpolation between the spot elevations and contour lines shown on the same 
topographic plan. The physical locations and elevations of the borings should be 
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our 
exploration.  However, it must be noted that groundwater levels may vary with seasonal 
rainfall, temperature, and other factors.  These data have been reviewed and 
interpretations made in the formulation of this report.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling 25 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 201 through 225, to depths of about 
15 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with auger equipment. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling and excavation operations on 
a near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified 
Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-25.  
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in 
general accordance with ASTM  D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving 
a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) 
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification 
and evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings 
at the appropriate sample depths. 
Six Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits.  The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.  Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Three one-inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils under 
different surcharge pressures.  The test results are summarized on Plate B-2. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D 3080) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the shear strength parameters. The test results are presented on Plate B-3. 
Three Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D 2166) were performed on 
selected in-situ cohesive soil samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength 
of the soils. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths and graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B-4 
through B-6. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D 1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plate B-7 and B-8. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D 1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The samples were remolded to near the optimum moisture content.  Test 
results are presented on Plates B-9 and B-10.
