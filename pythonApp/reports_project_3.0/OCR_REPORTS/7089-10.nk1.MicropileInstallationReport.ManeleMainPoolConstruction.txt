Dear Mr. Allan: 
As requested, Geolabs, Inc. provided geotechnical engineering observation and 
consultation services during the micropile foundation installation for the above project. 
The following letter report summarizes our geotechnical engineering observations 
during installation of the micropile foundations for the Manele Main Pool project located 
at the Four Seasons Manele Bay Resort on the Island of Lanai, Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Geolabs conducted a geotechnical engineering exploration for the project, and 
our findings and recommendations are presented in the report entitled “Geotechnical 
Engineering Evaluation, Manele Main Pool, Island of Lanai, Hawaii”, dated 
January 27, 2015. The purpose of our geotechnical engineering observation during 
construction was to provide engineering consultation during construction of the 
micropile foundation and to provide “Special Inspection” services by observing the 
micropile foundation installation at the project for general conformance with the project 
documentations. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located at the existing Four Seasons Manele Bay Resort and 
involved reconfiguration of the hotel main pool facility including re-grading and re-shaping 
the landscaped areas. Construction of the main pool facility required cuts of approximately 
12 feet within 5 feet lateral distance of the existing Dolphin and Plumeria Wings. To 
support the existing buildings, micropiles were required to underpin five column footings 
adjacent to the steep cut slope. 
The concrete retaining wall “D”, located on the south side of Dolphin Wing, was 
also designed with micropile foundation to provide additional resistance against 
over-turning and/or rotational failure. A total of sixteen retaining wall micropiles were 
required with an allowable tensile load capacity of 36 kips and a length of 20 feet below

MICROPILE INSTALLATION 
Seven underpinning micropiles, designated as Micropile Nos. B-8, B-7.5, A-6, 
C-9, K-1, K-2, and L-3 (named based on the project surveying gridlines), were installed 
for the project from July 8, 2015 through July 15, 2015. The approximate locations of 
the underpinning micropiles are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. Four underpinning 
micropiles were installed at Dolphin Wing and three at Plumeria Wing. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project vicinity, the 
underpinning micropiles were specified to extend to a minimum depth of about 20 feet 
below the bottom of the existing column footings in a 5.5-inch diameter borehole, for a 
design allowable compressive load capacity of 36 kips. To increase rigidity for 
underpinning bracket connection, the upper 2 to 3 feet of the micropiles was cased with 
steel pipes. 
Sixteen retaining wall micropiles, designated as Micropile Nos. MP-1 through 
MP-16, were installed for the project from July 28, 2015 through August 13, 2015. The 
approximate locations of the retaining wall micropiles are also shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the project vicinity, the 
retaining wall micropiles were initially specified to extend to a minimum depth of about 
20 feet below the bottom of the new retaining wall footing in a 5.5-inch diameter 
borehole, for a design allowable tensile load capacity of 36 kips. A proof testing 
micropile was selected after the initial eight wall micropiles (MP-1 through MP-8) were 
installed. Due to encountered favorable ground condition, the micropile length for the 
remaining eight micropiles (MP-9 through MP-16) was reduced to 17 feet below the 
bottom of the wall footing. 
In general, the micropile was drilled using a Berretta T24H track-mounted drill rig 
equipped with a down-the-hole hammer and advanced with 5.5-inch Outside Diameter 
(O.D.) temporary steel casing to the specified depth. Advancing steel casing to the 
bottom of the specified depth was required in order to facilitate the micropile drilling and 
ensure the quality of the grouting. Air pressure was utilized during drilling operation to 
create a bulb around the drilling bit for advancing temporary steel casing (5.5-inch O.D.) 
and to clean-out the drill cuttings. 
After the drilled holes were extended to the specified depth and cleaned with air 
pressure, a reinforcing bar consisting of Grade 75 all threaded #8 rebar for the 
underpinning micropile or Grade 150 all threaded #10 rebar for the retaining wall

micropile was placed in the cased hole with centralizers. Grout was batched on site 
utilizing a colloidal high-shear and high-speed ChemGrout mixer equipped with a grout 
pump. The grout mix consisted of one sack of 94-pound Portland Cement Type I and/or 
II, 85.7 pounds of sand and 5 gallons of water with a water/cement ratio of 0.45 and a 
design 28-day compressive strength of 4,000 psi. 
The grout was placed in the cased hole through a 1-inch PVC tremie pipe from 
the bottom of the cased hole. After sound grout appeared at the top of the cased hole, 
the temporary steel casing was removed one section (5-foot long) at a time. 
One section of the casing was left in the borehole for each of the underpinning 
micropile, while all steel casings were removed for the retaining wall micropiles. 
Details of the micropile installation are presented in Appendix A for the 
underpinning micropiles and Appendix B for the retaining wall micropiles. 
In order to evaluate the load carrying capacities of the installed micropiles, a 
pullout test was conducted on a selected micropile (No. MP-5) on August 7, 2015. In 
general, the micropile was loaded to a maximum test load of 72 kips (200 percent of the 
design load of 36 kips) in approximately 9-kip load (25% of design load of 36 kips) 
increments. The test micropile was held at the design load of 72 kips for 10 minutes to 
evaluate the creep effect. The results of testing are presented on Plate 3 - Micropile 
Load Test Data Sheet. The testing load versus micropile displacement is presented on 
Plate 4. 
The micropile MP-5 was loaded gradually to the maximum test load of 72 kips 
with about 0.194 inches of displacement. Subsequently, the micropile MP-5 was 
unloaded to zero load in about 14 kips decrement. 
Based on our review and engineering evaluation, the micropile MP-5 did not fail at 
the maximum test load of 72 kips. At the maximum test load of 72 kips, the creep 
movement between 1 and 10 minutes holding period was about 0.008 inches, less than 
0.04 inches typically allowed for the ground anchor system. 
CONCLUSIONS 
Based on our field observations and the proof test conducted, it is our 
professional opinion that the specified micropiles for the project have been installed to 
the required diameter and sufficient depth providing the design load capacity of 36 kips 
per micropile with a factor of safety of 2.0.
