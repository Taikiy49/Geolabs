Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and
engineering analyses performed in support of the design and construction of the new
septic system to be installed in conjunction with the Renovate Buildings
and 188 project at Pohakuloa Training Area in the Pohakuloa area of the Island of
Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations based
on our field exploration field percolation testing laboratory testing and engineering
analyses The recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of trench
excavation backfilling and leachfield design only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2
Project Considerations
The septic system project involves the installation of approximately 1 500 lineal
feet of new sewer line along Pohakuloa Street Mauna Kea Loop to the Multi Purpose
Field Parade Field and to the southeast of Buildings 121 and 133 Septic tanks will be
installed at the Parade Field and Building 121 133 locations for the treatment of
wastewater and leachfields will be used for the disposal of effluent from the septic
tanks Based on the information provided the new sewer line piping will be installed by
the cut and cover method
This project was conducted as a modification to the work performed under our
previous task order and report titled Percolation Testing Renovate Buildings 139 140
188 Pohakuloa Training Area Pohakuloa Island of Hawaii dated August 5 2014
1 3

Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from five shield
volcanoes Kohala Mauna Kea and Kilauea which have
coalesced to form one large island Of these five volcanoes Mauna Loa and Kilauea
are considered to be active and Hualalai is believed to be in a dormant stage with its
last activity in about 1803
Pohakuloa Training Area PTA is situated in the north central portion of the
Humu ula Saddle a broad plateau between Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa Based on an
available geologic map USGS 2007 the PTA area is underlain by rocks of the
Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea The map indicates that these Hamakua rocks
may be mantled by recent alluvium that would be outwash from the slopes of Mauna
Kea to the north Since these outwash materials are generally cindery in nature it is
difficult to differentiate between the alluvial soils and airlaid cinder from volcanic activity
from eruptions from adjacent cinder cones from Mauna Loa
In terms of engineering properties there is essentially no difference between the
cindery alluvium and airlaid cinder deposits Both behave as granular cohesionless
soils and are generally well drained

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
In general our field exploration indicated that the project site is underlain by
medium dense volcanic cinder materials Basaltic rock formation was encountered in
the deeper portion of some borings While the borings along the proposed sewer line
alignment did not encounter the rock formation there is a potential that isolated ledges
or high points of the rock may extend into the excavation limits
It is anticipated that the new sewer line would have invert depths on the order of
about 5 to 8 feet below the existing ground surface The borings generally indicate
medium dense sandy cinder soils within this zone Based on the relatively competent
subsurface soil conditions encountered we believe that conventional excavation
methods may be used for installation of the sewer line and for the proposed leachfields
However based on experience in the area and with similar deposits there is a potential
that hard rock formation or boulders and cobbles may be encountered within the cinder
horizon
Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of sewer line installation
and other geotechnical aspects are presented in the following sections
3 1
Trench Excavation
We anticipate that the new sewer inverts would be on the order of about 5 to
8 feet below the existing ground surface Installation of the proposed sewer system will
involve excavation into medium dense sandy cinder deposits It is anticipated that
these cinder deposits may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment
However based on experience in the area and with similar deposits there is a potential
that hard rock formation or boulders and cobbles may be encountered within the cinder
horizon
The above discussion regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials is
based solely on field data obtained from the borings drilled at the project site and
previous experience in the project vicinity

3 2
Trench Excavation Support
We anticipate that excavation depths of up to about 10 feet below the ground
surface will be required for the installation of the new sewer system Deeper
excavations may be required where the sewer line will traverse existing underground
utilities Where excavations greater than 4 feet in depth are planned temporary shoring
or sloping and benching should be used Based on the existing site conditions
i e active roadways in a military quarters and administrative area we believe that
temporary cut slopes for open cut excavations are not practical On this basis trench
walls would have to be cut vertically requiring the use of shoring during construction
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable
safety requirements The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and
safety of the shoring installation The contractor s representative i e competent
person should be on site at all times during excavation and construction work for the
opportunity to promptly observe changing or unforeseen conditions such as high
groundwater inappropriate construction sequence or techniques etc which may affect
the shoring stability
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential
for excessive ground movement
It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction The properly braced shoring is
essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent
ground into the excavation which may result in potential settlement or distress to
adjacent structures or other improvements
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation
Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to accurately estimate the
magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship such

Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important design
consideration For this project a minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular bedding
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be provided
below the pipe
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of
one fourth 1 4 the outside pipe diameter and should extend half way up the sides of the
pipe Prior to the placement of bedding material a Geolabs representative should
observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm materials are exposed at the
bottom of the trench or whether the bedding should be extended deeper
3 4
Trench Backfill
The first zone of backfill extending from the bedding material to at least 12 inches
above the top of the pipes should consist of select free draining granular material
ASTM C33 No 67 Gradation to reduce the compaction effort required and resulting
stresses on the pipe In addition it is critical to use this free draining material to reduce
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in
backfill settlement and pipe damage
Based on our field exploration the existing subsurface soils at the site are of
suitable quality for use as trench backfill In pavement areas the trench backfill from a
level 12 inches above the pipes to 18 inches below pavement subgrade may consist of
excavated on site material processed to less than 3 inches in size The cinder material

potential of continued crushing of the porous cinder under repeated wheel loading
Backfill within 18 inches of the pavement subgrade should consist of select
non expansive granular material such as crushed basalt or recycled asphalt The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches
in largest dimension The material should also contain less than 25 percent particles

with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use
As an alternative to facilitate the backfilling and reduce the time required for
applying compactive effort Controlled Low Strength Material CLSM may be used to
backfill the trenches The use of CLSM would also reduce the potential for future
settlement of the trench backfill under the repeated wheel loading from traffic on the
base roadways
3 5
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements
Backfills should be moisture conditioned to 2 percent above the optimum
moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted
to at least 90 percent relative compaction The upper 2 feet below the pavement grade
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers
vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during backfilling to
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper
moisture content Where compaction is less than required additional compactive effort

Settlements
Primary settlement of sewer lines is normally caused by the difference in the unit
weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill
material placed over the pipes The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the
underlying subsoils below the trench invert Based on our calculations settlement on
the order of less than 0 5 inch is anticipated for the project
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good
workmanship will be engaged during construction Additional settlement could occur if
improper trench support is used

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 which were drilled to depths ranging
from approximately 11 5 to 16 0 feet below the existing ground surface for the planned
sewer line and six borings designated as Percolation Test Nos 1 through 5 which
were drilled to depths ranging from approximately 10 9 to 15 5 feet below the existing
ground surface for testing of the proposed leachfield areas The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig Mobile B 53 equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer geologist who monitored the drilling operations
on a continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1 Deviations
made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil
Classification Log Key Plate A 0 2 Graphic representations of the materials
encountered during drilling are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD split barrel sampler with a 140 pound
hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and
third 6 inches a total of 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Pocket penetrometer test results
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
