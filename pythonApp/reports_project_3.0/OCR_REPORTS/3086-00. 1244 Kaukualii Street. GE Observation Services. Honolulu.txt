INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed new hydraulic elevator shaft installation at 1244 Kaumuailii
Street in the Kalihi Kai Kapalama industrial area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu

This report summarizes the findings resulting from our field exploration and
laboratory testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived
from our analyses based on the data obtained through the field exploration and laboratory
testing These recommendations are intended for design input only and may not be
appropriate to form the basis for construction cost estimates nor for construction bidding
purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located in the Kalihi Kai Kapalama industrial area of Honolulu
on the island of Oahu Hawaii The proposed new hydraulic elevator will be constructed
near the southeast corner of the existing warehouse building at Kaumualii and Moowaa
Streets as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
On the basis of information provided to our office it is our understanding that the
new hydraulic elevator will be approximately eight 8 by ten feet 10 in plan dimension
Below the ground floor level the elevator will have a two 2 to three foot 3 deep
mechanical equipment pit of the same plan dimension We also understand that the shaft
pit of the new hydraulic elevator will be ten inches 10 in inside diameter and will extend
to a depth of about 23 feet below the existing ground surface
Further details regarding the proposed new elevator construction were not available
at the time this report was prepared

The proposed project site is located at the northeast corner of the intersection of
Kaumualii and Moowaa Streets in the Kalihi Kai Kapalama industrial area of Honolulu on

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results it is our opinion that
development of the proposed new elevator is feasible from a geotechnical point of view
provided that our recommendations are followed The elevator structure may be
supported by spread footings bearing on the very stiff clayey silt fill soils encountered
near the surface The bottom of all footing excavations should be observed by a
representative of Geolabs Hawaii prior to the placement of reinforcing steel to evaluate
whether an adequate bearing surface has been exposed and if loose soils have been
removed prior to concrete placement We estimate that for footings designed and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3086 00 Page 6
constructed in accordance with the above criteria the potential total settlements for
foundations bearing on the existing stiff to very stiff fill soils should be less than one inch
1 with differential settlements less than inch
The sandy coral gravel encountered at about 20 feet below the existing ground
surface was loose and saturated and may cave in during excavation of the elevator shaft
pit unless the drill hole is cased Therefore special attention should be given to the
construction method used for the proposed hydraulic elevator shaft pit
Due to the high groundwater level encountered in our boring it is anticipated that
the elevator shaft pit will be subjected to buoyant uplift forces which should be considered
in the design
From our discussion with elevator contractors we understand that for elevators
with lift heights less than about 151 feet a deep shaft pit may not be needed Elevator
manufacturers now offer models that require only a mechanical pit on the order of five
feet 5 deep The owner may want to consider this alternative
Foundation Support
We anticipate that the proposed elevator building will be a two story metal frame
building with masonry block walls We recommend that the building be supported by
spread footing foundations An allowable bearing capacity of 2 000 pounds per square
foot p s f may be utilized for the design of foundations bearing on the near surface
clayey silt fill soils This value may be increased by one third for temporary wind and or
seismic loads The footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the
mechanical pit floor slab solated spread footings should have a minimum width of 18
inches and continuous footings should have a minimum width of 12 inches
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3086 00 Page 7
Footings located adjacent to underground utility trenches or easements should be
extended to a depth below the projection of an imaginary one horizontal to one vertical
1H 1V plane which extends up from the bottom edge of the utility trench or from the
invert of the pipeline within the utility easement
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against vertical or near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional
resistance between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil A passive pressure
developed by an equivalent fluid weighing 250 pounds per cubic foot p c f may be
used Passive resistance should be neglected in the upper foot unless confined by a slab
or paved surface A coefficient of friction of 0 30 may be used between the footings and
the supporting subgrade
The footing excavations should expose firm moist soils If soft clay pockets are
encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations the soft soils should be over
excavated to expose firm stiff soils The over excavation should be backfilled with
granular material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of the maximum dry density as
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Footing excavations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii
prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to evaluate whether an adequate
bearing surface has been exposed and if loose soils have been removed prior to concrete
placement
Slab on Grade
it is anticipated that the interior floor for the mechanical pit will consist of concrete
slab on grade The loads imposed by the planned mechanical equipment are unknown
at the time this report was written However we recommend that the floor slab be well

reinforced and thickened in the appropriate locations for the support of any planned
machinery The floor slab and its cushion may be supported by the existing moderately
expansive fill soils after preparation as follows The slab subgrade should be scarified
to a depth of eight inches 8 moisture conditioned to between two 2 and five percent
5 above the optimum moisture content and compacted to 90 percent of its maximum
dry density as determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 The subgrade soils
should be kept moist and should not be allowed to dry prior to placement of cushion fill
and slab concrete If shrinkage cracks appear in the subgrade the subgrade should be
scarified moisture conditioned again as outlined above and recompacted to 90 percent
relative compaction
A minimum six inch 6 thick layer of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should be placed over the prepared subgrade soils to serve as a slab cushion
and capillary break A moisture barrier overlying the cushion fill snould be incorporated
below the concrete slab to reduce the potential of infiltration of moisture One inch 1
of moist fine sand may be placed over the moisture barrier membrane to aid in curing of
the concrete slab
Elevator Shaft Pit Installation
It is our understanding that the shaft pit for the proposed new elevator will be
approximately 23 feet deep and the interior diameter of the shaft pit will be approximately
ten inches 10 Because of the high water table and the presence of loose sandy gravel
materials below the water table we anticipate that the shaft pit would be constructed by
augering in the manner of a cased drilled pier
In the anticipated procedure used by local elevator contractor no dewatering is
required The shaft pit is excavated in the manner of a cased drilled pier Steel casing
is placed down the drilled shaft behind the auger as it advances toward the bottom of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3086 00 Page 9
hole After withdrawal of the auger with the casing fully installed to the design elevation
of the bottom of the hole the interior form is placed in the hole The form would generally
be a steel pipe with a plate welded to the bottom forming a closed chamber The
chamber is filled with water to reduce the potential for flotation and is lowered into the
hole to the required depth The reinforcing steel if necessary is placed in the resulting
annular space and then concrete is placed via a closed system using either a concrete
pump or tremie pipe in order to displace the water
Whatever method is used for construction of the proposed new hydraulic elevator
shaft pit we recommend that a contractor be utilized who is skilled and experienced in
this specialty

Buoyant Forces
Due to the high groundwater level encountered in our boring it is anticipated that
the elevator shaft pit will be subjected to buoyant uplift forces which should be considered
in the design In general buoyant uplift forces may be resisted by a combination of
frictional resistance between the surface area of the structure and surrounding soils and
by the dead weight of the structure We recommend using a frictional resistance of 200
p s f for resistance against uplift forces
Existing Structure Foundations
The proposed new elevator will be closely surrounded by the existing structures
The elevator mechanical pit excavation is planned to be about two 2 to three feet 3
deep The proposed construction may impose an influence on the foundations of the
surrounding existing structures
Therefore we recommend that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained by the
contractor to evaluate this condition and if necessary underpinning of the existing
foundations should be considered
Existing Utility Lines
Underground utilities may exist at or in the vicinity of the proposed new elevator
location Since shaft drilling and excavation are required the project construction may
create potential hazards to these utility lines which if damaged could affect the normal
operation of the surrounding structures
Therefore we recommend that all existing utility lines within the project site be
located accurately prior to construction of the new elevator by the contractor who should
be given the sole responsibility to devise and implement an effective program to protect
the utility lines during the proposed construction
