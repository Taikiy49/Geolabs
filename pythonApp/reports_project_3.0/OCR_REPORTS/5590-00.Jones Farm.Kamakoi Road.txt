SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed dressage arena at the Jones Farm on Kamakoi Road
Lot 3 located in Kaneohe on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
project site is located to the east of Kamakoi Road The general location and vicinity of
the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended to assist in the design and construction of
the site grading only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this report
Since site grading was a major project consideration at the time this report was
prepared the discussions and recommendations presented herein are intended primarily
to address the grading work Discussions and recommendations pertaining to foundations
retaining walls and infrastructure construction are general in nature
1 2 Project Considerations
It is proposed to construct a dressage arena at the site which will be a relatively
level pad area about 230 by 98 5 feet in plan dimension Site grading to create the pad
area will involve cuts of up to about 10 feet deep and fills of up to about 10 feet thick
The pad surface will be covered by a compacted gravel base and topped with a sand
and crumbled rubber surface treatment
In addition to the arena area development it is proposed to construct several
agricultural outbuildings such as a tractor shed and stable spaces in a relatively level
area close to Kamakoi Road

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age
and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island
The project site is situated within the rift zone and caldera complex of
Koolau Volcano Volcano During the active phase of the Koolau Volcano hydrothermal
action altered the rocks within the rift and caldera complex to easily eroded materials As
erosion on the windward side of the Koolau dome breached the complex a broad
amphitheater headed valley formed and coalesced to form the Koolau Pali Deep deposits
of interbedded alluvium and colluvium accumulated at the base of the Pali and along the
valley walls The site is located on a broad terrace of the alluvial and colluvial deposits
2 2 Existing Site Conditions
The project site is located to the east of Kamakoi Road in Kaneohe on the Island
of Oahu Hawaii At present the site is used as pasture and the majority of the site is
covered with low grass There is an existing residence at the southern end of the site
In general the site is a broad swale sloping down towards the north from the
terrace at an average of about 30 to 35 percent The ground elevations range from
90 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the northern end of the property to about 200 feet
MSL at the existing residence The proposed arena site is a broad bench in the middle
portion of the slope The proposed outbuildings will be constructed in a relatively level
area near the Kamakoi Road gate to the pasture

3 1 Site Grading
Based on the current project plans the site grading work will generally consist of
cuts of up to about 10 feet deep and the placement of fills of up to 10 feet in thickness
over the majority of the site Items of grading which are addressed in the following
subsections include 1 Site Preparation 2 Keying and Benching 3 Fills and
Backfills 4 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 5 Excavation and 6 Cut
and Fill Slopes
The proposed outbuildings will be constructed in a relatively level area adjacent
to the Kamakoi Road gate into the pasture It is anticipated that site grading for these
buildings will be minimal
Site grading operations should be observed by a Geolabs representative It is
important that a Geolabs representative observe the site grading operations to evaluate
whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation process and to
confirm whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our
field exploration
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris deleterious material and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site or
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials In general the upper 8 to 12 inches of soil is contaminated
with organic matter and should be removed during the clearing and grubbing work
for disposal at an off site location or used as topsoil in non structural areas of the
site
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should
be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft soils may be
used as fill provided it meets the fill material requirements

Keying and Benching
Although the average slope on the site is on the order of about three horizontal to
one vertical 3H 1V there are local areas of steeper slopes In addition the
organically contaminated soils near the surface of the site will not provide stable
support for the fills planned Therefore extensive keying and benching is
recommended for the earthwork to be performed These keys and benches should
be firmly seated in the competent soils underlying the site at depths of about 5 to
7 feet below the existing ground surface
At the toe of the fill a keyway should be cut down to the very stiff to hard clayey silt
soil This key should be a minimum of 5 feet in depth at the toe of the fill and should
be a minimum of 15 feet in width extending back under the toe of the fill The floor
of the key should be sloped back under the base of the fill at about 10H 1V to bond
the fill into the existing slope
As fill placement progresses up the existing slope benches should be cut into the
existing slope to bond the mass of the fill to the existing ground These benches
should be a minimum of 5 feet in height and approximately 10 feet in width The
bench shelf should as much as practicable be founded in the very stiff to hard
clayey silt soil

Fills and Backfills
Except for the organically contaminated upper soils the on site soils may be used
as fill and backfill material When used as fill the material should be free of
vegetation and other deleterious materials
Imported fill if required should consist of non expansive granular material such as
crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded
from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and
should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of less than 1percent when tested in accordance with
ASTM D 1883 Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs for conformance
with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use

dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557

3 2 Building Foundations
Based on the assumed grading concept and the generally stiff subsoil conditions
encountered we believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used to
support the proposed new building An allowable bearing pressure of up to
2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of shallow foundations
bearing on recompacted stiff on site soils or compacted fills needed to achieve the
design finished grades This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
For footings bearing on the stiff on site soils the bottom of the footing
excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to
provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing
steel or concrete If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the
footing excavations they should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials The over excavation may be backfilled with the on site soils compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the bottom of footing may be extended
down to the underlying competent materials For footings bearing on new compacted
fills the bottom of the footing excavations should also be recompacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete

In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the

3 3 Retaining Wall Foundations
Retaining walls may be necessary to provide grade separation in some portions of
the proposed development The retaining walls are anticipated to have level backfill
conditions If solid fence walls are to be attached to the tops of retaining walls the
retaining walls should be designed for the additional lateral wind loads that will be
transferred from the fence walls
Wall Foundations
In general retaining wall foundations should be designed in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Building Foundations section of this report
except for the following Wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches
and should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the existing grade For
footing bearing on stiff recompacted soils a coefficient of 0 4 may be used
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent
fluid pressure of 450 pcf For footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping
ground the footings should be embedded an additional amount to provide a
minimum horizontal set back distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of
the footings to the slope face Retaining walls in cut areas where the wall
foundation will be lower than adjacent structures should be set back so the heel of
the new wall is no closer than an imaginary 1H 1V plane extending upward from the
heel of the new wall to the toe of the existing foundation Wall footings parallel to
the direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings

3 4 Slabs On Grade
As previously indicated the project site is generally underlain by soils that are
subject to shrinkage when allowed to dry out Unless slabs on grade constructed above
these soils are properly designed there is a potential for future distress to lightly loaded
slabs on grade resulting from shrinking of the clayey soils due to changes in the
moisture content To reduce the potential for structural distress we recommend that the
subgrade soils be properly prepared prior to fill placement In addition a minimum of
6 inches of non expansive select granular fill material should be provided below the
building slab areas The non expansive select granular fill should also extend beyond
the perimeter of the building a minimum of 24 inches or extend to the edge of any
concrete sidewalk surrounding the building
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that
a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below the slabs The open graded gravel
cushion fill would serve as a capillary moisture break and would provide more uniform
support of the slabs To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through the
slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings or goods stored within the facility an
impervious moisture barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the
No 3B Fine gravel cushion layer Where floor coverings are desired only flexible floor
coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better
mask minor slab cracking It is also recommend that interior walls be designed to

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 to depths of about 6 5 to 11 5 feet
below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger
equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
