Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity

during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the eastern flank of Mauna Loa which has been built
up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and pyroclastic materials The site is
near the contact between the southeast flank of Mauna Kea and the northeast flank of
Mauna Loa The near surface lava flows are from Mauna Loa and overlie the older
Mauna Kea Shield The lava flows of the Mauna Kea Shield are believed to be Pliocene to
Pleistocene in age and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series The
near surface lava flows of the Mauna Loa Shield may be on the order of only 750 to
1 500 years old However the southeastern boundary of the site generally follows the
1881 lava flow of Mauna Loa
In the northeast corner of the site there are two small cinder cones Puu Hono and
a smaller unnamed cone A larger cone Halai is located just to the northeast of the site
across Ponohawai Street These cones along with Kupapau Hill and Puu laone to the
northwest of the site follow a string of cinder cones that delineates the northeast rift of
Mauna Loa
The Quadrangle topographic map shows a small pond on the site
located to the south of Springs is situated to the west of the site
The lava formation encountered at the site appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe
type flows originating from the northeast rift of lava is typically
characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged
accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders Pahoehoe is
characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe lava
Cavities are formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which
may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and pockets are
generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and
along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they
serve as the major water transmission features

Previous experience in the vicinity of the project site indicates that with the
exception of the 1881 flow a surface layer of volcanic ash locally referred to as Pahala
Ash generally mantles the basalt rock formation This surface layer of ash generally
ranges from about 3 to as much as 10 feet thick The ash is characterized as having very
high in situ moisture contents and is usually highly compressible The ash also frequently
exhibits thixotropic properties i e the material loses strength when disturbed by

Existing Site Conditions
The project site is located to the west of Komohana Street on the western side of
the city of Hilo in the District of South on the Island of Hawaii The project site is
approximately 171 5 acres of vacant land and consists of three parcels identified by
the east existing residential development and vacant land to the south Mohouli Street to
the west and existing residential development to the north At present the site is vacant
unimproved land and is covered with dense vegetation and mature trees
The site is traversed by Alenaio Stream and two other unnamed drainage courses
one minor drainage course near the southern boundary of the site and a larger one to the
north of Alenaio Stream All of these drainage courses including Alenaio are poorly
defined where they enter the site along Mohouli Street There is an open channel and
culvert where Alenaio exits the site at Komohana Street There is also a smaller channel
with a large pipe culvert exiting the site at Komohana Street near the intersection with
Ponohawai Street
At the time of our site reconnaissance there was a significant amount of flow
through the Ponohawai culvert This flow originated from a small stream in the central
portion of the site which led to a small pond or pool just to the west of Ponohawai Street
The area is heavily forested with dense vegetation ranging from grasses vines and
shrubs to trees over 100 feet in height In general most of the area can be described as a
tropical forest canopy with dense undergrowth
Based on information provided the existing ground surface elevations at the site
range from about 240 to 410 feet above Mean Sea Level MSL In general the
topography of the site is undulating with higher elevations in the southwest and sloping
down towards Hilo Bay to the east In the northeast corner of the site two cinder cones
Puu Hono and a smaller unnamed cone form local topographic highs
In general the surface soils in the southern portion of the site consisted of a thin
mantle of dark brown clayey silts volcanic ash with some organics and cobble sized
basalt fragments The soils in the northern portion consisted of a much thicker surface
layer of reddish brown silts volcanic ash

Anticipated Subsurface Conditions
Based on our site observations test pits and a review of available information from
the vicinity of the project site we anticipate that the project site may be underlain by hard
basalt rock formation under a surface layer of volcanic ash It appears that the ash soil is
significantly thicker in the northern portion of the site The basalt rock formation is believed
to consist of both a a and pahoehoe lava rock with frequent cavities and potential buried
lava tube features Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project site near surface
lava tubes and cavities should be anticipated in the basalt rock formation
Based on available information from the USDA Soil Conservation Service the soils
at the project site have been mapped as

Keaukaha Extremely Stony Muck 6 to 20 slopes well drained organic soils

Excavation of the basalt rock formation will likely require hard rock ripping using
heavy construction equipment Deep excavations may require controlled blasting
techniques if permitted
We anticipate that other buried cavities may be encountered during construction
An early program of subgrade cavity detection and collapse using heavy construction
equipment such as a D 9 or D 10 Bulldozer and a 20 ton roller should be implemented to
collapse the near surface potential cavities A program of foundation cavity probing and
grouting may be necessary to stabilize potentially deeper cavities located below building
footings and other heavy loaded foundation systems
In general we envision that basal groundwater levels likely will not be encountered
during construction of the proposed development We anticipate that regional
groundwater levels are generally near MSL

upslope area but a substantial flow was leaving the site by the Ponohawai culvert

springs developed during periods of heavy precipitation This was attributed to the
development of a local perched ground water table Both regional and localized
groundwater levels will fluctuate depending on tidal fluctuations seasonal precipitation
groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other factors

Site Preparation
As mentioned previously the project site generally is underlain by basalt lava flows
covered with volcanic ash soils at the ground surface The ash soils generally have poor
structural characteristics with respect to foundation and pavement support Lava tubes

and cavities may be present in the volcanic rock formation underlying the site Therefore
we believe that special attention should be given to the site preparation aspects of the
proposed construction
The near surface ash materials are relatively soft and will compress when
subjected to structural and vehicular loads The soils encountered in our test pit
exploration had very high in situ moisture contents and were close to the liquid limit
These ash soils have thixotropic properties i e they temporarily lose strength when
remolded or are subjected to vibrations such as seismic events Therefore to the extent
practicable site grading should be designed to remove the ash soils from structural or
pavement areas It should be noted that in the northern portion of the site the ash soils
may be as thick as nine feet or more
The rock formation exposed by the removal of the ash soils and fill areas of the
project site should be ripped to a minimum depth of 3 feet below the existing ground
surface to assist in detecting and collapsing cavities and or voids contained in the porous
near surface materials In addition site preparation consisting of ripping the existing
ground surface should extend at least 10 feet laterally beyond the cult fill transition line
Proof rolling of the subgrades also will be necessary to assist in detecting and possibly
collapsing lava tubes and cavities The proof rolling process may be conducted using a
combination of Caterpillar D 9 or D 10 bulldozers and large vibratory drum rollers
minimum 20 tons static weight In addition the proof rolling operations should be
observed by a representative from Geolabs during earthwork construction to evaluate the
potential for presence of lava tubes and or cavities near the existing ground surface
In general soft loose weak or yielding areas or cavities disclosed during the site
preparation operations should be over excavated to expose firm and or dense ground
The resulting excavation should be backfilled with compacted fill materials It should be
noted that backfilling of lava tubes may be extensive due to the nature of these formations
Therefore we recommend that contract documents for the mass grading include additive
and deductive unit prices for over excavating the lava tube features and backfilling with
compacted fill to account for variations in the over excavation and backfill quantities
Proposed grading plans for the development were not available at the time this
letter report was prepared However we envision that grading consisting mostly of fills of
up to about 15 feet in height may be required to achieve the design grades at the project
site We also anticipate that minor cuts of less than about 5 feet deep with cuts up to
about 10 feet deep in localized areas also may be required for the development In
general the excavated on site rock materials may be used as a source of fill materials
provided that they are processed to meet the gradation requirements of the various fill
types In general structural fills should consist of well graded granular materials less than
6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in
the compacted mass General fills should consist of well graded granular materials the

Ground Water Seepage
During our site reconnaissance it was noted that there was a substantial flow of
surface water existing the site through the Ponohawai culvert This surface flow traced
back to a small pond or pool to the west of Ponohawai Street and then traced further
towards the west or northwest along a small stream which appeared to be a permanent
surface feature This area generally corresponded to the Zone A inundation area shown
on the Flood Insurance Rate Map and the stream appeared to extend further into the site
towards Mohouli Street Observation of the upslope side of the site did not indicate
surface flow entering the site along the Mohouli Street boundary Therefore all of the
water exiting through the culvert was originating on the site
Abundant seepage to the surface was also observed during our reconnaissance
including subsurface seepage in Test Pit No 2
It is our opinion that these very wet site conditions will impose significant constraints
on development of the site The soft wet soils and seepage can potentially bog down
construction equipment causing construction delays and increased costs Continued
seepage could impact site grading design and require increased costs for additional
infrastructure such as additional drainage to handle the surface water flows resulting from
the seepage
As mentioned above previous work at a site in nearby Kukuau encountered
ephemeral ground water seepage and springs during periods of heavy rainfall
Exploration at that site indicated that the seepage was most likely the result of the
development of a localized perched ground water table that exceeded the ground surface
elevations Recommendations for that site included providing subdrainage to intercept the
perched ground water before it could daylight at the ground surface Presently it is not
known if this condition is an actual hazard for the University Terrace site however it can
be further assessed with additional exploration and observation of subsurface conditions
during construction

that the most current USGS Quadrangle Map of the area Hilo 1992 indicates the

Lava Tubes and Cavities
Based on our experience in the area lava tubes and cavities commonly are
encountered in pahoehoe basalt formations One small tube was observed open to the
ground surface during our site reconnaissance Lava tubes and cavities may vary
significantly in size from small blisters to large lava tubes of 20 feet in diameter or more In
general the majority of small cavities are limited in lateral extent However it should be
noted that there is a potential for the occurrence of long lava tubes which may traverse
the project site The general orientation of the long lava tubes would generally be in a
mauka makai direction
Therefore special attention should be given to the site preparation requirements
prior to site filling and construction of improvements on the cuts and fills Based on our
experience on the adjacent sites we recommend that the subgrades in fill areas be ripped
and proof rolled prior to fill placement during the mass grading operations In addition the
finished cut surfaces should be proof rolled with a large vibratory drum roller in an effort to
detect potential near surface lava tubes and cavities In general lava tubes cavities and
blisters discovered during the site preparation operations and encountered during
construction should be collapsed and backfilled with appropriate fill materials

Excavation
It is anticipated that the surface basalt rock materials may be excavated with

However some of the deeper excavations into dense lava may require the use of

Geological Hazards
As discussed above there are geological hazards present with a potential to
have adverse impacts on the proposed project These hazards include but are not
necessarily limited to the following
e Lava flow encroachment and pyroclastic fallout ash deposition from
possible future volcanic eruptions
e Floodwater encroachment due to heavy storms
e Seismic activity
The US Geological Survey has divided the Island of Hawaii into nine lava flow
hazard zones with the highest risk assigned the designation of Zone 1 and the lowest risk
assigned Zone 9 The project site is situated in an area designated as Zone 3 In this
zone lava flows younger than the year 1800 cover between about 1 to 5 percent of the
zone s surface About 15 to 75 percent of the land surface in the zone is less than
750 years old Lava inundation risk in Zone 3 is less than Zones 1 and 2 due to the
distance from active rift and vent areas Since the entire city of Hilo is located within Zone
3 on a regional scale there is no greater risk for this project site than for any other
location in Hilo However it appears that the site is situated on or very close to the
northeast rift zone of Mauna Loa and there are two cinder cones located on the site
Therefore there would be a higher potential for hazard from volcanic activity in this area
than the southern portions of Hilo which are further away from the rift zone
For pyroclastic fallout it should be noted that Hawaiian volcanic eruptions are
generally quiescent in nature with very little explosive activity that would eject pyroclastic
material over widespread areas

Most volcanic eruptions produce fragments of lava that are airborne for at least a
short time before being deposited on the ground These fragments are called tephra and
include ash cinders and Pele s hair In Hawaii tephra is usually ejected by lava fountains
and poses a serious hazard only in the immediate vicinity of an erupting vent The ash
soils generally identified as Pahala Ash appear to be Pleistocene or earlier in age
Therefore it is most likely that pyroclastic fall is a very minor hazard for the site
The Federal Emergency Management Agency FEMA has mapped most of the
project site on the Flood Insurance Rate Maps FIRM as Zone X an area outside the
flood hazard area However portions of the site along Alenaio Stream and a small
unnamed drainage have been mapped as being subject to a 1 percent chance of
inundation Site layout and grading design will have to account for these areas Since
Alenaio Stream traverses the site channelization or other flood control measures may
need to be provided in the site development plans
With respect to seismic activity it should be recognized that since the Island of
Hawaii is the site of three active volcanoes there is an appreciable level of seismic activity
associated with the volcanism Since the earliest records in 1868 there have been at
least 14 seismic events on and around the Island of Hawaii that exceeded Magnitude 6 on
the Richter scale The Uniform Building Code UBC designates the entire island as
Seismic Zone 4 Therefore the level of seismic activity associated with development of
the site should be adequately addressed in the design of structural elements for the
project
As previously discussed the project site appears to be situated along the
northeastern rift of Mauna Loa Localized faulting and ground movements are common
along active rift zones during volcanic activity Therefore it should be recognized that the
project site has a somewhat higher susceptibility to this hazard than portions of Hilo that
are not along the rift zone
With the exception of the localized flooding hazard identified at the site and the
potential of occasional ground water seepage none of the geological hazards that have
been identified for this site present any substantively greater risk than would be posed for
any other site in the city of Hilo Although present the hazards associated with volcanic
activity are currently impossible to estimate due to limited amount of historic activity on
Mauna Loa All of the identified hazards except for those directly associated with volcanic
activity i e ground movements lava flows and pyroclastic fallout can be addressed
through proper project design However the magnitude of these hazards may present
economic constraints on development of the site
