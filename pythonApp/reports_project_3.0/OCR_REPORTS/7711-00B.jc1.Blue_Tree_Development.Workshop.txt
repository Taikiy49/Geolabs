Dear Mr. Selley: 
This letter report presents our geotechnical recommendations for foundation design 
of the Blue Tree Development project. The project location and general vicinity are shown 
on the Project Site Plan, Plate 1. 
This letter report summarizes our geotechnical engineering recommendations based 
on the anticipated subsurface conditions at the site. These recommendations are intended 
for the foundations only. The recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
Project Considerations 
The project involves the design and construction of the ground level mezzanine 
structure for commercial spaces, located at The Collection site in the Kaka’ako area of 
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. Based on the information provided, we understand 
that the maximum unfactored column load from the new mezzanine structure is about 
50 kips. We also understand that a new foundation system will be installed as a monolithic 
system separate from the existing main building foundation that is supported on a pile 
foundation. 
Details of the structural design were not available at the time this report was 
prepared. Borings were not drilled due to the restriction of drill rig access. Our evaluation of 
the foundation design was based on the boring information obtained previously for The 
Collection development and will be subjected to field verification and observation by 
Geolabs during the foundation construction. 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be

middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion 
incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a series of lava 
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred to as the 
Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is on the coastal plain of Southern Oahu. The coastal plain was built 
on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano, which forms the eastern two-thirds of the Island 
of Oahu. The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of alluvium derived from 
erosion of the volcano, interbedded with coral reefs and associated deposits. 
The project site generally is underlain by deposits consisting of calcareous 
sediments and lagoonal deposits. A surface layer of man-made fill was placed over these 
deposits for development of the Kaka`ako area within the last century. 
Subsurface Condition 
Based on the borings drilled previously at the site, we anticipate that the project site 
is underlain by approximately 5 to 6 feet of surface fills placed over highly compressible 
lagoonal deposits extending to depths of about 13 to 25 feet below the ground level. The 
surface fills may consist of stiff sandy silts and dense sandy gravel. The lagoonal deposits 
generally consist of light gray, very soft sandy silts and very loose clayey sands and gravel. 
Below the highly compressible lagoonal deposits, we anticipate coralline formation, 
consisting of medium dense coralline sands and gravel, interbedded with coral and 
sandstone formation/ledge, to depths of about 100 to 115 feet below the ground surface. 
Very hard basalt rock formation may be underlying and extended to the greater depth. 
We anticipate groundwater at depths of about 6 to 9 feet below the ground surface, 
corresponding to approximately Elevation -2 to +1 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). Due to the 
proximity of the project site to Honolulu Harbor and the Pacific Ocean, water levels are 
expected to vary with tidal fluctuations and storm events. It should be noted that water levels 
also may vary with seasonal rainfall, time of the year, and other factors. 
Relevant boring logs (Plates A-2.1 through A-2.4) are attached for reference. 
Seismic Design Consideration 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition) (IBC 2006), the project site 
would be subjected to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to be 
addressed, as well as evaluating for potential soil liquefaction. 
We anticipate that the project site may be underlain by surface fills over loose/soft 
lagoonal deposits extending to depths of about 13 to 25 feet below the ground surface. 
Below the compressible lagoonal deposits, medium dense coralline formation is anticipated.

The loose granular lagoonal deposits (about 15 to 20 feet thick) anticipated at the 
site may appear to have a factor of safety of less than 1.0 with regard to potential soil 
liquefaction. Therefore, we believe the project site could be subjected to ground settlements 
(on the order of up to about 4 to 5 inches) in the event of liquefaction during a moderate 
earthquake (M6+). However, we believe lateral spreading associated with liquefaction would 
not be a significant design consideration based on the relatively flat topography across the 
site. 
Because of underlying potentially liquefiable soils, the project site would need to be 
classified as Site Class “F” based on IBC 2006 Section 1613.5.1, which would require a site 
response analysis to determine the spectral accelerations for liquefiable soils. However, we 
envision that the proposed mezzanine structure will have a fundamental period of vibration 
less than 0.5 seconds. In accordance with Chapter 20, Site Classification Procedure for 
Seismic Design contained in ASCE 7-10 (Minimum Design Loads for Buildings and Other 
Structures), a site response analysis will not be required. 
Based on the subsurface materials anticipated at the project site and the geologic 
setting of the area, we believe the project site may be classified from a seismic analysis 
standpoint as a “Stiff Soil Profile”. Therefore, we believe that seismic design may be 
based on a Site Class “D” soil profile in accordance with the International Building Code 
(Table No. 1613.5.2), 2006 Edition.

of short-period (SDS) and one-second (SD1), we believe Seismic Design Category “C” may 
be adopted in accordance with the International Building Code, 2006 Edition (IBC 2006) and 
the City and County of Honolulu Amendments.

Discussion and Recommendations 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the site and light structural loads, 
we envision that the proposed mezzanine structure may be supported on shallow 
foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous strip footings. Detailed discussions and 
recommendations for foundation design are presented in the following sections. 
Shallow Foundations 
We envision that the new mezzanine structure with relatively light structural loads 
(less than 50 kips) may be supported on shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or 
continuous strip footings. In addition, structures supported on a shallow foundation 
system should be structurally separated from the structures supported on deep 
foundations. 
It should be noted that soft and/or loose subgrade soils may be encountered at the 
footing subgrades. Where soft and/or loose soils are encountered at the foundation 
subgrades, we recommend providing a stabilization layer (minimum 24 inches thick) 
below the footings to provide uniform support of the foundations. The stabilization layer 
also should extend a minimum of 2 feet laterally beyond the edges of the footing. The 
stabilization layer should consist of 24 inches of 3B Fine Gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation) wrapped on all sides in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent). 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used for the design of footings bearing on the re-compacted on-site materials (minimum 
90 percent relative compaction). This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may 
be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces. Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches. In addition, the bottom of 
footing elevation for the shallow foundations should be placed at or above 
Elevation +3 feet MSL. Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be 
embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge 
of the utility trench, or the footings should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts 
of the utility lines. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed as recommended herein, we 
estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated static design loads for footings 
bearing on the re-compacted on-site soils to be on the order of about 1 to 1.5 inches. 
Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on similar materials should 
be on the order of about 0.75 inches or less (static loading only). 
Lateral loads acting on the structures supported on shallow foundations may be 
resisted by a combination of friction between the base of the foundation and the bearing 
materials and by passive earth pressure developed against the near-vertical faces of the 
embedded portion of the footings. A coefficient of friction of 0.35 may be used for footings 
bearing on the re-compacted on-site fill soils. Resistance due to passive earth pressure 
may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pcf (above groundwater level)

the footing excavations, these materials should be over-excavated to expose the

layer of existing fill materials at the project site, provisions should be made in the contract 
documents to over-excavate below the footings and backfill with compacted select 
granular fill or lean concrete (controlled low strength materials) in the event that soft 
and/or loose materials are encountered at the footing subgrades for shallow foundations. 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We anticipate the ground floor slabs for the new building construction will consist of 
reinforced concrete slabs-on-grade. We envision the slabs-on-grade for the proposed 
mezzanine structure will be supported either on new compacted fills placed to raise the 
existing ground surface to the finished subgrades or on the existing ground. 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated, we recommend increasing the 
building slab thickness (5 inches minimum) and incorporating steel reinforcement into the 
concrete slab (No. 4 reinforcing bars spaced at 12 inches in each direction) to reduce the 
potential adverse effects on the building slab-on-grade resulting from potential ground 
settlement from the new fills. 
New fills needed to raise the project site to the finished subgrades will induce some 
ground settlements due to consolidation of the underlying compressible lagoonal deposits. 
Therefore, we recommend placing the new fills as soon as practical to allow the anticipated 
ground settlements to occur prior to slab-on-grade construction. 
For the interior building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic), we recommend 
providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. The open-graded gravel cushion fill would 
serve as a capillary moisture break and would provide uniform support of the slabs. To 
reduce the potential for excessive future moisture infiltration through the slab and 
subsequent damage to floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on 
top of the open-graded gravel cushion layer. Interior walls should be designed to incorporate 
some flexibility to accommodate a small amount of possible ground movements. 
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic, such as the ground floor parking 
level, we recommend providing a 6-inch layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs in lieu

of the 4-inch thick gravel cushion layer. In areas where clayey fill materials are exposed at 
the slab subgrade level, the aggregate subbase layer underneath the concrete slabs should 
be increased from 6 inches to 12 inches. The moisture barrier may be omitted for these 
slabs. The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates compacted to 
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
For the design of structural slabs supported on 6 inches of aggregate subbase, a 
modulus of subgrade reaction of about 200 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection 
(pci) may be used for the slab resting on the compacted aggregate subbase. Where slabs 
are intended to function as rigid pavements for trucks, a minimum slab thickness of 6 inches 
may be used for preliminary design purposes. In addition, provisions should be made for 
proper load transfer across the slab joints that will be subject to vehicular traffic. 
Exterior flatwork, such as sidewalks and walkways, should be supported on 6 inches 
of aggregate subbase compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The 
thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at least 12 inches 
below the lowest adjacent grade. It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the 
slab edges should be backfilled tightly against the edges of the slabs with relatively 
impervious soils. These areas should also be graded to divert water away from the slabs 
and to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs. 
Soft and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing within the 
structure footprint should be over-excavated to expose firm and/or dense materials, and the 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills. The excavated soft 
and/or organic soils should be properly disposed off-site and/or used in landscape areas, 
where appropriate. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices 
for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the 
over-excavation quantities. 
After clearing and grubbing, the structure subgrade should be scarified to a depth of 
about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to 
the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as 
determined by ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Imported materials required for site filling should consist of select granular fill 
materials, such as crushed coral and/or basaltic gravel. The materials should be well-graded 
from coarse to fine with particles no greater than 3 inches in largest dimension. In addition, 
the materials also should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 
sieve. The materials should have a California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and 
a swell value of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883. 
Fill materials required for the structure construction should be placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Compaction

LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
available information obtained from borings drilled previously. Variations of subsurface 
conditions between and beyond the borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear 
evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The boring locations indicated herein are approximate, having been estimated by 
taping from visible features shown on the Topographic Survey Map transmitted by Sato & 
Associates, Inc. on April 25, 2013. Elevations of the borings were interpolated based on the 
spot elevations shown on the same plan. The locations and elevations of the field borings 
should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on 
the graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. These data have been
