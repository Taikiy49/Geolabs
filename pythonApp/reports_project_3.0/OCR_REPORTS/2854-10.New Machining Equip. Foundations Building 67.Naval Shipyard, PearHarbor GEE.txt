INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our supplemental geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed new Vertical Torque Lathe and the Machining and
Turning Center equipment foundations in Building 67 in the Naval Shipyard in Pearl
Harbor on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
We previously performed a Geotechnical Engineering Exploration for five new
machinery foundations in Building 67 and presented the results in a report entitled New
Machining Equipment Foundations in Building 67 Naval Shipyard Pearl Harbor dated
Subsequent to that report the Vertical Torque Lathe and the Machining
and Turning Center equipment locations have been changed In our addendum to the
first report dated we recommended that a new boring be drilled for the
relocated Vertical Torque Lathe
This report summarizes the findings of our supplemental exploration and presents
our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from analyses of the data
obtained during the field exploration for the project These recommendations are
intended for design input therefore the information contained herein may not be
appropriate for construction bidding purposes

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the proposed equipment sites were explored by
drilling and sampling two 2 borings to depths of 32 and 17 feet below the existing
ground surface for Boring Nos 6 and 7 respectively The approximate locations of the
borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In the northern portion of the building Boring No 6 was drilled for the Machining
and Turning Center Equipment After coring through a 4 inch thick concrete slab the
boring encountered a fill layer approximately six feet 6 in thickness consisting of
medium dense coral sand and gravel overlying a two foot 2 thick layer of alluvium
consisting of medium stiff clayey silt Below the alluvium the boring encountered medium
dense to loose coralline sand and gravel deposits to a depth of about 27 feet below the
existing ground surface Stiff clayey sand marine sediments underlay the coralline
deposits to the maximum depth explored of 32 feet below the surface
In the southern portion of the building Boring No 7 was drilled for the Vertical
Torque Lathe The boring was located close to the northwest edge of the newly
constructed concrete pad After coring through a 4 inch thick concrete slab the boring
encountered a very dense volcanic tuff deposit about eight feet 8 thick The tuff broke
down into very dense sand with gravel below the upper two feet 2 From the bottom
of the tuff to the maximum depth explored of 17 feet the boring encountered coral
deposits of very dense gravelly sand overlying loose gravel

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
As indicated in our previous exploration at the site the current field exploration
disclosed that the two relocated equipment sites are also underlain by two 2 different
subsoil conditions In the northern portion of the building the boring drilled for the
Machining and Turning Center Equipment encountered a medium dense to loose coralline
sand and gravel deposit below a near surface fill layer of medium dense sand In the
southern portion of the building the boring drilled for the Vertical Torque Lathe Equipment
encountered a competent volcanic tuff deposit near the existing ground surface
Based on the anticipated bearing stresses we believe that a shallow mat foun
dation bearing on the existing medium dense sand fill may be used for the Machining and
Turning Center Equipment The proposed Vertical Torque Lathe Equipment may be
supported by the newly constructed mat foundation bearing on the competent tuff deposit
or the very dense existing compacted fill
Foundation Support
Machine and Turning Center Equipment
Based on information provided to us the mat foundation is planned to be eight
inches 8 thick The proposed mat foundation should be embedded just below
the existing grades at the new machine location If the mat foundation is elevated
above ground the weight of the above ground portion of the foundation would
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2854 10 Page 7
exert additional loading on the underlying medium dense to loose subsoils
However if the mat is founded below the existing surface the concrete weight of
the mat would be partially compensated by the unloading caused by the
excavation of the subgrade soils
We believe that a shallow mat foundation may be used even though the proposed
machine location is underlain by medium dense to loose sand and gravel deposits
The compressible deposits were encountered at about ten feet 10 and at that
depth the influence of the relatively small six 6 by nine foot 9 mat should not
exert stresses high enough to cause settlements greater than 12 inch Additionally
the net stress applied by the planned machine and mat should be on the order of
only about 125 p s f this additional stress should not cause significant settlement
Subgrade soils for mat foundation should be compacted to not less than 90
percent of the maximum dry density determined in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91
test procedures to provide a firm base for the mat
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressures
developed against the buried vertical and near vertical faces of the foundation
system and by frictional resistance developed between the bottom of the
foundation and the bearing material Resistance due to passive earth pressure
may be calculated using an equivalent fluid weighing 250 pounds per cubic foot
p c f This assumes that the soil around the foundations are properly backfilled
and compacted A coefficient of friction of 0 35 between the bottom of the mat
and the bearing material may be used

torque lathe mat foundation is three feet below the top of the existing concrete slab We

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of two
2 borings to depths ranging between 17 and 32 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled
using a trailer mounted drill rig advancing continuous flight augers or rotary and core
drilling tools as appropriate
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual inspection and
testing in the laboratory All soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered are
Soil samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Boring Logs at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of four inches 4 in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0
foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Sieve Analysis tests ASTM Test Method D 422 63 are performed to evaluate the
grain size distribution of the soil The grain size distribution is used for classification in
the Unified Soil Classification System and to provide indices on other engineering
properties The result of the one 1 sieve analysis performed is summarized on Plate B 1
The Direct Shear test ASTM Test Method D 3080 90 is used to evaluate the angle
of internal friction and the cohesion in a soil sample The data may be used in bearing
capacity analyses and other engineering calculations The result of the one 1 direct
shear test performed is presented on Plate B 2
