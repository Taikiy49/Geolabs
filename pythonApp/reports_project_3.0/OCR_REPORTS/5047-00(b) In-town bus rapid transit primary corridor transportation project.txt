SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the information obtained from the pavement condition survey conducted
along the BRT alignment the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures consist
of alligator cracking edge cracking block cracking rutting and weathering and raveling in
the roadways with asphalt concrete AC pavement sections and linear cracking divided
slabs and faulting in the roadways with Portland cement concrete PCC pavement
sections
Both flexible pavement sections and rigid pavement section are planned for the
project Due to the lack of pertinent information such as as built details of the existing
roadways subsurface conditions and traffic data our pavement section design was
derived from a parametric study based on our engineering assumptions of Traffic Index
and subsurface conditions
Three flexible pavement sections and one rigid pavement section are provided for
roadways to be repaired along the BRT alignment
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific
pavement design recommendations

SECTION 1 0 INTRODUCTION
This pavement report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering
analyses for the design of new pavements for the proposed Bus Rapid Transit BRT
primary corridor transportation project in the city of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii The Bus Rapid Transit BRT primary corridor transportation project will span
from downtown Honolulu to Waikiki
This report presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived
from our analyses for the design of pavements only The recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 1 Project Considerations
Based on the information provided we understand that the Bus Rapid Transit
BRT Primary Corridor Transportation project involves the construction of roadway
improvements including pavements raised medians sidewalks and utilities through
downtown Honolulu and Waikiki The project is intended to speed the movement of
buses between major bus stations along the BRT alignment
The BRT route will utilize existing roads However special designated lanes will
be provided for the BRT buses Concrete pavement will be used for the exclusive and
semi exclusive BRT lanes while BRT shared lanes will be constructed of asphalt
concrete pavement A visual pavement survey has been conducted to evaluate the
existing condition of the pavements along the route However pertinent information on
the pavement as built details traffic data and subsurface conditions along the alignment
were not available for our study

Methodology of Pavement Design
The procedures used in determining the new pavement sections generally follow
those described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual dated May 1995
The pavement design manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii Department of
Transportation Highways Division Material Testing and Research Branch The
design procedures for flexible pavements are based on the Hveem Stabilometer
method developed by the California Department of Transportation Caltrans The
design procedures for rigid pavements are based on the design procedures
described in the Portland Cement Association manual Thickness Design for
Concrete Highway and Street Pavements

2 3 Alternative Repair System for Kuhio Avenue
The concrete pavement distress observed along Kuhio Avenue consisted
primarily of divided slabs and faulting Many of the slabs appeared to deflect and rock
under traffic loads indicating inadequate base subgrade support Repair of the
concrete pavement section would generally involve the following procedure
Saw cut and remove the affected concrete pavement area
Recompact base material remove and replace if contaminated
Install dowels into adjacent pavement sections to provide load transfer
Pour concrete and allow to cure
The repairs would generally take two to four days per section during which time
traffic would not be allowed
To reduce the construction time and disruption to traffic an alternative pavement
repair method may be considered Since most of the cracked concrete slabs are
generally intact a high density polyurethane material may be injected below the slab to
raise and restore the alignment of the concrete pavement
Hawaii California
SECTION 2 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The method consists of drilling 0 5 inch diameter holes into the concrete slab to
the base soil Two separate components are injected simultaneously through the drilled
holes The two chemicals start a reaction immediately to form a rigid polyurethane
foam The reaction produces an expansive force that lifts the slab The slab lift is
monitored and controlled to minimize cracking and achieve vertical alignment The
expansion of the foam also fills voids in the subgrade improving the strength of the
supporting material and fills cracks between the concrete slabs The main advantage of
this system is that the polyurethane achieves 90 of its full compressive strength within
15 minutes from injection allowing traffic to resume Therefore with this system the
roads do not have to be closed off for extended periods
Preparation
Based on the information provided we anticipate that the reconstruction will
approximately follow the existing alignment At the on set of the construction the
existing pavement sections should be completely removed The new pavement section
areas should be excavated where necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e
bottom of the aggregate subbase layer The pavement subgrade soils should be
scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above
the optimum moisture content and compacted to no less than 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as
a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance
with AASHTO Test Method T 180 ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soils be scarified and re prepared as recommended above
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted fill Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices
for over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the
over excavation quantities

In general the on site soils may be re used as a source of general fill materials
provided that the materials are less than 3 inches in largest dimension and are free of
deleterious materials Imported fill materials if required should consist of non expansive
granular material such as crushed coral or basalt The material should be well graded
from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of
1 percent or less Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use
Select granular fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The compaction requirement
for fills placed within the pavement areas should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction
Aggregate subbase material required for the project construction should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch level loose
lifts and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Site grading
operations should be observed by a representative from Geolabs It is important that a
representative from our office observe the site preparation to evaluate whether undesirable
materials are encountered during the excavation and scarification process and whether
the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those assumed in our report
2 5 Pavement Joints
Considering the large areal extent of the rigid pavements for the BRT project an
adequate amount and pattern of joints should be provided In general joints should be
provided in rigid pavements for the following reasons
1 To reduce potential curling and warping stresses in the pavement caused by
temperature and moisture gradients across the pavement slabs
2 To reduce and control cracking due to volume changes in the concrete
To reduce damage to immovable structures
4 To facilitate construction

2 6 Pavement Drainage
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to
carry surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the
site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping
in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs extend a minimum
of 2 inches into the subgrade soil below the subbase course layer to reduce the
potential for migration of excessive landscape water into the pavement section
Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect excess water from
landscaping irrigation For long term performance we recommend that a subdrain
system be constructed adjacent to the paved landscaped areas
