INTRODUCTION
This preliminary subsurface investigation was undertaken to
define the subsurface conditions underlying the proposed
extension of Pier 52 on Sand Island and to provide recom
mendations for the design and construction of the pier
extension
SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located on the northern side of Sand
Island bordering Kapalama Basin See Project Location Map
Plate 1 The existing Pier 52 is located adjacent to the
eastern side of the site The existing Pier 51 is located
at a distance to the west
Most of the proposed extension area is presently under 20 to
40 feet of water Recent landfill operations in the south
ern portion of the old sluice area have raised the elevation
to approximately 8 feet above sea level The remainder of
this sluice area to the north is open with an area of
approximately 300 by 380 feet This area is presently under
water Dredging equipment is currently moored in this open
waterway area

PROPOSED CONSTRUCTION
The current plan is to construct an addition to the existing
Pier 52 This new construction will extend approximately
460 feet in the Ewa direction from the western end of the
existing Pier 52
This proposed pier extension will consist of concrete planks
supported by driven piles and have a sheet piling bulkhead
on the landward side to retain the future sluice area
fill Relatively high crane loads are anticipated on the
wharf structure
The existing open sluice area presently underwater will be
backfilled behind the new wharf structure and may at some
future time be used to support container handling
facilities
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The project site is located on the seaward fringe of the
Honolulu Coastal Plain This coastal plain is composed of
interbedded alluvial marine and post erosional volcanic
deposits Extensive development of the plain and reclama
tion of lowlying areas has given the land its present shape
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1066 00
4
There is evidence that a basaltic lava flow of the Honolulu
Volcanic Series possibly originating from Makuku vent in
Nuuanu or from Punchbowl reached the eastern portion of
what is now Sand Island During the Waipio stand of the sea
elevation minus 60 feet this layer of basalt would have
provided a resistant surface which could have diverted the
flows of Nuuanu and Kapalama streams to the west The
diversion of these two streams would have laid down exten
sive subaerial deposits of alluvial clays and silts These
exposed alluvial deposits would have been consolidated due
to seasonal moisture changes
Subsequently as sea level rose to the Waimanalo stand
elevation 25 feet and then fell to its present level the
stream laid alluvium was buried beneath backreef deposits of
sands as a fringing reef grew on the seaward margin of the
island
Land reclamation projects during this century have built
Sand Island to its current topography Harbor dredging to
minus 40 feet MLLW completed earlier this year has given
the bottom of Kapalama Basin its present configuration

Soil Conditions
The marine 2 and 5 generally encountered
four distinct sets of soil conditions underlying the bottom
of the harbor
The first condition encountered was a layer of gray organic
silt averaging 3 to 4 feet in thickness This layer is very
soft and represents recent sediments deposited within the
harbor and was encountered at depths between 26 and 39 feet
below the water surface The variance in depth is the re
sult of the recent dredging of Honolulu Harbor s main basins
to minus 40 feet MLLW whereas the old sluice area was not
dredged
Beneath the gray silts our test borings encountered a
strata of medium dense white to tan sand with some cemented
seams which represents backreef deposits laid down as the
sea level rose to its current elevation This layer varied
from a thickness of approximately 8 feet in Boring No 1 to
approximately 19 feet in Boring No 2 This difference is
partly due to the recent dredging operations which most
likely removed portions of this strata at the locations of
Boring Nos 1 and 5

stiff silty clay sometimes intermixed with coralline and
basaltic sands and gravels These materials were deposited
during a lower stand of the sea and were somewhat con
solidated through dessication The thickness of the clay
layer varies from approximately 22 feet in Boring Nos 1 and
2 to 32 feet in Boring No 5
The fourth condition was a series of medium dense tan sands
which extended to the maximum depths drilled These sands

forereef deposits or a combination of both No coral algal

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The three marine borings drilled along the wharf and bulk
head line encountered significantly different soil condi
tions from those previously encountered at the existing Pier
52 location
Hard coral layers were not encountered in the three marine
borings The soils consisted basically of medium density
sand and coral down to depths of about 140 feet below the
water surface A layer of stiff clay about 20 to 30 feet
thick was encountered at about elevation minus 50 feet

Therefore it appears that deep friction piles driven
through the clay layer and into the underlying coral sand
formation would be required for the wharf structure
The two borings drilled through the recently placed fill in
the sluice area indicates that the upper 10 feet of the new
fill is generally dense and grades to medium density with
depth It appears that most of the anticipated ground set

Wharf Structure
Prestressed concrete pile foundations are planned to support
the wharf structure Hard coral or rock layers capable of
supporting high capacity end bearing piles were not en
countered in the drilled borings which extended below the
water surface about 135 to 140 feet Therefore it appears
that longer piles will be required to develop the pile
capacity by frictional resistance
Based on the boring data laboratory testing and analysis
it appears that for a inch octagonal prestressed con
crete pile driven down to a pile tip elevation of minus
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W
P 10
90 feet a 75 ton allowable pile capacity could be developed
Most of the pile capacity would be developed by frictional
resistance in the stiff clay and the lower medium density
coralline sand layer
The piles should be driven with a hammer delivering a mini
mum of 30 000 foot pounds of energy The piles should be
driven to a minimum depth of elevation minus 90 feet and to
a final resistance of about 5 blows per inch
Based on the blow count from the upper coral sand formation
encountered in the three borings drilled it does not appear
that spudding would be required for the concrete piles If
spudding is found to be necessary during pile placement it
should be limited to no more than 3 feet below the bottom of
the soft harbor clay to minimize the loss of frictional
resistance for the pile
We recommend a minimum of 32 test piles should be driven to
verify the pile lengths and to observe the actual pile driv
ing characteristics The test piles should be distributed
throughout the project area to help determine possible vari
ations in the subsurface conditions in the north south as
well as east west directions The length of the test piles
should be about 100 feet long

Due to the many variables involved to develop the supporting
capacity of friction piles we recommend that a minimum of
one of the test piles be load tested to twice its design
capacity or 150 tons to verify the allowable pile load and
to evaluate the settlement characteristics of the pile The
location of the pile load test should be determined by the
soils engineers after all the test piles are driven The
pile load test should be performed per Uniform Building Code
The pile uplift capacity will also be developed through
frictional resistance and should be equal to the vertical
pile capacity of 75 tons per pile provided splices are not
utilized for the piles
Due to the long unsupported pile length up to 50 feet
large pile deflections at the wharf structure level would be
required in order to mobilize the lateral capacity of the
soil Therefore batter piles are recommended to resist the
lateral loads The same driving criteria for vertical piles
may be used for the batter piles

Existing Fill
The two and 4 drilled over the existing
sluice fill encountered about 34 feet of sand and coral fill
over a 5 foot layer of soft to medium stiff clay underlain
by the natural dense coral and sand formation
The upper 10 to 15 feet of the existing fill was found to be
in a dense condition while the lower portions were found to
be medium dense
Due to the granular nature of the existing fill material and
relatively thin underlying soft layer encountered it is our
opinion that most of the fill settlement has probably
occured in this area We recommended that two settlement
gauges be installed and monitored in this area to verify
that the fill settlement has reduced sufficiently to allow
construction of improvements

Sluice Area Fill
Up to about 30 feet of fill is planned for the Sluice area
behind the bulkhead line The depth of water in this area
is about 20 feet
It is anticipated that the fill will be placed by end dump
ing material into the sluice area It is our understanding
that the existing fill in the southern portion of the sluice
area was filled in this manner The two borings drilled in
the existing fill indicate the fill is presently in a
medium dense to dense condition and should perform satis
factorily The fill material was found to consist mostly of
gravel and course sand
The on site stockpiled sand is considerably finer than the
material previously used for the end dump fill Laboratory
tests on the on site fine sand indicate that end dumping
this sand under water would result in a loose condition
which would be susceptible to later settlement due to vibra
tion caused by earthquake or pile driving activity The
laboratory tests indicate that settlements up to about 10
of the underwater fill height could occur due to vibration
of the loose sand Therefore it is our opinion that the
on site stockpiled fine sand should not be used for the pro
posed underwater fill However laboratory tests indicated
GEOLABS HAWAII
P 18
that this sand has a high CBR and would be suitable to con
truct the upper portion of the fill above water
In general the underwater fill should consist of material
from gravel to small cobble size and have the capability to
attain a stable structure with minimal compaction effort
Boulder fill is not recommended under water due to the large
voids between boulders which could cause migration of fines
into the voids and subsequent ground surface settlements
It appears that the remianing stockpiles are not of suf
ficient quantity to fill the entire sluice area and since
off site material will be required for the underwater fill
it is our opinion that quarry material such as 3B Fine
aggregate could perform satisfactorily The advantages of
this material are as follows
1 Generally minimal compactive effort is required for
this material to obtain a stable and compact structure
Significant settlement due to vibration is not antici
pated
2 The voids between aggregates are relatively small
therefore the possibility of migration of fines and
settlements are greatly reduced

softening and pumping of the soils above the water
level

sand fill from migrating into the voids Compact the
base course to 95 compaction
