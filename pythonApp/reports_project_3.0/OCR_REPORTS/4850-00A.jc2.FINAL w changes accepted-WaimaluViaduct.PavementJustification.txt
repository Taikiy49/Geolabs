SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
New pavements will be required for the Interstate Route H 1 Widening
Westbound project Due to the clayey nature of the subgrade soils encountered at the
project site we believe that a permeable drainage layer should be incorporated into the
pavement design for the new highway lanes Therefore we have only considered
pavement structural sections with a permeable drainage layer in our analyses for the
project
Based on the traffic data provided the relatively low R Value of the subgrade soils
and the economic analyses performed we recommend that a rigid pavement structural
section be used for the new pavements Based on our analyses we recommend that a
rigid pavement section consisting of 12 5 inches 650 psi flexural strength and or
13 5 inches 450 psi flexural strength of PCC placed on 6 0 inches of UTPB over
6 0 inches of ASB be used for the design of pavements for the Interstate Route H 1
Widening Waimalu Viaduct Westbound project
It should be noted that ground settlement of the existing highway between
Station 103 00 and the Waimalu Viaduct has been occurring due to consolidation of the
soft alluvial soil deposits underlying the site Consolidation of the soft alluvial soils is
caused by the significant amount of embankment fills placed during construction of the
Interstate Route H 1 Highway We understand that the original pavement section
consisted of PCC pavements Due to the ground settlements significant distress to the
pavement between Station 103 00 and the Waimalu Viaduct was observed Therefore
the original PCC pavements were overlayed with AC pavements
Because new fills will be placed in the area between Station 103 00 and the
Waimalu Viaduct about Station 111 00 and the on going settlements we anticipate
some ground settlement would still occur due to consolidation of the soft alluvial soils
Therefore we recommend that a flexible pavement structural section be used for the new
pavements in this area Detailed discussions are presented in our geotechnical
engineering exploration report for the project Based on the traffic conditions we
recommend that a flexible pavement section consisting of 5 0 inches of AC on 12 0 inches
of ACB over 6 0 inches of UTPB over 15 0 inches of ASB be used for the design of
pavements between Station 103 00 and the Waimalu Viaduct about Station 111 00
The text of this pavement justification report should be referred to for detailed discussion
and specific recommendations for design of pavements

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two main shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield volcano Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island
Waianae Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active Therefore
Waianae Volcano s eastern flank was partially buried below the younger Koolau Volcano
lavas that banked against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a
broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau of Central Oahu
During the history of the evolution of the Island of Oahu multiple fluctuations in
sea levels occurred as a result of the advance and retreat of great continental glaciers
These sea level changes occurred more substantially in the Pleistocene Epoch and had
some effect on the geologic evolution of the Island of Oahu The changes in worldwide
sea levels affected the erosional baseline of streams and caused local submergence
and emergence of the coastal island landforms with respect to the level of the sea
The project site is located along the distal southern flank of the Koolau Volcano
therefore the project site traverses terrain that is mostly underlain by deeply weathered
volcanic deposits erupted from the Koolau Volcano The volcanic deposits have been
mapped and are referred to as the Tertiary Period Koolau Volcanic Series The thick
volcanic deposits consist of mainly thin individual layers of basaltic lava flows The
volcanic rock appears to be extremely weathered to form clayey and silty saprolitic soils
containing some decomposed rock fragments
The saprolitic materials grade to highly and moderately weathered rock material
at relatively shallow depths along the existing hillside cut locations At the large gulch
Waimalu Gulch located between about Station 103 00 and about Station 123 00 the

Methodology of Pavement Design
Two types of pavement structural sections flexible and rigid pavements were
considered in the pavement analyses for this project The flexible pavement sections
presented in this report were generally determined based on the methodology described in
Chapter 3 of the revised Pavement Design Manual dated March 2002 The Pavement
Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii Department of Transportation
Highways Division Material Testing and Research Branch The pavement design
methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer method developed and used by the
California Department of Transportation Caltrans

design procedures described in the Portland Cement Association Thickness Design for
Concrete Highway and Street Pavements Based on the information provided we
understand that the new PCC pavement structure will be subjected to traffic approximately

Subgrade Preparation Below Pavement Section
We anticipate that the majority of the new highway pavements will be constructed
near the existing grade in a cut condition or in a fill condition In general the area within
the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed at the on set of
earthwork Vegetation debris deleterious material and other unsuitable materials should
be removed and disposed of properly off site or disposed of in a designated area to
reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill or future
improvements should be over excavated to expose stiff and or dense materials The
resulting excavation should be backfilled with aggregate subbase materials The
excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
After clearing and grubbing the future pavement areas should be excavated where
necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the aggregate subbase course
layer The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with AASHTO Test Method T 180 ASTM D 1557 Optimum

Fill Materials
In general the excavated on site materials may be re used as a source of general
fill material Imported fill materials if required should consist of non expansive select
granular material such as crushed coralline or basaltic materials The materials should be
well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension
and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The
materials should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum
swell of 1 percent or less Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance with
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils It should be noted that the clayey
subgrade soils are susceptible to softening and loss of strength upon saturation
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
W O 4850 00 A
Page 16
Therefore special attention should be given to the surface drainage of the pavements As
a minimum the pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures
Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction To
collect infiltrated water into the pavement structural section we recommend that a
pavement edge drain system be provided along the sides of the new travelway
We envision that an edge drain will be needed to collect and discharge infiltrated
water into the pavement structural section from the untreated permeable base layer
The edge drain should consist of a 6 inch PVC perforated pipe with perforations facing
down surrounded by untreated permeable base A permeable separator non woven filter
fabric conforming to Section 648 of the HSS should be placed around the untreated
permeable base to reduce the potential for migration of fines into the drainage material as
shown on Plate 3 In addition a permeable separator should also be provided between
the untreated permeable base and aggregate subbase Care should be exercised when
placing the subbase base and or construction of the surface course to avoid damaging
the edge drain collector pipe A typical edge drain detail is provided on Plate 3 for
reference The edge drains should be daylighted into appropriate drainage structures for
proper discharge
