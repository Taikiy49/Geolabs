SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, the subsurface conditions at the site consists of fill underlain by clinker 
(basaltic fragments) over medium hard to hard basalt rock formation.  Our borings 
encountered some localized loose zones in the fill layer.  We did not encounter 
groundwater in our borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend 
using shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new structures 
planned. As an alternative, foundations for support of the new structures may also 
consist of thickened-edge slab footings. We recommend using an allowable bearing 
pressure of up to 3,500 psf for design of footings bearing on the re-compacted on-site 
fill or new compacted structural fills needed to achieve the design finished grades.  
In addition, we recommend implementing a program of cavity probing and grouting 
of the foundation locations supported on less than 5 feet of compacted fills or on-site soils 
due to the presence of cavities and/or voids in the basaltic lava flows. The primary purpose 
of probing and grouting the foundation locations is to reduce the potential for loss of 
foundation support resulting from the collapse of cavities and/or the migration of fine 
materials into voids below the foundation.  
One percolation test was performed at the proposed leach field location.  Based 
on the percolation test conducted, the percolation rate at the proposed leach field 
location is less than 1 minute per inch. In accordance with the "Manual of Septic-Tank 
Practice" prepared by the U.S. Department of Health, Education, and Welfare, the leach 
field location with the percolation rate obtained at the site would be suitable for siting of 
the absorption system. 
Also, one field infiltration test was performed at the retention pond location.  Based 
on the field infiltration test conducted, the measured infiltration rate at the proposed 
retention pond is greater than 8 inches per minute.  The appropriate factor of safety in the 
City and County of Honolulu Stormwater BMP Guide should be used to determine the 
design infiltration rate.  The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion 
and specific design recommendations.

a sewage disposal system consisting of a leach field and surface water runoff disposal 
system consisting of a retention pond are planned to service the facility.  We understand 
that the site grading work for the project will consist of cuts and fills on the order of 
about 2 feet or less.

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an 
area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes:  Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during recent 
geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 to 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea 
(both still active). 
The project site is situated on the northeastern flank of the Mauna Loa Shield 
Volcano, which had been built up by the successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows 
and pyroclastic materials. The lava formation encountered at the site appears to consist 
of both a'a and pahoehoe type flows, which spread and ponded as they approached the 
ocean. A’a lava is typically characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface 
resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders. 
Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal 
structure of vesicular (porous) rock. 
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas, especially pahoehoe type 
lava. Cavities form when lavas are still in a molten state and they represent both lava 
tubes (intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form 
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow, leaving a hollow tube-like structure, 
which may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets 
are generally smaller in horizontal extent. 
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water 
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions. In general, water is 
transmitted through the porous rock matrix, along joints, fractures and inter-flow 
contacts, cavities, and along clinker layers. Clinker and cavities serve as primary water 
transmission features because of their high permeability.

Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 
sixteen borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 16, extending to depths of about 
10 to 50 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, two borings designated as 
P-1 and P-2 were drilled at the proposed leach field and retention pond locations,

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition), the project site may be 
subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed. 
The following sections provide discussions on the seismicity, soil profile type for seismic 
design, and the potential for liquefaction at the project site. 
2.4.1 Earthquake and Seismicity 
Generally, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to 
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or 
during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the 
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not 
erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but 
most are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. 
However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause 
minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the active 
volcanoes located in the southern portion of the Island of Hawaii. 
The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 6 (M6+) including the October 15, 2006 earthquakes. Based on

SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site, we 
recommend using shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new 
structures planned. As an alternative, the foundations for support of the new structures 
also may consist of thickened-edge slab footings. 
Because cavities and/or voids are commonly present in the basaltic lava flows, 
we recommend implementing a program of cavity probing and grouting of the

potential for loss of foundation support resulting from the collapse of cavities and/or the

foundations are supported on more than 5 feet of compacted fills (clinker or basalt

the foundations may be omitted.

design of individual wastewater systems (IWS).  Based on the percolation test 
conducted, the percolation rate at the proposed leach field location is less than 1 minute 
per inch. In accordance with the "Manual of Septic-Tank Practice" prepared by the 
U.S. Department of Health, Education, and Welfare, the leach field location with the 
percolation rate obtained at the site would be suitable for siting of the absorption 
system.

retention pond is greater than 8 inches per minute.  It should be noted that the 
appropriate factor of safety in Table 5 of the City and County of Honolulu Stormwater

rate.

3.1 
Building Foundations 
We understand the new structures for the project generally will consist of one 
story buildings. Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site 
(after site grading), we recommend utilizing shallow spread and/or continuous strip 
footings to support the new structures planned. As an alternative, foundations for 
support of the new structures also may consist of thickened-edge slab footings. 
Our borings encountered a surface layer of sandy gravel fill ranging from about 
3 to 8 feet in thickness. Some loose soil pockets were encountered in the fill layer. 
Therefore, loose materials encountered at the foundation subgrades should be 
over-excavated to expose the underlying firm materials.  The over-excavation may be 
backfilled with the on-site granular soils or structural fill material, placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction.  As an 
alternative to backfilling with compacted fill, the foundations may be extended to bear on 
the underlying competent materials. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may 
be used for the design of footings bearing on compacted structural fill or on-site 
compacted granular materials. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may 
be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or 
seismic forces. 
In general, the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finished grade. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes 
or on slopes should be embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance 
measured from the outside edge (at the base of the footing) to the face of the slope is 
no less than 6 feet.

In addition, foundations next to other foundations, utility trenches, easements or 
other retaining walls should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane 
extending upward from the bottom edge of the structure or utility trench, or the footings 
should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is

recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the foundations to be less than

Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction between the 
bottom of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure acting 
against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of 0.45 
may be used for footings bearing on the compacted fill materials, re-compacted on-site 
materials, or basalt formation. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be 
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 450 pounds per square foot per foot of 
depth (pcf) (Kp = 3.5) assuming the soils around the footings are well compacted for a 
level ground surface condition. The passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches 
of the soil should be neglected unless covered by pavements or slabs. 
A Geolabs representative should observe the footing excavations prior to the 
placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions 
and the required embedment depths. 
3.2 
Probing and Grouting 
Based on our experience in the vicinity, cavities and/or voids are commonly 
encountered in the basaltic lava flows at the project site. To reduce the potential for loss 
of foundation support resulting from the collapse of cavities and/or the migration of fine 
materials into voids below the foundations, we recommend implementing a program of 
cavity probing and grouting of the foundation locations supported on 5 feet or less of 
compacted fills or on-site soils. Where new building foundations are supported on more

than 5 feet of compacted fills or on-site soils (clinker and basalt formation is at least 
5 feet or deeper below bottom of footings), probing and grouting of the foundations may 
be omitted. In addition, the probing and grouting program may be omitted for 
non-bearing walls, such as partition walls and site retaining walls. 
For estimating purposes, we recommend drilling probe holes, where required, at 
10-foot on centers for the thickened-edge slab footings (or other continuous strip 
footings) of the new structures. In addition, at least one probe hole should be drilled for 
every 50 square feet of isolated spread footing location. The probe holes should be at 
least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned 
bottom of foundation elevation. If cavities and/or voids are encountered or suspected 
during the probing operation, additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing 
to help delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and/or void. 
The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with controlled low 
strength material (CLSM) with a slump of about 6 to 9 inches. The CLSM should be 
injected at low to moderate pressures. As an alternate to CLSM, sand-cement grout 
with a slump of about 6 to 9 inches also may be used. 
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and/or voids at the project site, 
we recommend obtaining unit prices for additional probing and grouting during bidding 
or pricing of the work. In addition, the probe drill should be available on-site until the 
probing and grouting operations are completed.  The contractor also should be aware 
that a longer lag time between probing/grouting operations and foundation construction 
might be required in the construction schedule. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the probing and grouting operations to 
observe the presence of cavities and/or voids in the subsurface and to allow for 
additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and/or changed conditions

3.3 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We anticipate concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the ground floors of the 
new structures. For the interior building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic), we 
recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill below the slabs for 
uniform support. The cushion fill should consist of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, 
No. 67 gradation) and also would serve as a capillary moisture break. To reduce the 
potential for excessive moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings, 
an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel cushion fill layer. 
Flexible floor coverings should be considered above the floor slab because they can 
better mask minor slab cracking. 
Where slabs are subjected to vehicular traffic or sustained vibrations, a minimum 
of 6 inches of compacted aggregate subbase course (3-inch minus granular materials) 
should be provided below the slabs in lieu of the 4-inch thick cushion fill layer. The 
moisture barrier may be omitted for these slabs. The aggregate subbase course layer 
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted 
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. For the design of structural slabs, a 
modulus of subgrade reaction of about 300 pounds per square inch per inch of 
deflection (pci) may be used for design of the slabs. 
In addition, we envision that exterior concrete walkways and exterior flatwork 
likely will be required for the project. In general, we recommend providing a minimum 
4-inch thick cushion fill layer of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) or 
aggregate subbase below exterior concrete slabs that will not be subjected to vehicular 
traffic. The aggregate subbase should be compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction. To reduce the potential for substantial shrinkage cracks in the concrete 
slabs, crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the 
walkways (or slabs) with expansion joints at right-angle intersections.

3.4.4 Drainage 
In general, retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the potential for 
build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 
12-inch wide zone of permeable material, such as open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), placed directly around a perforated pipe 
(perforations down) at the base of the retaining structure. The perforated pipe 
should discharge to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. 
As an alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or 
EnkaDrain, may be used instead of the permeable drainage material. The 
prefabricated drainage product also should be hydraulically connected to a 
perforated pipe at the base of the retaining structure. Unless covered by concrete 
or asphaltic concrete, the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively 
impervious materials, such as well-graded soils, to reduce the potential for 
significant water infiltration behind the retaining structures.

3.5.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, structural fill should be used at the site. Structural fill materials should 
consist of well-graded granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum 
dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted 
mass. In addition, structural fill materials should contain less than 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve by weight. In general, the excavated on-site 
materials may be reused as a source of structural fill materials, provided that they 
are processed to meet the maximum particle size and gradation requirements 
presented above. 
Imported fill materials, if required, should be free of organics and deleterious 
materials and should be suitable for the intended use based on the 
recommendations for fill materials provided herein. In addition, a Geolabs 
representative should observe and/or test imported materials prior to being 
transported to the project site for the intended use. 
3.5.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in 
loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to 
the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, 
vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of 
rocks, boulders, and/or cobbles. Instead, a testing program should be conducted at 
the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a Geolabs 
representative to evaluate the number of passes needed to achieve the desired 
level of compaction. Based on this testing program, the number of passes may then 
be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction.

materials (aggregate base materials). Where the 1-inch minus, well-graded granular 
materials are used, the granular bedding and the initial trench backfill should be 
moisture-conditioned and compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction.

to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well-graded granular 
materials less than 6 inches in maximum particle size. The backfill should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in about 8-inch level loose 
lifts, and mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Where 
trenches are located in paved areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the 
pavement grade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction.

3.8 
Corrosion Potential and Chemical Attack to Concrete 
Resistivity, pH, sulfide, chloride and sulfate contents, and redox potential tests 
were performed by TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on a selected soil sample to evaluate 
the corrosivity characteristics of the in-situ soils at the site.  
The pH and sulfide were determined in general accordance with SW846 Test 
Methods 9045C and 9034.  The chloride/sulfate contents were determined in general 
accordance with the EPA 300.0 test method. The resistivity and redox potential were 
determined in general accordance with SM 2510B and SM 2580B, respectively. Results 
of the soil analyses conducted on the selected soil sample are summarized in Appendix 
B. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In 
general, the lower the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried 
metallic structure. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will 
contribute to corrosion to metallic objects.  Based on the resistivity value of the in-situ 
soils encountered, the subsurface materials may be classified as very corrosive for 
buried metallic structures.   
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in the 
soil.  In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 300 ppm, sulfate content of 
less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5 may be considered “non-corrosive” to 
underground concrete pipelines and structures.  Based on the test results, the in-situ

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling sixteen 
borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 16. In addition, two borings were drilled at 
the proposed leach field and retention pond locations, and these borings are designated 
as P-1 and P-2, respectively.  The borings extended to depths of about 5 to 50 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous 
flight augers and rotary coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.   Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-18. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3.   
The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting 
any drilling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the 
Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative 
quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks 
and Minerals."

The rippability (or excavation characteristics) of a rock mass is a function of the 
relative hardness of the rock, its relative quality, brittleness, and fissile characteristics. 
Dense basalt formations with high RQD values would be very difficult to rip and would 
probably require more arduous methods of excavation.

Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation 
of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Six Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification.  Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on 
Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Five Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were performed 
on selected core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the basalt 
rock encountered. Unconfined compression test results are presented on Plate B-3. 
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D1883) were 
performed on the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-4 through B-6. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationship. The test results are presented on Plate B-7. 
One set of Corrosivity tests, including pH (Method 9045C), Minimum Resistivity 
(SM 2510B), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were 
performed by TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on a selected soil sample obtained from 
our field exploration.  The test results are summarized on Plate B-8. 
One Redox Potential (SM 2580B) and one Sulfide Content (Method 9034) tests 
were performed by TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on a selected soil sample obtained 
from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-9.
