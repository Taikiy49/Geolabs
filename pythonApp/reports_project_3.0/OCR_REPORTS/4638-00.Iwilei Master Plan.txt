Dear Mr Kimura
In accordance with your request we have conducted a preliminary geotechnical
engineering study for the proposed Iwilei Master Plan project This letter report summarizes
our findings and preliminary geotechnical recommendations based on our field exploration
These recommendations are intended to inform the owner and designers about the
geotechnical considerations that may need to be addressed for the proposed project The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end
of this letter report

of Ilwilei Road and North King Street in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii as shown on

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling two borings extending
to depths of about 72 and 115 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate
locations of the drilled borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration generally encountered about a 3 inch thick layer of asphaltic
concrete pavement underlain by fill materials consisting of medium dense to dense silty gravel
and sand extending to depths of about 5 to 5 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
fill materials were underlain by lagoonal deposits consisting of very loose to loose silty sand
and gravel and very soft to medium stiff silty clay and clayey silts with some organics
extending to depths of about 55 to 65 5 feet below the existing ground surface In
Boring No 1 the lagoonal deposits were underlain by hard to very hard basalt formation
extending to a depth of about 80 feet below the existing ground surface The basalt formation
was underlain by alluvial colluvial deposits consisting of basalt boulders interbedded with silty
clay extending to a depth of about 112 feet below the existing ground surface The
alluvial colluvial boulder deposits were underlain by very hard basalt formation extending to
the maximum depth explored of about 115 5 feet below the existing ground surface In
Boring No 2 the lagoonal deposits were underlain by alluvial colluvial deposits consisting of
basalt boulders interbedded with silty clay extending to the maximum depth explored of about
72 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was encountered at depths varying from approximately 6 2 to 7 0 feet
below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration It should be noted that
groundwater levels are subject to change due to tidal fluctuation seasonal precipitation storm
surge conditions and other factors
For more detailed descriptions of the materials encountered please refer to the Logs
of Borings presented on Plates 4 and 5 of Appendix A Laboratory testing was performed on
selected soil samples obtained and the test results are presented on Plates 6 and 7

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface information it is our opinion that the proposed development
is suitable for the site from a geotechnical engineering standpoint Detailed discussions and
recommendations for preliminary design of foundations and other geotechnical aspects of the
project are presented in the following sections of this report
Pile Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site and the anticipated
high column loads for the proposed structures we recommend that a deep foundation system
consisting of driven piles be used to support the proposed towers and parking structures We
recommend that 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles driven into the basalt
formation and or alluviai colluvial boulder layer be used to support the proposed structures
The driven piles would derive its support primarily from end bearing on the basalt formation
and or the alluvial colluvial boulder layer encountered at depths of about 55 to 66 feet below
the existing ground surface
For preliminary design an allowable compressive load capacity of 75 tons may be used
for the 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles The allowable compressive
loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces We recommend that the 16 5 inch
octagonal piles be spaced a minimum of 3 5 feet from center to center The minimum pile
spacing would also avoid reduction in vertical capacity due to group action and also facilitate
pile driving in groups
As previously mentioned the basalt formation and alluvial colluvial boulder layer were
encountered at depths of about 55 to 66 feet below the existing ground surface Therefore
we anticipate the average lengths of the piles to be approximately 60 to 70 feet below the
existing ground surface The estimated pile engths are for initial cost estimation purposes
only
Uplift Load Resistance
Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead load of the driven pile and
shear along the pile surface and the adjacent soils An uplift load capacity of 35 kips
may be used for the 16 5 inch octagonal piles driven to the basalt formation and or
alluvial colluvial boulder layer encountered at depths of about 55 to 66 feet below the
existing ground surface The uplift load capacities provided include the weight of the
pile The capacity of the pile member in tension should be checked by the structural
engineer

Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on the proposed structures may be resisted by a combination
of the lateral load capacity of the driven piles and the passive pressure against the
vertical faces of the pile caps grade beams Passive earth pressure against the vertical
faces of the pile cap grade beam may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure
of 300 pounds per square foot per depth pcf above the groundwater level and 150 pcf
below the groundwater level
The lateral load resistance of driven piles is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the pile allowable deflection at the top of the pile and
the induced moment in the pile The lateral load analyses were performed using the
program LPILE plus for Windows which is a microcomputer adaptation of a finite
difference laterally loaded pile program originally developed at the University of Texas
at Austin The program solves for deflection and bending moment along a pile under
lateral loads as a function of depth The analysis was carried out with the use of
non linear p y curves to represent soil moduli The lateral deflection was then
computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths The analyses were
performed for the laterally loaded 16 5 inch octagonal piles

fixed against rotation are presented in the following tables The values provided in the
tables are based on an associated horizontal deflection of 0 5 inches assumed at the

Pile Foundation Settlements
Settlements of the pile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression of the
pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total settlement of the
pile supported foundations to be small provided that the upper basalt bearing layer is
sufficiently thick
The borings indicate that the upper basalt layer may have variable thickness
Therefore additional subsurface exploration is required to confirm the presence and
thickness of the upper basalt layer
Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering energies in the range
of 25 000 to 30 000 foot pounds in the field Significantly higher energy hammers
hammers with energies greater than 45 000 foot pounds are not recommended for
this project The hammer should be equipped with an energy level control such that
driving within the loose soft soils may be controlled at a lower energy to reduce tensile
stress development which could damage or cause cracking of the piles
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy to
the top of the pile and also reducing damage to the piles

Basement Level
We understand that construction of a basement level may be planned for the proposed
project As previously mentioned groundwater at the project site was encountered at
relatively shallow depths Groundwater was encountered at depths of about 6 2 to 7 feet
below the existing ground surface Due to the soft loose soils and the shallow groundwater
level encountered at the project site difficulty is anticipated during construction of a basement
level An adequately designed and properly implemented shoring and dewatering system will
be required for the basement construction
Because the proposed project is located in a developed area the basement excavation
will need to be adequately shored to reduce the potential for adjacent ground movement In
addition the dewatering operation should be conducted in such a manner that the dewatering
will not cause areal ground subsidence which may cause potential damage to existing
structures and utilities
The design and installation of the shoring and dewatering systems will generally be
governed by the contractor s construction methods and available equipment and these should
therefore be made the sole responsibility of the contractor and must comply with all applicable
safety requirements
