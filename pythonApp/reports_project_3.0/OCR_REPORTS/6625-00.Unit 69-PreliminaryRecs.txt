This technical memorandum presents the results of our geotechnical engineering 
exploration and engineering analyses performed for the Rehabilitation of City Streets, 
Unit 69 Lilipuna Road project in Kaneohe on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The findings and 
preliminary recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the 
end of this technical memorandum. A detailed summary of our findings and 
recommendations will be contained in our geotechnical engineering report. The final report 
should be consulted when it becomes available. 
Project Description 
The Rehabilitation of City Streets, Unit 69 project is part of an overall Pavement 
Survey Project covering various locations across the City and County of Honolulu. 
Generally, the Unit 69 project will involve rehabilitation of approximately 12,000 lineal feet 
of Lilipuna Road from Kamehameha Highway to Kamehameha Highway in the Kaneohe 
area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project location and general vicinity are shown on 
the Project Location Map, Plate 1. The roadway alignment of the pavement rehabilitation 
project is shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling 10 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 10, at selected locations along the roadway 
alignment. The borings generally extended to depths of about 5.5 to 11.5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface. In addition, the boring exploration was supplemented by coring 
the existing pavement section to depths of about 1 to 1.5 feet below the existing pavement 
surface at 18 selected locations, identified as C-1 through C-20 (excluding C-4 and C-6).  
The approximate boring and coring locations for this project are shown on the Site Plans, 
Plates 3.1 through 3.12. 
Our field exploration indicates that the existing pavement structure along Lilipuna 
Road generally consisted of an AC surface course with multiple overlays over a relatively 
thin layer of base course/subbase materials underlain by in-situ subgrade soils.  Based on 
our field exploration and laboratory testing program, the existing pavement structure and 
underlying subgrade materials encountered along Lilipuna Road are summarized below:

The base/subbase materials encountered consisted of sandy gravel and are 
generally dense in relative density. It should be noted that the base course encountered at 
the majority of the boring/coring locations is relatively thin, on the order of less than about 
4 inches in most cases. The subgrade materials encountered below the pavement section 
generally consist of stiff silty clays/clayey silts with moisture contents in excess of 
40 percent. 
We did not encounter groundwater in our shallow borings (maximum depth of 
11.5 feet below ground level) at the time of our field exploration. However, it should be 
noted that groundwater levels are expected to fluctuate depending on seasonal rainfall, 
time of year, surface runoff, and other factors.  
During our field exploration, our geologist classified the materials encountered in 
the borings by visual and textural examination in the field in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2488, Standard Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored 
the drilling operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were 
further reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general 
accordance with ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for 
Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.1. Rock formations were described in general accordance with the Rock 
Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. The Rock 
Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative 
Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock 
Mechanics (March 1977).  Graphic representations of the materials encountered are 
presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-28.  Photographs of the AC cores 
from the existing pavements are provided in Appendix D.

Design Subgrade Conditions 
Our field exploration program also consisted of collecting five (5) bulk samples, 
designated as Bulk-1, 3, 5, 7, and 10, of the pavement subgrade soils.  Each bulk sample 
consisted of soil cuttings of the pavement subgrade soils generated from Boring Nos. 1, 3, 
5, 7, and 10, respectively along the roadway alignment.  Laboratory California Bearing 
Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D 1883) were performed on the bulk samples to evaluate the 
swelling potential and pavement support characteristics of the pavement subgrade soils.  
The samples were remolded to near the optimum moisture content and saturated 
(Plates B-2 though B-6).  In addition, R-value tests were also performed on Bulk-1 and 
Bulk-7 for additional information (Plates B-7 and B-8).  The test results are summarized in 
the following table.

Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results, it appears that the 
pavement subgrade soils generally consist of stiff Silty Clay (CH) and Clayey Silt (MH) 
soils.  Laboratory CBR tests suggest that these pavement subgrade soils exhibit CBR 
values ranging between 1.7 and 16.8 in a laboratory compacted condition. 
Based on our field exploration, laboratory test results, and engineering analyses, 
we believe that a design CBR value of 11.5 (average of Bulk-1, 3, and 10) may be adopted 
for the in-situ pavement subgrade soils in the design of flexible pavements for Lilipuna 
Road. 
Pavement Structural Section 
Our field observations indicated that Lilipuna Road generally exhibits medium to 
high severity pavement distress. Based on the information provided, we expect no grade 
changes for the roadway for this project. Therefore, we recommend the street 
rehabilitation consist of milling (cold-planing) and replacing with new asphaltic concrete. 
Based on the above assumptions, we recommend utilizing the following flexible pavement 
section for design.  Detailed calculations of the flexible pavement design section are 
presented on Plate C-2.

  Remove 6 Inches of Existing Pavement to Accommodate New Pavements 
  2.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete  
  4.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete Base (92 Percent Max Theoretical Specific Gravity) 
  6.0-Inch Total New Pavement Thickness 
After removal of the existing asphaltic concrete layer to accommodate the new 
pavement structural section, the exposed base materials or asphaltic concrete remnants 
should be proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton (static weight) drum roller for a minimum of 
eight passes to compact the exposed base materials. The drum roller should be operated 
at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. If broken up asphaltic concrete is exposed after 
the milling process, consideration should be given to complete removal of the broken up 
asphaltic concrete to expose the underlying intact asphaltic concrete or aggregate base 
materials before proof-rolling the base materials. 
In addition, paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavements. In addition, surface water ponding 
should not be allowed on-site during or after construction. 
It should be noted that our field exploration results indicate that the base course 
layer underneath the AC surface course is generally thin and likely insufficient due to a 
clayey subgrade condition.  In addition, the moisture content of the subgrade soils are 
relatively high and the lack of a pavement drainage system could be a cause of the 
pavement distresses observed along the roadway. However, since this is a pavement 
rehabilitation project, we are only addressing the pavement design section at this time. 
Limitations 
The findings and preliminary recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, 
upon information obtained from our field exploration, laboratory testing, site 
reconnaissance, and past project experience in the project vicinity. Variations of 
subsurface conditions from those described in this technical memorandum may occur, and 
the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until additional field 
exploration is conducted at the site or construction is underway. 
The field exploration locations are approximate, having been estimated by pacing 
and measuring from visible features noted on the Topographic Survey Map transmitted by 
Parsons Brinckerhoff on February 21, 2012. Elevations of the field data points were 
interpolated from the contour lines and spot elevations shown on the same plan. The 
boring locations and elevations should be considered accurate only to the degree implied 
by the methods used.
