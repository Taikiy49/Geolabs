INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of the second phase of our geotechnical
engineering exploration for the design of the proposed Kapolei Golf Course Clubhouse
Maintenance and Support Facilities This second phase exploration was performed to
supplement a previous exploration comprising the subject of our report dated June 15
1990 which addressed more specifically the golf course mass grading ponds and water
features
The project site is located at the northeast edge of the proposed Villages of Kapolei
Golf Course approximately 2 000 feet south of Farrington Highway in Ewa on the island
of Oahu Hawaii The general location of the project site and its vicinity is shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
Our work on the project was performed in general accordance with our proposal
to ParEn Inc dated This report summarizes our findings and
recommendations
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Based upon the preliminary site plans provided to us it appears that the proposed
Clubhouse and Maintenance Facilities for the Villages of Kapolei Golf Course development
will consist of a clubhouse encompassing an area of approximately 20 000 square feet
two 2 maintenance buildings encompassing areas of approximately 1 200 and 4 400
square feet an access road of approximately 2 000 linear feet and paved service roads
maintenance yards and parking areas
The proposed golf course site encompasses approximately 185 acres of land area
and was until recently used for the cultivation of sugar cane

The project site is situated to the southeast of the junction of Farrington Highway
and Kapolei Boulevard in the Ewa District on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general

The project site has previously been used for the cultivation of sugar cane up until
the present time At the time of our exploration the project site was vegetated but the
cultivation of the cane had ceased

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and
its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Waianae and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level
during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of Pearl Harbor and the Ewa Coastal Plain
The subsurface conditions in the majority of the coastal plain are dominated by fossil
Pleistocene age coral algal reef deposits laid during high stands of the sea which
intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area
is Waianae Basalt
The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late Pliocene and
early Pleistocene Epochs by thin bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building
activities ceased approximately 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells
indicates that the island of Oahu has subsided as much as 1200 feet since the cessation
of this early volcanic activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2459 00 A Page 6
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across their mouths The growth of
the reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global
sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to the present sea level around the island of Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal sediments may have also resulted in
deposits of coral algal limestone and marls within the predominantly lagoonal sedimentary
unit
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation cf the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2459 00 A Page 7
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments
The geologic history of the project area has been further complicated by the
deposition of recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of the vents of the
Upper Member of the Waianae Volcanic Series including Puu Makakilo Puu Palailai and
Puu Kapolei These post erosional i e following the cessation of shield building events
were contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations
Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature owing to the interaction of
groundwater with the rising magma resulting in steam explosions which expelled large
quantities of pyroclastic material predominantly ash and cinder These deposits of
pyroclastics have consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally referred to as mudrock
Agricultural land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years
have brought the Ewa Honolulu area to its present form

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our exploration and analyses it is our opinion that development of the
proposed clubhouse and maintenance facilities is feasible from a geotechnical engineering
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2459 00 A Page 9
point of view The soils encountered at the site are generally competent and should
provide adequate bearing for proposed development However laboratory testing of the
surface soils indicates that they are potentially expansive and special design and
construction procedures must be incorporated to the foundations and pavement sections
bearing on these soils
Site Grading
Site grading is anticipated to be minimal for the proposed development of the
project In general based on the results of our exploration it appears that conventional
grading methods may be used for the mass grading portion of the project
It is recommended that a topographic map and a preliminary grading plan of the
project site be forwarded to the project geotechnical engineer for review with respect to
the considerations on which the design recommendations presented in this report are
based
The following recommendations are general guidelines for the design of the final
grading and for the development of the facilities
Prior to commencement of grading all areas within the proposed grading limits
should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting grubbed and
spoil material should be disposed of properly off site or stockpiled for muiching and use
in landscaping
Due to the dry and sometimes windy site conditions moisture conditioning of fill
material will be necessary It is also advisable to institute a dust contro program during
grading operations

Soft or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below any areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to firm natural material and the
resulting depression backfilled with well compacted engineered fill
In general the excavated soil materials should be suitable as general fill material
Any boulders or cobbles larger than twelve 12 inches in maximum dimension should be
removed from the excavated material and disposed of off site
The on site material should be adequate as a source of trench and structural
backfill to within two 2 feet of the finish subgrade Trench and structural backfill should
not contain any rock or lumps larger than six 6 inches in maximum dimension The final
two 2 feet below finish grade in areas under foundations or areas to be paved should
be filled with select non expansive materials
Imported material if required should be non expansive with no particles greater
than six 6 inches in size and no more than fifteen 15 percent passing the 200 sieve
Any imported material that may be required should be tested by the geotechnical
engineer to determine its suitability for the intended use prior to delivery to the jobsite
General fill snould be placed in level lifts not exceeding twelve 12 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 of the maximum dry density as
determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 Trench backfill and structural fill should
be placed in level lifts not exceeding eight 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted
to a minimum of 90 of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Method
D 1557 78

Excavation
Based on the results of our preliminary exploration it appears that the materials
encountered in the project development area may be excavated without untoward
difficulty utilizing conventional earthmoving equipment Some hard excavation and or
ripping may be encountered in localized cemented areas or when bouldery horizons are
encountered
Infrastructure Installation
It is anticipated that underground utilities such as water sewer drain and irrigation
lines will be installed as part of the development work Based on the results of our field
exploration it appears that conventional excavation methods may be utilized for utility line
trenching
In general the subsurface materials encountered at the site are relatively
competent and should require minimal shoring to support utility trench walls However
areas of loose sand or gravel may be encountered The requirement for trench shoring
will depend in part on the depth of excavation needed Design and installation of any
necessary shoring should be made the responsibility of the contractor However trench
shoring and bracing should conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements
Trench backfill should be placed in level lifts not exceeding eight 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 maximum dry density as determined by
ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Mechanical compaction equipment is highly suited to the materials encountered
at the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be used as a means to
attempt to compact the on site solic

Foundation Support
Our field exploration has indicated that the site is underlain by alluvial soils Our
laboratory testing for the second phase exploration indicates that the on site soils are
primarily silty clays and generally have moderate expansion potential It is our opinion
that relatively shallow spread or strip footing foundations may be used to support
structures Alternately thickened edge slabs on grade may be used for lightly loaded
structures
Special treatment should be given to the subgrade preparation for foundations to
preclude deleterious effects which may occur as a result of the expansive characteristics
of the on site soils The treatment to be used below foundations and slabs on grade
should comprise placement of a layer of capping material below the structural bearing
surfaces The capping material below shallow spread or strip footings should be 12
inches in thickness and that below slabs on grade should be 18 inches in thickness The
material below the foundations should be overexcavated as necessary and the resulting
surface scarified to a depth of six 6 inches moisture conditioned and recompacted to
a minimum density of 90 of its maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test
Method D 1557 78 After the compaction of the capping subgrade the soils should be
thoroughly moistened and kept in a damp condition until the placement of the capping
maiterials
The capping material should be selected granular material not exceeding three 3
inches in maximum size and with no more than 15 percent passing the No 200 sieve
It should be placed in horizontal layers not exceeding eight 8 inches loose thickness and
compacted as indicated above
The capping material should be submitted to the geotechnical engineer for testing
and approval prior to its delivery to the site

Shallow Spread and Continuous Footings
For structures bearing on the fill compacted in accordance with the
recommendations presented in this report an allowable bearing capacity of 3 000 p s f
may be utilized The bearing value is for dead and live loads and may be increased by
one third when temporary loads wind and seismic are considered
Spread and continuous footings should be embedded a minimum of twelve 12
inches below the nearest adjacent finished grade
If a footing is located next to a utility line it should extend to the bottom of the
utility trench to reduce the potential for footing subsidence due to settlement of trench
backfill
Provided that the above recommendations are followed footing settlements are
estimated to be less than three eights 8 8 of an inch for footings bearing on compacted
structural fills
Slabs On Grade
Where structures utilizing a slab on grade are to bear on the in situ soils the
deleterious effects which may be caused by the potentially expansive subgrade soils may
be reduced by providing a capping lift with a minimum of 18 inches of non expansive
imported select granular material The Capping material should be submitted to the
geotechnical engineer prior to its use for testing and approval
As discussed previously 18 inches of capping material should be placed below the
bottom of slabs on grade The capping material should extend a minimum of two 2 feet
beyond the perimeter of the building In addition the thickened edge of the slab should
be embedded to a minimum of 18 inches below the nearest adjacent subgrade to lessen
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2459 00 A Page 14
the potential of future waiter infiltration to the soils underlying the slab thus inducing
expansion of the soils
Where the slab on grade is to be subjected to forklift traffic it is recommended that
they be designed as concrete pavements as recommended below
The capping fills should be placed and compacted immediately after moisture
conditioning of the subgrade to reduce the drying of the underlying clayey subgrade soils
The subgrade should be thoroughly moistened and kept damp until the placement of the
capping materials The moisture conditioning will reduce the potential for subsequent
expansion of the clayey soils should re wetting occur
After the placement and compaction of the capping fill under and to two 2 feet
outside the building pad conventional slab on grade construction may proceed
In order to protect against future moisture infiltration an impervious moisture
barrier on top of the capping layer is recommended below the slabs One 1 inch of
moist fine sand may be placed above the plastic membrane to help concrete curing
Culvert Structures

To provide uniform support a minimum of twelve 12 inches of granular bedding
material such as 3B Fine gravel or ASTM C 33 No 67 should be placed under the
structure

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 9 at the appropriate
sample depths
Six 6 one inch ring swell tests were performed on remolded and undisturbed
samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils in both natural and compacted
conditions The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
Three 3 Atterberg Limits tests were performed to aid in the identification of the
soils The test results are summarized on Plates B 2 B 3
One 1 direct shear test was performed on a selected undisturbed sample to
evaluate the shear strength parameters of the subsoils The test result is plotted on Plate
B 4
Three 3 CBR tests were performed to aid in the design recommendations for the
pavement section The test results are summarized on Plate B 2
