SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered about 2 to 11 inches of asphaltic concrete 
overlying about 1 to 6.5 feet of fills consisting of medium dense to very dense sandy 
gravel, clayey gravel and silty sand, and medium stiff to stiff clayey silt and silty clay. 
Below the fill layer, alluvium and/or estuarine deposits were encountered to the maximum 
depth drilled of about 17.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. In one of the borings, 
very stiff to hard residual soil was encountered at a 3.5-foot depth. Alluvium encountered 
generally consisted of soft to hard clayey silt and sandy silt and very dense silty sand. 
Estuarine deposits encountered generally consisted of very soft to medium stiff clayey silt 
and silty clay and loose to very loose silty sand. 
Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at depths ranging from 
approximately 3.3 to 13 feet below the existing pavement surfaces at the time of our field 
exploration. The groundwater levels encountered generally correspond to about 
elevations of -1.8 to +12.5 feet MSL. It should be noted that the groundwater depth will 
vary based upon storm surge conditions, rainfall, surface water runoff, seasonal 
precipitation and other factors.   
We anticipate that majority of the water main inverts will be on the order of about 
4 to 5 feet below the existing pavement surface. However, the pipe invert will be extended 
to depths of about 10 to 15 feet below the pavement surface where the new water main 
traverses under the existing utility or box culvert. Installation of the proposed water mains 
will generally involve excavation into the underlying fills, alluvium and/or estuarine 
deposits. In general, we anticipate that the in-situ soils along the proposed water main 
alignments may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. 
Where deep excavations (greater than about 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary 
shoring or sloping and benching should be implemented for the trench excavations. Based 
on our site observations, we believe that temporary cut slopes for open-cut excavations are 
not practical. In this case, trench walls would have to be cut near vertical, necessitating the 
use of shoring during construction. For shallow excavations with minor dewatering, we 
envision that trench shields may be used for the shoring of the excavated trenches. For 
deeper excavations that may involve substantial dewatering, one possible method of 
shoring would be to use interlocking sheet pile shoring. The use of a sheet pile shoring 
system may also serve as a cut-off wall to aid in the dewatering operations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age. As volcanic activity at Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows from 
Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming the Schofield Plateau. 
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the late Pliocene Epoch and early 
Pleistocene Epoch by the extrusion of successive thinly bedded lava flows. The main 
shield-building stage ceased approximately 2.5 million years ago. Evidence from historic 
drilled wells indicates that the Island of Oahu has subsided by as much as 1,200 feet 
since the cessation of the early volcanic activity (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). During 
the period of island subsidence, coral-algal reefs began to grow along the southern coast 
of Oahu forming embayment protected by barrier reefs. A series of lagoons formed behind 
the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the lagoons 
(Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as a 
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers accumulated, the level of the oceans fell because there was less 
water available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded (melted), 
global sea levels rose because the volume of water increased. The land mass comprising 
the Island of Oahu remained essentially stable during these water level changes, and the 
fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands 
of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to the present sea level on the Island 
of Oahu.  
The higher sea level stands caused landform changes, including the accumulation 
of deltas and alluvial fans composed of terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old 
bays, the accumulation of reef deposits at correspondingly higher elevations, and the 
deposition of lagoonal and/or marine sediments in the quiet lagoonal waters protected by 
barrier reefs. The concurrent growth of reefs and the accumulation of lagoonal sediments

also resulted in the deposition of coral-algal limestone and marl materials within the

The lower sea level stands caused streams to carve drainages into the coastal 
plain platforms composed of sedimentary and coral reef deposits. In addition, subaerial 
exposure of the calcareous sediments caused the consolidation of the soft deltaic 
materials and lagoonal deposits and the induration of calcareous reef materials. 
Furthermore, renewed subaerial erosion acting at the upper elevations of the volcanic 
shield caused the downstream deposition of terrigenous alluvial sediments under 
relatively higher energy conditions.  
During periods of no significant sea level changes, continued meandering stream 
action extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvial materials 
overlying the marine-lagoonal sediments.  
The project site is located near the middle and east lochs of Pearl Harbor; with the 
passage of time, some of the drainage channels to Pearl Harbor were filled in by more 
recent soft alluvial sediments. 
Land developments along Farrington Highway and roadways within the project 
limits have resulted in alterations to the natural terrain, including the placement of earth 
fills, which may be of poor quality in terms of supporting large loads.

3.2 
Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate excavation depths of up to about 4 to 5 feet below the pavement 
surface will be required for the installation of the new water mains. Deeper excavations 
up to about 15 feet below the pavement surface may be required where the water main 
traverses under an existing utility or box culvert. Where deeper excavations (greater than 
about 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary shoring or sloping and benching should be 
implemented for the trench excavations. Based on our site observations, we believe that 
temporary cut slopes for open-cut excavations are not practical. In this case, trench walls 
would have to be cut near vertical, necessitating the use of shoring during construction. 
For shallow excavations with minor dewatering, we envision that trench shields 
may be used for the shoring of the excavated trenches.  For deeper excavations that may 
involve substantial dewatering, one possible method of shoring would be to use 
interlocking sheet pile shoring. The use of a sheet pile shoring system may also serve as 
a cut-off wall to aid in the dewatering operations, which is further discussed in the 
“Dewatering” section herein. The sheet piles should be driven with a suitable hammer to 
a sufficient depth to reduce the potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the 
amount of dewatering within the excavations due to the relatively permeable nature of the 
subsoils. It should be noted that the excavations will likely encounter very soft/loose soil 
deposits at the bottom of the excavation. Therefore, there is potential for bottom heave in

these very soft/loose soil conditions. The contractor should carefully evaluate the potential 
for the bottom heave and design the shoring system accordingly. 
It is important to install adequate sheeting prior to the excavation and to maintain 
it tightly against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation. The properly 
braced sheeting is essential to reduce appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent 
ground into the excavation, which may result in potential settlements or distress to 
adjacent structures or other improvements, such as roads or utilities. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for trench excavation 
and the adequacy and safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s site 
superintendent, who should be required to continuously present on-site during excavation 
and construction works, will have the best opportunity to promptly observe changing 
conditions during construction, such as unforeseen subsoils, unexpectedly high 
groundwater table, inappropriate construction sequence or techniques, etc., which may 
affect shoring stability. 
However, it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and 
the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. 
Due to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the 
magnitude of movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship, such 
as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed, the subsoil

press-in system instead of a vibratory hammer may be considered.

3.3 
Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of the pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the medium dense silty sand and sandy gravel 
surface fill soils, a minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular cushion material, such as 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), should be provided below the pipe. 
Where soft and/or loose soils are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, such as 
in the alluvial or estuarine deposits, a stabilization layer consisting of an additional 
24 inches of open-graded gravel, such as No. 3B Fine gravel, wrapped in a non-woven 
filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below the cushion layer for 
uniform support. A typical trench detail is provided on Plate 4. 
The granular cushion layer should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width 
of one-fourth (¼) of the outside pipe diameter, or trench width, whichever is greater, and 
should extend 12 inches above the pipe. Before the placement of cushion material, a 
Geolabs representative should observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm 
materials are exposed at the bottom of the trench or whether a stabilization layer should 
be provided below the cushion layer. 
3.4 
Trench Backfill 
It should be noted that loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the 
trench, the unit weight of the trench backfill material, and the friction resistance between 
the backfill material and the trench walls. To calculate the vertical loads on the buried 
pipes, we recommend using an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot (pcf) for 
the trench backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0.3. Earth forces acting upon the 
pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of the trench. Therefore, the width of the 
trench should be kept to a minimum. Traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be 
considered for the portion of the pipes located in roadway areas. 
In general, the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Water 
System Standards, State of Hawaii 2002. The first zone of backfill extending from the 
cushion material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes (or 12 inches above the 
groundwater level, whichever is higher) should consist of select free-draining granular

3.5 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfill materials consisting of the on-site soils should be moisture-conditioned to 
at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches 
in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. The upper 3 feet of backfill materials below the finished 
pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Aggregate subbase materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, 
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture 
is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry 
density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed to compact the excavated surface on-site fill soils or imported fill 
materials. Precautions should be taken against the occurrence of pipeline flotation before 
and during trench backfilling due to the possible influx of large quantities of groundwater 
into the trenches. The buoyant uplift forces due to groundwater influx may lift empty or 
even partially water filled pipes. 
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during backfilling to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper 
moisture content.

3.8 
Pipe Settlement and Construction Considerations 
The primary settlement of the new waterline installation is generally caused by the 
difference in unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted 
backfill material placed over the pipeline. The net increase in loading may cause settlement 
of the underlying subsurface soils below the trench invert. In addition, we anticipate that 
some of the new waterlines may be underlain by soft and/or loose estuarine deposits. 
Therefore, some consolidation and/or secondary settlement may occur below the planned 
waterlines. For waterlines supported on the stabilization layer as recommended above, total 
settlements on the order of about 1 inch may be anticipated. 
The settlement estimates assume that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be consistently practiced by the contractor during waterline installation 
operations. Based on our knowledge and experience with similar soft/loose soil 
conditions, significant settlement of the pipes may be attributed to the construction 
methodology and equipment used by the contractor. Therefore, special attention should 
be given to the installation procedures for the proposed waterlines (especially in soft 
and/or loose soil conditions) to reduce the potential for settlement distress in the future. 
As indicated above, the waterlines underlain by soft/loose estuarine deposits 
should be supported by an approximately 24-inch-thick stabilization layer consisting of 
free-draining materials wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric. The non-woven filter fabric 
provides separation between the open-graded aggregates and the surrounding soft 
and/or loose soils. It should be emphasized that the filter fabric should be pulled taut and 
relatively free of wrinkles prior to placement of the open-graded aggregates. In general,

3.9 
Dewatering 
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater level 
is above the bottom of the proposed excavation. Groundwater was encountered in the 
borings drilled at depths ranging from about 3.3 to 13 feet below the existing pavement 
surface during our field exploration. Based on the groundwater levels anticipated and the 
permeable nature of the granular soils to be encountered during the excavations, 
dewatering will be required at the planned excavations of the project construction. 
Therefore, dewatering provisions will need to be included in the contract documents for 
the proposed construction.  
It is anticipated that the granular soils encountered are relatively permeable. 
Therefore, dewatering of the excavation may involve moderate to large volumes of water. 
Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of the excavations is not 
recommended. The resultant areal depression of the natural groundwater table could 
induce consolidation of the compressible subsoils resulting in potential ground 
settlements, which could affect the existing structures and utility lines. The potential 
impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural groundwater table 
must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering. 
It is our opinion that a cut-off wall system, such as interlocking sheet piles, should 
be considered to aid in dewatering the deeper excavations. However, sumps will be 
needed to collect water that percolates up into the base of the excavation or infiltrates 
through the sheet piles. The sheet piles should be driven to an adequate depth to reduce 
the potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the amount of dewatering within 
the excavations in areas underlain by granular subsoils with generally high permeability. 
The use of an interlocking sheet pile shoring support system with a tremie concrete plug 
is relatively watertight, which should allow the groundwater levels outside the excavations 
to be maintained close to the original pre-construction levels. Therefore, some type of 
groundwater control requirement should be specified in the contract documents.

The contractor must carefully evaluate the potential impact of the dewatering system 
selected on depressing the natural groundwater table prior to dewatering. The 
contractor should retain a qualified geotechnical engineer to design and evaluate the 
dewatering system used. 
The contractor should be solely responsible for the impact and safety of the 
dewatering operations. The contractor’s qualified representative, who should be 
continuously present on-site during dewatering activities, will have the best 
opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and to 
implement, as soon as possible, necessary precautionary or remedial measures 
including, but not limited to, slowing down or stopping the dewatering operations. 
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations, permeable granular subsoils may 
be susceptible to piping and "quick" conditions. The dewatering operations should be 
carried out without creating a "quick" condition or softening at the excavation 
bottoms. Therefore, the project dewatering operations should be performed without 
pumping out soil fines (pumping clear water only) and should be coordinated with the 
shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not adversely affected. 
Excessive pumping, which removes soil fines, may result in "blowing" or heaving of 
the excavation bottom or sides.

3.12 Corrosion Potential 
Nineteen sets of laboratory corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum 
Resistivity (ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), 
were performed by our laboratory and Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. to evaluate 
the corrosivity of the on-site soils on selected soil samples obtained from our field 
exploration.  The test results are summarized on Plate B-12 of Appendix B.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower 
the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure will 
have. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the 
corrosion of metallic objects.  Results of the resistivity testing indicate that the on-site soils 
have resistivity values of about 450 to 1,700 ohm-cm.  Therefore, the on-site near-surface 
soils may be considered as Corrosion Category A (moderately to severely corrosive) based 
on the Board of Water Supply, City & County of Honolulu, Water System External Corrosion 
Control Standards dated 2021.  
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete structures is 
dependent on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in the soil. The pH 
values based on the test results range from about 6.51 to 8.43. Based on the chloride 
and sulfate content tests performed on the on-site soils, the tested chloride content values

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling 19 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 119, to depths of about 16.5 to 17.5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface at the approximate locations shown on the General Site Plan, 
Plate 2, and Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.5. We used a truck-mounted drill rig equipped 
with continuous flight augers to advance the borings. 
Our field engineer and/or geologists classified the materials encountered in the 
borings by visual and textural examination in the field in general accordance with 
ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored 
the drilling operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were 
further reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general 
accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering 
Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-19. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used.  The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
A pocket penetrometer test was performed on a selected cohesive soil sample 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test result is 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Fourteen Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Nine Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
Seven 
Unconsolidated-Undrained 
Triaxial 
Compression 
(TXUU) 
tests 
(ASTM D2850) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear 
strength of the silty and clayey soils encountered. The approximate in-situ effective 
overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the relatively 
“undisturbed” soil sample. The test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on 
Plates B-5 thru B-11. 
Nineteen sets of Corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) tests were 
performed by our laboratory and Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on selected soil 
samples obtained from our field exploration.  Results of the corrosivity tests are presented 
on Plate B-12.

Field Permeability Tests 
The permeability of the subsurface materials at the project site was evaluated by 
performing constant or falling head permeability tests in Boring Nos. 104, 106, 108, and 115 
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.5. The tests were 
performed at a depth of about 15.0 feet below the existing pavement surface. 
During the falling head test, the test hole was initially saturated. After saturation, the 
falling head permeability test was performed by filling up the test boring with water. Time 
intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which calculation of the 
permeability values was based. The results of the falling head permeability tests are 
presented on Plates C-1, C-3, C-5 and C-7. 
During the constant head test, a water pump was used to pump water into the boring. 
The flow rate was recorded by timing and calculating the volume as a relatively constant 
flow rate was achieved. The results of the constant head permeability tests are presented 
on Plates C-2, C-4, and C-6.
