2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago with an area
of approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the northern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield Volcano
which has been built up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and pyroclastic
materials These lava flows are believed to be late Pleistocene in age and belong to the
Hamakua Volcanic Series Cavities are commoniy encountered in these lava flows The
cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and pockets
inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches
in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum
dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of rock
and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of passes of a
compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be conducted
at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs On the basis of this testing program the number of
passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction For
preliminary estimating purposes compaction may be accomplished by using a
large vibratory drum roller having a static weight of at least 10 tons traveling at a
speed of about 300 feet per minute The roller should pass over the fill surface at
least eight times for structural fill
The aggregate subbase course should be placed in level lifts not exceeding
8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction

3 2 Impervious Liner and Underlining
We understand that a synthetic liner will be provided for the reservoir to reduce the
potential for significant water infiltration through the underlying material It is our opinion
that the material type performance installation and protection details of the impervious
liner should be designed in accordance with recommendations by the liner manufacturer
It is critical that the liner maintain its integrity to prevent saturation and build up of seepage
water pressures
Our experience with similar synthetic liner installations indicates that there is
sometimes a tendency for the liners to become detached at the top of the water
impoundment It appears that this may be the result of inadequate anchoring of the top of
the liner and insufficient flexibility of the liner material We recommend that the liner
manufacturer be consulted regarding proper construction detailing and installation of the
liner with particular attention to proper anchoring to reduce the potential for liner failure
Effects of fluctuating water levels on the performance of the impervious liner near the top
anchor should also be considered The anchorage trench should be backfilled with
aggregate base course material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
Technical representatives of the manufacturers of the impervious liner should be
required to be on site during the installation process to assure that proper construction
procedures and precautions are followed A sales representative of the supplier s may
not have the technical expertise to adequately perform this function
An underlining consisting of a heavy duty geotextile fabric will be required beneath
the impervious liner to increase the puncture resistance of the liner The use of a
geotextile fabric is preferred over a gravel cushion due to the ease of installation and the
increased puncture resistance of the synthetic liner when combined with a layer of

3 4 Bridge Foundations
Considering that the existing ditch is lined with concrete we understand that the
effects of scour on the bridge foundation will not need to be considered in our foundation
analyses Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site we believe that a
shallow foundation system consisting of continuous footings may be used to support the
new bridge structure planned at the site An allowable bearing pressure of up to
4 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of new footings bearing on
the weathered basalt formation This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade For footings constructed near the tops of the slopes or on slopes
that are not lined with concrete the footings should be embedded deep enough such that
the horizontal distance measured from the outside edge at the base of the footing to the
face of the slope is not less than 6 feet
Where the bridge foundation is located next to utility trenches or easements the
footings should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary
plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench As an alternative the
footing should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by excavating and sampling
seven test pits extending to depths of approximately 1 5 to 10 feet below the existing
ground surface The test pits were excavated with a backhoe at the approximate locations
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the test pits were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the field exploration operations on
a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System Logs of the materials encountered are
presented on Plates A 1 through A 4
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths
