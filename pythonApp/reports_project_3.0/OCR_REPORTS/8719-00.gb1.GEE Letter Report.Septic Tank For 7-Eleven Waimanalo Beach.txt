In accordance with our fee proposal dated June 27, 2023, we have performed a

This letter report summarizes the findings from our geotechnical exploration and

recommendations are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located at 41-849 Kalanianaole Highway in Waimanalo on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. We understand that a new septic tank and seepage pit are 
planned for the 7-Eleven Waimanalo Beach store. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that the new septic tank and 
seepage pit will be in the parking lot near the southern corner of the existing store building. 
We also understand that the existing cesspool will be converted into a seepage pit for the 
disposal of wastewater. As part of our field exploration, we performed a percolation test 
in the 10-foot deep boring to evaluate the percolation characteristics of the subsurface

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is located at 41-849 Kamehameha Highway in Waimanalo on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project site is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The new septic tank and seepage pit are located at the eastern corner of the 
parking lot.  The parking lot consists of an asphaltic concrete (AC) surface that is generally 
in fair condition. A landscaped area was observed east of the new septic tank and 
seepage pit locations. An existing concrete rubble masonry (CRM) wall, with a chain-link 
fence along the top of the CRM wall, was observed along the east side of the property 
line.  Based on the topographic survey, the site is relatively flat with existing ground 
surface elevations between about +15 and +16 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration program at the site consisted of drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, to depths of about 16.5 and 10 feet below 
the existing ground surface, respectively. Boring Nos. 1 and 2 were drilled near the 
proposed septic tank and seepage pit locations, respectively. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, our field exploration encountered 3 to 4 inches of AC underlain by fill 
materials extending to depths of about 0.5 to 1 foot below the existing ground surface. 
The fill materials consisted of medium dense silty gravel. The fill materials were underlain 
by aeolian and beach deposits consisting of loose to medium dense sand with varying 
amounts of silt extending to the maximum depth explored of about16.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface.  
We encountered groundwater in Boring No. 1 at a depth of about 14.1 feet below 
the existing ground surface. It should be noted that groundwater levels may change due 
to tidal fluctuations, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration and Logs of Borings are provided in 
Appendix A. Results of the laboratory tests performed on selected samples are presented 
in Appendix B.

Field Percolation Testing 
A field percolation test was performed in general accordance with the State of 
Hawaii, Department of Health’s Administrative Rules, Chapter 11-62-31.2, “Wastewater 
Systems” and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards to evaluate the permeability of the 
subsurface soils for wastewater disposal.  The percolation test was performed by drilling 
a 5.5-inch diameter borehole to a depth of 10 feet below the existing ground surface.   
Approximately one inch of gravel was then placed at the bottom of the borehole to 
protect the bottom of the hole from scouring and sediments. During testing, the hole was 
carefully filled with water to a minimum depth of 6 inches above the gravel pack. The time 
intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which the calculation of 
the percolation test was based. Field percolation test results are summarized in the 
following table. In addition, the completed Site Evaluation/Percolation Test Form is 
presented as Plate 3.

Based on the State of Hawaii, Department of Health’s Administrative Rules, 
Chapter 11-62-34, seepage pits should be constructed in soils having a percolation rate 
faster than 10 minutes per inch. A percolation rate on the order of about 0.33 minutes per 
inch was obtained for the test conducted in the sand encountered near the proposed 
seepage pit location.  Therefore, it is our opinion that the infiltration characteristics of the 
sand are suitable for the use of a seepage pit. 
Septic Tank 
We anticipate that the bottom of the new septic tank will be about 7 feet below the 
existing ground surface. Installation of the new septic tank will the involve removal of existing 
pavement and excavation into loose to medium dense coralline sand to the bottom of the 
septic tank excavation. 
To provide uniform support and a firm bearing condition for the septic tank, we 
recommend placing 1 foot of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) wrapped 
in a non-woven filter fabric, such as Miraf 180N or equivalent, below the septic tank. An 
allowable bearing pressure of 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used to design

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and discussions submitted herein are based, in part, upon information 
obtained from the field borings and percolation test. Variations of subsoil conditions between 
and beyond the borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not 
become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be 
necessary to re-evaluate the findings and discussions provided herein. 
The locations of the field borings indicated in this report are approximate, having 
been estimated by taping from the features shown on the Site Layout and Utility Plan, 
prepared by Bryce E. Uyehara, A.I.A., Inc., dated June 2023. Elevations of the field borings 
were interpolated using the spot elevations shown on the same plan. The physical locations 
and elevations of the borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by 
the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on graphic representations of the borings depict the 
approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual transition.  
Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the graphic 
representations and/or in the text herein. These data have been reviewed and 
interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, it must be noted that 
fluctuation may occur due to variations in rainfall, tides, temperature, and other factors.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling two borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 10 and 16.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous 
flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in 
general accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Graphic 
presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plate B-1. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected soil sample to 
evaluate the shear strength parameters.  The test results are presented on Plate B-2. 
One set of Corrosivity test, including pH (ASTM G51), Chloride Content (EPA 
300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), was performed by Geolabs, Inc. and Eurofins 
TestAmerica on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-3.
