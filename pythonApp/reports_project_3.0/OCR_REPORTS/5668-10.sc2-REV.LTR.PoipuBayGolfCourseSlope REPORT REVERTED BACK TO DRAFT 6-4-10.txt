This letter report presents the findings from our updated preliminary short term
slope stability evaluation conducted at the Poipu Bay Golf Course in Poipu on the Island of

reconnaissance of the project site A previous short term slope stability evaluation was

The intent of this evaluation is to provide the owner with updated information about
the anticipated short term stability of a portion of sea cliff adjacent to the golf course
facility The preliminary findings presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the
end of this letter report
Project Considerations
The project site is at the existing Poipu Bay Golf Course which is in the coastal
Poipu area on the Island of Kauai Hawaii A large multiple block type slope failure
occurred in March 2006 along a section of steep sea cliffs bordering the golf course The
cliff failure occurred following an extended period of very heavy rainfall in March 2006 The
collapsed portion of the cliff is within the existing shoreline conservation zone The
collapse area involved the full height of the sea cliff and extended approximately 300 feet
long by about 25 feet wide

Geologic Conditions
The project site is along a section of the southeast facing coastline in the Poipu
area on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The coastline fronting the golf course is comprised of
tall sea cliffs composed of lithified dune sand sandstone overlying weathered basaltic
rock formation of the Koloa Volcanic Series The geologic contact between the weathered
basalt rock formation and overlying coralline sandstone formation is sharp and is near the
elevation of the present day mean sea level The coastline is exposed to strong ocean
swells that focus on the exposed weathered basalt sandstone geologic unit contact The
ocean wave action has created erosional notches undercuts by eroding the weaker
highly weathered basaltic rock material underlying the sandstone cliffs
Evidence of continual notch undercutting and resulting large block type rockfall
failures along the sandstone cliff is real as evidenced by accumulations of fallen blocks of
sandstone lining the length of the shoreline In general it appears that where the fallen
blocks of rock are present they should provide some natural armoring of the cliff base The
natural armoring should help reduce ocean wave attack and material erosion In addition it
is our opinion that the cliff stability may improve locally following the release of
overhanging blocks and the resultant decrease in cantilevered load
Based on our observations the shoreline cliffs adjacent to the 15th and 16th Holes
represent a dynamic and evolving geological condition The steep cliffs and boulder strewn
shoreline exhibit a history of large block type rockfall slope failures The erosion and
retreat of the exposed cliff face is anticipated to continue with the passage of time
Background of 2006 Landslide
On March 27 2006 after an extended period of heavy flooding a large slope failure
involving a substantial span of sea cliff approximately 300 lineal feet long and about 20 to
30 feet wide fell into the ocean The slope failure released several very large blocks of
coralline sandstone rock that were deposited along the toe of the cliff As a result of the
slope failure avulsion the top edge of the cliff is currently about 10 to 30 feet inland from
the pre existing cliff face The approximate location of the slope avulsion area is shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2
The slope failure resulted in the loss of a portion of the existing top of cliff shoreline
access trail within the existing shoreline conservation zone The slope failure occurred
adjacent to the existing 15th Green and 16th Tees A portion of the existing cart path
traverses within approximately 18 lineal feet inland from the new top of cliff After the
landslide temporary fencing and warning signage was erected in an effort to limit public
access
The slope failure eliminated most of the unstable sandstone rock overhang that was
above the basalt sandstone geologic contact at the toe of the cliff The landslide created a
new cliff slope that appears to stand at a typical inclination of about one quarter horizontal

Site Reconnaissance
Our site reconnaissance was conducted on to observe the existing
site conditions with respect to potential shoreline slope instability The purpose of our
updated reconnaissance was to evaluate the probable zone s of potential cliff instability
for visual changes in the site conditions and to render an opinion for short term less than
about 1 year safe setback and public access along the top of the cliff
The reconnaissance was conducted by our geologist and engineer at low tide
under generally cloudy weather with some sporadic heavy rain showers The area
encompassed by our reconnaissance spanned approximately 1 800 linear feet along the
top of cliff adjacent to the 15th and 16th Holes In addition the reconnaissance
encompassed a short portion of the rugged shoreline accessible by a foot traverse
extending towards the east from the existing rock heiau at the west end of the study area
It should be noted that foot access along the length of the bottom of the cliff is restricted by
large fallen blocks of rock and rough ocean conditions Shoreline access by foot traverse
is considered unsafe easterly of the 16th Red Tee because of the rough wave swept
shoreline conditions
Topographic Base Map

height were conducted Based on the field measurements we determined that the
topographic map used previously in our analysis was based on an assumed elevation
datum therefore the previous plotted elevations do not reflect the actual ground surface
elevations in relation to a sea level datum

Top of Slope Reconnaissance
Based on our reconnaissance along the top of the cliff visible signs of imminent
slope failure such as ground subsidence or ground cracking were not observed We
observed that vegetation had been trimmed to expose the ground surface adjacent to the
active and abandoned 16th Gold Tees Based on our observations we encountered no
visible discernable evidence of significant changes to the top of the cliff conditions we
previously observed adjacent to the 15th and 16th Holes
It should be noted that possible future large block type cliff failures are anticipated
to occur The failures are anticipated to occur as sudden events with minimal advance
warning such as visible signs of developing stress cracks or ground subsidence therefore
visual review of the site conditions may be of only limited use in the prediction of when and
where a new slope failure may occur However the implementation of a survey monitoring
program to establish ground surface control points may assist in detecting small visually
imperceptible elevation changes and lateral ground movements that could precede
large scale ground movement provided the data is collected at relatively short time
intervals and reviewed by experienced personnel in a timely manner
The existing exposed sandstone cliffs appear to be generally unchanged and
continue standing at sub vertical to one quarter horizontal to one vertical
inclinations Portions of the cliff that do not appear to have experienced failure in a longer
period of time typically stand vertically with some slight seaward overhang The cliff height
spanning between the 16th Tees and the 15th Fairway generally ranges between about
50 and 80 feet above the ocean Localized loose and unstable blocks of sandstone were
observed at multiple locations along the crest of the slope adjacent to the 15th and 16th
Holes The loose unstable blocks pose a substantial risk and danger to persons accessing
the shoreline along the base of the cliff A substantial precariously perched boulder with
limited basal support was observed at the crest of the cliff at the approximate location
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The boulder is estimated to be about 12 feet in length by
about 6 feet in width and appears to be at high risk for detachment
We observed an existing temporary fence line spanning the area just makai of the
abandoned 16th Gold Tee and active 16th Gold Tee The wire safety fence is aligned to
limit public access along the top of the cliff The existing temporary fence length is not as
extensive as previously suggested by Geolabs on Exhibit A Temporary Fence Layout
Plan dated June 2006

Bottom of Slope Reconnaissance
Based on our reconnaissance significant new cliff avulsion evidenced by additional
fallen blocks of rock or fresh rock exposures was not observed The maximum cliff
undercut depth of 22 feet measured beneath the accessible portion of the cliff remains
essentially unchanged from our prior observation However the approximate location of
the previously measured undercut is revised and is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Furthermore the approximate limit of our shoreline traverse below the existing Red 16th
Tee is also shown on Plate 2
Based on our reconnaissance and review of available aerial photographs provided
by Kawailoa in 2006 we believe the measured maximum depth of cliff undercut 22 feet
may be used as a reasonable estimate of the potential maximum undercut depth residing
at other cliff undercut locations visible on the aerial photographs The aerial photographs
used in our evaluation are presented on Plates 3 and 4
Based on our reconnaissance and review of available aerial photographs there
appears to be two primary sections of cliff that exhibit some potential for future large block
failure The two primary areas of concern are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Based on
our observations the first cliff area appears to span about 550 linear feet in length and
appears to encompass the cliffs adjacent to the existing five Tees at the 16th Hole The cliff
has a relatively high potential for large block type slope failure due to the existing
cantilevered condition of the sandstone rock mass and the relatively open exposure to
wave erosion at the sandstone basalt formation interface The cliff undercut of about
22 feet was measured at the western end of this cliff section Other cliff areas westerly of
the 16th Tees appear to have a lower risk for large block type slope failure due to the
observed lower magnitude undercutting on the order of about 5 feet or less and better
natural armoring of the shoreline protection by large boulder debris
Based on our observations the second cliff area appears to span at least about
350 linear feet in length and appears to encompass the cliff adjacent to a portion of the
15th Fairway as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 This area of potential cliff instability is not
accessible from the shore nor is it clearly visible from the top of the cliff The undercut cliff
area was identified using the available aerial photographs Based on our review of the
aerial photographs the cliff undercut conditions appears to be similar to the undercut
visible below the 16th Tees As a result we have included this area in our current
evaluation
Aerial photographs of the project site recorded in 2006 and depicting the general
conditions described in this report are presented on Plates 3 and 4

Limitations
This report is based in part on a visual assessment of the exposed and accessible
areas of the project site It is not possible to observe all of the existing site conditions
during a brief reconnaissance and literature review The preliminary findings and
discussion submitted in this report are based in part upon information obtained from the
available topographic survey prepared by others our site reconnaissance and literature
review Variations of surface and subsurface conditions from those described in this report
may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until a
geotechnical engineering exploration is conducted at the site Our services were
performed in accordance with generally accepted standards of geotechnical engineering
practice no warranty is expressed or implied
The findings presented in this report are considered preliminary and should be used
only for the short term less than about 1 year evaluation of the slope stability at the
project site Geological processes such as erosion and weathering are natural on going
evolutionary processes that may be dependent upon other significant external natural
factors such as seismic disturbance storm events and tsunami activity As a result the
site conditions may change with the passage of time and the preliminary findings
discussed in this report may require re evaluation
