SLOPE STABILITY
Based on our past experience slopes that are susceptible to movements or
failures are usually underlain by highly plastic clay soils These clay soils which are
classified as CH according to the Unified Soil Classification System locally referred
to as Adobe Clay exhibit high swelling shrinkage potential and will lose considerable
shear strength when saturated When the CH clay soils experience significant
downslope movement shear slip surfaces will then develop which will further reduce
the shear strength along the slip surfaces to a very low residual shear value Our
three boring field exploration work performed at the subject lot indicated that this type
of Adobe Clay which is a major component in the driving forces for slope failures
was not encountered within the drilled depths of the borings performed at the subject
site
Stability of the lower portion of the subject house lot would also be affected by
the performance of the existing retaining wall that provides the grade separation
between the subject lot and the adjacent Waiomao Stream at the southeastern side of
the property Based on our understanding and field observations the retaining wall has
been in existence for a long time possibly over 60 years and it appears to be in
relatively stable condition with no observable sign of distress or movement In addition
it is noted that the stream bank in front of the retaining wall is either heavily vegetated
or large boulders are present to reduce the potential stream bank erosion problem that
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 3282 00 Page 7
may jeopardize the future stability of the retaining wall Based on the above
observations and our field exploration results it is our opinion that the subject lot should
be relatively stable against any future slope movement or failure
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicates that the site is underlain by very stiff residual soils
over dense saprolites and hard basalt formation at deeper depths Based on the
competent subsoil conditions encountered at the project site it is our opinion that
conventional shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings may
be used for support of the proposed single family residence
Laboratory tests performed on the on site clayey soils indicate that the material
generally has a moderate shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations
Therefore we recommend that concrete slabs on grade placed over this clayey
subgrade soil be supported directly on a layer of capping fill consisting of non
expansive select borrow to reduce the potential effects of the moderately expansive
near surface soils Detailed recommendations are presented in the following sections
Foundations
Detailed information pertaining to the structural loadings were not available to us
at the time of preparation of this report Because of the generally competent subsoil
conditions encountered at the site we believe that spread and or continuous footings
may be used for support of the proposed new residence

used for design of footing foundations bearing on the stiff in situ soils encountered at

differential
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure

Slab On Grade
The project site is underlain by a deposit of clayey silt soils which are
considered to be moderately expansive when subjected to moisture fluctuations
Unless floor slabs supported by these soils are properly designed there is a potential
for future distress to lightly loaded slab on grade construction resulting from the
shrinking and swelling of these soils due to changes in the moisture content To
reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from the swelling of the on site
moderately expansive subgrade soils we recommend that the concrete slab on grade
be supported on 18 inches of non expansive select fill material The non expansive
select fill should extend at least 2 feet beyond the perimeter of the proposed structure
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3282 00 Page 10
The select fill material should be placed as soon as possible after the
scarification and recompaction of the existing subgrade soils The prepared capping
fill and the underlying subgrade soil should be wetted and kept moist prior to final
placement of concrete Subgrade soils should be prepared in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Site Grading section of this report The select fill
would serve as a capping fill layer of moist soil above the underlying potentially
expansive subgrade soils and this capping fill would also reduce the potential loss of
moisture from the prepared moist subgrade soils The reduction in future subgrade
saturation should reduce the potential swelling of the underlying moderately expansive
clayey soils Compaction requirements for the subgrade soils and select fill are
discussed in the Site Grading section of this report
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM
C 33 No 67 gradation cushion layer be installed below the slab to provide uniform
support To reduce future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to the floor
coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is
recommended on top of the gravel cushion layer Two inches of moist fine sand may
be placed above the plastic membrane to provide protection for the membrane and to
aid in curing of the slab concrete The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any
unpaved area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
Where the slab is subjected to vehicular traffic such as the garage slab a 6 inch
layer of base course compacted to 95 percent relative compaction is recommended in
lieu of the No 3B Fine gravel capillary break layer Provisions should be made for
proper load transfer across the slab joints which are subjected to vehicular traffic
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer of No 3B Fine gravel be

slab and pavement areas may be reduced to 12 inches if some amount of slab cracking
can be tolerated Construction joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width

Site Grading
Detailed information pertaining to the site grading work were not available at the
time of preparation of this report The following recommendations are general
guidelines for design of the grading and for the development of general specifications
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of off site
Slabs and foundations of the existing structures that are to be demolished should
be completely removed Any over excavation resulting from the demolition should be
backfilled with compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned
should be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with 6
to 8 inch thick horizontal lifts of compacted select granular fill material Utilities that are
to be abandoned in place under structure should be backfilled by pumping lean
concrete under low pressure
After demolition clearing and grubbing all areas beneath future slabs and
pavement areas should be over excavated to a depth of at least 18 inches below the
future slab or pavement subgrade level i e bottom of No 3B Fine gravel cushion layer
or bottom of base course layer If zones of soft loose or saturated soils are
encountered at the over excavated subgrade level deeper over excavation may be
required to expose firm material The resulting excavation should be backfilled with
compacted select granular fill material The clayey material resulting from the over
excavation should be removed and properly disposed of off site or stockpiled for use
in yard or landscaping areas
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3282 00 Page 14
Areas to receive fills should be scarified to a depth of 10 inches moisture
conditioned to at least 2 percent above optimum moisture content and compacted to
at least 90 percent relative compaction Any fills to be placed on existing slopes having
ratios steeper than five horizontal to one vertical 5H 1V should be keyed and benched
into firm in situ soil or weathered basalt formation to lessen potential instability of the
new fill The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above recommendations for fill
placement A two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V cut and fill slopes are
recommended
Select granular fill material for fills and backfills under the slab and pavement
areas should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Select granular fill material
should consist of imported granular material such as crushed coral basalt gravel or
cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles
greater than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a laboratory
California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of not more
than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It
should contain less than 20 percent passing the No 200 sieve Any imported fill
material should be tested and approved by Geolabs Hawaii prior to delivery to the
project site for its intended use The on site expansive clayey soils generated from the
grading work may be used only for fills in yard or landscape areas

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the project A granular
bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
is recommended under the pipes Self compacting granular materials such as No 3B

relative compaction

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings to depths ranging from 23 5 to 46 5 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings
were drilled using both truck mounted drill rig and portable drilling equipment
The material encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer who monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered are
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18
inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring
Logs as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of
the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals

Six 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate the

cohesion of the soil sample Results of the direct shear test is presented on Plate B 2
