2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age
and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct
while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau
lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad
plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
Puu Makakilo is a cinder and spatter cone that was built by one or more eruptive
events which produced large quantities of pyroclastic material such as cinder spatter
and ash In some areas welding and or hypothermal action has fused or cemented the
pyroclastic material into weak and strong rock e g tuff
In addition to the pyroclastic material the eruptions produced lava flows of very
dense basalt which are interbedded with the pyroclastic materials Structurally
Puu Makakilo is a heterogenous mixture of interbedded cinder tuff and dense basalt
The units are discontinuous and difficult to differentiate on an areal basis in the field
In situ chemical weathering of the pyroclastic material and basalts has produced
a surface layer of residual soil which mantles most of the project site This residual soil
grades rapidly to the underlying basalt formation

Seismic Refraction Geophysical Surveys
Seismic refraction geophysical surveys are often conducted to supplement and
enhance traditional subsurface geotechnical exploration such as test borings The
refraction surveys may provide quick and affordable general subsurface
characterization of the strata thickness and lateral continuity and an estimation of
the rock rippability for excavation However the seismic refraction methods do rely
on some simplified assumptions that may limit its application in some settings
Hawaii California
SECTION 2 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
In general the seismic refraction geophysical exploration method involves using a
signal enhancement seismograph and seismic data processing software to record
and analyze an induced seismic energy source expressed as compressional
P waves which travel through and refract on layered subsurface geologic
mediums consisting of varying density and hardness Seismic energy travels with a
compressional velocity that is characteristic of the density porosity structure and
water content of each distinguishable geologic layer encountered The primary
constraint of the P wave refraction method assumes the subsurface geologic
layers increase in material density generation of higher P wave velocities with
increasing depth The refraction method is not effective where the materials P
wave velocity decreases with increasing depth a condition known as a velocity
inversion Normally over 80 percent of natural geological settings conform to
increasing material density corresponding with increasing depth of burial
Our seismic refraction survey incorporated the use of a Geometrics Smartseis
S 12 a 12 channel seismograph coupled with an array of 12 geophone sensors
The seismic energy source was induced using a 12 pound sledge hammer striking
a metal plate coupled with the ground surface at selected source shot points The
geophone array consisted of 12 individual geophones spread out at 10 foot
intervals Five discreet shot locations were utilized at each survey location to trigger
the seismograph recording of the seismic energy response at the geophones At
each shot location approximately 6 to 18 hammer strikes were conducted to stack
the recorded wave forms in an effort to filter out random environmental noise such
as vehicle traffic and other potential signal interferences Processing and
interpretation of the field data records included the use of Rimrock Geophysics SIP
seismic refraction interpretation software which was used to generate first break
wave form picks assignment of geologic layers and calculation of P wave
velocities and to generate the graphic presentation of the data interpretation in an
idealized profile format
Five seismic refraction surveys were performed at selected locations of the project
site in an effort to supplement the information obtained from the test borings The

3 9 Utility Trenches
We envision new utility lines and utility connections may be required In general
good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the
trenches for the new site utilities The method and equipment to be used for trench
excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety
considerations In addition the excavations should comply with the applicable federal
state and local safety requirements Trench shoring design and installation should be
the contractor s responsibility
Based on the results of our field exploration it is likely most of the utility trench
excavations will encounter weathered rock formation as described in the logs of borings
which may pose some excavation difficulties during the trench excavation In addition
the contractor should anticipate significant over break of the sides of the trench
excavations considering the nature of the materials at the site
In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes for support
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction
effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to
use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the

haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper
trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of excavated on site soils The
backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed

3 10 Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Surface water ponding should
not be allowed on site during or after construction Well defined drainage swales should
be established and maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the house slab
and foundations Blockage or clogging of these drainage features could cause water
ponding at the house structure and foundations resulting in potential foundation and
house distress
It is highly recommended gutter systems be installed around the buildings and
discharge be diverted away from foundation and driveway areas Excessive landscape
watering near foundations should be avoided Planters next to foundations should be
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration
into subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after the
concrete sets to reduce the potential for water infiltration In addition drainage swales

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Nine 1 inch Ring Swell tests were performed on samples of the remolded soils to
evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are presented
on Plate B 1
Five Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification
Results of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depth Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Five Unconfined Compression strength of cohesion soil tests ASTM D 2166
were performed on selected samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strengths
of the underlying clayey soil The test results are presented on Plates B 3 1 through
B 3 5
Two sets of laboratory soil Resistivity tests ASTM G 57 and three sets of
pH tests AASHTO T 289 were performed to determine the resistivity and pH of the soil
and identify conditions under which the corrosion of metals in the soil The test results
are presented on Plate B 4
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed
on bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The results of the test are presented on Plate B 5
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1883 was performed on bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plate B 6

APPENDIX C
Seismic Refraction Geophysical Survey
APPENDIX C
Seismic Refraction Geophysical Survey
The subsurface conditions at the project site were also explored by the
performance of five seismic refraction survey lines performed during September 2005
The approximate locations of the seismic refraction surveys are shown on the General
designated as SR 1 through SR 5
Refraction seismography is based on the transmission of elastic energy
compressional waves through geologic materials The seismic refraction surveys were
conducted using a 12 channel signal enhancement seismograph with a 12 pound
sledge hammer as a seismic energy source Generally the denser or more
consolidated a material is the faster the transmission of the compressional waves
These waves are refracted at interfaces between materials with contrasting densities
The refracted waves are received by geophones on the surface and recorded by the
operator
The recorded data is input into a desktop computer for processing which
involves the interpretive selection of the arrival times of the refracted waves After the
interpretive selection of arrival times the technique permits mathematical calculations
that yield approximate depths or thickness of materials below the survey lines and
provide their approximate compressional wave velocities The velocities are averaged
along the travel path of the compressional waves The calculated velocities may be
empirically related to the excavation characteristics of the materials
The typical estimated seismic velocities for the various materials underlying the
site are classified as follows A contractor wishing to bid on this project should retain a
qualified professional to assist in the interpretation of these geophysical survey results

Stiff to Very Stiff Residual Soil 960 to 1 500
