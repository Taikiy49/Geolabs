2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and forms
the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking
against its eastern flank Both Waianae and Koolau Ranges have lost most of their
original shield outlines and are now long narrow ridges shaped largely by erosion
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of
lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of
jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred during the last 1 to 2 million years The
weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils and saprolite In general
saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of
volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with depth

3 2 Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed building
structures Based on the existing topography and the proposed finish floor elevation we
anticipate that about 2 to 4 feet of cuts and up to 40 feet of fills will be required to achieve
the proposed finished subgrade The near surface soils encountered at the proposed site
consist of clayey silt and silty clay which exhibit a low to moderate expansion potential
when subjected to moisture fluctuations Therefore to reduce the potential for structural
distress to lightly loaded slabs we recommend that a minimum of 12 inches of
non expansive select granular fill material be provided below the concrete slab The select
fill material should be extended beyond the footprint of the new slab on grade areas by
minimum 24 inches The subgrades for slabs on grades should be scarified to a depth of
at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
The non expansive select granular fill recommended below the floor slabs should
consist of imported granular material such as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder sand
The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a laboratory California Bearing
Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve All select granular fill materials should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least
95 percent relative compaction The slab subgrade should be kept moist until covered by
base course

ranging between about 10 and 40 feet high will be required for the project In addition the
retaining wall will need to retain the fill materials that are supporting the foundations of the
building Based on these conditions we recommend the use of a segmental retaining wall

wall system is likely one of the most economical retaining wall systems for the project
considering the significant heights of the walls

density polyethylene or other reinforcing elements to reinforce the backfill zone and
improve the shear strength of the reinforced soil zone This composite system essentially
forms a gravity wall structure with an ability to tolerate significant total and differential

3 5 Liquefaction Potential
Liquefaction occurs when a build up of excess pore water pressure is induced on
saturated cohesionless soils resulting in a substantial loss of strength The most common
cause of this phenomenon is the cyclic loading due to earthquakes This process may
cause both vertical and horizontal movements in the soil and if not confined can result in
significant deformations Loose uniformly graded fine grained sands and loose silts with
little cohesion are most susceptible to liquefaction Generally liquefaction is not likely to
occur if the loose deposit is deeper than 50 feet below the ground surface The greater
overburden pressure can be sufficient to prevent liquefaction in deeper deposits
Based on our field exploration the project site is mostly underlain by very stiff to
hard cohesive soils Additionally the natural groundwater table is greater than 50 feet
below the site Therefore we believe that there is no potential for liquefaction at this
project site

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B 1
Three sieve analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on Plate B 2
Five direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct
shear tests are presented on Plates B 3 through B 7
Five laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of in situ soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils
The test results are presented on Plates B 8 through B 12
One Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plate B 13
Two consolidation tests with time rates ASTM D 2435 were performed on
samples of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics
of the materials encountered Results of the consolidation test are presented on
Plates B 14 and B 15
Eight Collapse Tests ASTM D 5333 were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the collapsibility characteristics of the soils The test results are plotted on
Hawaii California

remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils under
different surcharge loads The results of these tests are summarized on Plates B 17 1 and
B 17 2
Three sets of corrosion tests including minimum resistivity
chloride content and sulfate content were
performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration The results of
these tests are summarized on Plate B 18

APPENDIX C
Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the on site soils was evaluated by performing two falling head
permeability tests at depths of about 5 and 10 feet below the existing ground surface The
locations of the field permeability tests designated as Boring Nos 110 and 111 are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
During the falling head test the borehole was carefully filled with water to the top of
the hole The time intervals and water drops in the borehole were then recorded to
provide data upon which to base calculation of the permeability values of the subsurface
soils The test results of the field permeability tests are presented on Plates C 1 and C 2
