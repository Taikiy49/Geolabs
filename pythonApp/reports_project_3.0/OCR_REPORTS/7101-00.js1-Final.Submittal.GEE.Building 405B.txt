SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the site consists of asphaltic concrete about 
7 inches thick. Below the pavement, fills consisting of stiff sandy silt and medium dense 
silty sand and gravel extending to depths between about 5 and 7.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface, were encountered. The fills were underlain by lagoonal deposits 
consisting of very soft to soft silty clay and clayey silts extending to depths of about 80 feet 
below the existing ground surface. Below the lagoonal deposits, stiff alluvial deposits 
consisting of clayey silt soils were encountered in the borings.  We encountered 
groundwater in the borings at depths between about 6.9 and 7.7 feet below the existing 
ground surface at the time of our field exploration. It should be noted that the groundwater 
levels are subject to change due to tidal fluctuations, seasonal precipitation, and other 
factors. 
The existing asphalt concrete within Building 405B has undergone appreciable 
settlements. According to the project RFP documents, the asphaltic concrete floor has 
undergone settlements up to 3.5 feet since the 1940’s. The settlements were due to 
consolidation of the soft lagoonal deposits caused by the weight of the fill. We believe the 
primary consolidation settlements are complete. However, due to the low in-situ strength 
characteristics and the relatively large thickness of the lagoonal deposits, a small amount 
of secondary consolidation (on the order of 1-inch) may occur over the next 20 to 40 years. 
Most of the existing interior asphaltic concrete pavement is in fair to good 
condition. The existing pavement is about 7 inches thick which is thicker than required 
for the design traffic loading. Therefore, the pavement can be rehabilitated by cold 
planning 2 inches and overlaying with 2 inches of new asphalt concrete. 
In areas where the existing pavement section is removed due to soft, weak, and 
yielding areas, or cavities, we recommend replacement with a new asphaltic concrete 
section of 5.0 inches of asphaltic concrete over 6.0 inches of aggregate base course 
placed over the existing subgrade compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. 
We understand that a new interior reinforced concrete slab with thickened-edge 
footings will be required to support the renovated toilet room. In general, we believe that 
an allowable bearing pressure of up to about 1,000 psf may be used for the design of 
the foundations for the new toilet room. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads 
and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by 
wind or seismic forces.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The 
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) 
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The 
Koolau Volcano reached the end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years 
ago. 
The geologic conditions underlying the Pearl Harbor area and the project site are 
the result of a complex series of deposition and erosional events brought on by 
glacio-eustatic sea level changes and post-erosional volcanic activities during the 
Pleistocene Epoch. Following the cessation of volcanic activity of the Koolau Volcano, 
alluvial soil materials were deposited in a deltaic or estuarine environment in the 
Pearl Harbor area. 
The sea level fluctuated several times, receding perhaps 300 feet below and 
rising about 100 feet above the present sea level. During the Recent Epoch, as sea 
level rose to its current stand, the area was covered with soft and loose lagoonal marine 
deposits. These deposits were subsequently covered by man-made fills placed during 
the middle portion of this century for land reclamation projects during construction of the 
existing Pearl Harbor area.

2.4 
Seismic Design Considerations 
The project site will be subjected to seismic activity and should be evaluated for 
potential soil liquefaction. Seismic design of the proposed project will be based on the 
Unified Facilities Criteria “Seismic Design of Building” (UFC 3-310-04), which references 
the 2012 International Building Code (IBC 2012) and UFC Structural Load Data 
(UFC 3-310-01). The following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity and 
soil profile type for seismic design. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic 
activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so 
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of 
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of 
Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of 
Hawaii.

Site Preparation 
Existing asphaltic pavement to be demolished should be completely removed. 
Over-excavations resulting from the demolition and sinkholes should be backfilled with 
flowable or compacted granular fill material. Soft and/or yielding areas encountered 
during removal of the existing asphaltic and concrete slabs should be over-excavated to 
expose firm and/or dense materials, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled 
with compacted fills. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly 
disposed of off-site or where appropriate.  
After removing the existing asphaltic pavement, the subgrade should be scarified 
to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

well-graded from coarse to fine with particles less than 3 inches in largest dimension. In 
addition, the materials also should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing 
the No. 200 sieve. The materials should have a California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 
20 or higher and a swell value of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D1883.

3.4 
Ground Settlements 
The existing asphaltic concrete pavements within Building 405B have undergone 
appreciable settlements. According to the project RFP documents, the asphaltic 
concrete floor has undergone settlements up to 3.5 feet since the 1940’s. In general, 
ground settlements are anticipated when substantial new fills or structural loads are 
placed over a soil profile of thick, very soft to soft lagoonal deposits. In general, the 
existing ground settlements are primarily the result of the following two processes: 
compression of the compacted fill material under its own weight; and 
consolidation and/or compression of the underlying in-situ soils induced by 
fills and structural loading.  
The renovation and repair of Building 405B will consist of rehabilitating or 
replacing the existing pavement with new asphaltic concrete. We envision that the new 
interior asphaltic concrete pavement will be supported on the near-surface fill material. 
Based on information contained in the RFP documents, the existing fill was placed over 
74 years ago. In order to minimize additional loads on the soft deposits, we recommend 
that the finished floor elevation remain close to the existing elevation so that there will 
not be additional fills and structural loading acting on the soft lagoonal deposits 
significantly beyond what the deposits had previously experienced. By maintaining the 
same finished floor elevations, we believe the existing soft lagoonal deposit will not

experience a large vertical stress increase, which would lead to additional substantial 
ground settlements. 
In general, consolidation takes place in two phases, comprised of primary and 
secondary consolidation. Primary consolidation takes place after an increase in vertical 
stress is applied to the soil. During primary consolidation, excess pore pressures build 
up within the soil and will slowly dissipate resulting in settlement. Secondary 
consolidation occurs after primary consolidation when excess pore pressure has 
dissipated. During the secondary consolidation stage, soil particles within the soil 
structure will slowly yield and compress. The rate at which secondary consolidation 
occurs largely depends on the soil type. Typically, consolidation of a lagoonal deposit 
may take a substantial amount of time due to its low permeability characteristics. 
Based on the consolidation tests and the length of time the existing fill and 
structural loads have been in place, we believe that the thick lagoonal deposits have 
reached the end of the primary consolidation phase and are in secondary consolidation. 
Due to the low in-situ strength characteristics and the relatively large thickness of the 
lagoonal deposits, a small amount (estimated on the order of 1-inch) of secondary 
consolidation may occur over the next 20 to 40 years.

Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, 
No. 67 gradation), also should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 
12 inches above the pipes or about 12 inches above the groundwater level to provide 
adequate support around the pipes. It is critical to use the free-draining materials 
around the pipes to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of 
pipes and to provide adequate support around the sides of the pipes. Improper trench 
backfill around the pipe could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes 
or the groundwater level to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the 
on-site soils having particles less than 3 inches in largest dimension or select granular 
fill material. The backfill should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
placed in about 8-inch loose lifts, and mechanically compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. Where trenches are located in paved areas, the upper 
3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement surface should be compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same 
soil determined in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Mechanical compaction equipment should be used to compact the backfill materials. 
Compaction efforts by water tamping, jetting, or ponding should not be allowed.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 103, extending to depths between about 
53.0 feet and 103.0 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. We used a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools to drill the borings. 
Our geologist observed the drilling operations on a near-continuous basis and 
classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and textural examination in 
the field. These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and testing 
in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil 
Classification System, as shown on Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil 
classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil Classification 
Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are 
presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in 
general accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving 
a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Test results are summarized on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. Test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
test results are provided on Plate B-1. 
One Fines Content test (ASTM D1140) was conducted on a selected soil sample to 
evaluate the percent passing the No. 200 sieve. The test was performed in accordance 
with ASTM D1140. The test result is summarized on Plate B-2 and on the Logs of Borings 
at the appropriate sample depth. 
Five Consolidation tests (ASTM D2435) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the on-site soils. The test results are 
plotted on Plates B-3 through B-7. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C136 & D1140) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils 
and to aid in soil classification.  Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are 
provided on Plate B-8. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationship. The test results are presented on Plate B-9.  
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D1883) were 
performed on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-10 and B-11.
