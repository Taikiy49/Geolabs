This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Wildland Fire Fighting HPD Dip Tank Service Area project at the
Lualualei Valley site in the District of Waianae on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations derived
from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses The
recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of concrete pad

findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at
the end of this report

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau Volcano reached the
end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
The project site is situated in Lualualei Valley along the southern base of
Paheehee Ridge a high ridge extending from Puu Paheehee on the makai side to
Mauna Kuwale on the mauka side Lualualei Valley was formed by the lower and middle
members of the Waianae Volcanic Series during the Tertiary Period Erosion has since
removed most of the western slope of the Waianae shield and exposed the internal
structure of the volcano This erosion formed the large broad Lualualei Valley which is
filled with substantial accumulations of alluvium and colluvium The older consolidated
alluvium of the western portion of the Waianae Range appears to be graded to various
elevations reflecting higher stands of the sea during the Pleistocene Epoch These alluvial
deposits are largely a result of stream deposits and to a lesser extent mud flows Toward
the mouth of the valley the non marine alluvium grades to marine deposits of stratified
clay with lenticular beds of sand and gravel
Eustatic fluctuations of sea level in the Hawaiian Islands have caused changes in
the shoreline in the past The alluvial deposits in the valley and abutting the
Waianae Range grade in part to former high sea levels and are being slowly removed by
present day stream erosion Weathering of the upper portions of the ridges has led to the
accumulation of colluvium found at their bases

Site Description
The project site is an undeveloped area located to the north of the northern
terminus of Kuwale Road near Paheehee Ridge in the Lualualei Valley in the District of
Waianae on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
Based on our field observations the project site is generally barren land with a
sparse cover of vegetation Some large boulders and scattered trees are observed in
the proximity of the project site Based on the topographic survey map the project site
is slightly sloping upward towards the north with existing ground surface elevations
ranging from about 106 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the beginning of the new
all weather surface AC access road to about 180 feet MSL at the proposed dip tank
pad area

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
placed directly around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of
the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also
be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of compacted on site
materials or free draining compacted fills where specified by the designer Unless
covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should also consist of
low expansion relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for excessive
water infiltration behind the walls

should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that conventional excavation
methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation The contractor should
determine the method and equipment for trench excavation subject to practical limits
and safety considerations In addition the excavations should comply with the
applicable federal state and local safety requirements The contractor should be
responsible for trench shoring design and installation
In general we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or clean sand for support under the
pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation or clean sand should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to
about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes It is
critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site soils with a
maximum particle size of 6 inches or select granular fill material The backfill should be
placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground
subsidence Where trenches are located below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the

Soil Corrosion
We performed one set of laboratory corrosion tests including pH and Minimum
Resistivity to evaluate the corrosivity of the near surface sandy clay soils encountered
at the project site The test results are presented on Plate B 4 of Appendix B The
minimum resistivity value of the near surface soil sample tested is on the order of about
1 200 ohm cm This minimum resistivity value correspond to Corrosion Rating of 2
very corrosive based on the guidelines provided by the City County of Honolulu
Board of Water Supply Therefore we recommend properly designing all metallic
substructures such as ductile iron water lines in contact with the soils with protection
against potential corrosion A corrosion engineer should be consulted for detailed
recommendations on corrosion protection
3 7
Drainage
The finished grades outside the dip tank pad and retaining structures should be
sloped to shed water away from foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for
ponding Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be
avoided Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to
drain surface run off away from the slabs and foundations
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface clayey
soils The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings
immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration
