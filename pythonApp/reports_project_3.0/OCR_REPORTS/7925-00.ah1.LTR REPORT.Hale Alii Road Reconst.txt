PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The proposed project is within the Joint Base Pearl Harbor-Hickam on the Island 
of Oahu, Hawaii. We understand that an existing service road adjacent to Hale Alii 
Avenue exhibiting pavement distresses and low spots will be reconstructed. The 
existing service road diverges to the south from Hale Alii Avenue (near the Hale Alii 
Lodging), bends toward the northwest and proceeds for about 1,380 linear feet parallel 
to Hale Alii Avenue. This service road provides access to driveways for residents and 
garbage collection. 
Based on the available drawings, we understand that the project includes the 
reconstruction of the asphaltic concrete pavement, construction of a Portland Cement 
Concrete paved turn-around at the end of the service road, construction of the concrete 
header, and some minor site grading for the service road. We also understand there are 
few low spots, which require spot repairs, and some subgrade issues are present at the 
project site. It is our understanding that the existing underground utilities will remain 
untouched and all site grading will exclude any excavations exceeding 1 foot in depth 
from the existing ground surface.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield 
volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older volcano, Waianae, is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in Age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger 
shield, Koolau, is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Koolau shield reached the 
end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
The geologic conditions underlying the project site are the result of a complex 
series of deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio-eustatic sea level changes 
and post-erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch. Following the 
cessation of volcanic activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial soil materials were deposited 
in a deltaic or estuarine environment in the area of Pearl Harbor. 
The sea level fluctuated several times, receding perhaps 300 feet below and rising 
about 100 feet above the present sea level. Concurrent with a rise in sea level during the 
late Pleistocene era, the eruption of the Salt Lake-Makalapa member of the Honolulu 
Volcanic Series mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash, which 
consolidated to form volcanic tuff (locally known as “mudrock”) layers. Subsequent erosion 
dissected and thinned those mantles of volcanic tuff. The Hale Alii Avenue service road is 
generally located on the western extent of the volcanic tuff layers. The subsurface 
conditions of the project site generally consist of fill materials overlying weathered volcanic 
tuff materials and volcanic tuff formation at greater depths. 
SITE DESCRIPTION 
The project is generally located south of the Southeast Loch in Joint Base Pearl 
Harbor-Hickam on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The Hale Alii Avenue service road is 
currently an asphalt paved road of about 1,380 feet long providing access to several 
driveways. The service road starts from Hale Alii Avenue near the Hale Alii Lodging and 
connects to the northwest end of PH Parking Lot D. 
Based on our field observations and the existing site topography plan provided to 
us, the southeastern half of the existing roadway alignment is mostly flat with pavement 
surface elevations between about +22.5 and +23.5 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). The 
northwestern half of the existing roadway has up to a 2 percent incline between 
approximate Stations 6+50 and 8+00 and up to a 1 percent decline between approximate 
Stations 10+50 and 13+80. 
Landscaping was observed to be directly abutting the existing pavement. The 
landscaping generally consists of grassy lawns and trees, including Monkey Pod trees.

SUBSURFACE CONDITION 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to a depth of about 10 feet 
below the existing ground surface.  In addition, two bulk samples of the near-surface soils 
were collected at selected locations to evaluate their pavement support characteristics. 
The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Our field exploration suggests that the project site is generally underlain by fill 
materials over weathered volcanic tuff and volcanic tuff formation at greater depths. In 
general, our drilled borings encountered fill materials consisting of hard sandy silts and 
medium dense sandy gravel extending to depths between about 1 and 1.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface at Boring Nos. 1, 4, and 5. Beneath the fill materials, our drilled 
borings encountered weathered volcanic tuff consisting of stiff to hard silty clays extending 
to depths between about 1.5 and 4.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Underlying 
the weathered volcanic tuff, our borings encountered medium hard to hard volcanic tuff 
formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about 10.1 feet below the existing 
ground surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater fluctuations may occur due to 
variation in tidal fluctuations, rainfall, runoff, seepage, temperature, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration methodology and the Logs of Borings 
are presented in Appendix A. Results of the laboratory tests performed on selected soil 
samples obtained from our field exploration are presented in Appendix B. 
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered fill materials over weathered volcanic tuff and 
volcanic tuff formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about 10.1 feet below 
the existing ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the 
time of our field exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels can vary 
significantly depending on tidal fluctuations, rainfall, temperature, surface runoff, and other 
factors. 
Based on the information provided, the service road is an existing private roadway 
without regular bus routes. We understand the pavements for this roadway will consist of 
flexible asphaltic concrete. Based on the City and County of Honolulu’s pavement design 
criteria, it is our opinion that this service road  may be considered as “low-volume 
pavements.”  
Based on the results of our field exploration, the pavement subgrades along the 
existing service road alignment is generally underlain by stiff to hard sandy silts and silty 
clays. Based on the laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) test results, a design CBR

Pavement Design 
Based on the information provided, the pavements planned for the project will 
consist of both flexible and rigid pavements. In general, we anticipate vehicle loading will 
consist primarily of passenger vehicles, light pick-up trucks, and occasional heavy 
vehicular traffic, such as garbage trucks, which will result in less than 50,000 equivalent 
single-axle loads (ESALs) assuming a pavement design life of 30 years. 
Design Subgrade Conditions 
We envision that the majority of the service road will generally be constructed at or 
near the existing grades or in a fill condition. Based on our field exploration, we anticipate 
that the majority of the pavement subgrade soils will generally consist of stiff to hard sandy 
silts and silty clays. 
We have performed our pavement design assuming the pavement subgrade soils 
will generally consist of the near-surface sandy silt and silty clay soils.  Laboratory testing 
of two bulk samples of the near-surface soils (Bulk-1 and Bulk-2) resulted in CBR values of 
1.5 and 2.1 with swell values of 12.24 and 8.38 percent, respectively. The results of the 
laboratory CBR tests are presented in Appendix B.   
Based on the above, a design CBR value of 1.5 with a swell value of 12.24 percent 
has been adopted for the subgrade soils in our pavement design analyses for this project. 
If site grading exposes soils other than those assumed in the pavement design, additional 
tests should be performed to confirm and/or revise the recommended pavement sections 
for actual field conditions.

As traffic loading transfers between two pavement sections of different stiffnesses, 
depressions or sags may develop near the change in pavement section and lower the ride 
quality. Therefore, we recommend gradually transferring the pavement loading between 
the flexible and rigid pavements by increasing the aggregate base course thickness 
beneath the flexible pavement (for a length of no less than 3 feet longitudinal to the 
roadway) to match the bottom elevation of the aggregate base course beneath the rigid 
pavement. The triaxial geogrid beneath aggregate base course should be extended to 
beneath rigid pavement section for a length of no less than 3 feet longitudinal to the 
roadway. 
Site Preparation Below Pavement Areas 
The near-surface clayey soils at the project site were moist at the time of our field 
exploration and exhibited very high shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to 
fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. Prior to placing the aggregate base course layer 
(or the select borrow subbase for the alternate flexible pavement section), we recommend 
properly scarifying the subgrade to a depth of at least 12 inches, moisture-conditioning to 
no less than 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and recompacting to 
90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of 
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

Pavement Drainage 
The recommended pavement sections provided above assumes that adequate 
drainage will be provided for the paved areas which should be sloped and drainage 
gradients maintained to carry surface water off-site. Surface water ponding should not be 
allowed on-site during or after construction.  
When concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the 
pavement areas, we recommend extending the curbs a minimum of 2 inches into the soils 
below the aggregate base or select borrow layer to reduce the potential for migration of 
excessive landscape water into the pavement section. Alternatively, a subdrain system 
could be constructed to collect excess water from landscaping irrigation. For long-term 
performance, we recommend constructing a subdrain system adjacent to the 
paved/landscaped areas.

Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious and other unsuitable 
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft and/or yielding areas 
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill should be 
over-excavated to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled 
with well-compacted fills.  
After clearing and grubbing, finished subgrades and areas designated to receive 
fills should be scarified to a depth of about 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to no less than 
2 percent above the optimum moisture, and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction.  
Where shrinkage cracks are observed at the ground surface after compaction of 
the subgrade, we recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather 
and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
well-compacted fill at no additional cost to the Owner. A Geolabs representative should 
evaluate the need for over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil conditions. 
Soft and/or yielding areas encountered at the bottom of the over-excavation below 
areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over-excavated to 
expose stiff and/or dense materials. The resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted general fill materials. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be 
disposed of properly off-site or used in landscaped areas, if appropriate. Contract 
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and 
compacted fill placement to account for variations in the over-excavation quantities. 
After the soil is moisture-conditioned and recompacted, it should be protected to 
retain the moisture by keeping it moist or immediately capped with new fill. If the 
recompacted soil dries out and shrinkage cracks develop, it should be scarified, 
moisture-conditioned, and compacted again.

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to no less than 2 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The non-expansive 
select granular fill materials should be placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to 
at least 90 percent relative compaction (or 95 percent relative compaction for finished 
subgrades subjected to vehicular traffic).

It should be noted that our recommendations require elevated moisture contents 
and reduced compaction requirements to reduce the potential swelling/heaving of the 
clayey soils encountered at the site. Therefore, we wish to emphasize that 
moisture-conditioning of the fill materials is an integral and critical part of the earthwork 
recommendations provided herein. Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot 
rollers, vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water 
tamping, jetting, or ponding should not be allowed to compact the fills.

Excavations 
It is anticipated that the on-site fill materials and weathered volcanic tuff soils may 
be excavated with conventional heavy excavation equipment. However, the results of our 
field exploration and our experience in the project area indicate that elevated volcanic tuff 
formations and cobbles/boulders could be encountered. Therefore, excavation for 
subsurface infrastructure construction may encounter hard volcanic tuff and/or 
cobbles/boulders which may require the use of hoerams. 
The above discussion regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials is based 
on data obtained from the field borings. Contractors bidding on this project should be 
encouraged to examine the site conditions and soil data to make their own interpretation.

LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the borings drilled and bulk samples collected. Variations of 
subsoil conditions beyond the field data points may occur, and the nature and extent of 
these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then 
appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The boring locations indicated in this report are approximate, having been taped 
from existing visible features at the project site. Elevations noted on the borings were 
interpolated from spot elevations (change to contour lines if appropriate) shown on the 
Existing Site Topography, dated April 29, 2019, prepared by Coffman Engineers, Inc. The 
physical locations and elevations of the borings should be considered accurate only to the 
degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote 
a gradual transition. Water level data from the borings was measured at the time shown on 
the graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. These data have 
been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. It should be noted 
that groundwater fluctuations may occur due to variation in tidal fluctuations, rainfall, 
runoff, seepage, temperature, and other factors.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface condition along the service road alignment was explored by drilling 
and sampling five exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending 
to a depth of about 10 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled with a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch OD solid-stem augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock materials were described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested 
Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the 
International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.
