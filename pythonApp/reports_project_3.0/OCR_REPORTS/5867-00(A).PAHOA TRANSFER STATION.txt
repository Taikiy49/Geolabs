SECTION 1.0 - GENERAL
1.1. Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed County of Hawaii Transfer Station Replacements, Pahoa
Transfer Station project in Pahoa on the Island of Hawaii. The project location and
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering
analyses. These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations,
slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, site grading, wastewater systems, drainage and
pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report.

solid waste transfer stations at various locations throughout the County to conform to
the County’s increased recycling and waster diversion goals and improve the overall
efficiency of the solid waste management system. The first phase of this replacement or
renovation program consists of four stations:

transfer station facilities, including one-story buildings, retaining walls, roof structures for

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1. Regional Geology
Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an area of
approximately 10,360 square kilometers (4,000 square miles). The island was formed
by the activity of the following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea
(activity during recent geologic times), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 - 1803), and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both still active).
The site is situated near the village of Pahoa on rocks of the Puna Volcanic
Series. These rocks are typified by relatively thin-bedded basalt lava flows in both the
pahoehoe and a’a forms.
The U.S. Department of Agriculture classified the surface soils in the area as
Olaa extremely stony silty clay (OID) on 0 to 20 percent slopes. Typically, the surface
soil layer is very dark brown organic material and averages only a few inches to about a
foot in thickness. The soil is extremely permeable and the underlying rock formation has
much lower permeability. It should be noted that the hydraulic conductivity of the
on-site materials (basalt formations) can be highly variable due to the presence or
absence of significant voids in the lava or the presence of embedded clinker seams.
2.2 Existing Site Conditions
The project site is to the west of the village of Pahoa in the Puna District, on the
Island of Hawaii adjacent to Cemetery Road between Apaa Road and Kauhale Street,
as shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
The site was previously graded for the existing transfer station. It appears that
most of the grading consisted of leveling the existing ground to create a yard area for
the facility. There is an existing timber retaining wall on the chute side of the trailer
holding area. Existing ground surface elevations are on the order of about 750 feet
above Mean Sea Level (MSL). Details of the proposed new site grading were not
available at the time this report was prepared.

3.1. Building Foundations
Based on the conditions encountered in our exploration, we believe that shallow
spread and/or continuous footings may be used to support new building structures at
the site. An allowable bearing pressure of 4,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be
used for the design of foundations bearing on compacted subgrade prepared in
accordance with recommendations presented herein.
The bottom of footing excavations (excluding rock formation) should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction prior to the placement of

reinforcing steel or concrete. Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of
the footing excavations should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials. The over-excavated area may be backfilled with new fill material compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the bottom of footing may be
extended down to bear on competent materials at a greater depth.
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grades. Retaining wall foundations should bear on in-situ basalt
rock formation. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes, or on sloping ground,
should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of
8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of the slope. Footings
adjacent to planned (or existing) retaining walls should be embedded deep enough to
avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations, or the planned retaining walls should
be designed to resist the additional structural loads.
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a
45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench
or embedded at least as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging the adjacent below-grade utility lines or structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement.
Lateral loads acting on building structures may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of
friction of 0.4 may be used for footings bearing on the dense on-site soils or new
compacted fills. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). This
assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted. Unless covered by
pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be
neglected. In addition, the passive earth pressure for foundations located on slopes
should be reduced.

3.2 Foundation Probing
We did not encounter voids and/or cavities in the borings drilled for our
exploration. However, based on our experience in the area, there is a potential for
presence of lava tubes, blisters and/or cavities in the basalt formations. In order to
reduce the potential for structural damage due to collapse of voids/cavities, a probing
and grouting program is often conducted below structures. However, due to the
relatively light structures anticipated for this project, we believe that the potential for loss
of foundation support resulting form collapse of the cavities below the foundation is
relatively small and the probing and grouting program may be omitted if this small risk is
tolerable. However, if it is desired to further reduce the potential for loss of foundation
support, then a program of cavity probing and grouting at the foundation locations
should be conducted during construction.

3.3.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture,
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the
in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture
is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry
density.
Aggregate base course materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during the site grading
operations to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction
and the proper moisture content on each fill lift. Where compaction is less than
required, additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture
content, as necessary, to obtain the specified compaction.

3.3.5 Cutand Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, we believe
that cut slopes for this project will expose competent basalt rock materials. In
general, the cut slopes planned may be designed with a slope inclination of
one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) or flatter.
Permanent fill slopes constructed using the on-site materials (or imported fill
materials) may also be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills
placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers
in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations. Fill slopes should be
constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a
well-compacted slope face. In addition, slope planting should be provided as soon
as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes.
3.4 Slab-On-Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the new structures.
For interior building slabs that will not be subject to vehicular traffic, we recommend
placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No. 3 Fine gravel
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. The open-graded gravel cushion fill
would serve as a capillary moisture break and would provide uniform support of the
slabs. To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to
the floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel
cushion layer.
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic, such as trucks and/or
forklifts, we recommend providing a 6-inch layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs
in lieu of the 4-inch cushion fill layer. The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed
basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.

3.5.3 Dynamic Lateral Earth Forces
Because the project site is on the seismically active Island of Hawaii
(Seismic Zone 4 in accordance with the 1997 Uniform Building Code), forces due to
dynamic lateral earth pressures will need to be considered in the design of retaining
structures. The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures (amax=0.40g) may be
estimated using 16H” pounds per foot of wall length for level backfill conditions,
where H is the height of the wall in feet. For a maximum 3H:1V sloping backfill
condition, dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading may be estimated
using 28H* pounds per lineal foot of wall.
lt should be noted that the forces due to dynamic lateral earth pressures provided
assume that the wall will be allowed to move laterally in the event of an earthquake
by up to about 2 to 4 inches. If this lateral movement amount is not acceptable, the
retaining wall should be designed with higher dynamic lateral forces for a
semi-restrained wall condition. For a semi-restrained wall condition, the forces due
to dynamic lateral earth pressures may be estimated using 28H? pounds per lineal
foot of wall for level backfill conditions.
The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures would generally act at the
mid-height of the wall. The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures presented
above is in addition to the static lateral earth pressures. An appropriately reduced
factor of safety may be used when dynamic lateral earth pressures are accounted
for in the design of the retaining structure.
It should be noted that due to relatively high dynamic lateral forces, sloping backfill
behind the retaining structures should be limited to an inclination of 3H:1V or less.
The sloping backfill inclination of 3H:1V should extend beyond a 45-degree
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the retaining wall
footing. The sloping backfill may be steepened to a maximum slope inclination of
2H:1V behind this horizontal setback distance.

3.5.4 Drainage
In general, retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the potential for build-up
of hydrostatic pressures.
For site retaining walls, the typical drainage system may consist of 1 cubic foot of
permeable material, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation),
wrapped with non-woven filter fabric placed at each of the weephole locations. The
weepholes should be spaced no more than 6 feet apart. As an alternative, a
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used
instead of the permeable drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product
should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the
retaining wall.
Unless covered by concrete slabs, the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of
relatively impervious material (well-graded materials) to reduce the potential for
significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure.
3.5.5 Backfill Materials
The backfill material for the retaining walls should consist of imported select
granular materials. The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with
particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should also
contain less than 15 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material
should have a CBR value of 25 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or
less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883.
Backfill materials should be placed in level loose lifts not exceeding 8 inches in
loose thickness and be compacted to at least 90 percent of the maximum dry
density established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 at moisture contents above
the optimum.

3.7. Percolation Testing
One constant head field percolation test was performed in Boring No. 1. The
percolation test was performed at about 19 to 20 feet below the ground surface. The
results of the percolation test performed are presented in Appendix C.
Based on our field percolation test, the subsurface materials generally have
coefficient of permeability (k) of about 1.8 x 10° feet per minute, which correspond to
about 9.2 x 10° centimeters per second at the locations tested.
Based on an average hydraulic conductivity of about 0.01 centimeters per
second, we estimate a capacity on the order of 0.5 cfs for a drywell 6 feet in diameter
and 20 feet deep for preliminary design. It should be noted that the estimated drywell
capacities should be re-evaluated during construction by performance of injection
testing in the constructed drywells.
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the on-site materials
(basalt formations) is highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids
in the lava or the presence of embedded clinker seams, which greatly affect the
disposal capacity of a drywell (or sump pit). In general, the greater the number of
clinker seams and/or voids encountered by the drywell or sump pit excavation, the
higher the overall permeability and disposal capacity.
3.8 Wastewater Disposal
It is our understanding that a new comfort station will be constructed as part of
the project. Since the site is in an area that is not served by a public sewer system, it
will be necessary to utilize an on-site wastewater treatment and disposal system for the
comfort station.
We conducted a falling head percolation test in one of the borings at a depth of
about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. This test indicated that the shallow
subsurface permeability at the site is extremely rapid (i.e., the time required for one inch
of water to percolate from the boring was considerably less than one minute). Generally,
this rate is considered too rapid and unsuitable for a leach field. However, if the bottom

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, each extending to a depth of
approximately 20.0 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. We used a truck-mounted drill rig
equipped with rotary coring tools.
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field. He monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3.
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113, Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method, which uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the
bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description.
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. If 3.7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5.0-foot core run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock, based on the “Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals."

APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
As part of our field exploration program, we conducted one percolation test in
Boring No. 1. The percolation test was performed using the constant head method. In
general, the boring drilled had a diameter of about 3.5 inches and extended to a depth
of about 20 feet below the ground surface at the time of the test. A water-truck was
used to pump clear water into the boreholes for the constant head test method. The flow
rate was recovered by timing and calculating the volume as relatively constant flow rate
was achieved. Results of the percolation test conducted are presented on Plate C-1 and
Plate 3.
