SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered a surface fill approximately 0.25 to 7.5 feet in 
thickness underlain by older alluvium extending to the maximum depth explored of about 
12 feet below the existing ground surface. The fill material generally consisted of medium 
stiff to hard silty/sandy clay and loose to dense silty/gravelly sand and silty/sandy gravel. 
The older alluvium generally consisted of soft to hard clay, clayey silt and silty clay, and 
loose to very dense sandy gravel, silty sand and gravelly cobbles. Dense to very dense 
coralline detritus was encountered at various depths in three of our southern borings.  
The pavement structure along Kualakai Parkway generally consisted of 10 to 
12 inches of PCC pavement underlain by up to 10 inches of base material. The boring 
drilled at the intersection of Kualakai Parkway and Ho’omohala Avenue encountered a 
pavement section consisting of about 12 inches of AC underlain by 3 inches of base 
material. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due 
to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, perched groundwater, 
and other factors. 
We anticipate that the new transmission main invert will be about 4 to 5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. However, deeper excavations may be required where the 
new transmission main traverses under existing utilities. Installation of the proposed 
transmission main will involve excavation into the underlying fills, older alluvium, cobbles, 
and coralline detritus encountered during our field exploration. In general, we anticipate 
that the in-situ soils along the proposed transmission main alignment may be excavated 
with normal heavy excavation equipment. However, trench excavations into cobble layers 
may require chipping and/or the use of hoerams to remove the materials and to achieve 
the design invert elevations. 
Where deeper excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary 
shoring or sloping and benching should be implemented for the trench excavations. 
Where temporary cut slopes are not feasible (e.g., roadway crossings), trench walls may 
need to be cut near vertical, necessitating the use of temporary shoring during 
construction. 
In general, the excavated on-site soils encountered during our field exploration may 
be used in the upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the pavement/finished subgrade, provided that the materials are free of vegetation,

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased, lava 
flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad 
plateau, now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the southeastern flank 
of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering, followed by erosion of the Schofield Plateau, 
generated sediments which were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to 
the south, these sediments have accumulated and are interbedded with marine 
sediments and coral/algal reef formations, forming a sedimentary wedge. The 
thickness of the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate 
Route H-1 Highway to over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain 
and serves as the confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of 
Southern Oahu. Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through 
the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials.

Existing Site Conditions 
The proposed 16-inch R-1 transmission main originates from the East Kapolei

The transmission main alignment generally traverses undeveloped/grassy areas 
on the sides of Farrington Highway and Kualakai Parkway. Within the project limits, 
Farrington Highway is paved with asphaltic concrete (AC) pavement and Kualakai

inclination. Based on the topographic survey map provided, the existing top-of-pavement 
elevations range from approximately +150 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) near the East 
Kapolei 215 Reservoir access road to about +56 feet MSL near the intersection of 
Kualakai Parkway and Kapolei Parkway.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered a relatively thin layer of surface fill 
approximately underlain by older alluvium extending to the maximum depth explored of 
about 12 feet below the existing ground surface. Dense to very dense coralline detritus 
was encountered at various depths in three of our southern borings. We did not encounter 
groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
We understand that the construction of the water mains will generally consist of 
trench excavations, pipe placement, trench backfill, and pavement restoration. Detailed 
discussions for the design of the reclaimed water system project, including trench

3.1 
Trench Excavation 
We anticipate that the invert elevation of the new 16-inch diameter transmission 
main will be about 4 to 5 feet below the existing pavement surface. Installation of the 
proposed transmission main will involve excavation into the underlying fills, older 
alluvium, and coralline detritus encountered during our field exploration. In general, we 
anticipate that the in-situ soils along the proposed water main alignments may be 
excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. 
As mentioned above, our field exploration indicated that cobbles were encountered 
in a few of our borings within the older alluvial deposits. Therefore, the contractor should 
anticipate encountering cobbles during trench excavation. Trench excavations through 
cobble layers may require chipping and/or the use of hoerams to remove the materials 
and achieve the design invert elevations.  
The above discussion regarding the excavation and rippability of the subsurface 
materials is based on field data obtained from the borings drilled at the project site. We 
recommend encouraging contractors bidding on this project to examine the site conditions 
and soil/rock data to make their own interpretation.

3.2 
Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate that excavations up to about 5 to 6 feet below the existing ground 
surface will be required for the installation of the new transmission main. Deeper 
excavations may be required where the transmission main traverses existing 
underground utilities. Where excavations greater than 5 feet in depth are planned, 
temporary shoring or sloping and benching should be implemented for the trench 
excavations. Where temporary cut slopes are not feasible (e.g., roadway crossings), 
trench walls may need to be cut near vertical, necessitating the use of temporary shoring 
during construction. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and 
safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative should be on-site at all 
times during excavation and construction work for the opportunity to promptly observe 
changing or unforeseen conditions, such as high groundwater, inappropriate construction 
sequence or techniques, etc., which may affect the shoring stability. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for 
excessive ground movement. 
It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the 
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction. The properly braced shoring is 
essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent ground 
into the excavation, which may result in potential settlement or distress to adjacent 
structures or other improvements. It should be noted that relatively loose, granular layers 
of sandy/gravelly fill and older alluvium were encountered in several of our drilled borings.  
The loose sands and gravels may tend to slough and “run” during excavation, causing a 
loss of support to the surrounding area.  Special attention should be paid to properly brace 
the trench excavations into the underlying loose deposits. 
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the 
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. Due 
to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the magnitude

3.3 
Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the underlying fills, older alluvium, and/or coralline 
detritus, a minimum of 6 inches of granular cushion material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) should be provided below the pipe. Where soft and/or 
loose soils are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, a stabilization layer 
consisting of an additional 18 inches of open-graded gravel, such as No. 3B Fine gravel, 
wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below 
the cushion layer for uniform support. 
The granular cushion layer should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width 
of one-fourth (¼) of the outside pipe diameter or trench width, whichever is greater, and 
should extend 12 inches above the pipe. Before the placement of cushion material, a 
Geolabs representative should observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm 
materials are exposed at the bottom of the trench or whether a stabilization layer should 
be provided below the cushion layer. 
3.4 
Trench Backfill 
In general, the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the 
Water System Standards, State of Hawaii 2002. The first zone of backfill extending from 
the cushion material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes (or 12 inches above 
the groundwater level, whichever is greater) should consist of select free-draining 
granular material (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) to reduce the compaction effort required 
and resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is critical to use this free-draining material

to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide 
adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill 
settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the pavement subgrade and/or existing ground surface should consist of the excavated

organic material, and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum

Imported fill materials used for trench backfill should be well graded from coarse 
to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material 
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell 
potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Geolabs 
should test the imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations. 
As an alternative, to facilitate the backfilling and reduce the time required for 
applying compactive effort, Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) may be used to 
backfill the trenches. The use of CLSM in trenches traversing roadways would also 
reduce the potential for future settlement of the trench backfill under the repeated wheel 
loading from vehicular traffic. 
3.5 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfill materials consisting of the on-site soils should be moisture-conditioned to 
at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches 
in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The 
upper 3 feet below roadway pavements should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. 
Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a

minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course and subbase 
materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed 
in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of

3.7 
Valve Vault Foundations 
We envision that concrete valve structures may be required for the proposed 
transmission main. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we 
recommend an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) 
for foundations bearing on the underlying fills, older alluvium, and/or coralline detritus. 
This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for 
transient loads, such as those caused by seismic forces. 
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the valve vault structure to be less 
than 1 inch. 
Lateral loads acting on the valve vault structure may be resisted by friction between 
the base of the foundation and on-site soils and by passive earth pressure developed 
against the near-vertical faces of the embedded valve vault. A coefficient of friction of 
0.35 may be used for foundations bearing on the compacted on-site soils. Resistance to 
lateral loads due to passive pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure 
of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). Unless covered by pavements or 
slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be 
neglected.

Thrust Blocks 
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide

acting on the pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of 
the thrust block. We anticipate that most of the planned inverts will be about 4 to 5 feet 
below the existing pavement surface. However, some of the thrust blocks may need to 
be located at deeper depths in areas where the new transmission main traverses existing 
utilities.

3.9 
Settlements 
Primary settlement of the transmission main is normally caused by the difference 
in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the 
underlying subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our engineering analyses, 
settlement on the order of less than ½ inch is anticipated for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could occur if 
improper trench support is used. 
3.10 Corrosion Potential 
Forty-one sets of laboratory corrosion tests, including pH, minimum resistivity, 
chloride content, and sulfate content, were performed on selected samples obtained 
during our field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the near-surface soils at the 
project site. The test results are summarized and presented in Appendix B. Detailed 
results of the Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) tests 
performed by Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. are presented in Appendix C. 
Design of metallic substructures, such as metallic piping, should consider the 
effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. Resistivity is generally 
recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics regarding the corrosivity of 
the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the resistivity, the greater the 
potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. Conversely, the higher the 
resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion of metallic objects. Results 
of the resistivity testing indicate that the on-site soils have resistivity values ranging from 
610 to 5,400 ohm-cm with pH values varying from 6.97 to 9.28. Therefore, the on-site 
near-surface soils may be considered moderately to extremely corrosive based on the

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling 41 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 41, extending to depths of about 5.5 to 12 feet below 
the existing pavement surface at the approximate locations shown on the Overall 
Site Plan, Plate 2, and the individual Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.19. We used a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers to advance the borings. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-41. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration 
sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Five Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Four Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) were 
performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the in-situ 
soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the applied 
confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test results and the 
stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-2 through B-5. 
Forty-One sets of pH (ASTM G51) and Minimum Resistivity (ASTM G57) tests 
were performed by our office, and Chloride (EPA 300) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300) 
tests were performed by TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on selected soil samples 
obtained from our field exploration. The test results are presented on Plates B-6 and B-7.

Job ID: 320-72680-1
Project/Site: Kualakai Parkway 16-inch R-1
Job ID: 320-72680-1
Laboratory: Eurofins TestAmerica, Sacramento
Narrative
Receipt 
The samples were received on 4/21/2021 9:50 AM. The temperature of the cooler at receipt was 17.3º C.
Receipt Exceptions
The samples were received at the laboratory outside hold time and required temperature criteria (0 to 6º C.).
Method 300.0
The following were diluted due to the nature of the sample matrix: B24 RS3 5-6.5ft (320-72680-1), (320-72680-A-1-B MS) and 
(320-72680-A-1-C MSD), analytical batch 320-484353. Elevated reporting limits (RLs) are provided.
The matrix spike / matrix spike duplicate (MS/MSD) recoveries for sulfate were outside control limits, analytical batch 320-484353. The 
matrix spike (MS) recovery for chloride was outside control limits, analytical batch 320-484353. Sample matrix interference and/or 
non-homogeneity are suspected because the associated laboratory control sample (LCS) recovery was within acceptance limits.
The following were diluted due to the nature of the sample matrix: B27 RS3 5-6.5ft (320-72680-4) and B28 SPT2 3-4.5ft (320-72680-5), 
analytical batch 320-484651. Elevated reporting limits (RLs) are provided.
No additional analytical or quality issues were noted, other than those described above or in the Definitions/Glossary page.
