performed geotechnical drilling and field percolation testing for the proposed Individual
Wastewater System (IWS) replacement at the General Services Administration (GSA)
and U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA) facility at 91-325 Kauhi Street in Kapolei, on

design input only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report.
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project involves the replacement of an existing cesspool with septic tank and
leach field system at the above-described GSA and USDA facility in Kapolei.
In order to evaluate the permeability of the subsurface soils and the disposal
capacities of the proposed leach field site for wastewater disposal, field percolation tests
were conducted within the proposed leach field site in general accordance with the State
of Hawaii, Department of Health’s Administrative Rules, Chapter 11-62-31.2, “Wastewater
Systems” and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards.
PURPOSE AND SCOPE

conditions at the proposed leach field location primarily to evaluate the subsoil water
infiltration characteristics to formulate geotechnical recommendations for the design of
the on-site sewage disposal system. The work was performed in general accordance
with our fee proposal dated September 15, 2011. Our scope of work included the
following tasks and work efforts:

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program at the proposed leach field site consisted of drilling
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, to depths of about 4 feet below the
existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan,
Plate 2.
In general, our drilling disclosed 1.5 to 2-inch thick asphalt concrete (AC) paving
over a base course of dense sandy coralline gravel fill underlain by coralline formation
(Coralline detritus) consisting of silty coralline sand with coral gravel to the maximum depth
drilled of 4 feet below the surface.

FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS
We understand that on-site sewage treatment and disposal by septic tank and
leach field method are proposed for the existing GSA-USDA facility in Kapolei. Therefore,
we conducted field percolation tests to obtain data for the design of the sewage disposal
system.
Field Percolation Testing
Field percolation testing was performed in general accordance with the State of
Hawaii, Department of Health’s Administrative Rules, Chapter 11-62-31.2, “Wastewater
Systems” and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards to evaluate the permeability of the
subsurface soils for wastewater disposal. The percolation tests were performed by drilling
two 4.0-inch diameter boreholes to depths of about 4 feet below the existing ground
surface. The boreholes were presoaked overnight prior to the percolation testing.
Approximately 1 inch of gravel was placed at the bottom of the boreholes to protect
the bottom of the holes from scouring. Initially the holes were each filled with 12 inches of
water (introduced through a 2-inch-diameter pipe) above the gravel pack and the time for
the water to seep into each hole was recorded. This step was repeated to determine the
appropriate method of testing based on the second infiltration rate. Each hole was then
filled with water to a minimum depth of 6 inches above the gravel pack. The drops in water
level inside each hole for a specified fixed time interval were repeatedly measured and
recorded to provide data from which the percolation rates were derived.

Relatively good percolation rates were obtained for the tests conducted at the new
leach field site with percolation rates generally between 3 and 11 minutes per inch. Based
on the generally favorable test results obtained, we believe that construction of the new
leach field at this location may be considered. Due to the variability of the subsoil
conditions, the absorption capacity of the disposal system should be confirmed by
additional percolation tests during construction.
Subsoil conditions inherently common in coral formation such as the occurrence of
solution cavities may be encountered during construction and the absorption capacity of
the disposal system may change. If changes in subsurface soil conditions are observed,
the permeability rates should be confirmed by additional percolation tests during
construction.
LIMITATIONS
The findings and discussions submitted herein are based, in part, upon information
obtained from the field boring observations and percolation tests. Variations of subsoil
conditions between and beyond the borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these
variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear
evident, it will be necessary to reevaluate the findings and discussions provided herein.
The field boring and percolation test locations indicated herein are approximate,
having been estimated by taping from the features shown on the Site Plan by Bryce E.
Uyehara AIA Inc. dated December 2010. The physical locations of the borings should be
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used.
