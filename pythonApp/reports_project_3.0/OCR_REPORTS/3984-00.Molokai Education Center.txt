INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Molokai Education Center in the Kaunakakai area on the
island of Molokai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses The recommendations presented herein are intended for design of
foundations site grading and trench excavation and backfill only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is our understanding that two one story buildings Distance Learning and
Technology Wing and Administration Wing will be constructed as the initial phase for the
proposed Molokai Education Center A parking lot with asphalt concrete pavement will
also be constructed for the proposed Molokai Education Center More buildings will be
constructed for the later phases of the proposed project
We understand that the proposed buildings will be constructed near existing
grades Therefore we anticipate that grading at the proposed building site would
generally consist of minor cuts and fills on the order of about 1 foot or less to achieve a
level pad for the new buildings

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Molokai was built during the early to middle Pleistocene Epoch by
basaltic lava flows from two shield volcanoes East Molokai Mountain and West Molokai
Mountain The project site is located on the Hoolehua Plain between the East and West
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3984 00 Page 4
Molokai Domes The Hoolehua Plain or isthmus of Molokai is composed of lava flows
from the East Molokai Volcano banking against the older West Molokai Volcano
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global
sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Molokai remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to the present sea level on Molokai
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of
the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy
conditions The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments at correspondingly higher elevations in areas protected by fringing
reefs and beach stands During periods of no significant sea level changes continued
stream action extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward
The project site is located near the coast line and is underlain by alluvial soils
overlying beach sand deposits The alluvial soils generally consist of fine grained clayey
sands and silts with gravels and may be of substantial thickness

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the subject site generally encountered approximately 4 to
5 feet of medium stiff clayey sands to fine sandy clays with silts over beach sand deposits
extending to the maximum depth explored of 7 feet below the existing ground surface
Based on the relatively stiff clayey subsoil conditions encountered in our test pits we
believe that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3984 00 Page 6
bearing on the recompacted near surface soils may be used to support the proposed
buildings We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii to verify that the exposed footing subgrade conditions are similar to those
encountered in our field exploration
Our laboratory tests indicate that the in situ sandy clays exhibit moderate swelling
potential when subjected to moisture fluctuation To reduce the potential for structural
distress to the future slabs on grade resulting from the potential swelling of the in situ
sandy clays we recommend that concrete slabs on grade be supported on at least
12 inches of non expansive select granular fill materials Detailed discussion of these
items and our geotechnical engineering design recommendations are presented in the
following sections of this report
Building Foundations
Based on the current design concept and the generally competent subsoil
conditions encountered at the site we believe that shallow spread and or continuous
footings may be used to support the proposed education center buildings An allowable
bearing pressure of up to may be used for design of foundations bearing on the
recompacted in situ soils or new engineered fills needed to achieve the final design
grades This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces In
general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent
finish grade
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3984 00 Page 7
For footings bearing on in situ soils the bottom of the footing excavations should
be recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction prior to placement of
reinforcing steel or concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations
they should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The
over excavation should be backfilled with select granular material compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down
to the underlying competent material
Lateral loads acting on the buildings may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted in situ
soils or new engineered fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf
This assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted The passive pressure
should be reduced for foundations located on slopes Unless covered by pavements or
slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new engineered fills as
recommended herein to be less than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the order
of inch
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed buildings
Our laboratory tests indicate that the surface fine sandy clays with silts encountered in
the test pits exhibit moderate potential for swelling when subjected to moisture
fluctuations To reduce the potential for structural distress of the slabs on grade resulting
from the swelling of the sandy clay soils we recommend that the subgrade soils
underneath the slab areas be properly prepared and capped with at least 12 inches of
non expansive select granular fill material below the slab cushion The capping fill should
also extend beyond the building perimeter a minimum of 24 inches
The non expansive select capping fill should be placed as soon as possible after
the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in
the Site Preparation section of this report The prepared capping fill and the underlying
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to the final placement of concrete
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that
the soils be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks Saturation and subsequent yielding
of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer consisting of
free draining materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be
placed below the slab to provide more uniform support of the slab To reduce the
potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on
top of the cushion layer Two inches of moist fine sand may be placed above the
plastic membrane to provide protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab
concrete The thickened edges of slab adjacent to unpaved area should be embedded
at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finish grade Areas adjacent to the slab
should be backfilled tightly against the slab with non expansive impervious soil and
graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding
around the foundations

Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that concrete walkways be supported on 12 inches of granular soils
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Control joints should be
provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways and at right angle intersections
Pavements
We understand that new pavements are planned for the driveways and parking lot
area In general we anticipate that the vehicle loading for this project will consist
primarily of passenger cars and light trucks with occasional delivery trucks only We do
not anticipate that heavy trucks will travel on the pavements on a regular basis Based on
the above assumptions and the laboratory CBR test results we believe that the following
pavement section may be used for preliminary design purposes
Flexible Asphaltic Concrete Pavement
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
6 0 Inch Select Borrow 95 Percent Relative Compaction
14 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction The aggregate base course should consist of crushed
basalt aggregate compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction CBR and
density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during
construction to confirm the adequacy of the above section
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere on
the site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate
landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be
extended a minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soil below the base course to
reduce migration of landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a

Site Preparation
We envision that minimal site grading work will be required for the proposed
project construction In general the following guidelines should be used for site grading
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly
cleared Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed
and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing
below areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated
to expose firm or dense material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be
properly disposed of off site Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and
the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with granular fill material placed in
6 to 8 inch loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
After clearing and demolition areas beneath future slabs should be
over excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 12 inch thick capping layer of
non expansive select granular fill material below the slab subgrade level i e bottom of
gravel cushion or base course The select granular fill should extend a minimum of 2 feet
beyond the perimeter of the proposed building slabs on grade
Prior to placement of select capping fill the subgrade soils should be scarified to a
depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to about 2 to 3 percent above the optimum
moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction In
areas where the finished grades will be subjected to vehicular traffic the compaction
requirement should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3984 00 Page 11
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor
drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted engineered fill at no additional cost to the Owner Contract documents
should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill
placement to account for variation in the over excavation quantities
Granular materials for fill and backfill under the slab and driveway areas should be
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative
compaction Imported fill materials should consist of non expansive granular material
such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be
well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension
and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum
swell of 1 percent or less Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use
Site preparation should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii It is

process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in

Sewage Disposal System
We understand that on site sewage treatment and disposal by seepage
pit leaching field method may be considered for the future development In light of
generally uniform soil conditions encountered in our test pits we conducted one field
percolation test to obtain data pertaining to the preliminary design of the sewage disposal
system
The approximate location of the percolation test designated as P 1 is shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 Based on the percolation test conducted a very slow percolation
rate of about 120 minutes per inch is obtained Data and results of our percolation test
are presented on Plate C 1 Due to the low absorption capacity of the in situ sandy clay
soils high groundwater at a depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface and
close proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean it appears that on site sewage
treatment and disposal may be difficult for this site
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the project to connect to the
main lines In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be
used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate
support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus
reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining
material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result
in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finish grade may consist of the on site soils or select fill
material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3984 00 Page 13
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence
Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement subgrade should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finish grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away from
the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive landscape watering
near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations
should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into
the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce waiter infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain surface run off away from the slab and footing
foundations

APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
The field percolation rate of the on site soils was estimated by performing one
falling head percolation test at location P 1 as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Field
percolation testing was performed in general accordance with the State of Hawaii
and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards to evaluate the permeability of the
subsurface soils for wastewater disposal The test results are presented on Plate C 1
The percolation test was performed by hand augering a 4 0 inch diameter
borehole to a depth of approximately 2 feet below the existing ground surface The
borehole was pre soaked for at least 4 hours
During testing the hole was carefully filled with water The time intervals and
water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which calculation of the percolation
rate was based
