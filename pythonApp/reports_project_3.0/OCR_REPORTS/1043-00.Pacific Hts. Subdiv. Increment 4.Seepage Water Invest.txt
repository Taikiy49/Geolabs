This report summarizes the findings of our seepage water
investigation and presents our recommendations for the design
of a disposal system for the seepage water
The purpose of the investigation was to evaluate the subsur
face conditions of the seepage area in order to determine
the causes of reported seepage water in this area during
periods of heavy rainfall
The investigation consisted of drilling three 3 borings
in the seepage area indicated to us by your firm visual
observations at the site and percolation testing of the
underlying rock strata
W Q 1043 00 P 2
SITE DESCRIPTION
The seepage area is located at the northern corner of the
proposed Pacific Heights Subdivision Increment 4 adjacent
to Kukuau Street in Hilo At the time of our field explora
tion the north eastern half of the seepage area is being
used as a landscaped open area behind a series of townhouse
condominiums The south western half is densely vegetated
vacant land The seepage area slopes gently to the north
east and is bounded on the northwest by the Kukuau Street
embankment on the southeast by a low knoll on the north
east by a cut bank and on the southwest by a densely over
grown vacant lot
REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The site is located on the eastern flank of the Mauna Loa
Shield Volcano which has been built up by deposition of
basaltic lava flows These flows generally have two types
of structures identified as Pahoehoe and Aa lava flows
The pahoehoe flows are characterized by a smooth ropy or
billowy surface and a very porous or vesicular interior
The Aa flows are characterized by a rough broken surface of
sharp gravelly material called clinker and a dense inner
core

The lavas on the site are of recent and Pleistocene origin
belonging to the Ka u volcanic of the Mauna Loa Volcano
The lava flow of 1881 is exposed in areas adjacent to the

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our borings revealed a layer approximately 12 feet thick
of clinker underlain by a layer of massive dense basalt
In the areas of seepage this clinker material is essentially
exposed at the surface In the adjacent areas where no seep
age has occurred the clinker is capped by a recent Pahoehoe
flow believed to be the 1881 flow
Below the massive dense basalt there are a series of vesi
cular fractured Pahoehoe flows to at least the depths of
the borings
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at
the time of our field exploration
Detailed description of the soils and rock encountered in
the borings are presented in the attached boring logs

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our boring data it appears that the near surface
clinker layer is acting as an extensive natural subdrain
carrying water from up slope and collecting water percolating
through the overlying Pahoehoe flow
This collected water cannot percolate through the underlying
dense basalt at a fast enough rate to prevent the build up
of a hydrostatic head It is our opinion that seepage occurs
in this particular area because unlike the surrounding areas
there is no Pahoehoe capping over the clinker layer The Pa
hoehoe caprock was either never deposited or was removed by
previous grading and therefore the groundwater is free to
escape at the surface
Remedial Measures
Field reconnaissance shows that it is probably not feasible
economically and physically to attempt to intercept the ground
water up slope from the seepage area as the majority of the
up Slope area appears to be mantled by the recent 18817
Pahoehoe flow and the clinker layer is most likely too exten
sive to isolate and drain effectively and economically
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1043 00 P 5
Ideally an interceptor trench extending around the affected
area could be constructed to intercept the seepage through
the clinker layer and allow it to dissipate in the underlying
vesicular basalt layer The trench would have to extend through
the upper clinker and dense basalt layer into the porous vesi
cular basalt layer encountered at about 22 to 28 foot depth
Due to the deepness of the trench and anticipated hard excava
tion through the dense basalt this method would be very costly
It is our opinion that a more economical solution would pro
bably be to establish a curtain of large diameter dry wells
around the perimeter of the seepage affected areas
The dry wells should be designed with a perforated casing
extended through the upper pervious clinker layer to the under
lying dense basalt The dry well would not need to be cased
below this level but the annular space between the casing and
the excavation should be filled with a free draining clean
granular material such as 3B Fine gravel to allow drainage
of the ground water These dry wells could also be utilized
for disposal of surface water The attached illustration
Plate 2 shows a typical dry well installation
Percolation tests run by our firm indicate that a 3 inch dia
meter hole drilled through the dense basalt ledge into the
underlying permeable vesicular basalt will accommodate 100
g p m per 3 square foot of wetted area This value may be used

to calculate the optimum spacing and depth of the dry wells
It is our opinion that this dry well curtain would greatly
reduce the seepage in the area However it should be noted
that some seepage could still flow out of the areas between
the dry wells The amount of seepage experienced would depend
on the actual spacing of the wells and the amount of subsurface
seepage water
Future Construction
In general cutting of the upper Pahoehoe flow which effectively
caps the porous clinker layer should be avoided otherwise
similar seepage conditions may occur
Due to the possibility of some seepage even after the dry wells
are installed post and beam construction is recommended for
the houses in these two affected lots The post footings
should be embedded a sufficient depth to resist erosion should
some amount of seepage be experienced
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the installation of the dry wells be
observed by a soils engineer during construction to see that
adequate penetration has been made into the underlying per
meable basalt and that the subsurface conditions are consis
tent with the findings of our investigation

APPENDIX B
Percolation tests were performed in all three borings
to evaluate the suitability of the underlying rock formation
for dry well disposal
During the rock coring in these borings the wash water
return was monitored to detect porous zones which could provide
suitable percolation
The upper portion of the rock formation was found to be
very dense and appeared unsuitable for drainage disposal How
ever a vesicular and fractured zone was encountered at about
24 foot depth The borings continued and extended into this
zone prior to percolation testing
The percolation testing consisted of pumping water from
a fire hydrant into the drilled holes and observing the increase
in water level or head in the bored hole Inflow rates of about
125 to 132 g p m were utilized in all the borings
In each hole the percolation test was run for a minimum
of one hour and a half and the inflow water was dissipated with
only a small build up of the water level
Based on our test results it is our opinion that the
porous fractured rock zone encountered below 24 feet could
provide suitable percolation for dry well disposal
