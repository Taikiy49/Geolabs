INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed CSK Auto Store in Lihue on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses The
recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of foundations slabs on
grade site preparation and pavements only The findings and recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Based on the information provided it is proposed to construct a new one story auto
parts store with associated parking to be located in Lihue on the Island of Kauai Hawaii We
understand that the proposed project site is located in a commercial retail area located along
Kaumualii Highway near the intersection with Nawiliwili Road The project site encompasses
an area of approximately 34 343 square feet which includes the new one story store and
parking areas as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The site is currently a vacant lot adjacent
to the existing Taco Bell and Burger King stores
The new store building will have a footprint of about 10 000 square feet Based on the
surface conditions at the site we anticipate that the finished floor of the new store will be near
the existing grades Therefore we envision that only minor site grading consisting of cuts and
fills on the order of about 1 foot or less would be needed to achieve the design finished grades
Further details pertaining to the site grading were not available at the time this report was
prepared
Based on our experience in the project vicinity we anticipate that the subsurface
conditions at the site may consist of some surface fill materials underlain by residual soil
deposits Special attention was given to the foundation support characteristics and expansion
potential of the near surface materials for the design of the proposed store

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the extrusion
of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch more than
2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield building phase
there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the post erosional
Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial sediments of the Palikea
Formation
Most of the soils in the project area consist of residual and saprolite soils derived from
the in situ weathering of the Koloa Volcanic Series igneous rocks In general residual and
saprolite soils grade to weathered basalt formations with increasing depth Rocks of the Koloa
Volcanic Series are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded from
groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various locales Associated with the vents are
pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at the vent Rocks of the Koloa
Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of Kauai including the proposed
project site
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental glaciers
accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill the oceanic
basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels rose because more
water was available The landmass of Kauai remained essentially stable during these
changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations
resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to the present sea
level of the Island of Kauai

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As previously mentioned our field exploration indicates that the majority of the new
building site is generally underlain by medium stiff to hard clayey silts surface fills and residual
soils extending to the maximum depth explored of about 16 5 feet below the existing ground
surface Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration
Based on generally stiff subsurface conditions encountered in our borings we
recommend that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings be used
for support of the proposed new building Based on our laboratory tests the surface clayey
silt soils exhibit a slight shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture
content As a result we recommend that a minimum of 6 inches of non expansive select
granular fill material be provided below the building slab cushion Detailed discussion of these
items and our geotechnical engineering recommendations are presented in the following
sections of this report
Foundations
Based on the generally competent subsurface conditions encountered at the site we
believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the
proposed store building An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square
foot psf may be used for the design of foundations bearing on the re compacted on site soils
or new compacted fills This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased
by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements the
foundations should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from
the bottom edge of the utility trench or should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts
of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable
foundation settlement
GEOLABS INC
W O 4538 00 Page 7
As previously mentioned the bottom of the footing excavations should be
re compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to the placement of
reinforcing steel or concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedure Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Where shrinkage
cracks are noted after compaction of the footing subgrade we recommend that the soils be
thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or prepared again Saturation and subsequent
yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill at no additional cost
to the owner
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations
the soft and or loose materials should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials The over excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill materials
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing may be extended
deeper to bear directly on the underlying competent material
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our recommendations
we estimate the total footing settlements under the anticipated design loads to be less than
1 inch Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on the re compacted
on site soils or new compacted fills should be on the order of about 0 5 inches or less
Lateral loads acting on the building structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the re compacted on site soils or new
compacted fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes
that the soils around the footings are well compacted or relatively undisturbed Unless covered
GEOLABS INC
W O 4538 00 Page 8
by pavements or slabs the passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should
be neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative from Geolabs
prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the competency of the
foundation bearing materials and the required embedment depths
Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used in the interior of the proposed
building As previously indicated our laboratory tests indicate that the clayey silts encountered
in our borings exhibit a slight potential for shrink swell when subjected to moisture fluctuations
To reduce the potential for structural distress of the slabs on grade resulting from swelling of
the clayey silt soils we recommend that the subgrade soils underneath the slab areas be
properly prepared and capped with at least 6 inches of non expansive select granular fill
material below the slab cushion The non expansive select granular fill capping fill should
also extend beyond the building perimeter a minimum of 24 inches
The non expansive select granular fill should be placed as soon as possible after the
subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the Site
Preparation section of this report The capping fill and the underlying subgrade soils should
be thoroughly moistened and kept moist until the placement of concrete Where shrinkage
cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils be
thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or prepared again Saturation and subsequent
yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill at no additional cost
to the owner
For interior slabs we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer consisting
of free draining materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be
provided below the slab The cushion layer would provide for uniform support of the slab and
serve as a capillary moisture break To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration and
GEOLABS INC
W O 4538 00 Page 9
subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic
membrane is recommended on top of the cushion layer Two inches of moist fine sand may
be considered above the plastic membrane to provide for protection of the membrane and to
aid in curing of the slab concrete Provision of the 2 inches of moist fine sand above the vapor
barrier will be left up to the discretion of the project structural engineer Flexible floor
coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask
minor slab cracking
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at least
12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab should be
backfilled tightly against the slab with relatively impervious soils on site soils and graded to
divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around foundations
Site Preparation
Based on the surface conditions at the site we envision that minimal site grading will
be required for the proposed project construction In general we recommend that the
following guidelines be used for the minor site grading anticipated At the on set of earthwork
the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared Vegetation debris
rubbish and other unsuitable materials including the lawn grass within the grading limits
should be removed and disposed of properly off site
Soft loose and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to
receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or dense material
and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The excavated soft
loose and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site Contract documents should
include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and compacted fill placement
to account for variation in the over excavation quantities
In general existing utilities that are to be abandoned within the building footprint and or
limits of the proposed improvements should be removed The resulting excavation should be
properly backfilled with on site fill materials moisture conditioned to above the optimum
GEOLABS INC
W O 4538 00 Page 10
moisture content placed in 6 to 8 inch loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction
In areas to receive fills the subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of about
8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The subgrades for areas subjected to vehicular
traffic such as pavement areas should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or scarified and prepared again as
recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft and or loose
areas and replacement with well compacted fill at no additional cost to the owner
If imported fill materials are required for the project construction imported fill materials
should consist of non expansive granular material such as crushed coral basalt or cinder
sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension In addition the material should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR
value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less Imported fill
materials should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these recommendations prior to
delivery to the project site for the intended use

Corrosion Evaluation
One of the subsurface soil samples retrieved from our field exploration at the site was
subjected to chemical analyses for soluble chloride and sulfate concentrations The chemical
analyses were performed by Environmental Laboratory of the Pacific The results of these
tests are presented on Plate B 5 of Appendix B Minimum resistivity and pH analysis tests
were also conducted to evaluate the potential for corrosion to metallic substructures The
results of these tests are presented on Plate B 5 of Appendix B Based on a minimum
resistivity of 12 000 ohm cm the on site soils tested may be considered to be virtually
non corrosive to buried metallic substructures The pH of the on site soils is 7 2
Based on the test results the soluble chloride and sulfate ion concentrations in the soil
sample tested were 13 5 and less than 100 parts per million ppm respectively Based on
California Test Method 532 Method for Estimating the Time to Corrosion of Reinforced
Concrete Substructures the on site soils tested may be considered to be generally
non corrosive to reinforced concrete in contact with the soils However normal precautions
to reduce the potential effects of corrosion such as minimum concrete cover should be
observed for reinforced concrete substructures
Pavement Design
We understand that asphaltic concrete pavements will be required for the parking areas
around the proposed building In general we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the
proposed store will consist primarily of passenger vehicles pick up trucks and occasional
moderately loaded trucks Our laboratory tests performed on the surface clayey silts
encountered at the proposed parking areas appear to have a CBR value of 41 with a swell
value of less than 1 percent Considering the generally light to medium traffic conditions
anticipated for the proposed project we recommend that the following pavement design be
used for preliminary budgeting purposes

Flexible Pavements
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
8 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Moist Subgrade
The subgrade soils for the new pavement areas should be scarified to a depth of about
8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not
less than 95 percent relative compaction The aggregate base course should consist of
crushed basalt aggregate compacted not less than 95 percent relative compaction CBR and
field density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during
construction to confirm the adequacy of the above section
In general paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed
on the site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping
in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be extended a minimum
of 2 inches into the subgrade soils below the aggregate base course to reduce the potential
for migration of excessive landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a
subdrain system is recommended to collect the excess water from landscape irrigation For
long term performance we believe that a subdrain system should be considered adjacent to
the paved landscaped areas
Utility Trench
We envision that some new utility lines and utility connections may be required for the
proposed project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes for support Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also
be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes It is critical to use
this free draining granular material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench
backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes

W O 4538 00 Page 13
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils The backfill
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in about 8 inch
level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction
to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are below
pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from the
foundations to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems be
installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation and
pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should be
avoided Planters next to foundations should also be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration into the subsurface
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce
the potential for significant water infiltration into the subsurface
In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings extending to depths of about 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings
were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight hollow stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of this test
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Two 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on the relatively undisturbed samples of the
near surface soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils The test results are presented
on Plate B 2
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed on
a bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soils The results of the tests are presented on Plate B 3
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk sample
of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content relationships The
test results are presented on Plate B 4
One set of chemical analysis tests including Chlorides EPA Test Method 325 2 and
Sulfates EPA Test Method 375 2 was performed on a selected sample of the soils to
evaluate the corrosion potential to concrete substructures The chemical analysis tests were
performed by Environmental Laboratory of the Pacific The results of the tests are
summarized on Plate B 5
One set of corrosion evaluation tests including pH ASTM D 2976 and minimum
resistivity ASTM G 57 was performed on a selected sample of the soils to evaluate the
corrosivity of the soils to buried metallic substructures The results of the tests are also
summarized on Plate B 5
