SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built up during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene by
the activities of two basaltic shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau Koolau is the
younger of the two and reached the end of its main shield building pnase about 2 million
years ago The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau
Koolau was duilt by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from northwest to southeast
trending rift zones These lava flows are interbedded pahoehoe and aa flows with
pahoehoe being the dominant form
After the cessation of the main shield building phase of Koolau there was a long
period of erosion The project site is situated in Manoa Valley which is an amphitheater
headed valley which was deeply eroded into the Koolau massif
During the Pleistocene Epoch there was a renewal of volcanic activity with the
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series Along the crest of the ridge along the
westerly side of Manoa Valley there are the Tantalus Roundtop and Sugarloaf vents
When these vents were active one or more lava flows from these vents spilled
down the westerly wall of the valley and moved across the valley floor These lavas
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2992 00 Page 5
ponded and filled the lower portion of the valley The ponding allowed the lava to densify
as it cooled forming dense massive rock
Other eruptive events of the Honolulu Volcanic Series mantied these lava flows with
pyroclastic material particularly sand sized cinder Subsequent fluvial action has
transported minor amounts of alluvial soils onto the site
During the development of the university campus man made fills were placed to
grade the area

Soil Conditions
In general the borings drilled and sampled for this preliminary exploration
encountered a surface layer of stiff brown clayey silt about two 2 to four feet 4 in
thickness This surface soil is generally alluvial soils or man made fill
Underlying the surface soils our borings encountered medium dense to dense
sands and gravels which are interbedded pyroclastic cinder and basalt clinker These
sand and gravels were in turn underlain by basaltic rock formation which was
encountered at depths ranging between 10 0 and 13 5 feet below the existing ground
surface This generally corresponds to elevations ranging between 60 0 and 65 0 feet
above Mean Sea Level

The underlying basalt formation was generally very hard massive rock with very
little evidence of weathering Our borings did encountered what appear to be pockets
of clinker in the rock mass Basalt in this condition has good foundation support
characteristics but is generally difficult to excavate
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings during our field
exploration However seepage is frequently encountered in excavations in rock
depending upon weather conditions and site specific geological factors Therefore there

Lateral Loads
Resistance to lateral loads on the proposed structures may be developed by
passive earth pressures against the buried faces of foundations and basement walls A
value of 300 p s f per foot of depth may be used to estimate the passive earth pressure
The upper foot of earth against the structure should not be included in the estimate
unless it is covered by a slab or pavement
Resistance to sliding may be developed by frictional contact between the concrete
of the foundations and the underlying soil or rock A base friction factor of 0 4 may also
be used to estimate the resistance against sliding
Drilled Shaft Fountiations
As an alternate to spread footing or mat foundations consideration could be given
to the use of drilled shaft foundations These foundations would provide higher
foundation capacities and greater resistance to lateral loads
The proposed tower structure may develop an overturning moment due to wind
loads on the side of the structure This moment could translate to increased loads on
one side of the structure and an uplift on the opposite side Drilled shaft foundations can
provide resistance to this uplift
For preliminary design of the drilled shaft foundations an axial vertical load bearing
capacity of about 60 tons may be developed by a 24 inch diameter shaft This load
support capacity is derived primarily from end bearing of the shaft against the in situ rock
formation
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2992 00 Page 12
Greater load support capacity and resistance to lateral loads or uplift may be
developed by frictional contact between the skin of the shaft and the surrounding rock
formation Generally this would require the use of a non shrink concrete mix for the
construction of the shaft to maintain intimate contact between the skin of the shaft and
the rock An axial load capacity in excess of 100 tons could be developed for a 24 inch
diameter shaft depending upon the depth of embedment of the shait
Cavity Probing
Although our exploration did not encounter cavities or voids in the drilled borings
cavities and other voids are commonly encountered in basalt formations To reduce the
possibility of foundation settlements resulting from the collapse of such voids we
recommend that a program of probing and grouting the footing locations be considered
when preparing specifications and engineering estimates for the project
For preliminary purposes it should be assumed that the probes would be drilled
at each column location and on ten foot 10 centers for line footings The probe holes
should be a minimum of three inches 3 in diameter and should extend a minimum of
ten feet 10 below the footing subgrade The holes should be backfilled with
sand cement grout injected at low pressures
lf a cavity or void is suspected at a footing location additional probes should be
drilled to attempt to delineate the extent of the suspected void
In order to avoid having to drill the probes inside the footing excavations which
would increase drilling costs it is suggested that the probing and grouting work be
performed after site clearing but prior to the commencement of excavation work
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2992 00 Page 13
It should be further assumed that the probing and grouting operations would be
observed by the geotechnical engineer to see that the above recommendations are
properly implemented and to evaluate other solutions to backfill potential voids

Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and testing
Recovery RIC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material that is in excess of four inches 4 in length without any discontinuities
discounting any drilling induced fractures or breaks if two and one half feet 2 5 of
sound material is recovered from a five foot 5 core run the RQD would be 50 and
would be shown on the Boring Logs as REC 50 Generally the following is used to
describe the relative quality based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of
Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX 8B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materiais
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in determining certain properties such
as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight
of the tested sample is shown at the appropriate location on plates summarizing test
results and at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
Sieve Analysis tests ASTM Test Method D 422 63 are performed to determine the
grain size distribution of the soil The grain size distribution is used for classification in
the Unified Soil Classification System and to provide indices on other engineering
properties The results of these tests are summarized on Plate B 1
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of a soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining to
the performance or suitability of a soil
Expansion tests are performed to evaluate the swelling potential of soils under
structural loads These tests aid in evaluating the materials for use as foundation support
and embankment These test results are summarized on Plate B 2
