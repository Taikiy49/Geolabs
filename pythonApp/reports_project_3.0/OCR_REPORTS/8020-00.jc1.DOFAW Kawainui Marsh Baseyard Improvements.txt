PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project involves the construction of a new storage building within the Kawainui 
Marsh Baseyard facility behind the Castle Hospital in the Kailua area on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. Based on the information provided, we understand that the evaluation of 
the slope stability and the report are required to address the near-vertical slope 
excavation by the construction activity. 
Based on our site observation, we understand that the new building construction 
requires excavation at the toe of the slope for the installation of footings and concrete 
drainage ditch. The excavation at the toe of the slope is near-vertical and approximately 
10 to 15 feet in height. Based on the current design scheme, the bottom 6 to 8 feet of the 
excavation will be backfilled with the on-site soils or imported select granular fills prior to 
forming the 25-foot tall by 3.5-foot wide drainage ditch. The lateral earth pressure will be 
supported by the retaining structure, including the drainage ditch wall and the building wall.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and 
Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion 
incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series of lava 
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred to as the 
Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
In addition, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of continental glaciation. 
As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such 
that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When the glaciers melted and

receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose to above the 
present sea level.  
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea 
level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was correspondingly 
lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. When the sea level 
was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher 
elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosional processes are dominated by 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls, and the soil materials are 
transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once 
these materials reach the steam in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become 
dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. In general, stream 
flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the stream flows transmit large volumes 
of water for a very short duration. Because of this, transport of sediments is intermittent, and 
the bulk of the stream’s hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders, 
cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines. When the erosional base levels change, these sediment 
loads are left as deposits. 
When deposits are left in-place for long periods of time, chemical processes begin to 
alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material. 
Chemical processes also cause induration, or cementation, of the coarse-grained portion of 
the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate. Simultaneously, 
erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in headwaters. This 
continued erosion generates material, which is transported downslope covering the older 
alluvial deposits. Depending on the local base level and rate of transport, these newer 
sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time. In addition, their consistency 
and density are generally less than those of the older, partially consolidated deposits. 
EXISTING GROUND AND SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The slope between the upper access road and the new maintenance building site 
was observed with moderately ground cover and trees at the time of our site 
reconnaissance, as illustrated in the photograph below. A concrete-lined drainage swale 
located on the north side of the construction site traversed along the slope to the low land 
marsh area. 
The slope excavation exposed reddish-brown stiff to hard saprolite soils, consisting 
of clayey silt. The saprolite soils were underlain by the weathered basalt rock at the greater 
depth. Seepage water seam was not observed on the near-vertical cut slope at the time of 
the site reconnaissance. However, groundwater levels likely will change in response to 
seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the topographic map provided and the site measurements of the 
near-vertical cut slope during our site reconnaissance, slope stability analysis was 
performed using the computer program Slide, Version 7.0, from Roc-Science, Inc. The 
model for the slope stability analysis is established using the two dimensional (2D) finite 
element stress analysis method to compute the factor of safety of the critical slip circle. The 
soil parameters were selected based on the back-analysis of the slope stability at the 
near-vertical cut slope condition during the construction, as summarized below.

The additional driving force from traffic surcharge (estimated as 100 psf) was also 
modeled in the slope stability analysis. Based on the results of the slope stability analyses, 
it appears that the maximum 15 feet of the near-vertical excavation at the toe of the slope is 
unlikely causing the slope instability. A minimum factor of safety of about 1.2 against the 
slope failure is achieved. Upon the completion of the new maintenance building and slope 
backfill, the slope condition will be restored with sufficient factor of safety of greater than 2.0. 
Based on the above results of the slope stability analyses, we recommend monitoring 
the near-vertical excavation slope condition during the construction, including visual and 
settlement monitoring. Consideration should be given to maintain the moisture-conditioning 
of the near-vertical cut slope. We envision the relatively short construction period during the 
winter season, therefore, the consistent moisture-condition of the cut slope may be likely 
achievable.

The values provided above assume that the on-site soils will be used to backfill 
behind the walls. It is assumed that the backfill behind retaining structures will be 
compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction. Over-compaction of the 
retaining structure backfill should be avoided. 
In general, an active condition may be used for gravity walls and retaining walls 
that are free to deflect by as much as 0.5 percent of the wall height. If the tops of the walls 
are not free to deflect beyond this degree or are restrained, the walls should be designed 
for the at-rest condition. 
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, line loads, and point loads within a 
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be considered in the design. For 
uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall, a rectangular

distribution with a uniform pressure equal to 33 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure 
acting over the entire height of the wall, which is free to deflect (cantilever), may be used 
in the design. For walls that are restrained, a rectangular distribution equal to 50 percent 
of the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire height of the wall may be used 
for design. Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge 
effects of point loads and line loads. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction between the base 
of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure developed 
against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of foundations. A coefficient of 
friction of 0.35 may be used for foundations bearing on the on-site recompacted soils. 
Resistance to lateral loads due to passive earth pressure may be calculated using an 
equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pcf for foundations embedded in the on-site materials 
and/or compacted select granular fill materials. These values assume the soils around 
the foundations are well-compacted. Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive 
pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches below the finished grade should be neglected. 
Retaining structures (above the groundwater) should be well-drained to reduce the 
potential for the build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system for site 
retaining walls would consist of 1 cubic foot of permeable material, such as open-graded 
gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), wrapped with non-woven filter fabric placed at each 
of the weep hole locations. The weep holes should be spaced no more than 6 feet apart. 
The backfill from the bottom of the wall to the bottom of the weep hole should 
consist of relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for significant water 
infiltration into the subsurface. In addition, the upper 12 inches of the retaining wall backfill 
should consist of relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for significant water 
infiltration behind the retaining structure unless covered by concrete slabs at the surface. 
LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from field reconnaissance. Variations of subsoil conditions between 
and beyond the field reconnaissance may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear 
evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein.
