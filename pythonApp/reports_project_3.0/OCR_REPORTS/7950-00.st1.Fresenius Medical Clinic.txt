SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered fill materials underlain by saprolite and basalt 
formation. Fill materials of up to about 2 to 7 feet thick were generally encountered at the 
ground surface. Fill materials generally consisted of medium stiff clayey silts and medium 
dense silty gravel with some sand.  Various amount of cobbles were also encountered in 
the fill materials. Fill materials were underlain by saprolite or basalt formation.  Saprolite 
encountered consisted of very stiff clayey silts. Basalt formation was generally 
encountered at depths ranging from about 5 to 7 feet below the ground surface and 
extended down to the maximum depth explored of about 22 feet below the ground 
surface. Basalt formation encountered were generally hard to very hard and slightly 
weathered. It should be noted that cavities and/or voids are commonly present in the 
basaltic lava flows of the vicinity. Therefore, the presence of cavities and/or voids should 
be expected during construction. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using 
shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new building planned. As 
an alternative, foundations for support of the structural loads of the new structure planned 
also may consist of thickened-edge slab footings. In general, the new structure should be 
constructed on similar foundation subgrade materials in order to reduce the potential for 
differential settlement and to provide for uniform support across the structure. In order to 
provide similar foundation subgrade materials for the new building, a minimum of 
12 inches of compacted structural fills should be provided below the foundations of the 
new building. 
To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from the collapse 
of cavities below foundations, consideration may be given to implementing a program of 
cavity probing and grouting of the building foundations during construction.  Based on the 
relatively light structural loads anticipated for the single-story building, we believe the risk 
of potential collapse of cavities below the foundations would be low. Therefore, we believe 
probing and grouting may generally be omitted for the new building planned at the site. 
However, if a higher degree of assurance against potential collapse of cavities below 
foundations is desired, a probing and grouting program below the foundations may be 
implemented.

performed for the new Fresenius Medical Clinic at 85 West Lanikaula Street in Hilo on the 
Island of Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and 
laboratory testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations for the design of the 
proposed medical clinic. 
The recommendations provided herein are intended for the design of foundations, 
slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, site grading, underground utility lines, and 
pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project consists of constructing a new medical clinic in the District of Hilo on 
the Island of Hawaii.  The new single-story building will have interior space of about 
10,600 square feet.  Approximately 48 parking stalls will also be provided.  We understand 
that seepage pits or other storm water disposal system will be required for the 
development of the project.  Therefore, permeability tests were performed as part of our 
field exploration program.  In addition, site retaining walls of less than about 6 feet in

Based on the finish floor elevation of about +58.5 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL), we 
envision that minor site grading consisting of cuts and fills of less than about 4 to 6 feet 
high may be required to achieve the design grades at the site.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an area 
of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of fives shield 
volcanoes, which include the following:  Kohala (long extinct); Mauna Kea (activity during 
recent geologic time); Hualalai (erupted in 1801) and Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both still 
active). 
The project site is on the northeastern flank of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano, 
which had been built up by the successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and 
pyroclastic materials. The lava formation encountered at the site appears to consist of 
both a'a and pahoehoe type flows, which spread and ponded as they approached the 
ocean. A’a lava is typically characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface 
resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders. 
Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal 
structure of vesicular (porous) rock. 
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas, especially pahoehoe type 
lava. Cavities form when the lava is still in a molten state, and they represent both lava 
tubes (intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form 
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow, leaving a hollow tube-like structure, which 
may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets are 
generally smaller in horizontal extent.

Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling four borings, designated as 
Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths of about 10 to 22 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on our borings drilled, the subsurface conditions at the site generally consist 
of fill materials underlain by saprolite and basalt formation. Fill materials of up to about 2

consisted of medium stiff clayey silts and medium dense silty gravel with some sand.

commonly present in the basaltic lava flows of the vicinity. Therefore, the presence of 
cavities and/or voids should be expected during construction.

2.4 
Earthquakes and Seismicity 
Generally, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground movement 
of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii 
experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small that they can 
only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of the earthquakes are strong 
enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of magma 
are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island of 
Hawaii. Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) are related to 
volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with 
increased distance from the active volcanoes located in the southern portion of the Island 
of Hawaii. 
The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 6 (M6+) including the recent May 4, 2018 earthquake. Based on information 
obtained from the United States Geological Survey (USGS) Bulletin 2006, the following

3.1 
Foundations 
The new medical clinic will consist of a single-story structure. Based on the 
subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using shallow spread 
and/or continuous strip footings to support the new building planned. As an alternative, 
foundations for support of the structural loads of the new structure planned also may 
consist of thickened-edge slab footings. 
Based on the finish floor elevation of about +58.5 feet MSL, we envision that the 
majority of the building footprint will be in a fill condition.  The southwestern portion of the 
new building footprint may be graded to a cut condition. In general, the new structure 
should be constructed on similar foundation subgrade materials in order to reduce the 
potential for differential settlement and to provide for uniform support across the structure. 
In order to provide similar foundation subgrade materials for the new building, a minimum 
of 12 inches of compacted structural fills should be provided below the foundations of the 
new building.  Where site grading would not provide at least 12 inches of compacted 
structural fills under the foundations, the building footprint should be over-excavated 
during mass grading to provide the minimum 12 inches of compacted structural fills under 
the foundations. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used for the design of footings bearing on a minimum 12 inches of compacted structural 
fill. The bearing value is for supporting dead-plus-live-loads and may be increased by 
one-third (⅓) when considering transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces. 
In general, the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches 
below the lowest adjacent exterior grade. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or

3.2 
Probing and Grouting 
As mentioned above, cavities and/or voids are commonly present in the basaltic 
lava flows. To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from the 
collapse of cavities below foundations, consideration may be given to implementing a 
program of cavity probing and grouting of the building foundations during construction. 
Based on the relatively light structural loads anticipated for the single-story 
building, we believe the risk of potential collapse of cavities below the foundations would 
be low. Therefore, we believe probing and grouting may generally be omitted for the new 
building planned at the site. However, if a higher degree of assurance against potential 
collapse of cavities below foundations is desired, a probing and grouting program below 
the foundations may be implemented. 
Where the probing and grouting program is implemented, we recommend drilling 
probe holes at 10-foot on center for the continuous strip footings and/or thickened-edge 
wall foundations. In addition, probe holes should be drilled at each isolated spread footing 
(or column) location. The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should 
extend to a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of the foundation. 
If cavities and/or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation, 
additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the vertical 
and lateral extent of the cavity and/or void. The probe holes and cavities discovered 
should be backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) with a slump of about 
6 to 9 inches. The CLSM should be injected at low to moderate pressures. As an alternate 
to CLSM, sand-cement grout with a slump of about 6 to 9 inches also may be used for 
the grouting operations. 
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and/or voids at the site, we 
recommend obtaining unit prices for additional probing and grouting during bidding. In 
addition, the probe drill should be available on-site until the probing and grouting

3.3 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We anticipate concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the ground floor of the new 
structure. For the interior slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic), we recommend 
providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill below the slabs for uniform support. 
The cushion fill should consist of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) to 
serve as a capillary moisture break. 
To reduce the potential for excess moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to 
floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel 
cushion fill layer. Where provided, flexible floor coverings should be considered above 
the floor slab because they can better mask minor slab cracking. 
Where slabs are subjected to vehicular traffic or sustained vibrations, a minimum 
of 6 inches of compacted select borrow subbase materials (3-inch minus granular 
materials) should be provided below the slabs in lieu of the 4-inch thick cushion fill layer. 
The select borrow subbase material layer should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. The impervious moisture barrier may be omitted for these slabs. 
In addition, exterior concrete walkways and exterior flatwork may be required for 
the project. Generally, we recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick cushion fill layer 
of open-graded 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No.67 gradation) or select borrow subbase 
below exterior concrete slabs that will not be subjected to vehicular traffic. Select borrow 
subbase, where utilized, should be compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.

3.5.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, fill materials required to achieve design grades for the project should 
consist of structural fill materials. Structural fill materials should consist of 
well-graded granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension

addition, structural fill materials should contain less than 30 percent particles 
passing the No. 200 sieve by weight.  
In general, the excavated granular on-site materials (sands, gravel, and cobbles) 
may be reused as a source of structural fill materials, provided that they are 
processed to meet the maximum particle size and gradation requirements 
presented above. Due to their high in-situ moisture content, we recommend that 
surface clayey and silty fill materials be disposed properly off-site or placed in 
landscape areas of the project site.

are defined as areas where the compaction equipment is limited in size to less than 
2 tons in static weight. In addition, if the contractor elects to use smaller-sized 
compaction equipment (less than 2 tons in static weight), the maximum particle size 
of the fill and backfill materials should be limited to 3 inches in dimension. 
Aggregate base and subbase course materials required for the project should 
consist of crushed basaltic aggregates and should conform to Sections 31 and 30

same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other 
types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of rocks, 
boulders, and/or cobbles. Instead, a testing program to evaluate the number of 
passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be 
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a 
Geolabs representative. Based on this testing program, the number of passes then 
may be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction.

a large vibratory drum roller having a static weight of at least 10 tons traveling at a 
speed of about 300 feet per minute (about 3½ miles per hour). The roller should 
pass over the fill surface at least eight times for structural fill.

Permeability Testing 
Two constant head permeability tests were conducted in selected drilled borings 
to provide data for the design of the storm water disposal system planned at the project 
site. The permeability tests were performed at a depth of about 10 feet below the ground 
surface. The permeability test results are presented in Appendix C. 
The permeability test conducted at Boring No. 1 indicate that this area has a 
coefficient of permeability (k) of less than 1 x 10-3 feet per minute.  Therefore, a storm 
water disposal system may not be suitable in this area. Based on our field permeability 
tests, the subsurface materials generally has a coefficient of permeability (k) of about 
5.5 x 10-2 feet per minute near Boring No. 2.  Based on the field permeability test results, 
we believe an average hydraulic conductivity of about 0.05 feet per minute may be 
assumed for the subsurface materials encountered near Boring No. 2 at a depth of about 
10 feet below the existing ground surface. The hydraulic conductivity value roughly 
corresponds to an infiltration rate of about 540 gallons per day per square foot of surface 
area (0.00083 cubic feet per second per square foot) at this location. 
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the on-site materials is 
highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids/fractures in the lava or 
the presence of embedded clinker seams, which greatly affect the disposal capacity of 
the seepage pits and/or drywells. In general, the greater the number of clinker seams 
and/or voids/fractures encountered in the seepage pits and/or drywell excavation, the 
higher the overall permeability and disposal capacity.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 
four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths of about 10 to 
22 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted 
drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on 
the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond 
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow 
bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is 
mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3.  The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material is

Permeability Tests 
As part of our field exploration program, we conducted two permeability tests in 
selected borings using the constant head test method. In general, the borings had a 
diameter of about 4 inches and extended to a depth of about 10 feet below the ground level 
at the time of the tests. 
The constant head tests were performed by pumping water into the boreholes until 
the water surface stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time. The 
water height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which to 
base calculation of the subsoil permeability. Results of the permeability tests conducted in 
selected borings are presented on Plates C-1 and C-2.
