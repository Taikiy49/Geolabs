SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, our borings drilled for the roadway rehabilitation project encountered a 
pavement consisting of about 2.75 to 6 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by residual 
and saprolitic soils extending to the maximum explored depth of about 6.5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface. The residual and saprolitic soils encountered generally 
consisted of soft to very stiff sandy silt and clayey silt, and medium dense to dense sandy 
gravel, silty gravel, silty sand and gravelly sand. Basalt formation was encountered in one 
of the drilled borings at depths of about 3 to 5.1 feet below the existing pavement surface.   
We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow borings drilled at the time of our 
field exploration.  It should be noted that groundwater level may vary with seasonal rainfall, 
time of the year, and other factors. 
Our field survey indicated that the existing pavements of Piiholo Road from Makana 
Road to Ehu Road under consideration for this project appear to exist in fair to poor 
conditions. Based on our engineering evaluation and construction considerations, we 
believe that the areas in fair condition may be rehabilitated with a regular AC overlay 
consisting of cold-planing 2.0 inches of the existing pavements and replacing with 
2.0 inches of new PMA overlay. 
We observed pavement failures along majority portions of Piiholo Road within the 
project limits. These highly distressed pavements should be reconstructed with a new 
pavement structural section designed based on a 20-year pavement life, in accordance 
with the guidelines presented in the pavement design manual prepared by the State of 
Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing and Research 
Branch. In general, the existing highly distressed pavements may be reconstructed with a 
new pavement structural section consisting of 2.0 inches of PMA on 4.0 inches of ACB 
over 6 inches of ASB.   
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface 
should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off 
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
pavement design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering analyses performed for the Piiholo Road Pavement Rehabilitation (Makana 
Road to Ehu Road) project in Makawao on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location 
and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the rehabilitation and reconstruction of the existing pavements only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
Based on the information provided, we understand that it is desired to rehabilitate 
Piiholo Road from the intersection with Makana Road extending southerly 
approximately 2.2 miles to near the intersection with Ehu Road. It is our understanding 
that there are numerous areas with fair to poor pavement conditions with moderate to 
severe alligator cracking and some areas with damages due to tree roots.  
We understand that Polymer-Modified Asphalt (PMA) pavement surface course 
will be used for the pavement rehabilitation. In general, the existing pavements showing 
minor distresses and severity will be rehabilitated with cold-planing and an asphaltic 
concrete (AC) overlay. Reconstruction of the existing pavements will be performed for 
those areas where the observed distresses are likely caused by failures in the 
underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials. We understand that the proposed 
pavement rehabilitation project will be federally funded. Therefore, a pavement 
justification report conforming to the requirements and guidelines of the State of Hawaii 
- Department of Transportation will be required. The new pavements will be designed 
based on the methodology described in the Pavement Design Manual dated 
March 2002, prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways 
Division, Materials Testing and Research Branch.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene 
Epoch).  The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located between these 
two volcanoes. 
The bulk of the Haleakala shield was built during the late Pliocene and early 
Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows of the Honomanu Volcanic 
Series.  During the Pleistocene Epoch, the characteristics of the lavas changed to very 
hard, thickly bedded flows of andesitic composition.  These lavas have been grouped as 
the Kula Volcanic Series.  Typically, the basalt consists of thinly to thickly bedded a’a 
and pahoehoe type flows. 
These lava flows have undergone in-situ weathering over time.  This in-situ 
chemical weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils that grade to 
saprolite with depth.  In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is 
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks.  The project site is located on the 
western slopes of the Haleakala shield.  
2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The existing Piiholo Road within the project limits is generally a two-lane 
undivided roadway.  The approximate limits of the roadway extend from the intersection 
of Makana Road in the vicinity of Station 10+00 to the end of the county roadway in the 
vicinity of Station 124+00 at the Makawao National Forest Reserve and are shown on 
the General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The shoulder areas beyond the edge of pavement generally consist of cut slopes 
lined with numerous eucalyptus trees, grass, traffic signs and utility posts. The surface 
soils along the shoulder areas below the vegetation generally consist of brown clayey 
silt and volcanic ash with sand and are generally in a damp to moist condition. The 
existing Piiholo Road alignment generally slopes down from south to north between the 
project limits.

Based on our site reconnaissance, a portion of the roadway from the start of the

generally minor to moderate bumpy.  
2.2.2 Areas with Structural Defects 
In general, the majority of Piiholo Road not shown in green within the project limits 
falls under this category and was observed to be in poor to very poor condition with 
moderately to highly distressed areas. Alligator and alligator block cracking were 
observed to be widespread and generally moderate to high in severity especially 
within the tire lanes. The poor areas consist of elongated, severely distressed 
zones of alligator block cracking with depressions of ½ to 1¾ inches, rutting and 
some shoving of the pavement within the tire lanes, contained depressions varying 
from 1 to 2½ inches in depth. Numerous potholes and subsurface utility trenches 
patched with AC material were observed at various locations along the alignment. 
In addition, the roadway was buckled up in numerous locations from 3 to over 
15-inch high bumps due to roots of the Eucalyptus trees which line the edge of the

3.6 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignment by drilling and 
sampling 24 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 24, extending to depths of 
about 5.1 to 6.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan-1 through Site Plan-11, Plates 3.1 through 3.11. The 
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-24. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket Penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Seven Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits of the near-surface fine-grained soils. 
The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depth. Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Six Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Nine laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D2844) were performed by 
Ninyo & Moore on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement 
support characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-4 through 
B-12.
