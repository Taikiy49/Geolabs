SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration and the geophysical survey conducted by Global 
Geophysics, indicate that competent weathered basalt formation is present behind the 
existing retaining wall at a depth of about 10 feet below the pavement grade of 
Krauss Street. It is our opinion that a feasible and economical remedial repair for the 
failing wall is the use of a reinforced shotcrete facing with drilled tie-back anchors. 
Based on our exploration, the anchors should be installed at an inclination of 
20 degrees and extend a minimum of about 25 feet beyond the existing wall face 
surface, or 5 feet into the basalt rock formation, whichever is less.  A schematic of this 
repair alternative is shown on Plate 3.  The anchors should be placed in vertical pairs on 
about 5-foot centers and grouted in place.  A reinforced shotcrete facing should be 
installed over the face of the existing wall.  Previous discussions with the structural 
engineer indicated that the reinforcing would most likely consist of a double layer of 
#4 bars on 10-inch centers both ways.  
An average bond stress of 2,000 psf may be used for the weathered basalt rock; 
and, 750 psf for the fill and clay materials. The bond stress used in the design will need 
to be confirmed during construction by load testing.   
Weepholes should be provided at about 5-foot centers to relieve hydrostatic 
pressure behind the walls.  This would require drilling weepholes through the existing wall.  
It is our opinion that the drilling should be done after installation of the new facing to 
minimize the potential of destabilizing the existing wall.

Project Considerations 
The project site is near the intersection of Krauss Street and Tantalus Drive in 
the Papakolea area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. We understand that the 
subdivision was developed in the 1940’s.  The wall is situated on the downslope side of

downslope side.  It is believed that the base of the wall may have shifted downslope.

patches applied over some of the displacements.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu is comprised largely of the weathered remnants of the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of 
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of 
the eastern two-thirds of the island.  It is believed that Waianae became extinct while 
Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in 
central Oahu. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to 
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.  
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters 
protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in 
the sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous 
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and 
induration of the calcareous reef materials.  
2.2 
Project Site Geology 
Following the extrusion of lavas in the early Pleistocene Epoch, the island 
underwent a long cycle of erosion and weathering forming the prominent ridgelines and 
summits as we know today. During the erosion period, the interfluvial ridges, and areas 
such as the Schofield Plateau, were subjected to deep chemical weathering, forming a 
mantle of residual soil and saprolite which grades with depth to saprolite and weathered 
basalt rock formation.

Existing Site Conditions 
As previously discussed, the project site is near the intersection of Krauss Street

patches applied over some of the displacements.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration and the geophysical survey conducted by Global 
Geophysics indicate that competent weathered basalt formation is present behind the 
existing retaining wall at a depth of about 10 feet below the pavement grade of 
Krauss Street.  As discussed in our previous consultation report dated April 26, 2012, it 
is our opinion that a feasible and economical remedial repair for the failing wall is the 
use of a reinforced shotcrete facing with drilled tie-back anchors. 
Based on our exploration, the anchors should be installed at an inclination of 
20 degrees and extend a minimum of about 25 feet beyond the existing wall face 
surface, or 5 feet into the basalt rock formation, whichever is less.  A schematic of this 
repair alternative is shown on Plate 3.  The anchors should be placed in vertical pairs on 
about 5-foot centers and grouted in place.  A reinforced shotcrete facing should be 
installed over the face of the existing wall.  Previous discussions with the structural 
engineer indicated that the reinforcing would most likely consist of a double layer of #4

Weepholes should be provided at about 5-foot centers to relieve hydrostatic 
pressure behind the walls.  This would require drilling weepholes through the existing 
wall.  It is our opinion that the drilling should be done after installation of the new facing 
to minimize the potential of destabilizing the existing wall. 
3.1 
Permanent Tieback Anchor System 
Based on our field exploration, the subsurface conditions behind the existing CRM 
wall generally consist of medium dense fill underlain by medium stiff clay grading to 
weathered basalt rock formation. Based on the generally competent subsurface 
conditions and the current design concept, we recommend using a permanent tieback 
anchor system consisting of two levels of the tieback to provide the lateral support for a 
new reinforced shotcrete facing in front of the existing CRM wall. In general, the 
permanent tieback anchor system will be designed to provide resistance to the lateral earth 
pressures and lateral loads imposed from the traffic loading on the roadway. Details for

In general, the permanent tieback anchor system is constructed by drilling a 
borehole, installing a reinforcing bar into the borehole, and grouting the borehole.  The 
basic components of a grouted permanent tieback anchor consist of the anchorage and 
the bonded and unbonded lengths of the reinforcing bar. In general, the unbonded 
length of the tieback anchor is the portion of the reinforcing bar that does not have direct 
contact with the grout, allowing the reinforcing bar to elongate elastically and transfer 
the resisting force from the structure to the bonded length.  The bonded length of the 
tieback anchor is the portion of the reinforcing bar that is directly bonded to the grout 
allowing it to transfer the applied load into the ground. 
We envision that the permanent tieback anchors would derive support principally 
from the bond between the grout and the weathered basalt rock anticipated at the 
project site.  The bond stress, which is the pullout resistance per unit area of the 
grout/soil interface contact, is variable depending on the type of soil/rock and grout, the 
overburden stress, and the construction procedures used in installing the permanent 
tieback anchors.  An average bond stress of 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used for the weathered basalt rock; and, 750 psf for the fill and clay materials.  The 
bond stress used in the design will need to be confirmed during construction by load 
testing.  The following guidelines may be used in designing the permanent tieback 
system for this project. 
3.1.1 Construction Considerations 
The performance of the permanent tieback anchors depends significantly upon 
the contractor's method of construction and construction procedures.  As a result 
of the potential variations in the subsurface materials encountered at the project 
site, a Geolabs representative should be present to observe the installation of the 
tieback anchors during construction.  Construction of the tieback anchors should 
be performed by a specialty contractor experienced in the construction of 
permanent tieback anchors.  In our opinion, the following may have a significant 
impact on the effectiveness and cost of the tieback anchors.

Based on the subsurface conditions in the area, drilling of the tieback anchor 
holes may encounter hard basalt rock formation.   Therefore, special drilling tools 
for drilling into and through the hard rock may be required.  Temporary casing of 
the drilled holes for the permanent tieback anchors may be required if caving-in 
conditions occur during drilling of the tieback anchor holes especially in the fill 
and clay layers. 
Grouting of the tieback anchors has a major impact on the load carrying 
capacities of the tieback anchors.  A neat-cement grout or sand-cement grout 
mix should be used.  Placement of grout should be by tremie methods beginning 
from the bottom of the hole.  The grout should be placed through the tremie pipe 
by pressure methods.  The grouting operations should be conducted promptly 
(within 8 hours) after drilling to reduce the potential for significant caving-in of the 
sides of the drilled holes. 
Our borings encountered some fractured basalt rock and clinker material.  As the 
nature of the basalt rock formation, it is not uncommon that grout loss from the 
drilled hole exceeds a few times the volume of the annular space between the 
drilled hole and tieback.  The contractor should anticipate the potential grout loss 
and incorporate the necessary costs into the unit prices during the bidding process. 
3.1.2 Tieback Anchor Testing 
In general, we recommend performing a creep test on the first tieback anchor 
installed before constructing the remaining tieback anchors. The creep tests 
should consist by subjecting the tieback anchor to at least 150 percent of the 
design loads, and the load should be held for at least 8 hours. 
Pullout tests (proof tests) should be performed on all the tieback anchors during 
construction to confirm the bond stresses used in the design.  The pullout tests 
should consist of subjecting the tieback anchors to at least 150 percent of the 
design loads and the load should be held for at least 10 minutes.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to a depth of about 30.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using track and truck-mounted drill rigs 
equipped with continuous flight augers and/or coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D 2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the soil sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are presented 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the volcanic tuff materials encountered at the project site were 
obtained by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling 
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the 
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock 
cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown 
on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2.

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material 
is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality 
of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in 
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling induced 
fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the 
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%.

GEOPHYSICAL METHODS, INSTRUMENTATION AND FIELD PROCEDRUES 
Multi-channel analysis of surface wave (MASW) was used for this study. The following 
paragraphs describe the method and field procedure. 
Multi-channel analysis of surface wave (MASW) 
The Multichannel Analysis of Surface Waves (MASW) method determines variations in 
surface wave velocities with increasing distances and wavelengths.  The data from these 
measurements are used to model the shear wave velocities of the subsurface.  This 
information can then be used to infer rock/soil types, stratigraphy and soil conditions.   
The MASW survey requires a seismic source, to generate surface-waves, and at least 24 
geophones, to measure the ground response at increasing distances from the source.  Surface 
waves are a special type of seismic wave whose propagation is confined to the near surface 
medium.  The depth of subsurface penetration of a surface-wave is directly proportional to its 
wavelength.  In a non-homogeneous medium, surface-waves are dispersive, i.e. each 
wavelength has a characteristic velocity stemming from subsurface heterogeneities.  The 
relationship between surface-wave velocity and wavelength is used to calculate the shear-
wave velocity of the medium with increasing depth. 
The seismic source can be either active or passive, depending on the application and location 
of the survey.  Examples of active sources include explosives, weight-drops, and vibrating 
pads.  Examples of passive sources are drill rigs, road traffic, micro-tremors, and water-wave

The surface-wave velocity with respect to frequency (called the ‘dispersion curve’) is 
determined by measuring the delay time in wave propagation between the geophones. The 
dispersion curve is then matched to a theoretical dispersion curve using an iterative forward-

shear modulus of the medium as a function of depth. 
The MASW survey was conducted using a Geometrics Geode 24-channel digital 
seismograph.  The sensors were Mark Products hydrophones placed at 5 ft spacing and the 
seismic energy source was a 20 lb sledge hammer.

Global Geophysics services was conducted in a manner consistent with the level of care and
