SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Hawaiian Airlines GX/GSE Facility project at the Kona International 
Airport at Keahole (KOA) in the District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. This report 
summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing and presents 
our geotechnical recommendations for the design of the proposed project. 
The recommendations provided herein are intended for the design of foundations 
only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations 
noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project consists of constructing a tent canopy structure for Hawaiian Airlines 
Line Maintenance (MX) and Ground Service Equipment (GSE) departments at the Kona 
International Airport at Keahole (KOA) on the Island of Hawaii. We understand the 
structure will have a footprint of about 40 feet by 20 feet.  The tent structure will be erected 
directly on top of the existing pavements. 
1.2

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers 
an area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during the 
recent geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea 
(both still active). 
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain, which makes 
up the west-central portion of the island. The lava formation encountered at the site 
appears to be of both a'a and pahoehoe type flows, which spread and ponded as they 
approached the ocean. A’a lava typically is characterized by a porous, rough, and 
irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including 
cobbles and boulders. Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy 
surface and an internal structure of vesicular (porous) rock. 
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas, especially pahoehoe type 
lava. Cavities form when lavas are still in a molten state, and they represent both lava 
tubes (intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form 
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow, leaving a hollow tube-like structure, which 
may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets generally 
are smaller in horizontal extent. 
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite 
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions. In general, water is transmitted 
through the porous rock matrix, along joints, fractures and inter-flow contacts, cavities, 
and along clinker layers. Clinker and cavities serve as the primary water transmission 
features because of their high permeability.

Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling one boring, designated as 
Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 20 feet below the existing ground surface. 
The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on our boring drilled, the subsurface conditions at the site generally consist 
of surface fills underlain by basalt formation extending down to the maximum depth 
explored of about 20 feet below the ground surface.  Surface fills of less than about 
1.5 feet thick consisting of dense silty gravel was generally encountered below the 
asphaltic concrete pavement.  Basalt formation encountered was generally medium hard 
to hard and slightly to moderately weathered.  It should be noted that cavities and/or voids 
are commonly present in the basaltic lava flows of the vicinity. Therefore, the presence of 
cavities and/or voids should be expected during construction. 
We did not encounter groundwater in our boring at the time of our field exploration. 
However, groundwater levels likely will change in response to seasonal precipitation, 
surface water runoff, and other factors. Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered 
during our field exploration are presented on the Log of Boring, Plate A-1 of Appendix A. 
2.4 
Earthquakes and Seismicity 
Generally, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground movement 
of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii 
experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small that they can

enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of magma 
are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island of 
Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) are related 
to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes 
with increased distance from the active volcanoes located in the southern portion of the 
Island of Hawaii.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our boring generally encountered surface fills underlain by basalt formation 
extending down to the maximum depth explored of about 20 feet below the ground 
surface.  Surface fills of less than about 1.5 feet thick consisting of dense silty gravel were 
generally encountered below the asphaltic concrete pavement. Basalt formation 
encountered was generally medium hard to hard and slightly to moderately weathered.  
We did not encounter groundwater in our boring at the time of our field exploration. 
We understand that the new tent structure footings will generally consist of 12-inch 
by 12-inch steel plate with a pair of ground anchors to resist lateral shear forces.  In 
addition, we understand that two more ground anchors are required to resist uplift loads. 
Lateral and uplift loads acting on the structure may be resisted by ground anchors 
installed at each footing location.  The tent structure will require two ground anchors to 
resist lateral and uplift loads, respectively, for a total of four ground anchors at each 
footing location. The ground anchors will be battered in opposite direction at about 
22 degrees from vertical. Based on the readily available construction equipment on the 
Island of Hawaii, the diameter of the lateral and uplift load resisting ground anchors should 
be at least 5 inches.  The ground anchors should extend down to a depth of at least 5 feet 
below the ground surface.

other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following sections.

Foundations

consist of 12-inch by 12-inch steel plate with a pair of ground anchors to resist lateral 
shear forces.  In addition, we understand that two more ground anchors are required to

3.2 
Ground Anchors 
As mentioned above, the tent structure will require two ground anchors to resist 
lateral and uplift loads, respectively, for a total of four ground anchors at each footing 
location. A ground anchor generally consists of a reinforcing bar, such as rebars or 
threaded bars, placed in a hole drilled into the rock formation and is grouted. 
3.2.1. Lateral Load Resisting Ground Anchors 
The permanent ground anchors required to resist lateral loads would derive 
support principally from the stiffness of the surrounding soil/rock, the stiffness of 
the ground anchors, allowable deflection at the top of ground anchors, and induced 
moment in the ground anchors. 
Based on the preliminary design submitted by the structural engineer, we 
understand that each footing location will have two lateral load resisting ground 
anchors.  The pair of lateral load resisting ground anchors will be battered in 
opposite direction at about 22 degrees from vertical.  Based on the information 
provided, each pair of lateral load resisting ground anchors will need to resist up 
to about 6,210 pounds of lateral force.

3.2.2. Uplift Resisting Ground Anchors 
We envision the permanent ground anchors required to resist uplift loads would 
derive support principally from the bond between the grout and the on-site basalt 
rock anticipated at the project site. The bond stress, which is the pullout resistance 
per unit area of the grout/rock interface contact, is variable depending on the type 
of soil/ground and grout, the overburden stress, and the construction procedures 
used in installing the permanent ground anchors. The bond stress used in the 
design will need to be confirmed during construction by proof load testing. 
Based on the preliminary design submitted by the structural engineer, we 
understand that each footing location will have two uplift load resisting ground 
anchors.  The pair of uplift load resisting ground anchors will be battered in 
opposite direction at about 22 degrees from vertical.  Based on the information 
provided, anchor cables need to have an ultimate uplift load capacity of about 
6 kips each. 
Based on the readily available construction equipment on the Island of Hawaii, the 
diameter of the uplift load resisting ground anchors should be at least 5 inches.  
The uplift load resisting ground anchors should extend down to a depth of at least 
5 feet below the ground surface. 
In general, we believe the uplift load resisting ground anchors should require some 
form of corrosion protection, such as a fully encapsulated double corrosion 
protection system, due to the potentially corrosive nature of the on-site materials. 
As an alternative, we believe the reinforcement may be oversized to accommodate 
potential corrosion of the reinforcing bars. 
It should be noted that field testing of the permanent uplift load resisting ground 
anchors will be required during construction. A Geolabs representative should be 
present to observe the installation and testing of the uplift load resisting ground 
anchors. Uplift load resisting ground anchors should be proof tested to 133 percent 
of the design load and should be held for at least 10 minutes.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the drilled boring. Variations of the subsurface conditions 
beyond the boring may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not 
become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be 
necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The boring location indicated herein is approximate, having been taped from visible 
features at the project site shown on the Site Plan transmitted by Hawaiian Airlines on 
October 12, 2017. Elevation noted on the boring log was based on interpolation between 
the spot elevations shown on the available topographic survey data at the project site. 
The physical location and elevation of the boring should be considered accurate only to 
the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the boring depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote 
a gradual transition. Water level data from the boring were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text herein. These data have been 
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. We did not encounter 
groundwater in our boring at the time of our field exploration. However, it must be noted 
that fluctuation may occur due to variation rainfall, temperature, and other factors.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling one 
boring, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 20 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The boring was drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped 
with continuous flight augers and coring tools. The approximate boring location is shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in 
the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Log of Boring, Plate A-1. 
A sample was obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depth. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Log of Boring indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond 
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow 
bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is 
mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Log of Boring as REC = 74%.

The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Log 
of Boring as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality 
of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and 
Minerals."

require more arduous methods of excavation.
