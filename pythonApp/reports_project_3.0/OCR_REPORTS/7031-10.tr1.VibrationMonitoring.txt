We have provided geotechnical engineering observation and testing services in 
support of the Egg-Shaped Digester Facility Upgrade project within the Sand Island 
Wastewater Treatment Plant in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
This letter report summarizes the work performed on the project and presents 
monitoring results/recommendations derived from our vibration monitoring performed while 
driving test piles. The vibration monitoring locations and new digester location are shown 
on the Schematic Vibration Monitoring Plan, Plate 1, for reference. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
A new egg-shaped digester will be constructed to the north of the existing 
Digester #1 within the Sand Island Wastewater Treatment Plant (SIWWTP). The new 
digester facility will be supported on 45-foot long, 24-inch square precast/prestressed 
concrete piles, end bearing on the coral reef deposit encountered at the site at depths of 
about 25 to 30 feet below the ground level. 
We understand that the design engineers have evaluated the potential effects of 
the vibrations generated from the pile driving operations and do not have significant 
concerns related to the vibrations generated from the pile driving operations. However, it 
was desired to monitor the ground and sensitive areas for vibrations during test pile 
driving to confirm that there are no significant impacts to the existing infrastructure at the 
site. Some of the more sensitive areas that were identified by the City personnel include 
the following: 
The existing 60-inch diameter force main installed along the access 
roadway at about 200 feet south from the new digester. We understand the 
60-inch force main is not supported on a deep foundation system.

Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the available borings, the 
project site is underlain by a thin surface fill layer over the relatively thick, soft and/or 
loose lagoonal deposits extending to about 25 to 30 feet deep. Below the lagoonal 
deposits, medium dense to dense coral reef deposits, which also serve as the bearing 
strata, were encountered. Based on the engineering evaluation conducted by others, we 
understand the soft and/or loose lagoonal deposits have potential for liquefaction when 
subjected to vibrations.  
Our vibration monitoring was focused on measuring the peak particle velocities 
induced by pile driving, especially in the coral reef deposits. We monitored vibrations 
during test pile driving between July 16 and 18, 2014. Hawaiian Dredging Construction 
Company drove the test piles using a Delmag D30-23 hammer with a maximum rated 
energy of 69,990 foot-pounds at the maximum pump setting.  
VIBRATION CRITERIA 
Vibration damage thresholds for driven pile induced motions have not been 
specifically established. A number of researchers have presented a variety of proposed 
criteria. The criteria that we normally use are related to the type of construction, age and 
general condition of structures, and are adopted from a variety of studies of actual damage 
thresholds observed for blasting operations and other vibration-inducing sources. Based 
on Chae (1978)1, the following recommended threshold of damage criteria should provide 
reasonable indicators for reducing the potential for direct vibration damage.

INSTRUMENTATION 
An Instantel Minimate Plus and/or Vibra-Tech MultiSeis Plus were used to monitor 
the vibrations generated during test pile driving. Sensors (geophones) were placed at 
varying distances from the pile driving, in order to assess magnitudes of the vibration in 
relation to horizontal distances from the vibration source. A Schematic Vibration Monitoring 
Plan is illustrated on Plate 1. 
The vibration monitoring equipment (Instantel or Vibra-Tech units) consists of a 
3-component geophone and a signal conditioner/recorder unit. The geophone senses 
ground motion along three mutually perpendicular (orthogonal) axes; thus providing 
detection of ground motion in the vertical, transverse, and longitudinal directions. The 
signal conditioner/recorder unit receives and processes these signals to provide a record 
of vibrations. Resultant peak particle velocities (RPPV) are automatically computed by 
vector summation of the motions along the orthogonal axes. 
For this project, the vibration monitoring equipment was set at a scanning interval of 
every two seconds during the vibration monitoring process. During the two-second 
scanning interval, the Minimate Plus samples the vibration data at a rate of 2,048 data 
points per second and stores the resultant peak level of vibration. The magnitude of the 
highest vibration level observed and its corresponding time was plotted and a histogram 
was generated at the completion of each vibration event period. A summary (event 
report) of each vibration event was processed and produced by the computer software 
program Blastware, as presented in the Appendix. 
RESULTS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Since the vibration amplitudes for a given energy source attenuate with increasing 
horizontal distance from the source (pile driving) in exponential form, the resultant peak 
particle velocities versus the horizontal distance were plotted on log-log scale as shown on 
the Egg-Shaped Digester Vibration Attenuation Plot, Plate 2. We selected six locations 
near critical structures, as shown on Plate 1, to measure peak particle velocities 
induced by the pile driving in order to evaluate the vibration amplitude relationship. 
The magnitude of the resultant ground peak particle velocities at each location 
generated by the pile driving operation ranged from about 0.031 to 0.174 inch per second 
at a horizontal distance of about 75 to 270 feet from the source (pile driving).

Two parallel lines were constructed on the plot (Plate 2) to capture the upper and

Based on the results of the vibration monitoring, the magnitudes of the vibration
generated during pile driving operation were lower than the threshold of 1.0 inches per
second criterion for the relatively old structures in poor condition. The maximum measured
vibration amplitude induced by the proposed pile driving equipment at or near each
critical/sensitive location is within the acceptable range. It is our opinion that additional
vibration monitoring for the remaining pile driving will not be necessary.
