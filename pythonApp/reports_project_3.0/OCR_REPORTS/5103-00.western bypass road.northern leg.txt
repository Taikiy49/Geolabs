SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered alluvial soils consisting of very stiff to
hard silty clays extending to depths of about 12 to 16.5 feet below the existing ground
surface. In the borings drilled along the roadway alignment closer to the stream and the
borings drilled for the proposed bridge crossing, the alluvial soils below about 12 feet
graded to soft to medium stiff silts and clays extending to depths of about 68 to 69 feet
below the existing ground surface. Basaltic boulders and cobbles were encountered
between the depths of about 27 and 46 feet below the existing ground surface. The
alluvial soils were underlain by saprolite soils consisting of medium dense to dense silty
sands and stiff sandy silts extending to depths of about 92 feet below the existing ground
surface. Highly weathered and medium hard basalt rock was encountered extending to
the maximum depths explored. Groundwater was encountered at depths of about 7 to
8.4 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration.
Based on the subsurface conditions near the existing stream, the anticipated
structural loads, and the potential effects of scour at the proposed bridge foundations, we
recommend that a deep foundation system consisting of cast-in-place concrete drilled
shafts be used to support the proposed bridge. The cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts
would derive vertical support primarily from friction between the concrete shaft and the
surrounding soils encountered in our borings. The contribution from end bearing of the
drilled shafts has been discounted in our analyses due to the difficulties in obtaining a
clean bottom for end-bearing shafts during construction.
Based on the available plans, we understand that embankments on the order of
about 5 to 10 feet in thickness may be required to raise the proposed roadway grades
above the anticipated flood level. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and
the proposed areas to be filled, we do not anticipate significant ground settlements would
occur when new fills are placed at the project site.
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific
recommendations for design of the project.

SECTION 1.0 - GENERAL
1.1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the Western Bypass Road — Northern Leg project located in
the Koloa area on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes our findings and _ preliminary geotechnical
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering
analyses for the project. These recommendations are intended for planning and
preliminary design of the proposed project. The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.

the Koloa area on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. We understand that Kukui’ula
Development Company (Hawaii), LLC (KDC LLC) has entered into a Letter of Intent and
Right-of-Entry Agreement with McBryde Sugar Company, Limited in regards to Tax Map

100 feet wide. We understand that the proposed roadway will begin from Koloa Road

We envision that the bridge will be a three to four span bridge.

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lava of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
(more than 2% million years before present). Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase, there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lava of
the post-erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation. The project site is located on the southern flank of
the shield volcano.
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as thick flows of
dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north-south trends in various
locales. Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials, which usually form low
cinder cones at the vent. Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern
half of the Island of Kauai.
During the Pleistocene Epoch, there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great continental
glaciers advanced and accumulated, the level of the ocean fell since there was less
water available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded, or
melted, global sea levels rose because more water was available. The land mass of
Kauai remained essentially stable during these changes, and the fluctuations were
eustatic in nature. These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that
were both higher and lower relative to the present sea level on Kauai. Most of the soils
in the project area are residual and saprolitic soils, derived from the in-situ weathering of
igneous rocks, and alluvial soils deposited from streams.

SECTION 3.0 - DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered alluvial soils
consisting of silty clays extending to depths of about 12 to 16.5 feet below the existing
ground surface. In the borings drilled closer to the stream, the alluvial soils below about
12-foot depth graded to soft to medium stiff silts and clays extending to depths of about
68 to 69 feet below the existing ground surface. Basaltic boulders and cobbles were
encountered within the alluvial soils. The alluvial soils were underlain by saprolite soils
consisting of medium dense to dense silty sands and stiff sandy silts extending to
depths of about 85 to 92 feet below the existing ground surface. Highly weathered and
medium hard basalt rock was encountered below the saprolite soils extending to the
maximum depths explored. Groundwater was encountered in the borings drilled close
to the existing stream at depths of about 7 to 8.4 feet below the existing ground surface.
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered near the existing stream, the
anticipated structural loads, and the potential effects of scour at the proposed bridge
foundations, we recommend that a deep foundation system consisting of cast-in-place
concrete drilled shafts be used to support the proposed bridge. The cast-in-place
concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support primarily from friction between the
concrete shaft and the surrounding soils encountered in our borings. The contribution
from end bearing of the drilled shafts has been discounted in our analyses due to the
difficulties in obtaining a clean bottom for end-bearing shafts during construction.
Based on the available plans, we understand that embankments on the
order of about 5 to 10 feet in thickness may be required to raise the proposed roadway
grades above the anticipated flood level. Based on the subsurface conditions
encountered and the proposed areas to be filled, we do not anticipate significant ground
settlements would occur when new fills are placed at the project site.
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering
recommendations for preliminary planning and design are presented in the following

SECTION 3 — DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
3.1. Bridge Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, the
anticipated structural loads, and the consideration for the effects of scour at the bridge
foundations, we recommend that a deep foundation system be used to support the
proposed multi-span bridge for the proposed roadway project.
Boulders and cobbles were encountered within the soft alluvial soils encountered
near the stream location. If driven piles are used for the bridge foundation support, the
driven piles may have difficulty penetrating through the boulders and cobbles during
driving. Therefore, we recommend that cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts be used to
support the proposed bridge structure. The cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts would
derive vertical support primarily from friction between the concrete shaft and the alluvial
soils, saprolite soils, and weathered basalt encountered in our borings. The contribution
from end bearing of the drilled shafts has been discounted in our analyses due to the
difficulties in obtaining a clean bottom for end-bearing shafts during construction.

3.2. Retaining Structures
We envision that retaining structures may be required along the new roadway for
separation of grade. In addition, the bridge abutments will have to be designed as
retaining structures. The retaining structures that are structurally connected to the
proposed bridge structure, such as abutments and wing walls, should be supported on
drilled shaft foundations. Detailed discussions of drilled shaft foundations are presented
in the “Bridge Foundations” section of this report. Other retaining structures (not
structurally connected to the proposed bridge structure) may be supported on shallow
continuous footings bearing on the stiff alluvial soils encountered in our borings.
Non-expansive, select granular fill materials should be used to backfill behind the
retaining structures.
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic
pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of about 1 cubic foot of permeable
material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), wrapped with
non-woven, filter fabric at each of the weephole locations. The weepholes should be
spaced not more than 6 feet apart.
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of granular fill
materials less than 3 inches in maximum dimension or the on-site soils. Unless covered
by concrete slabs, the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious
soils (on-site soils) to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration behind the
retaining structures.

SECTION 3 — DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
3.3. Site Grading
Based on the conceptual plans, we understand that embankments on the order
of about 5 to 10 feet in thickness may be required to raise the proposed roadway grades
above the anticipated flood level. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and
the proposed areas to be filled, we do not anticipate significant ground settlements
would occur when new fills are placed at the project site.
In general, areas within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared
and grubbed at the on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft,
loose, and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to
receive fill or future improvements should be over-excavated to expose firm or dense
material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill. The
excavated soft, loose, and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site.
Due to the high moisture contents of the in-situ soils, we believe that it may be
difficult to scarify and to compact the areas to receive fill and finished subgrades in cut
areas. Therefore, we recommend that the areas to receive fill and finished subgrades in
cut areas should be proof-rolled to provide a firm and unyielding base. Where
shrinkage cracks are noted after preparation of the subgrade, we recommend that the
soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks and recompacted or they should be
prepared as mentioned above.
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft and/or loose areas
and replacement with well-compacted fill.
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation, the pumping
subgrade may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for pumping
subgrade conditions. As a guide, one sack of cement may be used for approximately
25 square feet of subgrade area. Alternatively, the subgrade may be proof-rolled and a

SECTION 3 — DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
3.4 Cutand Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered during our field exploration, we
believe that planned cut slopes will expose the very stiff to hard alluvial soils
encountered at the project site. In general, cut slopes for the project planned at the site
may be designed with a maximum slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.
Permanent fill slopes planned at the project site may be designed with a
maximum slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills placed on slopes steeper than
5H:1V should be keyed and benched into firm and stable materials to provide stability of
the new embankment fill against sliding. The filling operations should start at the lowest
point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the fill
placement recommendations presented in the “Site Grading” section of this report. Fill
slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to
obtain a well-compacted slope face. Excessive surface water should be diverted away
from the tops of slopes, and slope planting should be provided as soon as the cut and
fill slopes are completed to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the finished
slopes.
3.5 Utility Trench
We envision that new underground utility lines may be required for the project. In
general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of free-draining granular materials
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) is recommended under the pipes for uniform support.
Free-draining granular materials (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) should also be used for
the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate
support around the pipes. It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the
potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of
the backfill and damage to the pipes.
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on-site soils
with a maximum particle size of 3 inches. The backfill should be moisture-conditioned

APPENDIX A

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling six borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending to depths of
about 16.5 to 95.6 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring
tools. The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer
falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586, Penetration Test and
Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration
Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths.
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths.
Core samples of the rock formation and/or boulder materials encountered at the
site were obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation.
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and classification.
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5-foot core run, the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 80%.

The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any
drilling-induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5-foot core run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock, based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals."

The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock, its
relative quality, brittleness, and fissile characteristics. A dense rock formation with a
high RQD value would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous
methods of excavation.

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937)
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties. The results of these tests are presented on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification.
Results of the test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depth. Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1.
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D 3080) was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics. Results of the direct shear test
are presented on Plate B-2.
One Consolidation test (ASTM D 2435) with time rates of consolidation was
performed on a sample of the soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the
materials encountered. The results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate B-3.
One Laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) test (ASTM D 1883) was performed
on the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils.
