SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Modification of Ocean Villas in the Turtle Bay Resort on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and
engineering analyses These recommendations are intended to assist in the design of
the new Ocean Villas Modification only The findings and recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
Based on information provided we understand that it is desired to construct and
remodel the existing Ocean Villas located at the northern end of Turtle Bay Resort
adjacent to the existing Turtle Bay lagoon in the Kahuku area on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii The existing Ocean Villas consist of five two story buildings that are supported
by slab on grade with thickened edge shallow foundation system Based on the as built
drawings provided we understand that the existing shallow footings were embedded
approximately 12 to 24 inches below the lowest adjacent grade

western sides of the existing villas and remodel the four existing buildings to provide a
new three story configuration In addition the proposed modification project involves
renovation of the existing swimming pool construction of new dining areas and a new
access roadway
We understand that separate footings will be constructed adjacent to the existing
footings to support the structural loading from the additional floor We anticipate that
these new footings will not be embedded deeper than the adjacent existing footing to
prevent disturbance of the existing footing subgrade Special attention was given to the

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu encompassing approximately 604 square miles of land area
may be divided into four basic geographic provinces The provinces include the
Waianae Mountains Ko olau Mountains Leilehua Plateau and Coastal Plain The
Island of Oahu was formed by the merging of basalt lavas erupted from the two
prominent volcanoes the Wai anae and Ko olau Volcanoes Of the two volcanoes the
Waianae Volcano is the older having breached the ocean surface nearly 4 million years
ago The Ko olau Volcano rose above sea level approximately 2 7 million years ago
making it the younger of the two volcanoes
In general the two volcanoes were built by the long term eruption of thinly
bedded a a and pahoehoe lava flows which were erupted from elongate and linear
trending volcanic vents associated with the prominent rift zones of each of the
volcanoes The Ko olau lavas banked against the lava flows of the Wai anae Volcano to
form the Leilehnua Plateau of the central interior portion of Oahu The Waianae
Mountains which form the western third of the Island of Oahu contain the oldest basalt
rock formations on the Island of Oahu The Ko olau Mountains form the eastern
two thirds of the island and include the rock layers exposed along the
extending from Waimanalo to Kahuku
During the final stages of eruption and continuing well after the cessation of
Waianae and Ko olau eruptions the island mass began to subside thousands of feet
under the tremendous weight of the rock mass In addition to the subsidence of the
island the force of stream erosion worked in conjunction with the large scale fracture
and slumping of portions of the land mass to form the topography of today Between
850 000 and 70 000 years ago volcanic activity returned to the Island of Oahu with the
eruption of ash cinder and basalt lavas from vents located throughout the eastern and
southeastern portions of the island These most recent eruptions are collectively known
as the Honolulu Volcanic Series Subsequently erosion by streams and ocean waves

The Island of Oahu is no longer sinking beneath the sea however the effects of
the early whole island subsidence combined with the added effect of major Pleistocene

stands of the sea in prehistoric time

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling four 4 test borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 to depths of about
15 to 20 feet below the existing ground surface Boring Nos 1 and 4 were located in
the areas for the proposed new buildings Boring Nos 2 and 3 were drilled adjacent to
the existing buildings The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2

granular fill overlying 8 to 9 feet of medium stiff to stiff silty clay soil extending up to

maximum depth drilled of about 20 feet below the existing ground surface

formation extending to the maximum depth drilled of about 20 feet below the existing
ground surface

at depths of approximately 11 to 14 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of
our field exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to
change due to seasonal precipitation tidal fluctuations storm surge conditions and
other factors

Based on the current design concept we understand that it is proposed to
construct two new three story buildings adjacent to the existing villas in the approximate
areas of Boring Nos 1 and 4 as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 It is also proposed to
modify the existing buildings by adding one more level to provide three story ocean
villas Based on the as built drawing provided we understand that the existing buildings
were constructed in early 1970 s and are supported on slab on grade with thickened
edge foundation system The footings are embedded approximately 12 to 24 inches
Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered at the project site we
believe that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be
used for support of the new three story buildings planned at the site An allowable
bearing pressure of up to may be used for the design of foundations bearing
on compacted on site silty clay or sand Footings should be embedded a minimum of
24 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
We understand that separate new footings may be designed to support the
additional level proposed for the existing buildings To minimize the disturbance of the
existing footings the embedment of the new footings may be reduced to match the
existing footings embedment Based on the information provided we understand that
embedment of these existing footings varies from 12 to 18 inches Therefore the
allowable bearing pressure should be reduced to provided that subgrade of
footings be scarified and re compacted to 90 percent relative compaction

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As mentioned previously soft or loose pockets were encountered within silty clay
and beach sand deposit in our borings If soft and or loose materials are encountered
at the bottom of the footing excavations the soft and or loose materials should be
removed to expose the underlying firm or dense materials The over excavation may be
backfilled with the excavated on site materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction
Where foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements the
foundations should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward
from the bottom edge of the utility trench Alternatively the foundations should be
extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
Footings should have a minimum horizontal setback distance of 6 feet measured from
the outside edge of the footing to the slope face
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate total settlements to be less than 1 inch with differential
settlements on the order of about 0 5 inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on compacted sand
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the
soils around the footings are well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs
the passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative from
Geolabs Inc prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the
competency of the foundation bearing materials and the required embedment depths

3 2 Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed new
buildings In general we recommend that the subgrade soils below the concrete
slabs on grade be prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the
Site Preparation section of this report Where shrinkage cracks are noted after
compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils be thoroughly moistened to
close the cracks or prepared again Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed
subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of
the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill
For concrete slabs that will not be subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend
that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill be provided below the slabs to provide
uniform support and to serve as a capillary break The cushion fill layer should consist
of open graded materials such as No 3 Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation If
desired an impervious moisture barrier may be provided above the cushion fill layer to
reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through the slab
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at
least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab
should be backfilled tightly against the slab with relatively impervious soils and graded
to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the
foundations and slabs

3 4 Utility Trench
New utility lines and utility connections will likely be needed for the proposed
project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should
also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to
provide adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this free draining
material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and
to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could
result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils
The backfill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground
subsidence Where trenches are located below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the
trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction
3 5 Drainage
The finished grades outside the new buildings should be sloped to shed water
away from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised

the foundations and pavement areas should also be avoided Planters next to

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 to depths of about 15 to 20 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using portable drilling equipment
equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered during drilling are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1
through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 2 inch Outside Diameter OD standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD
split barrel sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches a total of 12 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight determinations ASTM D 2937
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1 Results of the test are
also summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two sets of Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 136 D 1140 were performed on
selected samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to
aid in soil classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented
on Plate B 2
