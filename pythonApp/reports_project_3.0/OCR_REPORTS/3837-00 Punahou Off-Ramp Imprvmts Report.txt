INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Punahou Off Ramp Improvements project located alongside the
Punahou off ramp in the Makiki area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The
general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

The proposed project is located alongside the existing Punahou off ramp in the
Makiki area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the information
provided it is desired to improve the Punahou off ramp by adding an extra lane along the
right hand side of the off ramp The new lane measuring approximately 12 feet wide will
begin at about Makiki Street and terminate at Punahou Street as shown approximately
on the Site Plan Plate 2 We envision that a new retaining wall will be constructed
alongside the new lane to provide grade separation between the off ramp and the
adjacent properties such as the Jodo Mission of Hawaii and Shriners Hospital for
Crippled Children Based on the information provided part of the new lane will traverse

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations such as Punchbowl These series are
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of Punchbowl Hill The
Punchbowl Hill Puowaina is a tuff cone near the center of Honolulu built against the end
of a spur of the Koolau Range The tuff consists mostly of brown palagonitized vitric ash
and lapilli with scattered fragments of coral limestone and Koolau basalt
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3837 00 Page 5
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration along the Punahou off ramp site generally encountered stiff to
very stiff clayey silts and silty clays overlying medium dense to dense cinder sands
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 21 5 feet below the existing
ground surface
Based on our laboratory tests the near surface clayey soils exhibit poor
pavement support characteristics with a relatively low California Bearing Ratio CBR of
about 5 Therefore we recommend that a pavement structural section consisting of
4 inches of AC on 7 inches of asphalt concrete base over 15 inches of aggregate
subbase be used for the design of the new pavements for the off ramp lane The select
borrow layer may be eliminated by increasing the thickness of the asphalt concrete
base from 7 to 16 inches Alternatively a pavement structural section consisting of
5 5 inches of AC on 8 inches of aggregate base course over 16 inches of aggregate
subbase may be considered for the new travelway
Based on the information provided we understand that part of the new off ramp
will traverse an existing underground box culvert near the property line between Shriners
Hospital for Crippled Children and the Jodo Mission of Hawaii For the design of new
pavements over the existing box culvert we recommend that a rigid pavement section
consisting of 6 inches of Portland Cement Concrete bonded to the concrete of the
existing box culvert be used for preliminary design purposes The pavement section
should be increased to 13 inches over 6 inches of asphalt concrete base beyond the
edges of the box culvert and extend a minimum of 15 feet on both sides for improved
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3837 00 Page 8
pavement transition The rigid pavement section should be properly reinforced to
accommodate some differential movements at the edge of existing box culvert transition
New retaining walls will be required to provide grade separation between the new
off ramp lane and the adjacent properties Based on the subsoil conditions encountered
in our drilled borings we believe that a shallow foundation system bearing on the
recompacted in situ soils may be used to support the retaining wall structure In addition
we understand that two existing maintenance utility structures will need to be relocated for
construction of the proposed project Based on the relatively stiff subsoil conditions
encountered in our borings and the anticipated light structural loads we believe that a
shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the
recompacted near surface soils may be used to support the proposed chiller structure at
the relocated site
Laboratory tests indicate that the near surface clayey soils exhibit a slight
shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations To reduce the potential for
distress to future slabs on grade resulting from the swelling of the near surface soils we
recommend that the subgrade be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture
conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and recompacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The slab subgrade should be kept moist
and free of shrinkage cracks prior to placement of cushion fill and or slab concrete
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering recommendations
are presented in the following sections of this report

Slabs On Grade
Our field exploration and laboratory testing disclosed that the proposed new chiller
site is generally underlain by near surface clayey soils with a slight shrink swell potential
when subjected to moisture fluctuations To reduce the potential for structural distress to
future slabs on grade resulting from the swelling of the near surface clayey soils we
recommend that the subgrade soils underneath the slab areas be scarified to a minimum
depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO Test Method T 180
ASTM D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
The slab subgrades should be kept moist prior to placement of cushion fill and
concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soils be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor
drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted engineered fill
We envision that the slabs on grade will be subjected to equipment loads and
vibration Therefore a 6 inch layer of aggregate base course compacted to a minimum of
GEOLABS HAWAII

95 percent relative compaction is recommended for support of the concrete slabs For
design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per cubic inch

materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing below any areas designated to receive fill or future
improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or stiff material and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft
and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
Existing structures and pavements that are to be demolished should be completely
removed Any over excavation resulting from the demolition should be backfilled with
compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed
and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with granular fill material
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch loose lifts
and compacted to a minimum of 95 relative compaction Utilities that are to be
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3837 00 Page 18
abandoned in place under the proposed structures should be backfilled by pumping lean
concrete under low pressure
After clearing grubbing and demolition the future pavement areas should be
excavated where necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the base
course or subbase layer The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of
about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO Test Method T 180
ASTM D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density The compaction requirement for the slab
subgrades at the new chiller location may be reduced to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the pumping
subgrade may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for pumping
subgrade conditions As a guide one sack of cement may be used for approximately
25 square feet of subgrade area Alternatively the subgrade may be proof rolled and a
woven geotextile fabric such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent should be placed on the
subgrade prior to placement of the base or subbase layer
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soils be scarified and re prepared as recommended above
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted engineered fill at no additional cost to the Owner Contract documents

In general the on site soils encountered in the borings may be reused as a source
of fill materials provided that the materials are less than 3 inches in largest dimension and
are free of deleterious materials Imported fill materials if required should consist of
non expansive granular material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand
The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles
passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or
more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less Imported fill material should
be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with these recommendations prior to
delivery to the project site for its intended use

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the project In general a
granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the trench
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the
pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of
damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential
for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for
the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill
and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils or select fill
material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence
Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement subgrade should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base course subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement
surface should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all surface water off
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should not be
allowed anywhere on site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to
isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3837 00 Page 21
extend a minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soils below the base and subbase
course layers to reduce migration of landscape water into the pavement section
Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect excessive water from
landscaping irrigation For long term performance we recommend that a subdrain
system be constructed along the sides of the roadway
The finished grades outside the new chiller building should be sloped to shed
water away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive
landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters
next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce
water infiltration into the subsoils These drainage requirements are essential for the
proper performance of the above foundation recommendations since ponded water could
cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The
foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings
immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales
should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain all surface
run off away from the slab and foundations
