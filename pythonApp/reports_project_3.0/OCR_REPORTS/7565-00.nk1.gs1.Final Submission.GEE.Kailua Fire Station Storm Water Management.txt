SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Kailua Fire Station Storm Water Management project in 
Kailua on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown 
on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These recommendations are intended to assist in the design of storm water management 
improvements proposed for the project. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located on the southern corner of the intersection of Kuulei Road 
and South Kainalu Drive in Kailua on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  Based on the 
Recommended BMPs Concept Plan provided in Appendix K of the report dated 
August 30, 2016, we understand that the project includes the installation of five seepage 
wells, two infiltration basins, and shallow trench drains. 
Two seepage wells are planned on either side of the South Kainalu Drive entryway 
that are fed by multiple drain inlets in the concrete parking lot. A long infiltration basin will 
be installed along the eastern chain link fence separating the fire station from the tennis 
courts. An additional infiltration basin and seepage well are planned on either side of the 
fire station’s main driveway along Kuulei Road. Both drainage structures will be fed by a 
filtered trench drain installed in front of the main driveway. We understand that the bottom 
of the seepage wells will be about 7 to 8 feet below the existing ground surface and the 
bottoms of the infiltration basins will be about 1.5 feet below the existing ground surface.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of the extinct 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of the 
western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of the 
eastern two-thirds of the island. It is believed that the Waianae Volcano became extinct 
while the Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below the 
Koolau lavas in Central Oahu. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays, accumulated reef 
deposits at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine 
sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused 
streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. The project site is situated 
near the Kawainui Marsh where the subsurface soil deposits are dominated by lagoonal 
deposits and coralline soil deposits. Land development and reclamation projects within 
the last century have brought the project area to its present form.

(22,050 square feet) is considered impervious by current improvements such as concrete

The perimeter of the fire station is generally covered with landscape. At the rear of 
the station building, a driveway and parking area surfaced with concrete pavement were

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a medium dense surface fill layer 
underlain by medium dense coralline beach sand extending to about 5 to 10 feet below 
the existing ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at 
the time of our field exploration. 
Five seepage wells, two infiltration basins, and one shallow trench drain are 
planned for the project. Based on the results of the field infiltration testing, it appears that 
these storm water management systems are feasible for the project site.  
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design 
recommendations are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Subsurface Infiltration 
We understand that the project includes a retention and disposal system consisting 
of seepage wells and infiltration basins for the on-site management of storm water run-off.  
In order to obtain subsurface infiltration information in support of the design of the 
drainage structures, we conducted falling head infiltration tests in each of the borings, as 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The infiltration tests were performed in general accordance with the procedures in 
Appendix D of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment, “Stormwater 
Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (Rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with 
other states’ procedures and may generally be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by augering borings to selected test 
depths of about 5 and 10 feet below the existing ground surface for the infiltration basins 
and seepage wells, respectively.  Upon reaching the test depth, a 4-inch diameter PVC 
solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled hole to allow infiltration only through the 
soil exposed on the bottom of the boring.  Falling head infiltration tests were performed 
to determine the infiltration rates of the underlying subsurface materials.  Each test 
consisted of four trials of filling the casing with 24 inches of water and taking periodic 
readings over a one hour trial period. The infiltration rate is calculated based on the result

Based on the infiltration testing, the infiltration rates ranged from about 3 to 
131.7 inches per hour corresponding to drainage rates of approximately 45 to 
1,970 gallons per square foot per day. Based on these infiltration rates, it is our opinion 
that the planned storm water management improvements are feasible.  It should be noted 
that the infiltration tests were conducted through a 4-inch diameter borehole, which may 
not represent the actual infiltration conditions within the finished basin and seepage well. 
The results of the infiltration tests at the above-specified test locations indicate that an 
infiltration/detention system and similar methods of storm water management would be 
feasible for this project. Due to the variability of the subsurface conditions, the infiltration 
capacity of the disposal system should be confirmed by conducting additional infiltration 
tests during construction. 
3.2 
Construction Considerations  
We anticipate that the seepage wells will be constructed by drilling a hole down to 
the design bottom elevation.  Due to the potential for smearing of the sidewalls of the hole 
during drilling, we recommend raking the walls of the well excavation rough to minimize 
the sidewall smearing which reduces the infiltration capacity of the well.  The bottom of 
the seepage well should be leveled and not compacted. The excavations for the infiltration 
basins should also minimize sidewall smearing and the bottoms should be leveled and 
not compacted.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analysis and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field borings and infiltration testing.  Variations of conditions 
between and beyond the field borings and infiltration tests may occur, and the nature and 
extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is underway.  If 
variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to reevaluate the recommendations 
presented in this report. 
The field boring and infiltration test locations are approximate, having been 
estimated from the features shown on the Recommended BMPs Concept Plan provided 
in Appendix K of the report dated August 30, 2016 and the Topographic Survey Map 
transmitted to our office by Belt Collins Hawaii Ltd. on August 2, 2017 and September 7, 
2017, respectively.  The elevations of the field borings were estimated by interpolation 
from the spot elevations shown on the same maps. The boring locations and elevations 
should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks on the Logs of Borings depict the approximate boundaries 
between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a gradual transition. Water level data 
from the borings were measured at the times shown on the graphic representations 
and/or presented in the text herein.  These data have been reviewed and interpretations 
made in the formulation of this report.  However, it must be noted that water level 
fluctuation may occur due to variations in seasonal rainfall, and other factors.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths of about 5 and 
10 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on 
the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  These classifications were further reviewed visually and by 
testing in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified 
Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations 
made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in 
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Infiltration Tests 
In order to provide information on the infiltration rates of the on-site soils, we 
conducted four falling head infiltration tests at the boring locations shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2.  These tests were performed in general accordance with the procedures in 
Appendix D of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment, “Stormwater 
Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (Rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with 
other state’s procedures and may generally be considered an industry standard. 
The procedures used for the infiltration tests consisted of augering boreholes to 
depths of about 5 and 10 feet below the existing ground surface. After reaching the test 
depth, a solid PVC pipe, or well casing, was installed in the borehole flush with the bottom 
so that water could only infiltrate into the soil through the exposed bottom soil. 
Water was placed into the open pipe and allowed to pre-saturate the soil prior to 
conducting the infiltration test.  Falling head infiltration tests were performed to determine 
the infiltration rates of the underlying subsurface materials.  Each test consisted of 
four trials of filling the casing with approximately 24 inches of water and taking periodic 
readings over a 1-hour trial period.  Results of the infiltration tests are provided on 
Plates C-1 through C-4.
