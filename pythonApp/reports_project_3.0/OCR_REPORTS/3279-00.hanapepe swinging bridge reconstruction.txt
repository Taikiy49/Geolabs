INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Hanapepe Swinging Bridge Reconstruction in the area of
Hanapepe on the island of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed bridge reconstruction project These recommendations are
intended for design input only and may not be appropriate to form the basis for
construction cost estimates nor for construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project involves the reconstruction of the Hanapepe Swinging
Bridge which was damaged by Hurricane Iniki in 1992 Currently only remnants of the
bridge such as the bridge abutments exist at the site It is proposed to reconstruct a
similar bridge at the exact location of the previously damaged bridge Where feasible
the existing bridge abutments at the site will be used in the construction of the
replacement bridge
The proposed replacement bridge will be a suspension type bridge supported by
two towers with deadman anchors The bridge stretches across Hanapepe River with
a span of 210 feet and is about 3 feet wide The bridge is a pedestrian foot bridge
We anticipate the structural loads for the bridge to be generally light Based on
information provided to us we understand that the maximum compression load on the
tower foundations will be on the order of about 40 kips The estimated maximum
pullout load due to the cable tension is on the order of 60 kips

We anticipate that grading of the site will not be required in the construction of
the replacement bridge However minor earthwork such as backfill around foundations
and abutments are anticipated in the bridge reconstruction

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of the lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene
more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3279 00 Page 5
of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic series cover most of the eastern half of the island
of Kauai Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic series are generally characterized as thick flows
of dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various
locales Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low
cinder cones at the vent
During the Pleistocene there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available
to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea
levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Kauai remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to present sea level on Kauai
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
In the area of the project alluvial soils associated with the Hanapepe River have
accumulated on the basalt rock formation The alluvial deposits generally consist of
clayey silts sandy silts and silty sands

SITE DESCRIPTION
The proposed project site is located in the Town of Hanapepe on the island of
Kauai Hawaii The locations of the bridge foundations are presently vacant and
vegetated with bushes and grasses Based on our field exploration and the
construction drawings prepared by Associates Inc
dated for the previous bridge the existing ground surface elevations
range from approximately 5 to 18 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
The abutments of the damaged bridge are visible on the south and north banks
of Hanapepe River The north abutment is behind the levee fronting Hanapepe River
and the south abutment is on the levee on the town side A concrete wall of about 3 to
4 feet high was also observed along the top of the south levee
EXISTING BRIDGE STRUCTURES
The previous Hanapepe Swinging Bridge was damaged by Hurricane Iniki in
1992 and only remnants of the bridge currently exist at the site We understand that
it is desired to replace the damaged bridge with a similar bridge constructed at the
exact location Therefore the existing abutments and foundations will be used in the
reconstruction of the replacement bridge where feasible The exposed portions of the
bridge abutments appear to be in relatively good condition However the structural
integrity of the abutment and foundation should be verified by a structural engineer
Based on the construction drawings provided the previous swinging bridge
consisted of a tower and an anchor system at each abutment with a span of about
210 feet between the two towers The north abutment consisted of Tower No 2 and
a deadman anchor to support the bridge Tower No 2 is about 28 feet high and is
supported on a pile foundation Based on the available plans we understand the pile

27 feet long The pile tip elevation is shown on the plans at elevation 20 7 feet MSL
The existing north deadman anchor is shown on the plans to be 12 feet long 6 feet

are buried below the ground surface therefore the size and condition of the existing

plan dimensions of 9 feet by 9 feet and 2 feet thick was used for the bridge

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the proposed project site is underlain by soft to
medium stiff and or loose to medium dense soils extending to depths of about 60 to 70
feet below the existing ground surface The soft and loose soils encountered in the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3279 00 Page 9
borings are potentially compressible when subjected to new loads Additional loads
due to filling and or new structural loads would induce consolidation of the underlying
compressible soils resulting in settlement of the bridge tower and levee Therefore
new fills around the bridge structure or significant additional loads on the bridge
structure should be avoided
As previously indicated a swinging bridge of similar dimensions and loads to the
damaged bridge is planned to be reconstructed at the same location The exposed
portions of the existing tower foundation and the deadman anchor system appear to be
in relatively good condition Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered in
the borings and the assumed foundation depths as described in the Existing Bridge
Structures section we believe that the existing tower foundation and deadman anchor
system at the north abutment Tower No 2 may be reused to support the structural
loads of the new bridge provided that the foundations are in good condition The
structural integrity of the foundation and anchor system should be verified by the
structural engineer
Tower No 1 on the south side is supported on a shallow foundation system
bearing in the levee fill material Based on Boring No 1 the levee fill appears to be
bearing on potentially compressible alluvial soils We did not observe significant
distress of the exposed portions of the existing foundations during our field exploration
Based on the granular nature of the subsoils it is believed that settlement of the
subsoils due to the loads of the levee fill and bridge structure has completed It is our
understanding that the damaged bridge was in service up to 1992 with no significant
distress associated with settlement of the south tower foundation Therefore it appears
that the south bridge tower may be constructed on the existing foundations provided
that the structural loads are the same as the previous bridge damaged by Hurricane
Iniki and no new fills are planned around the tower foundation If distress is found in
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3279 00 Page 10
the existing foundations on the south bank use of the existing shallow foundation to
support the new bridge should be reevaluated Therefore we recommend that the
structural integrity of the foundation be verified by the structural engineer
For the anchor system at the south abutment the existing concrete slab anchor
is shown on the plans as 9 feet by 9 feet in plan dimension by 2 feet deep Based on
our preliminary evaluation the existing anchor does not appear to be adequate to resist
the anticipated lateral loads Therefore it is our opinion that a new anchor block will
be required to resist the uplift and lateral loads imposed on the deadman anchor

Foundations for North Abutment Tower No 2
As previously indicated Tower No 2 is supported on a pile foundation system
consisting of four 12 inch square precast concrete piles of approximately 27 feet long
We believe that the driven piles are deriving their capacities from friction between the
pile shaft and the surrounding soils Based on our field exploration data laboratory test
results and the above assumptions we believe that each of the driven piles is capable
of providing allowable capacities of 30 kips in compression and 10 kips in tension The
structural capacity of these four piles should be evaluated by the design engineer The
allowable compressive load capacity for the piles is for dead plus live loads and may
be increased by one third for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces
We anticipate that settlements of the pile foundations will result primarily from
elastic compression of the pile member and subgrade response We estimate that the
total settlement of the pile supported foundation to be less than 1 inch with differential
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3279 00 Page 11
settlements between piles not exceeding about one half that amount We believe that
these settlements would be essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are
applied
Lateral loads imposed on the Tower No 2 foundation may be resisted by a
combination of 1 the resistance of the pile soil system to deflect 2 passive
resistance against the vertical faces of foundation elements such as pile caps and
abutments and 3 friction between the pile cap and the supporting subgrade Passive
resistance against the vertical faces of pile caps may be estimated using an equivalent
fluid pressure of 120 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f due to the sloping
ground conditions in front of the abutment The frictional sliding resistance between the
pile cap and the supporting subgrade may be evaluated using a coefficient of sliding
friction of 0 3
Lateral load resistance for driven piles is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the pile allowable deflection at the top of the pile and
induced moment in the pile The lateral load capacity of the 12 inch square precast
concrete pile should be on the order of 5 kips with an associated horizontal deflection
of inch assumed at the top of the pile based on a free head condition The maximum
induced moment as a result of the lateral load is 40 kip feet The maximum moment
can be assumed to occur at a depth of about 12 feet below the top of the pile
Deadman Anchor for North Abutment Tower No 2
The deadman anchor is used to resist both lateral and uplift loads imposed on
the bridge Lateral loads acting on the deadman anchor may be resisted by a
combination of 1 sliding resistance of the deadman anchor and the supporting
subgrade 2 passive pressure against the front face of the anchor and 3 side shear
on the sides of the anchor block The frictional sliding resistance between the

Earthwork
We understand that site grading is not planned at the site As previously
indicated additional filling at the site is not recommended and should be avoided If
new fills are planned Geolabs Hawaii should be consulted for supplemental recom
mendations pertaining to the new fills and bridge foundations We anticipate that some
earthwork such as backfill of the foundation excavation and abutment structure may
be required in the bridge reconstruction project
In general areas that are to receive fills or are subgrades for improvements
should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting grub spoil
material should be disposed of properly off site After clearing and grubbing all
subgrades should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches properly moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
The on site materials may be used as backfill materials provided that they
contain no lumps or fragments greater than 3 inches in greatest dimension Cobbles
and boulders if encountered should not be used as backfill material Where used as
backfill material the on site soils should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches
in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Backfills behind abutment
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3279 00 Page 16
walls should be compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction Over
compaction of the abutment wall backfill would exert additional lateral earth pressures
on the walls and should be avoided
Any imported soil and the structural fill should be select fill Import materials
should be tested and approved by Geolabs Hawaii prior to delivery to the job site
Imported granular materials such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand may
be used The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles
larger than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a laboratory CBR
value of 15 or more with a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 In addition the fill material should
contain a maximum of 20 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve Imported soils
should be brought to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction All surfaces should be finished to create smooth
unyielding subgrades and should be kept moist until covered by concrete slabs or
pavements
