This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed New Fuel Tanks Project located within the Aloha Petroleum
Ltd Hilo Terminal in Hilo on the island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses The recommendations presented herein are intended for design of
foundations site grading and trench excavation and backfill only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATION
The project site is located off Kalanianaole Avenue within the existing
Aloha Petroleum Terminal in Hilo on the island of Hawaii It is proposed to construct two
new steel tanks with a capacity of about 10 000 barrels bbl for each tank and associated
piping network Based on the information provided we understand that the new fuel
tanks will have a diameter of about 49 feet and a height of about 32 feet The roof
structure of the new tanks will span between the perimeter wall and the center column

Details of the proposed project was not available at the time this report was
prepared However based on the available information and site conditions we anticipate
that grading at the proposed site would generally require cuts and fills of less than 7 feet
to achieve a level pad for the new fuel tanks
Our previous experience in the area suggest that the site may be underlain by
near surface soils overlying basalt rock formation at shallow depths Therefore special
attention was given to the characteristics of the basalt formation with respect to
foundation support for the proposed tank structures

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3568 00 Page 5
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the eastern flank of Mauna Loa The lava formation
encountered at the site appears to be of pahoehoe flow which spread and ponded as it
approached the ocean Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy
surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities voids are commonly
encountered in pahoehoe lavas These cavities voids were formed when the lava was
still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities voids and
inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes intra flow cavities are
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are
generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalts are generally considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit
water quite readily in the horizontal and vertical directions Water is normally transmitted
through the porous rock matrix along fractures and cavities voids and along clinker
layers Therefore the permeability of the basalt will be highly dependent on the presence
of fractures cavities and clinker layers

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the proposed fuel tank site were explored by
drilling coring and sampling two borings to depths of about 20 and 28 feet below the
existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2
Based on our field exploration medium dense to dense silty sands and sandy
gravels with cobbles and boulders were generally encountered to depths of about to
5 feet below the existing ground surface at the proposed fuel tank sites Below the
near surface soils hard basalt formation was encountered in the borings extending to the
maximum depths drilled of about 20 and 28 feet below the ground surface The basalt
formation encountered ranged from slightly to severely jointed and ranged from slightly to
moderately weathered Based on our field exploration it appears that cavities voids of
about 1 to 3 feet in height were encountered at depths ranging from about 14 to
27 feet in Boring No 2 However it should be emphasized that the vertical and lateral
extent of cavities voids in the basalt formation can be highly variable
Groundwater was measured at a depth of about 10 feet below the ground surface
in Boring No 1 at the time of our field exploration However groundwater was not

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions at the project site consist
generally of a thin cover of soils overlying hard basalt formation with some cavities voids
at greater depths Based on our evaluation of the subsurface conditions with respect to
the proposed fuel tank structures the principal geotechnical engineering considerations
for the proposed tank structures include the following
1 foundation support for the proposed tank structures
2 proof rolling of the subgrades beneath the foundations and
3 trench excavation and backfill of utility lines
Based on our exploration we believe that a shallow foundation system bearing on
the basalt formation encountered at shallow depths or bearing on a compacted subgrade
may be used to support the fuel tank structures Due to the potential for presence of
cavities and or voids as encountered in the basalt formation at the project site we
recommend that the foundation subgrade and subgrades to receive fill be proof rolled
with a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy equipment a minimum of 8 passes to aid
in detecting and collapsing near surface cavities and or voids A representative from
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3568 00 Page 8
Geolabs Hawaii should observe the proof rolling operation to evaluate the presence of
cavities voids below the subgrade level Because of the relatively low bearing pressures
needed for footings bearing on basalt formation the requirement for probing and grouting
the foundation locations may be omitted
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report
Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we recommend that a shallow
foundation system bearing on the basalt formation or compacted structural fill be used to
support the new fuel tank structures The walls of the new tank structures may be
supported on a perimeter ring footing embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent grade The perimeter ring footing should be bearing entirely on basalt
rock formation or entirely on compacted structural fill The interior of the tank structure
and the center column may be supported on a compacted subgrade prepared as
recommended below An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 p s f may be used
for design of foundations bearing directly on the basalt formation An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 4 000 p s f may also be used for design of foundations bearing on the
compacted structural fill These allowable bearing pressures are for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as temporary wind
and or seismic forces
Because of the potential for presence of cavities and or voids in the basalt
formation below the tank locations the subgrade should be proof rolled with a Caterpillar
D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy equipment a minimum of 8 passes to aid in detecting and
collapsing near surface cavities and or voids prior to construction of tank footings To
provide more uniform support for the tank structures bearing partially on basalt formation
and partially on fill we recommend that a minimum of 12 inches of compacted structural
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3568 00 Page 9
fill be provided below the ring footing which straddles cut and fill conditions The
structural fill should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91
We anticipate that the bottom of the tank structure will be supported on a bedding
layer of sand or similar material to provide more uniform support of the steel tank
structures We recommend that the sand bedding layer consist of a uniformly graded
sand of 4 to 6 inches in thickness The sand bedding should be compacted using a
vibratory plate to provide a firm base for the bottom plate of the tank structure
Where the foundations are to be located adjacent to new or existing utilities the
foundations should extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane projected
upward from the bottom of the invert of the utility pipe This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent utilities with additional structural loads and to reduce the
potential for foundation settlement
If the footings are designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total settlement of footings is estimated to be on the order of less than 1 inch with
differential settlements on the order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 4 may be used for footings bearing on the basalt formation
and or compacted subgrade Resistance to lateral loads due to passive pressure may be
calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 400 pounds per square foot per foot of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3568 00 Page 10
depth p c f for footings embedded in compacted fills This value assumes that the soil
around the footings is relatively undisturbed or well compacted Unless covered by
pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade
should be neglected
Site Grading
Based on the information provided we anticipate that grading at the proposed site
will consist of cuts and fills of less than 7 feet Site grading operations should be
observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii to evaluate whether the exposed soil
conditions are similar to those encountered in our exploration The following
recommendations are intended to provide guidelines for the design of site grading work
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site The stockpile materials
consisting of rubbish concrete debris cobbles and boulders observed at the site should
be completely removed to expose firm ground
Following clearing and grubbing the exposed subgrade should be proof rolled with
a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy equipment a minimum of 8 passes to aid in
cavities voids disclosed during proof rolling should be backfilled with compacted fill or
grouted with lean concrete
Generally the on site granular materials may be used as a source of fill and
backfill materials provided that they are free of rubbish concrete debris and other
unsuitable materials They should also contain no particles larger than 6 inches in
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3568 00 Page 11
maximum dimension Where used as fill and or backfill material the on site granular soils
should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
We envision that imported material may be needed for the proposed construction
Any imported material should be tested and approved by Geolabs Hawaii for its intended
use prior to delivery to the job site Where needed imported granular materials should
consist of crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be
well graded from coarse to fine with no particles greater than 3 inches in largest
dimension The material should have a laboratory CBR value of at least 20 and a swell
value of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1883 The imported material should also be moisture conditioned and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
The finished grades outside the new tanks should be sloped to shed water away
from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Drainage swales should be
provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain all surface run off away from the
tank foundations
Utilities
We envision that new utility lines and or piping network will be required for the
proposed project For pipes bearing on the dense soils and basalt formation encountered
in the borings a poorly graded granular bedding material with little fines such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation S4C or equivalent is recommended
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3568 00 Page 12
below the pipes as bedding material Other alternate pipe bedding material should
consist of granular material containing minimal fines and no particles larger than 1 inch in
maximum dimension Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for the trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce
compaction of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipe to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of granular material containing
no particles greater than 6 inches in largest dimension Trench backfill should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in 12 inch maximum level
loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to
reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are located below the
fuel tank structures and pavement areas the trench backfill should be compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

Field Exploration
ee ee er eee
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling coring and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
depths of our borings ranged about 20 and 287 feet below the existing ground surface
The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight
augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch O D standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Boring as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Boring as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
