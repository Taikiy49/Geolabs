EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
This basis of design report was developed in support of the use of Horizontal
Directional Drilling HDD methods to install a fiber optic cable conduit from Ford Island to
the Pearl Harbor Naval Shipyard across the Pearl Harbor Main Channel This HDD drive
will be referred to as the Harbor Crossing
The installation of the fiber optic conduit consists of an HDD drive of 610 meters in
length The drive alignment will have both entry and exit angles of about 12 degrees The
maximum on shore ground cover of the drive is about 24 meters and the maximum
offshore channel cover is about 11 meters
Based on the available borings drilled along the proposed Harbor Crossing
alignment for the adjacent sewer force main project MCON Project P 491 the
subsurface materials anticipated during the HDD conduit installation consist of fill volcanic
tuff coralline deposits coralline sandstone and limestone alluvial deposits and estuarine
deposits Groundwater was encountered in the borings ranging from about 0 6 to
3 2 meters below the existing ground surface
During the drilling operations of the fiber optic conduit one of the critical
considerations for the HDD operations is that the drilling fluid pressure in the borehole
should not exceed the hydraulic fracture pressure of the in situ soil rock formation If
hydraulic fracture of the surrounding formation occurs there is a higher potential for heave
and or blowout of the ground surface or channel bottom during the drilling operations In
addition the fiber optic conduit to be installed must be able to withstand the loads imposed
on the conduit during the service life of the conduit For the long term or service life
loading condition 50 year design life we considered the external pressure due to earth
and live loads acting on the conduit
Based on the results of our analyses we believe that a steel Grade G 105 drill rod
or casing with a nominal outside diameter of 127 mm and a nominal wall thickness of
may be installed for the fiber optic cable conduit using HDD methods below the
Pearl Harbor Main Channel from Ford Island to the Pearl Harbor Naval Shipyard

SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This basis of design report presents the results of our engineering analyses
performed for the Fiber Optic Conduit Installation by Horizontal Directional Drilling from
Ford Island to the Pearl Harbor Naval Shipyard in the Pearl Harbor area on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This basis of design report summarizes our general assumptions design
considerations and design analyses from our review of the available geotechnical
engineering reports and literature for the proposed installation of the fiber optic conduit by
Horizontal Directional Drilling HDD methods
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is located across the Pearl Harbor Main Channel from Ford Island
to the Pearl Harbor Naval Shipyard PHNSY in the Pearl Harbor area on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii Based on the information provided we understand that it is planned to
install a fiber optic cable from Ford Island to the Pearl Harbor Naval Shipyard The
approximate location of the project site is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the fiber optic cable will traverse below the Pearl Harbor Main Channel
from Ford Island to PHNSY Based on the available information the fiber optic cable will
be encased in a conduit consisting of a steel drill rod or casing with an outside diameter
of 127 millimeters mm We understand that the steel casing conduit will be installed by
HDD methods The fiber optic conduit alignment will be offset by about 20 feet to the west
from the Sewer Force Main B alignment installed under the FYO1 MCON Project P 491
project
In general a pilot hole will be drilled using drill rods of Grade G 105 steel with a
nominal outside diameter of 127 mm and nominal wall thickness of from
Ford Island to PHNSY After completion of the pilot hole the steel drill rods will be left
in place and will be utilized as the steel casing conduit for the fiber optic cable This basis

SECTION 2 0 HORIZONTAL DIRECTIONAL DRILLING HDD DRIVE
The Harbor Crossing consists of a 610 meter drive commencing at the open area
between Building 98 and First Street PHNSY and terminating at a parking lot located
south of Intrepid Boulevard Ford Island The HDD drive alignment includes an
approximately 12 degree entry angle maximum on shore ground cover of about
24 meters maximum offshore cover to the mudline of about 11 meters and an exit
angle of about 12 degrees The ground surface elevation near the entry and exit pits
ranges from about 2 2 to 3 4 meters Mean Sea Level MSL The elevation of the
mudline within the Pearl Harbor Main Channel along the alignment ranges from about
2 0 to 15 5 meters MSL
OW 2 OW 3 and OW 4 of Reference 1 were used to characterize the subsurface
conditions for this drive The approximate locations of the borings used in our analyses
are shown on Plates 3 1 through 3 4 Based on the borings the Harbor Crossing
alignment near the entry pit may encounter fill materials consisting of medium dense to
dense silty sands extending to a depth of about 1 5 meters below the existing ground
surface The fill materials may be underlain by low to moderately hard volcanic tuff
extending to a depth of about 4 5 meters below the existing ground surface The
volcanic tuff may be underlain by hard to very hard coralline limestone and sandstone
The coralline limestone and sandstone were generally closely to moderately fractured
The Harbor Crossing alignment near the south side of the channel edge may
encounter coralline deposits consisting of medium dense gravel and sands with varying
amounts of silt and alluvial deposits consisting of stiff to very stiff silts and clays
Moderately hard coralline sandstone and limestone may be encountered from depths of
about 3 4 to 6 9 meters below the ground level
The Harbor Crossing alignment below the harbor channel may encounter
estuarine deposits consisting of very soft clay and alluvial deposits consisting of very
soft silts and clays

The Harbor Crossing alignment near the north side of the channel edge may
encounter alluvial deposits consisting of medium stiff to very stiff clays and silts and
medium dense to dense clayey sands and gravel The Harbor Crossing alignment near
the exit pit may encounter surface fill materials consisting of medium dense to dense
silty sands underlain by highly weathered volcanic tuff very dense silty sands with
varying amounts of gravel cobbles and boulders The above materials were underlain
by coralline deposits and hard coralline sandstone which was generally closely to
moderately fractured The coralline deposits generally consisted of loose to medium
dense silty gravel and sand
Groundwater was encountered at depths of about 0 6 to 3 2 meters below the
ground surface at the time the borings were conducted The groundwater levels
encountered in the borings correspond to about Elevations 0 2 to 0 9 meters MSL
Based on the proposed Harbor Crossing alignment and available utility
information we do not anticipate encountering underground utility lines during drilling of
the pilot hole We envision that the soil conditions for the entry and exit pits would be in
the fill materials consisting of medium dense to very dense silty sand extending to
depths of about 1 to 1 5 meters below the existing ground surface followed by slightly to
highly weathered volcanic tuff extending to depths of about 2 6 to 3 meters below the
existing ground surface Deeper excavations for the pits may encounter moderately
hard to hard coralline sandstone which is closely fractured In general we believe that
normal heavy excavation equipment such as backhoes may be used to excavate the fill
materials and the highly weathered volcanic tuff It should be noted that use of a
hoeram may be required for excavations in the hard volcanic tuff and moderately hard
to hard coralline sandstone

SECTION 3 0 DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS
3 1 Installation Considerations
One of the most critical considerations during the drilling of the pilot hole is to
determine whether the drilling fluid pressure in the borehole will exceed the hydraulic
fracture pressure of the in situ soil or rock formation The drilling fluid pressure is the sum
of the hydrostatic pressure and circulation pressure required to overcome friction losses
during flow of the drilling fluid If the drilling fluid pressure exceeds the hydraulic fracture
pressure of the in situ soil or rock formation there is a potential for heave and or blowout
of the ground surface or channel bottom
3 2 Service Life Considerations
During the service life of the steel casing for the fiber optic conduit long term loads
are imposed on the steel drill rods that are utilized as casings for the fiber optic conduit
The long term load that will need to be considered is the external pressure due to earth
and live loads imposed on the steel drill rod casing
After drilling of the pilot hole the drilled hole may deform considerably or even
collapse Drilled hole deformation is normally limited when arching in the soil is
mobilized usually in semi consolidated or consolidated soils Where arching in the soil
develops the earth pressure applied to the pipe is less than the overburden pressure
Typically a depth of cover of at least five pipe diameters above the top of the pipe
would be sufficient to develop arching in the soil In unconsolidated soils we anticipate
only minimal arching in the soil to occur When minimal arching in the soil is expected
to occur the earth pressure applied to the pipe is equal to the overburden pressure

Drilling Fluid Pressure versus Hydraulic Fracture Pressure
During drilling operations the drilling slurry exerts a pressure in the borehole If
this pressure exceeds the hydraulic fracture pressure of the in situ soil rock
formation then heave and or blowout of the ground surface may occur Drilling
fluid pressure is determined from the sum of the hydrostatic pressure and
circulation pressure of the drilling fluid The hydraulic fracture pressure of the soil

soil The computer output of the bottom hole pressure versus hydraulic fracture

External Pressure Due to Earth and Live Loads
We are not aware of published reports that provide acceptable methods of
calculating the earth loads imposed on HDD installed pipes To estimate the
earth pressure on an HDD pipe a conservative method to estimate the earth
pressure on a jack and bored pipe developed by Stein et al 1989 may be used
This external pressure was compared to the critical buckling pressure of the pipe
using a factor of safety of 2 These calculations are presented in Appendix B

TruTracker
Surface Monitoring Signal Inducing System
Sharewell s TruTracker System provides an
accurate determination of the location of the drill bit
during drilling operations even in areas of magnetic
interference
This precise location of the drill bit is independent of
the driller s survey calculations Information provided is
not affected by limited amounts of outside magnetic
interference
TruTracker is designed for application in pipeline
river crossing utility and environmental drilling It is
used in conjunction with Sharewell s downhole Magnetic
Guidance System MGS TruTracker is a surface
monitoring and signal inducing system which
communicates with the downhole MGS probe to
determine the probe s position
The TruTracker System allows the driller to operate
with known positions and remain on course in areas
which previously had to be drilled blindly
Boring with critical exits can now be performed with
confidence when using the TruTracker System

Magnetic Guidance System
Sharewell s new Magnetic Guidance System MGS is
a directional steering system that provides real time
information for the Horizontal Directional Drilling Industry
The MGS provides the contractor with known
positioning by producing immediate information on tool
face azimuth horizontal angle and inclination vertical
angle This data is processed at the surface by steering
software to provide information on depth course length
and distance off a predetermined azimuth
Magnetic Guidance System
The MGS system has five main components A probe
driller s console interface unit computer and printer
The probe is located in a non magnetic bottom hole
assembly that typically includes a collar orientation sub
and a bent sub with a bit when jetting or a mud motor
Information from the probe is sent up to the surface via
a single conductor wireline The information is processed
at the interface unit and simultaneously transmitted to
the driller s console and computer to provide continuous
information on the borehole orientation
Horizontal Directional Drilling Contractors benefit by
using Sharewell s TruTracker System which is a
secondary independent verification of the boreholes
path TruTracker can be operated at depths in excess of
100 feet and is accurate to 2 of the vertical depth of
the probe

R K WATKINS Ph D
Associate Director Engineering Experiment Station Utah State University
23 1 INTRODUCTION
The designer of buried structures must consider the com
posite performance of both the structure and the soil in
which it is buried The structural phenomenon is usually
referred to as soil structure interaction Of course the soil
exerts pressure on the structure and if the structure is tela
tively noncompressible compared to the soil the structure
will feel pressure concentrations a hard spot develops in the
compressible soil On the other hand if the structure com
presses or deforms under the soil load soil pressure on the
structure may be relieved by the protective strength of the
soil The soil actually arches over the structure like a ma
sonry arch In fact masonry is but soil possibly with a
binder added to it Arching action of the soil thus reduces
the pressure on the structure Arching action depends on
soil strength and on the relative compression of soil and
structure and compression of the structure depends on
arching action of the soil Obviously the phenomenon is
statically indeterminate to a high degree so performance
data and simplified analyses are necessary
For the purposes of design it is convenient to classify
the buried structure as either rigid or flexible The rigid
structure is assumed to retain its original shape without de
forming compressing which may seem to be idealistic but
isanimportant limiting condition conservative with respect
to stress and is closely approximated if as is often the case
the ratio of soil compressibility to structural compressibility
is high
It is convenient to classify the enveloping soil as either
compressible or noncompressible The noncompressible soil
case is of relatively minor importance Most soils exhibit
some compressibility Noncompressible soil includes every
thing from a noncompressible liquid to an absolutely rigid
solid The design of a structure buried in a rigid solid soil is
trivial if not inconceivable and will not be considered here
However there are some noncompressible liquid soil cases
where design may be based on an assumed hydrostatic soil
pressure Such a case is unconsolidated saturated muck
It may be unconsolidated because of inadequate time for
consolidation or because of inadequate drainage

PRESSURE IN STEEL PIPE DESIGN
Water pipe must be designed to withstand the
conditions of service This section presumes that
the quantity of water and the route have been
determined in order to specifically address the
design of wall thickness to meet those
requirements
Nearly all plate and sheet steel mills today use the
continuous casting process which produces a high
quality uniform ductile material Steel water pipe is
manufactured from steel with yield strengths in the
range of 30 000 to 50 000 psi Higher strength
steels cost slightly more If pipeline design pressures
exceed 150 psi higher strength steels should be
analyzed for cost effectiveness Higher strength
steels have little advantage for low pressure lines
where wall thickness is governed by external loads or
handling
The most important step is in determining the wall
thickness is internal water pressure Once that
thickness is determined the pipe is checked for
external pressure or vacuum Deflection due to
backfill is also checked Adjustments can be made
to increase composite wall thickness or backfill
quality if required
When pipe is checked for deflection the Iowa
formula is used This formula uses a term called E
which is soil modulus has been greatly misused
throughout the years is not measurable it is a
derived empirical number Buried flexible pipe is
supported by a soil envelope and will not deflect
more than the soil around the pipe as discussed in
the 1996 edition of AISI Steel Plate Engineering
Data Volume 3 Welded Steel Pipe Soils
engineering can be used to determine the soil
properties deflections and loads on the pipe
The pipe can then be checked for deflection based
on appropriate E values
