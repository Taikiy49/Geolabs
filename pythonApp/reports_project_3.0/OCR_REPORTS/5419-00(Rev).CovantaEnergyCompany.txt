SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered fill materials
extending to depths of about 4 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface. The fill
materials consisted of very stiff silt and dense silty coralline sand with some gravel. The fill
materials were underlain by coralline detritus and coral formation extending to the
maximum depths explored. The coral formation ranged from dense to medium hard and
the coralline detritus ranged from medium dense to dense. Coralline detritus and coral
formation were encountered from the ground surface to the maximum depths explored at
the undeveloped lot. Groundwater was encountered at depths of about 4.4 to 5.4 feet
below the existing ground surface at the undeveloped lot and about 11.8 to 13.1 feet
below the existing ground surface south of the existing plant facility.
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered during our exploration, we
recommend that the new structures planned at the project site be supported on a shallow
foundation system consisting of spread and/or continuous footings and mat foundations
bearing on either the recompacted fill material or the coralline detritus/coral formation. An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for foundations bearing on the
recompacted fill material. If a higher allowable bearing pressure is desired, the
foundations may extend down to an elevation of approximately +7 feet MSL to bear on the
coralline detritus/coral formation. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 5,000 psf may be
used for foundations bearing on the coralline detritus/coral formation. These bearing
values may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads such as those caused by
wind and/or seismic forces.
Cavities and/or voids are commonly encountered in the coral formations underlying
the project site. Due to the potential for presence of voids and/or cavities, we recommend
that the subgrades underneath future structures, slabs, and pavements be proof-rolled
with a minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of six passes to detect the
presence of potential near-surface cavities or loose zones. The vibratory drum roller
should be operated at a speed not faster than 300 feet per minute. In addition, a program
of probing and grouting at the footing locations should be implemented during construction
to reduce the potential for loss of foundation support due to the potential for presence of
cavities at the site. The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and
specific recommendations for the design of the project.

This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Convanta Energy Company, H-Power Expansion project located in
the Ewa area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.

expansion project. We envision that both asphaltic and Portland Cement Concrete will
be used for the new pavements.

2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes - Waianae and Koolau. The older volcano, Waianae, forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield, Koolau, forms the majority of
the eastern two-thirds of the island. As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased, lava flows
from Koolau banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, now
known as the Schofield Plateau or Central Oahu.
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau, generating
sediments, which were transported to the coast. In the vicinity of the project site and
further south, these alluvial sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine
sediments and coral/algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging in
thickness from zero feet in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to over
1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining
formation, or “caprock,” over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu. Deposition
of these sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present.
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level.
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials. Land development and

2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but
most of the earthquakes are so small that they could only be detected by
instruments. However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and
a few cause minor to moderate damage.
In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on the
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over
90 percent of earthquakes) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of high seismic
activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the
Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the
Island of Hawaii.
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the
Islands of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example,
several small landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui

2.4.2 Soil Profile
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered stiff/dense surface
fills of about 4 to 5 feet thick across the project site. The surface fills were generally
underlain by medium dense to dense coralline detritus and dense/medium hard
coral formation extending to the maximum depths explored of about 43 feet below
the existing ground surface.
Based on the average penetration resistance (N-Values) of the subsurface
materials encountered below the foundation subgrade level and the geology of the
area, we believe that the project site may be classified as a “Stiff Soil Profile.”
Therefore, we believe that the seismic design of the building structures at the
project site will need to be designed based on a Soil Profile Type Sp in accordance
with Table 16-J of the Uniform Building Code (1997).
2.4.3 Liquefaction Potential
Liquefaction occurs when a build-up of excess pore water pressure is induced on
saturated cohesionless soils resulting in a substantial loss of strength. The most
common cause of this phenomenon is the cyclic loading due to earthquakes. This
process may cause both vertical and horizontal movements in the soil, and if not
confined, can result in significant deformations. Loose, uniformly graded,
fine-grained sands and loose silts with little cohesion are most susceptible to
liquefaction. Generally, liquefaction is not likely to occur if the loose deposit is

3.1. Building Structures
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, the
structures proposed for the H-Power Expansion project may be supported on shallow
foundation systems consisting of spread footings, continuous footings, and/or mat
foundations.
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grades. If foundations are located next to utility trenches or
easements, they should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V)
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep
as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below-grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the
potential for foundation settlement.
Where the new structures abut the existing structures, the new footings should
be embedded to the same depth as the footings of the existing structure to avoid
surcharging the existing footings. However, the new footings should not extend by
more than 6 inches below the existing footings to reduce the potential for undermining
the existing footings causing a loss in foundation support. If the new footings need to
extend below the existing footings, the new footings should be kept outside of a
45-degree stress influence zone extending downward from the edge of the existing
footing to avoid underpinning the existing footings.
The soils encountered at the footing subgrade level should be compacted to
provide a firm and unyielding base. If soft and/or loose materials are encountered at the
footing subgrades, the materials should be over-excavated to expose the underlying
firm materials. The resulting over-excavation should be backfilled with select granular

3.1.1. Spread and/or Continuous Footings
We recommend that the new structures such as the boiler, scrubber, baghouse,
and other smaller structures be supported on shallow spread and/or continuous
footings. For spread and/or continuous footings bearing on the recompacted fill
materials, an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot
(psf) may be used for design. If a higher allowable bearing pressure is desired, the
footings may extend down to an elevation of approximately +7 feet MSL to bear on
the coralline detritus/coral formation. An allowable bearing pressure of up to
5,000 psf may be used for design of the spread and/or continuous footings bearing
on the dense/medium hard coralline detritus and/or coral formation. These bearing
values are for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by
passive earth pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation
system. Coefficient of friction of 0.45 and 0.35 may be used for footings bearing on
the coralline detritus/coral formation and recompacted fills, respectively.
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using and equivalent
fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). This assumes
that the soil around footings is well-compacted. Unless covered by pavements or
slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected.

elevation of approximately +7 feet MSL to bear on the coralline detritus/coral

foundations bearing on the corralline detritus/coral formation. These bearing values

loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. A modulus of subgrade

Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by
passive earth pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation
system. A coefficient of friction of 0.385 may be used for footings bearing on the
recompacted fills and a coefficient of friction of 0.45 may be used for footings

pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pcf. This
assumes that the soil around the footings is well-compacted. Unless covered by
pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be
neglected.

3.2 Foundation Probing
Our experience suggests that cavities and/or voids are commonly encountered in
the coral formations underlying the project site. To reduce the potential for loss of
foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities and/or sinkholes below
foundations, we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting at the
foundation locations be implemented during construction.
For estimating purposes, we recommend that probe holes be drilled at each
spread footing location (one probe hole for every 50 square feet of footing area) and at
10 feet on-center along wall foundations. The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in
diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation
elevation. If cavities and/or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing
operation, additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacings to aid in
delineating the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and/or void. The probe holes and
cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand-cement grout injected at low to
moderate pressures. We recommend that a low strength sand-cement grout with a
slump range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the grouting operations. In-lieu of the
sand-cement grout, fluid lean concrete, such as controlled low strength material
(flowable fill) may also be used for the grouting operation.
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and/or voids at the site, we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding. In addition, the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and
grouting operation is complete. The contractor should also be made aware that a
longer lag time between probing/grouting operations and foundation construction might
be required in the construction schedule.

3.5 Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography, we anticipate that minimal site grading work
will be needed to achieve the design grades for the proposed project. At the on-set of
earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and

3.10 Corrosion Protection for Underground Utilities and Structures
Two sets of pH, resistivity, and chloride and sulfate content tests were performed
to evaluate the corrosivity characteristics of the subsurface soils encountered at the
project site. The selected samples were tested by Oceanic Analytical Laboratory, Inc.
to determine the pH value, resistivity, and the chloride and sulfate contents. The pH
value was determined in general accordance with EPA SW9045B test method. The
resistivity was determined in general accordance with EPA 120.1. The chloride and
sulfate contents were determined in general accordance with EPA 300.0 test method.
Results of the soil analyses (pH, resistivity, chloride content, and sulfate content)
conducted on selected soil samples are summarized on Plate B-1 of this report.
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In
general, the lower the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried
metallic structure. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will
contribute to corrosion to metallic objects.
Based on the resistivity values of the soils encountered at the project site, the
subsurface materials at the site may be classified as very corrosive for buried metallic
structures.
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and
structures is dependent on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in the
soil. For underground concrete pipelines or structures, the laboratory test results also
indicate that the soils encountered at the project site may be corrosive to underground
concrete pipelines and structures.

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
five borings extending to depths ranging from about 11.0 to 43.0 feet below the existing
ground surface. The borings were drilled at the approximate locations shown on the
Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were advanced to the desired depths using truck-mounted
drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer, who observed the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer
falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586, Penetration Test and
Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
Core samples of the coral formations encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral formation. The material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description.
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties. The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths.
Two sets of Corrosion tests, including pH (EPA SW9045B), Minimum Resistivity
(EPA 120.1), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) were
performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. The results of
these tests are summarized on Plate B-1.
Three Sieve Analysis tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification. The tests were
performed in accordance with ASTM Test Designations C 117 and C 136. Graphic
presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on Plate B-2.
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D 3080) were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics. Results of the direct shear tests
performed are presented on Plate B-3.
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D 1883) were
performed on selected samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils. Results of the tests are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5.
