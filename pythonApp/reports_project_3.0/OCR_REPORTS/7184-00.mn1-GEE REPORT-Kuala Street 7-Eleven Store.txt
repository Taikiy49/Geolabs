SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration indicates that the project site is generally underlain by residual 
soils consisting of stiff to hard silty clays and clayey silts extending to the maximum depth 
explored of about 16 feet below the existing ground surface.  Basalt rock was also 
encountered in one of the drilled borings from a depth of about 10 feet below the ground 
surface. We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. 
Based on the generally very stiff near-surface soil conditions encountered in our 
borings, we believe that shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous 
footings may be used to support the proposed convenience store building.  An allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of footings bearing on the 
recompacted on-site soils.  The bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades. 
Our laboratory tests indicate that the near-surface soils exhibit low to moderate 
shrink/swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture content.  
Therefore, special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation requirements and 
slab-on-grade design for this project. To reduce the potential for distress to the future 
slabs-on-grade resulting from swelling of the clayey soils, we recommend providing a 
12 inch layer of non-expansive, select granular fill material below the building and 
equipment enclosure slabs. In addition, this 12-inch capping layer of non-expansive, select 
granular fill should extend beyond the building and equipment enclosure footprints by a 
minimum of 24 inches. 
The text of this report should be referred to for more detailed discussions and 
specific design recommendations.

performed for the proposed 7-Eleven convenience store located at 1255 Kuala Street in 
Pearl City on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project location and general vicinity are

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct while 
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas 
banking against its eastern flank.  Both Waianae and Koolau Ranges have lost most of 
their original shield outlines and are now long narrow ridges shaped largely by erosion. 
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of 
lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series.  These rocks are generally characterized by flows 
of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers.  In-situ chemical 
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred during the last 1 to 2 million years.  
The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils and saprolite.  In general, 
saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of 
volcanic rocks.  The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with depth.

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition), the project site may be 
subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed. 
The following sections provide discussions on the seismicity, soil profile type for seismic 
design, and the potential for liquefaction at the project site. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so 
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of

the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of 
Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of 
Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands 
of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, small 
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of 
1938 (M 6.8). Some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a 
result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 (M7+). 
In the last 150 years of recorded history, we are not aware of earthquakes greater 
than Magnitude 6 that have occurred on the Island of Oahu. An earthquake of 
Magnitude 4.8 to 5.0 occurred along the Diamond Head Fault in 1948 on the Island 
of Oahu. The moderate tremor resulted in broken store windows, ruptured building 
walls, and broken underground water mains.

2.4.3 Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the International Building Code, 2006 Edition, the project site may be 
subjected to seismic activity, and the potential for soil liquefaction at the project site 
will need to be evaluated. 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the 
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess 
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by 
earthquakes. In this process, when loose saturated sand deposits are subjected to 
vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in 
volume causing an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is unable to occur 
rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective stress 
(internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore water 
pressure build-up could equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the 
soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During

SECTION 3.0 
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration indicates that the project site is generally underlain by a thin 
(about 0.5 to 2 feet) surface fill layer consisting of low to moderately expansive clayey 
silt soils underlain by residual soils consisting of very stiff to hard silty clays and clayey 
silts extending to the maximum depth explored of about 16 feet below the existing 
ground surface.  Slightly weathered basalt rock was encountered below the residual 
soils in Boring No. 2 from a depth of approximately 10 feet below the ground surface.   
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. 
Based on the competent subsurface soil conditions encountered, it is our opinion 
that the proposed 7-Eleven convenience store building and equipment enclosure may 
be supported on a shallow foundation system consisting of spread or continuous 
footings.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design 
of footings bearing on the recompacted on-site soils.  The bottom of footings should be 
embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades. 
Laboratory swell tests performed on the near-surface soils generally indicate a 
low to moderate potential for volume change when subjected to changes in the soil 
moisture content. Lightly loaded slabs-on-grade bearing on expansive soils are at risk of 
future distress unless properly designed.  To reduce the potential for future slab 
distress, we recommend providing a minimum of 12 inches of non-expansive, select 
granular capping fill material over the subgrade below the building and equipment 
enclosure slabs. The select granular capping fill should extend beyond the building and 
equipment enclosure footprints by a minimum of 24 inches. 
The near-surface soils at the project site are susceptible to heaving and/or loss of 
strength when saturated, and therefore, special attention should be given to the design 
of drainage at the site.  Proper surface drainage and reduction of water infiltration into 
the subsurface below the building foundations, slabs on grade, and pavements (except 
at the designated infiltration zone of the project storm water management system) are 
integral components to the recommendations provided in this report.

soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557 test procedures.  Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we 
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above.  Saturation 
and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and 
poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
well-compacted fill.  The need for over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil 
conditions should be evaluated by a representative from Geolabs in the field.

for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for 
the intended use. 
3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, 
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of 
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D1557 test procedures.  Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage 
by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

Similarly, aggregate base course and subbase materials required for the pavement

of the on-site clayey soils to shrink and swell in response to moisture content 
fluctuations.

3.3 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We anticipate that concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the new 7-Eleven 
building and equipment enclosure.  Based on the existing topography and the 
anticipated finish floor elevation, we envision that cuts and fills on the order of about 1 to 
2 feet will be required to achieve the proposed finished subgrade.  The near-surface 
clayey silt soils encountered at the project site exhibited low to moderate expansion 
potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations.  To reduce the potential for structural 
distress to the lightly-loaded slabs on grade, we recommend providing a minimum of 
12 inches of non-expansive select granular capping fill material over the subgrade 
below the concrete slab.  It should be noted that the 12-inch non-expansive select 
granular capping fill recommended herein is in addition to the cushion fill under the 
concrete slabs.  The non-expansive select granular fill material should extend beyond 
the building and equipment enclosure footprints by a minimum of 24 inches (or extend 
to the outside edge of the concrete sidewalk surrounding the building, if provided).  The 
subgrade for slabs-on-grade should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, and compacted 
to not less than 90 percent relative compaction. 
The non-expansive select granular fill recommended below the slabs-on-grade 
should consist of imported granular material such as crushed coral or basalt. The 
material should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches 
in largest dimension.  The material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio 
(CBR) value of 20 or higher, and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in 
accordance with ASTM D1883.  It should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles 
passing the No. 200 sieve.  All select granular fill materials should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to at least 
95 percent relative compaction. The slab subgrade should be kept moist until covered 
by the non-expansive select granular capping fill.

Underground Utility Lines 
We anticipate that the underground utilities for the proposed development will

utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the proposed utilities.  The 
method and equipment used for excavation should be determined by the contractor, 
subject to practical limits and safety considerations.  In addition, trench excavation 
should comply with the applicable local, state, and federal safety requirements.  Trench 
shoring design and installation should be the responsibility of the contractor.  Trench 
shoring and bracing should also conform to the appropriate health and safety 
requirements.  
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes for support of the 
utility lines.  The initial trench backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist 
of free-draining granular backfills, such as No. 3B Fine gravel, to reduce the potential for 
damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill.  It is critical that a free-draining 
granular material be used as the initial trench backfill to reduce the potential for 
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the

3.7 
Drainage 
As indicated above, proper surface drainage is critical to the performance of 
foundations and slabs constructed on potentially expansive soil.  Therefore, the finished 
grades outside the structure should be sloped to shed water away from the foundations 
and to reduce the potential for ponding.  It is also advised to install gutter systems 
around the building and to divert the discharge away from the foundation and pavement 
areas.  Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be 
avoided.  Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and 
drains to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration into the subsoils. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil 
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength.  The foundation excavations 
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of 
the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration.  In addition, drainage swales 
should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface 
water runoff away from foundations and slabs.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
a total of seven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 7, extending to depths 
ranging from about 2 to 16 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, one bulk 
sample, designated as Bulk-1, of the in-situ clayey soils were obtained to evaluate the 
pavement support characteristics. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill 
equipment equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock materials were described 
in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).  Graphic representations 
of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through 
A-7. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties.  The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three one-inch Ring Swell tests (ASTM D456)were performed on relatively 
undisturbed samples of the near-surface clayey soils to evaluate the swelling potential of 
the soils. The test results are summarized on Plate B-1. 
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits.  The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.  Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-2. 
One California Bearing Ratio (CBR) Test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a 
bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. A graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B-3. 
One Modified Proctor Test (ASTM D1557) was performed on a bulk sample of 
the near-surface soils to evaluate the optimum moisture and maximum density 
relationship. Data obtained from this test was used in determining the ideal conditions 
for the bearing ratio (CBR) test and may also be used for future field compaction 
criterion. A graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-4.
