This letter report presents the findings from our preliminary geotechnical 
engineering exploration in support of the preliminary feasibility study for the proposed 
Waikoloa Solar Farm in the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This letter report summarizes our findings and preliminary geotechnical 
recommendations based on our site reconnaissance, limited preliminary geotechnical 
engineering exploration, and past project experience in the vicinity only. These 
preliminary geotechnical recommendations are intended to inform the client regarding the 
potential geotechnical risks involved and the geotechnical considerations that may need 
to be addressed for the proposed development at the site. The findings and preliminary 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
letter report.

The proposed Waikoloa Solar Farm project is located off Waikoloa Road to the 
southeast of Waikoloa Village in the Waikoloa area on the Island of Hawaii.  Based on 
the information provided, we understand that it is desired to evaluate the feasibility of the 
proposed solar farm development at the subject site.  The general vicinity of the project 
site is shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Details of the development design were not available at the time this report was 
prepared. We understand a detailed geotechnical exploration program will be conducted 
for detailed foundation and earthwork design and construction.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our preliminary geotechnical engineering exploration consisted of drilling and 
sampling a total of six borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending down to 
about 16 to 18 feet below the ground surface.  The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2.   
Based on our borings drilled, the subsurface conditions at the site generally consist 
of volcanic ash and clinker materials underlain by basalt formation.  The thickness of the 
volcanic ash materials ranged from about 3 to 7.5 feet at the boring locations.  Volcanic 
ash materials generally consist of stiff sandy silts with some gravel.  In many areas, 
cobbles and boulders scattered at the ground surface were observed.  Therefore, we 
envision that the volcanic ash materials will likely contain various amounts of cobbles and 
boulders.  In some borings, volcanic ash materials were underlain by clinker materials 
with ash consisting of medium dense silty sands with gravel.  In two of the drilled borings 
(Boring Nos. 2 and 5), clinker materials extended down to the maximum depth explored 
of 18 feet below the ground surface.   
In four other borings drilled at the site, basalt formation was encountered from 
depths of about 3 to 12.5 feet below the ground surface and extended down to the 
maximum depth explored of about 16.5 feet below the ground surface.  Basalt formation 
encountered were generally relatively unweathered and hard to very hard.  Based on the 
limited field exploration conducted at the site, it appears that the depth to basalt formation 
varies significantly across the project site. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field 
exploration. However, groundwater levels likely will change in response to seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered during our field exploration are 
presented on the Logs of Borings in Appendix A, Plates A-1 through A-6.  Results of the 
laboratory tests performed on selected soil and rock samples are presented in Appendix B. 
Photographs of the core samples retrieved during our field exploration are presented in 
Appendix C. 
REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an 
area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during the recent 
geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both 
still active). 
The project site is situated on the western flank of Mauna Kea Mountain, which 
makes up the north-central portion of the island.  Based on available geologic information 
and geomorphological interpretation, the project site appears to be underlain by basaltic

lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea Volcano, which were deposited 
during the Pleistocene Epoch (Stearns and Macdonald, 1946). 
Following the cessation of volcanic activity in Kohala and Mauna Kea, activity from 
the other shields resulted in widespread aerial fall of ash over much of the island. This ash 
has weathered rapidly into a fine silty soil, which is typified by low in-situ densities and high 
field moisture contents. In some locales, when the field moisture content is sufficiently high, 
the ash soil exhibits thixotropic properties (i.e., the soil temporarily loses strength when 
remolded and regains its strength after internal pore pressures dissipate).  In more arid 
areas, the ash soil exists in a dry friable state even though field moisture contents can 
approach 100 percent of the dry unit weight.  Our exploration indicates that the site is 
underlain by a surface mantle of this drier volcanic ash soil, which should transition to basalt 
formation and clinker at a deeper depth. 
The lava formation underlying the site generally consists of a’a and pahoehoe basalt 
type flows, which spread and ponded as they flowed toward the ocean. A'a lava typically is 
characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged 
accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders. Typically, a denser and 
more layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core. Pahoehoe lava is 
characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular 
(porous) rock. Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas. Cavities form when 
lavas are still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes (intra-flow cavities) and blisters 
and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form when molten lava drains from the cooling 
flow leaving a hollow tube-like structure, which may extend for a large longitudinal distance 
along the flow. Blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities) are generally smaller in horizontal 
extent.

the site, we believe the proposed solar farm development project generally is feasible

design and construction of the proposed project. Based on our experience and a review

Volcanic Ash Materials 
In general, the surface materials at the project site consist of volcanic ash 
materials.  We envision that site grading required at the site will involve placement of fills 
consisting of volcanic ash materials.  In addition, volcanic ash materials may be present 
at the subgrades of new structures and pavements required for the development of the 
project. 
Volcanic ash materials are generally difficult to compact since the strength of the 
materials is very sensitive to moisture content.  Volcanic ash materials at the site generally 
exist in a drier condition.  Therefore, some moisture-conditioning may be required to 
facilitate compaction of the materials.  However, it should be noted that excessive 
moisture in the volcanic ash materials may create pumping ground conditions.  Therefore, 
the contractor should use caution not to overwater when moisture-conditioning volcanic 
ash materials. Where soft or pumping ground conditions are encountered during 
construction, over-excavation and replacement with suitable fills may be required.  
A consideration may be given to mixing the volcanic ash materials with crushed 
rock to provide a well-graded structural fill.  Mixing volcanic ash materials with crushed 
rock would reduce the moisture sensitivity of the material and may facilitate easier 
compaction. 
Cobbles and Boulders 
We envision that the solar arrays would be supported on a micropile system.  As 
mentioned above, cobbles and boulders are scattered around the ground surface of the site.  
We envision that cobbles and boulders may also be embedded in the volcanic ash layer.  
Therefore, selection of the new foundation system for the solar arrays would need to 
consider its constructability with respect to the potential presence of cobbles and boulders. 
A driven pile system may encounter difficult driving conditions due to the presence 
of cobbles and boulders.  In addition, drilled micropile system would need to have the 
capability to drill through cobbles and boulders. 
Basalt Formation 
Based on the limited field exploration conducted at the site, it appears that the 
depth to basalt formation varies significantly across the project site.  Our borings 
encountered basalt formation from about 3 to 12.5 feet below the ground surface.  Basalt 
formation was not encountered in two of the six borings drilled at the site. 
The selection of the new foundation system for the solar arrays would need to 
consider the potential to encounter basalt formation at relatively shallow depths.  A driven 
pile system will likely not be able to penetrate through the basalt formation.  In addition, 
drilled micropile system would need to have the capability to drill through the basalt 
formation.

Other Foundation Considerations 
We envision that some single-story buildings may be required for the project to house 
solar equipment and other appurtenances. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered 
at the project site, we believe shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings may be used 
to support the new buildings planned for the project. As an alternative, foundations for 
support of the new structures also may consist of thickened-edge slab footings. 
We envision that the building foundations will be supported on volcanic ash materials.  
Therefore, proper preparation and compaction of the foundation subgrades will be critical 
during construction.  Foundation subgrades should not be exposed to overwatering or 
inclement weather. 
LIMITATIONS 
The preliminary analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, 
upon information obtained from the drilled borings. Variations of the subsurface conditions 
between and beyond the field data points may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear 
evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. A 
design-level geotechnical engineering exploration should be conducted to confirm and/or 
modify the preliminary geotechnical recommendations provided herein. 
The field boring locations indicated in this report is approximate, having been 
transferring GPS coordinates obtained from a handheld GPS unit on to a Google Earth 
image. The physical locations of the borings should be considered accurate only to the 
degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict the 
approximate boundaries between the soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on 
the graphic representations and/or presented in the text herein. These data have been 
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. We did not encounter 
groundwater in our borings at the time of our field exploration. However, it must be noted 
that fluctuation may occur due to variation rainfall, temperature, and other factors. 
This letter report has been prepared solely for the purpose of assisting our client 
(The AES Corporation) in the planning and preliminary evaluation of the project only. 
Therefore, this letter report may not contain sufficient data, or the proper information, to 
serve as the basis for detailed design and preparation of construction drawings. A 
design-level geotechnical exploration will need to be conducted for the detailed project 
design.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
six boreholes, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6. The borings extended to depths of 
about 16 to 18 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill 
rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on 
the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. 
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond 
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow 
bit to cut into the rocky formation. The rocky material left in the hollow core of the bit is 
mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rocky cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6.  
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material is 
recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown 
on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties. The test results
