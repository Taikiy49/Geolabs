PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Fort Barrette Road Widening project is located between Milepost
to along Route 901 Barber s Point Access Road formerly Old Fort Barrette
Road in the Ewa District on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The purpose of the project is
to study and design roadway improvements that will ease the congestion along this
portion of Fort Barrette Road
We understand that a Project Assessment Report has been completed and that
Design Alternative No 2 Road Widening with Elevated Separation has been selected
for the project The roadway improvements will involve widening the existing two lane
undivided roadway to a four lane divided roadway with a raised median In addition
auxiliary lanes bike lanes concrete sidewalks curbs and gutters highway lighting an
underground drainage system traffic signals and improved roadway intersections will
be constructed
We understand that the roadway widening from Barbers Point gate to
Kamaaha Avenue will primarily be on the western portion of the existing roadway
right of way Between Kamaaha Avenue and Farrington Highway the widening will be
primarily on the eastern portion of the roadway right of way Based on the site

GEOLOGIC CONDITIONS
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the
island The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the
island As volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows from Koolau Volcano
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau known as Schofield
Plateau
Following the extrusion of lavas in the early Pleistocene Epoch the island
underwent a long cycle of erosion and weathering forming the prominent ridgelines and
summits as we know today During the erosion period the Island of Oahu began to
slowly subside by more than 1 200 feet in elevation resulting in the drowning and
sedimentation of the valleys and the formation of the steep Koolau Pali Coral reefs
continued to grow in the surrounding shallow waters of the island
From the mid to late Pleistocene Epoch the sea level rose and fell repeatedly in
response to global glaciation and the availability of surface waters to sustain the
oceans The various sea level elevations and their representative deposits are known
as stands and include from oldest to youngest the Kahuku Kahipa Kaena Laie
Waialae Waipio and Waimanalo Geologic deposits associated with the various sea
level stands including marine sediments and coral reefs were deposited and
subsequently altered or removed by later sea level fluctuation Therefore depositional
records reflecting the changes in sea level and the occurrence of emerged coral reef
deposits are often incomplete
The post erosion series of volcanoes of the Waianae Mountains Kolekole
Volcanics consists of five cinder cones on the south and southeastern flanks of the
Waianae Mountains The five cones are comprised of Puu Makakilo Puu Palailai
Puu Kapolei Puu Kapuai and Puu Kuua
The project site is situated on the Ewa Plain to the southeast of the Waianae
Mountains The Ewa Plain is a gently sloping alluvial plain formed by the deposition of
alluvial clays and silts derived from the weathering of basalt rock further up slope The

SURFACE TERRAIN
The terrain along the roadway widening project limits varies from fairly level along
the existing roadway to sloping along the roadway embankment slopes A brief description
of the topography along the proposed roadway widening is presented below These
descriptions are based on topographic plans provided
The roadway alignment travels along the existing Fort Barrette Road with ground
surface elevations ranging from about 51 feet Mean Sea Level MSL near the
Barber s Point gate to 117 feet MSL near Farrington Highway The existing roadway
surface on Fort Barrette Road generally slopes down towards the south at about ninety
horizontal to one vertical 90H 1V inclination The existing roadway embankment
slopes range from 10H 1V to 2H 1V inclinations

Expansive Soil Conditions
Based on our experience in the project vicinity the on site alluvial soils may exhibit
moderate to high shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations The lateral
extent and thickness of the alluvial soils should be delineated during the design phase for
the roadway development by conducting a detailed field exploration program

Rockfall Hazard
It should be noted that areas adjacent to relatively steep mountainsides and
hillsides are susceptible to rockfall Rockfall involves the detachment and fall of rock
material from the slope face that can present dangerous conditions for traffic and
pedestrians A cut slope exposing highly fractured rock is present along the existing
roadway area adjacent to the Puu Kapolei cone Cut slope heights of up to ten feet were
observed along this area One of the remedial measures consists of providing a rockfall
catchment zone between the toe of the cut slope and the roadway For a slope height of
about 10 feet a preliminary rock catchment zone of about 10 feet wide may be used
Because of the potential rockfall issues rockfall hazard mitigation measures should be
evaluated when the horizontal and vertical profiles of the alignment are established
Rock and Coral Excavation
Basalt rock formation may be encountered near the Puu Kapolei cinder cone
area In addition the southern portion of the project site may be underlain by coralline
materials and or coral formation near the existing ground surface We anticipate that the
basalt rock and coral formation will be moderately weathered and medium hard to hard
in nature It is anticipated that the basalt rock and coral formation may be excavated
with heavy excavation equipment such as the large dozers near the ground surface
However we anticipate that difficult excavation conditions may arise in localized areas
during construction Deep excavations for utility line trenches may require the use of
hoerams or chipping
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the materials are based on our
general knowledge of the basalt rock and coral formations anticipated at the project site
A detailed site specific field exploration program should be conducted to evaluate the
hardness of the basalt rock and coral formation if encountered
Cavities and Voids in the Subsurface
Basalt rock and coral formations generally are highly variable in terms of
hardness weathering and fracturing In addition cavities and or voids are commonly
encountered in coral formations in the project vicinity and in basalt rock formations In
many cases cavities and voids in the coral formation may be filled with loose coralline
deposits In areas where coralline or basalt rock formation is anticipated near the
