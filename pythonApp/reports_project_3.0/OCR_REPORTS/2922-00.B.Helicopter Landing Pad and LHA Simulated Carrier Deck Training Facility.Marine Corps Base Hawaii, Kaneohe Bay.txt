INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed helicopter landing pad and LHA simulated carrier deck
training facility within the Marine Corps Base Hawaii MCBH at Kaneohe Bay on the
island of Oahu Hawaii The general location of the project site and its vicinity are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommen
dations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These geotechnical engineering recommendations are intended for the
design of pavements and site preparation only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the Limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be of
the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of the late Pliocene
to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which
erosion cut valleys deep into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to
as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The site of the MCBH at Kaneohe Bay is located on Mokapu Peninsula which
was built by three volcanic vents of the Honolulu Voicanic Series These vents are
believed to be among the oldest of the series The pyroclastic deposits and lava flows
from vents formed salients permitting the growth of a coral reef that merged with the
main body of the island of Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age as a result of the lowering of the sea
level in response to continental glaciation sands from emerged coral reefs were blown
inland by the prevailing winds to form sand dunes along the northern portion of the
airfield
An extensive deposit of unconsolidated calcareous marine sediments primarily
consisting of beach sand made up of water worn coral shells and organisms covers
the western tip of Mokapu Peninsula Man made fills consisting of marine sediments
dredged from the ocean floor were used to build up the area around the southern
and western shorelines of Mokapu Peninsula

LIQUEFACTION POTENTIAL
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near
the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes
In this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 2922 00 B Page 8
as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an
increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough due to
a large areal extent of the soil deposit to dissipate the build up of pore pressure the
effective stress internal strength of the soils is reduced Under sustained vibration
the pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially
reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid
During liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal
and vertical movements and if not confined would result in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained sands
and loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that liquefaction would
not occur if the deposit is greater than 50 feet below the ground level In deeper
deposits the greater overburden pressure is sufficient to prevent liquefaction from
occurring The major factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit
include the following
1 Grain Size Distribution Fine grained and uniform sands and silts with
little cohesion are more susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well
graded sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose fine sands and silts are most susceptible
to liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
3 Saturation The potentially liquefiable deposit has to be saturated in order
to be susceptible to liquefaction
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2922 60 B Page 9
4 Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake
Our field exploration conducted at Boring Nos 1 through 3 eastern portion of
the site indicated that over 10 feet of very loose clayey sands and gravels are present
at the eastern portion of the project site It is our opinion that liquefaction of these
loose granular soils could occur when subjected to earthquake induced vibrations
generated by an earthquake of magnitude 6 0 with a peak horizontal ground
acceleration of 0 1g or stronger In the event of a moderate to large earthquake there
is potential for liquefaction of the loose granular soil resulting in ground deformations
at the eastern portion of the site Based on our engineering analyses ground
settlements on the order of about 4 to 8 inches may occur at the eastern portion of the
subject site due to liquefaction of the loose granular soils The estimated liquefaction
related settlements would result in cracking of the pavement structures
in general the proposed pavement structures may be constructed and performed
as intended under normal use conditions provided that the recommendations presented
herein are implemented during construction However liquefaction induced settlements
and damage to the proposed pavement structures may be expected in the event of a
moderate to large earthquake producing peak horizontal ground accelerations on the
order of 0 1g or greater The risk of liquefaction at the site may be reduced by
mitigating the site through relatively costly site improvement techniques It should be
noted that many structures in the MCBH at Kaneohe Bay are constructed above similar
type subsoils without mitigating the liquefaction potential

Preparation section

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared All debris unsuitable materials and existing surface pavements
that are to be demolished should be removed and disposed of properly off site to
reduce possible contamination of the excavated materials The excavated coralline
materials may be used as a source of subbase material for the pavement sections
provided that they meet the requirements of pavement subbase material In addition
the excavated coralline materials may be used as a source of controlled fill or backfill
material Detailed requirements for subgrade compaction are discussed in the following
section
Subgrade Preparation
Because of the presence of a shallow groundwater table we recommend that
the subgrade be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton smooth drum roller a minimum of
8 passes to obtain a firm and unyielding subgrade To reduce the potential for
subgrade pumping heavy or rubber tired equipment should not operate on the
excavated subgrade level In addition vibratory action of the compaction equipment
should not be used to reduce potential pumping of the subgrade If soft clayey spots
are encountered at the subgrade level during proof rolling the soft clayey materials

excavation and should extend a minimum of 3 feet beyond the edges of the over
excavation Therefore we recommend that unit prices for placement of the reinforcing
fabric and free draining material be obtained during construction bidding In addition
we also recommend that a budget be allocated for these items in the cost estimates

The new rigid and flexible pavement sections planned for the helicopter landing
pad and the LHA simulated carrier deck training facility will require aggregate base
course In general the aggregate base course should conform to the general

21 3 August 1978 which has been modified for local conditions and presented on

3 To reduce damage to immovable structures
4 To facilitate construction
