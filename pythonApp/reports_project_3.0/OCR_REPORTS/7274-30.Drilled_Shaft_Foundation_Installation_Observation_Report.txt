Dear Mr. Heaton: 
In accordance with the request from Laconia Development LLC, Geolabs, Inc. 
observed the installation of the drilled shafts for the above project. This letter report 
summarizes our geotechnical observations during the drilled shaft foundation installation 
for the South King Student Housing project in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The 
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Geolabs provided geotechnical engineering consultation services for the project. 
The purpose of our geotechnical observations was to monitor the geotechnical 
engineering aspects of the drilled shaft foundation installation for conformance with the 
plans and specifications and to provide subsequent recommendations, as appropriate. 
PROJECT DESCRIPTION 
The project site is at the former University Square development situated at the 
southeast corner of South King Street and University Avenue in Honolulu on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. The site is bounded by South King Street to the north, Kahuna Lane to the 
east, existing three-story buildings to the south, and University Avenue to the west. The 
site was previously occupied by three-story buildings along the southern boundary and in 
the central portion of the site. The remaining areas were asphalt paved parking.

The project involves the construction of a 14-story tower with retail spaces and 
employee parking on the ground level. Additional parking will be provided on the second 
and third levels consisting of 190 parking stalls. Based on the information provided by 
the structural engineer, the maximum column load may be up to about 1,200 kips. The 
building is designed to be supported on deep foundations consisting of 30-inch and 
42-inch diameter cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts. The drilled shafts were designed 
to derive vertical support primarily from skin friction between the drill shaft and the upper 
coralline materials (where permanent casing is not required) and/or the underlying stiff 
to very stiff alluvial clays starting at depths of about 20 to 30 feet below the existing 
ground surface. It should be noted that the Ewa portion of the project site could be 
traversed by a subsurface tunnel and cavern structures.  Therefore, permanent casing 
will be required for the upper portion of the drilled shafts in this area to reduce the 
potential for concrete runoff into the underground cave system. In general, the 
permanent casing should be embedded a minimum of 3 feet into the bottom of the 
cavern floor to create a seal to reduce potential concrete loss at the bottom of the 
casing. The project also required the performance of one bi-directional load test using 
an Osterberg load cell to evaluate and confirm the drilled shaft foundation design. 
The scope of our work discussed in this letter report includes observation of the 
drilled shaft foundation construction for the South King Student Housing project only. 
Albert C. Kobayashi, Inc. was the general contractor for the project and Healy Tibbitts 
Builders Inc. (HTBI) was the subcontractor that installed the drilled shaft foundation 
support system. 
DRILLED SHAFT INSTALLATION 
As part of the project requirements and our subsequent recommendations, 
one sacrificial load test shaft was constructed and tested.  Following completion of the load 
test, the drilled shaft lengths for the project were revised based on the results of the 
instrumented load test and the foundation probing program.  The following subsections 
provide descriptions of the load test shaft construction and discussions on the production 
drilled shaft installation. 
Load Test Shaft 
As part of the project requirements, one sacrificial 42-inch diameter load test 
shaft, designated as LT-1, was attempted at the location as shown on the Drilled Shaft 
Location Plan, Plate 2. The load test shaft was drilled to a tip elevation of -110.8 feet 
Mean Sea Level (MSL) but was subsequently abandoned because the concrete level 
could not be brought up above Elevation -87.3 feet MSL during concreting, possibly due 
to the encountering of very soft/loose zone or cavern, resulting in excessive concrete 
runoff. 
A replacement load test shaft, designated as LT-2, was installed at the

Representatives from Geolabs, Inc. observed the drilled shaft excavation, placement of 
reinforcing cage, and concrete operations during the installation of the production drilled 
shafts. 
In general, the production drilled shafts were excavated using a Watson 3100CM 
track-mounted drill rig. Excavation tools used for the 30-inch diameter drilled shafts 
included 30 and 36-inch diameter auger, digging bucket, core barrel, and a 30-inch 
diameter clean-out bucket. Excavation tools used for the 42-inch diameter drilled shafts 
included 42, 45, and 48-inch diameter auger, digging bucket, core barrel, and a 42-inch 
diameter clean-out bucket. For drilled shafts located within the karst limits of the site, 
permanent steel casings with a diameter of 36, 45, and 48 inches were installed to 
depths of about 15 feet to 45 feet below the ground surface for the 30 and 42-inch 
diameter production drilled shafts. 
During excavation, coral formation was generally encountered at depths of about 
5 to 26 feet, alluvial clays from about 26 to 70 feet, coralline detritus materials from 
about 70 to 80 feet, and alluvial clays from about 80 to 90 feet below the working 
platform elevation. The drilled shafts were extended to varying depths as required by 
the project specifications and our recommendations.  Permanent casings were used for 
the upper portion of the drilled shafts within the underground caverns to reduce the 
potential for running off of the shaft concrete into the underground cave system, which 
could extend laterally for a great distance.  Detailed information pertaining to the casing 
installation are provided on Plates 3.1 through 3.4.  
After excavation of the production shafts to their designated tip elevations, the 
reinforcing cages were lowered into the drilled holes to the appropriate elevations with 
the use of a crane. After the reinforcing cage was installed in the drilled hole, we 
observed that concrete was placed in the drilled shafts within 36 hours from substantial 
completion of the excavation. Concrete was placed through a 4-inch diameter steel 
tremie pipe starting from the bottom.  During concrete placement, we observed that at 
least 5 feet of concrete head was maintained above the bottom of the tremie pipe.  
Concrete with slump ranging from 7 to 9 inches was used for the drilled shafts. Slump 
tests and compression tests of concrete were performed by others.
