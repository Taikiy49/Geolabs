REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers
an area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of
the following five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both still active
The project site is situated on the western flanks of Hualalai and Mauna Loa
which make up the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at
the site appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe type flows which spread and ponded
as they approached the ocean A a lava is typically characterized by a porous rough
and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments cobbles
Belt Collins Hawaii Ltd
Page 3

and boulders Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and
an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially in pahoehoe
type lava Cavities form when the lava is in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and void blisters and void pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes
form when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow Blisters and void
pockets are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be relatively permeable and can transmit water quite readily
in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted through the
porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
rubbly clinker layers Clinker and cavities serve as primary water transmission features
because of their high permeability
The project site contains some gentle to moderate graded slopes and rolling
plateau areas adjacent to and surrounding Keauhou Bay Based on a review of available
geological maps the project site is mainly underlain by sequentially deposited basaltic lava
flows from Hualalai Volcano The approximate age of the relatively young near surface
rock materials is believed to range between less than about 500 years to over
10 000 years in age Based on our observation of the existing rock outcroppings in the
project site vicinity the exposed near surface rock formations may be characterized as
both a a and pahoehoe type lavas with individual dense rock and rubbly clinker bedding
generally on the order of about 1 to 5 feet in thickness

SUBSURFACE EXPLORATION
Since the golf course is in active use conventional exploratory core borings would
be problematic In order to reduce disruption to the golf course and exploration costs the
subsurface conditions were explored by conducting a combination of drilled probings and
seismic refraction geophysical surveys at selected locations proposed for future grading at
the existing golf course Nine drilled probings identified as Probings 1 through 9 were
drilled at the approximate locations shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2 In addition
six seismic refraction surveys identified as SL 1 through SL 6 were conducted at selected
probing locations as approximately shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2
Drilled Probings
The nine probings were drilled with an Ingersoll Rand air percussion
track drill rig advancing a 3 5 inch diameter hammer bit The probings were drilled to
depths of about 15 to 16 feet below the existing ground surface The drilled probings were
logged by visual observation Our drilling observations included the relative drilling rate
rock cutting production and borehole air circulation conditions The purpose for the drilled
probings is to provide general subsurface information such as relative rock hardness
throughout the depth of the drilled probing at the locations shown on the Site Plans
Representative core samples of the rock materials are not recovered by the percussion
hammer drilling method The Logs of Probings are presented in Appendix A on Plates A 1
through A 9
Seismic Refraction Surveys
The seismic refraction exploration method is a non intrusive geophysical means
of evaluating the relative density thickness and depth of subsurface earth materials
such as soil and rock layers Seismic refraction is used to record the compression wave
primary or P wave response as the waveform passes through or is refracted at the
interfaces of materials with different densities wave velocities in various types of soil
and rock media
Belt Collins Hawaii Ltd
Page 5
W O 6267 00
The seismic refraction method is a useful subsurface exploration tool where
geologic layers possess increasing material density with increasing depth a condition
that normally holds true for about 80 percent of naturally occurring geologic deposits
However the seismic refraction survey method cannot be used to profile the subsurface
conditions where the P wave velocity of the earth material decreases with depth
P wave velocity inversion
In addition the recorded P wave velocities in mixed thin bedded earth layers that
may have variable P wave velocities such as interbedded loose basalt clinker and
massive hard rock lava flow sequences are generally averaged to obtain approximate
layer velocities Therefore the seismic refraction exploration method should be used to
supplement other sources of subsurface information such as drilled probings
The seismic refraction method also requires experienced interpretive judgment by
the analyst to identify various trends and inconsistencies that may be present in the
recorded field data As a result the profile and wave velocity information obtained from the
surveys should be treated as an idealized data set where some variation should be
anticipated in the field conditions
The six seismic refraction geophysical surveys were conducted by Global
Geophysics out of Monroe Washington The seismic refraction surveys are identified as
SL 1 through SL 6 Data processing and presentation of the geophysical survey results
was performed by Global Geophysics Additional detailed information about the
geophysical survey is contained in the geophysical report by Global Geophysics which is
presented in Appendix B

Interpretation of Geophysical Survey
Based on our review of the geophysical seismic refraction survey results relatively
low velocity waveforms were recorded for the subsurface materials extending to depths of
about 15 feet below the existing ground surface The low velocity of the subsurface
materials may be a result of severe fracturing within the rock material or may be a result of
the analysts averaging of the velocities between the interbedded hard and soft to medium
hard basaltic rock layers Based on our review of the probing information and interpretation
of the seismic velocities it is our opinion the rock materials may be harder than indicated
solely by the geophysical survey The relatively low velocities averaged on the order of
about 1 200 to 2 200 feet per second These velocities typically suggest severely fractured
or moderately hard basaltic rock material
In general velocity values are greater for rock material that is dense older
unfractured and unweathered Highly fractured rock such as clinker generally results in
Belt Collins Hawaii Ltd

lower velocity values The concept of a seismic survey is generally based on the following
fundamental assumptions
1
Seismic velocities of the geologic layers must increase with depth This
requirement is typically met at most sites
2
Layers must be of sufficient thickness to permit detection
3
Seismic velocities of layers must be sufficiently different to permit resolution
of individual layers
The results of a seismic refraction survey should not be used as the only means of
estimating rock material rippability due to the known limitations of the non intrusive
geophysical survey method Interpretation of rock rippability and hardness should be made
in conjunction with other exploration methods such as drilled probings and direct
observation of the existing surface rock character or exposed profiles made by existing
profile exposures and cut excavations
In general we believe most of the golf course surface grading may be
accomplished by ripping with a large bulldozer such as a Caterpillar D 9 or equivalent
However some localized embedded hard layers may require hoe ram excavation Hard
rock requiring hoe ram excavation should be anticipated in the vicinity of the proposed lake
between Holes 4 and 5 Exploration at the proposed lake suggested competent hard rock
was encountered by the Probing P 7 and the relatively high seismic refraction velocity
ranging on the order of about 4 000 to 8 000 feet per second by survey SL 5 supports our
interpretation
Based on the information obtained from our field exploration significant lava tubes
or voids within the basaltic rock were not encountered during the field exploration
However voids such as continuous trending lava tubes and or localized blisters are
common in the basaltic lava flows of Hawaii Therefore there is a real potential for
encountering lava tubes or other voids during excavation of the basaltic rock materials

REPORT FOR THE SEISMIC REFRACTION AT KONA COUNTRY
CLUB MAKAI GOLF COURSE KONA HI
This letter report presents the results of the geophysical surveys performed by Global Geophysics
The seismic refraction survey was carried out at six locations in January 2010 for the golf course
grading at Kona Country Club Makai Golf Course Kona Hawaii The objective of the
investigation is to determine the depth to bedrock and rock rippability
GEOPHYSICAL METHODS AND FIELD PROCEDURES
Seismic refraction was used to determine the depth to bedrock and rock rippability The following
paragraphs describe the methods and field procedures
Seismic refraction
Seismic refraction is the traditional method for determining the rock velocity for rock rippability
using a controlled energy source hammer blank shotgun shells or chemical explosives to
generate a seismic signal The seismic signals are received by a series of geophones 24 for
example that are connected to a seismic cable laid on the ground surface in a linear manner The
geophones evenly spaced along the geophone cable are placed on the ground surface The
seismic energy source is discharged at several places along the array and off both ends
The seismic wavelets travel through the earth to the geophones that convert the acoustic energy in
the ground to an electric signal in the geophone cable The seismograph detects the arriving
electric signals with respect to time and stores the records for future data processing The seismic
data is processed to determine the seismic velocity of the earth material through which the energy
has traveled and to model the subsurface geology This geophysical model depicts the earth in
cross section showing the velocity and thickness of the subsurface layers below the seismic line
The seismic refraction survey was conducted using a Geometrics Geode 24 channel digital
seismograph The sensors were Mark Products vertical geophones and the seismic energy
sources were a 100 lbs weight drop The weight drop method appeared to provide sufficient
energy and it was subsequently used for the survey The typical field procedure consisted of
laying out the cables and planting the geophones at 5 ft intervals The weight drop was pounded

CLOSURE
Global Geophysics services will be conducted in a manner consistent with the level of care and
skill ordinarily exercised by other members of the geophysical community currently practicing
under similar conditions subject to the time limits and financial and physical constraints
applicable to the services However seismic refraction is a remote sensing geophysical method
that may not detect all subsurface conditions such as velocity and thickness variations
Furthermore the faster velocity layers i e lava flow layers result in velocity inversion and
hidden layers below which are beyond the limitations of seismic refraction Due to the lateral
discontinuity in the lava flow arrivals from different layers are observed which will introduce
errors in the interpretation In general the errors in the interpreted depths and velocities
dependent on the resolution of the technique are estimated to be approximately 15 of the true
depths and velocities
