INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Kapiolani Bandstand project within the existing Kapiolani
Regional Park in the Waikiki area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

The project site is located within the existing Kapiolani Regional Park in the Waikiki
area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the information provided it is
proposed to demolish the existing bandstand structure and to construct a new bandstand
complex near the south end of the existing structure off Monsarrat Avenue
The bandstand complex will be a single story structure with a basement level for
dressing and storage Based on the information provided the finish floor elevations for
the basement and stage levels of the bandstand structure are set at about 2 and
11 feet Mean Sea Level MSL respectively Based on the existing topography and the

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea
levels during the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4096 00 Page 5
Plain The subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene
Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea which
are intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this
area is Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result
of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to
present sea level of Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level
occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area were immersed In the last
10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and
marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in the
areas which were formerly valleys and drainageways
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought
the Waikiki area to its present form Many of the resulting fills are of poor quality in terms
of supporting heavy structural loads

Our field exploration at the proposed project site generally encountered a surficial
fill layer of silty clays overlying medium dense to dense sandy soils extending to a depth
of about 10 to 11 feet below the existing ground surface The upper sandy soils were
generally underlain by loose and or soft lagoonal deposits extending to a depth of about
25 feet below the ground surface Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at
depths of about 5 4 and 8 3 feet below the existing ground surface These groundwater
levels generally correspond to elevations of about 1 1 and 1 8 feet MSL

structure we believe that the basement level may be supported on a mat foundation
bearing on the medium dense to dense sandy soils encountered in the upper 10 feet of
subsoils An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 000 pounds per square foot psf may
be used for the design of mat foundation bearing on the medium dense to dense sandy
subsoils anticipated at the basement slab subgrade level

bearing on the recompacted near surface sandy soils and or new engineered fills
Footings located adjacent to the basement walls should be embedded deep enough to
avoid surcharging the basement wall foundations or the basement walls should be
designed to resist the additional footing loads
For the lightly loaded public restroom building we recommend that shallow
continuous footings bearing on the recompacted near surface sandy soils be used for
foundation support An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for the

Foundations

mat foundation may be used for support of the basement level of the bandstand structure
An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for the design of mat

For the pillar columns supporting the superstructure and the lightly loaded

In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades Footings located adjacent to planned basement walls such as
the pillar columns should be embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the basement
wall foundations or the basement walls should be designed to resist the additional footing
loads
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
We understand that a future pond is planned on the north side of the new
bandstand structure as part of the master development of the area In general footings
for the bandstand structure should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum
horizontal set back distance of 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to
the slope face of the future pond Care should be exercised in the construction of the
future pond to avoid undermining the foundations of the bandstand structure

For footings bearing on the near surface sandy soils and or new engineered fills
the bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91
test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations
they should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The
over excavation should be backfilled with non expansive select granular material
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom may be
extended down to the underlying competent material
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system
A coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted in situ
soils or new engineered fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf
This assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted The passive pressure
should be reduced for foundations located on slopes Unless covered by pavements or
slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for part of the stage level
of the bandstand structure and the lightly loaded restroom building Our laboratory tests
indicate that the surficial silty clays encountered in our borings exhibit high potential for
swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations To reduce the potential for structural
distress of the slabs on grade resulting from the swelling of the clayey soils we
recommend that the subgrade soils underneath the slab areas be properly prepared and
capped with at least 24 inches of non expansive select granular fill material below the
slab cushion The capping fill should also extend beyond the building perimeter a
minimum of 24 inches
The non expansive select granular capping fill should be placed as soon as
possible after the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations
presented in the Site Preparation section of this report The prepared capping fill and
the underlying subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to the final placement
of concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor
drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted engineered fill
In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer consisting of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or basaltic termite barrier BTB be
placed below the slabs to provide more uniform support of the slabs and to serve as a
capillary moisture break To reduce future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage
to floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is
GEOLABS HAWAII

recommended on top of the gravel or BTB cushion layer Two inches of moist fine sand or
BTB may be considered above the plastic membrane to provide protection of the
membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Flexible floor coverings such as
carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask minor slab
cracking
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that concrete walkways be supported on 12 inches of non expansive select

The thickened edges of slab adjacent to any unpaved area should be embedded

around the foundations

process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in
our field exploration
Excavation
Based on the existing topography and the design finish floor elevation for the
basement level of the bandstand structure we anticipate that excavations on the order of
about 5 feet below the existing ground surface will be required for the project
construction Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by
medium dense to dense sandy soils within the anticipated depths of excavation
Therefore we believe that conventional excavation equipment may be used for the
proposed excavation

Excavation Method
In general the method and equipment used for excavation should be determined
by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations Based on
our field exploration results we believe that the in situ sandy soils encountered by
our borings may be excavated by a bulldozer or other heavy excavation
equipment The excavated materials should be stockpiled at least 15 feet away
from any underground utilities to reduce the potential of ground movement or
subsidence which may damage the underlying utility lines In addition the
excavated soils should not be stockpiled adjacent to the planned excavation within
a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the excavation to reduce the potential
for ground movement
Excavation Support
Based on the relatively shallow excavation depth and the open space at the
project site we believe that an open cut excavation with a slope inclination of
or flatter may be used for the planned excavations in the medium dense
to dense sandy deposits Where an open cut excavation is not possible because
of physical limitations or the presence of loose sandy subsoils temporary shoring
of the excavation may be required to reduce the potential of adjacent ground
movement The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with all
applicable safety requirements
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site we believe that a
shoring system consisting of steel sheet piling may be considered for the subject
project if required In lieu of steel sheet piling it is our opinion that a cantilever
shoring system such as soldier pile and lagging may also be used for shoring
support of the anticipated excavations

The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole

However it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and
the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during

estimate the magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon

Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical and if necessary the adjacent ground should
be continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of movements
with instruments
Dewatering
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater level
is above the bottom of the proposed excavation As previously indicated shallow
groundwater level as a result of tidal fluctuations and storm surge conditions may be
anticipated due to the proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean Based on a finish

W O 4096 00 Page 20
floor elevation of about 2 feet MSL for the basement level of the bandstand structure it
is our opinion that localized dewatering may be required for the project construction
Therefore we recommend that provisions be made for localized dewatering during
construction Since the excavation may involve discharge of groundwater a National
Pollutant Discharge Elimination System NPDES permit may be necessary The
contractor should consult their independent consultant for the latest regulations and
information pertaining to the permit application
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and he she
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on the conditions encountered The dewatering method selected
should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the proposed
excavation The dewatering operations should be coordinated with the trench shoring
support such that the stability of the excavations is not jeopardized In addition the
operations should be carried out without softening the bottom of the excavations
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines will be required to serve the new facility It should
be noted that groundwater was encountered at 5 4 and 8 3 feet below the existing ground
surface Therefore any trench excavations extending deeper than 5 feet below the
existing ground surface depending on location and surface elevation may encounter the
groundwater level
In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes If soft loose lagoonal
soils are encountered at the proposed pipe inverts an additional 18 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel wrapped in a filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be provided below the
bedding layer for more uniform support Free draining granular materials such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the trench
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4096 00 Page 21
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes or groundwater level to provide adequate
support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus
reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining
material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result
in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
or groundwater level to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the
on site granular soils or select fill material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to
at least 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive landscape watering
near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations
should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into
the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce waiter infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain surface run off away from the slab and footing
foundations
