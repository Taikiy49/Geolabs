2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as
the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reach
the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream
flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this
phenomenon the transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s

hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and
fines When the erosional base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin
to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material
Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion
of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate
Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in
headwaters This continued erosion generates material that is transported downslope
covering the older alluvial deposits
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer sediments
are generally transient in terms of geologic time In addition their consistency and
density are generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits
2 2 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused solely by shifts in the
tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic
activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground movement
of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of Hawaii
experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most of the earthquakes are so
small such that only instruments can detect them However some of the earthquakes are
strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of magma
are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island of
Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent of
earthquakes are related to volcanism the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground
shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii The Island of

light poles may be supported by drilled shaft type foundations to resist the generally high
ground line moments induced by loading of the poles Foundation analyses were
conducted based on a combination of axial and lateral loads in addition to the ground line

Torsional Resistance
In general light pole foundations may be subjected to torsional moments resulting
in torsion on the drilled shaft foundations For single shaft foundations torsion may
be resisted by the side shear along the drilled shaft surface and adjacent soils In
our foundation analyses we have evaluated the drilled shafts subjected to a
torsional moment of 2 2 kip feet provided by the project structural engineer Based
on our analyses we believe that the recommended diameter of drilled shafts
extending to the depths recommended should be capable of resisting the torsional
moment without significant movement of the foundations We wish to emphasize
that the sides of the drilled holes should be relatively free of loose materials and
that the concrete for the drilled shaft should have close contact with the in situ soils
after the drilled shaft has been constructed to provide the torsional resistance
required

that temporary casing of the drilled holes may be needed based on the subsurface
conditions encountered at the site In addition care should be exercised during
removal of the temporary casing to reduce the potential for necking of the drilled
shaft concrete Therefore a minimum 5 foot head of concrete should be
W O INC Page 17

maintained above the bottom of the casing and or groundwater level during
removal of the casing
It should be noted that a layer of basaltic cobbles and boulders was encountered in
Boring No 8 from about 10 feet to about 15 feet below the existing ground surface
In addition weathered basalt formation with localized hard zones will likely be
encountered near the top of the vertical curve approximately between
Station 126 00 and Station 135 00 The presence of cobbles boulders
weathered basalt formation and potential localized hard zones will likely require the
use of special drilling tools core drilling tools to advance the drilled shaft holes to
the recommended embedment depths
The load carrying capacity of drilled shafts depends to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete in the holes The bottom of drilled
shafts should be cleaned of disturbed material prior to constructing the shaft The
bottom of the drilled shaft hole should also be relatively level Backfill against the
drilled shaft foundations should not be allowed
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids slurry is not generally recommended
Groundwater conditions are anticipated at the site therefore placement of concrete
by wet construction methods using a tremie pipe will be required A low shrink
concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch slump range should be used to provide
close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils The concrete
should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for segregation of the
aggregates from the concrete mix and to displace disturbed material from the
bottom of the drilled shaft
Placement of concrete using a tremie pipe will likely cause some groundwater and
soil to discharge out from the top of the drilled shaft The contractor should be
made aware that this fluid should be properly disposed of Because the water in

3 2 Existing Cut Slopes
As mentioned previously several cut slopes of up to about 45 feet in height are
present along the sides of the highway The majority of the cut slopes along the highway
generally consist of saprolitic materials highly weathered rock materials The cut slopes
at the top of the vertical curve near Station 126 00 to Station 135 00 generally exposed
weathered basalt formation with some saprolitic materials The basalt outcrops generally
appeared to be closely fractured and moderately weathered The condition of the cut
slopes generally appeared to be good with minor raveling of the surface materials In
addition most of the cut slopes are moderately to heavily vegetated Vegetation at the
existing cut slopes on the mauka side of the highway near Station 90 00 to Station 94 00
and Station 117 00 to Station 118 00 was generally sparse
Based on our visual observations the existing cut slopes generally appear to be
stable from a slope stability point of view Our analysis indicates that the global slope
stability of the existing cut slopes consisting of stiff saprolitic materials or highly weathered
basalt formation generally appears to be stable However signs of surface erosion were
noted during our site reconnaissance of the existing cut slopes Therefore erosion control
measures should be considered to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the cut
slope face
Excessive surface water runoff over the cut slope face may cause erosion of the
exposed soils thus jeopardizing the long term stability and performance of the cut slopes
Therefore we believe that consideration should be given to protecting the cut slopes by

the slope face to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the exposed soils

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
nine borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 9 The borings were advanced to
maximum depths of about 12 to 41 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings
were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight auger tools
The approximate locations of the borings drilled for the project are shown on the
Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 5
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 9
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The
results of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Three sieve analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in
soil classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B 2
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics The results of the direct shear test
are presented on Plate B 3
Three unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression tests were performed on
selected in situ samples in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 2850 to evaluate
the undrained shear strength of the silty and clayey soils The approximate in situ
effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the relatively
undisturbed samples The test results and the stress strain curves are presented on
Plates B 4 through B 6
One multi stage consolidated undrained triaxial compression test with pore
pressure measurements was performed on a selected in situ sample in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 4767 to evaluate the long term shear strength of the silty and
clayey soils The test results and stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 7 1 and
B 7 2
