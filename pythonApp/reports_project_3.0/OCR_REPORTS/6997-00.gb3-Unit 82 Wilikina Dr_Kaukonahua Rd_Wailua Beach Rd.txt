SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the existing pavement structure along Wilikina Drive 
generally consists of about 5.5 to 8.0 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by 3.0 to 
11.0 inches of base/subbase material. The existing pavement structure along Kaukonahua 
Road generally consists of about 5.0 to 9.0 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by 4.0 to 
18.5 inches of base/subbase material.  The subgrade soils encountered along Wilikina 
Drive and Kaukonahua Road consists of stiff silts and clays.  
The existing pavement structure along Waialua Beach Road generally consists of 
3.0 to 7.5 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by 4.0 to 7.5 inches of base/subbase 
material. Two of the borings drilled at Waialua Beach Road did not encounter 
base/subbase material below the AC layer. The pavement subgrade soils encountered at 
Waialua Beach Road generally consists of coralline sand and gravel with varying amounts 
of silt.  The relative density of the sand and gravel was generally loose to medium dense. 
Our field observations indicated that the pavements along Wilikina Drive, 
Kaukonahua Road, and Waialua Beach Road exhibits medium to high severity pavement 
distress.  Based on the traffic data provided and the subgrade conditions, the new flexible 
pavement sections should consist of the following: 
Wilikina Drive 
3.0 inches of AC on 4.0 inches of ACB over 6.0 inches of ASB; or  
3.0 inches of AC on 6.0 inches of ACB 
Kaukonahua Road 
2.0 inches of AC on 4.0 inches of ACB over 6.0 inches of ASB; or 
3.0 inches of AC on 4.0 inches of ACB  
Waialua Beach Road 
2.0 inches of AC on 3.0 inches of ACB over 6.0 inches of ASB; or 
2.0 inches of AC on 4.0 inches of ACB

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The 
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) 
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae Volcano 
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially 
buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank. Wilikina Drive and 
Kaukonahua Road are located on the Schofield Plateau. 
Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against 
the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch. The dips 
of the lava beds generally range from 3 to 5 degrees. The lava flows on the plateau have 
weathered to depths of about 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors of the 
soil. The deep narrow canyons, such as Waikakalaua, Kipapa, and Kaukonahua, are the 
result of this weathering and erosion.  
Waialua Beach Road is along a narrow coastal plain formed by the fluctuation of 
the sea levels which was in response to the cycles of continental glaciation.  As the 
continental glaciers advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such 
that the sea levels fell to below the present stand.  When the glaciers melted and 
receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose to above 
the present level.  
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations.  The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas 
and fans of terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef 
deposits at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine 
sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused 
streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the

Visual Pavement Condition Survey 
A pavement condition survey of the project area was performed on July 11, 2014 
and October 10 and 13, 2014. During the field survey, our engineer walked on both 
sides of the street and determined pavement distress types through visual classification. 
The visual classification was performed in general accordance with the 
definitions described in the “Distress Identification Manual for the Long-Term Pavement 
Performance Program” dated June 2003 prepared by the Federal Highway 
Administration (FHWA). Some of the pavement distress definitions are presented in the 
table below. 
ASPHALT PAVEMENT DISTRESSES  
Alligator Cracking 
(Fatigue) 
Alligator cracks are a series of interconnecting cracks caused by 
fatigue failure of the asphaltic concrete surface under repeated 
traffic loading. 
Block Cracking 
Block cracks are inter-connected cracks that divide the pavement 
into approximately rectangular pieces. 
Longitudinal 
Cracking 
Longitudinal cracks are cracks parallel to the pavement’s centerline 
or laydown direction. 
Transverse 
Cracking 
Transverse cracks are cracks extending across the pavement at 
approximately right angles to the pavement centerline or direction of 
laydown. 
Patch 
Patch is an area of pavement that has been replaced with new 
material to repair the existing pavement. 
Pothole 
Potholes are small bowl-shaped (usually less than 30 inches in 
diameter) depressions in the pavement surface. 
Rutting 
A rut is a surface depression in the wheel paths. 
Shoving 
Shoving is a longitudinal displacement of a localized area of the 
pavement surface generally caused by braking or accelerating of 
vehicles.  Shoving is usually located on hills or curves, or at 
intersections.  It also may have associated vertical displacement. 
Bleeding 
Excess bituminous binder occurring on the pavement surface, 
usually found in the wheel paths. 
Polished 
Aggregate 
Polished Aggregate is the area of pavement where the portion of 
aggregate extending above the asphalt binder is either very small, or 
there are no rough or angular aggregate particles to provide good 
skid resistance.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our analyses indicate that the existing pavement structure at Wilikina Drive, 
Kaukonahua Road, and Waialua Beach Road may be insufficient to support the 
medium-volume traffic conditions.  Therefore, the rehabilitation of these roadways 
should consist of reconstructing the pavement sections. Based on the traffic data 
provided and the subgrade conditions along the alignment, the new flexible pavement 
sections should consist of the following:  
Wilikina Drive 
3.0 inches of AC on 4.0 inches of ACB over 6.0 inches or ASB; or  
3.0 inches of AC on 6.0 inches of ACB 
Kaukonahua Road 
2.0 inches of AC on 4.0 inches of ACB over 6.0 inches of ASB; or 
3.0 inches of AC on 4.0 inches of ACB  
Waialua Beach Road 
2.0 inches of AC on 3.0 inches of ACB over 6.0 inches of ASB; or 
2.0 inches of AC on 4.0 inches of ACB  
If soft soils are exposed after the milling process, we recommend providing a 
stabilization layer in lieu of proof-rolling the soft subgrade.  The stabilization layer should 
consist of 8 inches of aggregate subbase course over a layer of geogrid (such as 
Tensar Triax TX 7 or equivalent) underlain by a woven geotextile fabric (such as Mirafi 
HP370 or equivalent). Our field exploration encountered clays and silts at some areas 
along Wilikina Drive that may not provide adequate pavement support.  If the clays and 
silts are encountered at the pavement subgrade level of Waialua Beach Road, a 
stabilization layer should be provided.

3.3 
Design Subgrade Conditions 
Laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D1883) were performed 
on eighteen bulk samples, which consisted of soil cuttings of the pavement subgrade 
soils, to evaluate the swelling potential and pavement support characteristics of the

3.8 
Site Preparation 
After removal of the existing AC and base layers to accommodate the new 
pavement structural sections, the exposed subgrade soils should be proof-rolled with a 
minimum 10-ton (static weight) drum roller for a minimum of eight passes to compact 
the exposed subgrade soils. The drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 
300 feet per minute.  
If subgrade soils with relatively high in-situ moisture are exposed, we recommend 
proof-rolling the pavement subgrade without the use of vibration.  Use of vibration 
during proof-rolling will induce a pumping condition of the subgrade soils with high 
in-situ moisture. 
If soft soils are exposed after the milling process, we recommend providing a 
stabilization layer in lieu of proof-rolling the soft subgrade, which may induce pumping 
condition.  The stabilization layer should consist of 8 inches of aggregate subbase 
course over a layer of geogrid (such as Tensar Triax TX 7 or equivalent) underlain by a 
woven geotextile fabric (such as Mirafi HP370 or equivalent) as shown on Plate 5. The 
aggregate subbase course should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage 
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO 
T-180 (ASTM D1557). Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and 
scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar 
to those encountered in our field exploration.

Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D2041. Asphalt concrete (AC)

Pavement of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction, and 
its amendments. 
3.9 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway rehabilitation by drilling 
and sampling twenty one borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 21, extending to 
depths ranging from about 5.0 to 7.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. In addition, 
the boring exploration was supplemented by coring the existing pavement section to 
depths of about 2.5 to 2.8 feet below the existing pavement surface at 20 selected 
locations, identified as C-101 through C-120.  The approximate boring and coring locations 
for this project are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.26. The borings were 
drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring 
tools. 
Our engineer/geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual 
and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A.  
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) 
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and 
evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths. 
Ten Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Nine Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification.  Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on 
Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Eighteen laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were 
performed on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-4 through B-21.
