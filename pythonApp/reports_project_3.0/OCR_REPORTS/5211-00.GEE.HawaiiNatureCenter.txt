2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned to the
Island of Oahu with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations
These most recent volcanic series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series The
Punchbowl crater located to the southwest of the project site is one such extinct tuff and
cinder cone
The project area is located on the southern flank of the Koolau Mountain Range
which is generally composed of basaltic rock formation built by the extrusion of lavas of
the Koolau Volcanic Series The rock formations are generally characterized by layered
flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt rock with interbedded rubbly clinker layers For
the past 1 to 2 million years weathering and erosion of the Koolau lava flows has
occurred creating the valley and ridge topography visible today on the southern side of
the Koolau Mountain Range
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii erosion is dominated by the mass transfer
process which is essentially the detachment of soil and rock masses from the steep
valley walls and transportation downslope as colluvium toward the axis of a valley
primarily by gravitational force Once these colluvial materials reach a flowing stream
typically on the floor of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments
may be re mobilized for further transportation and deposition as alluvium
The project site is located on the lower slopes and floor of the erosional valley
carved by the Kanealole and Moleka Streams which captures their flow from the
Tantalus summit region located approximately 1 0 to 1 5 miles northeasterly of the
project site As a result of the location of the project site within a valley the site is
generally underlain by significant thickness of clayey soils mixed with boulders

2 4 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused solely by shifts in the
tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic
activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most of the earthquakes
are so small that they can only be detected by instruments However some of the
earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate damage

In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the
Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent of

3 2 Slabs On Grade
Our field exploration indicates that the project site is generally underlain by gray
adobe clay soils near the existing ground surface Based on our experience with
similar soils we envision that the clayey soils encountered at the project site would
exhibit high shrink swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil
moisture content However due to the generally wet environment encountered at the
project site we believe that the potential for soil moisture fluctuations would be relatively

underlying clayey soils

3 5 Falling Boulder Hazard
Due to the valley floor site location it is anticipated that the adjacent valley
slopes may be composed of weathered basalt rock formation It appears that the
majority of development associated with the existing Hawaii Nature Center is located on
a terrace bluff above the Kanealole Stream channel Therefore it appears that the
incised stream channel provides natural protection from potential rockfall hazards that
may exist on the northwest facing valley slopes However the development may be
exposed to some potential rockfall hazards that could emanate from the southeastern
facing valley slopes The valley slopes are very densely vegetated with forest growth
and a surface veneer of soils is anticipated to cover much of the sloping ground
surfaces However isolated rock outcrops or layered rock ledge features may exist
higher on the valley slopes above the existing development These potential rock
outcrops may have the potential to produce falling and rolling rock hazards As a result
consideration may be given to the placement of future buildings incorporating a setback
from the steeper slopes composed of rock materials Other options include the
construction of appropriate barriers such as fences walls or catchment ditches to
reduce the potential effects of potential rockfall hazards
3 6 Utility Trench
We envision that new utility connections will be required to serve the new facility
In general good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and
backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities at the project site The method and
equipment to be used for trench excavation should be determined by the contractor
subject to practical limits and safety considerations In addition the excavations should
comply with the applicable federal state and local safety requirements Trench shoring
design and installation should be the responsibility of the contractor Based on the
results of our field explorations it appears that conventional excavation methods may
generally be utilized for utility line excavation at the project site The upper clayey soils
encountered at the project site are generally stiff to very stiff However some of the
utility trench excavations will likely encounter the very dense cobbles boulders and
possibly hard basalt rock formation as described in the boring logs In addition the

3 7 Drainage

structures It is also advised that gutter systems be installed around the buildings and
that the discharge be diverted away from the slab and foundation areas Excessive
landscape watering near the slabs and foundations should also be avoided Planters

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths ranging from
about 11 to 21 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment
equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
