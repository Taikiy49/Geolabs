INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the repair of the sheetpile walls at Wharves W4
and W5 U S Navy Project and R13 85 at the Naval Magazine
Iualualei West Loch Branch Hawaii The approximate location of the
project site is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
SCOPE OF SERVICES
The purpose of the exploration was to evaluate the subsurface
conditions below the site to provide pertinent soil parameters for the
design of the proposed new sheetpile walls at Wharves W4 and W5 and the
proposed replacement piles at Wharf W5
The scope of work included drilling and sampling six 6 borings
laboratory testing of samples obtained engineering evaluation of the
field and laboratory data development of design recommendations and
report preparation
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration boring logs and
laboratory test results are presented in the appendices of this report

PROJECT CONSTDERATION
Recent underwater inspection of the steel sheetpile wall at
Ammunition Wharves W4 and W5 revealed corrosion and perforations that
have created caverns beneath the pavement area adjacent to the wharves
It was also discovered that two existing piles supporting the deck of
Wharf W5 at the junctions of the pile lines A and 69 and D and 62 were
broken The broken piles were designed to support an allowable vertical
load of 25 tons Therefore a new steel sheetpile bulkhead without
tie back system and replacement piles are planned to repair the wharves
It is our understanding that except at the western end the
sheetpile walls of Wharf W4 are presently adequate and will not be
replaced However at various locations along Wharf W5 the sheetpiles
are badly damaged Therefore the wharves will thus be repaired by
driving new sheetpiles behind the existing sheetpile walls the top of
the new and existing sheetpile bulkheads will be encased in concrete and
cathodic protection is anticipated to be used to increase the life of the
repair work
Two groups of replacement piles each with two piles are planned in
order to replace the two existing broken piles supporting the deck of
Wharf W5
STTE DESCRIPTION
Ammunition Wharves W4 and W5 are approximately 1000 feet long and
are located adjacent to each other in the Naval Magazine at the southern
end of the West Loch Branch of Pearl Harbor Oahu Hawaii
The wharves are used to receive and distribute ammunition in support
of navy ships and other military organizations

Both wharves are concrete deck structures supported on long concrete
or composite piles with the top of the wharf deck at elevation of about
108 5 feet U S Navy Datum MLIW Elevation 100 feet Behind the
wharf decks are existing steel sheetpile bulkheads with the backfilled
area paved with asphaltic concrete along the wharves
It is our understanding that the mudline in front of the walls were
dredged to variable slopes with the toe of slope generally at an
elevation of about 65 feet where the ground becomes basically
horizontal Except at both ends the wharf decks are cast against the
existing sheetpile bulkheads
The existing asphaltic concrete pavement appears to be in generally
good condition However at areas behind the damaged sheetpiles the
pavement was also damaged by ground subsidence probably due to the
washing away of subsoils through the caverns in the damaged sheetpiles
Some of the subsided pavements have already been repaired with new
asphaltic concrete pavement sections
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the results of a
complex series of deposition and erosional events brought on by
glacioeustatic sea level chnages and post erosional volcanic activities
during the Pleistocene Following the cessation of activity of the
Koolau Volcano alluvial materials were deposited in an estuarine and
deltaic environment in the West Loch area of Pearl Harbor
The sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 300 feet
below and rising about 95 feet above the present sea level
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1747 00 Page 5
Dk No 23
As Oahu sank coral reef grew around the shorelines keeping pace
with the subsidence
The site is underlain at variable depths of about elevation 1 to
27 feet by coral reefs which indicates that this area was once in a
shallow marine environment Below the coral reefs the borings
encountered alluvial deposits consisting of alternating cohesive and
granular soil layers
Concurrent with a rise in sea level during late Pleistocene era
eruption of the Salt Lake Makalapa member of the Honolulu Volcanic Series
mantled the coral reefs and alluvium with deposits of volcanic ash which
consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally called mudrock layers
Subsequent erosion has dissected and removed the mantles of tuff at the
site
During the Recent epoch as sea level rose to its current stand the
area waS covered with loose coralline marine deposits Those deposits
were subsequently covered by man made fill placed during the middle
portion of this century for land reclamation projects during the
construction of the existing dock at the Iualualei Naval Base
Subsoil Conditions
The subsurface soil conditions at the site were explored by drilling
and sampling 6 borings to depths from about 20 to 77 feet below the
existing ground surface Boring No 1 was drilled at the end of Wharf w4
while Boring Nos 2 through 4 were drilled behind the existing sheetpile
walls at Wharf W5 and Boring Nos 5 and 6 were rilled at the broken pile
locations at Wharf W5 The locations of these borings are shown on the
Site Plans Plates 2 and 3

Behind the existing sheetpiles at Wharves W4 and W5 the drilled
borings generally encountered man made fills over hard coral reef
formation at variable depths At the end of Wharf W4 the hard coral
formation was encountered at the depth of about 21 feet below the
existing ground surface in Boring No 1 At Wharf W5 the hard coral
formation was encountered at depths of 7 to 13 in Boring Nos 4 and 3
respectively However the coral was not encountered in Boring No 2
Instead loose lagoonal sandy and gravelly deposits were encountered
below the fills to the drilled depth of about 30 feet in Boring No 2
The depths to coral formation generally agree with drawings showing the
old shoreline in the area prior to the wharf construction which indicate
that the western portion of Wharf W5 may be situated over a buried
drainageway
At the broken pile locations in front of the sheetpile wall at Wharf
W5 the drilled borings encountered complex subsoils which consisted
primarily of a thin hard crust probably of cobbles and boulders
underlain by layers of loose lagoonal deposits medium dense to dense
limestone gravel and cobbles and stiff Older Alluviun
Groundwater levels behind the existing sheetpiles at the wharves
were monitored during our field exploration A high of about 6 2 to a
low of about 7 3 feet below the existing ground surface were recorded in
Boring Nos 1 through 4
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our field exploration and engineering analysis it appears
that the most critical consideration in the sheetpile bulkhead design and
construction is the presence of the hard coral layer
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1747 00 Page 7
Dk No 23
Where the coral layer is encountered at shallow depths relatively
shallow lengths of sheetpiles about 25 feet may be used On the other
hand where the coral layer is not encountered very deep sheetpile
penetration about 60 feet into the loose sandy soils are necessary for
stability Additionally thicker sheetpile sections would also be
required for moment considerations
Boring Nos 3 and 4 indicate that the coral layer may be relatively
shallow in the eastern half of Wharf W5 However coral formations are
variable and we recommend that somewhat longer sheetpile sections should
be designed to acconmodate variations in the coral surface
Boring No 2 did not encounter coral formation therefore deep
sheetpile sections are required Since subsurface information regarding
the depth to coral between Boring Nos 2 and 3 is unavailable the design
length of the sheetpiles is uncertain If long piles are used where the
coral formation is shallow much of the sheetpile will be cutoff and
wasted On the other hand if short sections are used and coral is not
encountered difficult splicing would be required to attain the required
lengths
In light of these difficulties we recommend that additional probes
be drilled between Boring Nos 2 and 3 to more accurately define the
coral surface to aid in determining the design length of the sheetpiles

Sheetpile Bulkhead Construction
Based on our field explorations we believe that the proposed new
sheetpiles could be driven through the upper fill material and loose
lagoonal deposit without difficulty However hard driving may be
encountered when sheetpiles are to be driven into the hard coral
formation It is our opinion that a vibratory type hammer would be more
successful in obtaining the required penetration into the hard coral
formation

Replacement Piles
The drilled borings at the broken pile locations did not encounter
any hard and continuous bearing formation to their drilled depths
Therefore we recommend that friction piles be used to replace the broken
piles at the pile line junctions of A and 69 and D and 62
The piles would develop their vertical load capacity primarily from
frictional resistance or adhesion between the piles and the lower medium
dense lagoonal soils and partially from end bearing resistance of the
bottom stiff Older Alluvium or dense gravel and cobbles Sixteen and
one half inch octagonal prestressed concrete piles may be used
The pile should be driven to a minimm total depth of about 65 feet
below the existing wharf deck in order to develop sufficient frictional
resistance or adhesion between the pile and the subsoils to provide an
allowable vertical pile capacity of 18 tons per pile
The pile should be driven with a hammer delivering a minimm of
about 28 000 foot pounds of energy and to a resistance of 10 blows per
foot for the last foot or 2 blows for the last inch
For a 30 ton capacity the pile should be driven to a total depth of
about 80 feet below the wharf deck and to a resistance of about 15 blows
per foot

Pile settlements on the order of less than one half on an inch are

Backfill
Backfill material should consist of well graded granular material
generally less than 3 inches in size with less than about 25 passing the
No 200 sieve
The existing in situ backfill material of sandy coral gravel may be
reused for backfill behind the proposed new sheetpiles All backfills
should be compacted to a minimm of 90 of its maximm dry density
established by ASTM D 1557 78 The top 12 inches should be compacted to
95 of its maximum dry density
For underwater backfills clean granular material such as 3B Fine
ASTM C 33 No 67 is recommended to reduce the compactive effort
required

Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that all sheetpile and replacement pile
installation be observed by the soils engineer during construction to see
that adequate penetration and preparation have been made and that the
subsurface conditions are consistent with the findings of our
investigation
If conditions encountered during construction are different from
those presented in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on
selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the boring logs
at the appropriate sample depths
One sieve analysis test was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate its grain size distribution The test results are
presented on Plate B 1
Seven pH tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate the
corrosion potential of the soils behind the existing sheetpile walls
The test results are summarized on Plate B 2
Two direct shear tests were performed on the subsoils to evaluate
the shear strength parameters of the soils The test results are
presented on Plates B 3 and B 4
