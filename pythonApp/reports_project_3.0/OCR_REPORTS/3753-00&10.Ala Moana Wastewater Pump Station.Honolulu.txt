Based on our field explorations the project site is generally underlain by a thin
surface fill crust overlying compressible lagoonal deposits extending to depths of about
25 to 30 feet below the existing ground surface Deposits of coralline materials of varying
densities with occasional dense or cemented layers were encountered below the loose to
very loose lagoonal deposits and extended to the maximum depth explored of about
61 5 feet below the ground surface Shallow groundwater levels were encountered at
depths ranging from about 6 1 to 7 9 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of
our field explorations
Due to the presence of the soft and or loose lagoonal deposits at the site we
anticipate that structures supported on a shallow foundation system would be subjected to
appreciable total and differential settlements Therefore we recommend that a deep
foundation system be used to support the new 60 inch diameter off site force main 54 inch
diameter bypass force mains venturi meter box and valve boxes planned for the project
Based on our experience on the projects in the vicinity we believe that a deep foundation
system consisting of jet grouted column foundations may be used effectively to support the
new structures planned at the site In general the jet grouted column foundations should
consist of a minimum diameter of 60 inches and the tip of the jet grouted column
foundations should be embedded into the medium dense to dense coralline materials
encountered at depths of about 25 to 30 feet below the ground surface For preliminary
design and cost estimation purposes an allowable compressive load capacity of up to
100 tons per jet grouted column foundation with a column foundation tip elevation of about
25 feet MSL may be used for the recommended jet grouted column foundation design
For the underground fuel storage tank we believe that a concrete mat may be used
to provide the necessary bearing support and uplift load resistance for the tank structure
The concrete mat should bear on a minimum 3 foot thick gravel layer wrapped in a
non woven filter fabric for uniform bearing support However it is our opinion that the
3 foot thick gravel layer may be omitted if a tremie concrete plug or jet grouted bottom plug
is planned underneath the tank structure for dewatering purpose Concrete slabs on grade
bearing on the recompacted surface fill materials may be used for support of the
above ground structures such as the odor control systems and 250 gallon day tank
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific
recommendations for the design of the project

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae Volcano forms the
bulk of the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau Volcano forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano became extinct while
Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas
in Central Oahu
The Ala Moana WWPS property is located on the southern flank of the Koolau
Shield Volcano and its geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to
glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of
the Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions in the Kaka ako area are
dominated by fossil Pleistocene age coral algal reef deposits laid during high stands of the
sea that were intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic
section in this area is Koolau Basalt
About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that
sea level rise the valleys in the project area were drowned or submerged In the last
10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and
marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft lagoonal sediments along the coast in the
areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways including the alluvial channels that cut
through the coral reef ledges
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought
the Kaka ako area to its present form It should be noted that many of the resulting fills are
of poor quality in terms of supporting large structural loads

fill layer extending to a depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface Below the
surface fill layer loose to very loose lagoonal deposits consisting of silty and clayey sands
and gravel interbedded with occasional layers of very soft silty clays were encountered to
depths of about 25 to 30 feet below the existing ground surface It should be noted that
the loose to very loose lagoonal deposits encountered at the site are highly sensitive to
vibrations and other disturbances The loose to very loose lagoonal deposits would turn
into a viscous fluid temporarily known as liquefaction when subjected to vibrations
and or disturbed and would result in appreciable settlements at the ground surface The
highly compressible lagoonal deposits were underlain by coralline materials of varying

3 1 Jet Grouting
Jet grouting is a technique utilizing a special drill bit and injection monitor with radial
horizontal nozzles to produce stabilized soil cement columns The jet grouting technique
is a process that produces soil cement columns by pumping neat cement grout slurry
through horizontal jets injected at high pressures The horizontal jets of cement grout

Quality Control
The type of jet grouting system and grouting parameters for grout mix grout
pressures rotational speed lifting rate grout flow rate number and size of jet
nozzles and drilling methods greatly affect the performance of the jet grouted
columns Therefore an adequate quality control program should be implemented
by the contractor during the production jet grouting operations
In general grout mix uniformity should be verified by unit weight density
measurements of the mixed grout by mud balance Marsh Viscosity and or bleed
from samples taken from the grout return line in accordance with API Standard
13B test method In general we recommend that a minimum of one group of tests
be conducted for every 2 hours that the grout is mixed and pumped
A minimum of six cement grout samples should be fabricated in accordance with
ASTM C 109 Two grout samples should be subjected to compressive strength
tests at 7 days in accordance with ASTM C 39 or C 109 and ASTM D 1633

3 10 Corrosion Protection
Laboratory corrosion tests including pH minimum resistivity chloride content and
sulfate content were performed to evaluate the corrosivity of the underlying soils at the
project site Based on the test results presented on Plate C 3 the subsurface soils at the
project site generally exhibit relatively low minimum resistivity values in the range of less
than 200 ohm cm which indicate that the subsurface soils are extremely corrosive to
buried metallic structures Therefore we recommend that substructures especially
metallic substructures be properly designed for protection against potential corrosion In
addition the chloride content of the subsurface soils tested indicates that the chloride
content in the soils tested is about kg or parts per million ppm which appears
to be elevated A corrosion engineer should be consulted for detailed recommendations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 The borings were extended to
depths ranging from about 41 5 to 61 5 feet below the existing ground surface for our
current field explorations The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on
the Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with solid and hollow stem augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist field engineer who observed the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by
visual observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general
conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates
A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock coral formations encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
W O 2003 Page A 1
Hawaii California

material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would
be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals

methods of excavation

APPENDIX E
Field Permeability Testing
APPENDIX E
Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the in situ soils was evaluated by performing a constant head
permeability test in Boring No 2 The test was performed at a depth of about 15 feet
below the existing ground surface
The constant head permeability test was performed by pumping water into the
borehole until the water surface stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient
period of time The water height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide
data upon which to base the calculation of the subsoil permeability The test result of the
field permeability test is presented on Plate E 1
