Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain which makes
up the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the site appears
to be of pahoehoe flow which spread and ponded as it approached the ocean Pahoehoe
is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas These
cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes are
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are
generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted through
the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as
the major water transmission features

Subsurface Conditions
A subsurface exploration program consisting of drilling 61 probe holes to depths of
about 12 to 23 feet below the existing ground surface was conducted by South Pacific
Geotechnical Inc in November 1996 Based on a review of the subsurface exploration
program conducted by South Pacific Geotechnical Inc in November 1996 and observations
of the Phase mass grading operations the project site is generally underlain by slightly
weathered medium hard to hard pahoehoe type basalt formation extending from the
existing ground surface The basalt formation appears to be interbedded with layers of
cinder and clinker materials The probe hole logs indicate that cavities and or voids were
also encountered in many of the drilled probe holes Therefore the potential for the
presence of lava tubes at this site is high Localized deposits of volcanic ash may also be
present at the project site
As mentioned earlier boulders of up to about 24 inches in largest dimension were
buried near the ground surface during the Phase mass grading operations Therefore we
anticipate that some boulders will be encountered during construction of the Phase II building
pad
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled probe holes at the time of the field
exploration However groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on factors such as
seasonal rainfall storm surge conditions and other factors
Discussion and Recommendations
Based on the available subsurface information for the project site we believe that a
shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used to
support the proposed new buildings and retaining walls planned for the Phase II portion of
the project The foundations should bear on structural fill materials It should be noted that
boulders may have been placed near the existing ground surface at the site Therefore
some over excavation to remove the boulders and replacement with compacted structural
fill may be necessary to provide a level building pad for construction of the new buildings
Detailed discussion of our geotechnical engineering design recommendations is presented
in the following sections of this report

Building Foundations
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions at the project site we believe that
shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the new buildings
planned for the Phase II portion of the project Foundations may be designed based on an
allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per square foot psf for footings bearing
on basalt formation and or compacted structural fills This bearing value is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by
wind or seismic forces In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches
below the lowest adjacent finished grade
Where foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward
from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the footing subgrades they should
be over excavated and replaced with structural fill The over excavation should extend until
the underlying dense materials are exposed The over excavation should be backfilled with
structural fill materials moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the bottom of footing may be
extended down to the underlying competent material
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate total settlements to be less than 1 inch with differential
settlements on the order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 45 may be used for footings bearing on basalt formation and or on compacted
on site soils Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This
assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted Unless covered by pavements or
slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation
bearing conditions and the required embedment depths

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed buildings
For the interior building slabs which will not be subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend
that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation be placed below the slabs to provide more uniform support of the slabs
and to serve as a capillary break To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration and
subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a
plastic membrane is recommended on top of the gravel cushion layer Placement of
2 inches of moist fine sand above the plastic membrane may be considered to provide
protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project In general we
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer of No 3B Fine gravel be provided
below the concrete walkway slab To reduce the potential for slab cracking crack control
joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints
at right angle intersections

Retaining Structure Foundations
In general we believe that retaining structure foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the Building Foundations
section of this report The footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches In
addition foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth
of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade For sloping ground conditions
the footing should be embedded deeper to obtain a minimum 6 foot set back distance
measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope
Footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in
stepped footings

Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the
retaining structure discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an
alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may
be used instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product
should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the
retaining structure Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of
on site soils of less than 3 inches in maximum dimension and or select granular fill
materials Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should
consist of relatively impervious material to reduce water infiltration behind the
retaining structures

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
After clearing and grubbing the areas within the proposed grading limits should be
excavated where necessary to the design finished subgrades The excavated
materials may be re used as a source of fill materials provided that the materials meet
the gradation requirements presented in the Fill and Backfill Materials subsection of
this report Loose and or soft spots encountered at the excavated subgrade levels
should be removed and replaced with structural fill materials compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction
As previously mentioned boulders were placed near the ground surface during the
Phase mass grading operations Because these boulders were nested and placed
very close to the ground surface the boulders should be over excavated to expose
firm ground and replaced with properly compacted fill

Fill and Backfill Materials
We recommend that fill areas under the building footprints be capped with a
minimum of 36 inches of structural fill material to serve as a choking layer for the
larger sized general fill below and also to facilitate construction of the buildings and
improvements above This choking layer should extend a minimum of 2 feet
laterally beyond the building footprints The structural fill layer may be reduced to
a minimum of 24 inches thick below pavements
Structural fill materials should consist of well graded granular material of 6 inches in
largest dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the
compacted mass If the excavated materials do not contain sufficient fines to produce
fragments or boulders should be considered to provide the required gradation and
particle sizes to develop a well graded material
General fill and backfill materials may consist of on site materials with a maximum
dimension of 12 inches On site materials greater than 12 inches in maximum
dimension may be used as general fills provided that they are reduced in size to meet
the above requirements Care should be exercised to avoid placement of boulders
in the proposed utility alignment and within the depths of the proposed utility lines to
reduce the potential for encountering the boulders during excavation for the utility
trenches In addition boulders should not be exposed at the fill slope face Imported
materials should be observed and or tested by Geolabs Hawaii for their suitability
prior to being transported to the site
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general fills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction The compaction requirement within 3 feet vertically below the
pavements should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Where fills are composed of rock boulders and or cobbles conventional compaction
testing is not practicable Therefore a testing program to evaluate the number of
passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii On the basis of this testing program the
number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction
Excavation
Based on available information the project site is underlain by medium hard to hard
volcanic rock at shallow depths In addition boulders of up to about 24 inches in
dimension will likely need to be over excavated Generally the volcanic rock and
boulders at the site may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such
as ripping with large bulldozers where the rock is near the existing ground surface
However excavations in some areas may require the use of hoerams or chipping
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based on
our visual observation of the existing rock formations and field data from the probe
holes performed at the site We recommend that all contractors proposing to bid on
this project be encouraged to examine the site conditions to make their own
interpretation

Utility Lines
We envision that new utility lines such as water and sewer lines will be required for
development of this project It is anticipated that most of the trenches for utilities will be
excavated in the surficial fill materials placed at the site
In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation or 1 inch minus well graded granular materials is recommended under the
pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel or 1 inch minus
well graded granular materials should also be used for the trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the
compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is
critical that free draining materials be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes to
reduce the potential for settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes Where the 1 inch
minus well graded granular materials are used as bedding and initial backfill the bedding
and initial backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction

Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundation and slab areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs
should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete
bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab
edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce water infiltration In addition
drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain
surface run off away from the foundations and slabs
