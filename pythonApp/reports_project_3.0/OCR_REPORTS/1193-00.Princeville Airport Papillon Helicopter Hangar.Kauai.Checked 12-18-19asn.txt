Soil Engineering Report
Papillon Helicopter Hangar
Distresses to Building Floor Slab
Princeville Airport
In accordance with your request we have made a soils engineering
investigation concerning the distresses to the floor slab of the above
referenced building
Our investigation included the following
1 Drilling and sampling of two borings to depths of 9 0 and
21 5 feet below the surface at the hangar site
2 Visual inspection of the hangar and floor slab
3 Discussions with your office regarding the construction and
performance of the structure

4 Laboratory testing and engineering analysis
This report presents our findings and recommendations for remedial
repairs to the structure
BACKGROUND
The helicopter hangar was constructed in October November of
1982 It is of metal frame construction Supported by a thickened edge
slab on grade
It is our understanding that the building was constructed on an
old fill area However an inspection of the footing excavation was
made by Geolabs Hawaii at the request of your office on October 26
1982 During that inspection an area of soft soil was encountered on
the runway side of the building This area was indicated to the
contractor with the recommendation that it be removed and replaced
with compacted select material Additionally during that inspection
it was noted that a beach sand cushion was being placed in the slab
area It was recommended that compacted base course be used instead
of the sand cushion since the slab would be used to Support aircraft
helicopters
Shortly after construction the floor slab began to develop cracks
which progressively worsened

FIELD OBSERVATIONS
Visual inspection showed that the floor slab had developed a
significant amount of cracking The major trend of this cracking is
perpendicular to the hangar door Vertical and horizontal
displacements of up to one eighth of an inch were noted however no
trend to the displacement could be discerned
No other distresses to the hangar building such as cracking of
the gypboard walls sticky doors or cracks in the exposed portions of
the foundations were noted during our inspection
GEOLABS HAWAII
During the drilling of Boring 2 inside the hangar it was noted
that the concrete was rather soft i e easily penetrated by the
auger with minimal grinding Fragments recovered from the drilling
showed relatively poor aggregate to cement bonding Additionally
when chipping the concrete to repair the drilled hole it was noted
that the concrete easily broke under the impact of a 16 oz hammer
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Boring 1 which was drilled outside of the hangar on the west
side encountered 13 5 feet of fi11 overlying residual soil This
ill consisted generally of very stiff clayey silts with some organics
Boring 2 which was drilled inside the hangar encountered 4
inches of portland cement concrete underlain by 4 inches of medium
dense sand with gravel Below the slab section the boring
encountered 6 feet of fill consisting of very stiff silts with much
organics At a depth of 7 0 feet residual soil was encountered
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our
exploration
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our observations and exploration we do not feel that the
distresses to the hangar floor are the result of building settlement
It is our opinion that the cracking could be the result of
overstressing of the concrete slab by the wheel loads induced by
moving the helicopter in and out of the hangar
GEOLABS HAWAII
The floor slab of the hangar is in effect being used as a rigid
pavement to support aircraft When the helicopters are moved in and
out of the hangar they are supported by two small wheels
approximately 12 inches in diameter and 3 inches in width Using a
weight of 4000 lbs for the helicopter we calculate that a working
flexural stress of approximately 270 to 370 p S i is being exerted on
the concrete With the typical flexural strength of Class B concrete
at about 360 p s i this will yield a factor of safety ranging from
about 1 3 to 0 97
If the modulus of subgrade reaction is poor due to a loose cushion
or low quality fill below the slab the factor of safety would be
reduced to a range of 1 2 to 0 8 Furthermore if the actual
compressive strength of the concrete in the floor slab is 2000 p sei
the factor of safety would fall to a range of 0 9 to 0 77 However
confirmation of these points would require determination of the actual
strength of the concrete in the slab
In view of the low factors of safety it appears that
overstressing of the slab or overstressing in combination with
shrinkage cracks is most likely the cause of the cracking observed in
the slab

REMEDIAL MEASURES
There are several courses of action available for remedial
measures Among the alternatives are
1 Allow the slab to continue cracking
2 Pavement overlays and
3 Remove and replace the slab
The first alternative would be to do nothing and allow the slab
to continue cracking until such time as it is no longer serviceable
The advantage to this program is that it would not require any
financial outlay at the present time The disadvantages are that the
slab will eventually need to be repaired or replaced and that
construction costs may be significantly higher at that time
With respect to the application of overlays to the existing slab
the disadvantages far outweigh any advantages which could be
obtained The use of elastomeric overlays can effectively be ruled
out since their application is more of a cosmetic nature and they do
not increase the strength of the underlying concrete Therefore the
concrete would continue to crack and eventually rupture the overlay
Since the minimum thickness of a concrete overlay would be on the
order of 4 inches this would create problems in matching the existing
grades in the hangar doorway and the areas of the building that are
not used as hangar space An asphaltic concrete overlay would require
a similar thickness and create similar problems
GEOLABS HAWAII
It would appear that the most straight forward remedial measure
would be to remove and replace the existing slab or at least the
portion used for aircraft parking to create a hardstand This
hardstand should consist of the following pavement section
5 inch Class AA Concrete 4000 p s i
4 inch Compacted Granular Base Course
6 inch Compacted Subgrade
S inch Total Pavenent Thickness
The existing slab and cushion should be removed and disposed of
off site The elevation of the existing subgrade should be adjusted
to accommodate the new slab and base course The upper 6 inches of
the subgrade should then be scarified moisture conditioned to opt imum
moisture and then compacted to a minimum of 95 maximum dry density as
established by ASTM D 1557 78 The base course layer should also be
compacted to 95 maximum dry density
Design Review
Plans and specifications for remedial work should be forwarded to
the soils engineer for review and written comments prior to con
struction to see that the intent of our recommendations is included

CONSTRUCTION MONITORING
It is recommended that the grading and concrete placement
operations be observed by the soils engineer during construction
This is to see that adequate compaction has been obtained and the
subsurface conditions are consistent with the findings of our
investigation Samples of concrete used should be obtained for
compressive strength tests requirement
