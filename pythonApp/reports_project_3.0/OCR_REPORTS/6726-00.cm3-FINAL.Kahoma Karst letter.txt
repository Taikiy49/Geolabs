EVALUATION OF KARST FORMATION 
KAHOMA VILLAGE – WEINBERG SUBDIVISION 
LAHAINA, MAUI, HAWAII 
Dear Ms. Suyama: 
As requested, we prepared this letter to present our findings regarding the 
possible presence of karst formation within the project site. Our evaluation is based on a 
review of geologic information, soil maps, and recent borings and test pit information 
obtained at the site.  
Karst is a geologic term used to describe landscape underlain by limestone rock 
formation which has been eroded by dissolution, producing fissures, sinkholes, and 
undulating topography. In Hawaii, karst is associated with some hard coral formations. 
Therefore, limestone rock or hard coral formation must be present to have karst 
geology/formation. 
The project site is located on an alluvial fan along the northwestern portion of 
Maui. The project site is shown on Plate 1.  
GEOLOGIC CLASSIFICATION 
A comprehensive geologic map of Maui was developed by Harold T. Stearns and 
Gordon A. Macdonald in their publication, “Geology and Groundwater Resources of the 
Island of Maui, Hawaii”, dated October 1942.(1) A portion of this map covering the 
project site is attached as Plate 2. Stearns and Macdonald describe the geologic 
materials at the site as: 
Unconsolidated Deposits (Ra) – chiefly younger alluvium consisting of 
unconsolidated poorly sorted, poorly rounded, stream-laid brown silt, sand, and 
gravel. It carries small quantities of water near the mouths of the perennial 
streams, but the quality of the water is poor below lands irrigated with brackish 
well water.

SOIL CLASSIFICATION 
The surface soils underlying the project site are described by the U.S. Soil 
Conservation Service in their publication "Soil Survey of Islands of Kauai, Oahu, Maui, 
Molokai and Lanai, State of Hawaii," (1972).(2) A portion of the soil map covering the site is 
attached as Plate 3. The major soils at the site and some of their general properties are, 
as follows: 
Ewa Silty Clay Loam (EaA) – These are well-drained soils in basins and on 
alluvial fans. These soils developed in alluvium derived from basic igneous rock. 
Permeability is moderate, runoff is slow, and the erosion hazard is slight. 
Pulehu Silt Loam (PpA) – These are well drained soils on alluvial fans and 
stream terraces and in basins. Permeability is moderate. Runoff is slow and the 
erosion hazard is not more than slight.  
The soil maps indicate that the site is underlain by alluvium, not coral formation.  
TOPOGRAPHY 
The project site is situated near the mouth of Kahoma Stream as shown on 
Plates 1 and 2. As a general rule, coral formations do not grow near the mouths of 
streams because the fresh water and turbidity from the streams and alluvial deposits 
inhibit the coral growth.(3) Therefore, based on the project’s topographic location, coral 
formation is unlikely. 
ENGINEERING EXPLORATION 
Geolabs, Inc. conducted a geotechnical engineering exploration for the project 
site. Findings of our exploration are presented in our report dated December 11, 2012. 
The on-site exploration consisted of drilling and sampling ten borings to depths of about 
21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Additionally, eight test pits were excavated 
with a backhoe to depths ranging from about 10 to 11 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The approximate locations of the borings and test pits are shown on Plate 4. 
The borings and test pits encountered alluvial soils consisting of silt and clayey 
silt, to depths ranging from 8 to 18 feet below the existing ground surface. The alluvial 
soils were underlain by beach deposits consisting of relatively clean sand, to the depths 
drilled.

CONCLUSION
The actual soil data obtained from the borings and test pits at the site confirmed
the general information from the geology and soil maps, namely that the site is
underlain by alluvial deposits. Alluvial deposits do not contain karst conditions. As
described above, karst conditions are only found in some limestone or hard coral
formations. We did not encounter coral formation in our exploratory holes which
extended to about 21.5 feet deep and extended as much as 14.5 feet below the existing
ground water level.
