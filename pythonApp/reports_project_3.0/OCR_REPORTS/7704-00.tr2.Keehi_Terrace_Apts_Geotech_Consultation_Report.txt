Project Considerations 
The Building A Distress project is located within the Keehi Terrace Apartment 
complex at 2238 Kaluaopalena Street in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. Based 
on the information provided, we understand the X07 units in Building A (northeast corner) 
of the apartment complex have experienced distress due to ground movement, and it is 
desired to remediate this distress.  
Previously, several geotechnical investigations by others have explored the 
subsurface conditions at this northeast corner of the Building A project site. As part of the 
settlement agreement, we understand that two repair concepts to remediate the building 
distress were agreed to by the experts. In general, Repair Concept 1 involved 
underpinning the existing perimeter wall footings with micropiles, and Repair Concept 2 
involved compaction grouting and probing and grouting beneath the existing foundations 
along the exterior walls. A summary of these two approved design concepts are attached 
to this letter report (as Plates 7 and 8) for ease of reference.

Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored further by drilling and 
sampling two exploratory borings (designated as Boring Nos. 101 and 102) extending 
down to a maximum depth of about 15 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan (Plate 2). For ease of reference, 
boring locations by others at the project site also are included on the Site Plan.  
Based on our field exploration, the project site is underlain by surface fills and coral 
formation extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 15 feet below the 
existing ground surface. Fill materials extended from the ground surface to depths of 
about 1.2 to 4.5 feet below existing grades and consisted of medium stiff sandy clay and 
very dense silty sand. The underlying coral formation was classified as soft to medium 
hard with pockets of silty sand and gravel. A few possible voids were encountered in the 
coral formation. Groundwater was not encountered at the time of our exploration; 
however, it should be noted that water levels may vary with seasonal rainfall, time of the 
year, and other factors. 
Discussion 
Based on the available information and the results from the additional geotechnical 
investigation conducted, the distress to Building A likely was caused by ground movement 
due to vibrations from nearby construction activities. The high frequency content 
vibrations from construction activities can induce the types of cracks observed at the 
building site without causing permanent changes to the underlying ground conditions. It 
is also possible for construction vibrations to induce the cracks observed at the building 
site by causing some foundation movement.  
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the various geotechnical 
investigations, we believe the additional micropiling foundation suggested in Repair 
Concept 1 would NOT be necessary. The recommendations contained in this 
Geotechnical Consultation Letter are intended to augment the recommendations 
contained in Repair Concept 2 except that we believe the probing and grouting 
recommendations contained in Repair Concept 2 may be omitted due to the relatively low 
grout overages during boring backfilling operations. It is unlikely that the cavities and/or 
voids encountered in the borings are sufficiently extensive to cause appreciable future 
ground subsidence below the existing structure foundations under static loading 
conditions and the occasional seismic loading conditions. Furthermore, we also do not 
believe compaction grouting of the underlying loose zones would be beneficial to 
foundation support considering the depth (about 10 feet below the ground level) these 
loose zones were encountered in the borings. Therefore, we also do not believe that 
compaction grouting of the underlying loose zones contained within the coralline deposits 
suggested in Repair Concept 2 would be necessary and may be omitted. However, we 
do recommend repairing the slab-on-grade foundations as described in Repair Concept 2 
of the settlement agreement and included in this letter report as Plate 8, for ease of 
reference.

Further discussion of these items and our geotechnical recommendations are 
presented in the following sections. 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We understand a new concrete slab-on-grade will be constructed in Unit A107 to 
replace the existing ground floor slab. The existing slab to be demolished should be 
completely removed. Over-excavations resulting from the demolition operations should 
be backfilled with compacted select granular fill material. Select granular fill should consist 
of non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. 
The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles 
passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or 
more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance 
with ASTM D1883. Geolabs or an accredited testing laboratory should test the imported 
fill materials for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project 
sites for the intended use. 
Utilities to be abandoned in-place under the floor slab (where appropriate) should 
be backfilled by pumping lean concrete (or Controlled Low Strength Material) under low 
pressure. 
After clearing and demolition, areas designated to receive fills should be scarified 
to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative 
compaction, in this letter report, refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions, we recommend placing a 
minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, 
No. 67 gradation) below the new concrete slabs-on-grade. The open-graded gravel 
cushion fill would provide uniform support of the slabs and would serve as a capillary 
moisture break. To reduce the potential for appreciable future moisture infiltration through 
the floor slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings, we recommend providing an 
impervious moisture barrier (minimum 10 mil thickness with taped joints) on top of the 
gravel cushion fill layer.

Due to the relatively low grout overages during boring backfilling operations, it is

Limitations 
The geotechnical field and laboratory data submitted herein are based upon 
information obtained from the borings. Variations of the subsurface conditions between 
and beyond the exploration points may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until additional exploration and/or construction is 
underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the 
recommendations provided in this report. 
The boring locations indicated in this report are approximate, having been taped 
from visible existing features at the project site. The physical location of the borings should 
be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote 
a gradual transition. Water level data presented in the text herein were based on 
measurements recorded in the borings at the times shown on the graphic representations 
of the borings. However, it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in 
rainfall, seepage, tide, temperature, and other factors.
