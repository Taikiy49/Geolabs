PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The Ho’opili Development project is located to the south of the Interstate Route H-1 
Highway and west of Fort Weaver Road in the Ewa area. The Phase 4 area is located at 
the middle southwestern portion of the development. 
It is desired to evaluate a design foundation alternative, using the Helical Pile system, 
to support smaller single-family house structures using post-on-pier with raised floor planned 
at the Parcel 8 area. To evaluate the Helical Pile system, a test program was implemented 
consisting of installing various sizes of piles for pull-out testing. 
Although the houses will have raised floors, on-grade concrete slabs or pavements 
will be required for the garages. Therefore, to reduce the effects of the on-site expansive 
clayey soils, soil stabilization was proposed, using EcSS 3000 Electrochemical Soil 
Stabilizer, as an alternate to the “conventional” removal and replacement alternative. To 
evaluate the treatment’s effectiveness, Geolabs carried out a field exploration program 
consisting of drilling and sampling at selected locations within an 18-foot by 18-foot EcSS 
test section before and after treatment. Laboratory tests were conducted on the soil samples

HELICAL PILE INSTALLATION 
Helical Pile is a prefabricated steel foundation system consisting of a central shaft 
with one or more helix-shaped bearing plates. A bracket can be attached on top of the 
helical shaft providing structural connection in the concrete footing. Helical piles can be 
installed by advancing into the ground with the application of torque. The steel shaft with 
helix plates is used to extend the pile into the competent load bearing soil and to achieve 
design capacity. 
Multiple helical piles were installed at Phase 1, Parcel 3 and Phase 3, Parcel 8 areas, 
as shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2.  Kelikai, Inc. performed the installation and 
testing of the helical piles.  
CHANCE helical piles with either 6-inch/8-inch double helixes or 10-inch single helix 
were installed to depths of 8 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface. The helical piles 
were installed by a motor head attached on the backhoe excavator under manufacturer’s 
supervision at the site, as shown in the photo below.

It should be noted that uplift demand on the helical pile was estimated based on the 
soil expansion force against the concrete grade beam. Based on the schematic foundation 
layout and on-site soil swell characteristics, we anticipate an uplift force of about 15 kips to 
be supported by the helical pile. To examine the ultimate capacity of the helical pile, the 
maximum test load (MTL) of about 60 kips was applied on the test piles, which was about

four times of the design capacity. The helical piles (HP-3, HP-5 & HP-10) with 10-inch helix 
were able to sustain the MTL without reaching failure. However, the helical pile (HP-9) with 
6-inch and 8-inch double helix experienced pull-out failure. 
In addition, some amount of slack was left in the helical piles from hard torque and 
grinding near the end of the pile installation. This resulted in up to 0.4 inches of movement 
during the initial loading to remove the slack. We believe that hard torque and grinding 
should be avoided during the helical pile installation to reduce the creation of excessive 
slack in the pile system. 
Based on the above load test results, we believe the 10-inch single helical piles 
installed at the project site to a depth of 8 feet with a torque pressure of 5,000 foot-pound 
are capable of supporting an uplift load of 15 kips with an adequate factor of safety and with 
reasonable displacement characteristics. 
Therefore, we believe that the proposed 10-inch single helical piles may be a viable 
design foundation alternative to support the smaller plantation style post-on-pier 
single-family house structure planned at the Ho’opili Phase 3 Parcel 8 site. We recommend 
the top of the helical piles to be embedded in the concrete grade beam. Each helical pile, 
including steel shaft and helix plate, should be galvanized for corrosion protection. The 
helical piles should be installed to a minimum depth of about 8 feet with a torque of no more 
than 5,000 ft-lb, to minimize hard torque and grinding conditions. 
We also recommend performing pull-out tests (proof tests) on selected production 
helical piles during construction, to confirm the uplift load capacity. Based on the helical pile 
installation records, Geolabs should select test piles after completion of the production pile 
installation. The pullout tests should consist of subjecting the helical pile to at least 22.5 kips 
(approximately 150 percent of the design uplift load capacity). The helical pile should be 
loaded in 25% design load increments, and each load should be held for at least 5 minutes. 
The maximum test load should be held for a minimum of 10 or 60 minutes. 
Pullout testing on selected helical piles is an integral part of the design of the helical 
pile foundation system. Therefore, we recommend conducting the pullout tests under the 
observation of a Geolabs representative. 
EcSS ELECTROCHEMICAL SOIL STABILIZATION 
The main cause of expansion in clayey soils is the tendency of individual soil 
particles to attract and hold water.  Clay particles have a net negative electrical charge 
and attract positively charged ions present in the soil-water matrix.  In the attempt to 
attract positive ions, many layers of water molecules are held by the clay particle.  These 
layers of adsorbed water force individual clay particles away from each other, resulting in 
soil expansion. 
An electrochemical stabilization system, EcSS 3000, was proposed to reduce the 
swelling potential of the on-site expansive soils at the Ho’opili site.  EcSS 3000 is a

mixture of naphthalene oil, sulfur trioxide, sulfuric acid, and other additives.  A proprietary 
resin catalyst is also added to enhance the reaction times.  The ion exchange solution is 
mixed with water and introduced into the soils under pressure through injection points 
drilled at a grid pattern of about 3 feet on center. 
EcSS 3000 creates a cation rich environment in the injected zone and the 
individual clay particle’s negative charge is effectively neutralized.  The structure of the 
clay particles is changed physically and chemically from an expanding crystalline lattice 
to a non-crystalline, amorphous structure to greatly reduce the ability of the clay particle 
to attract and hold water.  This results in a great reduction in the shrink-swell potential of 
the clay soils. 
The soil stabilization operation was performed by Kelikai, Inc.  Prior to the injection 
operation, a grid pattern of 3 feet on center in a staggered pattern was laid out in the field 
within an 18-foot square area, resulting in a total of 46 injection points, as shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2.1.  Hand-held injection rods were manually forced downward with a 
hammer in approximately 12-inch vertical intervals, up to a depth of about 5 feet below 
the existing ground surface at the injection points, as shown in the photo below.

We understand that a diluted EcSS solution at a ratio of about 3 percent 
(1 gallon of chemical to 300 gallons of water) was injected at each 1-foot interval into the 
underlying expansive soils at a pressure of about 200 to 250 pounds per square inch 
(psi). 
A post-treatment evaluation program was conducted upon completion of the EcSS 
injection operations.  Five boreholes were drilled for soil sampling at each stage of 
evaluation (pre-injection, 2-week and 4-week curing), extending to a depth of about 5 feet 
below the ground surface. The boreholes were selectively located across the test site to 
obtain a wide range of treated soil samples to evaluate the effectiveness of the 
stabilization operations under different reaction times (2-week and 4-week).  The 
approximate locations of our borings are also shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.1. Boring 
logs are presented in Appendix A. 
Evaluation was conducted by comparing the soil shrink-swell characteristic at the 
outer, inner and center of the treatment area for pre-injection and post-injection 
conditions. Boreholes were numbered in the format to differentiate the above reaction 
stages and locations. For example, Boring No. P-X.Y, where X represents reaction stage 
(1 for pre-injection, 2 for 2-week post-injection, and 3 for 4-week post-injection), and Y 
represents location (1 and 5 for outer, 2 and 4 for inner, and 3 for center). 
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were retrieved at depths of about 3 and 5 feet 
below the ground surface at each boring location.  The laboratory test program was 
performed to examine the reduction in the shrink-swell potential of the on-site clayey soils.  
Details of the laboratory tests performed on the selected soil samples are presented in 
Appendix B and summarized on Plate 7 of this report. 
Based on the laboratory test results, it appears that some improvements in 
reduction of swell value and pressure are generally achieved by the proposed EcSS soil 
stabilization. However, the effectiveness is variable and may not significantly neutralize 
the on-site expansive pressures of the clayey soils. The swell pressure after 4-week 
post-injection is still up to 500 pounds per square foot (psf). 
Based on the results of the EcSS test program, we believe that the proposed EcSS 
soil stabilization may not be an effective and consistent alternative, to mitigate the on-site 
clayey soil shrink-swell characteristic that may adversely impact the lightly loaded 
single-family foundation performance. However, it may provide a cost-effective solution 
to support other smaller structures, such as carports, provided the foundation slab is 
designed to resist uniform swell pressure up to about 500 psf or allow for some smaller 
vertical movements.
