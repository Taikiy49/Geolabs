SECTION 1.0 - GENERAL
1.1. Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the restoration of the former settling ponds at the Grace Pacific Sand
Processing Plant at Barbers Point Harbor in the Ewa area on the Island of Oahu,
Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location
Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering
analyses. The recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of site
preparation and restoration of the ponds. The findings and recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.
1.2 Project Considerations
Based on the information provided, the site was formerly used for the processing
of sand from material that had been dredged from the construction of Barbers Point
Deep Draft Harbor. Part of the sand processing operations required the use of settling
ponds for the retention of wastewater generated during washing of the aggregate
material. It is proposed to restore the site to create a new industrial subdivision. A key
consideration for the sitework is restoration of the former settling ponds.
The available plans for the proposed site grading indicate that the pond area will
be graded into a broad pad area sloping down from northeast to southwest with
proposed finish elevations ranging from about 24 feet above Mean Sea Level (MSL) to
about 30 feet MSL.
1.3. Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the surface and
subsurface conditions within the former pond areas to develop an idealized soil/rock
data set to formulate preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations to assist in
the planning of the site preparation and mass grading of the project. Our work was

2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age)
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Koolau
Volcano reached the end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago.
The project site is situated on the Ewa Coastal Plain which extends seaward off
the southeastern flank of Waianae and the southwestern flank of Koolau. This plain
was formed by the accumulation of a thick wedge of sediments and coral reef
formations on the flanks of the two volcanoes.
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level.
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations. | Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of
the calcareous reef materials.

2.3. Subsurface Conditions
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by excavating and
sampling eight test pits, designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 through 8, extending to depths
ranging between about 7.0 and 17.0 feet below the existing ground surface. The
approximate test pit locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

are underlain by highly variable sediments ranging from very soft saturated silts to
medium dense to dense sands with some cobbles and boulders. Coral formation was

drilled; however, cavities are commonly encountered in coral formations.

3.1.1 Site Preparation
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other
unsuitable materials, should be removed and disposed of properly off-site or
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials.
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing, grubbing and over-excavation
work should be over-excavated to expose firm natural material, and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted engineered fill. The excavated
soft soils may be used as fill, provided that it meets fill material requirements.

3.1.2 Soft Surface Conditions
Based on our field exploration, there are soft surface soil conditions in portions of
the former pond areas. Conventional site preparation procedures in these areas
may result in the bogging down or sinking of earthmoving equipment. Therefore,
the following recommendations are presented to provide guidance for site
preparation in such areas.
In areas where excessive pumping, equipment bogging or sinking occurs during
clearing and grubbing work, we recommend constructing a working table over the
proposed fill area. This working table should consist of a minimum 2-foot thick layer
of boulders and cobbles ranging from about 6 inches to a maximum of about
18 inches in dimension underlain by a non-woven geotextile, such as Mirafi 180N,
or equivalent. Placement of the working table should start at one side of the fill
area and progress smoothly across the fill area to stable ground. If signs of
instability, such as excessive pumping or heaving of the working table, or formation
of a mud wave in front of the table placement, the work should be suspended and
the fill area should be allowed to rest until pore pressures in the soft subsoils
dissipate. It is estimated that this resting period would typically be a period of 1 to
2 days.
After installation of the working table, the surface should be proof-rolled a minimum
of ten passes with heavy earthmoving equipment, such as a Caterpillar D-9N. Any
yielding or pumping areas found during the proof-rolling should be stabilized by the
addition of additional boulder and cobble material.
Upon completion of the working table, the surface should be capped with a
minimum of 5 feet of structural fill compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTMD 1557. This capping layer is needed to
reduce the potential of migration of fines from the overlying new fill into the coarsely
graded working table material.

3.1.3 Keying and Benching
The ground surface in the former pond areas is nearly level; however, the adjoining
berms provide locally steep slopes. Therefore, this section is provided to give
guidance in design and construction of grading for those areas. Any keys and
benches required should be firmly seated into existing ground.
At the toe of fills on ground where the existing slope is steeper than five horizontal
to one vertical (5H:1V), a keyway should be cut down into stable material. This key
should be a minimum of 2 feet in depth at the toe of the fill and should be a
minimum of 8 feet in width extending back under the toe of the fill. The floor of the
key should be sloped back under the base of the fill at about 10H:1V to bond the fill
into the existing slope.
As fill placement progresses up the existing slope, benches should be cut into the
existing slope to bond the mass of the fill to the existing ground. These benches
should be a minimum of 2 feet in height and approximately 5 feet in width. A
schematic section illustrating the keying and benching recommendations is shown
on Plate 3.
Based on the site conditions encountered, the keys and benches should be
proof-rolled prior to fill placement with vibratory sheepsfoot compactor or similar
heavy equipment for a minimum of six passes to help verify that adequate support
for the fill has been achieved. The proof-rolling operations should be performed in
the presence of a Geolabs representative. Yielding areas, loose areas, or cavities
disclosed during clearing and proof-rolling operations should be backfilled with
compacted fill materials.

pond areas. The structural fill material should consist of non-expansive granular
material, such as crushed coral, basalt or cinder sand. The material should be
well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in maximum

3.1.5 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills consisting of structural fill material should be moisture-conditioned
to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding
10 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTMD 1557. Optimum moisture is the water
content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or
other types of acceptable compaction equipment.
Because moisture-conditioning and compaction of the fill soils, particularly for the
reduced compaction requirements, are critical elements of earthwork, Geolabs
should perform observations and soil density tests during site grading to assist the
contractor in obtaining the appropriate degree of compaction and the proper ranges
of moisture content. It should be noted that the moisture and reduced compaction
requirements for fills and subgrades are important criteria for use of the on-site
clayey soils. Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort
should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary, to obtain the
specified compaction. Similarly, in situations where the reduced compaction
requirements are applicable, if the required range of compaction is exceeded, it

It is anticipated that the on-site berms between the ponds may be excavated with

3.1.7 Cutand Fill Slopes
This section is provided to give guidance for design and construction of slopes in
the event that they may be required. Based on the anticipated site conditions, it
appears that any cut slopes required for this project would expose soils consisting
of medium dense to dense sands and gravels. In general, we believe that cut
slopes should be made at an inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills
should be keyed and benched into the existing slope, as discussed above, to
provide stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations should start at
the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance
with the above fill placement recommendations.
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Track-rolling of slopes should not be
accepted. In the event that over-cutting of a slope occurs, keying and benching
requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design
grade with sliver fills. Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and

3.2 Ground Settlements
Based on our field exploration, it appears that much of the old pond bottoms are
underlain by very soft and very loose sediments. The vertical and lateral extent of the
compressible deposits will need to be further evaluated in a detailed geotechnical
engineering exploration to be conducted in support of the design of the development.
Furthermore, the impact of the highly compressible lagoonal deposits on the proposed
development above these areas will also need to be evaluated and taken into
consideration in the planning and design of the proposed development at the site.
The laboratory consolidation test results indicate that the soft to very soft deposits
at the site appear to be normally consolidated. Based on the thickness of the soft soils,
the thickness of fills to be placed, and our engineering analyses, the range of the probable
consolidation settlements due to fills was calculated. The estimated consolidation
settlements due to 3 feet of new fills range from approximately 2 to 6 inches. For fills up to
about 6 feet in vertical height, the estimated consolidation settlements are approximately
4 to 10 inches.
Based on our analyses, the estimated time to achieve between 75 to 80 percent
consolidation will be about 12 months or more, depending on the rate of drainage in the
soft sediments. Because of the very high moisture contents in the silty sediments,
long-term settlements (post construction settlements) due to continued decrease in
moisture content will also occur. It should be recognized that it is difficult to accurately
predict the exact time required for the filled ground to settle, because settlement rates are
affected by variations in the subsoil structure and the history of the subsoil deposition.
Because the estimated settlement period could vary from the actual settlement
period, the actual settlement rates should be monitored, and a settlement monitoring
program should be established to evaluate the magnitude and rate of the estimated
settlements during the settlement waiting period prior to construction of utilities and other

improvements over the fills. In addition, provisions should be made for potential delays in
the construction schedule if a longer settlement waiting period is required.
To monitor the actual settlement rate, we recommend installing a series of
settlement gauges in the fill areas of the site. Geolabs should establish the settlement
gauge locations when the grading plan is developed for mass grading of the site. In

Readings should be taken once a month for at least 18 to 30 months or until the

of wick drains, however, the relative benefits of these methods can not be assessed using
the limited information collected in this current exploration. Therefore, additional
exploration and analyses would be required.
