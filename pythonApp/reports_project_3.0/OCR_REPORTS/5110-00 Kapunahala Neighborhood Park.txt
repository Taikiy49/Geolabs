REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two
shield volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older volcano, Waianae, is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield, volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series.
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level.
The process of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic sea
level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level.
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations.
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls, and the soil materials are
transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once
these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes
become dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. In
general, stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the stream flows
transmit large volumes of water for very short duration. Because of this, transport of
sediments is intermittent, and the bulk of the stream's hydraulic load consists of a poorly
sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines. When the erosional base
levels change, these sediment loads are left as deposits.
When deposits are left in-place for long periods of time, chemical processes begin
to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material.
Chemical processes also cause induration, or cementation, of the coarse-grained portion
of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate.
Simultaneously, erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in
headwaters. This continued erosion generates material which is transported downslope
covering the older alluvial deposits. Depending on the local base level and rate of
transport, these newer sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time. In
addition, their consistency and density are generally less than those of the older, partially
consolidated deposits.

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located within the existing Kapunahala Neighborhood Park
adjacent to the Kapunahala Elementary School in the District of Kaneohe on the Island of
Oahu, Hawaii. The site is bounded by Anoi Road on the east and the softball field on the
south and west. Access to the site is located on the north of the existing park from
Kapunahala Elementary School. Details of the location are shown on the Site Plan,
Plate 2.
The site is rectangular in shape and enclosed by a chain link fence. Two horizontal
to one vertical (2H:1V) cut slope is located on the eastern side of the park down to Anoi
Road. Concrete Retaining Masonry (CRM) walls about 3 to 8 feet high are located on the
north and northeast side of the park as grade separation purpose. A comfort building is
situated in the front the of park entrance. A water fountain is located southwest of the
comfort building. Water ponding was observed in the low grass area from the fountain
extending towards the paved maintenance access ramp.
The existing playcourts consist of one volleyball court and two basketball courts.
Total of eight existing light poles were observed around the playcourts. Rough surface
conditions were observed in the playcourt areas.
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling three
test borings extending to depth of about 15 feet below the existing ground surface. The
approximate locations of the test borings are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
In general, the site is underlain by 8 inches of asphaltic concrete over about 3 feet
of surface fill material consisting of hard clayey silt soil. The surface fill layer is underlain
by stiff to very stiff residual deposit extending to the maximum depth explored of
approximately 15 feet below the existing ground surface.
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration. However, groundwater levels are expected to vary with seasonal rainfall,
surface water runoff, and time of year.
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on
the Logs of Borings, Plates 4 through 6. Results of the laboratory tests performed on
selected soil samples are presented on Plates 7 through 10.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our borings drilled and light
pole structural loading provided, we recommend that drilled shaft foundations embedded
in the very stiff residual material be used to support the proposed lighting system. We
recommend that the drilled shafts be embedded a minimum of 8 and 9 feet below the
lowest adjacent finished grades for the single and double fixture light pole systems,
respectively. Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this letter report.

The cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally
from skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils. An allowable compressive

Lateral loads imposed on the light pole foundations may be resisted by

is a function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil, the stiffness of the shaft, allowable
deflection at the top of shaft, and induced moment in the shaft. As indicated above,
each drilled shaft may be subjected to a lateral load of up to about 930 pounds with an
associated overturning moment of up to about 25,200 foot-pounds at the ground
surface. Our lateral load analyses also indicated that the estimated deflection at the top
of the drilled shaft should be less than 0.1 inches. The results of our foundation
analyses are presented in the previous table.
Settlement of the light pole foundations due to the vertical and lateral loads should
be elastic and should occur as the loads are applied. The estimated settlement of the light
pole foundations due to the allowable compressive load should be on the order of less
than 0.25 inches.
Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the light pole foundations because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities wnen compared to that of shallow foundations.
However, the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor's method of construction and construction procedures. In our opinion, the
following conditions would have a substantial effect on the effectiveness and cost of the
drilled shaft foundations.
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend, to a large extent, on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils. Therefore, proper
construction techniques are important. The contractor should exercise care in drilling the
shaft holes and placing concrete into the holes. In the event that loose and/or soft soils
are encountered during drilled shaft construction, temporary casing may be required
during the foundation construction work in order to reduce the potential for caving-in of the
drilled holes. Therefore, we recommend that temporary casing of the drilled shaft holes be
included in the contract documents.
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is not recommended. Placement of
concrete by wet construction methods using a tremie pipe will be required where
groundwater is encountered in the drilled holes. Concrete should be placed in a suitable
manner by displacing the water in an upward fashion from the bottom of the drilled hole.

A low-shrink concrete mix with high slump (6 to 9-inch range) should be used to

Pavement Design
We understand that new asphaltic concrete pavements are planned for the
maintenance access ramp. In general, we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the
pavements will consist primarily of passenger vehicles and light pick-up trucks. We have
made our preliminary pavement design assuming that the pavement subgrade will consist
of the re-compacted on-site materials encountered near the surface. Based on the above
assumptions and CBR test results, the following pavement section may be used for
preliminary budgeting purposes:
Flexible Asphaltic Concrete Pavements
2.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction
8.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Moist Subgrade
The subgrade soils below the new pavement section should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. The aggregate base course
should consist of crushed basalt aggregates compacted to not less than 95 percent
relative compaction. CBR and field density tests should be performed on the actual
subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above
section.
Paved areas should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to
carry surface water off the site. Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site
during or after construction. Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or
adjacent to the pavement areas, we recommend that the curbs be extended a minimum of
2 inches into the subgrade soils below the aggregate base course to reduce the potential
for migration of excessive landscape water into the pavement section.

new lighting system. In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or
S4C is recommended under the duct lines. Free-draining granular materials, such as
clean sand or S4C, should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 6 inches

accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content
corresponding to the maximum dry density.
