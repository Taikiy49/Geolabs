Current Status of Earth Movements
The earth movements monitored at the Waiomao Landslide and study Areas A
and B have not stabilized and continue to experience slow downhill creep movements
Based on the history of the earth movement monitoring sudden catastrophic earth
movements are not likely but cannot be completely ruled out
Earth movements detected immediately after subdivision construction were
severe and occurred at substantially higher rates than the current movements recorded
during our monitoring at the project site Our record of the earth movements indicate
that the movements continue at a typically slow and relatively steady rate with some
inclinometers showing incremental patterns of accelerated and regressive movement
Extreme rainfall events and the increased subsurface groundwater seepage associated
with such rain events may have the potential to accelerate the movement in the short
term and cause significant disruption to the existing pavement and fixed structures in
the area
The typical rate of subsurface displacement at the Waiomao Landslide

The on going earth movements will continue to adversely affect City
infrastructure such as roadway surfaces and underground utilities Continued monitoring
and maintenance of the utility infrastructure is important to reduce sources of excessive
water infiltrating into the subsurface which can accelerate movements High volume
discharges in the storm drains have the potential to scour and create voids where line
offsets or breaks occur Utility line owners such as water supply wastewater and
drainage should be notified of the active earth movement condition and the need to
promptly detect and correct possible continued utility damage as a result of the ongoing
earth movements Prompt action to detect and correct leaks is recommended CCTV
video survey of existing drain and wastewater lines in the Kuahea Street and Waiomao
Road vicinity should be undertaken periodically in an effort to detect possible line
breaks and the undesired discharges that could aggravate the active earth movements
It should be noted that there is no evidence indicating that the areas of
documented earth movement have increased in size and area coverage
Short Term and Long Term Repair Options
We understand that engineered mitigation efforts to slow the movements within
the public right of way will be performed as funds become available For long term
landslide mitigation retaining structures such as drilled shaft and or tie back walls were
proposed to restrain the hillside areas adjacent to the public right of way
Short term or interim stabilization also was considered using reinforced earth
structures to stiffen up the pavement subgrade support to accommodate the ongoing
earth movements and to reduce the impact of differential movements near the
pavement surface These interim stabilization measures are intended to prolong the life
of the infrastructure The interim stabilization project at the Kuahea Street has been
divided into the upper and lower sections The Upper Kuahea Street Interim Repair
project was completed in 2013 2014 while the Lower Kuahea Street Interim Repair
project has been awarded to a Contractor for construction

In general our interpretation of the earth movement response to elevated rainfall
is that if a period of increased or above normal rainfall is experienced following a
prolonged period of normal or lower than normal rainfall the earth movement rates at
some of the inclinometers tend to increase abruptly following the period of elevated
precipitation and then settle back to normal levels In addition following extended
periods of below normal precipitation earth movements generally decrease slightly

installed in eight boreholes at the project site A list of the piezometer installations for
the project is provided in the Table entitled Summary of Completed Instrumented
Installations on Pages 7 and 8 of this progress report

Based on our review and interpretation of the groundwater levels recorded
near continuously by the vibrating wire piezometers it appears that periodic
groundwater influxes resulting from normal rainfall events head spikes are relatively
abrupt followed by a gradual dissipation of the head The gradual head dissipation
typically occurs over a period on the order of several days to several weeks Very high
spikes in head level as a result of extreme rainfall and or prolonged heavy rain events
typically take several months to fully dissipate the elevated groundwater head level

Kipona Place
Based on our observations the existing roadway and upslope conditions along
Kipona Place at the toe of the Waiomao Landslide appear to be generally static
with minimal visible signs of overt ground distress and deformation
However a localized earth slump previously reported at the slope toe abutting
Kipona Place and adjacent to the existing home at 2345 Kipona Place appears to
continue very slowly creeping toward the roadway The earth slump has
developed on a small portion of the graded slope upslope from Kipona Place
The overall graded slope appears to be generally stable and in a static condition
with the exception of the localized area comprising the creeping slump Based on
our observations the slump contains a small vertically oriented scarp face of
about 12 inches high and about 8 to 10 feet in length at the edge of the road
pavement The scarp presence indicates that the soil mass slump had at some
time encroached onto the roadway from the shoulder and may have been
truncated by excavation removal along the road edge
Because the slump appears to have remained active over the years the slump
and associated soil mass appear to have some potential for additional future
movement and possible encroachment onto the roadway Furthermore we
observed a broad area of topographic depression higher on the slope surface
above the embankment where the slump is located The topographic depression
may allow some ponding of water to occur Therefore some remedial slope
stabilization efforts generally consisting of slope drainage improvements and
surface grading are recommended to reduce the potential for a larger release of
earth material to affect the roadway below Future earth movements slumps
should be anticipated to occur following large magnitude rainfall events
Existing Waiomao Landslide Subdrain System
Based on our site reconnaissance no discharge of fluid has been observed
emanating from the existing groundwater subdrain outlets installed at the
Waiomao Landslide The subdrain system was installed as part of the early
landslide remediation work performed at the project site It appears the
groundwater subdrain system may be non functional
