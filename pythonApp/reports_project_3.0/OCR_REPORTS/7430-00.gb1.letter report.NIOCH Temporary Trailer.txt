REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and 
Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion 
incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a series of 
lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred to as the 
Honolulu Volcanic Series.

CLIMATIC CONSIDERATIONS 
The Hawaiian Islands are located in the tropics.  The islands’ climate includes mild 
temperatures throughout the year, moderate humidity, the persistence of northeasterly 
trade winds, significant differences in rainfall within short distances, and infrequent severe 
storms.  Most of the State of Hawaii has only two seasons.  The two seasons are 
"summer" (between May and October) and "winter" (between October and April). 
The average temperature on the Island of Oahu ranges from 74 to 88 degrees 
Fahrenheit with humidity of about 53 percent during the day.  The project site is located 
within an area considered to be an “interior lowland.”  The “interior lowland” is generally an 
area of sometimes intense local afternoon rain from well-developed clouds that form as a 
result of local heating of the land during the day.  The annual rainfall is about 25 to 
50 inches in most areas and as much as 60 inches in some.  Most of the rain falls during 
the winter season, between October and April.  
Because the project site is located within a relatively high rainfall environment, the 
contractor working on this project should be made aware that during the winter season, 
rainfall may affect the construction schedule during earthwork operations.  In addition, the 
on-site soils at the project site tend to have high in-situ moisture contents.  Therefore, use 
of the high in-situ moisture soils as fills generally would affect the rate of fill placement at 
the site, especially during the rainy season.  In addition, the potential for ponding and 
saturation of the on-site soils and fill materials would be high if good earthwork 
construction practices are not diligently practiced.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
A field exploration consisting of drilling and sampling borings was not conducted at 
the site.  Instead, research and review of available in-house soil and boring data in the 
project vicinity and a site reconnaissance were performed in order to evaluate the 
subsurface conditions at the site.  
Based on our research and review of available subsurface information in the project 
vicinity and our site reconnaissance, we anticipate that the subsoil conditions at the site 
would consist of residual soils.  The residual soils would generally consist of very stiff to 
hard silty clays and clayey silts. 
We do not anticipate encountering  groundwater in the relatively shallow 
excavations for this project.  However, it should be noted that groundwater levels may 
change due to rainfall, seepage, time of year, seasonal precipitation, and other factors.

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the anticipated competent subsurface conditions at the site, we 
recommend using shallow foundations to support the modular building.  Detailed 
discussions and recommendations for foundation design and site preparation are 
presented in the following sections. 
Modular Building Foundations 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used for the design of the foundations bearing on the in-situ soils.  This bearing value is for 
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as 
those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
The soils encountered at the bottom of footing excavations should be re-compacted 
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Soft and/or loose soils encountered at the 
bottom of the footing excavations should be removed to expose the underlying firm 
materials. The resulting over-excavation should be backfilled with non-expansive granular 
materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing may 
extend down to bear on the underlying firm material. 
In general, the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finished grades.  Foundations next to utility trenches or 
easements should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) imaginary 
plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts 
of the utility lines.  This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent 
below-grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for 
appreciable foundation settlement. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate that total footing settlements may be on the order of 1 inch 
or less. We estimate that differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on 
similar materials to be on the order of about 0.5 inch. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed 
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure 
acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation.  A coefficient of friction of 0.30 may 
be used for footings bearing on in-situ soils.  Resistance due to passive earth pressure 
may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per 
foot of depth (pcf).  This assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted.  
Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of 
soil should be neglected.   
A Geolabs representative should observe the footing excavations prior to the 
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions and 
the required embedment depths.

Site Preparation 
We believe that only minor site grading work will be required to achieve the finished 
grades at the site.  In general, areas within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly 
cleared at the on-set of earthwork.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site.  Soft, loose, and 
yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or future 
improvements should be over-excavated to expose firm or dense material, and the 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill.  The excavated soft, 
loose, and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site.  In addition, contract 
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and 
compacted fill placement to account for variation in the over-excavation quantities. 
After clearing, the areas to receive fill and finished subgrades in cut areas should 
be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above 
the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction.  The compaction requirement for the finished subgrades in areas subjected to 
vehicular traffic should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of 
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density.   
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft and/or loose areas and 
replacement with well-compacted fill.  A Geolabs representative should evaluate the need 
for over-excavation in the field. 
In general, the excavated on-site soils may be re-used as a source of general fill or 
backfill materials, provided that they are free of deleterious materials (vegetation) and are 
screened of particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension.  Imported fill materials 
needed for the site filling should consist of non-expansive granular materials, such as 
crushed coral or basalt.  The materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with 
particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve.  The materials should have a laboratory 
CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when 
tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.   
Fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, 
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  The top 36 inches of fill 
placed in areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction.  In general, compaction should be accomplished by 
sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other similar types of acceptable compaction 
equipment.
