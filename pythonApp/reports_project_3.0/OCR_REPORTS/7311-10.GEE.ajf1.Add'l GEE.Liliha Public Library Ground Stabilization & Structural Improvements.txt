SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the results from our field exploration, the project site is generally 
underlain by fill materials, alluvial soils, weathered clinker, and basalt rock formation. 
The fill materials and alluvium contain highly expansive clay soils and exist in a very 
moist condition. We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of 
our field exploration. 
Based on available construction drawings, the building is supported on shallow 
foundations consisting of spread and continuous footings. Grade beams are used to 
support the exterior walls and are connected to the spread footings. The interior 
concrete slab-on-grade appears to be underlain by a 6-inch layer of S4C (clean sand) 
and a 12-inch layer of select borrow. In addition, the interior concrete slab-on-grade 
appears to be “floating” and not structurally connected to the grade beams or the spread 
and continuous footings.  
Based on the results of our field exploration and the construction drawings, we 
believe the interior concrete slabs-on-grade, S4C, and select borrow materials are 
underlain by highly expansive clayey soils. Slabs-on-grade placed over expansive soils 
may be subjected to movement induced by the shrinkage and swelling of the soils due 
to moisture variations. Interior walls that are placed on these slabs-on-grade can be 
sensitive to these movements and will crack when the slab moves inducing excessive 
wall distortion.  
Results of our floor level survey indicate severe differential movement has 
occurred along portions of the interior slabs-on-grade at the western portion of the 
building. Based on our review of available information, the results of our additional field 
exploration, floor level survey, and site observations, we believe that the presence of 
highly expansive clays below the existing concrete slabs and the potential for water 
infiltration into the clayey subsoils may have contributed to the non-bearing interior 
partition wall distresses observed at the project site. 
To reduce the potential for water infiltration adjacent to the existing building, we 
recommend providing positive drainage adjacent to the building so discharge from the 
roof drain pipes will flow away from the building. In addition, the planter area and 
irrigation system should be removed or the irrigation water within the existing landscape 
area should be contained and properly drained so water does not get into the underlying 
clay soils. In addition, we recommend that the existing plumbing lines servicing the

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our additional geotechnical engineering 
exploration performed for the Liliha Public Library Ground Stabilization & Structural 
Improvements project located in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes our observations and findings and presents our 
discussions and recommendations with respect to the observed distresses on the 
interior wall, and slabs-on-grade distresses at the western portion of the building 
structure based on our site reconnaissance, floor level survey, field exploration, 
laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The Liliha Public Library is located on the eastern side of Liliha Street, south of 
the Interstate Route H-1 Freeway. The library is a one-story concrete structure with 
vehicular parking on the roof. The building was constructed in the 1960’s. Based on 
available construction drawings, the building is supported on shallow foundations 
consisting of spread and continuous footings.  
Signs of distress included cracks and separations in some of the interior 
non-bearing partition walls at the southwestern portion of the building structure. In 
addition, cracking and separation of exterior concrete walkways around the northeast 
and southeast sides of the building were noted along with tilting of perimeter masonry 
walls adjacent to these walkways.  
Our recommendations for remedial repairs of the exterior concrete walkways 
were contained in our report for the project entitled "Geotechnical Engineering 
Exploration, Liliha Public Library Ground Stabilization & Structural Improvements, 
Honolulu, Oahu, Hawaii" dated May 16, 2016. This supplemental report focuses on 
evaluating the interior wall, and slabs-on-grade distresses observed at the western 
portion of the building. A geotechnical engineering exploration and floor level survey

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located on the east side of Liliha Street, south of the Interstate 
Route H-1 Freeway. The library is a one-story concrete structure built in the 1960’s with 
vehicular parking on the roof. The site is generally rectangular in shape and is bordered 
by the Liliha Street On-Ramp to the Interstate Route H-1 Freeway to the north, 
residential apartment buildings to the south, Kauluwela Elementary School to the east, 
and Liliha Street to the west.  
In general, the topography of the site gradually slopes down from south to north 
with existing ground surface elevations ranging from about +43 to +40 feet Mean Sea 
Level (MSL) at the southeastern and northwestern portions of the site, respectively. 
2.2 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
one boring, designated as Boring No. 4, extending to a depth of about 20 feet below the 
existing ground surface. In addition, we also explored the subsurface conditions at the 
project site by drilling and sampling two hand-augered borings, designated as Hand 
Boring Nos. 101 and 102, extending to depths of about 4.5 and 6.3 feet, respectively, 
below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring and hand-augered boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our borings generally encountered a pavement structure consisting of about 
3 inches of asphaltic concrete and 9 inches of coralline base course material overlying a 
surface fill layer, alluvium, weathered clinker, and basalt rock formation extending to the 
maximum depth explored of 20 feet below the existing ground surface. The surficial fill 
material generally consisted of medium dense silty gravel and extended to depths of 
about 1 to 2.8 feet below the existing ground surface.  
The alluvial soils encountered generally consisted of medium stiff to very stiff clay 
with varying amounts of sand and gravel and extended to depths of about 4.5 to 8 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The weathered clinker consisted of medium dense

2.3 
Existing Distress Observations – Interior Walls and Slabs-On-Grade 
The distresses observed were generally located in the western portion of the 
building at the Men’s and Women’s Restroom, Janitor’s Room, Storage Room Nos. 2 
and 3, and the Meeting and Staff Rooms. The observed distresses to the interior walls 
were generally located below the ceiling and roof beams and above and on the sides of 
door openings. In addition, application of grout was observed where the floor tiles evidently 
separated from the building perimeter wall in the Men’s Restroom. 
Based on our site observations, the existing distresses observed to the interior 
walls generally consisted of vertical separation of the non-bearing interior partition walls 
at the ceiling and roof beam and horizontal and vertical cracks adjacent to several door 
openings. The observed separations varied from hairline to about 1-inch thick while the 
horizontal and vertical cracks were generally hairline. Photographs of these distresses 
are shown on Plates 4.1 through 4.6. In addition, the photograph location, number, and 
direction are also shown on the Floor Level Survey Plan, Plate 3.

3.1 
Potential Cause(s) of Observed Distresses 
Based on our review of available information, the results of our additional field 
exploration, floor level survey, and site observations, we believe that the following 
conditions may have contributed to the non-bearing interior partition wall distresses 
observed at the project site. 
1. 
Presence of highly expansive clays below the existing concrete slabs  
2. 
Potential for water infiltration into the clayey subsoils  
Detailed discussions of these findings and observations are presented below. 
3.1.1 Presence of Highly Expansive Clay Subgrade 
Based on the results of our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain 
by highly expansive clays near the ground surface. These clay soils are typically 
very sticky and plastic and are classified as “CH” according to the Unified Soil 
Classification System. These soils generally exhibit high shrink/swell potential, low 
permeability, and become compressible when saturated.  
Our previous laboratory tests indicate that the near-surface clays encountered 
exhibit high shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil 
moisture contents. Therefore, there is a significant potential for distress to the lightly 
loaded concrete slabs-on-grade resulting from swelling and compression of the 
clayey soils due to an increase in the moisture content, as evident by the very moist 
conditions encountered for these subsurface clay soils. 
3.1.2 Potential Water Infiltration into Clayey Subsoils

our borings generally were very moist. Based on our site observations, potential

3.2 
Recommendations for Remedial Measures 
Based on our findings and field exploration, recommended remedial measures were 
developed and are presented below. 
3.2.1 Interior Slabs-On-Grade 
Based on the results of the floor level survey, it appears that the exterior portion 
of the building slabs is settling more than the interior portion. Differential 
settlements measured were typically 1 inch or more in the staff toilet area, janitor 
room, and the men’s and women’s restrooms.  
To reduce the potential for water infiltration adjacent to the existing building, we 
recommend providing positive drainage adjacent to the building so discharge 
from the roof drain pipes will flow away from the building. In addition, the planter 
area and irrigation system should be removed or the irrigation water within the 
existing landscape area should be contained and properly drained so water does 
not get into the underlying clay soils.   
As indicated above, it appears that the largest settlements have occurred near 
the toilet and restroom areas. Therefore, we recommend that the existing 
plumbing lines servicing the toilets and restrooms be checked for leakage. 
Leaking pipes that are found should be repaired/replaced. 
After the above measures to reduce water infiltration into the underlying clay 
subgrade are performed, we recommend performing an additional floor level survey 
to verify that the slab movements have subsided prior to proceeding with the

remedial work for the slabs and walls. Once the floor level survey has confirmed 
that the slab movements have subsided, the slabs-on-grade may be patched or 
removed and replaced if desired. If the slabs-on-grade is removed and replaced, 
we recommend providing a minimum 3-foot thick layer of non-expansive select 
granular fill material below the slabs. 
Select granular fill should consist of non-expansive granular material, such as 
crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The material should be well-graded 
from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and 
should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. 
The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have 
a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D1883. Geolabs should test imported fill materials for conformance with 
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
The non-expansive select granular fill materials should be placed in level lifts of 
about 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Prior to 
placement of the select granular fill material, the over-excavated subgrade should 
be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent 
above the optimum water content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction. The underlying subgrade soils and select granular fill should be wetted 
and kept moist until the final placement of slab concrete.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field borings. Variations of the subsurface conditions 
between and beyond the field borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then 
appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations presented 
herein. 
The field boring locations indicated herein are approximate, having been 
estimated by taping from visible features shown on the As-Built Plans transmitted by 
KAI Hawaii. Inc. on December 16, 2014. Elevations of the borings were estimated from 
contours shown on the same plan. The field boring locations and elevations should be 
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on the graphic representations of the borings 
depict the approximate boundaries between soil and/or rock types and, as such, may 
denote a gradual transition. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time 
of our field exploration. However, it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to 
variation in seasonal rainfall, and other factors. These data have been reviewed and 
interpretations made in the formulation of this report. 
This additional geotechnical engineering exploration report has been prepared 
for the exclusive use of KAI Hawaii, Inc. and their project consultants for specific 
application to the remedial design of the Liliha Public Library Ground Stabilization & 
Structural Improvements project in accordance with generally accepted geotechnical 
engineering principles and practices. No warranty is expressed or implied. 
This report has been prepared solely for the purpose of assisting the design 
engineers in the remedial measures for the project. Therefore, this report may not 
contain sufficient data, or the proper information, to serve as a basis for detailed 
construction cost estimates.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
one boring, designated as Boring No. 4, extending to a depth of about 20 feet below the 
existing ground surface. In addition, hand augering and sampling two hand borings, 
designated as Hand Boring Nos. 101 and 102, to depths of about 4.5 and 6.3 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. The boring was drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig with continuous flight augers 
and coring tools, while the hand borings were drilled using portable hand augering 
equipment.  
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material 
is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting drilling 
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core 
run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as 
RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock, 
based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals."

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Six one-inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively “undisturbed” and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-3. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plate B-4. 
One Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) test was performed 
on a selected in-situ soil sample in accordance with ASTM D2850 to evaluate the 
undrained shear strength of the soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” sample. The 
test results and the stress-strain curve is presented on Plate B-5.
