REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea
both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain which makes up
the west central portion of the island The majority of the lava formation at the site appears
to be of pahoehoe type lava flows which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean
Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal
structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe lava
Cavities are formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when
molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend
for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and pockets inter flow cavities are
generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted through
the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
GEOLABS INC
W O 3308 00 A Page 5
clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as the
primary water transmission features

SEISMIC REFRACTION SURVEY
Seismic refraction surveys are normally used to determine the thickness and depth of
geologic layers by calculating the travel time or velocity of seismic waves within the different
layers Seismic waves transmitted into the subsurface travel at different velocities in various
types of soil and rock and are refracted at the interfaces between layers of different materials
This refraction affects their path of travel An array of geophones placed at the ground surface
measure the travel time of the seismic waves The seismic waves are generated at a source
location and progress outward to the geophone stations arranged at specific spacings The
time required for the seismic wave to complete this path is measured permitting a
determination to be made of the number of different layers the thickness of the layers and
their depths as well as the seismic velocity of each layer The seismic velocity may be used
to estimate the rippability of the material The wave velocity in each geologic layer is directly
related to the material properties such as density and hardness
GEOLABS INC
W O 3308 00 A Page 8
A seismic source geophones and a seismograph are required to make the field
measurements The seismic source employed is typically a sledge hammer which is used
to strike the ground surface at the source location The geophones implanted at the ground
surface translate the received vibrations of seismic energy into an electrical signal This signal
is displayed on the seismograph permitting measurement of the arrival time of the seismic
wave In general velocity values are greater for rock material that is dense older unfractured
and unweathered Highly fractured rock such as clinker generally result in lower velocity
values Since the seismic method measures very small ground vibrations as well it is
inherently susceptible to vibration noise from a variety of sources such as nearby traffic and
construction activity Our current seismograph is equipped with the capability to enhance the
signal to filter out such random noise The concept of a seismic survey is generally based
upon the following fundamental assumptions
1 Seismic velocities of the geologic layers must increase with depth This
requirement is generally met at most sites
2 Layers must be of sufficient thickness to permit detection
3 Seismic velocities of layers must be sufficiently different to permit the resolution
of individual layers

Subsurface Conditions
In general the geophysical surveys performed for this project reflect the subsurface
conditions for depths ranging from about 32 to 60 feet below the existing ground surface The
subsurface data generally indicates that the site is underlain by a relatively low velocity
surface layer that may be composed of medium hard clinker and ash with some hard basalt
This surface layer appears to range in thickness from about 2 to 8 feet Below this layer an
intermediate velocity layer that may consist of hard vesicular fractured pahoehoe lava and
clinker rock appears to extend to depths of about 20 to 38 feet below the ground level Below
this layer a relatively high velocity material appears to exist that may consist of very hard
dense basalt locally known as blue rock Based on the results of the seismic survey it is
anticipated that hard rock excavation may be encountered within the depths of the proposed
excavation requiring intensive ripping the use of hoe ram and or the use of blasting for the
deeper excavations
It should be noted that the interpretation of the seismic data is based on our ground
surface observations and the seismic refraction survey data only If more detailed and
accurate information of the subsurface rock formation is desired exploratory borings at
selected locations should be performed to confirm and correlate the seismic velocities with the
actual rock material obtained from continuous rock cores

optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with AASHTO T 180 ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other
types of acceptable compaction equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of
the majority of the rocks or 5 feet whichever is less Placement should be by haul
trucks traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the currently placed
lift Spreading of the rock fill should be by a large bulldozer to facilitate seating of
the rocks The rock fill should be watered heavily during placement by water trucks
traversing in front of the current rock lift face and spraying water continuously during
placement Compaction equipment such as a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar
heavy compaction equipment providing suitable energy to achieve the required
compaction should be utilized The number of passes usually six to eight passes to
GEOLABS INC
W O 3308 00 A Page 17
be made to achieve proper compaction should be determined at the on set of site
grading by the performance of a trial compaction test or by plate bearing tests
Subsequent layers of rock fill above the previously placed rock fill should provide a
choking layer of smaller sized boulder materials to fill in the voids between the boulders
of the preceding rock fill layer
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of rocks
boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of passes
by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Based on this testing program the number of passes
may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction For preliminary
estimating purposes compaction may be accomplished by using a large vibratory drum
roller having a static weight of about 15 tons traveling at a speed of about 300 feet per
minute The roller should pass over the fill surface at least eight times for structural fill
and six times for general fill For general fill a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar size
equipment may be used in lieu of the vibratory drum roller

Seismic Refraction Survey
The subsurface conditions along the Phase 1 portion of the highway alignment were
explored by the performance of six seismic refraction surveys at the approximate locations
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The seismic refraction surveys were conducted using a
12 channel signal enhancement seismograph with a 12 pound sledge hammer as a seismic
energy source
Refraction seismography is based on the transmission of elastic energy
compressional waves through geologic materials Generally the denser or more
consolidated a material is the faster the transmission of the compressional waves These
waves are refracted at interfaces between materials with differing densities The refracted
waves are received by geophones on the surface and recorded by the operator The recorded
data is input into a desktop computer for processing which involves the interpretive selection
of the arrival times of the refracted waves
After the interpretive selection of arrival times the technique permits mathematical
calculations that yield approximate depths or thickness of materials below the survey lines and
provide their approximate compressional wave velocities The velocities are averaged along
the travel path of the compressional waves The calculated velocities may be empirically
related to the excavation characteristics of the materials Results of the Seismic Refraction
Survey are provided on Plates A 1 through A 12 The following legend explains the terms
shown on the Seismic Refraction Survey Results Plates A 1 through A 12

Dip Dip of the top of the layer in degrees
