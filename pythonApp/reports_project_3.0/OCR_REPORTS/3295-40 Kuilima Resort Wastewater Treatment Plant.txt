INTRODUCTION
This report presents the findings of our injection well performance evaluation for
the Kuilima Resort Wastewater Treatment Plant at Kahuku on the island of Oahu
Hawaii The findings and recommendations presented herein are based on site
observations made during a site visit the performance of short term injection testing on
the wells and personal communication with operators and managers of the treatment
plant facility The approximate locations of the treatment plant facility and the injection
well field are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Our work was performed in general accordance with our proposal dated
August 29 1997 and the State of Hawaii Department of Health Underground Injection
Control UIC Injection Well Status Report Guidelines Version 1 dated June 1992
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Kuilima Resort Wastewater Treatment Plant injection wells are utilized for the
disposal of secondary treated effluent generated within the Kuilima Resort development
The majority of the effluent treated at the plant is used for irrigation of the resort golf
course within the Kuilima Resort complex Therefore the wells are used for the injection
of treated effluent only on a stand by basis during periods of time when the effluent
cannot be utilized for irrigation purposes generally due to wet weather conditions
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3295 40 Page 2
As a condition of the Underground Injection Control UIC permit UIC Permit
No UOQ 1532 issued for the facility it is required that periodic monitoring of the wells be
made by a professional Geologist or Engineer familiar with injection well operations
Geolabs Hawaii has prepared this injection well evaluation report to provide the Owner
the operator and the regulatory agency with information regarding the performance of the
injection wells to aid in the prevention of adverse public health effects from an injection
well failure This evaluation is intended to assist in ascertaining that the wells remain
operational

DISCUSSIONS AND FINDINGS
The wells are located on the easterly side of the tee boxes for the 10th Hole of
Golf Course G 2 The wells are situated in an open grass area between the tees and the
moat around Punahoolapa Marsh The topography of the well field area is generally level
with some topographic mounds and depressions associated with the adjacent golf course
terrain Based on a topographic map of the well field and vicinity ground surface
elevations range from about 5 to 6 feet Mean Sea Level At the time of our site visit on
March 9 1998 the weather conditions were generally dry under sunny skies No
evidence of surface ponding of water was visible in the vicinity of the well field
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3295 40 Page 5
The well heads are enclosed in below ground sump vaults of approximately 4 feet
by 4 feet in plan dimension and about 5 feet in depth The vaults are covered with hinged
steel plate doors and are approximately flush with the adjacent grass covered ground
surface
Inside the vaults the well heads consist of steel tees with flange plates bolted to
their tops for security and to allow the build up of additional head if needed These
plates were removed by plant representatives for the inspection and performance of the
injection tests In 1994 two inch diameter inspection ports were installed in the well
covers to eliminate the need for removal of the entire top plate during future
inspection testing However to allow complete access to the well interior for the
performance of the short term injection tests the top plate of each well was removed
Well Field Usage
In general only two of the three wells are placed in use for effluent disposal at any
time The inactive well is taken out of operation on a rotating basis so that at any given
time only two wells will be in operation Flow to an individual well is controlled by a valve
located adjacent to each well head A main control valve diverts the effluent flow from the
treatment plant to either the well field or the lagoon which serves as storage of water for
use as golf course irrigation
Based on the available Daily Operating Log records for the past 20 months the
following generalizations regarding effluent flow to the well field may be made For the
period of July 1996 through March 1997 injection well flows were relatively consistent in
occurrence with an average daily flow ranging between about 200 000 to 300 000 gallons
per day From April 1997 through the middle of September 1997 daily flow to the
injection wells virtually ceased with some intermittent flow on the order of about 150 000
gallons per day or less From mid September 1997 through mid February 1998 daily
flows increased to average on the order of about 180 000 gallons per day with only
sporadic days of zero flow occurring
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3295 40 Page 6
The effluent flows to the well field continue to reflect the general relationship
between the wetter and drier seasons of the year and the need to utilize lesser or greater
amounts of effluent for irrigation purposes The summer months typically result in greater
irrigation needs and thus less effluent is directed to the well field In contrast during the
wetter winter months the need for irrigation is lower and as a result more effluent is
directed to the well field for injection disposal
Based on our review of the Daily Operating Logs the individual injection wells are
placed in service on a rotating basis The frequency of well rotation was increased
significantly beginning in September of 1997 and continued through February of 1998
According to plant personnel the rotation schedule was increased as a result of the
observation of elevated head levels in the wells As a result of the recent redevelopment
of the wells and improved disposal capacity the well operation will continue to be rotated
but less often
Injection Well Redevelopment
Due to an observed declining trend in disposal capacity of the injection well system
over the past couple of years the three injection wells were redeveloped by airlift
methods The work was performed on February 2 and 3 of 1998 A 130 cfm air
compressor was utilized to blow out the injection wells by lowering the air line into the
bottom of the well The discharge of well water produced by the airlift cleaning was
contained within the well vault and pumped to a tanker truck for proper disposal
Plant personnel reported that the well cleaning procedure was effective in
removing significant sediment and debris from the wells Furthermore following the well
cleaning efforts the measured head levels during injection operation were observed to be
lower than previous measurements The lower head levels during operation indicate that
the injection wells appear to have sustained an increase in effluent disposal capacity as a
result of the well cleaning

Injection System
To regulate the system valves are used to isolate individual wells so that any one
or combination of the wells may be used for the injection of effluent At the time of our
site visit the system operation was set up such that Well Nos 2 and 3 were currently on
line as active wells for the injection of effluent Well No 1 was isolated from the system
as an emergency back up to the system
The system is equipped with a flow totalizing meter which measures the flow
volume in gallons This meter measures the total flow to the well field rather than to
individual wells In addition the meter registers the cumulative flow in gallons
Observation of valves which are open during an injection period may be used in
conjunction with the totalizing meter readings to roughly estimate the flow to an individual
well
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3295 40 Page 11
At the time of our site visit the totalizing meter registered a cumulative reading of
inspection in July 1996
Daily Flow to Well Field
Based on a review of the operational log of the injection well system for the
preceding 20 months daily flows ranged from zero to a maximum single day flow of
about 500 000 gallons per day Average daily flows appear to be on the order of about
200 000 to 300 000 gallons per day during heavy usage of the wells In general the
months of September through March appear to be the most active periods of injection
well usage due to the higher incidence of rainfall and reduced irrigation needs for the golf
course
In general for the period of April through August of 1997 flow to the well field was
low showing a pattern of several days to several weeks of zero flow to the well field At
other times throughout the last 20 months the wells were placed in use frequently with
only isolated periods of time out of operation
Short Term Injection Tests
Short term injection tests were performed on each of the three wells by diverting
treated effluent from the treatment plant into each well separately Each well was isolated
from the others for the duration of each test by adjusting the valves controlling the effluent
flow to individual wells
During injection testing the head level response was measured and recorded
Readings from the totalizing meter were recorded during the short term injection test to
obtain the total quantity of injected effluent and to estimate the average flow rate into
each well The duration of the injection test was dependent upon the head level rise in
the well In general the test was terminated when the head level rise reached near the
top of the well head casing prior to a potential overflow
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3295 40 Page 12
In general each injection test was commenced by opening the control valve to the
well being tested The head level was observed to rapidly rise and then began to fall as
the system flow reached equilibrium As the head level was observed to fall the other
operating well was closed so that the total flow would be flowing to the well being tested
At this time the head level would again begin to rise though at a slower rate Based on
these observations it is believed that as an injection well is opened or closed some delay
and fluctuation in the head level response is encountered as the system reaches
equilibrium in flow
Based on our observations of the static water level in the wells and the results of
our short term injection tests we conclude that the current operation of Well and
3 have improved significantly in relation to past well performance evaluations We believe
the well redevelopment cleaning effort had a_ significant positive impact on the
performance of the injection wells Based on the test results Well Nos 2 and 3 appear to
have the greatest disposal capacity followed by Well No 1 The injection test results are
presented in Appendix B of this report
Recommendations
Based on our observations the injection test results and the well usage data we
believe the injection wells have sustained a significant increase in disposal capacity since
the last inspection in July 1996 The increase in capacity is believed to be a result of the
recent well cleaning and redevelopment performed on the wells
It is a known fact that injection wells sustain losses in capacity over time due to
natural biological and chemical fouling processes or other introduced factors such as
suspended solids or debris contained in the effluent In addition extended periods of well
inactivity may cause acceleration of the normal losses in capacity
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3295 40 Page 13
It should be noted that some loss in capacity may be permanent and irreversible
however it has also been proven that redevelopment of injection wells can aid in the
recovery of lost capacity provided the proper methods are utilized on a regular basis
Based on our injection well evaluation we recommend that the Owner Operator
continue to operate the injection wells on a routine rotating basis in an attempt to
maintain the disposal capacity of the system In addition we recommend that the
Owner Operator closely monitor and document the operation of each of the wells to
assure that overflows or back ups related to a well failure are detected prior to their
occurrence In the event an overflow of a well or other failure occurs and the situation
cannot be remedied the Owner Operator should promptly notify the Department of
Health Underground Injection Control Branch
We believe that by increasing the use of the wells on a rotating basis especially
during the drier months of low effluent availability the current operational status of the
wells could be maintained Keeping the wells operational is not normally a problem
during the wetter winter months when effluent is more readily available for injection
However we believe it is crucial to maintain rotating operation of all the wells during the
summer months despite the lack of available effluent
We recommend that the Owner Operator consider recording additional field
observations such as the following 1 notes regarding the overall system operation
2 documentation of well maintenance performed 3 observation of the head levels
within the wells prior to and during injection events 4 daily flow volumes and 5 a log of
individual well usage This additional information will be used to evaluate the future
performance of these wells In addition the results of our short term injection testing of
the wells may be used to evaluate the operation of the wells with respect to prior well
inspection data
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3295 40 Page 14
Generally redevelopment should be considered if head levels continue to increase
over time or when well failures such as overflow or system back ups may be anticipated
Based on the effectiveness of the recent well redevelopment we believe it may be
prudent to consider scheduling well redevelopment at about two year intervals
Well redevelopment should be performed by a professional experienced with
coastal injection well construction and rehabilitation The redevelopment method selected
should be carefully evaluated based on the existing well design proper containment and
disposal of wastewater generated by the activity and other factors specific to the site and
operation of the wells
