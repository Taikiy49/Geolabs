PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
A new wine bar facility is planned for the Shirokiya store in the Ala Moana
Center The new facility will require renovation of existing spaces at the store
Renovation will be required at the Mall level Street level and the Coral parking level A
new elevator is required to access all three levels from the Coral parking level
Our consultation services are limited to the foundation design and construction of
the elevator only Based on previous explorations at the center we anticipate that the
subsurface conditions may consist of thin fill layers over soft lagoonal deposits underlain
by coral formation Due to the relatively small structure planned and the expense and
time for borings the existing subsurface information was used for the foundation design

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site consist of about 5 to 8 feet of
surface fill overlying 15 to 20 feet of loose soft lagoonal deposit overlying a coral ledge
coral limestone formation and coralline deposit extending to a depth of approximately
70 feet below the existing ground surface based on nearby borings
It should be noted that the coral ledge encountered at the site is highly variable in
thickness and in hardness The coral ledge was encountered within depths ranging from
about 20 to 25 feet below the existing ground surface Based on our past experience in the
vicinity the coral ledges are very erratic and usually not continuous
We encountered groundwater at a depth of about 2 feet below the ground
surface at the time of the micropile construction The groundwater level generally
corresponds to Elevation of about 0 0 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
MICROPILE INSTALLATION AND TESTING
Based on the structural evaluation the unfactored loads on the elevator are
120 kips and each micropile will need to support 30 kips assuming one micropile at
each corner for a total of four micropiles Based on the subsurface conditions at the site
and our experience with similar conditions each micropile was recommended to extend
to a minimum depth of about 55 feet below the bottom of pile cap The 55 foot depth is
based on a 20 foot unbonded length friction neglected within the fill and lagoonal
deposits and a 35 foot bond length load bearing grout bulb within the coral limestone
and coralline materials
In general the micropiles were drilled using TEI TD75 rig equipped with
casing to the specified depth Advancing steel casing to the specified depth was
required in order to facilitate the micropile drilling and ensure the quality of the grouting
Air pressure up to 300 pounds per square inch psi was utilized during drilling operation
to create a bulb around the drilling bit 4 inch diameter for advancing steel casing
After the drilled holes were extended to the specified depth and cleaned with the
air pressure Grade 75 all threaded 10 rebars were coupled and inserted into the
cased hole with centralizers Grout was batched on site utilizing a colloidal high shear
and high speed mixer equipped with a grout pump The grout mix
consisted of one sack of 94 pound Portland Cement Type I and pounds of

The grout was placed in the cased hole through a 1 inch PVC tremie pipe from

As part of the construction requirements one static uplift load test was conducted
on a selected micropile to verify the design parameters and evaluate the adequacy of
the foundation support system
Micropile Installation
Kelikai Inc installed a total of four micropiles from March 12 through 19 2012
The approximate locations of the micropiles installed are shown on the Micropile
Foundation Plan Plate 2 The top of the pile cap bottom of the deep foundation was
designed at 8 feet below the existing ground surface The micropiles generally extend to
a depth of about 65 5 feet below the existing ground surface equivalent to an Elevation
of about 63 5 feet MSL The four installed micropiles had about 35 5 feet of bond length
in the coralline section and about 22 feet of unbonded length in the permanent steel
casing Details of the micropile installation are presented in the Micropile Installation
Records in the attached Appendix
To facilitate drilling equipment in the low headroom space a square working pit of
about 12 feet was excavated to approximately 1 5 feet below the existing ground surface
The pit was also used as a basin to retain drilling water and cuttings
Positive grout in take was maintained during the grouting operation No micropile
necking was indicated based on the micropile grouting observation Actual grout placed
in each micropile is about 250 percent of the theoretical grout volume
Micropile Testing
In order to evaluate the load carrying capacities of the installed micropiles a
pullout test proof test was conducted on a selected micropile MP1 on
March 22 2012 In general the micropile was loaded to a maximum test load of 60 kips
200 percent of the design load of 30 kips in approximately 7 5 kip load increments
The test micropile was held at the design load of 30 kips for 10 minutes and the
maximum test load of 60 kips for 15 minutes to evaluate the creep effect The test
results are presented on the Micropile Load Test Data Sheets Plates 3 1 and 3 2 The
testing load versus micropile displacements are presented on Plate 4 and the creep
movements are presented on Plate 5

The test micropile was loaded gradually to the design load of 30 kips and the
maximum test load of 60 kips with 0 047 and 0 113 inches of vertical displacement
respectively Subsequently the micropile was unloaded to zero load in decrements of
about 15 kips and reloaded unloaded to further evaluate its capacity Based on our
review and engineering evaluation the micropile MP1 did not fail at the maximum test
load of 60 kips
CONCLUSIONS
Based on our field observations and the proof test conducted it is our
professional opinion that the specified micropiles for the project have been installed to
the required diameter and sufficient depth to provide the design load capacity of 30 kips
per micropile with a factor of safety of 2 0
