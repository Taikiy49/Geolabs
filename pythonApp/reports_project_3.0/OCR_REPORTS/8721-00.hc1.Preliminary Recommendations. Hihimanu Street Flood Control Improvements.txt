This memorandum summarizes our understanding of the project and presents
our preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations for the design of the
proposed Hihimanu Street Flood Control Improvements project planned in the
Waimanalo area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.
It should be noted that our preliminary recommendations presented herein are
based on our recently completed borings and laboratory testing of the recovered
samples. A detailed summary of our findings and recommendations will be contained in
our geotechnical engineering report. The final report should be consulted when it
becomes available.
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Hihimanu Street Flood Control Improvements project is located in the
Waimanalo area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. As part of the project, we understand
that a new drainage crossing consisting of new 36-inch diameter drainage pipes will be
installed across Hihimanu Street. The new drainage crossing will be adjacent to an
existing 30-inch diameter culvert crossing.
We understand that new headwall structures will be constructed for the new pipe
culverts. In addition, pavement design recommendations are desired for the restoration
of the roadway.
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings extending to depths of about 21.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.
Our borings generally encountered a pavement structure consisting of about
3 inches of asphaltic concrete over 6 to 9 inches of the sandy gravel base material. Fill
material, consisting of stiff to hard sandy clay and clayey silt with some gravel and
cobbles, was encountered below the pavement structure to depths of about 5 to 6 feet
below the existing pavement surface. Alluvium, consisting of medium stiff to very stiff

Seismic Design Parameters 
Based on the American Association of State Highway and Transportation, Load 
and Resistance Factor Design (AASHTO LRFD), Ninth Edition, 2020, the project site 
may be subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be 
addressed. The following provides discussions on the soil profile type for seismic design 
and liquefaction design consideration at the project site. 
Based on the subsurface materials encountered at the project site and the 
geologic setting of the area, we believe that the project site may be classified as “Stiff 
Soil Profile” from a seismic analysis standpoint.  Therefore, we believe the seismic 
design of the culverts may be designed based on a Site Class D soil profile in 
accordance with the AASHTO LRFD (Table 3.10.3.1-1), Ninth Edition, 2020.

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the information provided, we understand that construction of the 
retaining headwall structures for the new pipe culverts is planned for the project. In 
addition, we understand that AASHTO LRFD methods will be used for the design of the 
new headwalls. Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered during our field 
exploration performed, the proposed headwalls may be supported on a shallow footing 
foundation system. Detailed discussions and recommendations for foundation design 
and earthworks are presented in the following sections. 
Shallow Footing Foundations 
Based on the field exploration performed, we anticipate that headwalls supported 
on shallow footing foundations will bear on the medium stiff to stiff silty and clayey soils 
encountered in our borings below the existing pavement surface. We recommend 
providing a bedding layer consisting of 6 inches of compacted aggregate base course 
materials below the bottom of the footing for uniform support and to improve the bearing 
resistance. In addition, the bottom of the retaining wall footing foundations should be 
embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades.  Based 
on the above, we believe that the following values may be used to evaluate the bearing 
support, sliding resistance, and passive pressure resistance of the planned spread

Dynamic Lateral Earth Pressures 
For non-restrained conditions, dynamic lateral earth forces associated with 
seismic loading (PGA = 0.22g) may be estimated using 4.2H2 pounds per linear foot of 
wall length for level backfill conditions (H measured in feet). It should be noted that the 
dynamic lateral earth forces provided assume that the wall will be allowed to move 
laterally by up to about 1 to 2 inches in the event of an earthquake.  
If the estimated amount of lateral movement is not attainable or the retaining 
structure is restrained, the retaining structure should be designed with higher dynamic 
lateral forces for a restrained condition. For a restrained condition (less than 0.5 inches 
of lateral movement), dynamic lateral forces due to seismic loading may be estimated 
using 9.4H2 pounds per linear foot of wall for level backfill conditions. 
The resultant force should be assumed to act through the mid-height of the wall. 
The above dynamic lateral earth forces are in addition to the static lateral earth 
pressures provided above. An appropriately reduced factor of safety may be used when 
dynamic lateral earth pressures are accounted for in the design of the retaining 
structure.

hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system should consist of permeable material, 
such as AASHTO M43, Size No. 67 gradation material, placed directly around a 
perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of the wall discharging to an 
appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, 
such as MiraDrain, may be used instead of the drainage material.  The prefabricated

Site Grading 
We anticipate that the site grading work for the project will generally consist of 
minimal cuts and fills for the excavation and installation of the new pipe culverts and 
headwalls. At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared. Vegetation, construction debris, deleterious materials, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site or in a designated 
area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials with the spoils.  
After clearing and grubbing, the exposed subgrades should be scarified to a depth 
of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
In general, the excavated on-site soils should be suitable for use as general fill 
materials, provided that the maximum particle size is less than 3 inches in the largest 
dimension.  The on-site materials generated from the excavations may be used as a source 
of general fill or backfill materials, provided that they are screened of the over-sized 
materials and/or processed to meet the above gradation requirements (less than 3 inches 
in largest dimension). 
Imported fill should consist of non-expansive select granular fill material such as 
crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The materials should be well-graded from 
coarse to fine, with particles no larger than 3 inches in their largest dimension. The material 
should also contain between 10 and 30 percent fines (particles passing the No. 200 sieve). 
Select granular fill should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or 
more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance 
with ASTM D1883. 
Fill and backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. In areas where there will be 
pavements, the upper 3 feet of the fill/backfill below the pavement finished grade should be 
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to 
the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of

Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of the pipe has a significant effect on the 
load-supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the stiff silty/clayey soils, a minimum of 6 inches of 
compacted granular cushion material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation) should be provided below the pipe. Where soft and/or loose soils are 
encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, a stabilization layer consisting of an 
additional 24 inches of open-graded gravel, such as No. 3B Fine gravel, wrapped in a 
non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below the cushion 
layer for uniform support.  
The granular cushion layer should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of 
one-fourth (¼) of the outside pipe diameter, or trench width, whichever is greater and 
should extend 12 inches above the pipe. Before the placement of cushion material, a 
Geolabs representative should observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm 
materials are exposed at the bottom of the trench or whether a stabilization layer should be 
provided below the cushion layer. 
Trench Backfill 
It should be noted that loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the 
trench, the unit weight of the trench backfill material, and the friction resistance between the 
backfill material and the trench walls. To calculate the vertical loads on the buried pipes, we 
recommend using an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic feet (pcf) for the trench 
backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0.3. Earth forces acting upon the pipe 
generally increase rapidly with the width of the trench. Therefore, the width of the trench 
should be kept to a minimum. Traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be considered 
for the portion of the pipes located in roadway areas. 
In general, the first zone of backfill extending from the cushion material to at least 
12 inches above the top of the pipes should consist of select free-draining granular 
material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) to reduce the 
compaction effort required and resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is critical to use 
this free-draining material to reduce the potential for the formation of voids below the 
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper 
trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to the 
pavement subgrade should consist of the excavated on-site soils or imported fill materials 
that are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, organic material, clay lumps, and rock

Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement 
Backfill materials consisting of the on-site surface fill and/or alluvial soils should be 
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts 
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted 
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The upper 3 feet of backfill materials 
below the finished pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. 
Aggregate base course materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, 
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is 
the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other 
types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding should not 
be allowed to compact the excavated surface on-site fill soils or imported fill materials. 
Precautions should be taken against the occurrence of pipeline flotation before and during 
trench backfilling due to the possible influx of large quantities of groundwater into the

Pavement Restoration 
It is anticipated that AC pavement will be used to repave the excavations along the 
culvert alignments within the roadway. We have assumed that the pavement subgrade will 
consist of the on-site silty and clayey soils encountered near the existing pavement surface. 
Laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value test was performed on the near-surface 
soils obtained from our field exploration.  Based on the laboratory test results obtained, we 
have adopted a design CBR value of 9 for the pavement subgrade material in our 
pavement design analyses. Based on the assumed traffic loading information and 
pavement design analyses performed, the following flexible pavement section is 
recommended for the proposed pavement restoration:  
Flexible Pavement   
  4.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete 
  9.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
13.0-Inch Minimum Total Pavement Thickness Over Compacted Subgrade 
The above minimum pavement section is based on the assumption that the 
pavement subgrade soils can be dried to near optimum moisture content, and the subgrade 
could be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.
