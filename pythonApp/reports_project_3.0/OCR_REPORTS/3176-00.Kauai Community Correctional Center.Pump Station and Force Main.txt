INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Kauai Community Correctional Center KCCC Pump Station
and Force Main in the area of Lihue on the island of Kauai Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and may not be appropriate to form the basis for construction cost estimates nor for
construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS

and protection of near shore groundwater at KCCC it is proposed that the existing KCCC
cesspools be removed and that a raw sewage pump station and force main be
constructed to transport wastes from KCCC to the Wailua Sewage Treatment Plant for
ultimate treatment and reclamation or outfall disposal The location of the proposed pump
station and force main alignment are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3

macerating type The proposed wet well will likely require an excavation of about 10 to
12 feet below the existing ground surface Nominal interior size is about 6 feet by 10 feet

cast concrete elements The top of the wet well will extend to approximately elevation
12 feet Mean Sea Level MSL for flood protection

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of the lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene
more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of
the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic series cover most of the eastern half of the island of
Kauai Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic series are generally characterized as thick flows of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3176 00 Page 7
dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various
locales Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder
cones at the vent
During the Pleistocene there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Kauai remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio
eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to present sea level on Kauai
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
In the area of the project wind blown dune sands have been deposited on the
alluvial soil deposits The wind blown dune sands generally consist of fine grained sands
and may be of substantial thickness

SEISMIC CONSIDERATIONS
The island of Kauai is designated in the 1991 Uniform Building Codes as Seismic
Zone One Soil liquefaction is an important aspect of seismic considerations Soil
liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the ground
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore water
pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In this
process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such as during
an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in
pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build
up of pore water pressure the effective stress internal strength of the soils is reduced
Under sustained vibration the pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden
pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as
a viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both
horizontal and vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant
deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that liquefaction
will not occur if the deposit is greater than 50 feet below the ground surface In deeper
deposits the greater overburden pressure is generally sufficient to prevent liquefaction
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3176 00 Page 10
from occurring The major factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of a soil are
as follows
1 Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
3 Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake
In general the subsurface information obtained from the drilled borings appears
to indicate that the site is underlain by medium dense to dense fine sands Based on the
medium dense to dense nature of the sandy soils encountered and the design horizontal
acceleration for Kauai ground acceleration of 0 075g for Zone 1 we believe that the
potential for liquefaction at this site is low

estimate the magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon
workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring supports are installed
and on the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of
excavation

Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical if necessary and that the adjacent ground
should be continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of
movements with instruments
Dewatering
Dewatering of excavations for the force main and sewer line trenches may be
necessary depending on the depth of the excavation and groundwater conditions It
should be noted that borings were drilled only at the KCCC site and at the pipe jacking
pit on the Wailua Golf Course side Therefore subsoil and groundwater conditions along
other portions of the force main were based on information obtained from the trench logs
provided by International Archaeological Research Institute Inc
Dewatering of the excavations for the proposed pump station wet well and the pipe
jacking pits will be necessary since the groundwater levels measured were above the
bottom of the proposed excavations Because of the granular nature of the subsoil
conditions it is anticipated that the sandy soils encountered are relatively permeable
Therefore dewatering of these excavations may involve very large volumes of water if
dewatering with an open cut excavation method is selected Therefore it is our opinion
that a deep sheetpile cut off wall which will also provide the required excavation around
the proposed excavation be considered to aid in reducing the volume of water to be
removed within the excavation Dewatering within a deep sheetpile cut off wall will reduce
the areal extent of groundwater draw down outside of the deep excavation Therefore
dewatering provisions should be included in the contract documents for the proposed
construction
Since the excavation will involve discharge of groundwater dewatering a National
Pollutant Discharge Elimination System NPDES permit may be necessary The
GEOLABS HAWAIil
W O 3176 00 Page 22
contractor should consult their independent consultant or the State of Hawaii Department
of Health for the latest regulations and information pertaining to the permit application
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection
of equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and he she
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on the conditions encountered The dewatering method selected
should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the proposed
excavation
Dewatering operations should be performed such that only clear water is pumped
Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in a blowing or heaving of the
excavation bottom or sides
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents should
be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater table
and or to exclude the water from the excavation to allow construction of the proposed
structures under safe and dry conditions These works or systems may include but are
not limited to well pumping grouting cut off walls tremie plugs or any combination of
the above and or other possible methods

groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to

The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole

the best opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during
construction and to implement as soon as possible necessary precautionary or
remedial measures including but not limited to stopping or slowing down the
dewatering operations
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils may
be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations should
be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the excavation
bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be done without
pumping out soil fines bumping clear water only and should be coordinated with
shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not adversely affected

Any groundwater draw down outside the excavation may cause settlement due to
compression and consolidation of the potentially compressible soils Therefore
the use of a deep well system outside the excavation to draw down the
groundwater level should be avoided only allowed if the draw down of the
groundwater table would not affect the surrounding existing structures
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches connected
to excavations If this occurs the granular bedding and or backfill in the utility
trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater draw
down resulting in settlements and potential damage to utility lines and or other
existing adjacent structures

Closure of Existing Cesspools
We understand that the nine existing cesspools will be abandoned after the
completion of the proposed pump station and force main system Based on State of
Hawaii Department of Health Administrative Rules a permit under the Underground
Injection Control UIC program is required for abandonment of cesspools with a flow rate
of 1 000 gallons per day or more Therefore we recommend that the State of Hawaii
GEOLABS HAWAIi
W O 3176 00 Page 25
Department of Health be consulted first for proper abandonment of the existing
cesspools
However from a geotechnical engineering point of view the following procedure
for backfilling the cesspools is appropriate to reduce the potential for future ground
subsidence due to inadequate backfill compaction
Materials in the existing cesspools should be completely removed and disposed
of properly off site After removal of the materials in the existing cesspools any soft
and or loose materials should be over excavated to expose firm materials For ease of
compaction the select on site sandy soils or imported granular materials may be ideal
particularly to backfill the deeper portion of the excavated cesspools below the water
level The granular sandy backfill materials should be placed in horizontal lifts with loose
thickness not exceeding 8 to 10 inches Each lift of backfill material should be adequately
water tamped first and the water allowed to settle before the placement of subsequent
lifts The backfill materials within the upper 5 feet should be benched into the sides of the
excavation to bond the new backfill to the sides of the excavation The upper 5 feet of
backfill should also be moisture conditioned and mechanically compacted to obtain 90
percent minimum relative compaction
