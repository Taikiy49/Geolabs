1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering overview study
performed for the Kukuiula Resort Community development in Koloa on the Island of
Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 7
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our review of available geological and geotechnical
engineering information previous field explorations laboratory testing and engineering
analyses The findings and recommendations presented herein are general in nature
and are intended only to provide preliminary information for conceptual design of the
proposed development A more detailed exploration will be required for the various
portions of the resort community as they are developed The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
1 2 Project Considerations
The Kukui ula Resort Community development area encompasses approximately
1 045 acres of land in the Koloa District on the Island of Kauai Hawaii located north of
Lawai Beach Road generally between Poipu Road and the Spouting Horn The site is
being developed as a planned resort community and will include hotel commercial
recreational golf course and residential uses
The area was formerly used for sugar cane cultivation but is currently vacant
Portions of the site were mass graded in the early 1990 s for a previous development
plan The current master development plan calls for a golf course generally along the
southern side of the site with residential development spread throughout the remainder
of the development site A resort village is planned near the south central portion of the
site with a commercial development planned near the southeastern end About one
quarter of the area in the southeastern end of the development site was mass graded
earlier for a previously planned residential development

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano
built by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series beginning about
5 to 6 million years ago The eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series ended about
2 million years ago and was followed by a long period of erosion Following the
cessation of this main volcano shield building phase about 1 million years ago there
was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the post erosional
Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the thick alluvial sediments of
the Palikea Formation
Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as
thick lava flows composed of dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in
north south trends at various locales Associated with the Koloa Volcanic Series lava
flows are some deposits of pyroclastic materials volcanic ash and cinders which
usually form cinder cones surrounding the vent Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic
Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of Kauai including the project site
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The landmass of Kauai remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and
lower relative to the present sea level of the Island of Kauai
The project site is situated on ground underlain by hard basaltic rock lava flows
of the Koloa Volcanic Series In some places generally thin surficial soils consisting of
silty and clayey residual and alluvial materials may mantle the underlying rock
formation The basalt rock formation observed in the Koloa area appear to be
representative of the pahoehoe lava flow type which spread and ponded as it

horizontal and vertical extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is
transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and _ inter flow
contacts cavities and along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities
is high therefore they serve as the major water transmission features

3 1 Site Specific Geological Conditions
As discussed previously in the Site Characterization section the project site is
predominantly underlain by rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series Earlier mapping by
Macdonald Davis and Cox 1960 shows the site as being underlain by Koloa lavas
with eruptive vents situated at Manuhonuhono on the north central boundary of the site
and just to the east of Lawai Stream along the north western boundary of the site as
shown on the Regional Geologic Map Plate 3
Our review of available subsurface information indicates that the site specific
geology is somewhat more complex as shown on the Site Geologic Map Plate 4 It is
our opinion that there are relatively extensive pyroclastic deposits in the immediate
vicinity of the vents and associated lava flows to the south Our interpretation of the
data indicates that these pyroclastics and lavas are the older rocks underlying the site
These older lavas have developed a mantle of residual soils that generally range from
about 5 to 10 feet in thickness over the basalt formation
There are a series of Koloa vents across Waikomo Stream from the project site
which lie on a north south trend to the north of Makahuena Point The lavas from these
vents appear to be younger and spread to the west to bank against the older flows in
the western portion of the site These lavas have little or no soil mantle
Our geological model of the site also includes some areas of alluvial deposits
One of these areas is on the landward side of Kukuiula Bay and appears to be
associated with the contact between the older and younger Koloa lavas There is
another area of alluvial deposits in the northeastern portion of the site that most likely is

3 2 Groundwater Conditions
Groundwater in Hawaii generally occurs as a basal water table in conformance
with the Ghyben Herzberg Principle where due to density differences fresh water
floats on salt water in dynamic equilibrium forming a lens of fresh water With sufficient
recharge the lens of fresh water will build up so that the water table surface is above
sea level and the fresh water will displace salt water Thus for every foot of fresh water
above sea level there is about 40 feet of salt water below
Our review of available subsurface information indicates that there is little specific
information pertaining to groundwater occurrence at the site However deep bore holes
drilled as standby injection wells at the wastewater treatment plant encountered
relatively fresh water at about sea level elevation Injection testing of those wells
indicated that the volcanic rock formation underlying the site has relatively low
permeability on the order of about 10 feet per day
Therefore it is reasonable to assume that groundwater across the site generally
occurs near sea level However there may be locally high groundwater tables near
significant hydrological features such as reservoirs irrigation ditches or streams
Further given the humid climate in the region of the project there is a potential for
perched groundwater in the pyroclastic deposits and seepage along joints and
discontinuities in the basalt rock formation

3 3 General Soil Conditions
Based on the results of our exploration and data review the site is generally
underlain by competent soil and rock materials The residual soils encountered at the
project site generally have good engineering properties and are capable of supporting
moderate to high foundation and pavement loads when properly compacted These
soils generally have moderate to high in situ moisture contents which may require
aeration to improve their workability when used as fill material The residual soils
generally exhibit low expansion potential
The alluvial soils encountered at the site are generally more plastic have higher
expansion potential and higher moisture contents Therefore their engineering
properties are not as good as the residual soils Their use as fill material should be
limited to the deeper portions of fills below about 3 feet below structures Foundations
for structures situated in the alluvial soil areas may require special design
considerations such as overexcavation and replacement or the use of capping fills
The alluvial area adjacent to Kukui ula Bay may contain soft soil horizons and organic
soils
3 4 Geological Hazards
Our review of the available information indicates that there are only limited
potential for geological hazards associated with development of the project site There
has been no volcanic activity on the Island of Kauai for several thousand years and
there are no known active faults in the vicinity of the project site According to the
Uniform Building Code the Island of Kauai is designated as Seismic Zone 1 the lowest
in the State of Hawaii
No potential rockfall or slide areas have been identified within the site up to the
time of this exploration
The primary geological hazard that can be associated with the project site is the
potential presence of cavities or voids within the underlying volcanic rock formation
This hazard may readily be addressed during development of the site through cavity
detection programs such as heavy proof rolling or probing

a Flyrock
Flyrock is the blasters term for flying rock or other debris which is
launched into the air during open blasting by the force of the detonating
explosives These projectiles often have tremendous kinetic energy and
can crush or destroy objects in their flight path or at their point of impact
The occurrence of flyrock may be reduced and possibly eliminated by
increasing the confinement or the blast depth of the explosive charges
and or by using safety mats
6 Ground Vibration
With respect to the project ground vibration is probably the most critical
potential hazardous factor to be anticipated during blasting operations
When an explosive detonates below ground it generates intense stress
waves in the surrounding material These waves crush the rock
immediately adjacent to the explosion and permanently distorts and
cracks the rock for some distance
Beyond the area in which the intensity of the stress waves attenuate to
such a degree that there is no longer any permanent deformation of the
rock the stress is further propagated through the rock as elastic waves
These elastic waves create ground vibration
The potential of the ground vibration to damage a structure is a function of
the intensity of the vibration and the natural frequency of the structure
According to the DuPont Blasters Handbook a formula has been
developed to relate the intensity of ground vibration at a given point in
terms of particle displacement velocity and acceleration to the weight of
the explosive charge and the distance between the explosion and the
point of interest

The air blast from an explosion is a compression wave in the air Noise is

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano
built by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series beginning about
5 to 6 million years ago The eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series ended about
2 million years ago and was followed by a long period of erosion Following the
cessation of this main volcano shield building phase about 17 million years ago there
was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the post erosional
Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the thick alluvial sediments of
the Palikea Formation
Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as
thick lava flows composed of dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in
north south trends at various locales Associated with the Koloa Volcanic Series lava
flows are some deposits of pyroclastic materials volcanic ash and cinders which
usually form cinder cones surrounding the vent Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic
Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of Kauai including the project site
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The landmass of Kauai remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and
lower relative to the present sea level of the Island of Kauai
The project site is situated on ground underlain by hard basaltic rock lava flows
of the Koloa Volcanic Series In some places generally thin surficial soils consisting of
silty and clayey residual and alluvial materials may mantle the underlying rock
formation The basalt rock formation observed in the Koloa area appear to be
representative of the pahoehoe lava flow type which spread and ponded as it

approached the ocean from inland areas Pahoehoe lavas are typically characterized
by a smoother billowy surface and internal structure of vesicular porous rock with

rough and irregular flow surfaces resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments
including cobbles and boulders A a lavas typically contain thick sequences of clinker

Buried cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lava flows especially the
pahoehoe lavas Cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and
represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and localized blisters and pockets
inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains quickly from the
feeding conduit of a cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend
for large distances longitudinally along the flow Lava tubes may also consist of a
network of branching and merging segments that may change directional course over
relatively short distances Lava tubes may extend from far inland all the way to the
ocean since they serve as the primary lava conduit for advancing flows Blisters and
pockets are more isolated elliptical shaped features which are generally smaller in
horizontal and vertical extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is
transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and_ inter flow
contacts cavities and along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities
is high therefore they serve as the major water transmission features

3 2 Groundwater Conditions
Groundwater in Hawaii generally occurs as a basal water table in conformance
with the Ghyben Herzberg Principle where due to density differences fresh water
floats on salt water in dynamic equilibrium forming a lens of fresh water With sufficient
recharge the lens of fresh water will build up so that the water table surface is above
sea level and the fresh water will displace salt water Thus for every foot of fresh water
above sea level there is about 40 feet of salt water below
Our review of available subsurface information indicates that there is little specific
information pertaining to groundwater occurrence at the site However deep bore holes
drilled as standby injection wells at the wastewater treatment plant encountered
relatively fresh water at about sea level elevation Injection testing of those wells
indicated that the volcanic rock formation underlying the site has relatively low
permeability on the order of about 10 feet per day
Therefore it is reasonable to assume that groundwater across the site generally
occurs near sea level However there may be locally high groundwater tables near
significant hydrological features such as reservoirs irrigation ditches or streams
Further given the humid climate in the region of the project there is a potential for
perched groundwater in the pyroclastic deposits and seepage along joints and

a Flyrock
Flyrock is the blasters term for flying rock or other debris which is
launched into the air during open blasting by the force of the detonating
explosives These projectiles often have tremendous kinetic energy and
can crush or destroy objects in their flight path or at their point of impact
The occurrence of flyrock may be reduced and possibly eliminated by
increasing the confinement or the blast depth of the explosive charges
and or by using safety mats
6 Ground Vibration
With respect to the project ground vibration is probably the most critical
potential hazardous factor to be anticipated during blasting operations
When an explosive detonates below ground it generates intense stress
waves in the surrounding material These waves crush the rock
immediately adjacent to the explosion and permanently distorts and
cracks the rock for some distance
Beyond the area in which the intensity of the stress waves attenuate to
such a degree that there is no longer any permanent deformation of the
rock the stress is further propagated through the rock as elastic waves
These elastic waves create ground vibration
The potential of the ground vibration to damage a structure is a function of
the intensity of the vibration and the natural frequency of the structure
According to the DuPont Blaster s Handbook a formula has been
developed to relate the intensity of ground vibration at a given point in
terms of particle displacement velocity and acceleration to the weight of
the explosive charge and the distance between the explosion and the
point of interest

c Air Blast
The air blast from an explosion is a compression wave in the air Noise is
the portion of the air blast with frequencies between 20 and
concussion is the portion with frequencies below 20 Hz The DuPont
Blaster s Handbook also sets forth a scaled distance relationship for air
blast
The effects of the air blast which depends on atmospheric and or other
considerations can be a consideration for much greater distances than for
vibration
If blasting is to be used for rock excavation we recommend that the
scaled distances of the explosive charges be limited to those that will
generate peak particle velocities of 1 5 inches per second or less at
nearby structures Blasting should be performed in accordance with the

with a proven record We recommend the prequalification of contractors
and blasting operators bidding on the project
If blasting is to be utilized for rock excavation we recommend a program

Our geological model of the site also includes some areas of alluvial deposits with

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano
built by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series beginning about
5 to 6 million years ago The eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series ended about
2 million years ago and was followed by a long period of erosion Following the
cessation of this main volcano shield building phase about 17 million years ago there
was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the post erosional
Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the thick alluvial sediments of
the Palikea Formation
Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as
thick lava flows composed of dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in
north south trends at various locales Associated with the Koloa Volcanic Series lava
flows are some deposits of pyroclastic materials volcanic ash and cinders which
usually form cinder cones surrounding the vent Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic
Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of Kauai including the project site
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to
fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The landmass of Kauai remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and
lower relative to the present sea level of the Island of Kauai
The project site is situated on ground underlain by hard basaltic rock lava flows
of the Koloa Volcanic Series In some places generally thin surficial soils consisting of
silty and clayey residual and alluvial materials may mantle the underlying rock
formation The basalt rock formation observed in the Koloa area appear to be
representative of the pahoehoe lava flow type which spread and ponded as it

Buried cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lava flows especially the
pahoehoe lavas Cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and

inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains quickly from the
feeding conduit of a cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend

relatively short distances Lava tubes may extend from far inland all the way to the
ocean since they serve as the primary lava conduit for advancing flows Blisters and
pockets are more isolated elliptical shaped features which are generally smaller in
horizontal and vertical extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is
transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and_ inter flow
contacts cavities and along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities
is high therefore they serve as the major water transmission features

3 2 Groundwater Conditions
Groundwater in Hawaii generally occurs as a basal water table in conformance
with the Ghyben Herzberg Principle where due to density differences fresh water
floats on salt water in dynamic equilibrium forming a lens of fresh water With sufficient
recharge the lens of fresh water will build up so that the water table surface is above
sea level and the fresh water will displace salt water Thus for every foot of fresh water
above sea level there is about 40 feet of salt water below
Our review of available subsurface information indicates that there is little specific
information pertaining to groundwater occurrence at the site However deep bore holes
drilled as standby injection wells at the wastewater treatment plant encountered
relatively fresh water at about sea level elevation Injection testing of those wells
indicated that the volcanic rock formation underlying the site has relatively low
permeability on the order of about 10 feet per day
Therefore it is reasonable to assume that groundwater across the site generally
occurs near sea level However there may be locally high groundwater tables near
significant hydrological features such as reservoirs irrigation ditches or streams
Further given the humid climate in the region of the project there is a potential for
perched groundwater in the pyroclastic deposits and seepage along joints and
discontinuities in the basalt rock formation

6 Ground Vibration
With respect to the project ground vibration is probably the most critical
potential hazardous factor to be anticipated during blasting operations
When an explosive detonates below ground it generates intense stress
waves in the surrounding material These waves crush the rock
immediately adjacent to the explosion and permanently distorts and
cracks the rock for some distance
Beyond the area in which the intensity of the stress waves attenuate to
such a degree that there is no longer any permanent deformation of the
rock the stress is further propagated through the rock as elastic waves
These elastic waves create ground vibration
The potential of the ground vibration to damage a structure is a function of
the intensity of the vibration and the natural frequency of the structure
According to the DuPont Blaster s Handbook a formula has been
developed to relate the intensity of ground vibration at a given point in
terms of particle displacement velocity and acceleration to the weight of
the explosive charge and the distance between the explosion and the
point of interest

At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be

The over excavated subgrades finished subgrades and areas designated to

Fill Materials

sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883
