INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed North South Road project located in the District of Ewa on the
island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses for
the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of culverts
headwall structures site grading and sidewalk slab support only A pavement
justification report including pavement structural sections will be prepared for this project
by the State of Hawaii Department of Transportation The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two main
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau beginning in the mid Pliocene Epoch The
older volcano Waianae was the first to develop closely followed by the development
of Koolau Volcano For a period of time Oahu was actually comprised of two islands
formed by each of the volcanic centers The volcanoes continued to erupt and bank
against the slopes of each other with Waianae terminating its eruptive activity prior to
the cessation of Koolau
Following extrusion of the lavas in the early Pleistocene Epoch the island
underwent a long cycle of erosion and weathering forming the prominent ridgelines and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3860 00 Page 5
summits as we know today During the erosion period Oahu began to slowly subside
more than 1 200 feet in elevation resulting in the drowning and sedimentation of the
valleys and the formation of the steep Koolau Pali Coral reefs continued to grow in the
surrounding shallow waters of the island
From the mid to late Pleistocene Epoch the sea level repeatedly rose and fell in
response to global glaciation and the availability of surface waters to sustain the
oceans The various sea level elevations and their representative deposits are known
as stands and include from oldest to youngest the Kahuku Kahipa Kaena Laie
Waialae Waipio and Waimanalo stands Geologic deposits associated with the
various sea level stands including marine sediments and coral reefs were deposited
and subsequently altered or removed by later sea level fluctuation Therefore
depositional records reflecting the changes in sea level and the occurrence of emerged
coral reef deposits are often incomplete
The project site is situated on the Ewa Plain located to the southeast of the
Waianae mountains The Ewa Plain is a gently sloping alluvial plain formed by the
deposition of alluvial clays and silts derived from the weathering of basalt rock further
up slope The alluvial deposits accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments
and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to
over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the
confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu
Basalt rock formation resides below the marine deposits at a substantial depth
The majority of the project site is situated over the alluvial clay soils The coral
and marine deposits are believed to underlie the site but at a significant depth beneath
the alluvium The exception being the southern most portion of the project site where
coralline deposits may exist at or very near the existing ground surface as the alluvial
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3860 00 Page 6
wedge thins toward the southern shoreline Agricultural developments within the last
100 years have brought the area to its present form
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The proposed North South Road project will involve the construction of a new
divided four lane roadway with future widening to six lanes between Farrington Highway
and the proposed Kapolei Parkway in the District of Ewa on the island of Oahu Hawaii
as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The vacant area currently exists as previous
agricultural lands The area is heavily vegetated by wild grasses and sugar canes along
the proposed alignment At the time of our field exploration clearing of the vegetation
was in progress to facilitate the topographic surveying work and our field exploration The
agricultural area is enclosed with fences and locked gates Several paved and unpaved
access roads traverse the proposed alignment In addition power poles and electric
power lines have been installed along the proposed North South Road alignment
Based on field observation and the topographic map provided the area slopes
gently down at a gradient of approximately 0 5 to 1 percent toward the south The ground
surface elevations along the proposed North South Road alignment range from
approximately 16 to 48 meters Mean Sea Level MSL Several small drainage
courses and access roads were observed to traverse the proposed roadway alignment

Our field exploration generally encountered alluvium consisting of stiff silty clays
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 8 1 meters below the ground surface
In several of the borings located toward the southern end of the alignment the alluvium
was underlain by coralline materials consisting of medium dense to dense gravelly sands
and silty sands The coralline materials were encountered in Boring and
4 starting at depths of about 3 4 to 5 5 meters and extending to the maximum depth

groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration
Based on the preliminary grading plans provided the proposed excavations for
this project will generally extend up to about 3 meters below the existing ground surface

Therefore the excavations will likely expose only the clayey alluvium encountered in our
borings The soils generated from the planned excavations may generally be re used as
a source of general fill and backfill material for construction of the embankments provided
that they are properly moisture conditioned and compacted as recommended herein
However it should be noted that the near surface soils approximately 200 to
250 millimeters mm deep consist of organically laden materials which should not be

Based on our laboratory test results the remolded subgrade soils generally exhibit
a slight to moderate shrink swell potential when subjected to changes in the moisture
content Therefore improvements supported on grade such as concrete sidewalks and

materials to reduce the potential shrink swell effects on the concrete slabs supported
on grade In addition special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation
recommendations provided in this report

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site or in a
designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated
materials Based on our observations the upper 200 to 250 mm of the surface
soils appear to be contaminated with organic matter especially in areas previously
used for the cultivation of sugar canes The organically laden soils should not be
re used as fill material for the embankments and support of the pavement The
organically laden material should be classified as unsuitable and should be
removed from the limits of grading
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below any areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural material
and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered
fill In areas to receive fill the subgrade should be scarified to a minimum depth of
200 mm moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T 180 ASTM
D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density

Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the subgrade soil be re scarified moisture conditioned and

Fills and Backfills
In general we believe that the excavated on site soils may be re used as a source
of general fill material for the construction of embankments at the site provided that
the materials are moisture conditioned and compacted as recommended herein It
should be emphasized that the near surface organically laden soils as previously
indicated are not suitable for use as fill materials
General fill and backfill up to 1 meter below the finish grade of the pavement areas
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in
level lifts not exceeding 200 mm loose thickness and compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction Fill materials within 1 meter of the finish grade
imported fill materials aggregate subbase and aggregate base materials should
be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not
exceeding 200 mm loose thickness and compacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with AASHTO T 180 ASTM D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction
should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers multiple wheel
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3860 00 Page 11
pneumatic tired rollers or other similar types of acceptable compaction equipment
Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed to compact the fill
If imported material is required for the construction of embankments the material
should consist of non expansive granular material such as crushed coral
mudrock or cinder sand Imported material should be well graded from coarse to
fine with no particles larger than 75 mm in largest dimension and should contain
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve less than
The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and
should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less Imported materials should be
tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project
site for its intended use
Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the preliminary grading plans it appears that most of the proposed cut
slopes would expose the stiff alluvium encountered in the borings In general we
believe that cut slopes composed of the in situ alluvium may be designed with a
slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter It should be
noted that the clayey alluvium encountered appears to be highly susceptible to
erosion Therefore we recommend that cut slopes be protected by appropriate
slope planting or other means to reduce the potential for erosion of the exposed
soils as soon as possible after the slope faces are exposed
Permanent fill slopes constructed using the excavated on site soils or imported
materials may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed
on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing
slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should
start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in
accordance with the above fill placement recommendations Fill slopes should be
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3860 00 Page 12
constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a
well compacted slope face In the event that over cutting of a slope occurs keying
and benching requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope
to the design grade with sliver fill In addition slope planting should be provided as
soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes A
typical keying and benching detail is provided on Plate 3
Subdrainage
Seepage conditions may be present at localized areas along the proposed
roadway alignment especially where the existing drainage courses are to be
diverted and filled in Therefore we recommend that subdrains be provided in
these filled in areas to reduce the potential for saturating the embankment fills
which may lead to settlement of the embankment
In general subdrains should consist of 100 mm diameter perforated pipes with
perforations facing down All subdrains should be surrounded and underlain by at
least 100 mm of drainage material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation or equivalent The No 3B Fine gravel is also equivalent to the
Size 67 coarse aggregate in Section 703 2 of the Standard Specifications
Table 703 Il A non woven filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent should
wrap around the drainage material In general subdrain trenches should be at
least 300 mm wide 600 mm deep and should be capped with engineered fill A
typical section of the subdrain is provided on Plate 4

drainage purposes at some of the crossings for the proposed roadway project In
general we anticipate that shallow foundations bearing on the stiff alluvium
encountered at the project site may be utilized for support of the planned reinforced
concrete box culverts and headwall structures A bearing pressure of up to

A 200 mm gravel cushion layer should be provided between the box culvert and

upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement

Pipe Culverts and Other Utility Lines

the near surface soils encountered in the borings In general a granular bedding

recommended under the pipes If soft and or loose soils are encountered at the
proposed pipe inverts an additional 500 mm of No 3B Fine gravel wrapped in a
non woven filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be provided below the
bedding layer for more uniform support
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel should also be used

possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical that free draining materials be used around
the pipes to reduce the potential for the formation of voids below the haunches of pipes
and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes which could result in
settlement of the backfill

a minimum of 50 mm into the subgrade soil to reduce migration of landscape water into
the pavement section unless the pavement structure is properly drained by subdrains A
subdrain system could be constructed to collect excessive water from landscaping
irrigation For long term performance we recommend that a subdrain system be
constructed adjacent to paved landscaped areas
Sidewalk Slab On Grade
Concrete sidewalks 2 5 meters wide are anticipated on both sides of the
proposed roadway Due to the slightly expansive nature of the near surface soils we
recommend that the sidewalks be supported on a minimum 300 mm thick layer of
aggregate subbase material such as select borrow subbase compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction The subgrade soils beneath the sidewalks should be
scarified to a depth of about 200 mm moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
Construction joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the sidewalks with
expansion joints at right angle intersections Areas adjacent to the slabs should be
backfilled tightly against the slab as soon as possible and graded to divert water away
from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding
Corrosion Potential
Corrosivity tests consisting of pH minimum resistivity chloride and sulfate
tests were performed on the clayey alluvium at the site Results of the corrosivity tests
are presented on Plate B 17 of Appendix B Based on the minimum resistivity value of
2 300 ohm cm which corresponds to Corrosion Rating No 2 according to the
guidelines provided by the City County of Honolulu Board of Water Supply the
near surface soils may be classified as corrosive Design of metallic substructures
such as water lines or other metallic piping should consider the effects of the corrosive
environment on the substructure

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
14 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 14 extending to depths of about 6 1 to
8 1 meters below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 14
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
California sampler with a 63 5 kilogram hammer falling 760 mm The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 150 mm total of 300 mm of a 450 mm
drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field to provide an indication of the consistency of the materials sampled
The pocket penetrometer test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results
of these tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics Results of the direct shear test are
presented on Plate B 3
One consolidation test ASTM D 2435 was performed on a remolded sample of
the soils to evaluate compressibility characteristics of the remolded soil sample under
surcharge loads Results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate B 4
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils Results of these tests are graphically presented on
Plates B 5 and B 6
Six laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were
performed on the near surface soils to evaluate the materials for use as fill and pavement
support characteristics of the soil The six CBR tests were performed by molding the
samples in five lifts with each lift subjected to 56 blows using a 4 54 kilogram hammer
dropping 460 mm The samples were soaked in water for at least 3 days to evaluate their
swelling potential Results of these tests are summarized on Plate B 1 Graphic
presentations of the test results are provided on Plates B 7 through B 12
Two Standard Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 698 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils Results of the tests are summarized on Plate B 1 and
are graphically presented on Plates B 13 and B 14
Two CBR tests were performed by molding the samples in five lifts with each lift
subjected to 10 blows per lift using a 4 54 kilogram hammer dropping 460 mm The
samples were soaked for 6 days after the completion of the test Results of the tests are
summarized on Plate B 1 and graphic presentations of the test results are provided on
Plates B 15 and B 16

ASTM G 57 chlorides and sulfates were performed on
selected samples to evaluate the corrosivity of the materials Results of the corrosion
tests are presented on Plate B 17
