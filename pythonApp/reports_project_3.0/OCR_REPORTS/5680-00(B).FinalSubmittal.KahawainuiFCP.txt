The following sections of this geotechnical analysis report presents the basis of the
geotechnical engineering evaluation conducted on the I Wall type flood walls at the Kahawainui
Flood Control Project FCP located in Laie on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The geotechnical
engineering evaluation conducted was performed in accordance with the Phase II Interim
Guidance for Evaluating Existing I Walls prepared by the U S Army Corps of Engineers
USACE dated

Existing I Wall Loading Conditions
The water level in Laiewai Stream adjacent to the I Wall location was observed at
about 0 to 1 foot MSL at the time of our site reconnaissance conducted on
The highest water mark stains observed on the levee slope face were at about 3 to
4 feet MSL Storm surge and wave loading information were not available The
construction documents for the Kahawainui Flood Control Project indicated the design of
the I Wall section had a minimum freeboard of 3 feet measured from the top of the I Wall
Therefore we believe that the design water level for the I Walls is at about 15 feet MSL
about 3 feet below top of I Wall of 18 feet MSL
4
I Wall Site Classification
Considering the relatively complete construction documents available for our review
and the fairly well defined subsurface soil conditions and the physical soil properties the
project site may be classified as an Ordinary Site because the information contains
limited loading history Therefore the minimum factors of safety for the Kahawainui FCP
are based on the Ordinary Site classification
5
Depth of Crack
Based on the Phase II Interim Guidance for Evaluating Existing I Walls prepared
by the USACE dated it is unlikely that a crack will develop in the
saturated granular soil conditions present at the Kahawainui FCP site Because of the
sandy soil conditions present at the site a crack was not assumed in the idealized soil
profile used for our geotechnical analyses
6

Seepage Piping and Uplift
The evaluation for seepage is in general accordance with the applicable portions of
Design Guidance of Levees Design Guidance
for Levee Underseepage May 01 2005 and EM Seepage Analysis
and Control for Dams April 1993
The seepage analyses were performed using the computer program Phase2
version 6 from RocScience Inc The model for the seepage analyses was
established using the finite element method to simulate water seepage in a
saturated steady state flow condition under higher water levels on the levee side

Based on the seepage analyses the seepage flow rate was computed at top of the
ground on the landside of the I Wall with respect to total hydrostatic head present at
or near the tip of the I Wall Furthermore the upward gradient was calculated and
compared with the critical gradient which is defined as the numerical difference
between the saturated soil unit weight and unit weight of water
Therefore the seepage conditions were assessed based on the factor of safety of
gradient and the upward gradient in accordance with 555 Design
Guidance of Levees November 1997 and 569 Design Guidance for
Levee Underseepage May 01 2005
The results of our seepage analyses are summarized on Plate 5 for ease of
reference Details of the seepage analyses including the seepage flow nets and
hydrostatic head distributions are presented on Plates A 1 through A 20 in
Appendix A
Because sandy soil relatively free draining subsurface conditions with no
undrained soil blanket layer exists on the landside of the I Wall the potential for
heave is not likely and was considered not applicable in this idealized soil profile
for the Kahawainui FCP
Based on the seepage analyses medium to heavy seepage conditions with a
gradient factor of safety of less than the required 3 0 for the Ordinary Site
classification may occur when the water level on the levee side reaches 14 feet
MSL or higher The 14 feet MSL water level corresponds to about 4 feet below
the top of I Wall

Global Slope Stability
Global slope stability of the existing levee combined with the I Wall was evaluated
using the computer program ReSSA from ADAMA Engineering Inc The global
slope stability analyses take into account the additional water pressure applied on
the levee side to evaluate the rotational slope failure mechanism
Results of the global slope stability analyses are summarized on Plate 5 Details of
the critical slip failure circles are presented in Appendix C
Based on the results of the global slope stability analyses the existing levee
combined with the I Wall has a minimum factor of safety of 2 3 which is greater
than the required factor of safety of 1 6 for the Ordinary Site classification for
global slope stability
6 4
Wall Height and Cantilever Height Verification
Based on the sandy granular soil subsurface conditions at the site the potential for
appreciable settlement of the existing levee is relatively low There were no
apparent signs of appreciable levee embankment settlement based on our site
reconnaissance in April 2007 Please refer to the photographs of the levee and
I Wall in Appendix D for reference

Conclusions
Due to insufficient I Wall tip penetration especially for the portion of the I Wall with
tip elevation at 3 feet MSL excessive seepage and rotational failure conditions may
develop in the I Wall in the event water levels reach 13 to 14 feet MSL which is about 4
to 5 feet below the top of the I Wall We believe that these water level conditions 13 to
14 feet MSL are approximately 1 to 2 feet below the design water level approximately
15 feet MSL
The ground elevation on the landside of the I Wall was observed to be about
6 inches lower than the original ground elevation It is not clear how this might have
occurred considering that the area immediately adjacent to the landside of the I Wall is an
existing cemetery If the ground elevation on the landside continues to be lowered there
exist the potential for undesirable conditions such as excessive water seepage or I Wall
rotational failure to develop in the event the water levels on the levee side rises to the top
of the levee 11 feet MSL or higher
8
Limitations of Analyses
The analyses and conclusions submitted herein are based in part upon
information obtained from the subsurface conditions shown in the construction documents
as listed in the references Variations of the subsurface conditions between and beyond
the field borings may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become
evident until further investigation is underway If variations then appear evident it will be
necessary to re evaluate the analyses and conclusions provided in this report
