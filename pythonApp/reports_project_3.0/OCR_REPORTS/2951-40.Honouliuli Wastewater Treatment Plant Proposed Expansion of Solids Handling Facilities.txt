INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Expansion of Solids Handling Facilities within the existing
Honouliuli Wastewater Treatment Plant WWTP in the District of Ewa on the island of
Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses The recommendations are intended for the design of foundations site grading
and pavements The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The project site is located on the Ewa Coastal Plain on the southern coast of the
island of Oahu This coastal plain is composed of a thick wedge of interbedded marine
deposits and terrestrial sediments which have accumulated on the southern flanks of
Waianae and Koolau the two extinct volcanoes that built the island
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
growth and retreat of the great continental glaciers As the glaciers advanced less water
was available to fill the ocean basins and sea levels fell below the current sea level As
the glaciers melted and retreated more water became available and sea level rose to and
often above the current level When the stand of the sea was high and prolonged marine
deposits accumulated at elevations above the current sea level
At the project site the near surface materials are primarily coral algal deposits
which are composed of medium hard limestone with an open lattice like structure It is
believed that these coral deposits were laid during the Kaena 95 feet Mean Sea Level
MSL stand of the sea and subsequently planed to near their current elevation during
the Waimanalo 25 feet MSL stand The coral formation has also been subjected to
subaerial weathering
Coral algal materials are chemically composed of calcium carbonate During
subaerial weathering slightly acidic rain and groundwater can slowly attack the
carbonate dissolving the weaker portions of the formation This creates solution cavities
ranging from small pores to large caverns

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated within the existing Honouliuli Wastewater Treatment
Plant in the District of Ewa on the island of Oahu Hawaii It is approximately bounded by
Hanson Road to the north Geiger Road to the east a golf course to the south and an
abandoned airfield to the west
Based on our field exploration and a site plan provided by GMP Associates Inc
the existing primary treatment facilities in the plant are mostly located in the central and
eastern portions of the plant site The existing facilities include primary clarifier tanks
pre aeration tanks pump stations small buildings and other minor structures
Construction for the secondary treatment facilities are underway at the northwestern
portions of the plant site at the time of our field exploration A complex network of
underground utilities inter connects the existing facilities We anticipate some of these
existing utilities may traverse the locations of the proposed structures
Most of the proposed structures are concentrated at the southeastern portions of
the plant site The topography at the southeastern portion of the plant site is relatively
level with existing ground surface elevations ranging between 34 and 38 feet MSL

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions at the project site consist
generally of relatively competent coralline materials throughout the depths explored The
major geotechnical considerations for the expansion project are the quality and uniformity
of the coral formation at the locations of the proposed structures
We believe that the dense coral formation and coral detritus deposits encountered
in our borings would provide adequate foundation support for the proposed new
construction In general it is our opinion that continuous ring foundations with slabs on
grade bearing on the dense in situ coralline materials may be used to support the
proposed Gravity Thickener and Decant Tank structures For the pump rooms located
adjacent to these below grade tanks and other on grade structures such as the Thermal
Conditioning Building and Blend Tanks we recommend that spread and or continuous
footings be used for foundation support

Other Design Considerations
Wall footings should have a minimum width of at least 18 inches Footings should
be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade For
footings constructed on sloping ground the footings should be embedded deep enough
to provide a minimum horizontal set back distance of 8 feet measured from the outside
edge of the footing to the slope face Where footings will be located adjacent to below
grade structures or utilities the footings should extend to a depth below an imaginary
45 degree plane projected upward from the bottom of the below grade structure or utility
line trench This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for
footings bearing on the dense in situ coralline materials as recommended herein to be
less than inch total with differential settlements of about inch
In general lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by a combination
of frictional resistance between the base of the footing and the bearing materials and by
passive earth pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded
portion of footings A passive earth pressure with an equivalent fluid pressure of 350
pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f may be used in preliminary design
Unless covered by slabs or pavements passive resistance from the upper 12 inches
should be neglected A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on
the dense in situ coralline materials

Foundation Probing
Based on our previous experience in the area voids and or in filled cavities may
be present in the coralline deposits encountered at the project site To reduce the
potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities below a footing
we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be conducted during
construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each spread
footing location and 15 foot on centers for continuous footings and mat foundations The
probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet
below the planned bottom of footing If cavities and or voids are encountered or
suspected during the probing operation additional probes should be drilled at closer
spacings to help delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The
probe holes and any cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout
injected at low to moderate pressures A typical sand cement grout would consist of
1 part sand 1 part cement and 0 7 part water
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and
grouting operation is complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer
lag time between probing grouting operations and foundation construction may be
required in the construction schedule

Slabs On Grade
Slabs on grade for the proposed project may consist of concrete floor slabs and
exterior flatwork such as the Sludge Reactor Pad Based on our field exploration results
we anticipated that the majority of the slabs on grade will bear on dense in situ coralline
materials encountered at shallow depths However if potentially expansive subgrade
soils or loose soils are encountered at the subgrade level these soils should be over
excavated and replaced with select granular fill materials compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
For more uniform support slabs on grade should be supported on a cushion layer
consisting of 4 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation The gravel
cushion layer would also serve as a capillary break To reduce moisture infiltration
through the slab a plastic vapor membrane may be considered below the slab A 2 inch
thick moist fine sand layer should be placed on the plastic vapor membrane to aid in the
curing of concrete The slab subgrades should be prepared in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Site Grading section of this report
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic including forklifts we
recommend that the slabs be supported on a 6 inch layer of base course in lieu of the
4 inch gravel cushion layer In addition the 2 inch moist fine sand and vapor barrier may

be omitted for these slabs The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate
compacted to 95 percent relative compaction For the design of structural slabs a

Site Gradin
Besides the excavations required for the proposed Gravity Thickener and Decant
Tank structures minor site grading is anticipated for the project construction The
following subsections provide general guidelines for design of site grading and earthwork
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All existing vegetation rubbish old pavements
construction rubble and other deleterious and organic materials should be
removed and disposed of properly off site For the purposes of this discussion
subgrade level should be considered the bottom of the base course for pavement
sections bottom of the cushion for slabs and bottom of foundations
After clearing and grubbing areas at grade or are designated to receive fill should
be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches and recompacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as
determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density New
pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches and
recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
In areas where the hard coral formation is exposed the subgrades should be
proof rolied with a minimum 20 ton vibratory roller for a minimum 6 passes to help
detect and collapse near surface cavities Any loose areas or cavities disclosed
during clearing and proof rolling operations should be backfilled with well
compacted engineered fill materials
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2951 40 Page 18
Any expansive clayey soil encountered at or near subgrade levels should be over
excavated and the resulting over excavation should be backfilled with compacted
non expansive granular material Expansive clays encountered and over
excavated should be disposed of properly off site to reduce the potential for
contamination of other areas
Fills and Backfills
Based on our field exploration the excavated on site granular soils excluding
clays and organic materials may be re used as a source of fill material Some of
the on site clayey soils are moderately expansive particularly if compacted dry of
the optimum moisture content Therefore excavated on site clayey soils should
not be used as fills However the excavated clayey material may be used in
landscaping areas if appropriate and the surplus should be disposed of properly
off site
Fill material should be placed in level lifts up to a maximum of 10 inches in loose lift
thickness Fill and backfills should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction If required imported materials should consist of non
expansive select granular materials such as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder
sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles
greater than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a California
Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of not more than
1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It
should also contain between 10 and 30 percent passing the No 200 sieve Fill
materials should be tested and approved by Geolabs Hawaii prior to hauling to the

Site Drainage
Measures should be incorporated into the grading of the expansion project to
reduce the potential for erosion and to prevent concentration of water around
structures Proper drainage structures should be used where needed The
ground surface surrounding structures should be sloped away from structures
Surface water should be directed away from the slopes and drainage over the top
of any slope should be avoided
Excavation
Based on the current design concept excavations on the order of up to 10 feet
below the existing ground surface will be required for the project construction
Excavation Method
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the
contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations Generally the
in situ coral formation encountered by our exploration should be rippable by a
large bulldozer or other heavy equipment However due to the nature of coral
formation some hard excavation such as using hoerams and or chipping may be
required in localized areas Therefore allowances should be made for the
additional time and expense that may be required by the hard excavation
The excavated soils should be stockpiled at least 15 feet away from any
underground utilities to reduce the potential of ground movement or subsidence
which may damage the underlying utility lines The excavated soils should also be
stockpiled no closer than a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the excavation
from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for ground movement

Excavation Support
Based on the existing topography at the project site and the anticipated design
bottom elevations of the future structures it appears that excavations on the order
of about 10 feet below the existing ground surface will be required for the project
construction Our field exploration indicates that the site is generally underlain by
competent coral formation and coral detritus deposits Therefore it is our opinion
that excavations in competent coral formation may be able to stand unsupported at
relatively steep angles for temporary construction purposes
It should be noted that the quality of the coral formation and coral detritus deposits
can be highly variable Therefore where an open cut excavation is not possible
because of physical limitations or the presence of poor quality coral formation
temporary shoring of the excavation may be required to reduce the potential of
adjacent ground movement The excavation support and shoring system used
must comply with all applicable safety requirements
Since relatively hard coral formation was encountered at the project site the use of
sheet pile shoring does not appear to be feasible It is our opinion that a support
system consisting of soldier piles and lagging with temporary tiebacks or rakers
may be adopted to provide support of the excavations The use of horizontal
bracing or struts inside the excavation does not appear practical because of the
anticipated wide excavation For preliminary design of the excavation support
system the apparent lateral earth pressure diagram presented on Plate 3 may be
used as a guide We recommend that provisions for temporary shoring of the
planned excavations be incorporated into the contract documents of this project

Construction Precaution and Monitoring
The selected excavation shoring system if needed must comply with all
applicable federal state and local safety requirements The adequacy and safety
of the shoring installation should be made the sole responsibility of the contractor
His representative who should be required to be continuously present on site
during excavation and construction work will have the best opportunity to promptly
observe any changing conditions during construction such as unforeseen
subsoils unexpectedly high groundwater table inappropriate construction
sequence or techniques etc which may affect shoring stability
However it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and
the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during
excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to accurately
estimate the magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon
workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports
are installed on the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of
excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical if necessary and that the adjacent ground
should be continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of
movements with instruments Such monitoring is particularly critical in areas
adjacent to existing plant facilities and utilities It is recommend that a qualified
geotechnical engineer be retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the
shoring system proposed

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings to depths ranging from about 20 4 to 33 5 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were
drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment
The material encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
