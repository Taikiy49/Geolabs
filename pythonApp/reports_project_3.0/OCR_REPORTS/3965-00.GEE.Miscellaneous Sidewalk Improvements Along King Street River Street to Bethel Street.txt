PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Based on the information provided we understand that it is desired to evaluate the
subsurface conditions for the design of improvements to the existing sidewalks along
portions of King Street between River Street and Bethel Street in the downtown Honolulu
area on the island of Oahu Hawaii The proposed improvements include the widening of
existing sidewalks by 4 feet on both sides of King Street between River Street and
Kekaulike Street and the widening of the Mauka sidewalk by 4 feet between Kekaulike
and Bethel Streets New concrete curbs and gutters will also be provided
As a result of the sidewalk widening the existing concrete bus lane between
River Street and Kekaulike Street will need to be reconstructed The existing pavement
will also need to be reconstructed to match the curbs and gutters Design of new
pavements will utilize a design life of 20 years
Salvaging the existing pavement section and providing rehabilitation measures
were initially considered However since the finish elevation of the roadway will likely be
at or below the existing grade it was determined that measures such as cold planing and
overlay placement would not be possible

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older voicano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
_ was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by
thinly bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased
approximately 2 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the island of
Oahu has subsided as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic
activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global
sea levels rose The land mass of Oahu remained essentially stable during these
changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3965 00 Page 5
resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to the present
sea level of Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of
upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high
energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments During these stable periods the growth of fringing coral algal reefs formed
distinct ledges of reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea
during that period About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred
During that rise the valleys in the project area were submerged In the last 10 000 years
or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments
have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting
in thick deposits of soft ioose sediments along the coast in areas that were formerly
valleys and drainageways
The geologic history of the project area has been further complicated by the
deposition of recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of the vents of
members of the Honolulu Volcanic Series including Aliamanu Salt Lake Makalapa
Kamanaiki Punchbowl and other minor vents These
post erosional i e following the cessation of shield building events were
contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations

Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature due to the interaction of
groundwater with the rising magma resulting in the expulsion of steam under extremely
high pressure together with large quantities of pyroclastic material predominantly ash
and cinder These deposits of pyrociastics have consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally
referred to as mudrock The eruptions of the vents
blanketed much of what is now the metropolitan Honolulu area with a fine black cinder
locally known as black sand Man made fills have since been placed to attain the
existing ground elevation
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
A site reconnaissance was undertaken to collect general information pertaining to
the existing site conditions including existing pavement characteristics road grades and
drainage in the vicinity of the subject project
The project limits extend along King Street between its intersections with River
and Beretania Streets in the downtown Chinatown area of Honolulu In this area King
Street is a four lane one directional thoroughfare in the Diamond Head
bound southeasterly direction During off peak hours the far left Mauka lane is used as
a loading zone or for parking
Within the project limits King Street appears generally level with gradual
changes in elevation between about 6 5 and 19 feet Mean Sea Level MSL Except
at intersections the far right lane is paved with Portland Cement concrete and the
remaining three lanes are paved with asphalt concrete The Makai side of King
Street generally has either concrete or older lava rock curbs and concrete gutters The
Mauka side at one time probably had both curbs and gutters however the gutters have
since been covered by asphalt concrete overlays The curbs on the Mauka side are
also constructed of either lava rocks or concrete
GEOLABS HAWAII

In general the surface of the existing pavement appears to be in fair condition
However in some areas cracks appear to be reflecting through the recent overlays In
addition localized areas of distress consisting of moderate to high severity fatigue
cracking potholes and patching due to both trenching and pavement repair low
severity raveling and delamination were observed Faulting and raveling spalling of
both the asphalt concrete and the Portland cement concrete at its interface with each
other were also visible at the roadway surface
As previously indicated the Mauka gutters have been covered with asphalt
concrete overlays In many areas cracks at the interface of the original roadway
pavement and the gutter have reflected through the overlay
In general the existing bus lane is in fair condition Some slabs were observed
to have low to moderate severity transverse cracks faulting and or spalling at joints
The adjacent makai curbs and gutters appear to be in generally fair to good condition
with localized deterioration consisting of cracking and settlement The sidewalks
located on each side of the roadway are generally in fair to good condition
Based on our visual observation of the asphalt concrete pavements and an
analysis of the existing pavement the present structural section appears to be
inadequate for the current traffic conditions

Design Subgrade Conditions
Our field exploration generally encountered relatively consistent subsoil
conditions consisting of brown silty sand fill material within the project limits The silty
sand was generally in a dense to very dense condition and generally extended to the
maximum depths drilled One boring located between Maunakea Street and Smith
Street encountered a void at a depth of about 15 inches Since the pavement in the
area was not observed to be severely distressed the void is believed to be an isolated
condition
Based on the types of materials encountered the penetration resistances
obtained and our experience in similar materials a Resistance R Value of 30 was
selected for the subgrade soils

In order to reduce the potential for disturbance to vehicular traffic asphalt
concrete base ACB is generally used as the base material to facilitate the
reconstruction work to be performed in a timely manner Calculations are presented on
Plate C 2 in Appendix C of this report
Prior to construction of new pavements the existing asphalt concrete pavement
and all other unsuitable material should be removed and properly disposed of off site
The exposed subgrade materials should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and recompacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 or AASHTO T 180 test procedures
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density If pumping conditions are encountered during compaction of the
subgrade soils the subgrade soils may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the
potential for pumping subgrade conditions As a guide one sack of cement may be used
for 50 square feet of subgrade area
The asphalt concrete base material should consist of asphalt treated basalt
aggregate placed in 2 or more layers of approximately equal thickness and compacted to
a density of greater than 91 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity
determined in accordance with ASTM D 2041 or AASHTO T 209 The maximum
compacted thickness of each layer should not exceed 6 inches
Bus Lanes
The new bus lane may be designed with the City s standard 9 inch thick concrete
slab Six inches of compacted base course should be placed below the concrete slab to
provide a uniform bearing surface for the concrete slab

New concrete sidewalks should be underlain by 6 inches of select borrow
subbase The subbase should be moisture conditioned and compacted to a minimum

subgrade materials should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the subbase and base course and or subgrade soils Therefore the
pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water
ponding should not be allowed anywhere during or after construction Development of
good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or
premature failure of the pavements
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement
weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of soft areas and replacement
with well compacted engineered fill at no additional cost to the Owner

Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio Test was performed on a bulk sample of
the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils The
sample was remolded near its optimum moisture content Dynamic compaction of the
sample was performed by dropping a 10 pound hammer from a height of 18 inches The
sample was compacted in five equal layers with each layer receiving 10 blows The
sample was soaked for 4 days prior to the penetration test Results of the test are
presented on Plate B 1
