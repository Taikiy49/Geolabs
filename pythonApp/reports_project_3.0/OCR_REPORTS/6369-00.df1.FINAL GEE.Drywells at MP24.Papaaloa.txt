SECTION 1 GENERAL
1 1
Introduction
This report presents the results of our analyses performed for the design of new
storm water runoff disposal wells for the Drywells at Milepost 24 Hawaii Belt Road
Route 19 project located at Papaaloa in the District of Hamakua on the Island of
Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the design of drywells
only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report
1 2
Project Considerations
The project site is at Milepost 24 of the Hawaii State Route 19 Hawaii Belt Road in
the Papaaloa area on the Island of Hawaii The existing Hawaii Belt Road in the project
vicinity is a two lane undivided roadway About 300 feet northwest from the intersection
with Papaaloa Avenue there is an existing 36 inch pipe culvert that crosses Route 19
and drains to the north into a rectangular concrete lined ditch
Based on the preliminary information provided we understand that there have
been flooding issues associated with this culvert crossing and that it is desired to
install one or more new drywells at the site to relieve the excess flows at this location
In support of the design of the new well or wells we have conducted a
geotechnical engineering exploration that involved the drilling and sampling of five test
borings to depths of about 40 feet below the existing ground surface Permeability tests
were conducted in each of the borings at depths of about 25 and 40 feet below the
existing ground surface

Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers
an area of approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the eruptive
activity of five major shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea some activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and Mauna Loa
and Kilauea both active
The project site is on the northeastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield Volcano
which had been built up by the successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials The most recent lava flows that comprise the ground surface and
shallow subsurface at the project site are believed to be Pliocene to Pleistocene in age
approximately 12 million years to 2 million years before present time and belong to the
upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series These lava flows were capped by
Pahala ash deposits
The basalt formations of the project area typically are layered with thin flows of
dense basalts and scoria basalts Clinker layers also are found commonly interbedded
between the pahoehoe lava flows In situ weathering of the basalt formation has yielded
layers of soft highly weathered basalts interbedded with more competent hard basalts
Our borings drilled for this exploration generally encountered saprolite i e weathered
basalt that retains its parent structure but mechanically reduces to a soil material Drive
sampling reduces the material to a silty sand with much gravel and some cobbles
representing relatively unweathered corestones in the saprolite matrix Coring shreds
the soil like matrix so that recovery is limited to the cobble sized corestones
Cavities or voids are encountered commonly in pahoehoe lava Cavities form
when the lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities
and inter flow cavities blisters and pockets Lava tubes form when molten lava drains
from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large
distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in
horizontal extent However we did not encounter cavities in our drilled borings

Site Description
The project site is located along the portion of Hawaii Belt Road Route 19 at the
Honokaa side of its intersection with Papaaloa Avenue in the District of Hamakua on the
Island of Hawaii In this area Route 19 is a two lane undivided highway surfaced with
asphaltic concrete Overhead electric power lines parallel the Hilo bound lane of the
highway and there is an existing underground waterline along this side
Based on the available plans the existing ground surface elevations along the
highway range from about 291 to 294 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the roadway
level sloping up towards Papaaloa Avenue

Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling five borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 extending to depths ranging from about 41 5 to
42 6 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring locations are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings generally encountered a surface pavement section consisting of
4 inches of asphaltic concrete overlying base course and subbase course to a depth of
about 3 feet below the pavement surface Under the pavement section our borings
encountered a residual soil horizon consisting of stiff to very stiff brown sandy silt with
gravel At depths ranging between about 8 and 20 feet this residual soil graded to
saprolitic material consisting of dense to medium dense brown sandy gravel and
cobbles Saprolite is a form of highly weathered rock that retains the parent structure of
the original rock but can be mechanically reduced to a soil The saprolitic material
encountered at the site retains the relict structure of the parent basaltic lava flows
including remnant vesicles
Between the depths of about 20 and 30 feet below the pavement surface the
saprolite graded to highly weathered basalt

3 1
Percolation Test Methodology
Two types of percolation tests were performed in the borings drilled at the project
site Constant Head and Falling Head Constant head tests were conducted to assess
the average permeability within an open interval of soil rock material Falling head tests
were conducted at the end of the constant head test to ensure that the water was
infiltrating through the tested interval and not into one or more discrete subintervals
within the test depth i e a clinker horizon or cavity
In general the borings were drilled using PQ wireline coring tools advanced to
the scheduled test depths Upon reaching the nominal test depth the wireline rods
were pulled back approximately 10 feet for the test Typically the holes tended to cave
in somewhat filling the bottom 2 or 3 feet of the boring Due to the predominately
soil like nature of the shallow subsurface materials some of the tests at the nominal
25 foot depth were conducted in open auger holes
The constant head tests were conducted using either the pump mounted on the
drill rig which provided approximately 60 gallons per minute gpm or the water truck
pump which was capable of providing 150 gpm or more The tests were conducted for
a minimum of 30 minutes or until the water truck was drained depending on the time
constraints imposed by the terms of the roadwork permit i e the need to vacate the
road by
The falling head tests were conducted after the end of pumping in the constant
head tests by measuring the fall in the water level in the boring against time The falling
head tests were run for either about 30 minutes until the hole was drained or until the
water level stabilized

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
five borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 advanced to depths of about 41 5
to 42 6 feet below the existing ground surface using a truck mounted drill rig equipped
with auger and coring equipment The approximate boring locations are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A 0 1 Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the
bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description Rock cores were
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the
Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2 Photographs of the cores recovered are shown in
Appendix D
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

Field Percolation Tests
Two types of percolation tests were performed in the borings drilled at the project
site Constant Head and Falling Head Constant head tests were conducted to assess
the average permeability within an open interval of soil rock material Filter Point
Method Falling head tests were conducted at the end of the constant head test to
ensure that the water was infiltrating through the tested interval and not into one or
more discrete subintervals within the test depth i e a clinker horizon or cavity
The constant head tests were conducted using either the pump mounted on the
drill rig which provided up to approximately 60 gallons per minute gpm or the water
truck pump which was capable of providing 150 or more gpm The tests were
conducted for a minimum of 30 minutes or until the water truck was drained depending
on the time constraints imposed by the terms of the roadwork permit i e the need to
vacate the road by
The falling head tests were conducted after the end of pumping in the constant
head tests by measuring the fall in the water level in the boring against time The falling
head tests were run for either about 30 minutes until the hole was drained or until the
water level stabilized
Results of the field percolation test data and calculations are presented on
Plates C1 through C 6
