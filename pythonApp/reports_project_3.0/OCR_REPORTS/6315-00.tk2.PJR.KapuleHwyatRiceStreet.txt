Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene
Epoch more than 2¼ million years before present Following the cessation of this main
shield building phase renewed volcanic activity occurred with the extrusion of basaltic
lavas of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the
alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation
Most of the soils in the project area consist of residual and saprolite soils derived
from the in situ weathering of the Koloa Volcanic Series igneous rocks In general
residual and saprolite soils grade to weathered basalt formations with increasing depth
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as thick flows of dense
basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various locales
Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones
at the vent Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the
Island of Kauai including the site of the proposed project
During the Pleistocene Epoch many sea level changes occurred as a result of
widespread continental glaciation As the great continental glaciers accumulated and
advanced the level of the ocean fell since less water was available to fill the oceanic
basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels rose because
more water was available The landmass of Kauai remained essentially stable during
these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to
the present sea level of the Island of Kauai

Ultra Thin Whitetopping Pavements
In addition to the conventional flexible and rigid pavement design as presented
above we understand that the SDOT would like to evaluate the option of using
Ultra Thin Whitetopping UTW with Cement Treated Base CTB in place cement
treatment of existing base subbase for the pavement construction work
In general UTW is a relatively new pavement rehabilitation process where a thin
layer of concrete 2 to 4 inches usually high strength with closely spaced joints is
bonded to an existing prepared surface distressed asphalt The bonding allows the
concrete overlay and the underlying asphalt to act as a composite section reducing
critical stresses Short joint spacing further reduces the stresses caused by wheel loads
and temperature changes The new concrete provides an economical smooth riding
surface that is much stronger and more durable than the old asphalt pavement Most of
the concrete used for UTW has contained fibers such as polyproplylene steel and
nylon to improve crack resistance and enhance the toughness or ductility of the
concrete To be successful the UTW must be placed over asphalt that does not show
signs of deep pavement distress and has a minimum thickness of 3 inches If potholes
alligator cracking or deep fissures exist in the asphalt the concrete will not form an
adequate bond resulting in pavement that lacks adequate support Based on field tests
conducted by the Federal Highway Association FHWA asphalt rutting potential is a
significant factor in the UTW performance The UTW sections built on stiffer asphalts
showed less distress than those on softer asphalts
Due to the anticipated re grading work at the intersection area we understand
that most of the existing AC will be completely removed in order to achieve the design
finish grades for the highway intersection improvement project As a result the
conventional application of a thin layer of concrete bonding to an existing prepared
asphalt may not be feasible for this project Therefore we believe that a pavement
section consisting of 4 inches of UTW on 6 inches of CTB may be considered for this
project if deemed appropriate It should be noted that the proposed UTW pavement
section is generally used for pavement rehabilitation purpose and is not designed for a
pavement life of 30 years The use of this UTW pavement section in conjunction with

Subgrade Preparation Below Pavement Section
In general the area within the contract grading limits should be cleared and
grubbed thoroughly at the on set of earthwork Vegetation debris deleterious materials
existing pavements and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed
properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas
designated to receive the pavement section should be over excavated to expose firm or
stiff material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted
general fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of
off site
After clearing and grubbing the future pavement areas should be excavated
where necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the asphalt concrete
base or aggregate subbase layer The exposed subgrades should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO
T 180 ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Due to the potential presence of
soft and or wet soils contract documents should include additive and deductive unit
prices for over excavation and fill placement to account for variations in the
over excavation and backfill quantities
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and
scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar
to those encountered in our field exploration

Pavement Joints
Considering the large areal extent of the rigid pavements if elected for the highway
intersection improvement project an adequate amount and pattern of joints should be
provided In general joints should be provided in rigid pavements for the following
reasons
1
To reduce potential curling and warping stresses in the pavement caused by
temperature and moisture gradients across the pavement slabs
2
To reduce and control cracking due to volume changes in the concrete
3
To reduce damage to immovable structures
4
To facilitate construction
Paragraph Pavement Joints in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design
Manual prepared by the State of Hawaii Department of Transportation should be
referred to for pavement joint details and construction requirements
3 10 Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement
surface should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface water off
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should not be
allowed on the site during or after construction Development of good shoulder
drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage structures is
strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or premature
failure of the pavements
Subdrains should be provided in areas where seepage is observed during and or
after grading or as recommended by the Engineer during construction Subdrains
should conform to the requirements of Section 605 Underdrains of the State of Hawaii
Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction 2005 HSS As a minimum
the subdrain pipes should consist of 6 inch diameter perforated pipes conforming to
Subsection 706 12 of the HSS with the perforations facing down The subdrains should
be daylighted into appropriate drainage structures for proper discharge

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling three borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to a depth of about 11 5 feet below
the existing pavement surface The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling
equipment equipped with continuous flight augers The approximate locations of the
borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
