INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical
engineering evaluation performed for the proposed Nansay Resort
development in San Rogue on the island of Saipan Commonwealth of the
Northern Mariana Islands The general location of the site and its
vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The exploration
consisting of two phases of subsurface drilling was performed in general
accordance with our original and revised proposals dated April 4 and
respectively
This report summarizes the findings from the field exploration and
our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses
based on preliminary data obtained during the exploration These
recommendations are intended for design input and are not intended for
construction bidding estimation purposes

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located on the southwestern portion of Achugan
Point in the village of San Roque on the island of Saipan Commonwealth
of the Northern Mariana Islands
The site consists of approximately 18 3 acres 7 4 hectares of
beachfront land and is bounded to the north and west by Tanapag Lagoon
Philippine Sea south by Chalan Pale Arnold Highway east by Camacho
Equipment Company and southwest by existing residences
Topographically the site gently slopes up toward the north from a
southern lowlying wetland area An isolated hill exists in the
eastern corner of the site The existing ground surface elevations at the
site range from about 1 2 feet in the lowlying area to 23 6 feet on the
hill above Mean Sea Level A small occupied wooden house exists near the
eastern boundary of the site A drainageway trending northwest lies along
the southern boundary
The area is vegetated with various plants including coconut
ironwood abandoned sugar cane as well as other trees and grasses The
lowlying wetland area was covered by about two feet of water during
our second phase exploration in the month of August 1988
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Saipan is composed of an Eocene age rhyolitic core
which is overlain by Oligocene and Miocene volcanic rocks These volcanic
core rocks account for more than two thirds of the geologic colum of
Saipan However they outcrop on only about one tenth of the land
surface Overlying these volcanic rocks are Miocene Tagpochau
Pleistocene Mariana and recent Tanapag limestones which outcrop over
about 35 of Saipan s 48 square miles
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2040 00 Page 6
During the Pleistocene epoch global sea level fluctuated in response
to the great continental glaciers Fringing coral algal reefs around the
island continued to grow during the high stands of the sea leaving fossil
reefs and associated lagoonal environments which are now exposed above sea
level
At the project site such fossil reefs Tanapag limestone are
exposed along the beach in the northern portion of the site and are
present at shallow depths throughout the seaward margin of the site In
the lowlying inland portion the surface sediments are mainly of a
low energy depositional estuarine or lagoonal environment consisting of
poorly consolidated sands silts and clays Mariana limestone underlies
these deposits in the southern portion of the site
Interbedded with these coral algal materials and lagoonal deposits
are stiff alluvial clays originally transported from the eroding mountains
southeast of the site These clays are exposed at the surface on the
isolated hill located in the eastern portion of the site

Site Preparation
Settlement
HO ern ah
Extensive site filling is planned in the lowlying wetland area
where a surface layer of soft mud and loose sand was encountered
Settlements on the order of 3 to 6 inches are anticipated due to the
compression of the soft and loose soil from the weight of the 25 feet
of new fill
It is very difficult to accurately predict the time required for the
settlement to occur since it depends on minute characteristics of the
soil structure We estimate that 95 of the total settlements would
occur within two months of the fill completion Because of the
anticipated settlements we recommend that the site preparation and
fill placement for this area be given first priority to minimize
delays in the commencement of the building construction
If the above waiting period is not tolerable the upper 2 to 3 feet
of soft clay could be removed and replaced with granular fill to
lessen the waiting time to 2 weeks
Lowlying Wetland Area
te ee ts am Ne eS
Prior to site clearance and surface vegetation removal we recommend
the off site drainage water around the lowlying area be diverted
where possible to permit the existing soft area to develop a firmer
soil crust
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2040 00 Page 9
Clearing and grubbing of the wetland area should consist of removing
vegetation and trees above the ground surface Due to the very soft
soil conditions we anticipate that most of the clearing will be done
by hand Construction equipment could be used after either removal
and replacement of the upper soft mud or the completion of special
ground stabilization procedures as recommended below
The existing surface soil in the area is too soft and wet to support
construction equipment therefore we recommend that ground
stabilization procedures be utilized
The ground stabilization procedures consist of establishing a working
platform or drainage blanket after clearing and grubbing of a section
of the existing ground area prior to the site filling operation
The working platform should consist of a combination of a synthetic
filter fabric overlain by a gravel layer The required thickness of
the gravel layer will be affected by the water level conditions at
the time of placement Under normal conditions with the water level
at or below the ground surface an 18 inch thickness is anticipated
A sketch of the drainage blanket working platform within the lowlying
area is shown on Plate 5
The gravel should be generally between one fourth and three fourths
of an inch in size
The filter fabric will reduce the required gravel thickness and the
gradation requirements of the drainage blanket since it will act asa
filter medium to prevent the mud from contaminating the granular
blanket material It also provides tensile reinforcement to the
gravel layer and permits use of heavy equipment on a minimum
thickness of compacted aggregate
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2040 00 Page 10
The filter fabric is usually rolled onto the cleared soft ground by
laborers The gravel is then placed on the fabric by light
equipment Fill layers are added until a suitably stable platform
for heavy equipment is constructed
The replacement scheme consists of removing the upper 2 to 3 feet of
soft mud and replacing it with a gravel layer up to about 12 inches
above the groundwater level prior to the site filling operation
This will remove the potential long term settlements of the soft mud
under the new fill and accelerate the settlement The removed md
may be stockpiled or processed for future landscape uses
Depending on the season the lowlying area may be dry or covered with
a couple feet of surface water Any free surface water should be
drained or pumped out to facilitate the site filling work The work
may start from the edge of the lowlying area with light equipment by
removing a small portion of the mud and inmediately replacing with
gravel The depth of removal is estimated to be 2 to 3 feet below
the existing ground surface We recommend that the geotechnical
engineer should monitor the adequacy of the mud removal and
replacement work prior to the subsequent site filling in the lowlying
area

tn a a Nt i ey
Based on the borings the upper 15 to 20 feet of the subsoils at the
site consist of loose fine to medium grained sands Since the
project site is in a generally high seismic zone the underlying
loose sandy layer is believed to be highly susceptible to
liquefaction which could cause complete loss of strength during an
earthquake loading resulting in large ground settlements
Although there are methods to reduce the liquefaction potential by
deep densification of the sand these procedures require a specialty
contractor and are very costly
For these reasons pile foundations are recommended for the Junior

Cavity Probing and Grouting
During the field exploration a cavity of about 5 feet high was
encountered in the limestone in Boring 3 at a depth of 25 feet
Cavities are formed in coralline formations due to the presence of
fresh water streams during the growth stages of the coral which
prevent the growth of the coral Therefore it is possible that
other cavities may exist beneath the site
GEGLABS HAWAII
W Page 20
To reduce the possibility of footing settlement due to the collapse
of cavities near the footing bottom elevation in the underlying
coralline limestone formation drilled probes are recommended below
each column footing and at 15 foot centers for continuous wall
footings The probe holes should be a minimm of 3 inch in diameter
and extend below the bottom of the footing to a depth equal to two
times the footing width or a minimm of 10 feet The probe holes
should then be grouted with a sand water cement mixture injected

probe holes should be made to delineate the horizontal extent and
thickness of the cavity or loose lens and properly grouted The
probing and grouting operations should be monitored by the project

APPENDIX
Wave Equation Analysis of Pile Driving
0 ee A cs a SE eh tt RE Se i i
The wave equation analysis of pile driving was performed using
Program WEAPG which is a microcomputer adaption of the program WEAP
originally written by Goble and Associates for the Federal Highway
Administration to solve pile drivability problems
The program models hammer pile soil system by representing the pile
and driving system by a series of masses and springs The soil is modeled
by a spring and a dashpot attached to each mass The program output
provides ultimate capacity and maximum compressive and tensile stresses in
the piles as a function of hammer blowcount
The primary objectives of the analysis are
1 to analyze the driving stresses in the pile
2 to determine the suitable driving system to be used for the pile
driving and
3 to predict the ultimate pile capacity
The analysis was performed for inch octoganal prestressed
concrete piles using hammers DELMAG D 22 and KOBE K 25 The relationship
of ultimate pile capacity and hammer blowcount commonly known as the
bearing graph is presented in Plate C 1 togther with input information
The maximum driving stresses are presented in Plate C 2
During early stage of pile driving while the pile tip is in the loose
sand deposits tensile stresses may occur during driving The anticipated
tensile and compressive stresses during early stage of pile driving are
analyzed and presented in Plate C 3
