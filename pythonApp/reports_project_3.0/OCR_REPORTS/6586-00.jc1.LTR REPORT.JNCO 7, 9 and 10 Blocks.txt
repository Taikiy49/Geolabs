PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
It is proposed to renovate the existing historical buildings that consist of duplex 
units and apartments at the JNCO 07, 09 and 10 Blocks within Hickam Air Force Base. 
The project location is shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2. We understand that the 
existing buildings have a long history of distresses with numerous repairs including 
chemical grouting and other interior cosmetic repairs. Various distresses were 
documented including cracks on both the interior and exterior walls, slab-on-grade tension 
cracks, and settlement. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that the current building 
renovation project was previously awarded for construction. New distresses consisting of 
cracks and settlement were observed soon after construction was either completed or 
near completion in many units. It is desired to mitigate the damaged buildings and provide 
an alternative foundation design for the remaining building renovation.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the 
majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct while Koolau 
was still active; thus, Waianae’s eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in the 
central Oahu region. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation.  As the worldwide glaciers grew and advanced, less water 
was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands 
of the sea.  When the glaciers melted and receded, a larger volume of water became 
available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. Thus, the island 
shorelines have transgressed and regressed in response to the fluctuations.  The 
processes of terrestrial erosion and the associated depositional environments were 
affected by these glacio-eustatic sea level fluctuations.  When the sea level was low, the 
erosional base level was correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths 
below the present sea level.  As a result, terrestrial sediments were carried further 
seaward and deposited in what had been marine environments.  When the sea level 
was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at 
higher elevations.  In addition, it allowed for the deposition of marine deposits such as 
coral and limestone in environments that had been covered with terrestrial soils and 
rock. 
Interpretation of the historic geology of the coastal plain regions located on the 
Island of Oahu (such as the Hickam AFB project site) is complex due to a long history of 
changes in sea level that occurred in relation to the island land surface.  The overall 
changes in sea level in relation to the land surface resulted from a combination of tectonic 
forces (such as uplift and settlement of the island landmass) and the previously discussed 
glacio-eustatic ocean level changes.  In other words, the rise and fall of sea level in 
relation to the island produced shorelines of emergence and submergence.  In general, 
the overall changes of sea level in relation to the landmass also caused the erosional 
base level to fluctuate producing features such as drowned stream valleys filled with 
marine deposits and conversely, incised marine deposits filled with terrestrial sediments 
or rock. 
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of 
deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio-eustatic sea level changes and

post-erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch.  Following the cessation 
of activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial materials were deposited in a deltaic or 
estuarine environment in the area of Pearl Harbor. 
The ancient sea level fluctuated several times both above and below the present 
sea level.  Concurrent with a rise in sea level during the late Pleistocene Epoch, eruption 
of the Salt Lake-Makalapa member of the Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the older 
alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash, which consolidated to form volcanic tuff 
(locally called "mudrock") layers.  Subsequent erosion dissected and thinned those 
mantles of tuff. 
During the recent epoch, as the sea level rose to its current stand, the area was 
covered with layers of soft and/or loose lagoonal marine deposits.  These deposits were 
subsequently covered by man-made fill placed during the middle portion of this century 
for land reclamation projects during construction of the existing Hickam Air Force Base.  
Due to the proximity of the project site to the Pearl Harbor shoreline and Pacific Ocean, 
relatively shallow and brackish groundwater conditions may be anticipated at the project 
site. 
FIELD EXPLORATION 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
eight borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 108. The borings extended to about 
13 to 17 feet below the existing ground surface using either truck-mounted (for exterior 
borings) or portable (for interior borings) drill rigs equipped with continuous-flight 
solid-stem augers and HQ casing. The boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, 
Plates 3.1 through 3.3. 
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by 
testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D 2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1 of Appendix A. 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered and other observations, such as 
groundwater levels, are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-8 of 
Appendix A. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the

Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in

Core samples of rock and/or hard materials encountered at the project site were 
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation.  Core drilling is a rotary 
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation.  The rock and/or hard 
material left in the hollow core of the bit are mechanically recovered for examination and 
description. Rock cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description 
System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2 of Appendix A. 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of soil/rock masses.  Recovery is defined as the actual length of material 
recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.  For example, if 
3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 
74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) also is a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses.  RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting 
drilling-induced fractures or breaks.  If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5-foot core run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of 
Borings as RQD = 50%.  Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of 
the rock, based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and 
Minerals."

LABORATORY TESTS 
Samples recovered from our field exploration were visually examined and 
classified in our Honolulu laboratory. Selected soil samples were tested to evaluate the 
physical characteristics and engineering properties. The laboratory testing program was 
formulated with the emphasis on evaluating the moisture, density, gradation, plasticity, 
and expansion potential of the soils encountered. 
Moisture Content and Unit Weight tests were performed on selected soil samples to 
aid in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and correlating 
the layers between the borings. The moisture content tests were performed in general 
accordance with ASTM D 2216. The unit weight tests were performed in general 
accordance with ASTM D 2937. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths in Appendix A. 
Nine Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the 
liquid limit (LL), plastic limit (PL), and plasticity index (PI), as an aid in classifying the soils.  
The Atterberg Limits tests were performed in general accordance with ASTM D 4318.  
Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B-1 of Appendix B. 
To provide information on the particle size distribution of the soils encountered, 
three Sieve Analysis tests were performed to evaluate the gradation characteristics and to 
aid in soil classification. The tests were performed in accordance with ASTM C 117 and 
C 136.  Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B-2 of Appendix B. 
Thirteen 1-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on samples of the on-site soils to 
evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils. The test results are presented on 
Plate B-3 of Appendix B.

DISCUSSION OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the results of the field exploration, we envision that the perimeter CMU 
footings were likely installed on top of the weathered tuff rock. No bearing failure was 
observed within the three units. Based on the results of the laboratory testing and 
observation, the in-situ clayey soils exhibit moderate to high expansion when subjected to 
fluctuations in the moisture-content. We believe that the building distresses may be 
caused primarily due to expansive subsurface soil conditions combined with lack of 
irrigation plan and poor drainage. 
Unit 316 of JNCO 7 
We understand that JNCO 7 is currently under renovation construction. Due to 
constraints, such as historical nature of the buildings, we understand that it is not feasible 
to remove the in-situ expansive soil and replace with low expansive imported fill. We 
recommend structurally connecting the new concrete slab to the existing concrete CMU 
footing to reduce differential movements. The structural engineer should be engaged for 
design of structural slab and footing connection. 
We also recommend installing a concrete sidewalk along the building perimeter and 
a roof gutter system to control the run-off and rain water away from the building 
foundations. 
Provided the existing CMU concrete footing is supported on the weathered tuff 
rock, an allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used for the design and evaluation of the structural connection. 
Unit 351 of JNCO 9 and Unit 377 of JNCO 10  
We understand that renovations to Units 351 and 377 were completed before 
distresses were observed; therefore, structural connection of the slab and perimeter 
footing may not be feasible for these two units. As an alternative to the conventional 
removing and replacing and structurally connecting the slab and footing, consideration 
may be given to chemical stabilization of the expansive soils with Electrochemical Soil 
Stabilizer (EcSS 300) or equivalent.

In general, the chemical stabilization involves the injection of a diluted chemical 
(diluted in water) into the ground to chemically stabilize the expansive nature of the soils.  
The chemical injection reacts with the clay particles in the soil to form a soil mass that is 
less susceptible to shrink/swell effects when subjected to moisture fluctuations. The 
effectiveness of the system depends on factors, such as type of clay, presence of cobbles 
and penetration achieved, but has been effective on some previous projects. 
For preliminary estimating purposes, we believe that the chemical stabilization 
would need to be injected to a minimum depth of about 5 feet below the finished pad 
elevation, or to competent weathered tuff rock until injection refusal.  In addition, the 
spacing of the injection holes should be no further than 3 feet on center in both directions.  
The chemical stabilization should also extend a minimum of 5 feet beyond the edge of the 
building footprint. 
After injection of the chemical stabilizer, a minimum curing time of 72 hours should 
be allowed prior to post-treatment sampling and testing to evaluate the treated product. 
The post-treatment sampling and testing would include drilling and sampling about 
four borings (two interior and two exterior) extending to a depth of about 10 feet to obtain 
relatively undisturbed samples within the treated zone.  Two relatively undisturbed 
samples of the treated zone should be obtained from each boring.  The relatively 
undisturbed samples of the treated soils should be subjected to one-dimensional swell 
tests conducted in accordance with ASTM D 4546, Method B.  For acceptance purposes, 
the average swell in each of the post-treatment borings should be 1 percent or less. In 
addition, the results of the swell test of each treated soil sample should not exceed 
2 percent. 
Based on our assessment, the chemical stabilization alternative could be effective 
and deserves a more detailed evaluation and cost comparison. If the chemical stabilization 
alternative is to be implemented, Geolabs should be consulted to provide supplemental 
recommendations for implementation during the design and construction.

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted in this letter report are based, in part, 
upon information obtained from our review of the available information and our field 
borings conducted for the building renovation project. Variations of subsurface conditions 
from those assumed in our evaluation may occur, and the nature and extent of these

attain a properly constructed project.  Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund is 
recommended to accommodate these possible extra costs.
