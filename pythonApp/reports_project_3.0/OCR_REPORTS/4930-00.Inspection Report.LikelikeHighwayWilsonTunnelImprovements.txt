1 INTRODUCTION
1 1 Purpose Scope
This report summarizes data and results from geologic reconnaissance
and structural inspection of the eastern portion of both bores of the Wilson
Tunnel conducted between July 8 and The purpose of these
inspections was to gather the information necessary to form the basis of
recommendations for improvements to the tunnels This report was prepared by
Geolabs Inc of Honolulu and Jacobs Associates of San Francisco under
subcontract to R M Towill Corporation prime engineer for the project The
Wilson Tunnel Improvements Project is being implemented by the State of
Hawaii Department of Transportation Highways Division
The Wilson Tunnel consists of two tunnels along the Likelike Highway
Route 63 in the Districts of Honolulu and Koolaupoko on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location map Plate 1 The inspection limits for both tunnels began at the
Kalihi Portal and extended approximately 1 200 feet east into the tunnels Water
leakage in these sections of both tunnels is posing maintenance and aesthetic
problems
The scope of work for this inspection included the following tasks and
work efforts
1 Research and review of as built tunnel drawings
2 Geologic reconnaissance of the site conditions
3 Groundwater hydrology study
4 Inspection of the current conditions along both tunnel linings to
observe and map existing seepage and cracks
5 Drilling a total of eight horizontal cores 4 in each tunnel to
determine the thickness of the tunnel linings

during field exploration
7 Evaluation of the field data as input to determining remediation
measures for the tunnel improvements project

The Island of Oahu is located in the tropics in the Pacific Ocean The
Island of Oahu is characterized by mild temperatures throughout the year
moderate humidity persistent northeasterly trade winds significant differences in
rainfall within short distances and infrequent severe storms
The Island of Oahu is considered semitropical rather than tropical where
the temperature of the surrounding waters is about 10 degrees lower than that of
some other regions of equal latitude The temperatures on the Island of Oahu
typically range between 52 degrees and 90 degrees Fahrenheit The lowest
temperature varies with altitude Typically there is an average decrease of
about 4 degrees in the mean temperature with each increase of 1 000 feet in
altitude
Trade winds northeasterly winds are persistent on the Island of Oahu
throughout most of the year Wind velocities at Honolulu average about 10 miles
per hour mph However Kona winds southerly winds occasionally may
interrupt the trade winds but for no more than a few days at a time The Kona
winds tend to be associated with high humidity The relative humidity may reach
95 percent on days where Kona winds are present
The rainfall on the Island of Oahu may vary from as low as 6 inches per
year in the leeward lowland areas to as high as 250 inches per year over the

2 TUNNEL GEOLOGY
2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two
main shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older shield volcano Waianae
Volcano is estimated to be middle to late Pliocene Epoch in age and forms the
bulk of the western third of the island The younger shield volcano Koolau
Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene Epoch to early Pleistocene Epoch Ice
Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The
Waianae Volcano became extinct while the Koolau Volcano remained active
Therefore the older Waianae Volcano s eastern flank was partially buried below
the younger Koolau Volcano lavas that banked against the Waianae s eastern
flank These banked and ponded lava flows formed a broad upland plateau
referred to as the Schofield Plateau of Central Oahu
During the evolutionary history of the Island of Oahu fluctuation of the
ocean s sea level occurred as a result of the worldwide advance and retreat of
the great continental glaciers These sea level changes occurred more
substantially during the Pleistocene Epoch and had some effect on the geologic
evolution of the Island of Oahu The erosional baseline of terrestrial streams was
affected and caused local submergence and emergence of the coastal island
landforms with respect to the level of the sea In addition to sea level
fluctuations the entire island mass experienced settlement as the weight of the
volcanic deposits depressed the oceanic crust beneath the island Together the
sea level fluctuations and land mass adjustments have imparted some
complexity to the geologic evolution of the Island of Oahu

The end of volcanic activity associated with the Koolau Volcano was
followed by a long period of landmass erosion whereby deep stream valleys were
carved into the dome shaped Koolau Shield Volcano The deeper valley floors
were filled with thick accumulations of alluvial sediment and colluvial landslide
material as the island was eroded and slowly depressed beneath the weight of
the new landmass
Stream erosion landslide activity and ocean wave attack are believed to
have contributed to the formation of the steep and elongated Koolau Pali
cliffline located along the windward side of the Island of Oahu The existing
elongated spine of the Koolau Mountains represents the erosional remnant of the
primary volcanic rift axis of the Koolau Shield Volcano The width of the Koolau
Pali which separates the valley heads located on either side of the Pali
windward and leeward has been slowly narrowing in response to the erosional
retreat of the heads of the opposing windward and leeward valleys The
erosional retreat of the Koolau Pali is most apparent at the heads of Kalihi and
Nuuanu Valleys The erosion occurring on either side of the Koolau Pali is
contributing to the formation of narrow wind gaps of reduced mountain height and
thickness located at the heads of these valleys The erosional gaps in the
Koolau Pali are logical places for trans Koolau routes such as the Likelike and
Pali Highways as tunnel lengths are shorter
Approximately 850 000 to years ago some limited volcanic
activity resumed with the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series from
secondary vents scattered across the eastern portion of the Island of Oahu The
eruptions occurred along the established secondary rift zones of the Koolau
Volcano Some of the most notable landmarks located on the Island of Oahu
such as Salt Lake Koko Head and Diamond Head Craters were created by the
eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series The craters are composed of hardened
cinder and volcanic ash tuff cones and some basaltic lava flows The eruption

of the Honolulu Volcanic Series continued over a period of several hundred
thousand years which ended approximately 30 000 years ago
2 2 Site Geology and Groundwater Hydrology
The Wilson Tunnel project site is located at the upper head of Kalihi Valley
along the Koolau Pali The general location of the project site and vicinity is
shown on Plate 1
In general the Koolau Mountain Range and the Koolau Pali are composed
of layered volcanic rock that consists of sequential thin flows of basaltic a a and
pahoehoe lavas that were erupted from the rift zone vents of the Koolau Volcano
Regionally the bedding inclination of the lava flows dip gently seaward toward
the south and southwest from the summit region of the Koolau Pali The
individual lava flow beds range from less than about 5 feet thick to about 30 feet
in thickness
Typically the basalt rock grades with depth from extremely weathered and
soft saprolitic near the surface to progressively less weathered and harder rock
materials The weathering gradation may occur over intervals of tens of feet to
hundreds of feet in depth depending on the degree of chemical and physical
decomposition the rock has experienced The lavas of the summit of the Koolau
Volcano are typically deeply weathered due to the high rainfall and extensive
groundwater aquifers encompassed by the volcano
The layered basaltic rock is considered to be very porous due to the
presence of closely spaced rock joints rubbly clinker seams lava tubes and
voids and other irregular contacts that occur between the individual lava flow
layers Rainfall that does not runoff as stream flow percolates downward
through the porous volcanic layers to eventually reach the basal groundwater
reservoir which is located deep beneath the mountain mass The basal
groundwater reservoir represents the large scale accumulation of fresh
Page 10
State of Hawaii Department of Transportation
Wilson Tunnel Improvements Inspection Report
groundwater that deep pumping wells tap for the extraction of potable water The
basal groundwater surface aquifer is believed to reside at significant depths
below the Wilson Tunnel project site
Intrusive seams of fine grained dense basaltic rock referred to as
volcanic dikes transect the layered and porous basaltic rock which compose the
mass of the Koolau Volcano Volcanic dikes represent the volcano s internal
feeder conduits through which molten basalt rock was transported toward the
surface during eruptions The dike rock occurs as generally thin 3 to 10 feet
wide ribbons of non porous rock that are oriented near vertical and occur in
concentrated patterns referred to as dike swarms The dike swarms are most
prevalent along the elongated rift zones of the Koolau Volcano
The fine grained dense rock character of the volcanic dikes emplaced
within the surrounding porous layered volcanic rock acts to retard the free
percolation of groundwater The dikes by nature of their low porosity textural
character steeply inclined transecting orientation and close spacing in dike
swarms act to impound percolating groundwater in localized reservoirs well
above the basal groundwater aquifer These confined and generally localized
groundwater reservoirs are referred to as high level perched groundwater Many
water supply tunnels excavated in the Koolau Mountains tap the dike impounded
groundwater as a source of potable water however this condition is not observed
along the Wilson Tunnel
Kalihi Valley is a large erosional U shaped valley formed by the incision of
flowing streams and mass wasting landslide collapse of the adjacent valley
walls As a result the valley floor has been partially filled with thick
accumulations of alluvial and colluvial materials that represent the eroded and
transported products of the volcanic mountain range Since the valley filling
processes occurred over very long periods of time hundreds of thousands of
years the deeper alluvial and colluvial materials are more weathered and are
Page 11
State of Hawaii Department of Transportation
Wilson Tunnel Improvements Inspection Report
typically semi consolidated due to burial beneath thick overburden layers of more
recent alluvial and colluvial deposits The older alluvial colluvial materials are
referred to as Quaternary Age Older Alluvium Qa The Qa deposits generally
consist of decomposed basaltic cobbles and boulders in a tight matrix of clayey
and fine sandy soil The deposits may be so decomposed that the original rocky
materials may be crushed to form finer soil constituents such as clay silt sand
and gravel
The ground surfaces of the Kalihi Valley floor especially along the existing
stream drainages and other topographic depressions may be mantled with some
Recent Alluvium Ra of Holocene and Recent geologic time These deposits are
generally unconsolidated and consist of various eroded and transported earth
materials consisting of soft loose sediments clays silts sands and gravels with
some cobbles and boulders The recent alluvial deposits typically mantle the
older alluvial deposits and other underlying in situ volcanic products
Based on a review of available boring logs completed during the Bore 1
design descriptions of materials encountered during Bore 1 excavation and
regional geologic information pertaining to the Wilson Tunnel site it appears that
the tunnels penetrate older alluvial colluvial deposits Qa and some
saprolitic residual soils at the Kalihi end of the tunnels Less weathered basalt
rock materials associated with the Koolau Volcanic Series Tkb were
encountered approximately midway and through toward the Kaneohe end of the
tunnels The saprolitic residual soils encountered by the tunnels represent a
gradational zone of extremely to completely weathered basalt rock located at the
margin of the less weathered basalt rock penetrated by the bores The saprolitic
and residual materials represent the in situ deeply weathered product of the
basaltic rock therefore the materials resemble soil that may be broken to finer
components of silt sand and decomposed rock The saprolitic and residual soil
zone is sandwiched between the less weathered basalt rock Tkb encountered

by the central portion of the bore and the older alluvial colluvial materials Qa
which were encountered on the Kalihi side of the bore
Based on the general hydrological differences between the Qa and Tkb
geologic materials the basalt rock should be highly permeable and capable of
transmitting large volumes of groundwater down toward the basal groundwater
aquifer In addition the presence of volcanic dikes crosscutting within the basalt
formation likely has created confined compartments of perched groundwater
located within the basalt formation Thus there should be a greater volume of
groundwater moving vertically at higher rates within the basalt rock formation In
contrast the more clayey Qa deposits should be of significantly lower
permeability and should act to reduce the movement of groundwater A profile of

2 3 Geologic Reconnaissance

the existing ground surface conditions and geology in the vicinity of the tunnel
portals and ventilation building located on the Kalihi side of the tunnels The

some passing light showers The weather conditions for the week preceding the
site reconnaissance was generally normal in terms of rainfall with only light to
moderate rainfall recorded at the Wilson Tunnel rain gauge

3 1 History
History of the tunnels from design through construction and operation
was considered valuable in providing insight into the present day tunnel

Bore 1 Construction Honolulu Bound
Drawings for City and County of Honolulu Department of Public Works
Construction Contract 26 53 constitute the design record for Bore 1 Design
drawings show this bore as the Kalihi Tunnel which eventually became one of
the two tunnels comprising the Wilson Tunnel The record of construction for
Bore 1 is based on the published article by Peck 1981 and by unpublished
reports by Terzaghi 1955 Peck 1956 and Hirota 1954
which were prepared as a result of the tunnel collapse during construction In
addition drawing revisions for Contract 26 53 appear to document the as built
condition of Bore 1
Construction bids for Bore 1 were received and the
contract was awarded to a Joint Venture of Gibbons Read from Salt Lake City
Page 20
State of Hawaii Department of Transportation

and E E Black Ltd from Honolulu Bids were on a unit price basis which
included prices of steel tunnel support tunnel lagging and backpacking
Tunnel excavation commenced on from the Kaneohe
Portal at the eastern end in rock and was driven full face with little support Near
the end of May 1954 tunnel excavation transitioned from rock to earth which
ranged from highly weathered rock extremely weathered rock saprolite
residual soil and transported soil deposits Full face excavation was employed
with horseshoe shaped steel sets structural steel on approximately 3 ft centers
until difficulties were encountered in the earth soft ground section Excavation
was by drill and blast in rock and hand mining in earth
As the tunnel was advanced from sound rock into weathered earth like
conditions progressive sloughing and spalling was experienced in the upper part
of the tunnel face In the summer of 1954 several tunnel collapses and
subsequent surface sinkholes were experienced on and 28 ultimately
leading to a large collapse on August 14 which killed five construction workers
The largest sinkhole was recorded at the ground surface to be about 75 ft in
diameter and 30 ft deep Initial ground cover at that location was approximately
100 feet A continuous zone of disturbed material extended from the bottom of
the tunnel to the ground surface as noted by Peck 1981 The collapses are
documented in more detail by Peck and Terzaghi 1955
Following the collapse on August 14 construction ceased while
consideration was given to alternative methods of completion Of historical note
is that this effort included several notable individuals that consulted with the City
Ralph B Peck Professor Emeritus of Civil Engineering at the

Tunnel excavation restarted in February 1956 using a different approach

implemented This approach was distinctly different from that of classical
hard rock tunneling Small sized tunnels drifts were used to excavate the

3 3 Relevant Design Details
Several design details are relevant and notable for this tunnel inspection
These are called design details since there is no clearly indicated as built
drawing or other written record documenting construction It is believed that the
available documents reasonably reflect construction since some drawings show
several revisions that took place during the known time of construction
Construction Joints Water Stops
The lining of Bore 1 was laid out with 64 ft long structural modules of
common repetitive features Type B copper water stops were noted which
would have been at construction joints Bore 2 shows 60 ft modules but
construction joints at 30 ft intervals with a rubber water stop across the joint with
a key It is unclear if the longitudinal reinforcing steel crosses a joint
this is unlikely at least at the end of a module During the inspection
construction joints between the modules were recorded as expansion joints and
Page 26
State of Hawaii Department of Transportation
Wilson Tunnel Improvements Inspection Report
in cases where there was an aperture noted at the expansion joint it was
mapped as a crack see Section 4 for inspection methods and results
Construction joints were apparently vertical as opposed to sloping construction
joints sometimes used in tunneling and are spaced 30 ft apart
Despite the intent of the various types of water stops leaks at construction
joints in tunnel linings have been frequently observed worldwide It was not until
the late 1970 s that membrane linings came into use in the tunnel industry thus
improving these types of leakage situations
Drains and Drain Details
Starting from the invert and working upward a 24 inch diameter collector
and transmission pipe runs under the roadway The primary purpose of this pipe
is to collect the roadway drainage that comes from 6 inch diameter lateral drain
pipes that connect to drain inlets at both sides of the roadway Inspection of the
pipe and laterals was not in the current scope of work In Bore 2 weepers are
also shown that are believed to be intended to drain groundwater outside the
tunnel lining by means of a filter drain near footing level A weeper is usually
intended to reduce or eliminate the buildup of groundwater behind a concrete
wall in this case the tunnel lining Running up the lining from the invert are
weeper assemblies that are also believed to be intended to drain water from
outside the lining Refer to drawings in Appendix A for details These weepers
and assemblies cannot be inspected nor was an attempt made to observe any
discharge from these weepers because of no access Based on past tunnel
experience it is unlikely that the weepers are now or ever did provide a
substantial drain around the tunnel They do not have sufficient area to collect
groundwater on a widespread basis as these consist of a few 1 inch diameter
pipes spaced at approximately 15 ft intervals for a tunnel lining periphery of
approximately 44 ft Also if the pipe materials were galvanized steel they are
most likely very corroded and plugged Finally they do not have a configuration
that is cleanable or otherwise practical to inspect and maintain
Page 27
State of Hawaii Department of Transportation
Wilson Tunnel Improvements Inspection Report
For both Bore 1 and 2 ceiling drains are shown on the design drawings
The designers clearly envisioned water in the plenum Bore 2 shows details of a
subtle trough diverting water to a relatively small drain pipe See Section 4 for
postulation that some of the major leaks consist of infiltration through these
pipes which were originally intended to be drains
Contact Grouting
This type of grouting is done to fill voids in the crown which are typical of
a cast in place concrete lining For linings supporting ground loads this grouting
is essential to ensure continuous uniform contact of the structure concrete
lining with the ground In addition if done properly contact grouting also has
substantial value in sealing off leaks initially present in the lining by reducing the
flow of groundwater around the tunnel and by improving the general water
tightness of the lining
No contact grout holes are indicated on the design drawings for Bore 1
contact grouting could have been specified and not shown or not done at all
Bore 2 drawings clearly show holes that reflect contact grouting details that are
common in tunnel construction today However no construction records were
found for contact grouting in Bore 2 With knowledge of the consultants that
assisted with the completion of Bore 1 and design of Bore 2 it is likely that
contact grouting was performed in Bore 2 having some of the benefit on future
leakage that would be expected However design drawings indicate that only
Bore 2 had specific plans for contact grouting

Dear Mr Mimura
At your request Mid Pacific Testing Inspection performed a survey of approximate in place concrete
compressive strength at the subject site utilizing the James Instruments H Meter Rebound Hammer
Prior to applying the hammer to the surface of the concrete we utilized a pachometer to Jocate the
embedded reinforcing stee in order to make sure our survey was carried out directly on concrete and not
on covered reinforcing steel
We took readings at 36 locations encompassing both the north and south walls of each tunnel and ceiling
locations in the Kailua bound tunnel At each location the reinforcing steel was located and an
appropriate area without reinforcing steel was marked Then utilizing the James Instruments H Meter a
set of ten readings was obtained The readings were noted and an average R value was obtained Then
using compressive strength tables provided by James Instruments this average R value was correlated to
an approximate concrete compressive strength The specific locations tested readings obtained average
reading and approximate compressive strength results are shown in the attached reports along with graphs
showing H Meter readings in relation to tunnel stations In both tunnels the lowest estimated compressive
strengths exceeded 8200 psi with many locations testing in excess of 10 000 psi the top of our rebound
value charts In general the Kailua bound tunnel tested higher than the Honolulu bound tunnel and
showed a higher deviation in readings
If we may provide you with any further information regarding this matter please feel free to contact us at
your convenience
