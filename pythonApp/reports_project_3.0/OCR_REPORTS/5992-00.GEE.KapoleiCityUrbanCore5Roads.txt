PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration was to obtain an overview
of the surface and subsurface conditions to develop a soil data set to formulate
geotechnical engineering recommendations for the pavement design and earthwork
operations. To accomplish this, we conducted an exploration program consisting of the
following tasks and work efforts:
1. Research and review of available in-house soils and geologic information
for the project site and its vicinity.
2. Clearing of access trails to some of the boring locations with a front-end
loader.
3. Mobilization and demobilization of a truck-mounted drill rig and
two operators to and from the project site.
4. Drilling and sampling of six borings extending to depths of about 24.8 to
25.5 feet below the existing ground surface for a total of approximately
152.3 lineal feet of field exploration.
5. Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by our
geologist.
6. Laboratory testing of selected soil samples obtained during the field
exploration as an aid in classifying the materials and evaluating their
engineering properties.
7. Analyses of the field and laboratory data to formulate geotechnical
engineering recommendations for the design of pavements and site
preparation.
8. Preparation of this letter report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and recommendations.
9. Coordination of our overall work on the project by our senior engineer.
10. Quality assurance of our work and client/design team consultation by our
principal engineer.
11. Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting, word processing, and clerical
support.

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The proposed Urban Core Roads are in the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu,
Hawaii. The work under this project involves construction of the proposed street
extensions for Kapolei Parkway and Kamaaha Avenue as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
Based on our field observations, majority of the proposed roadway extensions
generally consist of vacant lands moderately vegetated with small trees, tall grasses,
and some soil stockpiles. Dry and loose soils were exposed at the existing ground
surface with potholes and trenches caused by soil shrinkage in the surface clayey soil.
Based on the plan and profile drawings provided, the existing ground elevations within
the new roadway alignments generally range between about +47 and +70 feet
Mean Sea Level (MSL).
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The portion of Kamaaha Avenue Extension under this project has been
previously explored by our office in 2007 for the Kapolei City Urban Core 3 Roads
project. The previous field exploration relevant to this project consisted of drilling and
sampling one boring, designated as Boring No. 13, and collection of one bulk sample,
designated as Bulk-5, at the locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The log of boring

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings, the principal
geotechnical considerations for the design of this project include proper site preparation
requirements to take into consideration the expansive nature of the near-surface soils and
the relatively dry condition of the soils and the site. Detailed discussion of our geotechnical
engineering recommendations for design of pavements and site preparation are presented
in the following sections herein.

under the Engineering and Policy Memorandum No. CEB-1-06 by the City and County

necessary for pavement analyses and design.

Design Subgrade Conditions
Based on our field exploration, the proposed new roadway alignments are
generally underlain by stiff clayey soils and dense silty sands at the existing
ground surface. Laboratory testing performed on the surface clayey soils at the
project site and the vicinity generally indicated a CBR value of about 4 and a
swell of around 5 percent. Based on these assumptions and test results, a design
R-value of 9 was adopted in our pavement analyses for the clayey soils subgrade
condition.

As mentioned previously, coralline materials consisting of dense silty sands may be
exposed at the pavement subgrade elevation along the western portion (Sta. 0+00 to
Sta. 11+50) of the Kapolei Parkway Extension alignment. It is our opinion that the 24-inch
aggregate subbase recommended above may be eliminated under such condition.
Therefore, CBR and field density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils
encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above sections. The
recommended sections assume that good drainage will be provided for the paved areas.
Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base course, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the
pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to
carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. Surface water
ponding should not be allowed on-site during or after construction.

Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the anticipated design finish grades for the
proposed roadway project, we anticipate that site grading work consisting of substantial
cuts and fills on the order of about 10 to 13 feet thick will be required for the project
construction. In general, the area within the contract grading limits should be cleared
and grubbed thoroughly at the on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious
material and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly
off-site. Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to
receive fill or future improvements should be over-excavated to expose firm and/or stiff
material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill. The
excavated soft and/or organic soils should be disposed properly off-site.
Existing structures and pavements to be demolished should be completely
removed. The over-excavations resulting from demolition should be backfilled with
compacted granular fill material. Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed,
and the resulting excavation should be backfilled properly with granular fill material
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in 8-inch level
loose lifts, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Utilities to
be abandoned in-place should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low
pressure.
Our field observations indicate that shrinkage cracks and ground depressions are
present at the existing ground surface along the roadway extension alignments. In
general, shrinkage cracks will develop in the ground due to the lack of maintenance of
the soil moisture content as the surface soils start to dry out under the sun and dry
weather conditions. The development of shrinkage cracks in the ground was apparently
intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions characteristic of the Kapolei area
resulting in large fluctuations of the soil moisture content.
If the existing ground cracks are left unattended for an extended period of time
and are allowed to penetrate deeper below the surface, the ground cracks could
adversely affect the performance of the future roadway. The ground cracks could serve
as conduits for water to percolate into the pavement subgrade soils and cause loss of
strength of the subgrade soils resulting in potential settlement of the pavement
structure. Therefore, remedial work to correct or mitigate the observed ground cracks
should be implemented prior to fill placement and construction of the proposed new
roadways.

After clearing and grubbing, the exposed subgrades should be proof-rolled with a
minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of six passes to assist in detecting
and collapsing near-surface voids and loose zones created by the shrinkage cracks and
ground depressions. The exposed subgrades should be proof-rolled without
moisture-conditioning of the subgrade soils (at the pre-existing moisture contents).
Loose areas disclosed during the proof-rolling operation should be backfilled with
general fill material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. The
proof-rolling operations should be conducted under the near-continuous observation by
our representative.
Subsequent to the proof-rolling operations, the subgrades should then be
scarified to a minimum depth of 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by
dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we
recommend scarifying the soils and preparing again as recommended above.
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well-compacted fill. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit
prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the
over-excavation quantities.
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity
determined in accordance with ASTM D 2041. The aggregate subbase material should
meet the requirements of Section 31 of the City and County Standard Specifications
(September 1986). The material should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content, placed in 8-inch level loose lifts, and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction.
In general, the excavated on-site soils generated from cut areas may be re-used as
a source of fill materials to raise the existing ground surface to the bottom of the subbase
course layer. Additional imported fill materials required for the project should consist of
3-inch minus materials free of vegetation, adobe clays, and other deleterious materials. In
addition, imported fill materials should also have a laboratory CBR value of 12 or more and
should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1883. It should be noted that the imported fill requirements
presented herein are intended as guidelines only and may be modified based on additional
laboratory testing and field observations on the available fill materials conducted by

We envision that the planned cut slopes will generally expose the upper stiff
clayey soils and dense silty sands as encountered in the borings. Based on this
assumption, we recommend designing the cut slopes with a slope inclination of
two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. Permanent fill slopes constructed using
the excavated on-site soils or imported fill materials may be designed with a slope
inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be
keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against
sliding. Construction of earth berms, interceptor ditches, and the use of geotextile
fabrics over the fill slope face should be considered to reduce the potential for
significant erosion, thus enhancing the long-term stability of the fill slopes. Appropriate
slope planting or other permanent erosion control measures to reduce the potential for
significant erosion of the exposed slopes (including a permanent irrigation system)
should be implemented as soon as possible after the finished slope faces are
completed.

whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and scarification
process and whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar to those encountered
in our field exploration.
Utility Trenches
We anticipate that the utilities will primarily consist of water, drain, and sewer lines.
In general, good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling
of the trenches for the new utilities at the site. The method and equipment to be used for
trench excavation should be determined by the contractor, subject to practical limits and
safety considerations. In addition, the excavations should comply with the applicable
federal, state, and local safety requirements.
Based on the results of our field exploration, it appears that conventional
excavation methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation at the site. The
clayey soils encountered are generally very stiff to hard. However, some of the utility
trench excavations may encounter dense coralline materials, which may pose some
excavation difficulties during the trench excavation.
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) for support under the pipes.
Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67
gradation), should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above

The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to

Where trenches are below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction.
Corrosion Protection for Water Lines
Two sets of pH and minimum resistivity tests were performed to evaluate the
corrosivity of the on-site soils generally encountered at the site. The test results are
presented on Plate B-1 of this report. The minimum resistivity values of the near-surface
soil samples tested are on the order of about 590 and 690 ohm-cm, which correspond
to a Corrosion Rating of 1 (extremely corrosive) based on the guidelines provided by the
City & County of Honolulu — Board of Water Supply. Therefore, all metallic
substructures, such as ductile iron water lines, in contact with the soils should be
designed properly for protection against potential corrosion.

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling six borings,
designated as Boring Nos. 40 through 45, extending to depths of about 24.8 to 25.5 feet
below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill
rig equipped with continuous flight, solid-stem augers. Two bulk samples, designated as
Bulk-14 and Bulk-15, were obtained from the near-surface soils to evaluate the
pavement support characteristics. The approximate boring and bulk sample locations
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System, as shown on the Log Legend, Plate A. The materials encountered
are provided on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a
3-inch outside diameter (OD) Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling
30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by
driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths.
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937)
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths.
Two sets of Corrosion Tests, including pH (ASTM G 51) and minimum resistivity
(ASTM G 57), were performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field
exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-1.
One Atterberg Limits Test (ASTM D 4318) was performed on a bulk sample
collected to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. Test results are presented on
Plate B-2.
One Sieve Analysis Test (ASTM C 117 and 136) was performed on a bulk sample
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Test
results are presented on Plate B-3.
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests (ASTM D 1883) were performed
on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5.
Two Modified Proctor Compaction Tests (ASTM D 1557) were performed on bulk
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-6 and B-7.
