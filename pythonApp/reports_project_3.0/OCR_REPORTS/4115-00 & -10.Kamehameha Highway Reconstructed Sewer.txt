basalt rock formation a seismic refraction survey was also conducted along the new gravity

A seismic refraction survey is a geophysical subsurface exploration method which
involves the application of energy to the ground surface at a source location and the
GEOLABS INC
W O Page 7
subsequent measurement of the rate of energy transmission in the form of elastic
compression waves passing through the subsurface soil and rock materials Using seismic
refraction methods successive layers of materials with increasing wave velocity
corresponding to increasing ground density may be measured In general the elastic energy
waves travel more slowly through relatively soft low density materials such as soil and highly
weathered rock The elastic waves travel more rapidly through relatively high density
material such as rock formation The boundaries between horizontal layers of substantially
differing energy wave velocities may be estimated by the analysis of the energy wave travel
times to the geophone array The geophysical survey would provide additional information
pertaining to the potential concern related to Item No 3 presented above
During our supplemental field exploration program geophysical surveys were
conducted in an attempt to evaluate the depths of the interface between the upper subsoils
and the underlying basalt rock formation However the interpreted subsurface profiles

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the
GEOLABS INC
W O Page 10
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was
still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava
During the later Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the continental glaciers advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below the present stand When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above the present level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans
of terrigenous sedimenis in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys
in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the
upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high
energy conditions
In some of the mountainous regions of Hawaii chemical weathering has altered the
basaltic flows pyroclastic ejecta to a soil like condition Erosional processes consisting of
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and transport downslope toward
the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium have also altered the natural condition
of the volcanic flows Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a
valley alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are transported and
deposited as alluvium
When alluvial colluvial deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical
processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering
of the material Erosion continually deposits newer material covering the older
GEOLABS INC
W O Page 11
alluvial colluvial deposits Depending on the rate of transport the newer sediments are
generally transient in terms of geologic time
The project area is generally underlain by these older and more recent alluvial soil
deposits or by the soil like highly weathered basaltic lava flows pyroclastic ejecta Urban
development has since altered the natural formation of the area to its present form

DISCUSSION AND PRELIMINARY RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our preliminary field exploration
it appears that the proposed reconstructed sewer alignment is generally underlain by very
stiff to hard clayey fill soils overlying medium hard to hard basalt rock formation at greater
depths Our supplemental field exploration indicates that the majority of the new gravity
sewer section is generally underlain by very dense alluvial soil deposits and saprolite
overlying medium hard to hard basalt rock formation at about the sewer pipe invert
elevations Relatively shallow groundwater levels were encountered at depths ranging from
about 8 2 to 14 3 feet below the existing pavement surface
The subsurface conditions encountered at the site within the depths of the proposed
reconstructed sewer line will pose difficulties to the reconstruction of the sewer line Both the
microtunneling method and open trench cut and cover method of sewer line construction
will be affected by the subsurface conditions encountered at the site The presence of the
medium hard to hard basait rock formation immediately above the sewer line invert
elevations will require special attention and considerations for insialling the sewer line to the
desired lines and grades Both methods of sewer line construction will result in relatively
higher installation costs over the more normal stiff soil subsurface conditions
For the microtunneling approach of new gravity sewer line installation the mixed face
conditions at the top of the bedrock or base of the weathering profile may cause potential
deviation of the microtunneling boring machine from the planned lines and grades In order
to reduce the potential for significant deviation of the lines and grades of the sewer line we
recommend that an over sized steel casing be installed along the sewer line alignment The
over sized steel casing should be at least 50 inches in diameter to account for the potential
horizontal and vertical deviations that may occur during the microtunneling operation In this
manner the 27 inch gravity sewer line may be installed at the correct line and grades within
the 50 inch diameter over sized steel casing In order to tunnel through the hard rock
formation as encountered in our field exploration we believe that the steel casing would need
to have a minimum wall thickness of 34 inch

W O Page 15
In order to reduce the potential for significant deviation of the microtunneling boring
machine from the planned lines and grades we recommend that a larger diameter
microtunneling boring machine 50 inches or larger be used for this project In general the
larger machine would be heavier and more powerful and therefore would be more stable
during the microtunneling operation This will serve to reduce the potential for significant

Microtunneling Alternative
Microtunneling is a trenchless process for installing underground pipelines ducts
culverts and utilities The technique involves the use of powerful hydraulic jacks that push
or jack successive pipe sections through the ground behind a remotely controlled excavating
unit or cutting disk head at the same time as the excavation is taking place within the
excavating unit The following subsections briefly describe the advantages and methods of
construction for microtunneling

A concrete thrust block constructed behind the jacking frame within the jacking pit
provides the reaction to the hydraulic jacks In weak poor ground conditions piling
or other arrangements ground improvement may need to be employed to increase
the reaction capability of the thrust block The hydraulic jacks pressing against the
back of the pipeline advance the disk cutting head into the soil formation To ensure
that the jacking forces are distributed around the circumference of a pipe being
jacked a thrust ring is normally provided with the design depending on the number

of jacks being used The jacks are inter connected hydraulically to ensure that the
thrust from each jack is the same The number of jacks used may vary depending on
the pipe size the strength of the jacking pipes the length to be installed and the
anticipated frictional resistance
The disk cutting head of the tunnel boring machine is controlled via cables from a
cabin on the surface where the operator monitors speed pressure and tunneling
accuracy At the face of the tunnel a rotating cutter head digs into the soil and rock
and mixes it with groundwater and water pumped from the surface Rocky material
is reduced to a maximum inch size in a crushing chamber located directly behind
the cutter disk The excavated material is pumped out of the tunnel as slurry The
waier introduced into the machine is matched in pressure and volume to the material
being displaced This prevents over excavation of the tunnel face thereby reducing
the potential for cave in of the soil surrounding the new casing
The cutting disk speed and direction of rotation the hydraulic jacking system as well
as the slurry system operations can be controlled from one control panel located
remotely at the launch pit The hydraulic steering cylinders located on the disk cutting
head can be extended to steer the head back on course should any deviation be
detected A laser guidance system provides information such as length of drive
vertical and horizontal heading vertical horizontal and roll deviation from the
programmed course and steering cylinder status data on a near continuous basis
so that adjustments can be made continuously to keep the disk cutting head on

Soft Loose Subsoil Deposits
Ground behavior for soft ground tunneling may be described based on six categories
as summarized on Plate 6 Categories of Ground Conditions for Soft Ground Tunnels
Loose sandy soils can be characterized as firm running or flowing ground depending
on the degree of cementation and on the groundwater conditions Above the
groundwater level these soils will not stand unsupported but will tend to ravel or run
GEOLABS INC
W O Page 22
until a stable slope is formed that buttresses the tunnel face If there are sufficient
fines present to give the soil some apparent cohesion these soils may be able to
stand unsupported in the heading for short periods of time Below the groundwater
level these soils may behave as flowing ground unless the cohesion or cementation
of the soil is high enough to resist the seepage forces Both running or flowing ground
behavior indicates potentially unstable ground conditions with low stand up time
Squeezing ground conditions may occur where the soil is observed to advance slowly
into the tunnel excavation without any signs of fracturing Squeezing occurs without
an increase in the water content or a volume change in the soil and is governed by
the soil strength in comparison to the overburden pressure Squeezing ground may
include soft to medium stiff or stiff clays depending on the overburden pressure at the
tunnel depth

loose recent alluvial soil deposits may encroach into the proposed new gravity sewer

Potential Surface Settlement
Surface settlement is usually considered to be the primary source of damage to
adjacent streets utilities and other surface facilities during tunneling operations For
the microtunneling technique loss of ground is generally associated with soil
squeezing or running into the heading losses due to over cutting by the shield shield
steering adjustments and from construction dewatering
Minor ground losses during the advance of the heading are unavoidable with normal
construction techniques and the actual magnitudes of these losses are largely
dependent on the type of ground groundwater conditions overload factor
in cohesive soils size and depth of the tunnel and the skill of the operator in
steering the shield or machine Use of an interlocking steel sheet piling shoring
support system with a tremie concrete plug or precast concrete caissons with tremie
concrete plugs are relatively watertight which should allow the groundwater levels
outside the jacking receiving pits to be maintained close to the original
pre construction levels Therefore we believe that dewatering at the jacking and
receiving pit locations should not cause significant areal settlement
Empirical methods have been developed for estimating surface settlement due to soft
ground tunneling by study of observed settlements on past projects The typical
settlement pattern that develops above a soft ground tunnel is a trough shaped
depression resembling an inverted normal probability curve with the maximum
settlement occurring above the tunnel centerline The behavior of the ground is also
influenced by the cover above the tunnel When the cover is greater than about
two tunnel diameters arching develops in the ground above the tunnel reducing the
potential for significant ground movement and surface settlement Based on the
subsoil conditions encountered in our field explorations we believe that the
anticipated tunneling operations are not likely to result in significant damage to the
roadways or utilities above the tunnel or to the surrounding structures

CATEGORIES OF GROUND CONDITIONS FOR SOFT GROUND TUNNELS
After Terzaghi 1950
Firm Ground A heading may be advanced several feet or more without immediate support
Hard clays and cemented sand or gravel generally fall into this category
Raveling Ground After excavation material above the tunnel or in the upper part of the
working face tends to flake off and fall into the heading Slightly cohesive sands silts and
fine sands gaining their strength from apparent cohesion typically exhibit this type of
behavior Very stiff fissured clays may be raveling materials also
Running Ground Cohesionless dry soils run from any unsupported vertical face until a
stable slope forms at the natural angle of repose i e approximately 3 degrees to
35 degrees Running ground consists of dry cohesionless materials such as clean loose
sand or gravel
Flowing Ground If seepage develops at the working face raveling or running ground is
transformed to flowing ground which advances like a viscous fluid into the heading Silt
sand or gravel below the water table without a high enough clay content to develop
significant cohesion will be flowing type soils
Swelling Ground A condition where the ground absorbs water increases in volume and
expands slowly into the tunnel This may occur in highly over consolidated clays that exhibit
high volume change characteristics upon wetting
Squeezing Ground Squeezing ground conditions are analogous to plastic flow and the soil
is observed to advance slowly into the tunnel excavation without any signs of fracturing
Squeezing occurs without an increase in the water content or a volume change in the soil
and is governed by the soil strength in comparison to the overburden pressure Squeezing
ground may include soft to medium stiff or stiff clays depending on the overburden pressure
at the tunnel depth

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the proposed reconstructed sewer alignment were
explored by drilling and sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3
extending to depths ranging from about 20 5 to 28 feet below the existing pavement surface
The subsurface conditions along the microtunneling section of the reconstructed sewer
alignment were further evaluated by drilling and sampling three additional borings
designated as Boring Nos 4 through 6 extending to depths raging from about 30 to 35 feet
below the existing pavement surface In addition the information obtained were
supplemented by drilling five probe holes designated as Probe Hole Nos 1 through 5 to a
depth of about 25 feet below the existing pavement surface The approximate locations of
the borings and probe holes are shown on the Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 3 The
borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and
coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer or geologist who observed the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6 Results of the
probe hole penetration are presented in the Logs of Probe Holes Plates A 7 through A 11
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches in addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as
the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows required
for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the
Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of ihe pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Appendix A continued
Core samples of the dense basalt rock formations encountered at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would be shown on
the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Two sets of corrosion tests were performed by E L Pacific on selected soil samples
obtained during our field exploration Each set of corrosion test includes the determination
of pH value chloride and sulfate contents The test results are presented on Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentation
of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Eleven sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 D 1140 including eight hydrometer tests
ASTM D 422 were performed on selected samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification Graphic presentations of the grain
size distribution are provided on Plates B 3 through B 5
Five direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the shear strength parameters of the soils encountered in the borings The test
results are presented on Plates B 6 through B 10
Three unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
rock cores to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the rock formation
encountered Results of the unconfined compression tests are presented on Plate B 11

APPENDIX C
Field Permeability Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the in situ soils were evaluated by performing three falling head
tests in selected borings Boring Nos 4 through 6 at depths ranging from approximately
12 to 20 5 feet below the existing pavement surface
During the falling head tests the boreholes were carefully filled with water to the top
of the boreholes and or casing The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to
provide data upon which to base the calculation of the permeability values of the subsoils
The test results of the field permeability tests are presented on Plates C 1 through C 3
