In accordance with our fee proposal dated May 4 1998 we have completed the
field exploration work pertaining to the Mauka Makai Walkway Connection project located
within the existing Salt Lake District Park This letter summarizes the work performed
thus far on the project and presents our preliminary findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our analyses for the subject project
Project Considerations
The project site is located within the Salt Lake District Park to the north of Honolulu
Country Club in the Salt Lake area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The
general location and vicinity of the site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 It
is proposed to construct an accessible walkway trail system from Ala Lilikoi Place to the
mauka park upper playing fields at the Salt Lake District Park
Based on the information provided the new trail will measure approximately
2 200 feet long and 12 feet wide consisting of 8 feet of concrete pavement with 2 feet of
unpaved shoulders on each side of the walkway It is anticipated that the alignment will
generally follow the existing terrain along the sloping hillside at an elevation of
approximately 30 feet Mean Sea Level MSL Cuts into the existing hillside up to about
15 feet high may be required for the project construction In addition we understand that
the following items are also included in the project scope of work

Bridge Foundations
Based on the information provided a 10 foot wide concrete bridge is originally
planned at the existing stream channel at an elevation of about 30 feet MSL
Subsequent to our completion of the field exploration work we understand that the
stream crossing has been relocated upstream to an elevation of about 50 feet MSL in
order to reduce the span length of the bridge structure Therefore additional field
exploration work should be conducted at the final stream crossing location to modify
and or confirm the preliminary recommendations presented herein
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we recommend that a shallow
foundation system bearing on the hard weathered tuff rock formation be used for
support of the planned bridge structure An allowable bearing pressure of up to
5 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of foundations bearing on
the tuff rock formation This bearing value is for dead pius live loads and may be
increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
In general footings should be embedded deep enough to bear directly on the
hard tuff rock formation Based on our field exploration results we anticipate that
bottom of footings will need to be located about 1 to 3 feet below the existing ground
surface in order to bear directly on the underlying tuff rock surface If soft and or loose
materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations they should be
over excavated to expose the underlying hard tuff rock formation The over excavation
should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete or the bottom of footing may be
extended deeper to bear on the tuff rock surface

Comfort Station
In general we envision that slab on grade foundations with thickened edge
footings may be used to support the proposed new comfort station anticipated for the
subject project Based on the current design concept retaining walls will be constructed
on the downslope side to create a relatively level area for the construction of the new
comfort station Depending on the final grading concept we anticipate that the new
comfort station may straddle the in situ tuff rock formation and the new compacted fills
placed behind the retaining walls
In order to reduce the potential for differential settlements as a result of differing
subgrade conditions we recommend that the in situ tuff rock formation be
over excavated where necessary to provide a minimum 12 inch layer of granular fill
material under the slab on grade foundations An allowable bearing pressure of up to
2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of shallow foundations
bearing on the compacted granular fill material This bearing value is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by
wind or seismic forces
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system
A coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the compacted granular
fill material Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the potential for build up of
hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 foot wide
zone of permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation immediately adjacent to the wail with a perforated pipe perforations
facing down at the base of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or
weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as
MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the drainage material The
prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a
perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of non expansive
select granular fill material Unless covered by concrete slabs or pavements the
upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious material to
reduce the poteniial for water infiltration behind the walls
Light Pole Foundations
Based on the information provided we understand that lighting system
consisting of 15 foot high light poles are also planned to serve the new walkway
improvement In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load capacities the
proposed new light pole structures may be supported by a foundation system consisting
of cast in place concrete drilled shafts Based on the anticipated structural loads and
the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we recommend that drilled shafts
with a diameter of about 18 to 24 inches be used to support the proposed light pole
structures Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site drilled
shaft embedment length of about 3 to 5 feet below the design finish grades may be
used for the light pole foundations
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally
from skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable
compressive load capacity of up to 10 kips per shaft may be used for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as wind or seismic
forces The end bearing component of the drilled shafts should be discounted due to the
potential for caving in of the surficial soils in the upper 3 feet that would preclude
obtaining a clean bottom during drilled shaft installation
