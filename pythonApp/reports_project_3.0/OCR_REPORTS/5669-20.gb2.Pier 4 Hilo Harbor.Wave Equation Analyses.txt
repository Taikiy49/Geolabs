In general accordance with the project contract documents we performed wave
equation analyses to evaluate whether the concrete piles may be driven to the specified
pile tip elevations with the proposed equipment for the above project The wave equation
analyses were conducted based on the information provided by your office the subsurface
conditions contained in the referenced report and project plans and specifications
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The new Pier 4 project is located west of the existing Pier 2 at Hilo Harbor on the
Island of Hawaii The new pier will be approximately 55 feet wide by 600 feet long The
proposed finished deck elevation is set at approximately 9 0 feet Mean Lower Low Water
MLLW
The new concrete pier will be supported on 20 inch octagonal precast prestressed
concrete piles The piles will extend to a tip elevation of approximately 115 feet MLLW for
an allowable capacity of 120 tons The plan pile cast length is approximately 120 feet
The test pile cast length will be 10 feet longer
To examine drivability with the proposed pile driving hammer a wave equation
analysis is required for the 20 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles

Hammer and Driving System
We understand that a Junttan HHS9 hydraulic impact hammer is proposed for the pile
driving operations Based on the pile driving equipment data form provided the Junttan
HHS9 hammer has a ram weight of approximately 19 500 pounds for a maximum rated
energy of 79 200 foot pounds at a maximum stroke of 4 feet
A pile cushion consisting of at least 9 inches of CDX plywood cushion block is
proposed for the pile driving operations The plywood cushion block generally has a cross
section area of approximately 441 square inches for the 20 inch octagonal concrete piles
Schematic details of the driving system are presented on the Pile and Driving Equipment
Data Form in the Appendix Page 1
Pile Details
As mentioned previously the specified concrete piles consist of 20 inch octagonal
precast prestressed concrete piles The test pile cast length is approximately 130 feet It
should be noted that the test piles are casted 10 feet longer than the plan pile cast length
We understand that the effective prestress level of the 20 inch octagonal piles is
approximately 1 390 pounds per square inch psi The piles will be cast from concrete
with a minimum 28 day unconfined compressive strength of 7 000 psi The maximum
allowable compression stress in the 20 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles
can be calculated to be about 4 500 psi The maximum allowable tensile stress in the
20 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles can be calculated to be about
1 600 psi
It should be noted that the maximum allowable compressive stress is defined as
85 percent of the 28 day concrete unconfined compressive strength minus the effective
prestress value while the maximum allowable tensile stress is defined to be three times of
the square root of the 28 day concrete unconfined compressive strength plus the effective
prestress value

Driving Stresses
Graphs showing the computed maximum compression and tensile stresses during
driving are provided on Plates 2 1 and 2 2 for the two analyzed cases Please note that the
graphs were prepared based on kips per square inch ksi which is equivalent to
1 000 psi
Based on our analyses the compression stresses developed during pile driving for
the 20 inch octagonal concrete piles did not exceed the allowable compression stress of
4 500 psi for the full range of driving resistance when using the Junttan HHS9 hydraulic
impact hammer
The tensile stresses developed during the pile driving estimated for the 20 inch
octagonal concrete piles are less than the allowable tensile stress of 1 600 psi for the full
range of driving resistance except when using the stroke heights between 3 and 4 feet
during soft pile driving less than 10 blows per foot
Based on the above results and the anticipated subsurface conditions it is our
professional opinion that the proposed pile driving equipment HHS9 hydraulic hammer is

capable of installing the 20 inch octagonal concrete piles to the specified tip elevation and
achieving the ultimate design compressive capacity without over stressing the concrete

no more than 2 feet during soft pile driving less than 10 blows per foot
A pile may be rejected when the pile driving operation is interrupted for more than
4 hours when the pile cannot be driven to the required depth The wave equation analysis
may be refined after completion of the test pile program to establish production pile
driving criteria for implementation
Based on our analysis we recommend using the following test pile driving criteria
for the 20 inch octagonal concrete test pile installation The pile driving criteria should be
refined after the test pile program for the production pile driving operation
The pile driving operation may be terminated when the pile tip achieves or exceeds
the specified tip elevation of 115 feet MLLW and the driving resistance is greater than
30 blows per foot or at least 4 blows per inch for the last 3 inches while driving with a
Junttan HHS9 hydraulic impact hammer with a stroke height of 4 feet The stroke height
should be reduced to 2 feet when blow counts are less than 10 blows per foot
It should be noted that the bearing layer consisting of basalt rock or very dense
clinker varies across the site between 105 and 115 feet MLLW The pile driving operation
may be terminated above the specified pile tip elevation when the pile tip achieves a
minimum tip elevation of 105 feet MLLW and the driving resistance is at least 8 blows per
inch for the last 3 inches or 12 blows per inch for the last inch of penetration while driving
with a Junttan HHS9 hydraulic impact hammer with a stroke of 4 feet
Care should be carried out during pile driving such as when pile driving resistance
is low or within predrilled depth to reduce the potential for pile damage and high pile
tensile stress The stroke of hydraulic hammer should be carefully controlled to reduce the
potential for pile damage and or over stressing

ABOUT THE WAVE EQUATION ANALYSIS RESULTS
The GRLWEAP program simulates the behavior of a preformed pile driven by
either an impact hammer or a vibratory hammer The program is based on
mathematical models which describe motion and forces of hammer driving
system pile and soil under the hammer action Under certain conditions
the models only crudely approximate often complex dynamic situations
A wave equation analysis generally relies on input data which represents
normal situations In particular the hammer data file supplied with the
program assumes that the hammer is in good working order All of the input
data selected by the user may be the best available information at the time
when the analysis is performed However input data and therefore results
may significantly differ from actual field conditions
Therefore the program authors recommend prudent use of the GRLWEAP results
Soil response and hammer performance should be verified by static and or
dynamic testing and measurements Estimates of bending or other local
non axial stresses and prestress effects must also be accounted for by the
user
The calculated capacity blow count relationship i e the bearing graph
should be used in conjunction with observed blow counts for the capacity
assessment of a driven pile Soil setup occurring after pile installation
may produce bearing capacity values that differ substantially from those
expected from a wave equation analysis due to soil setup or relaxation This
is particularly true for pile driven with vibratory hammers The GRLWEAP user
must estimate such effects and should also use proper care when applying blow
counts from restrike because of the variability of hammer energy soil
resistance and blow count during early restriking
Finally the GRLWEAP capacities are ultimate values They MUST be reduced by
means of an appropriate factor of safety to yield a design or working load
The selection of a factor of safety should consider the quality of the
construction control the variability of the site conditions uncertainties
in the loads the importance of building and other factors
