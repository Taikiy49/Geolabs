2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age
and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau shield
reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of
depositional and erosional events brought on by glacio eustatic sea level changes and
post erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch Following the cessation
of activity of the Koolau Volcano alluvial materials were deposited in a deltaic or
estuarine environment in the area of Pearl Harbor The sea level fluctuated many times
receding perhaps as much as 300 feet below and rising up to 95 feet above the present
sea level
Concurrent with a rise in sea level during the late Pleistocene Era eruption of the
Salt Lake Makalapa member of the Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the older
alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash which consolidated to form volcanic tuff
locally known as mudrock layers Subsequent erosion dissected and thinned those
mantles of tuff During the Recent Epoch as sea level rose to its current stand the area
was covered with lagoonal marine deposits During the construction of the existing
roadways man made fills were placed in localized areas to raise the site to the existing
grades

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Presence of Adobe Clays
Based on our experience in the area and the field exploration results the project
site is generally underlain by adobe clays in the shallow subsurface The
near surface adobe clays encountered within the project limits generally consists
of brownish to light gray clay with sand and gravel These clayey soils which are
typically very sticky and plastic are classified as CH according to the Unified Soil
Classification System These soils generally exhibit high shrink swell potential low
permeability and will lose considerable shear strength when saturated The soils
are known to be susceptible to sliding or creeping on slopes generally greater
than about 4H 1V
Laboratory test results and past experience with adobe clays indicates that the
material generally exhibit very low friction angle on the order of about 15 to
20 degrees Based on the steep back slope above the retaining wall and the low
friction angle of the adobe clays we anticipate the lateral earth pressure acting on
the retaining wall could be on the order of about 80 to 100 pounds per square foot
per foot of depth pcf Therefore we believe that there is a possibility that the
existing retaining wall constructed in the 1960 s may not have been adequately
designed to take into account the high lateral earth pressure exerted by the sloping
adobe clay soils
It should be noted that adobe clays are present in the back slope within the entire
project limits therefore one would expect that the retaining wall would exhibit
similar signs of distresses along the entire wall length especially in the area with
the higher wall height However our field observations indicated that the majority of
the distresses are concentrated toward the Ewa end of the retaining wall where the
retaining height is relatively low Therefore we believe that the presence of the
sloping adobe clays may be a significant but not the sole contributing factor to the
observed distresses at the retaining wall

Presence of Trapped Subterranean Seepage
The project site is situated at the lower Liliha area which is gently sloping uphill
towards the mauka direction until it reaches the base of the mountain ridge The
retaining wall provides the necessary grade adjustment between the upper School
Street and the lower Interstate Route H 1 Highway which was constructed by
excavating into the original ground Therefore the project site is susceptible to high
water infiltration and accumulation from the upslope run off and likely subsurface
seepage during heavy rainfalls It was reported that a significant amount of run off
or seepage could be observed flowing over and through the cracked walls during
periods of heavy rains suggesting that the existing drainage system weepholes
may not be functioning properly to relieve the subterranean water trapped behind
the retaining wall This was supported by the fact that relatively high moisture
contents on the order of about 40 to 50 percent were observed in the soil samples
retrieved during our field exploration The trapped subterranean seepage resulted
in additional hydrostatic pressure that may not have been fully accounted for during
the design of the existing retaining wall resulting in the distresses observed at the
site However we believe that the trapped subterranean seepage is generally a
transient condition and would be a significant but not the primary contributing factor
to the observed distresses at the retaining wall
Presence of Large Monkeypod Trees
Based on our field observations several large Monkeypod trees are present on the
back slope above the retaining wall where the majority of the distresses were
observed In addition to the wall distresses immediately below these Monkeypod
trees significant cracking and upheaval of the curb gutter and pavement could be
observed along School Street directly above these large trees Our field exploration
indicated that the back slope is generally underlain by approximately 6 feet of
adobe clays overlying in situ hard basalt rock formation
Based on our evaluation we speculate that the root system of these large
Monkeypod trees have extended through the upper adobe clays and encountered
resistance when it reached the underlying in situ hard basalt rock formation which

alternative proposed for the project should achieve the following objectives

hydrostatic pressure that might be built up in the back slope resulting from
the trapped subterranean seepage water

back slope above the distressed retaining wall

3 3 Drilled Shaft Retaining Wall
Based on the discussions presented above it is our opinion that the use of drilled
shaft retaining wall will provide the best solution to the existing retaining wall distresses
and address the potential shifting of School Street and underground utilities toward the
highway In this approach a series of closely spaced drilled shafts embedded into the
underlying basalt rock formation is installed along the top of the back slope alongside
School Street The drilled shafts will serve as retaining structures and temporary shoring
would not be required during construction After completion of the drilled shaft

Foundation Loads
Based on the topographic survey map provided the existing grade along School
Street is on the order of about 48 feet MSL Our field exploration indicated that the
top of basalt rock formation generally range from about 11 to 14 feet below the
School Street level Therefore the adobe clays are assumed to extend down to a
depth of about 15 feet below the School Street level for design purpose
Based on a friction angle of 15 degrees and a unit weight of 100 pcf adopted for the
adobe clays a lateral earth pressure of 90 pcf was adopted for the adobe clays
acting on the drilled shaft retaining wall assuming an at rest and saturated
condition In addition traffic loads acting on the drilled shaft retaining wall are
modeled as 2 feet of surcharge fill or about 240 psf With a shaft diameter of
3 5 feet and a shaft spacing of 5 feet the lateral load and bending moment acting at
the top of the drilled shaft were determined to be about 51 kips and 507 kip feet
respectively
Drilled Shaft Foundations
In general lateral load resistance of the drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of
the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of the
shaft and induced moment in the shaft Based on a center to center shaft spacing

considered in our lateral load analyses by including an efficiency factor in the
direction of loading

Construction Considerations
The performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures As a result of these potential
variations a Geolabs representative should observe the installation of drilled shafts
during construction In our opinion the following may have a significant impact on
the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a significant extent on the
friction between the shaft and the basalt rock formation Therefore proper
construction techniques especially during the drilling operations are important The
contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete
into the drilled holes
Based on our field exploration cobbles and boulders were encountered between
the upper adobe clays and the underlying basalt rock formation in the borings
Therefore the drilled shaft contractor will need to have the appropriate equipment
and tools to drill through these obstructions where encountered Appropriate
measures will also be needed to avoid dislodging boulders into the drilled shaft hole
during the drilling and shaft installation process
In addition the drilled shafts are designed to be embedded into the dense basalt
rock formation encountered at depths Therefore coring into the dense basalt rock
formation will be required The drilled shaft contractor will need to demonstrate that
the proposed drilling equipment and coring tools will be capable of installing the
drilled shafts to the recommended depths and dimensions
Based on our field exploration soft and or loose materials were not encountered in
the borings However there exists the potential for presence of soft and or loose
materials along the drilled shaft alignment To reduce the potential for caving in of
the drilled holes temporary casing of the drilled holes may be required during
drilled shaft installation where soft and or loose materials are encountered Care
should be exercised during removal of the temporary casing if used to reduce the
Hawaii California

potential for necking of the drilled shaft Therefore a minimum 5 foot head of
concrete above the bottom of the casing or adequate concrete head to counter
hydrostatic pressures should be maintained during removal of the casing
Groundwater is not anticipated within the depths of the drilled shaft excavations
However the project site is susceptible to high water infiltration and accumulation
from the upslope run off and likely subsurface seepage during heavy rains
Therefore concrete placement by tremie methods may be required during

shaft within three shaft diameters of the recently constructed drilled shaft should
not commence until the concrete for the recently constructed drilled shaft has cured

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling ten borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10 extending to depths of
about 40 1 to 65 5 feet below the existing ground pavement surface The approximate
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig and skid mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous
flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologists who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on the Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on the
Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 10
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the coral formations encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that
uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral formation The material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The test results are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Six Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B 1
Six direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 including two residual direct shear tests
were performed on selected samples to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of
the materials tested Direct shear test results are presented on Plates B 2 through B 7
Five unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on selected
in situ samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on site clayey
soils Individual stress strain curves of the unconfined compression tests are presented
on Plates B 8 through B 12
Ten unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
rock cores to evaluate their unconfined compressive strength of the rock formation
encountered Results of the unconfined compression tests are presented on Plate B 13
