1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Aquatic Feed Research and Pilot Production Facility project at
the UH Hilo s Panaewa Agricultural Farm in Hilo on the Island of Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations
slabs on grade site grading pavements and surface water disposal system only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the
end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is located at the existing University of Hawaii at Hilo s Panaewa
Agriculture Farm in South Hilo about 5 miles from Hilo International Airport It is proposed
to construct a permanent laboratory to conduct testing of aquatic feed formations and
emerging processing technologies at commercially significant scales Based on
information provided we understand that the project site is presently grazing land at the
northeastern end of Panaewa Agricultural Park The project limits are shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2
We understand that a relatively light weight warehouse type structure may be
constructed at the site associated with an access road and parking lot Details for the
proposed structure were not available at the time this report was prepared We envision
that the vehicle loading for the access road and parking areas will consist primarily of
passenger vehicles and light trucks In addition we understand that a drywell system
may be installed for stormwater runoff disposal at the project site

2 1 Regional Geology
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes which include the following Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna Kea
which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801
and Mauna Loa and Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield
Volcano which has been built up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials The near surface lava flows are from the Mauna Loa Volcano
overlying the Mauna Kea Shield The lava flows of the Mauna Kea Shield are believed to
be Pliocene to Pleistocene in age and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua
Volcanic Series The near surface lava flows of the Mauna Loa Shield may be on the
order of only 750 to 1 500 years old
The lava formation at the site appears to be pahoehoe flow which is characterized
by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous
rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava These cavities were formed
when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities
and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava
drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a
large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in
horizontal extent

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration program at the project site consisted of drilling and sampling
5 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 extending to depths ranging from about
20 5 to 26 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the
borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration indicated that the project was generally underlain by a thin
surface ash layer over basalt clinker lava flows The surface ash generally consisted of
medium dense sandy and stiff silty soil materials less than 1 foot thick Based on our field
exploration the project site may be divided into north and south areas At the north area
including borings No B 1 through B 3 the thin surface ash layer was underlain by
moderately weathered moderately to slightly fractured hard vesicular basalt formation
extending to the depth drilled of 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface Relatively
thin clinker was embedded in the basalt rock formation at depths of about 15 to 18 feet
At the south area including borings No B 4 and B 5 the thin surface ash layer
was underlain by 11 to 21 feet of dense clinker The clinker layer was underlain by
slightly weathered to unweathered massive vesicular very hard basalt rock formation
extending to the maximum depth drilled of 26 feet below the existing ground surface

Runoff Disposal Systems
In general basalt is considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit
water quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions Water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and
along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they
serve as the major water transmission features
Based on our field exploration the southern area of the site is underlain by porous
clinker at shallow depth Based on our experience in the vicinity areas we believe that an
average disposal capacity on the order of about 1 to 2 cubic feet per second cfs may be
used for the preliminary design of a typical 5 foot diameter 20 foot deep drywell
Drywells on the Island of Hawaii are typically 5 feet in diameter and 20 feet in
depth The actual size and depth of the stormwater runoff disposal system will depend on
the design quantity of the surface runoff Extension of the drywell from the planned 20 foot
depth may be required depending on the specific site conditions at the proposed drywell
locations Therefore we recommend that unit prices for additional drilling be obtained
during bidding Provisions should be incorporated into the construction documents to
extend the drywell depths from the planned 20 feet to about 40 feet below the ground
surface using a minimum 15 inch diameter extension hole or 5 foot diameter drilling
equipment The unit prices for drywell extension should be obtained in 10 foot increments
Considering the variablility of the subsurface conditions we strongly recommend that
testing of the drywells be performed during construction

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions for this project were explored by drilling and sampling
five borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 extending to depths ranging from
about 20 5 to 26 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the
test borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drill equipment with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our project geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD
would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of
