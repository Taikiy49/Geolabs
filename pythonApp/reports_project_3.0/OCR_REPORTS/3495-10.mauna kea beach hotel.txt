INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Drainage Channel Wall project within the Mauna Kea Beach
Hotel in the Ouli area of South Kohala on the island of Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed
project These recommendations are intended for design input only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the Mauna Kea Beach Hotel in the Ouli area of
South Kohala on the island of Hawaii It was originally proposed to construct a new
U shaped reinforced concrete concrete rubble masonry drainage channel between the
south wing of the Mauna Kea Beach Hotel and the 10th Hole of the Mauna Kea Golf
Course A field exploration was conducted by our office and the findings and
recommendations were presented in the report entitled Geotechnical Engineering
Exploration Mauna Kea Drainage Improvements Mauna Kea Beach Hotel Ouli South
Kohala Hawaii dated
We understand that the original design concept has subsequently been revised to
the construction of a new concrete rubble masonry drainage channel wall along the
existing Beach Access Road Under the new design concept the surface run off from the
golf course area on the east side will be intercepted by the proposed drainage channel
GEOLABS HAWAII

wall and diverted downward along the existing Beach Access Road Based on the
information provided to us the new drainage channel wall will be approximately 4 feet
high 490 feet long and will be designed to resist up to 3 feet of hydrostatic water head

Our previous experience in the area suggest that the proposed drainage channel
wall alignment may be underlain by surficial clinkers over basalt formation at greater
depths Special attention was given to the excavation stability and lateral load resistance
of the proposed drainage channel wall

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series
of Mauna Kea deposited during the Pleistocene Epoch Stearns and Macdonald 1946
The Hamakua Volcanic Series is typically composed of basaltic lava flows which are
mantled by a surface layer of palagonitized ash locally referred to as Pahala Ash
Based on previous experience in the area the lava flows underlying the site are
characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly surface of fragmental clinker underlain by a
dense core of massive basalt which frequently has a thinner less continuous layer of
clinker below the massive core Our experience in the vicinity of the project site indicates
that the upper clinker layers are generally cemented and generally have good strength
characteristics
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located within the Mauna Kea Beach Hotel in the Ouli area of
South Kohala on the island of Hawaii The proposed drainage channel wall alignment is
located along the west side of the Beach Access Road situated approximately between

The existing Beach Access Road is bounded by relatively gentle sloping grounds
of the 10th Hole of the Mauna Kea Golf Course along the east side The terrain along the
west side of the Beach Access Road where the new wall will be located varies
considerably along the entire length In general the majority of the terrain along the west

from about 52 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the north near the entrance to the Beach
Access Road to about 35 to 40 feet MSL at the south near the existing bathroom
facility

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by medium
dense to dense clinkers over basalt cobbles and boulders at greater depths
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration
Based on our field exploration the medium dense to dense clinkers encountered
at the project site should provide adequate support for the proposed drainage channel
wall An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be
used for the foundation design of the concrete rubble masonry drainage channel wall
bearing on the near surface clinkers recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction

In general we believe that the near surface clinkers may be excavated with a
hoptoe backhoe or other conventional heavy construction equipment However
excavations into the deeper formations may require the use of hoerams or chipping
Excavation for the wall footings should be conducted in short sections and backfilled as
soon as possible after the footing construction to reduce the potential for disturbance to
the existing slope that may cause hillside instability Detailed discussion of these items

Foundation Support
The medium dense to dense clinkers encountered at shallow depths in the drilled
borings should provide adequate support for the proposed concrete rubble masonry
drainage channel wall at the project site An allowable bearing value of up to
4 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for the design of the concrete rubble
masonry drainage channel wall bearing on the recompacted near surface clinker
materials The bearing value provided is for dead plus live loads and may be increased
by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind and or seismic forces
The bottom of the wall footings should be recompacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the
bottom of the footing excavations they should be over excavated to expose the
underlying firm materials The over excavation should be backfilled with select granular
fill material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the footing
bottom should be extended down to the underlying competent material Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3495 10 Page 9
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91
test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches and should be embedded
a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finish grade It should be noted that
portions of the drainage channel wall will be located adjacent to steep slopes For footings
located near the top of slopes or on slopes the footings should be embedded deep
enough such that the horizontal distance from the outside edge at the base of the footing
to the edge of the slope is not less than 5 feet If foundations are located next to utility
trenches or easements they should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical
1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as
deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the
potential for foundation settlement
We recommend that foundation excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to construction of the drainage channel wall to confirm foundation
bearing conditions and the embedment depths
Lateral Load Resistance
Based on the information provided to us we understand that the proposed
drainage channel wall will be subjected to approximately 2 to 3 feet of hydrostatic water
pressure from the surface run off For earth retention a lateral earth pressure of
35 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f in the form of equivalent fluid
pressure may be used for the design of the drainage channel wall
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3495 10 Page 10
Lateral loads acting on the drainage channel wall may be resisted by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing materials A
coefficient of friction of 0 45 may be used for foundations bearing on the recompacted
clinker materials encountered at shallow depths
Additional lateral load resistance may be provided by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system For wall footings located
on relatively level areas additional resistance due to passive earth pressure may be
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 450 pounds per square foot per foot of
depth p c f For wall footings located on or near slopes with a gradient of 2H 1V or
flatter a reduced passive earth pressure of 125 p c f equivalent fluid pressure may be
used Resistance due to passive earth pressure should be neglected for wall footings
located on or near slopes with a gradient steeper than 2H 1V These values assume that
the soil around foundations is well compacted or undisturbed Unless covered by
pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be
neglected
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce potential
contamination of the excavated materials Soft and or loose soils encountered during the
clearing operations should be over excavated and disposed of properly off site
The on site clinkers generated from the wall footing excavations may be stockpiled
and reused as a source of backfill material provided they are properly processed as
recommended below Generally the on site soils should be suitable for use as backfill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3495 10 Page 11
provided they are processed and reduced to a relatively well graded select granular
material with a maximum size of about 3 inches Excavated materials greater than
3 inches in diameter may be broken down for use as fill material or should be disposed of
properly off site
Any imported material if needed should be well graded from coarse to fine with
no particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a
laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 12 or more and should have a
maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883 In addition it should contain between 10 and 30 percent passing
the No 200 sieve Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for
conformance with our recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended
use
In general backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs

process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in

Excavation
We anticipate that excavations into the existing ground will be required for the
channel wall construction The method and equipment to be used for excavation should
be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
Generally the near surface clinkers encountered during our exploration could be
excavated by a hoptoe bulldozer or other conventional heavy construction equipment
However excavations into the deeper formations may require the use of hoerams and or
chipping Blasting may cause potential hillside instability and should not be permitted at
the subject site
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based
on field data obtained from the borings and field reconnaissance performed at the subject
site We recommend that contractors be encouraged to examine the site conditions and
boring data to make their own interpretation
Our field exploration indicates that the project site is generally underlain by
medium dense to dense clinkers at shallow depths It is our opinion that temporary cut
slope inclinations of up to 1H 1V or flatter may be used for excavations in the dense
clinkers during construction The temporary excavation should conform to the
requirements of local state and federal regulations Footing excavation should be
conducted in short sections and backfilled as soon as possible after the footing
construction to reduce the potential for disturbance to the existing slope that may cause
hillside instability

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling three
borings to depths ranging from about 3 to 11 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch O D Standard Penetration Sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampier the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Log of Boring as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Log of Boring as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content determinations ASTM D 2216 were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
One sieve analysis ASTM C 136 was performed on a selected sample to
determine the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on Plate C 1
