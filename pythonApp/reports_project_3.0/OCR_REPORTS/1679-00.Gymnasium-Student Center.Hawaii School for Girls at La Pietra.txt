following

Anticipated site grading consists of some major cutting for the new

Purpose and Scope
The purpose of the investigation was to explore the subsurface soil
conditions within the site in order to provide recommendations for
building foundation retaining wall and pavement design as well as
general subsurface soil data for site grading Particular attention was
directed to the back portion of the site where the retaining wall for the
gymnasium and the swimming pool wall would be designed against the
existing slope
The scope of our investigation included field reconnaissance
drilling and sampling of seven 7 borings laboratory testing of soil
samples obtained engineering analysis and report preparation
The boring logs and laboratory test results are presented in the
appendices of the report

Site Conditions
The subject site is located within the Hawaii School for Girls campus
at La Pietra Circle Waikiki Oahu Hawaii The site is bounded to the
east and south by the foothills of Leahi Crater Diamond Head and to
the west and north by the existing school campus
A relatively level pad about 60 feet wide is situated along the
back portion of the site and parallel to the southeastern boundary The
ground surface then slopes downward toward the front northwest of the
site at ratios of 2 1 to 8 1 H V and continues along a level playing
field and basketball court The existing ground surface elevation ranges
from 89 feet at the front of the site to 107 feet toward the rear
Ground cover consisted of well maintained lawn grass and trees along
the slope During our field exploration an abandoned steel ferris wheel
structure with slab on grade foundation was noted in the eastern corner
of the site
Geology
After the Koolau Volcanic Series that built up the northeastern side
of Oahu ceased about 2 2 to 2 5 million years ago MacDonald Abbott
1970 the coastal plain developed along the southern shores of Oahu
during the Pleistocene Epoch from a complex series of erosional and
depositional processes
GEOLABS HAWAIli
W O 1679 00 Page 4
Numerous glacial eustatic sea level variations caused the emergence
and submergence of the land mass which produced coral reef deposits at
various levels both above and below the present sea level
Post erosional volcanic activity renewed about 0 1 to 0 5 million
years ago with more than 30 separate eruptions which poured lava flows
over the eroded topography and out into the fringing reef These
constitute the Honolulu Volcanic Series which created some of the best
known landmarks such as Leahi Crater Diamond Head
Leahi Crater is a tuff cone formed by hydromagmatic explosions It
consists largely of consolidated ash tuff but can contain occasional
blocks of Koolau basalt and numerous fragments of coral Limestone torn
from the underlying reef
In more recent years erosion has cut shallow valleys into the crater
face and deposited thin surface soil layers at the base
Subsurface Conditions
Seven borings were drilled and sampled to depths of 5 1 to 20 feet
below the existing grade at the approximate locations shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2
In general the borings encountered weathered tuff consisting of
dense moderately cemented sandy silt which grades to silty sand with
increasing depth

Discussion and Recommendations
General
In general our soil exploration disclosed competent subsurface
condition for the proposed construction
The underlying weathered tuff can provide suitable bearing for the
proposed new gymnasium with minimal settlements For pavement design
minimum thickness can be used
Site grading work can be performed with the use of conventional site
grading equipment
Building Foundation
For the construction of the new gymnasium we believe that shallow
spread footing bearing on the underlying weathered tuff can be used An
allowable bearing pressure of 4000 pounds per square foot is
recommended The bearing capacity value is for total dead plus live
loads only and may be increased by one third for short duration wind or
seismic loading
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1679 00 Page 6
All footings should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade Resistance to lateral loads for the gymnasium structure
can be obtained by passive pressure developed by the buried part of the
footing and by frictional resistance A frictional coefficient of 0 4
may be used between the bottom of the footing and dense soil In
addition an equivalent fluid pressure of 400 p s f per foot of depth
may be used to determine the passive resistance
The proposed cutting of the hillside will remove a significant amount
of over burden pressure Therefore small settlements are anticipated
for the new gymnasium supported on spread footing Estimated settlements
under the recommended bearing pressure are anticipated to be on the order
of one half of an inch total and one fourth of an inch differential
Slab On Grade
Slab on grade is anticipated for the new gymnasium structure It is
recommended that slab on grade be placed over a 4 inch layer of base
course compacted to 95 of its maximum dry density
The subgrade should be kept moist and protected from drying prior to
concrete placement Subgrade should be compacted to a minimum of 90 of
maximum dry density In order to protect against moisture damage of the
flooring material a polyethylene moisture barrier should be incorporated
within floor slabs When the slab is located below the outside ground
surface a subdrainage system around the perimeter of the walls is
recommended to relieve seepage water

Retaining Wall
Retaining walls are required as part of the construction for the
gymnasium and the swimming pool These walls will be designed against
the existing hillside slope
We recommend that the retaining wall excavation and construction be
made in sections and backfilled as soon as practicable For the
temporary cut slope behind the wall a gradient of up to H V may
be used
Well graded granular material such as base course rock sand or
equivalent shouid be used as backfill material directly behind the
retaining walls This would minimize the compactive effort required for
the backfill and reduce the build up of additional lateral pressures on
the walls
Based on the above granular backfill we recommend that the walls
should be designed to resist the following lateral earth pressures
Unrestrained at top equivalent
active condition fluid pressure
Restrained at top equivalent
at rest condition fluid pressure
Additional loads due to surcharge should be included where
applicable
A coefficient of friction of 0 4 between the wall and the backfill
material can be used

Site Grading
Site grading for this project is anticipated to consist of some ma jor
cutting for the new gymnasium structure and some minor cut and fill work
for the remaining construction items Also large amount of excavation
is anticipated for the construction of the swimming pool
In general weathered tuff consisting of moderately cemented dense
silty sand was encountered at the proposed finished elevation of the new
gymnasium and the swimming pool Based on a review of available geologic
data the method of drilling and sampling techniques used it is our
opinion that the underlying weathered tuff may be excavated with
conventional site grading equipment such as a D 9 bulldozer or
equivalent Sone localized hard zones or large boulders may require
special techniques such as the use of a hoeram
Pavement Design
Based on the result of our soil exploration we believe that the
on site soil should perform as competent subgrade material Therefore
assuming a medium to light traffic condition the following pavement
section is recommended for the parking improvement and revetment area

Design Review
Plans and specifications for the proposed construction should be
forwarded to the soils engineer for review and written comments prior to

Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the excavation operations and backfilling of
the retaining walls be observed by the soiis engineer during
construction This is to see that an adequate bearing has been obtained
and the subsurface conditions are consistent with the findings of our
investigation The recommendations given in this report are contingent
upon the above inspections
Imported materials required should be as specified in the above
sections tested and approved by the soils engineer prior to hauling to
the site
IT conditions encountered during construction are different from
those presented in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made
