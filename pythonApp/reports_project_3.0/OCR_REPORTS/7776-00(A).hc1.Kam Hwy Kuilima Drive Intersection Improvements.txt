REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu is comprised largely of the weathered remnants of the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of the western 
third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms majority of the eastern 
two-thirds of the island.  It is believed that the Waianae Volcano became extinct while the 
Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas 
in central Oahu. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.  When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters

protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the 
sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous 
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and 
induration of the calcareous reef materials.  
The project site lies on the Kahuku Coastal Plain and the pediment of the northern 
end of the Koolau Mountain Range. The project area is generally classified from a 
geological standpoint as ‘Qa’ for Quaternary Alluvium (Sinton, et.al.) which consists of 
alluvial soils deposited since the Pleistocene Epoch.  Experience at the Turtle Bay Resort 
complex indicates that this alluvial material forms a thin mantle over residuum from 
Koolau Basalt at the southern side of the resort and over coralline and dune sand deposits 
to the north of Kamehameha Highway. 
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is situated at the intersection of Kamehameha Highway and 
Kuilima Drive in the Kahuku area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The existing single span 
Kuilima-Oio Stream Bridge is located east of the intersection of Kamehameha Highway 
and Kuilima Drive. Both Kamehameha Highway and Kuilima Drive are roadways paved 
with asphaltic concrete and appear to be in fair to good condition. Based on our field 
observations, the project site is relatively flat.  
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Three bulk soil samples, designated as Bulk-101 through Bulk-103, were retrieved 
from depths of about 0.5 to 1.5 feet below the existing ground surface at the locations 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. Based on our field observations and experience in the 
area, the pavement subgrade soils will likely consist of dense silty sands and stiff sandy silts 
with a little gravel and silty clays. Results of the laboratory tests performed on the selected 
soil samples obtained from our field exploration are presented in Appendix A, Plates A-1 
through A-5. 
Based on our previous field explorations performed for the proposed replacement 
bridge location, the project site is generally underlain by a surface layer of streambed 
deposit, consisting of medium stiff to stiff silty clays, of approximately 1 to 5 feet thick. Below 
the surface layer, our borings generally encountered coralline detritus materials consisting 
of very loose coralline gravel overlying medium dense to dense silty sands extending to 
depths of about 30 to 40 feet below the existing streambed elevation. Hard basalt rock 
formation interbedded with clinker layers were then encountered and extended to the 
maximum depth explored of approximately 80 feet below the existing ground surface. It 
should be noted that very dense zones and boulder layers were encountered at various 
depths within the coralline detritus deposit in the borings drilled for the project. 
Shallow groundwater levels were encountered in the borings drilled at depths ranging 
from about 2.4 to 12.3 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field 
exploration. The groundwater levels measured generally correspond to Elevations –0.3 to

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the laboratory R-value test results obtained for the bulk samples and the 
traffic parameters provided, we have performed additional pavement analyses and updated 
our pavement design recommendations. Detailed discussions and recommendations for 
pavement design are presented in the following sections. 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
It is proposed to widen Kamehameha Highway to provide designated turn lanes in 
to and from Kuilima Drive as part of the Kamehameha Highway/Kuilima Drive Intersection 
Improvements project. The widened and reconstructed pavement sections should be 
designed and constructed as new pavement sections.  The procedures used in 
determining the new pavement section were generally based on the methodology 
described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual dated March 2002. The 
Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department of 
Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research Branch. The 
pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer method developed 
and used by the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans). 
We understand that it is also desired to rehabilitate and/or reconstruct Kuilima 
Drive as part of the Kamehameha Highway/Kuilima Drive Intersection Improvements 
project. In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement areas 
would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing pavements should 
be performed. Existing pavements with minor distresses are generally rehabilitated with 
an AC resurfacing with cold-planing.  It is our recommendation that severely distressed 
pavements be reconstructed as new pavement sections.

The pavement subgrade should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and 
recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers 
to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of 
the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO-180 or ASTM D1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry 
density. Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding should 
not be allowed to compact the materials. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and 
scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to 
those encountered in our field exploration. 
Aggregate subbase course materials required for the project should conform to 
Subsection 703.17 of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge 
Construction (2005).  Aggregate subbase material should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction.

Rehabilitation of Existing Pavements 
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement areas 
would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing pavements should 
be performed.  Existing pavements with minor distresses are generally rehabilitated with 
cold-planing and AC resurfacing.  Based on our evaluation, it is our opinion that pavement 
areas with minor distresses may be rehabilitated with an AC resurfacing consisting of 
cold-planing 2.0 inches of the existing pavements and replacing with 2.0 inches of new 
AC overlay. 
In general, the contractor should vacuum-sweep the pavement areas immediately 
after cold-planing to remove gravel-sized materials and dust not picked up by the 
cold-planing operation.  It should be noted that the vacuum-sweep should not preclude 
the dust control operation during cold-planing. In general, the contractor should begin 
paving no later than three working days after cold-planing begins.  Finally, vacuum 
sweeping of cold-planed surfaces should also be performed immediately prior to paving. 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be maintained 
to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. Surface water 
ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development of good 
shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage structures 
is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or premature 
failure of the pavements.
