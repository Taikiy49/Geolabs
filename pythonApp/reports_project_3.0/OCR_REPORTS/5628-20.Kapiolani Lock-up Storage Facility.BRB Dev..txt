In accordance with our fee proposal dated October 5, 2006, we have performed
shear wave velocity testing in support of the detailed design of the building.
This letter report summarizes the additional field exploration performed and our
findings and recommendations in support of the seismic design of the building planned for
the project. These findings and recommendations are intended for design input only and
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report.
Project Considerations
The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map,
Plate 1. An eight-story storage structure is planned. Geolabs prepared a geotechnical
engineering report entitled “Geotechnical Engineering Exploration, Kapiolani Lock-Up
Storage Facility, 1901 Kapiolani Boulevard, Honolulu, Oahu, Hawaii” dated
October 4, 2006 for the proposed development.
Due to the relatively poor subsurface soil conditions, it is desired to perform shear
wave velocity profiling at the site in an effort to more closely analyze the seismic design
considerations. We used Cone Penetrometer Testing (CPT) with seismic shear wave
velocity tests for the additional field exploration. The CPT method involves inserting a
sensitive probe into the soil at constant speed. In general, subsurface explorations
consisting of CPT yields more accurate results for highly sensitive materials such as the
loose granular soils because the data is obtained directly from the in-situ soils. Therefore,
CPT with seismic shear wave velocity profiling is considered to be the industry standard
for higher order analysis, such as the detailed seismic analyses, especially in these
subsurface conditions.

Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our work on this project was to perform seismic shear wave velocity
testing of the subsurface materials. The seismic shear wave velocity profiling, extending
down to a depth of about 133.7 feet below the ground level, was conducted using seismic
cone penetration testing equipment. The scope of work included the following tasks and
work efforts:
1. Mobilization and demobilization of a truck-mounted drill rig and
two operators to and from the project site.
2. Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the cone penetration test
probe by our engineer.
3. Setting-up of the seismic cone penetration testing equipment.
4. Performance of seismic cone penetration test to a depth of approximately
133.7 feet below the existing ground surface. Backfilling of the probe hole
after drilling and seismic SCPT work.
5. Analyses of the field data to formulate an average shear wave velocity and a
“Soil Profile” for seismic analysis for the project.
6. Preparation of this letter report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and recommendations of a “Soil Profile” for seismic
analysis of the project.
7. Coordination of our overall work on the project by our engineer.
8. Quality assurance of our work and client/design team consultation by our
principal engineer.
Field Exploration
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling using (CPT) equipment was performed in an
effort to more closely analyze the seismic design considerations for the project site. Shear
wave velocity testing using the cone penetration test method was performed at a testing
location, designated as CPT-1, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The test location was
the same as the test boring location designated as B-102 in our geotechnical engineering
report. The test was performed to depth of about 133.7 feet below the existing ground
surface using seismic cone testing equipment (SCPT). After coring at the location, the
bored hole was backfilled with 0.25-inch coated bentonite pellets using the casing as a
tremie pipe. When the bentonite pellets encountered water, they swelled and softened as
a coupling medium from the cone penetrometer to surrounding soil.

The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10-ton
steel electronic subtraction cone with an apex angle of 60 degrees and an area of
1.55 square inches (10 square centimeters) into the ground. The depth of penetration was
recorded at about 2-inch (5-centimeter) intervals using a depth sensor attached to the
cone rod.
The cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the arrival of a shear
wave propagated from the ground surface. The seismic measurements are made when
the SCPT has stopped and a shear wave is sent into the subsurface. A shear wave is
generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a switched hammer. The
propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone was measured at each
depth interval. The vector difference of these depths divided by the time difference
provided the average shear wave velocity over the depth interval. The shear wave
velocity profile measured at the probe location is presented on Plates 3.1 and 3.2.
Soil Profile Type
The shear wave velocities measured and the weighted average shear wave
velocity for the top 101.4 feet of the soil profile at the location is presented on Plate 3. The
weighted average shear wave velocity was calculated based on the average shear wave
velocity method of the Uniform Building Code (Section 1636.2.1, 1997 Edition).
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, the
weighted average shear wave velocity was measured at about 1,190 feet per second.
Based on the shear wave velocity test results, we believe the project site may be classified
from a seismic analysis standpoint as being a “Stiff Soil’ site corresponding to an Sp soil
profile type based on the Uniform Building Code Table No. 16-J, 1997 Edition.
Limitations
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this letter report are based, in
part, upon information obtained from the seismic cone penetration test. Variations of
subsurface soil conditions and shear wave velocities between and beyond the test probes
may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until
construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to
reevaluate the recommendations provided in this letter report.
The location of the field cone penetration test indicated in this report is
approximate, having been taped from features shown on the Site Plan prepared by
Sullivan Goulette Architects on April 24, 2006. The physical location of the cone
penetration test should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method
used.
