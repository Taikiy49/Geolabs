SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered surface fills placed over lagoonal 
deposits, alluvium, and weathered limestone at the SPS-09 and SPS-23 project sites. In 
general, the granular portions of the submerged fill and lagoonal deposits are loose and 
are susceptible to soil liquefaction when subjected to a peak ground acceleration of 
0.65g corresponding to the Maximum Considered Earthquake at the project sites. The 
consistency and quality of the underlying weathered limestone encountered is 
anticipated to vary with increasing depth. Groundwater was encountered at an elevation 
of approximately +0.1 meters MLLW at SPS-09 and SPS-23. 
Generally, we recommend using 406-mm square precast prestressed concrete 
piles with an allowable compressive load capacity of 355 kN per pile to support the 
proposed structures. To develop the recommended pile capacity of 355 kN, a pile 
embedment of approximately 25 meters (SPS-09) or 29 meters (SPS-23) below the 
existing ground surface may be required. Based on the subsurface conditions 
encountered, we anticipate some minor predrilling of the surface materials may be 
needed to assist in setting the piles. Therefore, we recommend predrilling the pile 
locations to a depth not deeper than 6 meters to facilitate setting of the piles only. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Sanitary Sewer System Recapitalization project (FY16 
MCON Project P-535) at Apra Harbor Naval Base in Guam. The project location and 
general site vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the proposed project. These recommendations are intended for the design of 
foundations, floor slabs, concrete pads, earthwork, and utilities only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report.  
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The proposed design-build project involves upgrading the existing sanitary sewer 
system at Naval Base Guam, which includes the Navy Main Base, Nimitz Hill, Naval 
Hospital, Polaris Point, Navy Water Treatment Plant, Ordnance Annex, and Apra Palms 
Housing. In general, the project involves design and construction of new sewer pump 
stations, new force mains, and replacement/rehabilitation of existing sewer lines and 
manholes. Sewer pump station SPS-09 will include a new generator/electrical building and 
sewer pump station SPS-23 will include a new electrical building. The new sewer pump 
station at the Apra Palms housing area will not include a new building.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
The Island of Guam is the largest and southernmost of the Mariana Island 
archipelago, which is an island arc parallel to the trend of the subduction zone forming 
the Mariana Trench. The Mariana Trench is part of a belt of trenches extending from 
Japan to the Palau Islands including the Japan Trench, Mariana Trench, Yap Trench, 
and Palau Trench. 
The Island of Guam can be broadly divided into three geologic provinces: 
the southern mountains, the central mountains, and the northern plateau. The island 
formation started during the Eocene Epoch by the eruption of basaltic rhyolite lavas. 
The mountainous central portion of the island is composed of these Eocene volcanic 
rocks and conglomerates, tuffs, and limestones of the Eocene and Oligocene Epochs. 
Lava flows and limestone formations of the Miocene Epoch make up the majority 
of the southern one-third of the island. The plateaus of the northern portion of the island 
were formed while this part of the island was still beneath the sea level. During the 
Oligocene and Miocene Epochs, a complex series of coral and algal reefs formed an 
elongated platform of limestones and related deposits. Tectonic activity has lifted these 
coral-algal limestones above sea level to form the northern Guam Plateau. 
The central portion of the island consists of the transition from the volcanic 
materials of the southern mountains to the limestone formations of the northern plateau. 
The project site at Apra Harbor is located in the south-central portion of the island. The 
local geology is dominated by the volcanic materials and associated materials of the 
southern mountains. Due to surface drainage courses of the southern mountain terrain, 
the near surface soils in the area consist of Holocene age alluvium and associated 
deposits, such as coralline detritus, alluvium, and lagoonal deposits. These younger 
unconsolidated soil deposits generally are more susceptible to liquefaction and slope 
stability issues, especially around the shoreline areas. A geology map of the area is 
presented on Plate 3.

2.3 
Seismic Design Considerations 
The project site will be subjected to seismic activity and should be evaluated for 
the potential for soil liquefaction. Seismic design of the proposed project will be based 
on the Unified Facilities Criteria “Seismic Design of Buildings” (UFC 3-310-04), which 
references the 2015 International Building Code (IBC 2015) and UFC Structural 
Engineering (UFC 3-301-01). 
2.3.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
Guam is situated adjacent to the subduction zone that forms the Mariana Trench. 
The island is tectonically active and has experienced approximately 
20 earthquakes exceeding a magnitude of 7.0 since 1902. Of these events, 
fifteen occurred prior to 1950 and five have occurred subsequent to that date. 
Several of the larger faults and fault zones that traverse the Island of Guam 
include the following: 
Pago Bay - Adelup Point Fault,  
Santa Rita - Talofofo River Valley Fault Zone, and 
Tamuning - Barrigada Fault. 
In addition to the larger faults, several faults and fault zones within proximity to 
the project site include the Cabras Fault and Cocos Fault. 
2.3.2 Liquefaction Potential 
Liquefaction is a phenomenon during which loose, saturated, cohesionless soils 
(such as sands and non-plastic silts) temporarily lose their shear strengths due to 
the build-up of excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress 
applications induced by earthquakes. In this process, when the loose saturated 
sand deposit is subjected to vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil 
tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water 
pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up

of pore water pressure, the effective stress (internal strength) of the soil is 
reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could 
equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the soil shear strength to 
zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil 
acquires sufficient mobility to permit both horizontal and vertical movements, and 
if not confined, will result in significant deformations. 
Significant factors known to affect the liquefaction potential of soils are the 
characteristics of the materials such as grain size distribution and relative 
density; the plasticity of the fine-grained soils, the degree of saturation; the initial 
stresses (overburden pressures) acting on the soils; and the characteristics of 
the earthquakes such as the intensity and duration of ground shaking. The major 
factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of soil deposits are as follows: 
1. 
Grain Size Distribution - Fine and uniform sands and silts are more 
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well-graded sands. 
2. 
Initial Relative Density - Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to 
liquefaction. Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative 
density. 
3. 
Magnitude and Duration of Vibration - Liquefaction potential is directly 
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake. 
Because the project site will be subjected to seismic activity, we have evaluated the 
potential for soil liquefaction at the project site based on a peak ground acceleration 
of 0.65g (corresponding to the Maximum Considered Earthquake with a return 
period of 2,475 years). The subsurface conditions at the site generally consist of 
surface fills, lagoonal deposits, coralline detritus, alluvium and weathered limestone 
formation. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, the granular portions 
of the submerged fills, coralline detritus, and non-clayey lagoonal deposits 
generally are susceptible to liquefaction when subjected to a peak ground 
acceleration of 0.65g. In the event of soil liquefaction caused by a peak ground 
acceleration of 0.65g, the estimated seismically induced ground settlement due to 
soil liquefaction is 100 mm and 155 mm at SPS-9 and SPS-23, respectively.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the presence of potentially 
liquefiable lagoonal deposits, we recommend using a deep foundation system 
consisting of driven concrete piles deriving axial support from a combination of skin 
friction in the coralline detritus, lagoonal deposits (non-liquefiable), alluvium, and 
weathered limestone formation and/or end-bearing in the weathered limestone 
formation to support the two new buildings at the project site. Generally, we recommend 
using 406-mm square precast prestressed concrete piles with an allowable compressive 
load capacity of 355 kN per pile to support the proposed structures.  
To develop the recommended pile capacity of 355 kN, we anticipate a pile 
embedment of approximately 25 meters likely will be required for the generator building 
at SPS-09. For the electrical building at SPS-23, we anticipate the pile embedment 
likely will be about 29 meters below the existing ground surface. In addition, we 
anticipate subjecting each of the driven piles to a lateral load of approximately 35 kN 
with a corresponding moment demand of about 52 kN-meters due to seismic loading. 
Therefore, the piles will need to be structurally reinforced to provide the proper bending 
strength to withstand the lateral load and moment demands acting on the foundation 
elements. 
Detailed discussions of our geotechnical recommendations for design of the 
buildings at SPS-09 and SPS-23 are presented in the following sections of this report.

coralline detritus, lagoonal deposits (non-liquefiable), alluvium, and weathered 
limestone formation and/or end-bearing in the weathered limestone formation to support 
the two new structures. Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions, piles extending

3.1.2 Lateral Load Resistance 
Lateral loads imposed on the foundations may be resisted by a combination of 
the resistance of the pile-soil system to deflect and passive pressure resistance 
on the front side of pile caps and grade beams. For passive pressure resistance 
on the front face of pile caps and grade beams, an equivalent fluid pressure of 
70.7 kN per square meter per meter of depth may be used. Due to the potential 
for seismically induced ground settlement, the passive earth pressure 
contribution from the upper 100 mm and 155 mm of the ground should be 
neglected in the calculations at SPS-09 and SPS-23, respectively. 
Lateral load analyses were performed using the program LPILE-plus for 
Windows, which is a microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference, laterally 
loaded pile program originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin. The 
program solves for deflection and bending moment along a pile under lateral 
loads as a function of depth. The analysis was carried out with the use of 
non-linear “p-y” curves to represent soil moduli. The lateral deflection was then 
computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths.

3.1.5 Pile Hammer Selection 
Based on our experience, we recommend using a pile driving hammer having a 
maximum rated energy of about 40,000 N-meters to 108,000 N-meters (about 
30,000 to 80,000 foot-pounds), such as a Delmag D30 diesel hammer, or a 
Junttan HHK 5A hydraulic impact hammer, or similar pile driving hammers, to install 
the piles. Wave equation analysis should be performed to demonstrate that the 
proposed pile driving hammer and systems are capable of driving the piles to the 
design tip elevations without overstressing or damaging the piles. To avoid 
overstressing the piles during driving, plywood cushions with an uncompacted 
(fresh) thickness of about 200 to 300 mm or some other equivalent cushion blocks 
should be used at all times. 
The hammer should be equipped with an energy level control such that driving 
within the loose and/or soft soils may be controlled at a lower energy to reduce 
tensile stress development, which could damage or cause cracking of the piles. 
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into 
consideration the “matching” of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be 
driven. This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required 
energy to the top of the pile and also reducing damage to the piles. Geolabs 
should review the pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate if the equipment is 
capable of installing the piles to the recommended capacity. The contractor 
should forward pile and driving equipment data forms to Geolabs prior to 
construction for evaluation. Geolabs should review the information and assess 
the driveability of piles with the use of the selected hammer.

The dewatering method selected should have minimal impact on the groundwater 
level surrounding the proposed excavation. The dewatering operations should be 
coordinated with the shoring support such that the stability of the excavations is not 
jeopardized. The operations should be carried-out without softening the bottom of 
the excavations. 
3.7.3 Dewatering Considerations 
We suggest considering the following three basic criteria in selection of a suitable 
method of dewatering: 
a. 
The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or 
damage to existing buildings, roads, and environment. 
b. 
The dewatering method should maintain stability of, and also provide 
safe and dry working conditions in the excavation. 
c. 
The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow 
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions. 
3.7.4 Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring 
Dewatering activities should be continuously monitored to observe the effects of 
dewatering during construction and to implement, as soon as possible, necessary 
precautionary or remedial measures including, but not limited to, slowing down or 
stopping the dewatering operations. 
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations, permeable granular soils may be 
susceptible to piping and “quick” conditions. The dewatering operations should be 
carried-out without creating a “quick” condition or softening at the excavation 
bottoms. Therefore, the project dewatering operations should be performed without 
pumping out soil fines (pumping clear water only) and should be coordinated with 
the shoring installation (if needed) such that the excavation stability is not adversely 
affected. Excessive pumping, which removes soil fines, may result in “blowing” or 
heaving of the excavation bottom or sides.

3.8

Minimum resistivity, pH, chloride content and sulfate content tests were 
performed to evaluate the corrosivity of the in-situ soils at the site. We performed the pH 
and minimum resistivity tests on selected soil samples in general accordance with 
ASTM G51 and ASTM G57, respectively. The chloride and sulfate content tests were 
performed by Test America in general accordance with the EPA 300.0 test methods. 
Results of the corrosivity tests are presented on Plate B-11 in Appendix B.  
Resistivity generally is recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. 
Generally, a lower resistivity results in a greater potential for corrosion of buried metallic 
objects. Conversely, a higher resistivity means the soil is less likely to contribute to 
corrosion of metallic objects. Based on the resistivity values of the in-situ soils 
encountered, the subsurface materials may be classified as moderately corrosive to 
corrosive for buried metallic structures. A corrosion engineer should be consulted for 
detailed recommendations on corrosion protection of metallic substructures, if 
appropriate. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and

soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored further by drilling and 
sampling two deep borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 and 102, extending to depths 
of about 30.0 and 31.5 meters below the existing ground surface, respectively. The 
borings were drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 200-mm hollow stem 
augers. The borings were drilled at the approximate location shown on the Site Plan, 
Plates 2.1 and 2.2. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. 
Rock materials were described in general accordance with the Rock Description 
System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System 
is based on the publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of 
Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 
1977). Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs 
of Borings, Plates A-1.1 thru A-2.4. 
The majority of the soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general 
accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by 
driving a 50-mm (2-inch) OD standard penetration sampler using a 63.5-kg (140-pound) 
hammer falling 460 mm (30 inches). In addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil 
samples were obtained in general accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel 
Sampling of Soils, by driving a 75-mm (3-inch) OD modified California sampler with the 
same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and 
third 152-mm (6-inch) of a 460-mm (18-inch) drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil and limestone properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and 
graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Seven Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. The fines content of the sample tested (percent particles passing the 
No. 200 sieve) is indicated on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Two fines content tests (ASTM D1140) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve and to aid in soil 
classification. The fines content of the sample tested is indicated on the Logs of Borings 
at the appropriate sample depths and summarized on Plate B-4. We also included the 
fines content from the seven sieve analysis tests on the plates for ease of reference. 
Two unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression tests (ASTM D2850) were 
performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strengths of the 
soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressures were used as the applied 
confining pressures for the relatively “undisturbed” soil samples. The test results and the 
stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-5 and B-6. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557, Method C) were 
performed on bulk soil samples to evaluate the relationship between the moisture 
content and the dry density of the near-surface soils. The test results are presented on 
Plates B-7 and B-8. 
Two laboratory soaked California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were 
performed on bulk soil samples to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the 
soils. The bulk soil samples were remolded to obtain a relative compaction of 
approximately 100 percent (in accordance with ASTM D1557) at about the optimum 
moisture content. The test results are presented on Plates B-9 and B-10. 
Two sets of pH (ASTM G51) and minimum resistivity (ASTM G57) were 
performed by our laboratory on selected soil samples obtained from our field 
exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-11.
