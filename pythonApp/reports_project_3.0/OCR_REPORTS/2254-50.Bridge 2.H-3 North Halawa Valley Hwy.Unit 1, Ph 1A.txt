INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our additional geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed Bridge No 2 structure along the H 3 North
Halawa Valley Highway in Halawa Valley on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our additional field exploration and
laboratory testing and presents our design level geotechnical engineering recom
mendations derived from our analyses for the Bridge No 2 structure These recommen
dations are intended for design input only and may not be appropriate to form the basis
for construction cost estimates nor for construction bidding purposes
PROJECT BACKGROUND AND CONSIDERATIONS
The Unit 1 portion of the North Halawa Valley Highway extends from Station
376 00 northwestern side of Halawa Quarry to Station 480 00 approximately two 2
miles into the Valley The Phase 1A portion of the project approximately one mile long
includes Station 374 25 through Station 415 00
The proposed highway alignment generally follows the bottom of the valley
crossing Halawa Stream at several locations including the Bridge No 2 structure
addressed in this report The proposed bridge will be approximately 81 feet wide and will
accommodate 4 lanes of vehicular traffic The bridge structure will be a two span bridge
each span 100 feet in length supported on each end by the abutments and a center pier
We understand that the center pier foundation will be placed at an elevation of about
160 feet Mean Sea Level MSL due to scour depths on the order of 30 feet at this
location The bridge abutment foundations are outside the lateral design scour limits
therefore they may be placed at a higher elevation of about 190 feet MSL

SITE DESCRIPTION
The proposed Bridge No 2 structure is located in the vicinity of the existing
temporary Bridge No 1 at about Station 399 00 of the Phase 1A portion of the H 3
North Halawa Valley Highway project The existing Halawa Stream bed is about 20 to 40
feet wide and the stream flows intermittently Numerous boulders were observed in the
stream bed and along the banks
In the flood plain adjacent to the stream the ground surface is gently sloping
However the ground surface steepens rapidly up the hillside Except for a recently paved
access road the remaining area is heavily vegetated with trees and shrubs
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
In general the subsurface conditions encountered in the two borings drilled for this
additional exploration designated as Boring Nos 10E and 10F may be categorized into
four groups
Koolau Basalt
Koolau Basalts consist of thinly bedded basaltic flows with an average flow unit
thickness of 10 feet or less The flows occur both in the a a and pahoehoe
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 50 Page 7
forms with transitions between the two forms The lavas are generally vesicular
with jointing resulting from contraction during cooling and solidification of the
molten rock The Koolau Basalts grade from extremely weathered saprolites to
relatively unweathered rock with increasing depth
The saprolites are soil materials derived from the in situ chemical weathering of the
parent basaltic rocks These soils range from structureless residual clayey silts or
silty clays to saprolites extremely weathered basalt which are soils that retain the
relict structure of the parent rock such as joints vesicles and or flow structures
Older Alluvium
These materials are deposited during the later Pleistocene and are poorly sorted
alluvium with significant amounts of boulders In situ weathering and induration
have altered some of the materials into a highly weathered poorly consolidated
conglomerate Core samples of these older alluvium are typified by slightly to
highly weathered boulders cobbles and gravel in a matrix of cemented sandy silt
Recent Alluvium
Recent alluvium is generally composed of terrestrial sediments deposited by the
movement of water and is mostly silts and clays with gravel cobbles and boulders
The deposits are poorly sorted highly variable in consistency density and were
encountered in the upper portion of our drilled borings
Man Mace Fill
This unit consists of miscellaneous materials placed for site grading and part of the
construction of the pioneer and access roads for the H 3 Highway project

Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on the bridge foundations can be resisted by a combination
of the following
1 resistance of the drill shaft soil system to deflect and
2 passive resistance against the near vertical faces of pier caps

Lateral load resistance for drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of shaft and
induced moment in the shaft
Due to the potential for stream scour the lateral load resisting capacity of the
upper 9 feet of soil for the center pier has been neglected in our analysis We have also
assumed the soil in front of the abutment pier cap is sloping downward at an inclination
of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V in our analysis
We have evaluated the lateral load resistance of the shafts using the computer
program PILEDG The PILEDG program is based on the algorithm in the computer
program COM622 developed at the University of Texas Austin by L C Reese Effect
of group action was considered in our analysis by reducing the effective horizontal
modulus of subgrade reaction in the direction of loading A factor of 0 25 times the
horizontal modulus of subgrade reaction for a single shaft was used based on a drilled
shaft spacing of 2 5 times the shaft diameter center to center

Uplift Load Resistance
Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead load of the drilled shaft
and by shear along the shaft surface and the adjacent soils For intermittent uplift forces
an ultimate uplift capacity of 200 and 450 kips may be used to resist uplift loads for the
drilled shafts at the abutments and center pier respectively

be approximately 12 inch or less

Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable to the project because of the significant increase in
lateral and vertical load capacities when compared to driven piles However the
performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s method of
construction and construction procedures In our opinion the following conditions would
have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled pier foundations
Cobbles and Boulders
The subsoils at the bridge site consist predominantly of alluvial soils comprising of
boulders and cobbles in a soil matrix Drilling through the boulders and cobbles
is a slow process for any type of deep foundations To reduce the potential for
caving in of the holes temporary casing may be needed Potential difficulties such
as voids or inadequate shaft concrete may arise due to improper removal of the
temporary casing during concrete placement The anticipated high groundwater
levels may also pose further construction difficulties since proper observation of
the sides and bottom of the shaft may not be possibile
Proximity to Halawa Stream Wet Environment
The project site is adjacent to the Halawa Stream and nearly at the bottom of
Halawa Stream The general area is wet with frequent rains With heavy
rainstorms the water level of the Halawa Stream rises rapidly and could flood the
construction site causing delays We recommend that some protection measures
be implemented by the contractor to reduce the potential of flooding of the
construction areas due to heavy rain water runoff
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 50 Page 13
The weather conditions may also pose a potential severe impact on the
construction scheduling and the quality of the shaft construction The possibility
of a heavy storm and high water levels in Halawa Stream during or immediately
after construction of a drilled shaft will need to be considered
Excavation
To construct the drilled shafts and pier cap for the center pier an excavation on
the order of 20 feet would be required below the stream bed elevation An open
cut excavation may be feasible subject to archaeological and environmental
requirements If an open cut excavation is elected temporary side slopes of one
horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V or flatter may be used The side slopes should
be adequately protected against erosion especially during heavy rains which may
flood the excavation to reduce potential instability of the side slopes The
contractor should retain a qualified geotechnical engineer to evaluate the method
of excavation and or shoring of the excavation
It should be emphasized that it is the responsibility of the contractor to provide a
safe and stable excavation for the construction workers It should be noted that
the abundant cobbles and boulders in the stream bed and in the excavated
materials may pose a falling boulder hazard within the excavation Therefore
shoring of the sides of the excavation may be prudent
The excavation shoring system used must comply with all applicable safety
requirements The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made
the sole responsibility of the contractor His representative who should be
required to be present on site during excavation and construction work will have
the best opportunity to promptly observe any changing conditions during
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 50 Page 14
construction such as unforeseen subsoils unexpectedly high groundwater table
inappropriate construction sequences or techniques etc which may affect shoring
stability
However it must be noted that some movement of the shoring system and the
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during
excavation The magnitude of movement is difficult to accurately predict due to the
complexity of the stress changes The magnitude depends greatly upon
workmanship and also on the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and
the rate of excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical if necessary and that the adjacent ground
should be continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of
movements with instruments such as settlement markers and inclinometers Such
monitoring is particularly critical in areas adjacent to existing facilities and utilities
Dewatering
Groundwater was encountered in our borings at depths of approximately 11 to 12
feet below the surface corresponding to elevations 176 to 179 feet MSL Since
the bottom of pier cap is at an elevation of 160 feet MSL groundwater will be
encountered in the excavation requiring dewatering to provide a dry excavation in
which to work Therefore we recommend that dewatering provisions be included
in the contract documents for the construction of the proposed bridge structure
Furthermore periodic heavy rains may cause flooding of the excavation
Therefore these conditions should be anticipated by the contractor and provisions
should be incorporated in the construction plan to reduce their impacts and delays
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2254 50 Page 15
The method and equipment to be used for the excavation dewatering should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents
should be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural
groundwater table and or to exclude the water from the excavations to allow
construction of the proposed structures under safe and dry conditions We
suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selecting a suitable
method of dewatering
1 The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance and or
damage to existing facilities and the environment
2 The dewatering method should maintain stability of the sides of the
excavation and also provide safe and dry working conditions in the
excavation
3 The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions
These systems may include but are not limited to well pumping grouting cut off
walls tremie plugs or any combination of the above and or other possible
methods
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to
dewatering It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained
by the contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 50 Page 16
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His qualified representative who should be
required to be continuously present on site during dewatering activities will have
the best opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during
construction and to implement as soon as possible necessary precautionary or
remedial measures including but not limited to stopping or slowing down the
dewatering operations
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils may
be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations should
be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the bottom of
excavation Therefore the project dewatering operations should be done without
pumping out soil fines bumping clear water only and should be coordinated with
shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not affected

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 2
borings to depths ranging between 110 to 112 feet below the existing ground surface at
the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
a truck mounted drill rig
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System Graphic illustration of the materials encountered are presented on
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
fornia sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Boring
Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as RQD
50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based
on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content unit weight and Atterberg Limits determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample
depths
One Atterberg Limits test was performed on a selected sample of the soils to
evaluate their liquid and plastic limits The test results are shown on Plate B 1
Three sieve analysis were performed on selected samples of the soils to evaluate
the gradation characteristics of the soils The test results are also summarized on Plate
B 1
One unconsolidated untrained UU triaxial compression test was performed on the
alluvial soils to evaluate the strength parameters of the alluvial deposit The test results
are presented on Plate B 2
One remolded direct shear test was performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate their shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plate B 3
