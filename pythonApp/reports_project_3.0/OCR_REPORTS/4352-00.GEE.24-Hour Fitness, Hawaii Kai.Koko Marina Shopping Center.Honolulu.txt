INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the 24 Hour Fitness project located at the existing Koko Marina Shopping Center in the
Hawaii Kai area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations
slabs on grade site grading and utility lines only and are subject to the limitations noted at the
end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
in age When the building of the Waianae and Koolau volcanoes came to an end only a slight
embayment existed on the southern coast of Oahu
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Koolau Range and its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio eustatic
fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age The base of the stratigraphic
section in this area is Koolau Basalt The Koolau volcanic shield was built by thinly bedded
lava flows during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epochs The main shield building
activities ceased approximately 2 2 million years ago
GEOLABS INC
W O 4352 00 Page 5
After a long period of cessation of the shield building volcanic activity returned with the
formation of a series of lava flows cinder cones and tuff cones such as those associated with
Koko Crater Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature due to the interaction of
groundwater with the rising magma resulting in steam explosions which expelled large
quantities of pyroclastic material predominantly ash and cinder Some explosive eruptions
produced ash that cemented together to form a firm rock known as tuff locally known as
mudrock
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the
oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of accumulated
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at correspondingly
higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters protected
by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and
reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials Land development and reclamation projects within the last century
have brought the project site to its present form

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three borings extending
to depths ranging from about 35 2 to 40 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration at the proposed new building addition location
encountered fills consisting of stiff to very stiff clayey soils and dense gravelly soils extending
to depths of about 7 to 8 feet below the existing ground surface The fills were generally
underlain by lagoonal deposits consisting of very loose to loose silty sand and gravel extending
to depths of about feet below the existing ground surface The silty lagoonal
deposits were generally underlain by clayey lagoonal deposits consisting of very soft to soft
gravelly clay extending to depths of about 19 to 23 feet below the existing ground surface The
clayey lagoonal deposits were generally underlain by pyrolclastic deposits consisting of dense
to very dense cinder sand of volcanic origin with varying amounts of silt extending to the
maximum depths explored of about 35 2 to 40 feet below the existing ground surface
Relatively undisturbed samples of the lagoonal deposits very loose clayey gravel and
soft clays were obtained using a thin wall tube sampler A consolidation test was performed
on a relatively undisturbed sample of the lagoonal deposits and the results are presented
on Plate B 5 of Appendix B The consolidation test indicates that the soft lagoonal deposits
are normally consolidated to slightly over consolidated therefore the soft lagoonal deposits
may be considered highly compressible when subjected to loading
Groundwater was encountered during our field exploration at depths varying from
approximately 6 6 to 9 6 feet below the existing ground surface It should be noted that

surge conditions and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The subsurface conditions at the proposed new building addition location may be
generalized as 7 to 8 feet of stiff dense fill over 12 to 15 feet of soft loose lagoonal deposits
underlain by dense cinder sand to the maximum depths explored Groundwater was
encountered at depths of about 6 6 to 9 6 feet below the existing ground surface at the time
of our field exploration
Since the existing building is supported by driven pile foundations ideally the new
addition should also be supported by pile foundations to reduce differential settlements
between the structures However based on the anticipated light wall load and our engineering
analyses we believe that the new building addition may be supported on a shallow foundation
system consisting of continuous footings bearing on the recompacted fills provided some
differential settlement is tolerable The lagoonal deposits are highly compressible when
subjected to new loads Therefore to reduce the settlement of the new building addition the
new continuous footings should have a minimum width of 24 inches In addition the new
continuous footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent
finished grade
As previously mentioned the existing building is supported by a deep foundation
system consisting of driven piles Since the new building addition will be supported on shallow
footings and the existing building is supported on driven pile foundations we anticipate
appreciable differential movements would occur between the two structures To reduce the
differential settlements between the new building addition and the existing building we

New Building Addition Foundations
As previously mentioned our borings encountered soft lagoonal deposits Our tests
indicate that the lagoonal deposits are highly compressible when subjected to new loading
Based on the anticipated wall load and our engineering analyses we believe that the proposed
building addition may be supported on a shallow foundation system consisting of continuous
footings provided some differential settlement is tolerable An allowable bearing pressure of
up to 2 000 pounds per square foot psf bearing on the recompacted fill materials may be
used to support the proposed building addition This bearing value is for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces To reduce the potential for settlement of the new building addition we
recommend that the new continuous footings have a minimum width of 24 inches In addition
footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished
grade
Where foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward
from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
GEOLABS INC
W O 4352 00 Page 9
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for significant foundation settlements
The soils encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations should be
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction If soft and or loose materials
are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations the soft and or loose materials
should be removed to expose the underlying firm or dense materials The over excavation
may be backfilled with the excavated on site materials compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 85 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted fill materials
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils
around the footings are well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed building
addition The near surface soils encountered at the project site consist of a mixture of gravelly
and clayey soils Based on our laboratory tests the clayey soils encountered in our borings
exhibit a low potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations
To provide more uniform support and to serve as a capillary break we recommend
that a 4 inch thick cushion fill layer consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation be placed below the slab The slab cushion fill should be placed as soon as
possible after the scarification and compaction of the slab subgrade soils For slabs on grade
with moisture sensitive surfacing we recommend that an impermeable membrane be placed
over the gravel cushion fill to reduce the potential for migration of moisture vapor through the
concrete slab Two inches of moist sand may be considered above the plastic membrane to
provide protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Provision of the
2 inches of fine sand above the vapor barrier will be left up to the discretion of the project
structural engineer Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be
considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking
GEOLABS INC
W O 4352 00 Page 11
Because stiff clays were encountered in the surficial fill layer we recommend that the
soils at the slab subgrade level be scarified to a depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to
above the optinum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction After preparation of the slab subgrades they should be kept moist prior to
placement of cushion fill and or concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction
of the subgrade we recommend that the soils be moistened to close the cracks or prepared
as mentioned above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted fill
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We recommend
that concrete walkways be supported on a 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation To reduce the effects of slab cracking crack
control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with
expansion joints at right angle intersections
The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab with
relatively impervious soils and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the
potential for water ponding around the slab and foundation
Site Preparation
Based on the existing ground surface and the finished floor elevation of the existing
building we anticipate minimal site grading work will be required for the proposed project
In general site grading operations should be observed by a representative from Geolabs It
is important that a representative from our office observe whether the exposed soil conditions
during excavation are similar to those encountered in our field exploration The following
recommendations are intended to provide guidelines for the site grading work
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared Debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and
GEOLABS INC
W O 4352 00 Page 12
disposed of off site In addition the existing portland cement concrete walkways and asphalt
concrete pavements within the new building addition footprint and other improvements
should be removed to expose the underlying soils in order to facilitate the placement of new
fills
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and demolition should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material The over excavation resulting from the
clearing and demolition should be backfilled with well compacted fill material Existing utilities
were observed traversing the proposed new building addition footprint Existing utilities to be
abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled
Utilities to be abandoned in place under the proposed structure should be backfilled by
pumping lean concrete Controlled Low Strength Material under low pressure
After clearing and demolition soils beneath the slabs finished subgrades or areas to
receive fills should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
In areas subjected to vehicular traffic finished subgrades below pavement areas the
compaction requirement should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test
procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding
to the maximum dry density
The finished subgrades should be kept moist until covered by cushion material and or
slabs If shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that
the subgrade soil be moisture conditioned and recompacted as recommended above to close
the cracks In addition saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted fill
GEOLABS INC
W O 4352 00 Page 13
The on site gravelly and clayey soils generated from excavations may be re used
as a source of fill and backfill materials provided that they are well graded from coarse to fine
with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension In addition the materials should
be free of deleterious materials In general on site materials to be used as fill should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding
8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
In areas subjected to vehicular traffic the compaction requirement should be increased to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
If imported fill materials are required the imported material should consist of
non expansive select granular fill such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand
The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches
in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value
of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in
accordance with ASTM D 1883 Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended
use
Utility Line Trenches
We envision that new utility lines and connections will be required for the proposed
project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation is recommended below the pipes Free draining granular materials such
as No 3B Fine gravel should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes or about 12 inches above the groundwater level to provide adequate support
around the pipes It is critical that the free draining materials be used to reduce the potential
for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support around
the sides of the pipes
GEOLABS INC
W O 4352 00 Page 14
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes or
groundwater level to the top of the subgrade may consist of the excavated on site soils
provided that they are free of deleterious materials The backfill should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in maximum 8 inch level loose
lifts and mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce
the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches will be located below areas
subjected to vehicular traffic the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the new building addition should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised
that gutter systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away
from the foundation and slab areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
slabs should be avoided Planters next to foundations should also be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab
edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration
In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs

The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling three
borings extending to depths ranging from about 35 2 to 40 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled
using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with hollow stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate
sample depths
Relatively undisturbed samples of the soft soil deposits were retrieved by
hydraulically pushing a 3 inch OD thin wall Shelby tube into the soil formation The
hydraulic pressures needed to advance the sampler are recorded as the Penetration
Resistance in pounds per square inch psi per foot of penetration on the Logs of Borings
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Two one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil sampies to evaluate the
swelling potential of the near surface soils The results are summarized on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One sieve analysis test was performed on a selected sample of the soils to evaluate
the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification The test was performed in
accordance with ASTM Test Designations C 136 and D 1140 Graphic presentation of the
grain size distribution is provided on Plate B 3
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample of the soils
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics Results of the direct shear test are presented
on Plate B 4
One consolidation test ASTM D 2485 with time rates of consolidation was
performed on a sample of the very soft soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics
of the materials encountered The results of the consolidation test are presented on
Plate B 5
