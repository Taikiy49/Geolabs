INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering explorations
performed for the proposed Maui Sam s Wholesale Club Wal Mart located at the
intersection of Haleakala Highway and Dairy Road near the Kahului Airport on the island
of Maui Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommen
dations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses

W O 3045 20 Page 3
The recommendations presented herein are intended for design input for foundation
retaining wall site grading and pavement and may not be appropriate to form the basis
for the preparation of construction cost estimates nor for construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project consists of the construction of a new wholesale warehouse
facility in Kahului on the island of Maui Hawaii We understand the proposed warehouse
facility will include construction of an approximately 113 000 square foot building with a
below grade loading dock area for trucks and a paved parking lot with 722 stalls as
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The proposed building superstructure will consist of a combination of load bearing
concrete block walls and steel columns supporting the roof with steel joist girders and bar
joists The finished floor elevation is planned at about 10 75 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
The truck loading ramp area is anticipated to be a below grade ramp The height
of retaining wall associated with this structure is not known at this time We understand
that standard duty flexible pavements are planned for the parking lot areas and special
heavy duty flexible and or rigid pavements are planned for the truck drive areas
Based on information provided by Wal Mart maximum column loads on the order
of 145 kips including live loads are anticipated for the proposed warehouse structure
Maximum column uplift loads are about 30 kips The maximum uniform load on
continuous footings is 6 kips per lineal foot The estimated maximum uniform floor slab
load is 250 p s f with a maximum concentrated floor slab load of 16 kips

Airport on the eastern side of Kahului on the island of Maui Hawaii The site for the

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The exploration generally encountered dry to moist stiff to very stiff clayey silt and
silty sand fill soils to depths of about 2 to 9 feet below the existing ground surface at the
revised building location These fill soils were generally underlain by medium dense to
dense sandy soils to the maximum depths drilled in this area of about 25 feet below the
existing ground surface
The borings drilled at the parking and other areas generally encountered dry to
moist sandy and clayey silt fill soils extending to depths of about 4 to 12 feet below the
surface These fill soils generally were encountered in a matrix containing abundant
gravel rocks cobbles and boulders The consistency of the fills ranged from loose and
dry near the surface to very stiff and moist with increasing depth
The surficial fill soils were generally underlain by alluvial sandy soils residual silty
soils and basalt in a moderately to highly weathered state The alluvial sands are
generally medium dense to very dense containing abundant gravel at greater depth The
residual silts are derived from the underlying basalt formation and graded from medium
stiff to hard Although weathered the basalt formation encountered in our exploration
should be able to provide relatively good foundation support characteristics and should
be generally easier to excavate than unweathered rock
We believe that past uses for the site included cultivation of sugar cane Cane
fields are commonly ripped and turned to depths of 5 feet which results in a surficial soil
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3045 20 Page 8
profile that may appear to be fill The engineering properties of such disturbed native
soils are those of undocumented fill
Groundwater was encountered in the borings drilled at the time of our field
explorations ranging in depths from about 7 to 15 feet below the existing ground
surface corresponding to elevations of 1 to 7 feet MSL The higher groundwater level
encountered may be a perched groundwater condition We encountered groundwater
in only 4 of the 33 test pits excavated for this exploration at depths ranging from about
6 to 12 feet below the ground surface It should be noted that groundwater depths are
subject to change due to rainfall time of year and tidal fluctuations

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our field exploration and laboratory testing the significant geotechnical
engineering considerations for the proposed project are the vertical and lateral extent
strength characteristics and compressibility of the near surface fill soils
Based on the information from our subsurface exploration the revised building pad
site is presently underlain by undocumented fill material ranging from about 2 to 9 feet in
thickness The fill materials generally consist of sandy and clayey silts with some gravels
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3045 20 Page 9
cobbles and boulders The consistency of the fill material ranges from medium stiff to
very stiff
The fill materials encountered at the parking and other areas were found to range
from about 4 to 12 feet in thickness The fill materials generally consist of clayey silts with
abundant gravels cobbles boulders and debris Boulders on the order of 2 to 3 feet in
diameter were encountered in our test pits In addition large concrete pieces slab like
were also encountered within the fill material
The consistency of the fill materials in these areas ranges from loose and soft up
to very stiff to hard Within the upper 4 to 5 feet of the ground surface the fill soils were
generally dry to moist with moisture contents generally below the optimum moisture
content while deeper soils were moist to wet with moisture contents well above the
optimum As a result recompaction of the materials with a high moisture content may
be more difficult without processing and drying
Generally as expected the existing fill soils in the upper 5 feet below the ground
surface have moderate to low unconfined compressive strengths and are considered to
be low to non expansive Because of the potentially variable nature of the fill soils we
recommend that the fill soils be completely removed below the bottom of all footings The
maximum depth of over excavation below footings to remove existing fill soils should be
limited to about 4 feet below the bottom of footings We believe that for a large portion
of the proposed building site removal of about 2 feet of fill soils below the bottom of
footings would remove the fills entirely Therefore footings should be supported on at
least 2 feet of compacted engineered fill to reduce the potential differential settlements
between footings to about inch The existing fill soils may be reused as compacted
engineered fill provided that they are screened of over sized materials and debris
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3045 20 Page 10
replaced as compacted engineered fill Slabs on grade also should be supported on at
least 2 feet of compacted on site fill material provided that the material is processed and
screened of over sized materials and debris
The existing concrete drainage channel traverses the proposed parking area and
we understand that plans call for the channel to remain in place The concrete slab over
the channel should be designed as a simply supported beam with driven precast
concrete piles supporting the structural slab since the fill soils in this area extend to
depths of about 10 feet below the surface

Spread Footing Foundations
The proposed new warehouse will be a one story structure situated on a relatively
level site Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our explorations we
believe that a shallow foundation system may be used to support the structural loads
generated by the proposed warehouse facility Spread footings bearing on a minimum
of 2 feet of compacted engineered fill may be used to support the entire proposed
warehouse Based on a finished floor elevation of 10 75 feet we believe that over
excavating a minimum of 2 feet below the bottom of footings would remove the fill soils
below the western portion of the building footprint
Where existing fills still remain after the 2 foot over excavation below the bottom
of footings we recommend that additional over excavation of the fill soils up to a
maximum depth of 4 feet below the bottom of footings be conducted Removal of the
existing fills and replacement with compacted engineered fill should extend a horizontal
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3045 20 Page 11
distance of at least equal to the depth of the over excavation below the bottom of the
footing The over excavation should be backfilled with compacted engineered fill The
on site fill soils may be reused as a source of fill material provided that they are properly
processed and screened of over sized materials and debris Preparation of the over
excavated subgrade placement of compacted engineered fill and the requirements for
fill material are further discussed in the Site Grading section of this report
Based on our field explorations and laboratory testing we believe that an allowable
bearing pressure of up to 3 000 p s f may be used for the design of spread footings
bearing on the 2 feet of compacted engineered fill material The allowable bearing
pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient
loadings such as temporary wind and or seismic forces Footings should be embedded
a minimum of 2 feet below the lowest adjacent finished grade For the anticipated
maximum column loads of up to 145 kips we estimate total settlements of footings
bearing on engineered fill material to be on the order of 1 inch with differential settlements
of about 14 inch
The existing concrete drainage channel crosses the proposed parking area and
we understand that plans call for the channel to remain in place The concrete slab
bridge over the channel should be designed as a simply supported beam with driven
pre cast concrete piles supporting the structural slab Design parameters for concrete
piles are discussed in the following subsection
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the spread footing and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used between the base of the footings and the
supporting engineered fill Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated using
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3045 20 Page 12
an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f for
footings embedded in the select fill These values assume that the concrete for footings
is placed directly against well compacted engineered fill soils Unless covered by
pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade
should be neglected
Where footings will be located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities the
footings should extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane projected upward

It is important that a representative of Geolabs Hawaii observe the footing

Driven Pile Foundations
The existing concrete drainage channel traverses the proposed parking area and
we understand that plans call for the channel to remain in place A concrete slab bridge
is planned across the channel and should be designed as a simply supported beam with
driven precast concrete piles supporting the structural slab Piles should be driven to
end bearing refusal in the basalt formation underlying the site at a depth of about 27 feet
as encouniered in the three borings B 6 through B 8 drilled near the existing channel
We believe that either 12 inch square or 16 inch octagonal precast prestressed
concrete piles with an allowable bearing capacity of 55 and 90 tons respectively may be
used for dead plus live loads The allowable bearing capacity may be increased by one
third 14 for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces In addition we recommend
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3045 20 Page 13
that a flexible material be placed between the top of the channel walls and the new
structural slab such that loads from the new structural slab are not transferred down to
the concrete channel
Resistance to uplift loads may be mobilized by dead weight of the pile only skin
friction along the predrilled holes even if properly backfilled with sand should not be
relied upon for uplift resistance
Support of the concrete slab bridge over the channel by drilled piers was also
considered We believe that drilled piers would be difficult to install due to the frequency
of boulders and cobbles which would require temporary casing of the pier holes In
addition relatively high groundwater levels were encountered requiring placement of
concrete using tremie methods or dewatering of the pier holes As a result we believe
that a driven pile foundation system would be more viable

Foundation Settlements
Settlements of pile foundations will result from the compression of the pile member
and the subgrade response of the bearing basalt We estimate that the total
settlement of the pile supported foundations will be less than inch with
differential settlements between piles not exceeding about one half that
amount We believe these settlements will be essentially elastic and should occur
as the loads are applied
Predrilling
It should be noted that a high frequency of cobbles and boulders were
encountered in the fill during our exploration Additionally the borings also
encountered boulders below the fill to approximately the top of the basalt formation
about 27 feet below surface Because of the presence of the boulder
obstructions and the possibility for displacement of the adjacent concrete channel
walls due to the close proximity of the displacement type piles to the walls
predrilling of all pile locations to approximately the basalt formation is
recommended Predrilling by rock coring through the boulders will likely be
needed Care should be taken during predrilling to avoid dislodging boulders into
the predrilled holes The diameter of the predrilled holes should not be less than
the largest dimension of the pile
After pile driving is completed the predrilled holes should be backfilled with sand
The upper 3 feet of the backfill around the pile should be mechanically compacted
to provide more lateral support

Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a minimum rated
energy of 50 000 foot pounds The contractor should forward pile and driving
equipment data forms to Geolabs Hawaii prior to bidding for evaluation Geolabs
Hawaii will review the submittal and assess the driveability of piles with the use of
the selected hammer Due to possible variation in the length of the piles unit
prices should be obtained during bidding for add ons shorter piles etc
Pile Driving Criteria
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption with the exception of
splicing if needed A pile may be rejected or its design capacity may be reduced
when the driving resistance is interrupted for more than 4 hours and the pile
cannot be driven to the required depth
Because local variations in bedrock weathering and strength characteristics are
likely to exist in the bearing basalt the supporting capacity of each pile must be
confirmed in the field by our office during driving This may be accomplished by
using a driving resistance procedure such as the Engineering News Record ENR
formula or the more sophisticated Wave Equation Analysis to evaluate pile bearing
capacity
For preliminary design purposes driving may be terminated when the whole pile
length is driven down to the finished tip elevations or when aggregated blow
counts are 40 blows for the last foot Appropriate driving criteria will be developed
after selection of the pile driving equipment

Test Pile Program
A test pile program which includes at least one load test should be undertaken to
fulfill the following objectives
1 To examine the driveability of piles with the use of the selected
hammer
2 To estimate the production pile lengths
3 To assess the test pile driving criteria which may be modified for use
as the production driving criteria
To achieve these objectives we recommend that the test pile program consist of
a minimum of 10 percent of the total piles The test piles may be incorporated into
the actual foundation system Test piles should be casted 10 feet longer than the
estimated pile length to account for potential variation
The pile load test should be performed in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1143 loading the test pile to 200 percent of the anticipated design
load We recommend that a representative from Geolabs Hawaii be present during
all pile driving operations to observe the actual driving behavior and to further
evaluate the field performance It should be noted that the pile design and driving
criteria recommended were developed from our pile analysis using the field
exploration data Therefore observation of the pile driving operations by Geolabs
Hawaii is necessary to confirm our design assumptions
GEOLABS HAWAII

It is strongly recommended that the production pile driving be observed by
Geolabs Hawaii so that driving resistances tip elevations and axial load bearing
capacity may be confirmed
Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed wholesale
warehouse structure These slabs will be subjected to forklift traffic and will therefore
function as rigid pavements Due to the presence of uncontrolled fill materials anticipated
at the slab subgrade elevation we recommend that the area beneath the building footprint
be over excavated to a minimum depth of 2 feet below the slab subgrade level i e
bottom of slab cushion and replaced with compacted engineered fill The engineered fill
may consist of on site fill material provided that the material is screened of all debris and
material greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension Existing fills encountered below
the 2 foot over excavated levels which are saturated or soft should also be removed to
expose firm soils and replaced with engineered fill The over excavation should extend
a minimum of 5 feet laterally beyond the limits of the proposed building Preparation of
the over excavated surface and placement of the excavated materials as engineered
backfills should be performed in accordance with the recommendations presented in the
Site Grading section of this report
In general the on site soils may be reused as fill material provided that they meet
the requirements described in the Site Grading section of this report Subgrade soils
should be kept moist and should not be allowed to dry prior to placement of cushion fill
and slab concrete If shrinkage cracks appear in the subgrade the subgrade should be
scarified moisture conditioned to 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
recompacted to 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in
place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3045 20 Page 19
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum
dry density
Since the slab will be subjected to vehicular traffic a 6 inch layer of base course
compacted to 95 percent relative compaction is recommended for more uniform support
We recommend that the concrete slab be designed by a structural engineer for the
intended loads and should be reinforced as a structural slab to accommodate some
amount of differential settlement Provisions should be made for proper load transfer
across the slab joints which are subjected to vehicular traffic
The base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a

subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per cubic inch p c i may be used for the compacted
base course The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any unpaved area should be
embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade

Lateral Earth Pressures
All retaining structures should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due
to adjacent soils and surcharge effects We understand that Wal Mart desires at
rest pressures The recommended at rest lateral earth pressure for design
expressed in equivalent fluid pressure is 50 p c f for an assumed level backfill
The value provided above is for on site soils or imported granular soils Generally
walls whose tops are not free to deflect beyond about 0 5 percent of the wall
height or are restrained should be designed for the at rest condition This lateral
earth pressure does not include hydrostatic pressures that might be caused by
groundwater trapped behind the walls
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 foot to 2 foot wide zone
of permeable material No 3B Fine gravel or ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation

such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the drainage material with
filter fabric The prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically
connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads within a
horizontal distance equal to the height of the wall should be considered in the
design For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall
a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 45 percent of the vertical
surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of the wall which is restrained at
the top may be used in design Additional analyses during design may be needed
to evaluate the surcharge effects of point loads and line loads

Over Excavation and Subgrade Preparation
Areas that are to receive fills or are subgrades for slabs or other improvements
should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting
grub spoil material and demolition debris should be disposed of off site Organic
soils and or topsoil may be stockpiled for use in landscaped areas if appropriate
However care should be exercised to reduce contamination of the topsoil with on
site fill and backfill material
As previously indicated excavation of the existing fills of at least 2 feet to a
maximum of 4 feet below the bottom of footing elevation will be required beneath
all footing locations In addition the existing near surface fill materials should be
over excavated to a minimum depth of 2 feet below pad grade within the building
footprint to provide the 2 feet of engineered fill beneath the floor slab All
engineered fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above
the optimum moisture content and compacted to 95 percent relative compaction
Over sized materials greater than 3 inches in largest dimension should not be
reused as fill material and may be removed by screening
If zones of soft or wet soils are encountered at the subgrade levels of the over
excavations during grading further over excavation may be required to expose firm

The exposed surface following stripping and or over excavation should be scarified
to a depth of at least 8 inches properly moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less than 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
It should be noted that the test pits excavated for this exploration were backfilled

compactive effort Since structures located over these areas may experience

grading to reach design grades they should be over excavated and compacted
prior to construction of improvements in these areas
Fills and Backfills
The on site materials may be re used as engineered fill provided that they contain
no lumps or fragments greater than 3 inches in greatest dimension Cobbles and
boulders should not be allowed in the fill It should be noted that because of the
low to moderate expansion potential of the on site soils the on site soils should
be thoroughly moisture conditioned prior to use as engineered fill Engineered fills

Excavation for Utility Lines
It is anticipated that underground utility trenches of up to 10 feet deep will be
required during construction associated with the warehouse facility Based on our
borings we anticipate that conventional excavation techniques may be used for utility
trench excavation with the possible exception where boulders and concrete may be
encountered Because of the variable quality and composition of the fill soils on site we
anticipate that utility trenches greater than about 5 feet in depth below the existing ground
surface would need to be shored Because groundwater was encountered at depths
ranging from about 7 to 15 feet below the ground surface dewatering would also likely
be required Determination of the method and equipment used for excavation and
dewatering should be left to the contractor within practical limits and safety
considerations
Excavation Support
Because of the variable subsoil conditions and shallow to moderate groundwater
levels encountered in our exploration it is our opinion that continuous excavation support
will be required for utility excavations deeper than about 5 feet In general we believe
that the trenches may be sloped back in conformance with OSHA requirements If the
sequence of construction prevents sloping back of the upper sides of the trench then
standard temporary shoring consisting of plywood sheeting and hydraulic jacks in
conformance with OSHA requirements may be used
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3045 20 Page 27
The shoring and bracing system used to support the trenches must comply with
all applicable safety requirements The adequacy and safety of the shoring and bracing
installation operations should be made the responsibility of the contractor His
representative who should be required to continually monitor the excavation and
dewatering work at the site would therefore have the opportunity for observing changing
conditions which might affect the excavation stability during construction
Appropriate loads due to surcharge equipment traffic or sloping ground conditions
adjacent to the excavations should be added to the recommended earth pressures for
design purposes
Even if good construction procedures are followed some movement of the
excavation support bracing system may be anticipated because of changes in earth
stresses during excavation Movements depend greatly on workmanship e g how
quickly and tightly the support is installed It also depends on the types of subsoils the
depth and width of excavation and the type of support In view of the complexity of the
problem it is difficult to accurately estimate the amount of movement to be anticipated
of a bracing system and the adjacent ground Therefore it is important to realize that the
excavation support should be installed as early as practicable and that measurements of
vertical and horizontal movements are required especially in areas where ground
movements may result in damage to existing adjacent structures
The ground adjacent to excavations should be continuously monitored for cracks
or dips and other indications of movements and possible sloughing of the excavation
walls Such monitoring is particularly critical in areas adjacent to existing structures and
utilities

Excavation Dewatering
Dewatering of the utility excavations may be necessary since the groundwater
levels encountered in our field exploration ranged up to only about 7 feet below the
ground surface Dewatering for construction is the sole responsibility of the contractor
The dewatering operations should be coordinated with the trench shoring support so that
the stability of the excavations is not jeopardized The operations should be carried out
without softening the bottom of the excavations
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents should
be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater table
and to exclude the water from the excavations to permit construction of underground
utilities under safe and dry conditions Therefore these works or systems should include
but not be limited to pumping
Dewatering Method
The method and equipment to be used for the excavation dewatering should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selecting a
suitable method of dewatering
1 The method should result in the least disturbance or damage to the
adjacent existing structures
2 The method should maintain stability of and also provide safe and
dry working conditions in the excavations
3 The method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modifications to
accommodate various ground conditions
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3045 20 Page 29
Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of the excavations
is generally not recommended The resultant areal depression of the natural
groundwater table could induce consolidation of subsoils resulting in potential
ground settlements
Construction Precautions
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to
dewatering It is also recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be
retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His qualified representative who should be
required to be continuously present at the site will have the best opportunity to
promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and to implement
as soon as possible necessary precautionary or remedial measures including but
not limited to stopping or slowing down the dewatering operations
Where encountered at the excavation bottoms permeable granular subsoils may
be susceptible to piping and quick conditions Therefore the project dewatering
operations should be done without pumping out soil fines bumping clear water
only and should be coordinated with shoring installation so that the excavation
stability is not jeopardized The dewatering operations should be carried out
without creating a quick condition or softening at the excavation bottoms
If dewatering is necessary it should be noted that water from the dewatering
operations would likely be discharged to a County storm drain system A permit
under the National Pollution Discharge Elimination System NPDES will be required

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 14
borings to depths ranging between 7 and 37 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled
using truck mounted rigs advancing continuous flight augers and coring tools as
appropriate In addition we excavated and logged 33 test pits ranging in depths from 3
to 13 feet
The materials encountered in the borings and test pits were classified by visual and
textural examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling and excavating
operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by
visual inspection and testing in the laboratory All soils were classified in general
conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System A graphic presentation of the
materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 1
Soil samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Boring Logs at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74 percent
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
GEOLABS HAWAII

rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of the tests
are shown on Plates B 1 through B 6
Five sieve analysis were performed on selected samples of the soils to evaluate the
gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification The test results are
also summarized on Plate B 1 through B 6
Eight on inch ring swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the subsoils Test results are summarized on Plates B 1 through B 6
One consolidation tests with time rates were performed on the potentially
compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the materials
encountered Results of the consolidation tests are presented on Plate B 7
Five unconfined compression tests were performed on selected samples of
cohesive soils to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the material Results
of the unconfined compression tests are summarized on Plates B 1 through B 6
Four direct shear tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the
shear strength of the material Results of the direct shear tests are presented on Plates
B 8 through B 11
Two proctor ASTM D 1557 91 tests were performed on a bulk sample of the soils
to evaluate the maximum dry density and optimum moisture content relationship
Five sets of corrosion tests consisting of pH and minimum resistivity were
performed on the foundation soils to evaluate the corrosivity of the materials Results of
the pH and minimum resistivity values are presented on Plates B 1 through B 6
