for the proposed remodeling of the residence at 567 Portlock Road located in the Hawaii Kai
area of the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are

This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations

recommendations are intended for the design of foundations retaining structures site
grading and pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age When the building of the Waianae and Koolau Volcanoes came to an
end only a slight embayment existed on the southern coast of Oahu
After a long period of cessation of the shield building volcanic activity returned with
the formation of a series of lava flows cinder cones and tuff cones Many of the eruptions
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3889 10 Page 5
were rather explosive in nature due to the interaction of groundwater with the rising magma
resulting in steam explosions which expelled large quantities of pyroclastic material
predominantly ash and cinder Some explosive eruptions produced ash that cemented
together to form a firm rock known as tuff locally known as mudrock
The project site is located on the western flank of Koko Head Crater Because the
Koko Head eruption was hydromagmatic many fragments of coral limestone and Koolau
basalt torn from the underlying corals and rocks by the explosion are scattered
throughout the tuff During periods of higher sea level stands formation of deltas and fans
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays and lagoonal marine sediments
in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs These sediments overlie the tuff formation
found at greater depths Land development and reclamation projects within the last century
have brought the project site to its present form

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsoil conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings extending to depths of about 15 to 20 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our borings indicate that the project site is generally underlain by fill materials over
volcanic tuff formation Based on our field exploration the thickness of the existing fills vary
from about 2 5 feet at the eastern portion of the project site to about 14 feet near the central
portion of the project site In general the fills encountered in our borings consisted of stiff
sandy clays and dense clayey sands The fills were generally underlain by moderately
weathered medium hard to hard volcanic tuff formation extending to the maximum depth
explored of approximately 20 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings within the depths drilled at the time
of our field exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to
change due to fluctuations in tides rainfall time of year and other factors Considering the
proximity of the site to the Pacific Ocean groundwater levels are likely affected by tidal
fluctuations

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed structures
Both interior house slabs and exterior flatwork such as the pool deck and walkway slabs
are anticipated for the project The near surface soils encountered in our borings generally
consist of sandy clays and clayey sands which exhibit a slight to moderate expansion
potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations Therefore to reduce the potential of
structural distress to lightly loaded slabs we recommend that a minimum of 12 inches of
non expansive select granular fill material be provided below the interior and exterior
concrete slabs The non expansive select granular fill should extend a minimum of 2 feet
laterally beyond the edges of the slab
Subgrades for the slabs on grades should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to
not less than 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
The non expansive select granular fill recommended below the new floor slabs
should consist of imported granular material such as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder
sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension The material should also contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory California
Bearing Ratio CBR value of at least 20 and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when
tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 The select granular fill materials
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to
at least 90 percent relative compaction Slab subgrades should be kept moist until covered
by the select granular fill
To provide more uniform support under the interior house slabs we recommend that
the concrete slab on grade be supported on a minimum 4 inch cushion fill The cushion fill
GEOLABS HAWAII

layer should consist of open graded gravels such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation which would also serve as a capillary moisture break The slab cushion
fill should be placed as soon as possible after the placement of the select granular fill For
slabs on grade with moisture sensitive surfacing such as the interior house slabs we
recommend that an impermeable membrane be placed over the cushion fill to reduce the

Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the retaining
structures discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the retaining structures
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of the on site fill materials
or granular fill less than 3 inches in maximum dimension Unless covered by
concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious
material such as the on site sandy clays to reduce the potential for water infiltration
behind the retaining structures

5 feet in depth In general we envision that the proposed excavations will likely
expose the on site fill materials and highly weathered tuff formation Therefore we
believe that conventional excavation equipment such as backhoe excavators may
be used to readily excavate the on site fill materials and highly weathered tuff
formation However excavations in localized areas containing hard tuff formation
may require the use of hoerams or chipping
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based on
our visual observation of the existing site and field data from the borings drilled at the
site We recommend that contractors be encouraged to examine the site conditions
and the boring data to make their own interpretations
Pavement Design
We understand that the existing driveway leading into the residence may be
reconstructed We anticipate that the vehicle loading for this project will consist primarily of
passenger vehicles and light pick up trucks We have made our preliminary pavement
design assuming the pavement subgrade soil will be similar to the clayey sand and sandy
clay soils encountered in the borings As mentioned previously the on site soils exhibit a
slight to moderate expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations Based on
the above assumptions we recommend the following pavement sections be used for
preliminary design purposes

The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction If pumping of the subgrade
occurs during construction the subgrade should be proof rolled to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction The aggregate base course and select borrow subbase should consist
of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Laboratory CBR tests and field density tests should be performed on samples of the
actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the above recommended
pavement section The recommended pavement section is based on the assumption that
good drainage will be provided for areas adjacent to the pavements
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during
or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent
to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be extended to a minimum of 2 inches
below the subbase course to reduce the potential for migration of landscape water into the
pavement section
Utility Lines
We envision that new utility lines and utility connections may be required for the
proposed project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used
for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate
support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing
GEOLABS HAWAII

the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils or granular
soils The backfill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where
trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the
pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems
be installed around the structures and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface soils The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be
provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface water run off away from the
foundations and slabs

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 extending to depths of about 15 to
20 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings drilled
are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using truck mounted and
portable drilling equipment using continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD split barrel sampler with a
140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches a total of 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs
of Borings as REC 74
