Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the
island The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the
island
The project site is situated on a narrow coastal plain and pediment which was
formed by the accumulation of a relatively thick wedge of colluvium alluvium and
marine sediments along the base of an extensive wave cut cliff on the northern flank of
the Waianae Volcano Geological conditions at the site and in its immediate vicinity can
generally be delineated by the location and slope of the various portions of the site In
the area close to Farrington Highway where the slope is relatively level the shallow
subsurface conditions generally consist of marine sediments dune sands and recent
alluvium The soils in this area generally have poor engineering characteristics
e g soft soil conditions and high ground water table
As the slope steepens to the inland side of the site the subsurface conditions
become dominated by older alluvial materials that have accumulated in alluvial fans
originating from major drainageways draining to the north from the Waianae Range
These alluvial materials consist of interbedded clays silts gravels and some areas of
boulder conglomerate In some areas the alluvium has weathered to a residual type
soil and the underlying conglomerates have weathered to a saprolitic state The soils in
these areas are generally more competent however they exhibit moderate to high
shrink swell potentials
Further inland as the property approaches the base of the Waianae Range the
geology grades to an accumulation of talus and colluvium at the base of the wave cut
cliff This interbedded talus and colluvium generally consists of angular boulders and
cobbles with a matrix of highly expansive clay The large number of boulders and

Existing Site Conditions
The project site is in Mokuleia on the northern side of the Island of Oahu Hawaii
The project site is along a narrow coastal plain and the northern pediment of the
Waianae Range and is bordered to the east by agricultural land and to the west by
Dillingham Airfield The project location is shown on the Overall Site Plan Plate 2
Based on our field observations most of the project site is presently open ranch
land with a light to heavy cover of vegetation Based on our field observations and the
available topographic maps most of the project site generally slopes down from the
south toward the north with existing ground surface elevations ranging from about
8 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the northern boundary to about 350 feet MSL along
the southern boundary
Several existing ranch trails traverse the site The ranch trails were generally
crude and followed the existing terrain Scattered boulders were observed along the
upper reaches of the site
The water tank and well sites Well No 1 and Well No 2 are located on
the east side of Nike Road about 1 7 miles from the intersection of Farrington Highway
The water tank site generally slopes downward from east to west The existing ground
surface elevations at the water tank site range from about 450 to 470 feet MSL The
well sites appear to have been previously rough graded to a relatively flat condition
The existing ground surface elevations at the well sites range from about 464 to
468 feet MSL at Well No 1 and about 363 to 368 feet MSL at Well No 2
The WWTP is located to the north of the Dillingham Ranch Lodge The site is
relatively flat with existing ground surface elevations between about 31 and 33 feet
MSL Several existing building structures were observed at the WWTP site

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic
activity therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments However some of
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate
damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii
over 90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of
Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than
Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of
Hawaii To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced several
earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes
have occurred in the County of Maui The effects of earthquakes occurring on the
Islands of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu For example small
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of
Some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a
result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 M7
In the last 150 years of recorded history we are not aware of earthquakes greater
than Magnitude 6 that have occurred on the Island of Oahu An earthquake of
Magnitude 4 8 to 5 0 occurred along the Diamond Head Fault in 1948 on the Island
of Oahu The moderate tremor resulted in broken store windows ruptured building
walls and broken underground water mains Since the Island of Kauai is the

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS

of about 15 feet high are planned for the project Based on our field exploration the cut
slopes planned at the site will expose alluvial and colluvial soils Cut slopes exposing
these soils should be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical
2H 1V or flatter
Fill slopes should be designed at an inclination of 2H 1V or flatter and should be

erosion due to surface water runoff Where slope benches are provided swales should
be provided on the benches to collect and divert surface water away from the slopes to
appropriate discharge outlets
We anticipate that significant quantities of boulders and cobbles may be
generated from excavations in the alluvium and colluvium encountered at the site
Boulders generated during the excavation operations should be removed and either
stockpiled in a designated area for crushing to acceptable size 6 inch minus for reuse
as fill or disposed properly at a location outside of the grading limits

We performed three Double Ring Infiltrometer tests at the disposal basin to
evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered at
the proposed disposal basin The double ring infiltrometer tests were performed in
general accordance with ASTM D3385 using a Double Ring Infiltrometer at a depth
of about 2 feet below the existing ground surface The inner and outer infiltrometer
rings were set at about 2 to 4 inches into the ground surface The infiltrometer rings
were filled with water to depths of about 6 to 7 inches The water was allowed to
infiltrate into the ground surface and the fall in the water level within the inner and
outer rings was measured with respect to a timed interval After the timed interval
water was added to the inner and outer rings to maintain a constant water level of
about 6 to 7 inches The volumes of water added to the inner and outer rings to
maintain a constant water level was determined The volumes of water added to
the inner and outer rings correspond to a measure of the volume of water that
infiltrates into the soil The incremental volumes infiltrated during the timed intervals
were converted into infiltration velocities or infiltration rates The rings were refilled
after each timed interval and additional trials were run The trials were continued

evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the subsurface materials The tests were
performed in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of the State of
Maryland Department of the Environment Stormwater Design Manual Volumes I and
II rev 2009 These procedures are consistent with the other states procedures and
generally may be considered an industry standard
The field infiltration tests were performed by augering a boring to a selected test
depth of about 5 5 to 6 feet below the existing ground surface Upon reaching the test
depth a 4 inch inside diameter PVC solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled

test holes were filled and soaked overnight A falling head infiltration test was performed
on the next day to determine the average infiltration rate of the underlying subsurface
materials The test consists of four trials of filling the casing with about 24 inches of
water and taking periodic readings over a 1 hour trial period or until the hole drains
completely The infiltration rates are then calculated based on the results of the fourth
and last trial The infiltration test results are presented in Appendix E The calculated

Test Boring Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
24 borings designated as Boring Nos 101 through 117 and I 1 through I 5 I 7 and
I 8 extending to depths of about 6 0 to 51 6 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate boring locations are shown on the Overall Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using both track and truck mounted drill rigs
equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described
on the Soil Classification Log Key Plate A 0 2 Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 24
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would
be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot
core run in rock the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of
the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

Test Pit Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were also explored by excavating 15 test
pits designated as Test Pit Nos 101 through 115 to depths of about 5 5 to 11 feet
below the existing ground surface In addition eight bulk samples of the near
surface soils designated as Bulk 1 through Bulk 8 were obtained to evaluate the
engineering properties of the near surface soils The approximate test pit and bulk
sample locations shown on the Overall Site Plan Plate 2 The test pits were
excavated using a Cat 315D backhoe excavator
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the excavated test pits
by visual and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM
D2488 Standard Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the
excavation operations on a near continuous full time basis These classifications
were further reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were
classified in general accordance
with
ASTM D2487
Standard
Practice
for
Classification
of
Soils
for Engineering Purposes Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1 Deviations made to the
soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil
Classification Log Key Plate A 0 2 Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Test Pits Plates B 1 through B 15
Soil samples were obtained as grab samples of the excavated materials and placed
in bulk sample bags for transportation to the laboratory Consistencies textures and
relative moisture contents were determined subjectively in the field during the exploration
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer test results are
summarized on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Ten Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D4318 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentation of the test results is
provided on Plate C 1
Two Sieve Analysis tests were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on
Plate C 2
Sixteen one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on site soils The test results
are summarized on Plate C 3
Three Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D2166 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on site soils The test
results and stress strain curves are plotted on Plates C 4 through C 6
One Uniaxial Compression test ASTM D7012 Method C was performed on a
selected core sample to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the basalt rock
encountered The test results are presented on Plate C 7
Four Direct Shear tests ASTM D3080 were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested The test results are
presented on Plates C 8 through C 11
One Consolidation test ASTM D2435 was performed on a selected soil sample to
evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the materials encountered Results of the
consolidation test are presented on Plate C 12
Eight laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the engineering characteristics of the
soils The test results are presented on Plates C 13 through C 20
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D1557 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates C 21 and C 22
