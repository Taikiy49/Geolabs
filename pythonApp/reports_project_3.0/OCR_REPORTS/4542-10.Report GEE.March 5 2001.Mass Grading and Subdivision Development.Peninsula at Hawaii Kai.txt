REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the western
third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern two thirds
of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and its
eastern flank is partially buried below the Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology and
subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during
the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface
conditions within the project area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age coral algal reef
deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea which are intercalated with
terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to the
present sea level of Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level
occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area were drowned In the last
10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine
sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments
resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in the areas that were
formerly valleys and drainageways
With the urbanization of the area man made fills were placed to reclaim marshy areas
and lagoons The fill was placed upon lagoonal deposits consisting of silty sands with coral
fragments The lower clayey material was deposited during periodic influxes of non marine
alluvial deposits that apparently smothered the coral reef development Land development
and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought the area to its present form

Soil Profile Type for Seismic Design
As previously indicated the project site is generally underlain by surface fills
lagoonal deposits cinder sands and volcanic tuff formation In general the surface fills
consist of medium dense to very dense silty sands with gravel and stiff silty clays The surface
fills are generally underlain by lagoonal deposits consisting of very loose to loose silty sands
Based on our borings the thickness of the loose lagoonal deposits ranged from about 4 to
13 feet Our borings encountered dense to very dense cinder sands underlying the lagoonal
deposits Relatively competent volcanic tuff was generally encountered below the cinder
sands
Based on the average penetration resistance N values of the lagoonal deposits
encountered in the borings we believe that the project site may be classified as a Soil
Requiring Site Specific Evaluation Therefore we believe that the seismic design of the
building structures at the project site should be designed based on a Soil Profile Type S
in accordance with Table 16 J of the Uniform Building Code 1997
Liquefaction Potential
Liquefaction occurs when a build up of excess pore water pressure is induced on
saturated cohesionless soils resulting in a substantial loss of strength The most common
cause of this phenomenon is the cyclic loading due to earthquakes This process may permit
GEOLABS INC
W O 4542 10 Page 10
the soil to move both vertically and horizontally and if not confined can result in significant
deformations Loose uniformly graded fine grained sands and loose silts with little cohesion
are most susceptible to liquefaction Generally liquefaction is less likely to occur if the loose
deposit is deeper than 50 feet below the ground surface or the deposit contains an appreciable
percentage of clay
We believe that the loss of soil strength and associated ground movements could
potentially have a severe impact on the proposed development The subsurface information
obtained from the drilled borings indicate that the site is generally underlain by very loose to
loose silty sand lagoonal deposits at depths ranging from about 5 5 to 11 feet below the
existing ground surface Based on our analysis loose lagoonal deposits encountered at the
project site may have a potential to liquefy and lose soil strength under seismic conditions
assuming a maximum ground acceleration of 0 15g We estimate that the area within about
30 to 40 feet behind the marina wall or shoreline may experience some lateral ground
movements during a seismic event In order to reduce the potential structural damage due
to ground movements during a seismic event buildings should be set back at least 30 feet
from the marina wall at Parcels A and C The set back distance should be increased to 40 feet
at Parcel B where the thickness of loose lagoonal deposits are expected to be greater
We understand that several buildings will be located within the specified set back areas
To reduce the degree of damage to the buildings during a large seismic event we recommend
that special foundation designs should be incorporated for these buildings Specific
recommendations regarding the special foundations for buildings planned within the set back
areas will be issued under separate cover

Surcharge Program
To aid in the acceleration of the anticipated ground settlements we understand that a
surcharge program may be implemented at Parcel B prior to construction The surcharge
program should consist of the placement of an additional 6 feet of temporary surcharge fill over
the finished subgrades or finished grades prior to construction of various improvements Fill
materials required to raise the existing grades to the finish grades should conform to the
recommendations provided in the Site Grading section of this report
We recommend that a one month waiting period be implemented between completion
of the general site fill and placement of the temporary surcharge This waiting period is
required to allow subsoils to gain strength to reduce excessive lateral deformation and possible
failure within the soft subsoils
A slope ratio of up to one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V or flatter may be used
for the temporary surcharge fill material The surcharge fill should be placed in level lifts
of 18 inches in height and lightly compacted to maintain a stable embankment Adequate
precautions must be taken to preclude erosion of the surcharge fill slopes during
construction and the subsequent surcharge period to attain the required consolidation
In general the surcharge material should be similar to the construction fill material
Ideally if the surcharge material is the same as the construction fill material it may be used
on other fill areas at the project site after completion of the surcharge program
Based on our engineering analyses we believe that a minimum settlement period of
about 5 months after the completion of surcharge fill placement would be necessary to achieve
approximately 80 percent consolidation with a surcharge program However it should be
recognized that it is very difficult to accurately predict the exact time required for the filled
ground to settle since the settlement rate will be affected by variations of the subsoil structure
and the history of the subsoil deposition Therefore a settlement monitoring program should
be implemented as discussed in the following section

Settlement Monitoring
For a deposit of highly compressible soils such as the lagoonal deposits encountered
at the site we believe that the estimated settlement period could vary by as much as 50 to
100 percent from the actual settlement period required Therefore the actual settlement rate
should be monitored and provisions should be made for potential delays in the project
construction schedule if a longer period is required
To monitor the actual settlement rate we recommend that a minimum of
four settlement gauges be installed in the fill areas at Parcel B The approximate locations of
these recommended settlement gauges are shown on Site Plan Plate 2 A typical settlement
gauge detail is provided on Plate 3 The gauges should be read optically by a professional
surveyor and the readings should be transmitted to Geolabs for review in a timely manner
The settlement gauges should be established on the existing ground surface prior to any filling
operations A baseline elevation of the gauges should be established by two surveyed
readings taken a minimum of 24 hours apart Subsequently readings should be taken once
a week until the settlement rate has stabilized
A proper contingency plan should be devised to accommodate the potential delay in
construction schedule and the possible extra costs that may be involved

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
ten borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10 to depths of about 25 to 66 5 feet below
the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials encountered are
provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 10
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the volcanic tuff materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the tuff formation The tuff material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring
attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs
of Borings as REC 74

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Two one inch ring swell tests were performed on remolded samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of the tests are summarized on
Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The results
of the test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Ten sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 D 1140 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
Classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are presented on
Plates B 3 and B 4
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample of the soils
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics The results of the direct shear test are
presented on Plate B 5
One consolidation test with time rates of consolidation ASTM D 2435 was
performed on the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility
characteristics of the materials encountered Results of the consolidation test are
presented on Plate B 6
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils Results of the tests are presented on Plates B 7 and B 8
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The results of the tests are presented on Plates B 9 and B 10
Three sets of pH ASTM D 2976 and minimum resistivity ASTM G 57 tests were
performed to evaluate the corrosion potential of the soils The results of the tests are
summarized on Plate B 11
