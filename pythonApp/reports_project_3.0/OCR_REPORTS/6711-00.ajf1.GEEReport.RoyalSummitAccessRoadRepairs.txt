Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Road Repairs and Drainage Improvements Newtown Royal Summit
Access Road project in the Aiea area of the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project location
and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of site grading and
roadway pavements The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject
to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2
Project Considerations
The project site is a Board of Water Supply access road in the Newtown Royal
Summit area of Aiea on the Island of Oahu Hawaii This access road is paved with
asphaltic concrete and is along an existing waterline connecting Royal Summit and
Newtown Estates A portion of this access road has collapsed due to slope erosion and
subsequent undermining of the pavement We understand it is desired to realign the
road and provide a new paved surface
Based on preliminary information provided the new access road alignment is
roughly 200 feet long and will meander around the eroded area into the uphill side of the
sloping ground New fills on the order of about 4 feet or less are planned to fill in a
portion of the eroded area In addition a new concrete cutoff ditch will be provided
along the uphill side of the road

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which
time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These younger volcanic
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
worldwide cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less
water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present
stands of the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became
available such that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations and within the previously carved depressions
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by extrusion of the
lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows
of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The
weathering process has formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic soils In general
saprolite is composed mainly of silty material while residual soils are more clayey Both
residual and saprolitic soils are typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The
residual and saprolitic soils grade to basaltic rock formation with increased depth

Existing Site Conditions
The project site is a Board of Water Supply access road in the Newtown Royal
Summit area of Aiea on the Island of Oahu Hawaii This access road is paved with
asphaltic concrete and is located along an existing waterline connecting Royal Summit
and Newtown Estates A concrete cutoff ditch roughly 2 feet wide by 6 inches deep runs
along the eastern shoulder of the access road The location of the site is shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
The access road near the location of the eroded area traverses sloping ground
with an approximate inclination of about two and one half horizontal to one vertical
above and 2H 1V below the access road Based on the topographic survey
map provided the ground surface elevations range from about 447 feet Mean Sea
Level MSL at the failed portion of the access road to about 430 MSL at the bottom of
the eroded area
At the time of our site work the site was heavily overgrown with vegetation We
understand a section of the access road roughly 30 feet wide had been undermined
and subsequently collapsed due to excessive slope erosion on the downhill side of the
pavement A concrete drainage swale travels down the uphill side of the slope and
connects to a grated drainage ditch that crosses the access road We understand the
eroded slope may have been caused by concentrated runoff overflowing these drainage
structures and undermining the access road

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our borings generally encountered residual soils consisting of medium stiff to
hard silty clay to a maximum depth explored of 16 5 feet below the existing ground
surface Laboratory shear strength tests performed on samples obtained from the
borings indicate these residual soils have relatively high shear strength values
Based on the results from our slope stability analyses and the information
provided the slope and access road section failed from erosion by uncontrolled surface
runoff rather than a landslide which occurs when the shear strength within a soil mass
is exceeded Remedial designs and construction methods should be directed to
adequately support the new access road and protect the slope from additional erosion
in the future
In our opinion the proposed design concept of realigning the access road and
re grading the eroded out area is a viable remedial option provided effective drainage of
runoff is implemented to prevent excessive runoff from eroding the slope again Slope
stability analyses performed using the proposed slope geometry indicate the slope
should be stable in terms of global slope stability but may still be susceptible to slope
erosion and shallow raveling slides with a slope inclination of 2H 1V Therefore we
recommend implementing permanent erosion control matting consisting of a Turf
Reinforcement Mat TRM such as Tensar C350 or equivalent
We also recommend grading the slope to a relatively smooth surface removing
any rills and gullies These grading requirements are intended to provide a good contact
between the firm ground surface and the erosion control matting In addition the
matting should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
near the top edge of the slopes to prevent erosion extending beneath the matting
Another option we considered generally consisted of filling the eroded area with
grouted boulder rip rap This remedial option is technically feasible and can be
considered but is likely more expensive than the option provided above

optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness

Cut and Fill Slopes

and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement
recommendations

Pavement Design
We understand that flexible pavements are planned In general we anticipate the
vehicle loading for the pavements will primarily consist of passenger vehicles light pick
up trucks and occasional large trucks We have assumed that the pavement subgrade
will consist of the on site clayey soils encountered during our field exploration Based on
the above assumptions we recommend using the following pavement section for
preliminary design purposes
Flexible Pavements
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
8 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness over Compacted Subgrade
The pavement subgrade should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The subgrade soils should be
thoroughly moistened and kept moist until covered by the pavement structural section
The aggregate base course materials should consist of crushed basalt aggregate
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction CBR and field density tests
should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to
confirm the adequacy of the above sections
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to
carry surface water off site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on site during
or after construction

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling two borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 to depths of about 16 5 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 We used a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers to advance the borings
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field He monitored the drilling operations on a near continuous
basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by laboratory testing Soils
were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as
shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to
drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight determinations ASTM D 2937
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B 1
Two Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on
Plate B 2
One Modified Proctor Compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plate B 3
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a
bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The test results are presented on Plate B 4
Two Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 including one residual shear test was
performed on selected samples to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the
materials tested Results of the direct shear tests are presented on Plates B 5 through
