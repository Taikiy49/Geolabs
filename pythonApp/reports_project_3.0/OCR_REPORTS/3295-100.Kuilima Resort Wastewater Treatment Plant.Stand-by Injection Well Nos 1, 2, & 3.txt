INTRODUCTION
This report presents the findings of our injection well performance evaluation for the
Kuilima Resort Wastewater Treatment Plant in Kahuku on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
The findings and recommendations presented herein are based on observations recorded
during our site visit the performance of short term injection testing of the wells and
communication with the operators and manager of the treatment plant facility The
approximate location of the treatment plant facility and the injection well field are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
Our work was performed in general accordance with our fee proposal dated
March 15 2004 and the State of Hawaii Department of Health Underground Injection
Control UIC Injection Well Status Report Guidelines dated October 1999 This injection
well performance evaluation report covers the current monitoring period which extends
from June 2003 through May 2004
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Kuilima Resort Wastewater Treatment Plant injection wells are utilized for the
disposal of secondary treated effluent generated within the Turtle Bay Resort
development The majority of the effluent treated at the plant may be used for irrigation of
the resort golf courses located within the Turtle Bay Resort complex Therefore the wells
were used for the injection of treated effluent on a stand by basis during periods of time
when the effluent was not being utilized for irrigation purposes
As a condition of the Underground Injection Control UIC permit UIC Permit
No UQ 1532 issued for the facility it is required that routine monitoring of the wells be
GEOLABS INC
Hawaii California
W O 3295 100 Page 2
performed by a professional geologist or engineer familiar with injection well operations
Geolabs Inc has prepared this injection well evaluation report to provide the owner the
operator and the regulatory agency with information regarding the performance of the
injection wells to aid in the prevention of adverse public health effects from an injection
well system malfunction or failure This evaluation is intended to assist in ascertaining that
the well system remains operational

DISCUSSIONS AND FINDINGS
The wells are located on the easterly side of the tee boxes for the 10th Hole of Golf
Course G 2 The wells are situated in an open grass area between the tees and the moat
around Punahoolapa Marsh The topography of the well field area is generally level with
some topographic mounds and depressions associated with the adjacent golf course
terrain Based on a topographic map of the well field and vicinity ground surface
elevations range from about 5 to 6 feet Mean Sea Level MSL At the time of our site
visit on May 4 2004 the weather conditions were partly cloudy with passing rain showers
During our site visit the ocean tide was noted as being generally low in height and rising
rapidly throughout the morning
Based on the available tidal information for Haleiwa Harbor a low tide of 0 34 feet
occurred at 0705 hours which subsequently rose to a high tide of 1 71 feet at
1518 hours Thus the recorded head levels in the wells may have shown the delayed
effects of the large range of tidal fluctuation that occurred on the morning of May 4 2004
No evidence of surface water ponding was visible in the vicinity of the well field Some
residual rainwater from earlier rain showers was observed within the well vaults at the time
of our inspection
The well heads are enclosed in below ground sump vaults of approximately 4 feet
by 4 feet in plan dimension and about 5 feet in depth The vaults are covered with hinged

Hawaii California

surface
inside the vaults the well heads consist of steel tees with flange plates bolted to
their tops for security and to allow the build up of additional head if needed These plates
were removed by plant personnel for the well inspection and performance of the injection
tests In 1994 two inch diameter inspection ports were installed in the well covers to
eliminate the need for removal of the entire top plate during future inspection testing
However to allow complete access to the well interior for the performance of the
short term injection tests and visual inspection of the exposed well casing the top plate of
each well was removed
Well Field Usage
In general only two of the three wells are placed in use for effluent disposal at any
time The inactive well is taken out of operation on a rotating basis so that at any given
time only two wells are in operation Flow to an individual well is controlled by a valve
located adjacent to each well head Each valve is housed in a steel cased vault of about
12 inches in diameter A main control valve diverts the effluent flow from the treatment
plant to either the well field or the irrigation pond which serves as a storage reservoir for
golf course irrigation water
The following information pertaining to the effluent flow to the well field is based on
a review of the available WWTP Daily Operating Log records for the period May 2003
through April 2004 and personal communication with the plant manager According to the
plant manager irrigation of the golf course using the WWTP effluent was again
discontinued since about November 2003 due to concerns about possible turf yellowing
caused by the effluent use Therefore during the period of November 2003 through the
date of our current inspection the injection wells were operating on a near daily basis
since the effluent was not being used for turf irrigation purposes According to the WWTP
manager during the last 12 months the wells were typically activated for about eight
hours per day or less

injection Well Redevelopment
Based on information provided by the treatment plant manager the three injection
wells are now aerated and pumped out twice per year instead of once per year as
previously performed The injection wells were aerated and pumped out between
November 1 and and again between April 13 and in a
routine effort to maintain the disposal capacity of the system
A 130 cubic foot per minute cfm air compressor was utilized to blow out the
injection wells by lowering the air line into the bottom of the well The discharge of well
water produced by the airlift cleaning was contained within the well vault and pumped to a
tanker truck for proper disposal Approximately 6 000 gallons of sludge and debris was
removed on November 6 and on It was reported that Well Nos 1 and 2
produced occasionally moderate to heavy fine sediment while Well No 3 produced mostly
light to moderate fine sediment during the aeration and pumping procedure

Short Term Injection Tests
Short term injection tests were performed in each of the three wells by diverting the
effluent into each well separately Each well was isolated from the others for the duration
of each test by adjusting the valves controlling the effluent flow to individual wells
During injection testing the head level response was continually measured and
recorded using an electronic piezometer pressure transducer and data logging recorder
linked to a portable computer Thus the head level response could be monitored
continuously and simultaneously to record the fluctuation of head levels within the wells
Manual readings collected from the totalizing flow meter were recorded during the
short term injection test to obtain the total quantity of injected effluent and to estimate the
average rate of flow into each well The duration of each short term injection test was
approximately 40 minutes in length The average incoming flow rate to the well field from
GEOLABS INC
Hawaii California
W O 3295 100 Page 15
the WWTP was calculated to range between about 560 gallons per minute and
615 gallons per minute during the injection testing Occasional fluctuation of the incoming
flow was observed ranging between about 500 gallons per minute and 700 gallons per
minute The incoming flow rate to the well field was not adjusted during the short term
injection testing

Recommendations
Based on the injection test results and the well usage data we believe the injection
wells have generally maintained their disposal capacity since the last inspection which
occurred in June 2003 The maintenance of the injection capacity is believed to be a
GEOLABS INC
Hawaii California
W O 3295 100 Page 20
result of the recent well cleaning performed on the wells and the continued rotation of the
injection well usage
Injection wells sustain losses in capacity over time due to natural biological and
chemical fouling processes or other introduced factors such as suspended solids or debris
contained in the effiuent In addition extended periods of well inactivity and the
introduction of poor quality effluent containing excessive suspended solids may cause
acceleration of the normal losses in capacity It should be noted that some loss in capacity
may be permanent and irreversible however it has also been proven that redevelopment
of injection wells can aid in the recovery of lost capacity provided that the proper methods
are utilized on a regular basis
Based on our injection well evaluation we recommend that the owner operator
continue to operate the injection wells on a routine rotating basis in an attempt to maintain
the disposai capacity of the system In addition we recommend that the owner operator
closely monitor and document the operation of each of the wells to assure that overflows
or back ups related to a well failure are detected prior to their occurrence In the event an
overflow of a well or other failure occurs and the situation cannot be remedied the
owner operator should promptly notify the Department of Health Safe Drinking Water
Branch
We believe that by maintaining the use of the wells on a rotating basis especially
during periods of low effluent availability the current operational performance of the wells
should be maintained Keeping the wells operational is not normally a problem however
we believe it is crucial to maintain rotating operation of all the wells during the periods of
time when low effluent flow are experienced
We recommend that the owner operator continue recording field observations such
as the following 1 notes regarding the overall system operation 2 documentation of well
maintenance performed including well cleaning and replacement of faulty parts and
instruments 3 observation of the head levels within the wells prior to and during injection
GEOLABS INC
Hawaii California
W O 3295 100 Page 21
events 4 daily flow volumes 5 a log of individual well usage and 6 notes pertaining to
the increase or decrease of the total flow to the plant for treatment This information will be
used to evaluate the future performance of the injection wells In addition the results of
our short term injection testing of the wells may be used to evaluate the operation of the
wells with respect to prior well inspection data
Generally redevelopment should be considered if head levels continue to increase
over time or when well failures such as overflow or system back ups may be anticipated
However when the wells begin to fail it may often be too late to restore the capacity
Therefore based on the effectiveness of the recent well redevelopment we believe it may
be prudent to continue scheduling well redevelopment at annual intervals
Well redevelopment should be performed by a professional experienced with
coastal injection well construction and rehabilitation The redevelopment method selected
should be carefully evaluated based on the existing well design proper containment and
disposal of wastewater generated by the activity and other factors specific to the site and
operation of the wells
Based on our observation of the valves that control the flow to each injection
well the valves appear to be working satisfactorily however the valves are not visible
due to their continual submergence below water Therefore the valve cannot be
visually inspected In addition the long term and continual submergence of the valve
may accelerate corrosion of the hardware We do not believe that this is a critical
concern however consideration may be given to reconstructing the valve vault so that
it is sealed from the infiltration of groundwater to facilitate inspection for signs of wear
In consideration of other possible long term improvements that could facilitate
monitoring of the injection well performance it is suggested that individual flow metering
devices be installed between each well control valve and wellhead The installation of
additional flow meters would provide direct readout of flow data for each individual well
which would improve the direct correlation between the flow rate and the head level
GEOLABS INC
Hawaii California
W O 3295 100 Page 22
response in the well As a result changes in the well flow rate could be monitored and
adjustments to the control valve could be made to achieve the desired flow rates that
can be compared from year to year Currently only the total flow to the well field is
known therefore when more than one well is operating simultaneously it is not possible
to estimate the actual flow rate into the individual wells This scenario occurs when the
actual capacity of a well is lower than the flow entering the well field from the plant and
a second well must be opened to avoid overflow at the well being tested Currently the
only means to vary the individual well flow rate is to make adjustments to the total flow
entering from the treatment plant However due to the significant distance between the
treatment plant and the well field a 15 to 20 minute delay is typically experienced
before the flow adjustment is observed at the well field In addition individual well flow
meters may be used to detect possible leaks and could be used to record the total flow
to each well for comparison between well usage
Finally based on our understanding of the requirements of the facility UIC permit
the annual injection well inspection should be performed in the month of May since the
annual monitoring period is set to run from June through May The owner and facility
operator is reminded to periodically review the requirements of the UIC permit set forth
by the State of Hawaii Department of Health Safe Drinking Water Branch to assure
that the operational procedures and reporting requirements are adhered to
