PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose for our engineering evaluation was to obtain the necessary field data to
supplement the UIC permit application for the subject drywell In order to accomplish the
objectives our exploration program generally consisted of the following tasks and work
efforts
1 Research and review of the available information pertaining to the UIC permit
application The information was obtained from the Department of Heaith
Safe Drinking Water Branch located in Honolulu
2 Mobilization and demobilization of equipment and labor to assist in providing
access to the drywell vault
3 Mobilization and demobilization of down hole video camera equipment and
operators to the project site and back
4 Performance of down hole video in the drywell to obtain recorded information
for use in preparing a lithologic log and description of the condition of the
drywell
5 Measurement of the depth and diameter of the drywell including observation
of the well head for signs of damage or unsuitable conditions
6 Mobilization demobilization and rental of a 4 000 gallon tanker truck and
operator at the site
7 Performance of a short term injection test by our field engineer to evaluate
the capacity of the drywell
8 Analyses of the video and injection test data by a project geologist from our
firm The data were used to prepare the lithologic log and estimate the
capacity of the drywell
9 Coordination of our overall work by a project manager from our firm
10 Preparation of six copies of this report summarizing our work and presenting
our findings
11 Quality assurance of our overall work on the project and consultation by a
principal engineer from our firm

FINDINGS DISCUSSION
The subject drywell facility is on the southern side of Alehela Street located
between Lono Street and Molokai Hema Street intersections in the town of Kahului on
the Island of Maui Hawaii The drywell evaluation was performed on January 13
and 26 under generally fair weather conditions The steel grate covering the concrete
catch basin was removed and the steel elbow pipe drywell inlet was unbolted and
removed for access to the vertically oriented well casing
Physical Charateristics
Our observation of the visible portion of the concrete catch basin and well casing
indicated that the system was in generally good condition with no overt signs of leakage
or deterioration The static water level existing in the well prior to testing was measured
at 2 feet below the top of the well casing Based on our observation of the existing rust
and scale coating on the interior well casing the relatively high static water level
observed in the well should reflect normal groundwater level condition
The depth of the well was measured by sounding with a weighted tape to locate
the bottom of the well The measured well depth was confirmed during the down hole
video recording effort The depth and diameter measurements including a sketch of the
drywell are presented on the attached Summary of Drywell Dimensions and Lithologic
Log Plate 1
Down Hole Video
The down hole video recording was conducted to evaluate the conditions within
the cased and open hole segments of the well Wailani Drilling Co of Maui performed
the video work under the direction of our field engineer The equipment used was a
GeoVision Micro Down Hole color video camera The video obtained from the well was
recorded on a VHS tape for later review and analyses
The camera recorded a continuous video of the well condition along with
corresponding depth data Based on our review of the video recording the interior of
the well appeared to be in generally fair condition Visibility below the water table was
poor at times especially within the cased portion of the well where the water appeared
cloudy with a relatively high concentration of suspended particles Water clarity
generally improved within the open hole segment of the well
Due to the solid steel casing lining the upper portion of the well the lithology of
the cased portion of the well was inferred based on available geologic data and our
experience in the vicinity of the project site We believe that the well casing was
installed to protect against caving in of the potentially loose sands alluvial soils and
weathered rock comprising the upper portion of the drywell The lithologic log for the
well is presented on the attached Summary of Drywell Dimensions and Lithologic Log
Plate 1

Injection Testing
A short term injection test was conducted by our field engineer to evaluate the
disposal capacity of the drywell A 4 000 gallon capacity tanker equipped with a pump was
used to introduce clear fresh water into the drywell The usable volume of the tanker
source water to perform the test was estimated to be about 3 900 gallons A pressure
transducer and electronic data logger were utilized to collect and record the head level
response within the drywell
in general the static water level in the well was recorded prior to the introduction of
water for the injection test Approximately 3 600 gallons of fresh water was pumped into
the well during the injection test The average flow rate was estimated at about
140 gallons per minute gpm The test was terminated after pumping the usable volume
of the tanker source water into the well The duration of the test was approximately
26 minutes
Based on the injection test results the drywell appeared to be operating
satisfactorily at an average injection rate of about 140 gpm despite the occurrence of the
relatively high static water level in the well With an average injection rate of about 140
gpm the head level rose approximately 0 16 feet above the static water level Thus it
appears that the drywell disposal capacity may be somewhat greater than about 140 gpm
The results of the short term injection testing are presented on the attached Injection Test
Results Plates 2 and 3
LIMITATIONS
The evaluation presented in this report is based upon visual observations
down hole video recording and short term testing of the injection well Variation from
our findings may occur and the nature of such variations may not become evident until
at a later point in time If variations then appear evident it will become necessary to
reevaluate the findings in this report
