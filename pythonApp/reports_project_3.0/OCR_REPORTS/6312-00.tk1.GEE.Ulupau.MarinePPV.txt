performed for the Ulupau site of the Hawaii Regional Housing Privatization Phase 5
Marine Sites project within the Marine Corps Base Hawaii MCBH at Kaneohe Bay on

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was partially
buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank The Koolau Volcano
reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
The project is on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano and is composed of
basaltic rock built by the extrusion of the lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks
are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin
clinker layers In situ chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the
last 1 to 2 million years The weathering process has formed a surface mantle of residual
soils which grade to saprolite with depth In general saprolite is mainly composed of silty
material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks Saprolite grades to
basaltic rock with increasing depth

Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we envision
that the new dwellings at the Ulupau MCBH site will be underlain by stiff dense surface
fills weathered volcanic tuff materials and coralline deposits encountered at the project
site Therefore we recommend supporting the new dwellings on a shallow foundation
system consisting of spread and or continuous footings As an alternative foundations
for support of the structural loads of the residential structures may also consist of
thickened edge slab footings An allowable bearing pressure on order of about
2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used in the design of footings bearing on the
on site soils This bearing value is for sizing footings based on dead plus live loads and
may be increased by one third 1 3 when considering transient loads such as those
caused by wind or seismic forces
Typically we recommend embedding the footings 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent exterior grade based on the subsurface conditions at the site Due to localized
fine grading for the drainage swales we believe that the footing embedment may be
reduced to a minimum of 20 inches locally provided that the surface is adequately
sloped minimum 2 percent for drainage purposes and maintained at all times It should
be noted that the recommended allowable bearing pressure of remains
applicable for the project with this reduced footing embedment depth Foundations next
to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below an imaginary 45 degree
plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or the footings should
extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
If the shallow foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with
our recommendations we estimate the total settlements of footings to be on the order of
about 1 inch or less Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on
similar materials should be on the order of about 0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth

Slabs On Grade
We envision that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the new residential
structures constructed at or near the existing grades Based on the laboratory test
results the clayey fill soils encountered at the project site exhibit a moderate to high
potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations
To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade
resulting from shrinking and swelling of the surface silty and clayey soils we
recommend preparing the subgrade properly and capping the subgrade with a
18 inch thick layer of non expansive select granular fill materials which should
extend a minimum of 2 feet beyond the edges of the structures A schematic detail
of the slabs on grade with thickened edge footings is shown on the attached
Plate 4
Prior to placing the non expansive select granular fill we recommend scarifying the
clayey subgrade soils to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioning the soils
to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacting to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The non expansive select granular fill
should also be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction

Garage Slabs and Concrete Driveways
We envision that concrete garage slabs and driveways will be required for the
new residential structures In addition we anticipate that the garage slabs and
driveways will be constructed near the existing grades In general we recommend
supporting the garage slabs and concrete driveways directly on the 18 inch thick layer
of non expansive select granular fill materials or aggregate subbase in lieu of the
cushion fill layer The moisture barrier and the 2 inch moist fine sand layer may also be
omitted for garage slabs and concrete driveways
The compaction requirement for the finished subgrade of the 18 inch thick layer
of non expansive select granular fills or aggregate subbase supporting the garage slab
and or driveway should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
The non expansive select granular fill materials or aggregate subbase should be
placed as soon as possible after preparation of the slab subgrade To reduce the
potential for substantial shrinkage cracks in the slabs crack control joints should be
provided at intervals equal to the width of the slabs with expansion joints at right angle
intersections
We recommend designing the concrete slabs for the intended loads For design
of structural slabs subject to vehicular traffic a modulus of subgrade reaction of
200 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted
non expansive select granular fill or aggregate subbase In addition provisions should
be made for proper load transfer across slab joints that will be subject to vehicular
traffic

Corrosion Potential
A total of 60 soil resistivity surveys were conducted at fifteen selected locations
designated as R 101 through R 115 on the Site Plan Plate 2 using the in situ
Wenner 4 Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured by
Nilsson Electrical Laboratory Inc The method utilizes four surface probes or pins
electrodes to evaluate the average resistivity of the underlying soil mass The average
resistivity values of the soil mass extending from the existing ground surface to the test
depth was assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 Results of the field soil resistivity surveys are presented on
Plates C 1 and C 2 of Appendix C
In addition we conducted laboratory tests including pH Minimum Resistivity
Chloride Contents and Sulfate Contents to evaluate the corrosivity of the underlying
subsurface soils encountered at the project site Based on the test results presented on
Plate B 10 the subsurface soils at the project site generally exhibit a minimum
resistivity value of about 1 300 ohm cm indicating that the subsurface soils are very
corrosive to buried metallic structures Therefore we recommend properly designing
substructures especially metallic substructures to protect against potential corrosion A
corrosion engineer should be consulted for detailed recommendations on corrosion
protection

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the housing project site by drilling and
sampling eleven borings designated as Boring Nos 101 through 111 extending to
depths of about 19 7 to 21 5 feet below the existing ground pavement surface The
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 We used a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 11
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Pocket penetrometer test results
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
Eight one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are
summarized on Plate B 1
Five Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The
results of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths The test results are presented on Plate B 2
Two Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 and 136 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification The test results are presented on Plate B 3
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The samples were remolded to near the optimum moisture content and
saturated The test results are presented on Plates B 4 through B 7
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 8 and B 9
Two sets of minimum resistivity ASTM G57 chloride content
and sulfate content tests were performed by our office and
TestAmerica Analytical Laboratory Corporation on selected soil samples obtained from
our field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the in situ soils to buried metallic and
concrete substructures The test results are summarized on Plate B 10

Field Resistivity Tests
Resistivity generally is recognized as one of the most significant soil
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects In
general the lower the resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried
metallic structure Conversely the higher the resistivity the less likely the soil will
contribute to corrosion of metallic objects
Geophysical survey equipment i e resistance meter was mobilized to the
project site on for the field survey Sixty soil resistivity surveys were
conducted at fifteen locations designated as R 101 through R 115 on the Site Plan
Plate 2 using the in Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance
meter manufactured by Nilsson Electrical Laboratory Inc Our field resistivity work was
performed under sunny to cloudy conditions with generally damp to moist surface
conditions
The method utilizes four surface probes or pins electrodes to evaluate the
average resistivity an indication of corrosivity of the underlying soil mass The average
resistivity values of the soil mass extending from the existing ground surface to the test
depth was assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations
The average soil resistance was evaluated at each survey location with the
electrodes aligned at equal spacings of various distances based on the anticipated
utility depths The depth of penetration of a resistivity sounding is a function of the
electrode spacing
The resistance meter Nilsson Model 400 provides a voltage between the outer
electrodes causing an electrical current to flow between them The voltage drop
between the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive built in voltmeter As a
result the soil resistance in ohms is obtained from the voltmeter dial reading The
average soil resistivity in ohms cm which is directly related to both resistance and
spacing can then be computed Results of the field resistivity surveys are presented on
Plates C 1 and C 2 It should be noted that the soil resistivity may be influenced by
temperature weather conditions soil moisture existing underground utilities and other
factors
