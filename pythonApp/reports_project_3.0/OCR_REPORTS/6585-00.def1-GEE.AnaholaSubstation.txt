SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This geotechnical engineering exploration report presents the results of our 
analyses and geotechnical recommendations for the Kauai Island Utility Cooperative 
Anahola Substation project in the Anahola area on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The 
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our 
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for site grading and design of 
foundations, retaining structures, site preparation and pavements only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report.  
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is presently about 2 acres of fallow land that was formerly used 
for the cultivation of sugar cane.  The site gently slopes down towards the east. 
It is proposed to construct a new electrical substation on the site to serve a future 
photovoltaic generating facility that will be constructed adjacent to the site.  It is 
anticipated that grading for the new substation will consist of shallow cuts and fills to 
construct pads for the various equipment that will be located on-site.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from a 
single shield volcano.  Following the extrusion of shield building lavas, the main Kauai 
dome underwent a long cycle of erosion and weathering deeply dissecting the island. 
After this erosional period, there was a renewal of volcanic activity with the Koloa 
Volcanic Series covering large portions of the island, including the Lihue Basin, a large 
caldera-like structure on the southeastern portion of the island, extending from Lihue to 
north of Anahola.  In many areas, the Koloa lavas have been subjected to deep 
chemical weathering, forming a mantle of residual soil and saprolite which grade with 
depth to weathered basalt rock formation. 
The project site is situated on the northeast portion of the Lihue Basin.  The 
project site is underlain by residual clayey silt soils derived from the Koloa Volcanic 
Series. 
2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is situated in the Anahola area immediately to the west of 
Kuhio Highway on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii, as shown on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1.  The project site is presently about 2 acres of fallow land that was formerly used 
for the cultivation of sugar cane.  The site gently slopes down towards the east. 
The site is roughly rectangular in plan view.  The site is bounded to the north, 
south and west by vacant land that was formerly used for the cultivation of sugar cane; 
and, to the east by Kuhio Highway.   
The terrain within the site generally slopes down gently to the east towards the 
highway.  Based on the topographic map provided by Esaki Surveying and Mapping, 
the ground surface elevations at the site range from about +190 feet Mean Sea Level 
(MSL) at the western boundary of the site to about +180 feet MSL near the eastern 
boundary.  The site is covered with light brush and grass.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered competent residual soils underlying 
most of the site.  Based on our laboratory tests, these soils exhibit a low to moderate 
expansion potential.   
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field 
exploration. 
Minor cuts and fills are anticipated for the site grading.  We believe that cuts and 
fills will range from about 2 to 4 feet to achieve the finished grades for the proposed site 
improvements.  In general, cut slopes and permanent fill slopes constructed of the 
on-site native and imported fill soils may be designed with a slope inclination of 
two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. 
In order to provide uniform bearing for the foundations, we recommend providing 
a 6-inch thick layer of compacted base course below the foundations of the structures.  
The compacted base course should extend beyond the edges of the foundations or 
structures by at least 2 feet.  The fill materials should be placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 6 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may 
be used for design of foundations bearing on the compacted base course placed over 
re-compacted on-site materials.  This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may 
be increased by 50 percent for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces.

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
In general, the areas within the contract grading limits should be cleared and 
grubbed thoroughly at the on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious 
material, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly 
off-site or disposed in a designated area to reduce the potential for contaminating 
the excavated materials. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during the clearing and grubbing should be 
over-excavated to expose firm natural material, and the resulting excavation should 
be backfilled with well-compacted engineered fill. In general, the excavated soft and 
wet soils may not be re-used as a source of fill and backfill materials. 
After clearing and grubbing, the existing ground surface in areas designated to 
receive fill or to remain at grade should be scarified to a depth of 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum

3.1.2 Fill and Backfill Materials 
In general, the excavated on-site materials may be used as a source of general fill 
materials, provided that they are free of deleterious materials.  
Imported fill materials required for the project construction should consist of 
non-expansive select granular material, such as crushed coral and basalt. The 
material should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR 
value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when 
tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883. 
3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fill materials (on-site soils and imported materials) should be placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. Compaction should be

3.1.4 Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, cut slopes and permanent fill slopes constructed of the on-site soils may 
be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills placed on slopes 
steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide 
stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations should start at the 
lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance 
with the above fill placement recommendations. 
When the existing ground slope is steeper than 5H:1V in an area designated to 
receive fill, keys and benches should be provided to bond the fill to the existing 
slope.  These benches should be cut horizontally into the existing ground with a 
minimum width of about 5 feet and a height not exceeding about 3 feet.  The bench 
shelf should, as much as practicable, be founded in firm undisturbed natural 
ground.  
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. In the event that over-cutting of a slope 
occurs, keying and benching requirements should be implemented instead of 
backfilling the slope to the design grade with sliver fills. Water should be diverted 
away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as 
possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes. 
3.1.5 Excavation 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by stiff to very 
hard clayey silts. We anticipate that the near-surface materials may be excavated 
with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as a backhoe excavator, or ripped 
with a large bulldozer.  However, based on our understanding of the subsurface 
conditions at the site, there is a potential for encountering remnant boulders in 
excavations at the site.

3.2 
Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we envision 
that shallow footings, such as continuous strip foundations and spread footings, may be 
used to support the new structures and pads planned for the substation.   
In order to provide uniform bearing support for the foundations, we recommend 
providing a 6-inch thick layer of compacted base course below the foundations of the 
structures.  The compacted base course should extend beyond the edges of the 
foundations or structures by at least 2 feet.  The fill materials should be placed in level 
lifts, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to at 
least 95 percent relative compaction. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for design of 
foundations bearing on the compacted base course placed over re-compacted on-site 
materials.  This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by 
50 percent for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
For design of on-grade switchgear pads, a modulus of subgrade reaction of 
200 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection (pci) may be used for the compacted 
base course. 
In general, the foundations should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below 
the lowest adjacent finished grade.  Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on 
sloping ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal 
setback distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of 
the slope.

In order to reduce the potential for appreciable differential settlement, the new 
structures should be situated entirely over cut or entirely over fill.  They should not 
straddle across cut and fill transitions. If foundations are designed and constructed in 
accordance with our recommendations, we estimate that the total settlements of 
foundations to be less than 1 inch. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed 
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth 
pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system.  A coefficient 
of friction of 0.40 may be used for footings bearing on the base course.  Resistance due 
to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf).  This assumes that the soils around 
the footings are well compacted.  Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive 
pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected. 
3.3 
Exterior Flatworks 
We envision concrete pads and other exterior flatwork may be constructed at the 
project site. In general, we recommend providing a minimum 6-inch thick cushion fill 
layer consisting of aggregate base course below the concrete pads for uniform support.  
The aggregate base course should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative 
compaction. To reduce the potential for substantial shrinkage cracks in the concrete 
slabs, crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the 
concrete pads with expansion joints at right-angle intersections. 
3.4 
Retaining Structures 
We anticipate that retaining walls may be required to provide grade separation at 
various locations on the site.  In general, we believe that retaining structure foundations 
may be designed in accordance with the recommendations and parameters presented 
in the "Foundations" section herein. However, the retaining structure footings should 
have a minimum width of 18 inches. In addition, wall foundations on relatively flat areas 
should be embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished 
grade.

3.5 
Pavements 
We understand that access roads with flexible pavements will be constructed for 
the substation project.  In general, we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the access 
road for this project would be very light and would consist primarily of occasional service 
trucks only.  Therefore, we have assumed generally light to medium traffic loading 
conditions for preliminary pavement design purposes.   
Based on our borings and laboratory testing, we recommend that the following 
preliminary pavement design be used for this project: 
Flexible Pavement Section 
2.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete 
6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction)  . 
8.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade 
The subgrade soils under the pavement areas should be scarified to a minimum 
depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted 
to at least 95 percent relative compaction.  Due to the high moisture content of the 
on-site soils, we anticipate that there may be a potential for pumping subgrade 
conditions during the subgrade preparation. If the subgrade soils are pumping during 
preparation of the subgrade soils, we recommend providing a woven geotextile fabric, 
such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent, on the subgrade prior to placement of the aggregate 
base or subsequent lifts of fill.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from in-house literature research and our field exploration. 
Variations of subsurface conditions between and beyond the field exploration points 
may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until 
construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to 
re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The boring locations indicated in this report were located in the field by taping 
from reference points shown on the topographic plan transmitted by Esaki Surveying 
and Mapping on February 12, 2011.  Elevations of the borings were estimated based on 
interpolation between the spot elevations and contour lines shown on the same 
topographic plan. The physical locations and elevations of the borings should be 
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings or test pits at the 
time of our exploration.  These data have been reviewed and interpretations made in 
the formulation of this report. However, it must be noted that groundwater levels may 
vary with seasonal rainfall, temperature, and other factors.

Anahola Substation project in accordance with generally accepted geotechnical

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, to maximum depths of about 
21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with auger equipment. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling and excavation operations on 
a near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified 
Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in 
general accordance with ASTM  D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving 
a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) 
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification 
and evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings 
at the appropriate sample depths. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D 3080) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the shear strength parameters. The test results are presented on Plates B-1 
and B-2. 
One Unconfined Compression test (ASTM D 2166) was performed on a selected 
in-situ cohesive soil sample to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the 
soils. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depth and graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-3. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic representations of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-4.
