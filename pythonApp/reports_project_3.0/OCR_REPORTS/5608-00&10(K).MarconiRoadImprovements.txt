In accordance with our fee proposals dated March 10 and May 1 2008 we
performed a geotechnical engineering exploration for the Marconi Road Improvements
project in the Kahuku area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii This report summarizes the
findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing and presents our
recommendations for the project design The findings and recommendations are

letter report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is near the existing Marconi Road in the vicinity of the Turtle Bay
Resort in the Kahuku area in the District of Koolauloa on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Based on the information provided we understand that the new Marconi Road
alignment will be located approximately 44 feet to the southeast of the existing paved road
The majority of the new Marconi Road will be a 64 foot right of way roadway transitioning
to a 56 foot right of way roadway near the entrance to the future Marconi Point and
Onomea Bay development as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Details of the site grading
work were not available at the time this report was prepared In addition new utilities such
as water drain and sewer lines may be planned for the project to serve the future
agricultural subdivision development

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about 11 and 11 5 feet
below the existing ground pavement surface In addition two bulk samples of the
near surface soils designated as Bulk 1 and Bulk 2 were collected for laboratory CBR
and R value tests to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the near surface
soils The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
In general our field exploration disclosed that the southern portion of the new
roadway alignment towards Kamehameha Highway side is underlain by alluvial deposits
of hard silty clays extending to the depth explored of approximately 11 feet below the
existing ground surface The alluvial deposits reduce in thickness towards the central
portion and the alignment is generally underlain by coral and sandstone formation from the
existing ground surface at the northern end towards the ocean side
Groundwater was encountered in two of the borings at depths of about 5 2 and
6 feet below the existing ground pavement surface at the time of our field exploration The
groundwater levels measured generally correspond to about Elevations 1 8 and 1 5 feet
Mean Sea Level MSL Due to the proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean it
should be noted that groundwater levels are expected to change with tidal fluctuations
Seasonal precipitation storm surge condition surface water runoff and other factors may
also influence the groundwater levels at the project site Descriptions and graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 3 of Appendix A
FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site and the traffic data
provided for our pavement analyses we recommend that new pavements for the new
Marconi Road consist of 2 inches of AC on 3 inches of ATB over 6 inches of AB in areas
where non expansive granular soils are exposed at the pavement subgrade level A
thicker pavement section consisting of 2 inches of AC on 4 inches of ATB over 12 inches
of AB should be used for the new pavements where expansive clayey soils are exposed at
the pavement subgrade level It should be noted that CBR tests and or field observations
should be performed on the actual subgrade soils during construction to confirm that the
above pavement design sections are adequate
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement surface
should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Detailed discussion of our geotechnical
engineering recommendations for design of pavements and site preparation are presented
in the following sections of this report

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base course subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the
pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to
carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water
ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction
Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the
pavement areas we recommend the curbs extend a minimum of 2 inches below the
aggregate base layer to reduce the potential for migration of excessive landscape water
into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to
collect excess water from the landscaping irrigation For long term performance we
recommend considering a subdrain system along the sides of the roadway
Site Preparation
In general the area within the contract grading limits should be cleared and
grubbed thoroughly at the on set of earthwork Vegetation debris deleterious material
and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site Soft
and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or
future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm and or stiff material and
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The excavated
soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed off site
After clearing and grubbing the future pavement areas should be excavated
where necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the aggregate base
course layer Where expansive clayey soils are exposed at the pavement subgrade
level the pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and re compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction If granular materials or coral formation are
exposed at or near the bottom of the aggregate base course layer the subgrade should
be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of eight passes
to help detect near surface cavities and or loose zones prior to the pavement
construction
If pumping conditions are encountered during compaction of the subgrade soils
the subgrade soils may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for

Imported fill materials below the aggregate base course layer if required should
consist of non expansive select granular material such as crushed coral or basalt The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches
in largest dimension In addition the material should contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value
of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in
accordance with ASTM D 1883 The materials should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Geolabs should test imported fill materials
for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use
A Golabs representative should monitor site grading operations to observe

In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open graded ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation should be provided below the pipes Free draining granular materials
such as No 3B Fine gravel also should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes or groundwater level to provide adequate support around the
pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation
of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of
the pipes Improper backfill material and placement of the initial trench backfill could
result in backfill settlement and pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
or groundwater level to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of
excavated on site soils with a maximum particle size of 6 inches or select granular fill
material The backfill should be placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are below
pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Based on our field exploration the project site is underlain by coral sandstone
formation near the existing ground surface towards the central and northern end of the
roadway alignment The coral sandstone formation encountered in the borings
appeared to be moderately to highly weathered and soft to medium hard in nature It is
anticipated that the majority of the coral sandstone formation may be excavated with
normal heavy excavation equipment such as excavating with large backhoe
excavators However coral formations typically contain localized hard and crystallized
zones Therefore we anticipate that some difficult excavation conditions may arise in
localized areas during construction It may be necessary to excavate the trenches in
some areas with the use of hoe rams or by chipping
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the materials are based on our

project site We recommend encouraging contractors proposing to bid on this project to

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about 11 and 11 5 feet
below the existing ground pavement surface The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers The
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Log Legend Plate A The materials encountered
are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch outside diameter OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Pocket penetrometer test results are
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Traffic Impact of Proposed Project The proposed project will improve Marconi Road
and subdivide some of the land served by it The proposed subdivision will create seven
buildable 20 acre parcels at Onomea Bay and 29 Agricultural lots at Marconi Point The
likely development would be the construction of one dwelling unit per buildable lot
The further subdivision of these lands could increase the number of dwelling units
that affect traffic on Marconi Road so a maximum density case was also considered
Table 3 shows estimates of the traffic generated by the proposed project and by a future
use of these lands with the conservative assumption that any dwelling units will be used as
typical suburban dwellings with residents commuting to and from work and other
activities the use of the subdivided property for agricultural purposes or use of any
dwelling units by retirees as recreational homes or on a part time basis would generate
less traffic
