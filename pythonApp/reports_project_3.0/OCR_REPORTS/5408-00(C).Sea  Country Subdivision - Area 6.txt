SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As required by the City and County of Honolulu a slope assessment and potential
rockfall hazard evaluation was conducted for the proposed Sea Country Subdivision
Area 6 in an effort to study the potential rockfall hazards present at the project site and to
provide recommendations for mitigation of the potential rockfall hazards Our ground
surface reconnaissance and mapping effort at the project site consisted of identifying the
source s of potential rockfall on the hillside and mapping the general distribution of
surface boulders that appear to have rolled to the lower elevations of the hillside In
addition rockfall simulation using the Colorado Rockfall Simulation Program CRSP was
performed to estimate the rockfall behavior emanating from the identified source area
Based on our evaluation of the conditions at the project site we recommend the
installation of a rockfall impact barrier system above the existing concrete drainage
channel located along the upslope boundary of the development to provide some level of
protection emanating from potential rockfall hazards upslope of the project site The
rockfall impact barrier system should consist of two rockfall barrier alignments totaling
about 2 738 linear feet in combined length The rockfall impact barrier alignments should
consist of multiple barrier segments with rated energy absorption capacities of about
74 foot tons 184 foot tons and 295 foot tons In addition the height of the rockfall impact
barriers should be a minimum of 8 feet above the ground surface for all of the barriers In
addition some existing boulders located downslope from the proposed barrier system and
upslope from the existing drainage channel should be demolished and removed from the
project site
Based on our evaluation and analyses it is our professional opinion that the
location and the design capacity of the rockfall impact barrier system recommended herein
should be capable of containing most of the potential rockfall hazards located on the
mountainside upslope from the Sea Country Subdivision Area 6
It should be noted that there are no guarantees in the professional engineering and
architectural design fields with respect to potential rockfall hazards However installation
of the rockfall impact barrier as described in this report will provide a high level of safety
against rockfall hazard for the Sea Country Subdivision Area 6 based on past applications
of similar mitigation methods The text of this report should be referred to for detailed
discussion and presentation of the rockfall hazard mitigation plan

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes identified as Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is
estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of
the island The younger shield Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the
island Waianae Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its
eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank
These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield
Plateau
The Waianae Range is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and some
pyroclastic materials which are collectively grouped and classified as belonging to the
Waianae Volcanic Series The Waianae Volcanic Series is divided into the lower
middle and upper volcanic members The lower member is comprised of lava flows
and associated pyroclastic rocks that built the main mass of the Waianae Shield
Volcano The middle member consists of rock that accumulated and gradually filled the
vast volcanic caldera The upper member is a relatively thin capping layer that covered
the entire top of the shield volcano late in its history of evolution
Once the majority of the Waianae Shield Volcano formed a long period of deep
erosion landslide activity sedimentation and subsidence of the Island of Oahu
occurred producing the large amphitheater valleys located on the west side of the
Waianae Shield Volcano The deep erosional valleys were carved by down cutting
streams and landslide mass transfer processes which gradually cut back the valley
walls and filled the valley floors with large accumulations of alluvial and colluvial
materials With the passage of time and as a result of multiple sea level fluctuations
some of the valley filling alluvial and colluvial deposits were buried and through varying
degrees of consolidation and cementation formed relatively dense conglomerate
materials These consolidated and or semi consolidated older alluvial and colluvial

Based on our field observations the basalt rock formation comprising the
mountainside consists of layers of lava flow that have eroded over time to form
substantial rock cliffs and stepped topographic slope profiles The stepped slope profile
is a result of differential erosion and weathering processes acting upon the rock layers

2 6 Rockfall Simulation
The Colorado Rockfall Simulation Program CRSP is a computer program that is
a widely accepted engineering tool used by experienced personnel to estimate potential
rockfall behavior by simulating rockfall activity based on input parameters that are
selected on a site specific basis The input parameters for this project were selected
based on the observations and measurements collected in the field Basically the
program provides a statistical evaluation of potential rockfall behavior based on the
hillslope topographic profile and other input information such as rock size rock shape
and other parameters used to characterize the ground surface irregularities
Information obtained from the CRSP analysis included the predicted falling rock

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Rockfall impact barriers are used worldwide as an effective rockfall mitigation
and protection system Rockfall impact barriers are constructed commonly on hillsides
where protection of downslope areas from falling rock is necessary or desired Rockfall
impact barriers are considered a viable rockfall protection system where the source of
rockfall cannot be readily identified and stabilized or where the source area
encompasses extensive land area that makes other mitigation measures impractical to
implement
The rockfall impact barrier is a specialized barrier fence structure designed to flex
and absorb the design impact energy of falling rock and rolling boulders The barrier
system essentially consists of a series of steel posts linked by wire rope cables with
steel ring nets and wire mesh spanning between the posts Lateral and upslope wire
rope anchors that are installed in drilled holes and grouted in place with cement grout
restrain the barrier posts Rockfall impact barriers of varying capacities and heights are
fabricated based on the probable rock bounce heights and the anticipated design
loading rockfall impact energy Therefore it is critical to carefully evaluate the existing
site conditions and perform statistical simulation of the probable rockfall behavior to
assist in the engineered design of an appropriately sized rockfall impact barrier system
in terms of both height and kinetic energy absorption capacity
