INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed inactive ships pier project at the Naval Inactive Ship
Maintenance Facility NISMF in Middle Loch of Pearl Harbor on the island of Oahu
Hawaii The general location of the project site is shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from analyses of data which were
obtained during this exploration and supplemented with data from previous explorations
for other projects in Middle Loch These recommendations are intended for design input
only and may be used by the A E design team as the basis to prepare their preliminary
cost estimates However our recommendations are not intended to be used by the
prospective bidders as a basis for their bid estimates since the report may not contain
sufficient data for their use
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Currently NISMF has two 2 piers in service One of the piers Pier 7 was built
in the 1940 s and repaired in 1982 of severe deck and piling damage The pier had
deteriorated since then to a poor condition and it had now been demolished and replaced
by a temporary floating pier
It is proposed to build a new Pier 7 at the same location on the western shore of
Middle Loch The new pier will have a wider and longer concrete deck approximately 26
feet by 300 feet The pier deck will be supported on prestressed concrete pile
foundations penetrating below the mudline and its harbor sediments into a suitable

Regional Geology
The geologic conditions underlying the Pearl Harbor lochs were the results of a
complex series of depcsitional and erosional events due to glacioeustatic sea level
changes and post erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene
Following the cessation of activity of the materials were
deposited in an estuarine and deltaic environment in the area of Pearl Harbor
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2866 00 Page 6
The sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 300 feet below and rising
about 95 feet above the present sea level
As Oahu sank coral reef grew around the shorelines keeping pace with the
subsidence of the island
Concurrent with a rise in sea level during the late Pleistocene Era eruption of the
Salt Lake Makalapa member of the Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the coral reefs and
alluvium with deposits of volcanic ash which consolidated to form volcanic tuff layers
Subsequent erosion had dissected and thinned the mantles of coral reefs and tuffs at the
lochs
During the recent epoch as sea level rose to its current stand the Middle Loch
area was covered with a thick deposit of highly compressible harbor sediments
comprising primarily of cohesive soils with localized granular soils over the underlying
alluvium

Lateral Pile Capacity
In general the lateral capacity of a long pile depends on the pile size the pile
embedment the shear strength of the surrounding soils and the bending moment
capacity of the pile
Based on the generalized subsoil profile encountered we have performed lateral
pile analyses to evaluate the six different pile schemes to utilizing a computer
program PILEDG which is based on the P Y curve approach developed at the
University of Texas at Austin
For each lateral loading condition the program calculated the pile top deflections
by iterations using relationships between lateral deflection and lateral subgrade
modulus values of the subsoils along the piles The deflection versus subsoil
modulus relationships were represented by P Y curves generated by the program
using input soil parameters The pile responses including bending moments and
shear forces induced along the piles were then computed for the pile deflections
calculated by the program
For analysis purposes it vas assumed that the pile tops were at the elevation of
105 7 feet MLLW or 5 7 feet above MLLW
Based on our findings we believe that the piles should be able to resist the design
lateral loading without undergoing excessive bending and deflections
Alternately we believe that a coefficient of horizontal subgrade reaction of two 2
pounds per cubic inch may be used for the highly compressible harbor sediments
for lateral piie analyses utilizing other approaches such as the Equivalent Fixed

be test driven as probe piles at strategically selected locations along the pier site
These probe piles may be incorporated into the actual pile foundation system
To evaluate the individual vertical support characteristics of the three 3 different
pile bearing strata a minimum of three 3 static load tests should be performed
on selected probe piles The probe piles selected for static load tests should be
loaded to two 2 times the design vertical compression capacity in accordance
with the standard loading procedure of ASTM Test Method D 1143

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling five 5 off shore
borings to depths of 41 0 to 140 3 feet below the Mean Lower Low Water MLLW level
and one 1 on land boring to a depth of 101 5 feet below the existing ground surface at
the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The bottoms of two 2 of the
off shore borings were probed using a conical tipped rod to depths of 59 5 and 109 3
feet below MLLW The borings were drilled by a skid mounted drill rig on a fabricated
floating platform and a truck mounted rotary drill rig advancing continuous flight augers
or rotary and core drilling tools as appropriate
The materials encountered in the borings were examined and classified in general
accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2488 84 Description and Identification of
Soils Visual Manual Procedure by a geologist from our firm The logs of the borings
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the boring logs at the appropriate sample
depths
In soft soil conditions soil samples were obtained by the use of thin walled tube
sampler Shelby tubes in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 1587 83
Thin Walled Tube Sampling of Soils using both 2 5 and 3 inch diameter tubes The
samples were obtained by pushing the samplers into the in situ soils using the hydraulic
feed system of the drilling rig The hydraulic pressure shown on the feed gauge as
required to advance the tube sampler per 6 inch increment are shown on the boring logs
at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of soil and rock materials encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material
left in the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and
testing
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a
coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on the Boring
Logs as REC 74

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests to aid
in determining pertinent engineering properties of the selected samples of soil and or
rock materials obtained during our field exploration The data from these tests were used
in our geotechnical engineering analyses and the formulation of our recommendations
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in determining certain engineering
properties The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight of the
tested sample is shown at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
Atterberg Limits tests ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 are used to determine the
plasticity of the fine grained portion of soils The Plasticity Index of the material is used
to classify the soil under the Unified Soil Classification System The results of these tests
are summarized on Plates B 1 through B 4
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of a soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining to
the performance or suitability of a soil
Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests ASTM Test Method D 2850 87
are performed to evaluate the shear strength parameters of the soils The results of these
tests are summarized on Plates B 5 through B 9
Unconfined Compression tests ASTM Test Method D 2166 85 are performed to
evaluate the compressive strength of the soils The results are presented on Plates B 1
through B 4
Torvane Shear tests are used to determine the coefficient of cohesion in a soil
sample The results of the Torvane Shear tests are presented on Plates B 1 through B 4
