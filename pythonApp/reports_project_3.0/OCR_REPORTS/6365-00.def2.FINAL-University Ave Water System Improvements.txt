SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
In general our field exploration disclosed that under the surface pavement section
of 4 25 to 9 0 inches of asphaltic concrete and 6 inches of base course the project site is
generally underlain by man made fill consisting of varied materials ranging from clay to
cobbles on the order of about 2 0 to 3 5 feet thick overlying alluvial sands and silts to the
maximum depth explored of 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface Our exploration
did not encounter large cavities or voids in the borings drilled however cavities are known
to exist in the coral formations in the project vicinity
We encountered groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration at depths ranging from about 7 3 to 8 5 feet below the existing ground surface
However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall
time of year seasonal precipitation surface water runoff and other factors
This project will involve very minimal grading work followed by the installation of a
new water line and related improvements Details of the project design were not available
at the time this report was prepared
In general we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation for support under the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this
free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill
could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage
Where soft loose subsoil conditions are encountered at the invert elevations of the
utility lines we recommend providing a subgrade stabilization layer consisting of 18 inches
of No 2 Rock ASTM C 33 No 4 gradation wrapped in a non woven filter fabric
Mirafi 180N or equivalent below the bedding layer for more uniform support
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific
design recommendations

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The project site is
located on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano
The Koolau Volcano was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the
northwest to southeast trending rift zones These lavas consist of interbedded
pahoehoe and a a type flows with pahoehoe lava being the dominant form A a is the
Hawaiian term for basaltic lava flows typified by a rough jagged spinose clinkery
surface Pahoehoe is the Hawaiian term for basaltic lava flows typified by smooth
billowy or ropy surfaces After the cessation of the main shield building phase of
Koolau Volcano there was a long period of erosion
During the Pleistocene Epoch there was a renewal of volcanic activity with the
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series Along the crest of the ridge along the
westerly side of Manoa Valley there are the Tantalus Roundtop and Sugarloaf vents
When these vents were active one or more lava flows from these vents spilled down
the westerly wall of the valley and moved across the valley floor These lavas ponded
and filled the lower portion of Manoa Valley The ponding allowed the lava to densify as
it cooled forming dense massive rock The new lava flows from these vents blocked
Manoa Stream and forced the stream to the east between the East West Center and
Waahila Rise These flows generally terminated in the Moiliili District where the basalt
rocks were quarried in the past Reef limestone related to the Waimanalo stand of the
sea underlies the basalt at the former Moiliili Quarry
In some areas the reef limestone was dissected by old stream channels and
alluvial materials were deposited At the project site our exploration generally
encountered man made fill overlying alluvial deposits

Site Description
The project site is along University Avenue between its intersections with King
Street and Varsity Place in the Moiliili area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

Excavation
Installation of the proposed trench or trenches will involve cuts and trenching into
the underlying fill and alluvial soils It is anticipated that the on site soils may be
excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such as excavators or
trenchers
The above discussion regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials is based
on field data obtained from the borings performed at the project site We
recommend encouraging contractors bidding on this project to examine the site
conditions and soil data to make their own interpretation
a Excavation Support
We anticipate that excavation depths of up to about 5 to 10 feet below the
ground surface will be required for the installation of the new water system
pipes Based on our site observations and the heavily used urban location
of the proposed water system improvements we believe that temporary
cut slopes for open cut excavations is not practical In this case trench
walls would have to be cut vertically necessitating the use of shoring
during construction
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with
applicable safety requirements The contractor should be solely
responsible for the adequacy and safety of the shoring installation The
contractor s representative should be on site at all times during excavation
and construction work for the opportunity to promptly observe changing or
unforeseen
conditions

stability
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance
equal to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to
reduce the potential for excessive ground movement

It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against
the excavation walls with proper bracing during construction The properly
braced shoring is essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral
movements of the adjacent ground into the excavation which may result
in potential settlement or distress to adjacent structures or other
improvements
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and
the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses
during excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is
difficult to accurately estimate the magnitude of movement The
magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship such as how quickly
and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed the subsurface
conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be
installed properly and as early as practical The adjacent ground should be
continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of
movements until the subsurface pipes are finally backfilled
b Dewatering
We encountered groundwater at depths of about 7 3 to 8 5 feet below the
existing ground surface across the project site We anticipate that the
proposed water system improvements will be above the groundwater table
and dewatering will not be necessary
If required dewatering should be conducted in such a manner that
dewatering will not cause areal ground subsidence which may cause
potential damage to the nearby existing structures Furthermore
consideration should be given to a dewatering system that includes a
deep cut off wall to reduce the volume of water to be removed within the
excavation and to reduce the areal extent of groundwater drawdown
outside of the trench excavation

Backfill Materials
In general we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation for support under the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use
this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage
If soft loose subsoil conditions are encountered at the invert elevations of the utility
lines we recommend providing a subgrade stabilization layer consisting of
18 inches of No 2 Rock ASTM C 33 No 4 gradation wrapped in a non woven
filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent below the bedding layer for more uniform
support
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the pavement subgrade should consist of crushed coral or basalt with a
maximum particle size of 3 inches The material should have a laboratory CBR
value of 25 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less
The excavated trench material may be re used as backfill provided particles larger
than 3 inches and adobe clay are removed
As an alternative to facilitate the backfilling and reduce the time required for
applying compactive effort Controlled Low Strength Material CLSM may be used
to backfill the trenches The use of CLSM would also reduce the potential for future
settlement of the trench backfill under the repeated wheel loading from traffic on
University Avenue
Backfill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Backfills should be moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum
moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths ranging
between approximately 15 5 and 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings
were drilled with a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger equipment at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and observed the field exploration operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general
conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log
Legend Plate A Logs of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of
Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 and D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler and a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler
the second and third 6 inches of an 18 to 24 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight determinations ASTM D 2937
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
One Sieve Analysis test ASTM C 117 C 136 was performed on a selected
soil sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil
classification A graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B 1
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B 2
Two Minimum Resistivity ASTM G 57 and two pH AASHTO T 289 tests were
performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration The test results
are summarized on Plates B 3 and B 4
