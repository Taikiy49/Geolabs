PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project involves drainage improvements to allow under road drainage for
storm runoff and prevent flow over the roadway at a portion of Hawaii State Road 11
near Milepost 58 7 within Ka u on the Island of Hawaii The overall project is shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
Work will involve vertical realignment along approximately 1 500 feet of the
roadway which may involve raising the highway at least 5 feet We understand that it is
proposed to construct new concrete pipe culvert structures along the roadway An
intake box structure will be necessary at the existing outlet to redirect the discharging
water at each culvert Retaining walls may also be required for raised roadway

Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and

Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals

The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense rock formation with a
high RQD value would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous
methods of excavation

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by a thin
layer of fill and volcanic ash overlying hard to very hard basalt formation We understand
new concrete culverts will be constructed as a part of drainage improvement measures
Retaining walls may be required for the proposed vertical realignment of the road A
detailed discussion and our geotechnical engineering design recommendations are
presented in the following sections of this report
Concrete Pipe Culvert
We understand that new concrete pipe culverts will be constructed to mitigate the
drainage problem caused by storm runoff A headwall will also be constructed at the
ends of the culvert The concrete pipe drain and headwall structures should be
designed to resist lateral and vertical earth pressures
Foundations
Based on our field exploration the site is underlain by 1 to 3 feet of fill and volcanic
ash overlying basalt formation Foundation excavation may expose underlying
basalt formation Therefore we recommend leveling the exposed basalt formation
with a Caterpillar D 10 bulldozer or similar size for a minimum of four passes to
provide a relatively level surface for foundation construction
Based on our experience near surface cavities and voids are common in basalt
formation To help detect and collapse near surface cavities and or voids after the
rugged surfaces have been leveled we recommend proof rolling the subgrades
with a large vibratory drum roller minimum 20 tons static weight for a minimum of
eight passes The vibratory drum roller should operate at a speed of about 300 feet
per minute Yielding areas loose areas or cavities disclosed during the clearing
and_ proof rolling operations should be over excavated and backfilled with
compacted fill materials The depth of over excavation should extend until dense
underlying materials are exposed and should be evaluated in the field by our
representative

Backfill
The backfill around the culvert structures should consist of structural fill Imported
structural fill should consist of non expansive granular material such as crushed
coral or basalt The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with
particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain less
than 15 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of less
than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883 The backfill
should be compacted to 90 to 95 relative compaction
Backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 Compaction should be accomplished by suitable compaction
equipment i e sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers multiple wheel
pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed when compacting the
backfill
Because compaction of fills are critical elements of earthwork Geolabs should
conduct observations and soil density tests during backfill to assist the contractor
in obtaining the required degree of compaction Where compaction is less than
required additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of
moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction

Hard to very hard basalt formation is anticipated at the bottom of the new culvert
retaining structures Based on the competent subsurface conditions an allowable
bearing pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used on the

increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as those caused by seismic
forces

Dynamic Lateral Earth Forces
Because the project site is on the seismically active Island of Hawaii Seismic
Zone 4 forces due to dynamic lateral earth pressures will need to be considered
in the design of retaining structures The force due to dynamic lateral earth
pressures associated with seismic loading may be estimated using
15H pounds per foot of wall length for level backfill conditions where H is the
height of the wall in feet For a sloping backfill condition up to three horizontal to
one vertical 3H 1V a dynamic lateral force of 30H pounds per lineal foot of
wall H in feet may be used in design The dynamic lateral earth force would
generally act at the mid height of the wall
The dynamic lateral earth forces provided assume that the wall will be allowed to
move laterally by up to about 2 to 4 inches in the event of an earthquake If this
amount of lateral movement is not acceptable the retaining wall should be
designed with higher dynamic lateral forces for a semi restrained wall condition For
a semi restrained wall condition dynamic lateral forces due to seismic loading may
be estimated using 46H pounds per lineal foot of wall for level backfill conditions
The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures would generally act at the
mid height of the wall The force due to the dynamic lateral earth pressures
presented above is in addition to the static lateral earth pressures An appropriately
reduced factor of safety may be used when dynamic lateral earth pressures are
accounted for in the design of the retaining structure Due to relatively high dynamic
lateral forces sloping backfill behind the retaining structures should be limited to an
inclination of 3H 1V or less

Drainage
In general retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the potential for
build up of hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a
12 inch wide zone of permeable material such as open graded gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation placed directly around a perforated pipe
perforations down at the base of the retaining wall The perforated pipe should
discharge to an appropriate outlet or weepholes
As_ an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or
EnkaDrain may be used instead of the permeable drainage material The
prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a
perforated pipe at the base of the retaining wall Unless covered by concrete or
asphaltic concrete the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively
impervious materials such as well graded soils to reduce the potential for
significant water infiltration behind the walls
