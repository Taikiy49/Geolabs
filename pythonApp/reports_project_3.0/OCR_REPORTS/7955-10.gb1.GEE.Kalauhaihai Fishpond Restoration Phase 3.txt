SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered variable subsurface conditions at the site.  
The subsurface conditions at the mauka side of the highway generally consist of fills 
comprised of stiff to very stiff sandy clay and medium dense silt gravel extending to depths 
of about 1.5 to 3.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The fill in Boring No. 6 extended 
to a depth of about 14 feet below the existing ground surface.  Below the fill, colluvial 
deposits were encountered to about 2.5 to 4.5 feet in depth.  The colluvium consisted of 
very stiff to hard sandy clay and clayey silt, and boulders. The colluvium was underlain by 
medium hard to hard highly weathered basalt formation extending to the maximum depth 
explored of about 15.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  
The subsurface conditions at the makai side of the highway consist of fills extending 
to depths of about 1.5 to 4 feet below the existing ground surface.  The fills consisted of 
medium dense sandy gravel and soft to stiff silty clay.  Below the fills, coralline detritus 
consisting of very loose to medium dense silty sand and lagoonal deposits consisting of soft 
clayey silt were encountered, extending to the maximum depth explored of about 24 feet 
below the existing ground surface.  Hard coral was encountered between the depths of 
about 9 and 12 feet below the existing ground surface in the boring drilled at 5841 
Kalanianaole Highway.  We encountered groundwater in the borings at about 1.6 to 5.3 feet 
below the existing ground surface at the time of our field explorations.  
Based on the design depths of the manhole structures, the new manhole at the 
makai side of the highway could potentially be underlain by very loose coralline detritus. 
Where soft and/or loose soils are encountered at the bottom of the manhole structure, we 
recommend over-excavating the soft and/or loose soils a minimum depth of 2 feet below 
the bottom of the manhole structure and backfilling the over-excavation with a stabilization 
layer consisting of No. 2 Rock (ASTM C33, No. 4 gradation) wrapped in a non-woven 
filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) for uniform support.   
We understand that the drain line crossing Kalanianaole Highway will be installed 
using trenchless HDD methods.  Based on our analyses, we believe that the 6-inch diameter 
HDPE drain line installed by HDD methods should have an SDR of 13.5 or smaller to safely 
handle the loads imposed into the pipe during installation and the service life of the pipe.  
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
recommendations for the design of the project.

1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Kalauhaihai Fish Pond Restoration, Phase 3 project in the Hawaii Kai 
area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses for the project. These recommendations are intended for the design of drain 
manholes, and underground drain line installation using open trench and trenchless 
methods only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located at 5841 Kalanianole Highway (makai side) and the area 
across Kalanianaole Highway (mauka side)  in the Hawaii Kai area of Honolulu on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii.   
An existing fish pond is situated at 5841 Kalanianaole Highway.  This fish pond 
was supplied with freshwater by an artesian water source on the mauka side of 
Kalanianaole Highway.  We understand that the artesian water flowed through a lava tube 
beneath the highway.  In addition, we understand that the flow of water was cutoff during 
the widening of Kalanianaole Highway in 1997. 
Our previous geotechnical study on the mauka side of the site concluded that there 
may still be an artesian water source in the area.  Our study measured an artesian water 
head of about 0.7 to 1.2 feet. 
We understand that it is planned to restore the fish pond close to the conditions 
prior to the widening of Kalanianaole Highway.  The restoration consists of installing an 
underground catchment pipe to intercept artesian water on the mauka side of the site.  
The intercepted water will be piped into a manhole structure.  The water collected in the

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
two shield volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age, and the Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. 
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Koolau Range, and its 
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio-eustatic 
fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age).  The base of the 
stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt.  The Koolau volcanic shield was built 
by thinly bedded lava flows during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epochs.  The 
main shield-building activities ceased approximately 2½ million years ago. 
After a long period of cessation of the shield building, volcanic activity returned 
with the formation of a series of lava flows, cinder cones, and tuff cones, such as those 
associated with Koko Crater.  Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature due 
to the interaction of groundwater with the rising magma, resulting in steam explosions, 
which expelled large quantities of pyroclastic material, predominantly ash and cinder. 
Some explosive eruptions produced ash that cemented together to form a firm rock known 
as tuff, locally known as “mudrock.” 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.  When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and

Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located at 5841 Kalanianole Highway and the area across 
Kalanianaole Highway in the Hawaii Kai area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  
The location of the project site is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered variable subsurface conditions at the 
site.  The subsurface conditions at the mauka side of the highway generally consisted of 
stiff to very stiff fills underlain by very stiff to hard colluvium over medium hard to hard 
highly weathered basalt formation. 
The subsurface conditions at the makai side of the highway consisted of medium 
dense granular fills and medium stiff to stiff cohesive fills underlain by very loose to 
medium dense coralline detritus and soft lagoonal deposits.  Hard coral was encountered 
between the depths of about 9 and 12 feet below the existing ground surface in one of 
the borings.  We encountered groundwater in the borings at about 1.6 to 5.3 feet below 
the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the design depths of the manhole structures, the new manhole at the 
makai side of the highway could potentially be underlain by very loose coralline detritus. 
Where soft and/or loose soils are encountered at the bottom of the manhole structure, we 
recommend over-excavating the soft and/or loose soils a minimum depth of 2 feet below 
the bottom of the manhole structure and backfilling the over-excavation with a stabilization 
layer consisting of No. 2 Rock (ASTM C33, No. 4 gradation) wrapped in a non-woven 
filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) for uniform support.   
We understand that the drain line crossing Kalanianaole Highway will be installed 
using trenchless HDD methods.  Based on our analyses, we believe that the high-density 
polyethylene (HDPE) drain line installed by HDD methods should have a Standard 
Dimension Ratio (SDR) of 13.5 or smaller to safely handle the loads imposed onto the pipe 
during installation and the service life of the pipe.

3.1 
Excavation  
In general, the contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used 
for excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations. Based on our field 
exploration and the available information, the surface fill soils and the underlying very stiff 
to hard colluvial deposits, soft lagoonal deposits, and loose to medium dense coralline 
detritus encountered in the borings may be excavated with conventional earthmoving 
equipment.  However, the deeper excavations extending into the medium hard to hard 
basalt formation and hard coral formation may require the use of hoerams or chipping. 
The excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance 
equal to the depth of the excavation measured from the outside edge of the excavation 
in order to reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement.  In addition, the 
excavated soils should be stockpiled away from existing underground utilities to reduce 
the potential for appreciable ground movement or subsidence, which may damage the 
existing underground utility line. 
3.2 
Excavation Support 
We anticipate excavation depths of up to about 4 to 5 feet below the existing 
ground surface will be required for the subdrain installation.  Deeper excavation depths 
of about 10 to 15 feet below the existing surface will be required to install the drain 
manholes.  Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, we believe that shoring of 
the sides of the excavations will be necessary to protect personnel working in the 
excavations.  Temporary shoring of the excavations using steel sheet pile shoring, 
especially where dewatering of the excavations will be necessary, should be considered.  
However, it should be noted that the planned excavations may likely traverse other utility 
lines; therefore, care should be exercised to avoid truncating the existing utility lines if 
sheet piles are to be used as shoring. 
The use of a sheet pile shoring system may also serve as a cut-off wall to aid in 
the dewatering operations, which is further discussed in the “Dewatering” section herein. 
The sheet piles should be driven with a suitable hammer, as discussed below, to a 
sufficient depth to reduce the potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the 
amount of dewatering within the excavations due to the relatively permeable nature of the

3.3 
Dewatering 
Groundwater was encountered in the borings at elevations of about +0.1 to +2 feet 
MSL at the time of our field exploration. Dewatering of manhole excavations will be 
necessary since the bottom of the manholes will be several feet below mean sea level. 
Therefore, dewatering provisions will need to be included in the contract documents for 
the proposed construction.  Since the excavations may involve the discharge of 
groundwater, a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permit may be 
required for this discharge. 
It is anticipated that the coralline materials and weathered basalt encountered at 
the site are relatively permeable. Therefore, dewatering of the excavation may involve 
moderate to large volumes of water. Dewatering by means of a well point system along 
the outside of the excavations is not recommended. The resultant areal depression of the 
natural groundwater table could induce settlement of the soils, which could affect nearby 
existing structures and utility lines. The potential impact of the dewatering system 
selected on depressing the natural groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the 
contractor prior to dewatering. 
It is our opinion that a cut-off wall system in conjunction with a tremie concrete plug 
should be considered to aid in dewatering the excavation. However, sumps may be 
needed to collect water that percolates up into joints between the cut-off walls and tremie 
concrete plug or infiltrates through the cut-off walls. The use of an interlocking sheet pile 
shoring support system with a tremie concrete plug is relatively watertight, which should 
allow the groundwater levels outside the excavations to be maintained close to the 
original pre-construction levels. Therefore, some type of groundwater control requirement 
should be specified in the contract documents. 
It should be noted that installation of the sheet pile shoring to the desired depths 
at the site may require predrilling of the sheet pile locations.  The presence of these

 Backfilling of Manhole Excavations 
The manhole excavations will need to be properly backfilled.  In general, 
free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), should be used for the initial backfill up to about 12 inches above the 
groundwater level.  The excavated on-site soils may be used as backfill above the 
free-draining gravel up to the finished subgrade. Imported select granular material 
may also be used to backfill above the free-draining gravel.  Imported fill materials 
used for trench backfill should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no 
larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension. The material should contain between 
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have 
a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell 
potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
Geolabs should test the imported fill materials for conformance with these 
recommendations. 
The soil backfill above the No. 3B Fine gravel should be placed in thin lifts (normally 
8 inches in loose lift thickness) with each lift mechanically compacted to a minimum 
of 90 percent relative compaction.  Within the roadway area, the top 3 feet below 
the finished pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
determined in accordance with ASTM D1557.

3.7 
Horizontal Directional Drilling 
We understand that the drain line crossing Kalanianaole Highway to connect the 
two drain manhole structures will be installed using HDD methods to minimize 
disturbance and traffic impacts to the highway.  Based on the available plans, the drain 
line will be a 6-inch diameter HDPE pipe. 
Horizontal directional drilling is a trenchless, steerable installation method using a 
multi-axis drilling machine to bore a small diameter pilot hole in the subsurface. The pilot 
hole is bored using either controlled fluid jetting or fluid-assisted mechanical cutting, or a 
combination of the two methods. Depending on the subsurface conditions, water only or 
SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS  
W.O. 7955-10 
GEOLABS, INC.

Hawaii • California 
water mixed with bentonite and/or polymer can be used as a drilling fluid in a directional 
drilling application. The pilot hole is reamed, as necessary, to accommodate the pipe(s) 
to be installed. The pipe is pulled back into the reamed hole by the drilling machine. Most 
HDD systems use slurry to stabilize the walls of the borehole and to reduce the frictional 
drag on the pipeline being installed. Steering corrections are made in the subsurface 
materials by rotating the slanted cutter shoe to the desired orientation and then advancing 
the shoe and string without rotation. 
In general, the HDD pilot hole is drilled nearly horizontally with typical entry and 
exit angles between 8 and 15 degrees measured from horizontal. However, steeper entry 
angles (about 20 degrees) may be required to avoid underground utilities and/or 
obstructions. Shallow working pits may be required at each end of the bore to allow 
placement of the drill pipe and to allow transition to the conventional pipe trench 
installation. The production pipe should be installed in one continuous operation, 
preferably without pause, and should protrude a sufficient distance beyond the entry and 
exit points to allow proper termination or connection at a later time.  In general, a minimum 
radius of curvature of about 200 feet should be used for the HDD alignment crossing 
Kalanianaole Highway.  
The vertical and horizontal alignments of the drill hole typically are monitored from 
the surface by a locator, which tracks signals from the transmitter contained inside the 
drill head housing. Other methods of tracking the cutting tool, such as in-hole steering, 
generally are recommended to provide more precise alignment and grade information. 
The contractor should carefully monitor the drilling operation when approaching property 
boundaries and existing underground utilities or structures to assure proper clearances 
are maintained. 
The feasibility of the HDD technique should be established based on ground 
conditions, the diameter and length of the pipe or duct line, and the required/achievable 
degree of accuracy in both alignment and grade.  Horizontal directional drilling is less 
attractive where precise line and grade tolerances are required in soils containing cobbles 
and boulders in a soil matrix, especially with larger diameter pipelines.  The HDD method 
is best suited for soils with some cohesion (i.e., clays).  The HDD method is not

 HDD Geotechnical Considerations 
The technical feasibility of the HDD method depends on the characteristics of the 
subsurface materials at and along the depth of the pipeline profile, as well as the 
length of the drill path and diameter of the pipes to be installed (American Gas 
Association, 1995). 
The subsurface conditions at the Kalanianaole Highway crossing are variable.  The 
mauka side of the highway would encounter stiff to hard fill and colluvial soils 
underlain by medium hard to hard weathered basalt rock. The makai side of the 
highway would encounter a thin layer of medium stiff to stiff fills underlain by loose 
coralline detritus and soft lagoonal deposits.  Therefore, it appears that the drain line 
designated for HDD installation will traverse mixed soil conditions from soft clayey 
silts (lagoonal deposits) to medium hard to hard basalt rock.  
Due to the relatively hard basalt formation encountered at the site, it is imperative 
that the contractor select the appropriate HDD equipment, drilling tools, and reamers 
to drill through the basalt rock formation. 
Based on the site conditions, we anticipate that the HDD entry and exit pits will be at 
the makai and mauka sides of Kalanianaole Highway, respectively.  A potential 
change in the drill path alignment may occur at the mauka side of the HDD alignment. 
The pilot hole would encounter soil conditions at the makai side. Deflection and 
“skidding” could occur when the drill bit exits the soil and encounters basalt rock 
formation near the mauka side of the HDD alignment. The skidding of the pilot drill 
bit is due to the difference in hardness between the soil and basalt rock materials.

 Pipe Size and Material 
The most important design consideration for the pipe is the tensile stresses imposed 
on the pipe when it is pulled into place through the drilled hole. The magnitude of 
these tensile stresses is a function of the length of the installation as well as the 
subsurface conditions. Other important pipe design considerations are the internal 
and external pressures acting on the pipe during and after installation. Factors 
affecting the loads and stresses on the pipe include the following: 
1. Radius of curvature 
2. Modulus of the pipe material 
3. Thickness of the pipe (SDR) 
4. Diameter (ID and OD) of the pipe 
5. Temperature 
6. Tension 
7. Operating pressure 
8. Differential pressure 
In addition to the pipe design considerations mentioned above, the pipe material must 
have adequate corrosion resistance or be protected to resist internal and external 
corrosion. 
As mentioned previously, the drain line will be HDPE pipes.  High-density 
polyethylene pipes may be susceptible to failure during installation unless they are

 Type of Drilling Fluid 
Depending on the subsurface conditions, water only or water mixed with bentonite 
and/or polymer may be used as a drilling fluid in a directional drilling operation. Water 
alone is normally suitable for drilling in basalt rock formation and silty or clayey soils. 
For soft and/or loose soils, polymer mixed with water will improve the stability of the 
borehole. 
The amount of drilling additives (bentonite or polymer) depends on the subsurface 
conditions (and the experience of the contractor). The water used in the drilling fluid 
should not be hard or salty because this will cause poor mixing or yield with the 
bentonite or polymer and the flocculated bentonite may tend to settle to the bottom 
of the tank. Hard water should be treated with soda ash, and saltwater generally 
should be avoided.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling two borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 101 and 102, to depths of about 24 and 21.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface, respectively, at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. We used a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary 
coring tools to advance the borings. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock materials were described in general accordance 
with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The 
Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods for the 
Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International Society for 
Rock Mechanics (March 1977).  Graphic representations of the materials encountered are 
presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration 
sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depth. A Graphic presentation of the test results is 
provided on Plate B-1. 
Four Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plate B-2. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested.  Results of the direct 
shear tests are presented on Plates B-3 and B-4.
