INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of a seismic site response study
for the proposed Hospital Replacement P 223 U S Naval Hospital at Subic
Bay on the island of Luzon in the Republic of the Philippines It
incorporates the findings from the geotechnical engineering investigation
performed by Geolabs Hawaii for the referenced project during the period
from June to August of 1985 as well as up dated information from an
additional geotechnical engineering investigation which is currently
being performed for the final location of the proposed hospital

the methods of evaluating predominant period of an earthquake In
Appendix B details of the computer program SHAKE which was used for
this site response study are discussed Appendix C presents a
comparison between the results obtained for this analysis and those
obtained from analysis carried out in Japan and in the U S A Also
discussed in this appendix is the developnent of the proposed response

The purpose of this study is to develop suitable set s of response

included literature review of the geology and seismicity of the Subic Bay

GEOLOGY AND SEISMICITY OF SUBIC BAY
General
The following sections constitute an assessment of the geologic and
seismic characteristics of Subic Bay They include an evaluation of the
regional geology seismology local geology and soil conditions
As the study of the seismology and the past historical earthquake
data in the project area were not included in our scope of work we have
utilized existing reports obtained from the Naval Civil Engineering
Laboratory in California References 1 and 12 as reference for the above
information
Regional Geology
The geology of Luzon are well published by various geological
journals in the Philippines The geologic information obtained from
these sources indicates that Central Luzon may be divided into seven
geologic provinces based on lithologic variations or structural
developnent Western Margin Province Western Ultramaphic Metamorphic
Province Western Volcanic Intrusive Province Central Volcanic Province
Central Plain Eastern Volcanic Province Eastern Margin Province and the
Cordillera Central Province

The site is located in the western Ultramaphic Metamorphic Province
and is underlain by four major rock types volcanic including lava
flows and pyroclastic rocks sedimentary metamorphic and intrusive
Major structural features of central Luzon can be divided into two
groups Large scale structures associated with the Manila Trench
subduction zone system are in all geologic provinces of Central Luzon
Smaller scale structures are related to individual processes of
emplacement or deformation in distinct geologic areas of Central Luzon
Central Luzon is composed of two mountain ranges separated by a
deep intermontane basin The southern Sierra Madre Range forms the
eastern segment consisting of metamorphic and sedimentary rocks
Faulting is common throughout the range The margins of the Central
Valley consist of folded Tertiary sedimentary units The entire basin
fill has been folded and faulted into broad synclines and anticlines
Faulting is also found along the boundary of the Central Valley and the
southern Sierra Madre Range The Central Valley is bounded to the north
by the Philippine Fault a major tectonic feature that forms the southern
boundary of the Cordillera Central and Carballo Mountains and separates
the northern and southern segments of the Sierra Madre
Central Luzon is mainly the product of volcanic constructional
processes Many major structural stratigraphic and lithographic

features of Central Luzon are typical of subduction zone volcanic arc
systems The eastern margin of central Luzon is formed by the Sierra
Madre range a block of metamorphic sedimentary and volcanic rocks
formed before and during westward subduction of the Philippine Sea Plate
in the Eocene During the Oligocene and Miocene a shift of active
subduction from beneath the Sierra Madre to the west coast caused
emplacement of the ultramafic Zambales block as well as subsidence in
the area of the Central Valley and development of an associated
volcanic arc complex
The West Luzon Trough is a fault bounded basin 6 km deep filled
with 4 km of horizontal sediments of unknown age The east side of the
West Luzon Trough is fault bounded against a mass of ultramafic rock
similar to that exposed in the Zambales Range to the north The southern
extension of the Central Valley lies to the east beneath Manila Bay
This fault bounded basin is more than 7 km deep in the center and filled
with thick sequences of Tertiary deep water mainly clastic rocks
overlain by younger marine sediments terrigenous clastic deposits and
volcanic rocks

Local Geology
The project site is located within the Western Volcanic Intrusive
Province of Luzon and comprised of Recent to Pliocene Quaternary Volcanic
deposits The E W Trending San Antonio Fracture Zone is probably the
closest identified major Fault zone about 8 to 10 miles northerly of the
site
At the project site the volcanic deposits are primarily of
pyroclastic origin and consist of lithic tuffs volcanic breccia
agglomerate and volcanic mudflows lahar believed to have been
generated by explosive eruptions from Mount Natib Mount Santa Rita and
Mount Balakibok three of the major volcanic centers which comprises the
Zamoales Bataan Volcanic Highlands
Examination of the geonorphology of the area leads to the
conjecture that the site is located on a marine deltaic flat located at
the toe of a rugged mountain terrain The lowlying area was cut during
low stands of the sea by streams originating in the mountainous areas to
the east of the site
Numerous glacial eustatic sea level variations caused the emergence
and submergence of the land mass which subsequently produced soil
deposits at various levels both above and below the present sea level

The site investigation revealed that the subsurface materials in
the lowlying portins of the study area are mainly of a low energy
depositional estuarine or lagoonal environment which would be encountered
in a sheltered bay area such as Subic Bay
Owing to the low energy estuarine or deltaic environment of
deposition these soft and highly compressible sediments would tend to be
coaser sand and gravel deposits close to the hillside and grade to poorly
consolidated clays and silts containing traces of organic matter away
from the hillside The soft deposits are underlain at depths by deposits
of ash flow tuffs and mudflow debris from the eruption of Mount Natib
The upper soft deposits were probably contemporaneous with the
Pleistocene sea level changes
Based on the drilled borings the soil conditions at the project
site can generally be divided into the following three areas
Swampy Low Ground Area Elevation 3 to 6 Feet
This condition encompasses most of the site The soils consist of
very loose silty sand which grades to very soft silts and clays
with increasing distance from the hillside This compressive layer
is as deep as 95 feet along Binictican Drive and thins out to 10 to
20 feet along the base of the hillside The loose and soft soil
were underlain by stiff saprolite tuff to about 20 to 115 foot
depths Below this was rock formation consisting of very dense
tuff agglomerate

SEISMIC SITE RESPONSE ANALYSIS
General
Subic Bay is located in one of the most active seismic zones of the
world It has been categorized in the Navy Design Manual NAVFAC P 355 as
a Seismic Zone 4 area on a 0 to 4 zone scale indicating the potential for
great damage due to earthquake Therefore seismic responses of the site
and the proposed buildings during anticipated earthquakes should be
analyzed so that appropriate strengthening measures can be included in
the design of the building
GEOLABS HAWAII
27 93a
The purpose of our analysis was to develop a response spectra for
the site for use in the structural earthquake resistant design of the
buildings
A detailed analysis of the soil structure interaction problem would
involve a determination of the response of a structure when it is
subjected to earthquake ground motions which vary from point to point in
the soil and rock around and underlying the structure and travel in some
unknown way across the base of the structure This admittedly complex
problem is usually idealized for purpose of analysis so that motions in
the vicinity of the structure are considered to be due to vertical
propagation of body waves from the underlying stiffer formation to the
ground surface Reference 9
The procedure which was followed for our analysis is illustrated on
the flow chart on Plate 7 and can be further generalized into four
general tasks as described below
l A Design Earthquake Level must be selected Based on
historical earthquake data and empirical relationships between
the length of fault and the maximum possible earthquake
generated a statistical analysis can be performed to
determine the risk probabilities for the occurrence of various
sizes of earthquakes within 200 miles from the site and their
attenuation to the site From this analysis a specific size
of earthquake with a corresponding risk level e g 10
probability of occurrence in 100 years can be selected by the
user

The soil profile at the site must be determined The

Development of Response Spectra
Definition of Response Spectrum
Because of its geometry and composition each structure possesses
unigue values of fundamental period and damping factor If we
consider a group of different structures with different fundamental
periods and damping factors excited by the same ground motion
during an earthquake then the combined influence of the amplitude
of ground accelerations and their frequency components will create
different responses in terms of accelerations velocities
displacement stresses strains bending moments shears and other
parameters on each of these structures Graphs showing the maximum
responses induced in the entire range of such structures i e
different fundamental periods and damping factors are called
response spectra
In the present analysis much of the attention was directed to the
development of the acceleration response spectra

Program SHAKE
8 mS mi Se ne ma
Program SHAKE computes the responses in a system of homogeneous
visco elastic horizontal layers subjected to vertically travelling
shear waves The program incorporates the average non linear
relationships between the dynamic shear moduli and damping ratios
of various soils as functions of shear strain and static
properties Thus by inputting the static properties of the soils
into the program the dynamic properties can be established by
these functions with a sufficient degree of accuracy
The program can compute the responses anywhere in the system from a
selected design motion given also anywhere in the system In the
current analyses the selected ground motion from the represen
tative earthquake is applied at the rock surface and the responses
are computed for the final finished grade of the project site
A more detailed description of the program SHAKE and an output from
a program run are shown in Appendix B

Evaluation of Predominant Period of Earthquake

From Existing Time Acceleration Record
The predominant period of a recorded earthquake can be determined
by performing a Fourier Series Analysis on the recorded data The
analysis transforms the earthquake time acceleration record into a series
of equivalent periodic functions The period of the function which has
the largest amplitude is then determined and taken as the predominant
period
For Design Earthquake
The relationship between magnitude of earthquake distance from
causative fault and the predominant periods for maximum acceleration in
rock as shown on Plate A 1 is available for earthquakes occurring in
the Western United States Ref 11 Such relationship however are not
available for the Japanese or Philippine earthquakes which are typically
of the subduction type
In the present analysis an attempt was made to plot the calculated
predominant periods of 5 recorded Japanese earthquakes using the method
