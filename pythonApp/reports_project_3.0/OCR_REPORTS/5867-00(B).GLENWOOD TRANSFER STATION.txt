SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicated that the project site is generally blanketed by a
surface layer consisting of soft, wet volcanic ash soil ranging in thickness from about 6 to
8 feet. We did not encounter groundwater at the time of our field exploration.
Based on the competent subsurface conditions encountered during our field
exploration, we envision that the buildings and structures for the proposed replacement
transfer station facility may be supported on a shallow foundation system consisting of
spread or continuous footings. However, the volcanic ash soil present at the site is soft,
wet, generally has thixotropic properties, and poor support characteristics for foundations
and pavements. Therefore, special considerations will be required for structural and
pavement areas underlain by ash soils.
A falling head percolation test indicated that the shallow subsurface permeability at
the site is very slow (i.e., the time required for one inch of water to percolate from the
boring was about 150 minutes per inch). Generally, this rate is considered too slow and
unsuitable for leach field or similar infiltration management methods. Therefore, alternate
methods of wastewater management should be considered.
Proper surface drainage and reduction of water infiltration into the subsurface are
integral components to the recommendations provided herein. Because moisture
conditioning and compaction of the existing ground and new fill materials are critical
elements of earthwork, Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during
site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the
proper moisture content on each fill lift.
Our geotechnical engineering recommendations for the design of foundations,
slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, site grading, and pavements are further discussed in
the following sections herein.

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an area of
approximately 10,360 square kilometers (4,000 square miles). The island was formed
by the activity of the following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea
(activity during recent geologic times), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 - 1803), and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both still active).
The site is situated on the northeastern flank of Mauna Loa on the rocks of the
Prehistoric Member of the Kau Volcanic Series. In the area of the site, these rocks are
typified by relatively thin-bedded pahoehoe basalt lava flows with a surface mantle of
soft, wet silty soil (Pahala Ash). The ash is characterized as having very high in-situ
moisture contents and is usually highly compressible. The ash also frequently exhibits
thixotropic properties (i.e., the material loses strength when disturbed by remolding or
when subjected to earthquake vibrations).
The U.S. Department of Agriculture classified the surface soils in the area as
Ohia silty clay (OHC) on 0 to 10 percent slopes. This soil is found at lower elevations on
the windward side of Mauna Loa near Glenwood and Kurtistown.
Typically, the surface layer is dark brown to very dark brown in color and grades
to dark reddish-brown and dark-brown with depth. In most areas, it is underlain by
unweathered pahoehoe lava at a depth of about 62 inches.
This soil dehydrates irreversibly into fine gravel-size aggregates. It is extremely
acid in the surface layer and strongly acid to medium acid in the subsoil. The surface is
extremely stony in places.

3.1 Building Foundations
Based on the conditions encountered in our exploration, we believe that shallow
spread and/or continuous footings may be used to support new building structures at
the site. A layer of select granular material should be placed under the building footprint,
as described in the “Site Grading” section below. An allowable bearing pressure of
4,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design of foundations bearing
on compacted subgrade prepared in accordance with recommendations presented
herein.
Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations
should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm materials. The over-excavated
area may be backfilled with new fill material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction or the bottom of footing may be extended down to bear on
competent materials at a greater depth.
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades. Retaining wall foundations should bear on in-situ basalt rock
formation. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes, or on sloping ground, should be
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of 8 feet
measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of the slope. Footings
adjacent to planned (or existing) retaining walls should be embedded deep enough to
avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations, or the planned retaining walls should
be designed to resist the additional structural loads.
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a
45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench
or embedded at least as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging the adjacent below-grade utility lines or structures with

Lateral loads acting on building structures may be resisted by friction developed

3.2 Foundation Probing
Voids and/or cavities were not encountered in the borings. However, based on
our experience in the area, there is a potential for presence of lava tubes, blisters and/or
cavities in the basalt formations. In order to reduce the potential for structural damage
due to collapse of voids/cavities, a probing and grouting program is often conducted
below structures. However, due to the relatively light structures anticipated for this
project, we believe that the potential for loss of foundation support resulting form
collapse of the cavities below the foundation is relatively small and the probing and
grouting program may be omitted if this small risk is tolerable. However, if it is desired to
further reduce the potential for loss of foundation support, then a program of cavity
probing and grouting at the foundation locations should be conducted during
construction.

3.3 Site Grading
Details of the proposed site grading were not available at the time this report was
prepared. The following discussions of grading are general in nature and are intended
to provide guidance for design of grading work that may be required.
As previously mentioned, moisture conditioning and compaction of the subgrade
soils and new fill materials would be critical elements of earthwork; therefore, our
representative should monitor site grading operations to observe whether undesirable
materials are encountered during the excavation and scarification process and to
confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our
field exploration.
3.3.1 Site Preparation
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. The near-surface ash materials are relatively soft
and will compress when subjected to structural and vehicular loads. The soils
encountered in our exploration had very high in-situ moisture contents and were
close to the liquid limit. These ash soils have thixotropic properties (i.e., they
temporarily lose strength when remolded or are subjected to vibrations, such as
seismic events).
Therefore areas to receive fill, pavement areas and building areas should be
over-excavated down to a minimum of 2 feet below subgrade level, or to basalt rock
formation if it is encountered at shallower depths. The bottom of the excavation
should be lightly compacted using non-vibratory equipment and covered with a
layer of geotextile, such as Mirafi 600X or similar. The geotextile should extend up
the sides of the excavation for a minimum of 3 feet. After placement of the
geotextile, the excavated area should be backfilled with compacted select granular
fill. The first lift of backfill should be no more than 12 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction using non-vibratory
equipment. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in

accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content

The remainder of the excavation should then be backfilled using select granular fill

Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for

The thixotropic properties of the ash soils encountered on the site can make the soil
prone to pumping or liquefaction during earthwork operations. The use of vibratory
compaction equipment is not recommended for portions of the site underlain by ash
materials.

3.3.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture,
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the
in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture
is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry
density.
Aggregate base course materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during the site grading
operations to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction
and the proper moisture content on each fill lift. Where compaction is less than
required, additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture
content, as necessary, to obtain the specified compaction.

3.3.5 Cut and Fill Slopes

and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above
fill placement recommendations. Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and
cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. In
addition, slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the
potential for erosion of the finished slopes.

would serve as a capillary moisture break and would provide for uniform support of the

Because the project site is on the seismically active Island of Hawaii

dynamic lateral earth pressures will need to be considered in the design of retaining
structures. The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures (A@max=0.40g) may be
estimated using 16H* pounds per foot of wall length for level backfill conditions,
where H is the height of the wall in feet. For a maximum 3H:1V sloping backfill
condition, dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading may be estimated
using 28H? pounds per lineal foot of wall.
It should be noted that the forces due to dynamic lateral earth pressures provided
assume that the wall will be allowed to move laterally in the event of an earthquake
by up to about 2 to 4 inches. If this lateral movement amount is not acceptable, the
retaining wall should be designed with higher dynamic lateral forces for a
semi-restrained wall condition. For a semi-restrained wall condition, the forces due
to dynamic lateral earth pressures may be estimated using 28H* pounds per lineal
foot of wall for level backfill conditions.
The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures would generally act at the
mid-height of the wall. The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures presented
above is in addition to the static lateral earth pressures. An appropriately reduced
factor of safety may be used when dynamic lateral earth pressures are accounted
for in the design of the retaining structure.

3.5.4 Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the potential for build-up of
hydrostatic pressures.
For site retaining walls, the typical drainage system may consist of 1 cubic foot of
permeable material, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation),
wrapped with non-woven filter fabric placed at each of the weephole locations. The
weepholes should be spaced no more than 6 feet apart. As an alternative, a
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used
instead of the permeable drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product
should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the
retaining wall.
Unless covered by concrete slabs, the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of
relatively impervious material (well-graded materials) to reduce the potential for
significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure.
3.5.5 Backfill Materials
The backfill material for the retaining walls should consist of imported select
granular materials. The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with
particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should also
contain less than 15 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material
should have a CBR value of 25 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less
when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883.
Backfill materials should be placed in level loose lifts not exceeding 8 inches in
loose thickness and be compacted to at least 90 percent of the maximum dry
density established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 at moisture contents above
the optimum.

3.7 Wastewater Disposal
We understand that on-site sewage treatment and disposal by septic tank and
leach field method are planned. Therefore, we have conducted a field percolation test to
obtain data pertaining to the preliminary design of the sewage disposal system at the
proposed site.
Field percolation testing was performed in general accordance with the State of
Hawaii, Department of Health’s Administrative Rules, Chapter 11-62-31.2, “Wastewater
Systems” and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards to evaluate the permeability of the
subsurface soils for wastewater disposal. The percolation test was performed by drilling a
4.0-inch diameter borehole to a depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface.
The percolation test was performed within the near-surface ash soils generally
encountered in the project area.
Approximately one inch of gravel was then placed at the bottom of the borehole
to protect the bottom of the hole from scouring and sediments. During testing, the hole
was carefully filled with water to a minimum depth of 6 inches above the gravel pack.
The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which
calculation of the percolation test was based.
Based on the test results, a percolation rate of more than 60 minutes per inch
was measured within the near-surface sandy clay soils. The results of the percolation
test are presented on Plate C-1 herein. Based on the slow percolation rate measured in
the drilled boring, it is our opinion that the feasibility of using septic tank and shallow
leach field method for sewage disposal may not be appropriate. Therefore, the disposal
system site should be relocated or alternate disposal methods, such as holding tanks or
composting toilets, should be considered.

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling three borings,
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to depths of approximately 16.0 to
21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. We used a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with rotary
coring tools.
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3.
Core samples of rock materials encountered were obtained using diamond core
drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for
Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method, which uses a hollow bit to cut
into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and description.
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. If 3.7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5.0-foot core run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock, based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals."
