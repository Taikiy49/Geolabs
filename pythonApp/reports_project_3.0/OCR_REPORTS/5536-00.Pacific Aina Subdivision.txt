REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and forms
the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking
against its eastern flank Both Waianae and Koolau Ranges have lost most of their
original shield volcano shape and are now expressed as elongated mountains that have
been incised and eroded by long term stream flow and mass transfer processes
The project site is generally underlain by weathered basalt rock representing lava
flows belonging to the Koolau Volcanic Series The rock materials are typically
characterized by sequential lava flows consisting of jointed dense vesicular basalt rock
with thin interbedded clinker layers In situ physical and chemical weathering of the
Koolau lava flows has occurred during the last 1 to 2 million years The weathering
process has formed a near surface mantle of soils and highly weathered rock consisting of
residual soil and saprolite In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty materials and
is typical of the deep tropical weathering of volcanic rock The saprolite materials typically
grade with depth to less weathered basaltic rock formation
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site encompasses generally undeveloped hillside terrain located in the
Waimalu area on the Island of Oahu as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The
project site is bounded by the Kaahele Street and Komo Mai Drive intersection toward the
north and some existing residential development located downslope and toward the south
and east
The parcel identified as 073 002 resides on a southeasterly facing
moderately sloping hillside above the valley containing Waimalu Stream The 1 2 acre
project site encompasses a portion of the larger land parcel and is located at the top half of
a valley hillside with ground surface elevations ranging between about 240 feet and
175 feet Mean Sea Level MSL Some existing residential development is located
downslope from the project site on the lower valley slopes at elevations of less than about
100 feet MSL
Based on the available regional topographic map of the site and vicinity the overall
hillside slopes down toward the southeast with an average of about two horizontal to
one vertical 2H 1V inclination Some local flatter interslope segments averaging about
5H 1V were encountered at about the middle portion of the hillside In addition some local
steeper slope segments averaging about 1H 1V were encountered near the crest of the

Our site reconnaissance was performed under generally dry weather and ground
surface conditions Based on our site reconnaissance the hillside project site is vegetated
with relatively thick haole koa shrubbery and dry land grass The hillside area was
observed to contain scattered basalt rock outcroppings with some occasional loose
surface boulders Loose surface boulders are described as individual rocks that reside at
the ground surface with no attachment to in situ rock material Loose rocks often can be
moved by manual force to initiate rolling motion
The top portion of the project site located adjacent to and along Kaahele Street
consists of a wedge shaped area of relatively flat ground topography ranging in width
between about 20 feet wide at the southwestern end to about 40 feet wide at the
northeastern end of the project site The flat ground area appears to be underlain by fill
soils consisting of reddish brown clays and silts Based on our observation of some to
Y2 inch wide ground cracks that have developed in the near surface soils the clayey soils
may exhibit a moderate shrink swell character when subjected to soil moisture fluctuation
The distribution of the ground cracks was observed to be more predominant along the
outer edge of the fill slope extending back toward the street a distance of about 10 to 15
feet Based on our visual observations it appears that some very slight localized ground
settlement exists along the slope crest suggesting that some possible slow soil creep
movement of the fill materials may have occurred
Our site reconnaissance consisted of a foot traverse across and down the hillside
toward the existing homes located along the base of the slope Based on our
reconnaissance of the hillside the ground surfaces appear to be underlain by weathered
basalt rock and saprolitic soils extremely weathered soft rock Several discontinuous
hard rock ledge features of about 20 to 40 feet in length and about 10 to 15 feet in height
were encountered near the middle portion of the hillside The rock ledges encountered
appear to be in generally stable condition with minimal rock overhang and loose fractured
rock A widely scattered distribution of individual boulders typically ranging in size
between about 2 and 4 feet in dimension were encountered on the hillside surfaces both
within and downslope of the proposed five lot development Some of the boulders were
observed to be in loose ground settings slight movement with hand force with minimal
embedment in the subsurface However large dimension boulders observed as imminent
rockfall hazards requiring prompt stabilization work were not encountered
However an oblong shaped non embedded surface boulder measuring about
6 feet in largest dimension was encountered on the hillside at the southern end of the
project site The boulder appears to be located just downslope of the proposed Lot No 1
development boundary The boulder is perched on the hillside with limited point ground
contact on the underside of the boulder The boulder is considered to be a potential
rockfall hazard to the existing homes located at the bottom of the hillside Demolition and

ANTICIPATED SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Based on our site observations and a review of available test boring information
from near the vicinity of the project site we anticipate that the project site may be underlain
by generally stiff clayey and silty saprolitic soils mixed with some gravelly decomposed
rock and hard rock materials Hard basalt rock was encountered exposed at the existing
ground surface and mixed with the near surface saprolitic material exposures
Along the top of the hillside the surface clayey fill soils encountered adjacent to
Kaahele Street may have a moderate shrinkage swelling potential when subjected to
ground moisture fluctuation Due to the observed soil shrinkage cracks and some possible
soil tension cracks with localized slight ground settlement which was encountered along
the top edge of the fill embankment the quality of the existing fill material should be
investigated by subsurface exploration such as test borings
PRELIMINARY FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS
Based on our literature research and site reconnaissance performed for the
proposed five lot subdivision project we anticipate that residual saprolitic soils and
weathered rock materials underlie the hillside project site The stiff soils and medium hard
to hard basalt rock encountered on the existing hillside ground surfaces should provide
suitable support for residential structures No overt visible signs of existing slope instability
or past landslide activity were observed Therefore it is our opinion that the site is suitable
for residential development from a geotechnical engineering standpoint
Based on our visual observation of the ground conditions located at the top of the
slope portions of the residential structures may be underlain by moderately expansive fill
soils which may require a near surface capping layer consisting of non expansive
granular fill material below the house structures In addition proper moisture conditioning
of the subgrade soil may be necessary to reduce the shrink swell potential of the fills
Based on the existing topography of the project site we envision that the new
homes would be constructed in a downslope setting that would require the use of retaining
walls for grade separation and embedded foundations for building support The
excavation for the retaining walls and foundations located on the existing sloping ground
surfaces is anticipated to encounter stiff saprolitic soils and weathered rock materials
which may require hard rock excavation methods
The excavation work may expose boulders and rocky excavation spoils which
could become rockfall hazards if not carefully controlled during construction on the hillside
For short term rockfall hazard risk management an appropriate temporary rockfall
containment barrier should be constructed downslope of the proposed subdivision work
