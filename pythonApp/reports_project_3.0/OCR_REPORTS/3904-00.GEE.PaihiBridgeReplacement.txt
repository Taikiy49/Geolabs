Our borings drilled at the project site encountered a layer of near surface fill
consisting primarily of gravel with clayey silt ina medium dense condition extending down

encountered moderately to highly weathered medium hard severely fractured basalt
formation and or clinker extending to depths of about 13 5 to 16 5 feet The weathered
basalt formation and or clinker generally broke down into a soil like material consisting of
sand with gravel and silt
A medium hard to hard moderately to highly weathered closely fractured
gray colored basalt formation was encountered below the moderately to highly weathered
basalt formation and or clinker extending to depths of about 21 5 to 23 feet Moderately to
highly weathered medium hard severely fractured basalt formation and or clinker was
encountered below the gray medium hard to hard basalt formation extending to the
maximum depths drilled of approximately 51 and 63 5 feet below the existing ground
surface Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our exploration

bridge supported on a shallow foundation system we understand that drilled shafts
embedded in the moderately to highly weathered basalt rock formation will be used to
support the replacement bridge to reduce the amount of excavation required for
construction of the replacement bridge Based on the loading on the bridge we
recommend that a drilled shaft diameter of 3 feet and a shaft length of 30 feet be used for

of 400 kips and an extreme event limit state capacity of 615 kips

encountered during installation of the drilled shafts due to the presence of the moderately
to highly weathered medium hard basalt rock formation encountered in our borings

through the basalt rock formation to the design tip elevation

1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Paihi Bridge Replacement project in the District of Hana on
the Island of Maui Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the bridge
replacement project These recommendations are intended for the design of bridge
foundations retaining structures and grading only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Maui is the second largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago with an
area of approximately 728 square miles This island was formed by the activity of two
volcanoes the older West Maui Tertiary Epoch and the more recent East Maui also
known as Haleakala Pleistocene Epoch The Isthmus of Maui is the narrow gently
sloping plain located between these two volcanoes
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Haleakala Shield
Volcano in the District of Hana Haleakala was generally built in three distinguishable
series of lava flows The lower unit the Honomanu Volcanic Series late Pliocene early
Pleistocene Epoch consists of thinly bedded tholeiitic basaltic pahoehoe and a a lava
flows These lavas tend to be very permeable and carry basal water Overlying this unit is
the Kula Volcanic Series late Pleistocene Epoch composed primarily of thicker alkalic
a a flows which may contain many inter stratified thin ash soil layers
A long quiescent period with few eruptions followed during which time canyons
were carved in the volcano due to eustatic changes higher and lower stands of the sea
level At the end of the resting period copious flows erupted along the southwest and
east rifts This is the third and upper rock unit known as the Hana Volcanic Series
Holocene Epoch The lavas from this volcanic series range in composition from
ultra basic olivine porphyries to non porphyritic alkalic basalts

3 3 Stream Channel Material Scour Analysis
One of the most common causes of bridge failure stems from scouring of bridge
foundations from flood or other water flow damage Therefore the foundation design of
the bridge abutments will need to take into consideration the potential for stream channel
SCOUr
Scour is the result of erosive action of flowing water excavating and carrying away
material from the bed and banks of streams channels Total scour over a period of time
generally consists of three components 1 Aggregation and Degradation 2 Contraction
Scour and 3 Local Scour The rates of scour depend on a number of factors such as the
shape and dimensions of a pier or abutment depth of flow velocity of approach flow size
and gradation of stream channel bed material and bed configuration
Considering the geologic conditions at the site moderately to highly weathered
basalt rock we do not believe that significant lateral migration of the stream would occur
at the location and within the anticipated life of the bridge Therefore consideration for the
potential for stream channel scour at the abutment locations would not need to be included
in the analyses for the replacement bridge foundations

Dynamic Lateral Earth Pressures
Because the project site is located in a seismically active area on the Island of
Maui dynamic lateral earth pressures will need to be considered in the design of
retaining structures For seismic loading conditions a force of 12 7H pounds per
lineal foot H in feet due to dynamic lateral earth pressures based on amax of
0 26g may be used for design The dynamic lateral earth force presented above is
in addition to the static lateral earth pressures The resultant force should be
assumed to act through the mid height of the wall An appropriately reduced factor
of safety may be used when dynamic lateral earth forces are accounted for in the
design of the retaining structures
Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the potential for build up of
hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide
zone of permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation immediately adjacent to the wall with a perforated pipe perforations
facing down at the base of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or
weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain
or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the drainage material The prefabricated
drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the
base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of Type A Structure
Backfill Material conforming to Section 703 20 of the HSS Unless covered by
concrete slabs or pavements the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of
relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration
behind the walls

Where required imported material should consist of select granular fill such as
crushed basalt or cinder sand Select granular fill materials should be well graded from
coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension Select granular
fill should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more with a maximum swell value of

should be kept moist until covered by concrete slabs or pavements Imported materials
should be tested and approved by Geolabs prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use

well graded granular fill material conforming to Type A Structure Backfill Material of
Section 703 20 of the HSS The material should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content placed in level loose lifts not exceeding 8 inches and
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the replacement bridge site were explored by drilling
and sampling two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of
about 51 and 63 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using
a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers and rotary
coring tools The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
