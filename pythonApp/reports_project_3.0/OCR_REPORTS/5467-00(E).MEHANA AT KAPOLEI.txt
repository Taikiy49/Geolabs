In accordance with our fee proposal dated May 1 2007 we conducted a
geotechnical engineering exploration for the proposed Borrow Pit site at Kapolei Green
Park in the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project location and general
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended to assist in evaluating the suitability of the
material within the borrow pit for fill materials The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed Kapolei Green Park is near the central portion of the
Mehana development The proposed park measures about 360 feet by 560 feet in plan
dimension and about 4 7 acres of total area The park is within the southern portion of the
site which is generally underlain by coralline deposits It may be desirable to
over excavate the coral for use as structural fill in other parts of the development
Additionally it is desired to delineate the boundary between the portions of the site
underlain by coral versus clay for planning of the mass grading sequence

May 1 2007 The scope of our work included the following tasks and work efforts

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau Volcano reached the
end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago

The project site is on the Ewa Coastal Plain which extends seaward off the
southwestern flank of the Waianae Volcano The forces of weathering and erosion
attacked this plateau generating sediments that were transported to the coast In the
project vicinity and further north these alluvial sediments were interbedded with marine
sediments and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging in
thickness from zero feet in the area of H 1 freeway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This
wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock over the
artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of these sediments has continued
from earlier geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials Land development and reclamation projects
within the last century have brought the project site to its present form
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 to depths of about 26 5 feet below
the existing ground surface In addition to the three borings we also explored the
proposed park area by excavating sampling and backfilling twelve test pits designated as
Test Pit Nos 1 through 12 extending to depths ranging from about 2 5 to 10 5 feet below
the existing ground surface The approximate boring and test pit locations are shown on
the Site Plan Plates 2 1 and 2 2 We excavated nine additional test pits designated as
Test Pit Nos 3 to 21 in the future grading areas
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings and test pits by
visual and textural examination in the field He monitored the drilling and test pit
excavation operations on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general
conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Log Legend

Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths Moreover we dug the test pits using a Case 780C backhoe
Our field exploration generally encountered a surface soil layer about 0 5 to 2 feet
thick The surface layer consisted of soft sandy silt to very stiff clayey silt underlain by
coralline deposit On average the top 2 feet of the coralline deposit was coral formation
and was generally dense to hard The coralline deposit then graded to less dense at
deeper depth The coralline deposit extended to the maximum depth of our field
exploration in all the borings and test pits except in Boring No 1 Boring No 1
encountered alluvial deposit containing medium stiff clayey silt below the coralline deposit
at about 20 feet below the surface and the alluvial deposit extended to the full depth of the
boring
We understand that the Mehana at Kapolei project generally encountered
two different types of soil at shallow depth Our Test Pit Nos 13 to 21 shows that coral
formation was encountered at the south side of the project while alluvial deposits were
encountered at the north side A superimposed contact line was drawn and is shown on
the Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings and test pits at the time of our
field exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels at the project site
may fluctuate depending on tidal fluctuations seasonal precipitation and other factors
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered during the field exploration are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3 and on the Logs of Test Pits
Plates A 4 through A 25 Results of the laboratory tests performed on selected soil
samples are presented on Plates B 1 and B 2
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
We understand that it is desired to over excavate the coralline material at the
proposed park location for use as part of the structural fill in other parts of the development
site The proposed borrow excavation pit is underlain by surface alluvial deposit coralline
deposits and alluvial deposits at deeper depth We did not encounter groundwater in the
borings at the time of our field exploration
Based on the results of our exploration we believe that the majority of the materials
within the borrow excavation pit is suitable for use as non expansive structural fill material

In general coralline deposits consisting of sands and gravels sandy clay coral and
sandstone formation may be used as structural fill material The coral fragments should be
reduced to less than 3 inches in largest dimension
Oversized material may be generated from the excavation and require crushing to
meet the 3 inch maximum size requirement for structural fill
It is our opinion that surface soil material consisting of sandy silt or clayey silt may
be considered suitable for general fill material but are not suitable for structural fill material
It is anticipated that the excavations will extend as deep as 25 feet below the
existing ground surface Temporary slope cuts of one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V
may be used during the borrow excavation
It is anticipated that the coralline deposit may be excavated with normal heavy
excavation equipment such as ripping with large bulldozers
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based
on field data obtained from the borings and test pits performed at the project site
Contractors bidding on this project should be encouraged to examine the site conditions
and soil data to make their own interpretation
