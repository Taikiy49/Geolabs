SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The subsurface conditions encountered at the site consisted of generally dense 
surface fills and medium stiff/dense alluvial materials extending to the maximum depth 
drilled about 51.5 feet below the ground surface. Groundwater was not encountered in 
the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. It should be noted that groundwater 
levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface 
water runoff, and other factors. The results of the groundwater monitoring indicate that 
the water level in the subsurface is likely fluctuated due to the natural rainfall. 
The results of the slope stability analysis indicate that the existing slope geometry 
with the existing CRM wall may have an insufficient factor of safety against rotational 
failure during the seepage water and seismic conditions. We believe that the reported 
ground heave may be caused primarily due to the excessive seepage water. The seepage 
water may also weaken/soften the soil subgrade at the toe of the CRM wall, further 
reducing the resistance from the soil shear strength against rotational failure. 
It is desirable to mitigate the reported ground heave, providing safe roadway 
conditions to the public motorists and maintaining underground utilities under operational 
conditions. It is our opinion that the mitigation option consisting of a concrete structural 
slab and micropiles may sufficiently increase the resistance against the existing CRM wall 
and slope from rotational creeping movement/failure. 
We recommend installing micropiles with a minimum grout bulb diameter of 
5.5 inches to extend to a depth of about 35 feet below the bottom of the pile cap for an 
allowable tensile load capacity of 24 kips. The 35-foot long micropile consists of a 25-foot 
long load-bearing grout bulb (bonded zone) developed within the stiff alluvial soils and a 
10-foot cased zone (unboned zone). 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed in support of the 2949 Kalawao Street CRM Retaining Wall project in the 
District of Manoa area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location 
and general site vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. These findings 
and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the evaluation of the existing 
Concrete Rubble Masonry (CRM) wall stability and the remediation design only. The 
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at 
the end of this report.  
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located at 2949 Kalawao Street in the district of Manoa, on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. We understand that portion of the asphalt concrete pavement 
was heaving adjacent to a private CRM retaining wall about 20 feet high. The heaving 
conditions have impacted the roadway performance and drivability. It is desired to 
investigate the cause of the ground heaving and evaluate for mitigation.  
We anticipate the existing retaining wall to be a gravity retaining wall system 
supporting on the shallow foundation. Detailed of the existing CRM retaining wall as-built 
drawings were not available at the time this report was prepared.  
Based on the information provided, we understand the pavement heaving occurred 
a few years ago and was repaired by resurfacing. New pavement heaving reoccurred and 
was reported last year. Considering recurrent pavement heaving conditions, our 
geotechnical exploration focused on the subsurface conditions, groundwater level 
fluctuation, and the impact on the ground rotational stability from the existing rock 
retaining wall. The intent is to provide the engineering solution within the City’s 
Right-of-Way to mitigate the pavement heaving.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield 
volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle 
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae became extinct while 
Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas 
banking against its eastern flank. These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau 
referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, there was a renewal of volcanic activity with the 
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series.  Along the crest of the ridge on the 
westerly side of Manoa Valley, there are the Tantalus, Roundtop, and Sugarloaf vents. 
When these vents were active, one or more lava flows from these vents spilled down the 
westerly wall of the valley and moved across the valley floor. The lava flows ponded and 
filled the lower portions of the valley. The ponding allowed the lava to densify as it cooled, 
forming dense, massive rock. 
During this epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of continental 
glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic 
basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When the glaciers 
melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose 
to above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosion processes are dominated by 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope

Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located along the roadway fronting 2949 Kalawao Street in the 
Manoa district of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. Along the northbound direction 
of the roadway, pavement and curb and gutter were observed to have heaved in an 
isolated location approximately 50 feet northeast of the private community entrance at the 
end of Kalawao Street.  
Along the north-central portion of the site, the soil of the landscaped area heaved 
up to approximately 17 inches above the pavement surface. The height of the heaved 
landscaped area diminished at the north end of the property (near the property driveway) 
and the south end of the property, where the curb and pavement heaving were observed.  
The 2949 Kalawao Street property is generally located at the bottom of 
Kalaepohaku Ridge of the Koolau mountain range. We anticipate the original grade of the 
property sloped down towards the northwest at relatively steep grades. We envision that 
the existing CRM wall was erected and backfilled to construct a level residential pad. The

assumption the wall was constructed symmetrically on both sides and the estimates 
described above, we anticipate the base of the wall may be up to about 19 feet wide. 
Details of the as-built CRM wall plans were not available at the time this report was 
prepared. 
The roadway fronting the CRM wall generally was about 28 feet wide with 7-foot 
and 10-foot wide landscaping areas on the mauka and makai sides of the roadway,

Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling two borings, designated as 
Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to a depth of approximately 51.5 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. The boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on the information obtained from our field exploration, the project site is 
generally underlain by surface fills and alluvium at greater depths. The project site was 
generally covered with asphaltic concrete pavement consisting of about 2.5 inches of 
asphalt and about 6 inches of base course. Beneath the pavement section, our borings 
generally encountered fill materials consisting of medium stiff silty clay and medium dense 
silty gravel extending to depths of about 2.5 and 6.5 feet below the existing pavement 
surface at Boring Nos. 1 and 2, respectively. Underlying the fill materials, our borings 
encountered alluvium consisting of silty clay with stiffness increasing with depth and 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 51.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. It should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to 
rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.

3.1 
Global Slope Stability Evaluation 
Global slope stability analyses were performed to evaluate the stability of the slope 
geometry with the existing CRM wall along the northwest boundary of the 2949 Kalawao 
Street property. 
Bishop’s simplified method of limit equilibrium was used to evaluate the balance of 
forces and moments acting within the slope sections to determine the factors of safety of 
the slope stability based on the generalized subsurface profile and estimated shear 
strength parameters. The Bishop’s simplified method of slope stability analysis assumes 
a circular slip surface, which is divided into slices with finite mass. The following 
parameters were used in our global slope stability analyses to evaluate the rotational 
slope failure mechanism: 
1. 
Slope geometry. 
2. 
Density and shear strength of the in-situ fill materials and alluvium based 
on the borings drilled at the project site and empirical correlations. 
3. 
Inclusion of residential surcharge and seismic excitation. 
The slope stability analyses were performed under static, seepage water, and 
seismic conditions for long-term consideration. The slope stability analyses were modeled 
with a residential surcharge load of 100 psf acting at the top of the slope. To evaluate the 
potential for slope instability due to seismic ground motion, a horizontal seismic coefficient 
of 0.22g was applied to the seismic conditions at the project site.  
Based on the subsurface profiles encountered during our field exploration, we 
adopted the following soil parameters in the slope stability analyses for the project to 
evaluate the rotational slope failure mechanism.

downward pressure by the anchor block. Based on the experience in the vicinity of the 
Manoa valley, the level of the post-tensioning may have difficulties to maintain over time 
due to the high plasticity of the in-situ clayey soils. Therefore, the tieback anchor option 
is also not desirable. 
3.2 
Micropile Supports 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site and the limited space 
constraints, we recommend utilizing micropiles to provide resistance against the rotational 
failure of the CRM wall/slope and minimize further ground heave.

A micropile consists of a small diameter (usually less than 12 inches) drilled and 
grouted pile with steel reinforcing. The micropile foundation is typically constructed by 
drilling a borehole, placing reinforcing steel in the hole, and grouting the borehole. 
Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed readily in access-restrictive 
environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions. In addition, the 
installation of the micropiles generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent structures, 
the adjacent soils, and the environment. 
Based on the availability of local material inventory, we envision that a micropile 
system with a minimum grout bulb diameter of 5.5 inches may be used. We recommend 
designing each micropile based on an allowable tensile load capacity of 24 kips. The 
load-supporting capacity of the micropile foundation would be derived primarily from skin 
friction between the micropile bonded zone and the in-situ soils encountered at the project 
site. The allowable tensile load capacity for the micropiles is for supporting dead-plus-live 
loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as wind or seismic 
forces. 
To achieve the allowable tensile load capacity with a factor of safety of 2, we 
estimate that each micropile would need to extend to a depth of about 35 feet below the 
bottom of the pile cap. The 35-foot depth is based on a 25-foot deep load-bearing grout 
bulb (bonded zone) developed within the competent in-situ stiff soils with a 10-foot cased 
zone. 
To facilitate the micropile drilling and ensure the quality of the grouting, we 
recommend advancing steel casing to the bottom of the micropile, defined as the “Casing 
Method,” during the drilling operation. The steel casing may be withdrawn during grouting 
operation while a minimum of 5 feet of grout is maintained above the bottom of the casing 
at all times. To increase the structural stiffness of the micropile within the critical rotational 
failure circle surface, we recommend 10 feet of permanent steel casing to be left in-place 
at the top of each micropile (unbounded zone). The permanent casing should have an 
outside diameter (OD) of about 5.5 inches, the same as the grout bulb size.

In general, the micropiles should have a minimum center-to-center spacing of 
about 24 inches to avoid a reduction in vertical load capacity due to group action and to 
facilitate the drilling of the micropiles. 
The excavation of the concrete structure slab should be conducted in phases no 
more than one section (less than 5 feet long) would be allowed at a time. This is to 
minimize the risk of potentially reducing resistance to the slope stability.  
It should be noted that the tensile load capacity of the micropiles is highly 
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the 
contractor to install the micropile.  Therefore, the tensile load capacity of the micropile 
may vary considerably between different contractors and micropile foundation systems.  
In order to determine whether the contractor’s methods of micropile installation are 
adequate and to determine the ultimate tensile load capacity, we recommend conducting 
a performance tensile load test on a sacrificial vertical micropile installed at the site.  In 
general, the purpose of the performance load test on a micropile is to fulfill the following 
objectives:

To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed by the

In general, the performance tensile load test should be conducted in accordance 
with ASTM D3689. Based on experience, we believe that the load test should be 
conducted at least 7 days after completion of the micropile installation to allow the grout 
adequate time to cure. 
The load test micropile should be loaded gradually to at least 200 percent of the 
allowable tensile design load of approximately 24 kips.  In addition, we recommend 
holding the test loads at the design load and the maximum test load for a minimum of 
4 hours, depending on the recorded movements of the load test micropile. After 
successfully holding 200 percent of the design load, the performance micropile should 
continue extending the testing load at 25 percent of the design load until achieving failure 
or rear yield strength, whichever occurs earlier and then be unloaded in five equal 
decrements. The performance load test is an integral part of the design of the micropile 
foundation system. Therefore, we recommend that a Geolabs representative observe the 
performance load test. 
A specialty contractor experienced in the construction of a cased micropile 
foundation system (minimum five projects) should perform micropile installation. It should 
be noted that hard drilling, such as cobbles and boulders, may be encountered within the 
subsurface materials similar to what was encountered in the borings at the project site.  
The micropile contractor should anticipate relatively high grout overage, especially 
in the basaltic cobbles and boulders, and have sufficient contingency funds for grout 
overage in the bid. Typical grout overage in the cobbles and boulders may vary 
significantly from 33 to 100 percent of theoretical volume or higher.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of 
approximately 51.2 and 51.5 feet below the existing pavement surface, respectively. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 3. The borings were 
drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch solid-stem augers and coring 
tools.  
Our logger classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-2.2. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch O.D. standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. 
In addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general 
accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch O.D. modified California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number 
of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be 
factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture content (ASTM D2216) and unit weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Six Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM D6913) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil classification. Graphic 
presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B-2. 
One one-inch Ring Swell test was performed on a relatively undisturbed sample to 
evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils.  The test results are summarized 
on Plate B-3. 
Three Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the peak and/or residual shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. 
The test results are presented on Plates B-4 through B-6. 
Four 
Unconsolidated 
Undrained 
Triaxial 
Compression 
(TXUU) 
tests 
(ASTM D2850) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear 
strength of the silty clay soils encountered.  The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil 
sample.  The test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-7 through 
B-10. 
One Consolidation test with time rates (ASTM D2435) was performed on a sample 
of the potentially compressible soil to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the 
material encountered. Results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate B-11. 
One set of Corrosion test, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), was 
performed by our office and Eurofins TestAmerica on a selected soil sample obtained 
from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-12.
