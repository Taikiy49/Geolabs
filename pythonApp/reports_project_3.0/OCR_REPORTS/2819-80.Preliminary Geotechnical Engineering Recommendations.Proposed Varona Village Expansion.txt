Preliminary Geotechnical Engineering Recommendations
Proposed Varona Village Expansion
This letter report serves to present our preliminary geotechnical engineering
recommendations for the design of site grading foundations and pavements in support
of the Environmental Impact Statement EIS preparation for the proposed Varona Village
Expansion project The preliminary recommendations presented herein are based on our
knowledge and past experience in the vicinity of the project site A detailed geotechnical
engineering exploration program should be conducted at the subject site to confirm
and or modify the preliminary recommendations presented herein
Project Considerations
The project site consisting of two parcels of land designated as Sites A and B for
ease of reference is located in the vicinity of the existing Varona Village in the Honouliuli
area of the District of Ewa on the island of Oahu Hawaii Site A encompasses
approximately 28 acres in area and is located approximately between the existing Varona
Village and North South Road Site B encompasses approximately 10 acres in area and
is located approximately between the existing Varona Village and the railroad track
Based on the information provided to us it is proposed to develop the subject sites into
residential housing with plantation type home design The project will also include the
installation of new utilities including sewer water and drainage lines as well as
construction of new roadways

The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
of the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of the late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age When the building of the Waianae and Koolau volcanoes came
to an end only a slight embayment existed on the southern coast of Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When
the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials Agricultural land development and
reclamation projects within the last century have brought the project site to its present
form

General Site Conditions
Based on previous subsurface information and other reconnaissance performed in
the vicinity of the project site we anticipate the subsurface conditions at the site will likely
consist of very stiff to hard clayey silts and or silty clays at shallow depths Below the
clayey silts or silty clays medium dense to very dense coral sands and gravels or coral
formation are anticipated The near surface soils in the area appear to be dry and grades
to moist with increasing depth Based on our previous laboratory tests performed for the
near surface soils in the area the silty clay and clayey silt soils have moderate to high
shrink swell potential when subjected to changes in the moisture content
Discussions and Recommendations
The near surface silty and clayey soils in the vicinity of the project site generally
have a moderate to high shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the
moisture content Therefore special attention should be given to the design of drilled pier
foundations subgrade preparation and slab on grade designs for this project To reduce
the potential for distress to future slab on grade construction resulting from swelling of the
near surface clayey soils it is recommended that the underlying expansive clayey
subgrade soils be scarified moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to about 90 percent relative compaction prior to
capping with a non expansive select granular fill material The thickness of the
non expansive select granular fill material may generally be between 12 and 18 inches
We understand that post and beam foundations will be used to provide support of
the proposed single family residential structures Because of the moderately to highly
expansive nature of the on site soils we believe that the post and beam foundations
should be supported by cast in place drilled piers extending through the zone of moisture
variation In order to reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from loss of
foundation support due to near surface soil saturation house foundations should consist
of 12 inch diameter 8 to 10 foot deep drilled piers for support of the proposed residential
structures at the subject site
Based on our experience with projects in the Ewa area and with projects having
similar soil conditions there is a strong tendency for water to accumulate at the interface
between the granular subbase material and the underlying clayey pavement subgrade
soils Uniess the pavement subgrade soils are properly prepared and adequate drainage
is provided the accumulated water will likely soften the silty and clayey subgrade soils
and cause premature failure of the new pavement The potential sources of water
infiltration generally include landscape irrigation and seepage To provide adequate
subsurface drainage in order to reduce the potential for premature pavement failure due
to subgrade saturation we recommend that a longitudinal edge drain be provided below
the new pavement structures

Site Grading
In general all grading work should conform to the Standard Specifications for
Public Works Construction City and County of Honolulu and the site specific recommen
dations contained in this letter report Items of grading that are addressed in the following
subsections include the following 1 Site Preparation and 2 Fills and Backfills
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce
potential contamination of the excavated materials Any soft weak or yielding
areas disclosed during the clearing and demolition operations should be over
excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavations should be
backfilled with engineered fill compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Prior to placement of the backfill the over excavated subgrade
should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Slabs and foundations of existing structures that are to be demolished should be
completely removed Any over excavation resulting from the demolition should be
backfilled with compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned
should be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with
select granular fill material placed in 8 inch loose lifts and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities that are to be abandoned in place
should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure
All subgrades and fill areas should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches
properly moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The
compaction requirement for pavement subgrades should be increased to at least
95 percent relative compaction The pavement subgrade should be kept moist
until covered by the subbase course Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as

Fills and Backfills
We envision that the on site silty and clayey soils and granular materials
generated from the excavation may be reused as a source of general fill and
backfill material General fill and backfill materials should be moisture conditioned
to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch loose
level lifts and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction
All permanent fill slopes should have slope inclinations of two horizontal to
one vertical 2H 1V or flatter All fills placed on Slopes steeper than 5H 1V should
be keyed and benched Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling past the
design fill slope and then cutting back Surface water run off should be diverted
away from the top of slopes and slope planting should be utilized to reduce the
potential for erosion
If imported fill materials are required such as the select capping fill layer the
select fill should consist of non expansive granular material such as crushed coral
mudrock or cinder sand The imported fill and or backfill material should consist of
well graded granular materials of less than 3 inches in maximum dimension The
material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 15 or
more and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 In addition it should contain
between 10 and 30 percent passing the No 200 sieve Imported fill material
should be tested for conformance with the project specifications prior to delivery to
the project site for its intended use Imported materials should be brought to
above the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch thick loose lifts and
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction The final lift of fill
below the pavement section bottom of subbase course should be compacted to
not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Aggregate base course and subbase course materials should meet the material
requirements for Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in Sections 30
and 31 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction Department
of Public Works City and County of Honolulu September 1986
Observations and soil density tests should be performed during grading to assist
the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper
moisture content on each fill lift Where compaction is less than required

House Foundations
We understand that post and beam construction will be used for the proposed
structures for the subject housing project In order to reduce the potential for structural
distress resulting from loss of foundation support due to near surface soil saturation
house foundations consisting of 12 inch diameter 8 to 10 foot deep drilled piers are
recommended for support of the proposed residential structures at the subject site For
foundation design of these drilled pier foundations an allowable compressive load of 7 to
9 kips per pier may be used for preliminary design We envision the upper 3 feet of near
surface soil under the post and beam structure will exhibit soil shrinkage and or saturation
in the future due to loss of soil moisture under an exposed environment or excessive
moisture infiltration Therefore no allowance for frictional load support should be
provided for the upper 3 feet in the foundation design
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines We estimate the footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for cast in
place drilled piers embedded in the very stiff to hard in situ silty clayey soils to be less
than 1 inch total and inch differential
Foundation Perfor ce C iderations
We understand that it is desired to maintain the appearance of plantation homes
with elevated floors supported by drilled pier foundations for the proposed Varona Village
Expansion project The performance of drilled pier foundations is highly dependent on
the workmanship of the drilled pier foundations and the implementation and maintenance
of water infiltration control in the vicinity of the pier foundations Based on the laboratory
test results obtained from similar projects in the Ewa Villages areas the in situ clayey
soils generally have a moderate to high shrink swell potential when subjected to
fluctuations in the moisture content In addition they will lose substantial strength when
saturated and will shrink when subjected to drying resulting in shrinkage cracks
Therefore proper surface drainage at the site is essential in the performance of the
foundations In order to reduce the potential for bearing strength loss of the foundation
soils we recommend that the following be implemented at the subject site

It has been our experience with projects in the Ewa area that shrinkage cracks
could develop in the yard area and under the house structures where maintenance of the
moisture content of the house pad soils are not evident Due to the high plasticity of the
on site clayey soils shrinking of the near surface soils started to develop as soil moisture
evaporated from the soil system under the prolonged dry climatic conditions characteristic
of the Ewa area These shrinkage cracks may close up to a certain degree upon wetting
as the soils expand during periods of wet weather or heavy rain storms However
shrinkage of the on site soils and subsequent cracking of the ground surface will recur
when the dry season returns
Since the development of shrinkage cracks is largely due to fluctuations in the
moisture content of the on site expansive soils we believe that efforts should be directed
at maintaining the soil moisture content at a relatively constant level Therefore we
recommend that measures such as landscaping or placement of concrete sidewalks
and or non expansive fills in the house pad areas should be considered by the future
homeowners This will serve to reduce the shrink swell behavior of the on site clayey
soils resulting from moisture content fluctuations thereby reducing the potential for the
development of shrinkage cracks

Siabs On Grade
Concrete slabs on grade construction is anticipated for the proposed carport
Structures at the subject housing development Our past experience indicates that the
project site may be underlain by moderately to highly expansive clayey soils Unless
concrete slabs on grade supported by these expansive soils are properly designed there
is a potential for future distress to the concrete slabs resulting from wetting and
subsequently swelling of these expansive clayey soils
To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from the swelling of the
on site expansive soils we recommend that the expansive clayey soils be capped with a
minimum of 12 to 18 inches of non expansive select granular fill material The select
granular fill should be placed as soon as possible after the scarification and recompaction
of the clayey subgrade soils The capping fill and the underlying subgrade soil should be
wetted and kept continuously moist prior to final placement of concrete
The select granular fill would serve as a capping layer of moist soil above the
underlying potentially expansive subgrade soils This capping fill would also reduce the
potential loss of moisture from the prepared moist subgrade soils In our opinion the
reduction in potential drying and wetting of the clayey subgrade soils should reduce the
impact on the slab due to potential heaving of the underlying expansive clayey soils when
wetted Compaction requirements for the subgrade soils and select granular fill are
discussed in the Site Grading section of this letter report
To reduce the potential for drying of the subgrade soils and to reduce the costs of
form construction we recommend that the slab edges be cast neat against the soils
The slab thickened edges should incorporate a sufficient amount of top and bottom
longitudinal steel reinforcement The top and bottom steel reinforcement bars should be
connected by stir ups The ends of the stir ups should extend sufficiently into the slab
area and should be tied to the welded wire mesh The welded wire mesh in the slab on
grade should be embedded at the proper height in the concrete to provide adequate
reinforcement Blocks or saddles may be used to raise the wire mesh A stiff grade of
wire mesh is thus recommended The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled
tightly against the slab with non expansive impervious soils and graded to divert water
away from the slab to reduce the potential of future water ponding around the garage
slab

Longitudinal Edge Drain
As previously indicated based on our experience with projects in the Ewa area
there is a strong tendency for water to accumulate at the interface between the granular
subbase material and the underlying clayey pavement subgrade soils Therefore unless
the pavement subgrade is properly prepared and adequate drainage is provided the
accumulated water at the interface will likely saturate and soften the silty and clayey
subgrade soils and cause premature failure of the new pavement structure
To collect and divert free water from the pavement section we recommend that an
edge drain system with outlet pipes on the sides of the roads and streets be provided
The outlets must discharge into appropriate drainage outlet Structures such as drainage
ditches culverts or catch basins The typical edge drain would consist of a 4 inch PVC
perforated pipe with the perforations facing down surrounded by a permeable backfill
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation A typical edge
drain section is presented on Plate 2 The bottom of the edge drain trench should be at
least 6 inches below the bottom of the subbase layer A filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N
or equivalent should be placed around the permeable backfill material to reduce the
potential for migration of fines into the drainage material Care should be exercised when
placing the permeable base and or construction of the surface course to avoid damaging
the edge drain collector pipe As an alternative a geocomposite fin drain may be used
in lieu of a PVC collector pipe as shown on Plate 2
