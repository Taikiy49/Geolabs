PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project is located in the southern portion of the Waikapu area on the Island of
Maui Hawaii It is bounded by Waikapu Stream to the north Maui Tropical Plantation to
the east and vacant land to the south and west A small reservoir was observed on the
west side of the project site The site is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Based on the information provided the proposed coffee farm is approximately
208 acres The site measures approximately 4 000 feet from west to east and 2 400 feet
from north to south It slopes down towards the ocean from west to east The elevation at
the project site is about 1 000 feet Mean Sea Level MSL along the western boundary
and 450 feet MSL along the eastern boundary
Site development involves cuts and fills cut slopes installation of RCP and
installation of drainage ditches with CRM outlet and scour stops Details of the mass
grading and layout are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Maui the second largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago was formed by the
activity of two volcanoes East Maui Haleakala and West Maui The broad gently
sloping plain connecting the two volcanoes known as the Maui Isthmus was formed
when lavas of the Haleakala Volcano banked against the already existing West Maui
Volcano
The East Maui Volcano better known as the Haleakala Mountain makes up the
eastern portion of the Island of Maui Haleakala Mountain is considered to be dormant
with the last eruption taking place on the southerly side of the mountain around 1790
Haleakala Mountain consists largely of the Honomanu Volcanic Series that built the
mountain from three rift zones probably during the Pliocene and early Pleistocene
Epochs
The volcanic rocks of the West Maui Volcano have been divided into
three volcanic series The oldest Wailuku Volcanic Series is comprised of basaltic lava
flows and associated pyroclastic and intrusive rocks which built the major shield volcano
The Wailuku Volcanic Series was covered by a thin discontinuous frosting of andesitic
and trachytic flows domes and pyroclastic deposits known as the Honolua Volcanic
Series After a long period of inactivity where erosion incised deep valleys into the
volcanic flows volcanic activity returned with eruptions that produced the flows and cones
of the Lahaina Volcanic Series
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts
of the two volcanoes During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fell to stands lower than
the present sea level exposing large areas of coralline sand on what was previously the
ocean bottom Wind action transported these coralline sands inland forming extensive
areas of sand dunes Over time portions of the dunes cemented and lithified The dune
sands are typically tan to cream like colored cross bedded fine grained overlying some
areas of older alluvium In some areas the dune sands have been covered with more
recent aeolian wind blown deposits of finer sands with some alluvial silts forming an
overburden on the higher quality dune sands
The project site is along the southeast flanks of West Maui Based on our review of
available geological maps the project site appears to be on a gently sloping plateau
region along the northeastern foot of the Hanaula Mountain and near the eastern exit of
Waikapu Valley as indicated on the Project Location Map Plate 1 We anticipate that the
project site is underlain by weathered soils over basaltic rock belonging to the
Wailuku Volcanic Series

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of excavating eight test pits designated as
Test Pit Nos 1 through 8 extending to depths of about 10 and 11 5 feet below the existing
ground surface Test pit locations are also shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Currently low bushes generally cover the entire site Based on the field exploration
conducted at the site the subsurface conditions consist of a thin layer of agricultural
topsoil over alluvium The alluvium generally consists of very stiff to hard clayey silt with
some sand and weathered gravel More gravel was encountered within the alluvium at
about 8 to 10 feet below the ground surface to the maximum depth excavated at about
10 to 11 5 feet It should be noted that some weathered boulders and cobbles were
encountered from 6 to 8 feet below the existing ground surface in Test Pit No 2
We did not encounter groundwater in the test pits excavated However the water
levels may vary with seasonal rainfall time of the year and other factors
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration methodology and the test pit logs are
presented on Plates 5 through 12

Keying and Benching
Based on our field exploration and site plan provided there may be some localized
areas with steep slopes Therefore some keying and benching may be required for
earthwork at the site These keys and benches should be firmly seated into existing
ground
At the toe of fills on ground where the existing slope is steeper than five horizontal
to one vertical 5H 1V a key should be provided This key should be a minimum of
2 feet in depth at the toe of the fill and should be a minimum of 15 feet in width
extending back under the toe of the fill The floor of the key should be sloped back
under the base of the fill at about 10H 1V to bond the fill into the existing slope
As fill placement progresses up the existing slope benches should be cut into the
existing slope to bond the mass of the fill to the existing ground These benches
should be a minimum of 3 feet in height and approximately 10 feet in width A
schematic section illustrating the keying and benching recommendations is shown
on Plate 3
Based on the site conditions encountered we recommend that prior to the
placement of fill the keys and benches be proof rolled with vibratory sheepsfoot
compactor or similar heavy equipment for a minimum of six passes to help verify
that adequate support for the fill has been achieved The proof rolling operations
should be performed in the presence of a Geolabs representative Yielding areas
loose areas or cavities disclosed during clearing and proof rolling operations
should be backfilled with compacted fill materials

Fills and Backfills
In general the on site materials generated from the excavations may be used as a
source of general fill or backfill materials provided that they are screened of the
over sized materials less than 6 inches in largest dimension
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill and backfills should be moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above
the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density We wish to emphasize that moisture
conditioning of the fill materials is an integral part of the fill placement
recommendations provided herein Compaction should be accomplished by
sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed to compact the
fills
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the subgrade soils are critical
elements of earthwork Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests
during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of
compaction and the proper moisture content Where compaction is less than
required additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture
content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction It should be noted that
the moisture requirement of the fills and subgrades about 2 percent above the
optimum moisture is an important requirement for the use of the on site clayey
soils

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the test pits it appears that cut
slopes required for the mass grading work would expose soils consisting of stiff to
very stiff clayey silt In general we believe that cut and fill slopes may be made at
an inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Mid point slope benches are recommended for
slopes exceeding 20 feet in total height Fills should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope as discussed above to provide stability of the new fill against sliding
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement
recommendations
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope
ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face Track rolling of slopes should not be
accepted In the event that over cutting of a slope occurs keying and benching
requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design
grade with sliver fills Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and
slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for
erosion of the finished slopes
REINFORCED CONCRETE PIPES
We understand that RCP drainage lines are planned for the proposed coffee farm
development We anticipate that most of the trenches for the RCP pipes will be excavated
in the compacted fill and or clayey silt encountered in the test pits
In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open graded gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be provided below the pipes for uniform bearing
support Free draining granular materials such as open graded gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the
sides of the pipes Improper backfill material around the pipes and improper placement of
the backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site materials less than
6 inches in maximum particle size The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to
at least 90 percent relative compaction

RETAINING WALL STRUCTURES
We understand that drainage ditches with CRM outlets and scour stops are
planned for the proposed coffee farm development We anticipate that the CRM wall
structures will be relatively shallow and bear on the stiff on site soils or compacted fill
material We believe that an allowable bearing value of 3 000 pounds per square foot
psf may be used for the wall structures bearing on the stiff on site soils or compacted fill
In order to reduce scour effects on the retaining wall structure we recommend
embedding the CRM wall foundations a minimum of 3 feet below the lowest adjacent
finished grades The CRM wall foundations should be at least 18 inches wide For sloping
ground conditions the footings should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 6 foot setback
distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the
slope Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in
stepped footings
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a
45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or
as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
If the foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with our
recommendations we estimate the total settlements of the foundations to be less than
0 5 inch Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on similar materials
may be on the order of about 0 25 inches or less
Lateral loads acting on the retaining structure may be resisted by friction between
the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure acting
against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 35
may be used for footings bearing on the on site stiff alluvium Resistance due to passive
earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 250 pounds per
square foot per foot of depth pcf if the footing is above the water table and 160 pcf if the
footing is below These values assume that the soils around the footings are well
compacted Please note that the passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of
soil should be neglected
The CRM wall structures should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressures
due to the adjacent soils and surcharge effects Based on our field exploration we
recommend designing the CRM wall structures to resist the following lateral earth
pressures expressed in equivalent fluid pressures of pounds per square foot per foot of

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight determinations ASTM D 2937
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The test results are presented on the Test Pit Logs at the appropriate
sample depths
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification
Graphic representation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
One Modified Proctor Compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plate B 2
Two 1 inch Ring Swell tests were performed on remolded samples of the surface
soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are
presented on Plate B 3
Two pH tests AASHTO T 289 were performed on selected soil samples obtained
from our field exploration The test results are summarized on Plate B 4
