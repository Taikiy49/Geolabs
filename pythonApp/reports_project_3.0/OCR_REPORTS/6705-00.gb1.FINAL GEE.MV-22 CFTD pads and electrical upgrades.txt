Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed in support of the Pad and Electrical Upgrades project within the
Marine Corps Base Hawaii MCBH at Kaneohe Bay on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the design of foundations
slabs on grade and site preparation only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2

The project site is located on the west side of C Street between 3rd and
5th Streets within the Marine Corps Base Hawaii at Kaneohe Bay on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii

and supporting electrical power LAN and telephone to support the MV 22
Containerized Flight Training Device CFTD The new concrete pad will be located
adjacent to an existing concrete pad with similar dimensions

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity
during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano Shield volcanic
activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations
These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The site of the Marine Corps Base Hawaii at Kaneohe Bay is located on
Mokapu Peninsula which was built by three volcanic vents of the Honolulu Volcanic
Series These vents are believed to be among the oldest of the series The pyroclastic
deposits and lava flows from vents formed salients permitting the growth of a coral reef
that merged with the main body of the Island of Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age many sea level changes occurred as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level of Oahu

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the borings drilled for this exploration we encountered about 4 to
12 feet of silty clay fill underlain by poorly graded sand medium hard to hard coral and
sandy silt
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the relatively light loads
anticipated we recommend supporting the proposed structure on thickened edge slab
footings bearing on the recompacted silty clay soils The soils encountered at the
bottom of footing excavations should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Soft and or loose soils encountered at the bottom of the footing
excavations should be removed to expose the underlying firm materials The resulting
over excavation should be backfilled with engineered fill or the footing may extend down
to bear on the underlying firm material
Our laboratory tests indicate the near surface silty clay soils at the project site
exhibit a low to moderate shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the soil
moisture content Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and our laboratory
testing we recommend placing a minimum 12 inch thick layer of select granular fill
material or aggregate subbase below the concrete slab The select granular fill or
aggregate subbase course should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Detailed discussions of these items and the other geotechnical aspects of
the project are presented in the following sections

Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the relatively light loads
anticipated foundations may consist of thickened edge slab footings bearing on the
recompacted silty clay soils An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per
square foot psf may be used to design thickened edge slab footings bearing on the
recompacted silty clay soils This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces

In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grades Foundations next to utility trenches or easements
should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of
the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent
below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for
appreciable foundation settlement
The soils encountered at the bottom of footing excavations should be compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Soft and or loose soils encountered at
the bottom of the footing excavations should be removed to expose the underlying firm
materials The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with engineered fill or the
footing may extend down to bear on the underlying firm material
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate that total footing settlements may be on the order of
1 inch or less We estimate that the differential settlements to be on the order of about
0 5 inch within 20 feet
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient
of friction of 0 40 may be used for footings bearing on in situ soils Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils around
the footings are well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
A Geolabs representative should observe footing excavations prior to placement of
reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions and the
required embedment depths

Slabs On Grade
Based on the preliminary plans provided we understand that the concrete slab
will be about 8 and 12 inches thick Our laboratory tests indicate the near surface silty
clay soils at the project site exhibit a low to moderate shrink swell potential when
subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture content Based on the subsurface
conditions encountered and our laboratory testing we recommend placing a minimum
12 inch thick layer of select granular fill material or aggregate subbase course below the
concrete slab Select granular fill material should consist of crushed coralline or basaltic
materials The materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no
larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The materials should have a laboratory
CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when
tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883 The aggregate subbase course should meet
the requirements of the City and County of Honolulu Department of Public Works
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction dated September 1986
Prior to placement of the non expansive select granular fill or aggregate subbase
course the subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The non expansive select
granular fill or aggregate subbase should also be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the slab edges should be
backfilled tightly against the edges of the slabs with relatively impervious soils These
areas should also be graded to divert water away from the slabs and to reduce the
potential for water ponding around the slabs

characteristics of the underlying soils In general very stiff to hard clayey soils were
encountered in Boring No 3 to a depth of about 12 feet below the existing ground

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to a depth of
approximately 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1
Rock cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as
shown on the Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2 Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Pocket penetrometer test results
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths and
