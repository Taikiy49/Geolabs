Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions along the proposed roadway

During our site reconnaissance localized block type failures were observed at the

alternative would be to provide periodic maintenance of these areas

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank The project site is generally located at the west to
northwest flanks of the Koolau
Koolau was built during the late Pliocene Epoch and early Pleistocene Epoch by
the accumulation of thinly bedded basaltic lava flows extruded from vents along a
fissure line over thirty miles in length which roughly paralleled the trend of what is now
the Koolau Range The main shield building activity ended in the early Pleistocene
Epoch approximately 2 5 million years ago In situ chemical weathering of the Koolau
lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The weathering process has
formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic soils In general saprolite is composed mainly
of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite
grades to basaltic rock formation with increased depth
After the end of the main shield building activity the forces of erosion began to
cut deep valleys into the volcanic dome During the middle to late Pleistocene Epoch
Ice Age sea levels fluctuated eustatically in response to the cycles of continental
glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the ocean
basins thus sea levels fell to stands below the present sea level When the glaciers
melted and retreated an excess of water became available thus sea levels rose to
elevations above the present sea level

The processes of erosion and deposition were effected by these glacioeustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below present sea level
When the sea level was high the base level was raised so that sediments accumulated
at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
the detachment of soil and rock mass from the slopes and movement of the material
downslope toward the axis of the valley by gravity to form colluvial deposits Once the
colluvium reaches streams in the valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium Large areas of alluvium can be
found in the lowland areas and bordering streams
When alluvial and colluvial deposits are left in place or buried for long periods of
time chemical processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a
breakdown or weathering of the material The chemical processes also cause
induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion of the sediment resulting in a
poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate Simultaneously erosion
continues in areas above the valley floors and upstream in headwaters This continued
erosion generates material which is transported downslope covering the older alluvial
deposits

SECTION 2 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
The existing site conditions along the proposed roadway alignment were observed
during a ground surface reconnaissance The proposed roadway alignment generally
follows the existing alignment of Drum Road At the time of our reconnaissance the
weather condition ranged from sunny to cloudy with heavy showers The existing dirt road
along the flatter and higher areas was observed to be in relatively good condition The
portions of the dirt road located in the low lying areas such as the gulches were in
relatively poor condition and seemed to require frequent maintenance In general ponded
water was observed in the low lying areas indicating that these areas have poor surface
drainage conditions The existing roadway generally consisted of brown to reddish brown
silty clay and clayey silt Basaltic gravel material was placed along certain portions of the
dirt road
The existing cut slopes along the roadway typically ranged in inclination from about
one half horizontal to one vertical to near vertical The slopes ranged in height
from as low as about 3 meters to as high as about 15 meters The majority of the existing
cut slopes along the existing roadway exposed residual and saprolite soils The residual
soils were generally stiff to very stiff and moist to wet Weathered basalt rock formation
and moderately to slightly weathered basalt rock formation were observed at the existing
cut slopes in the lower portions of the gulches The moderately to slightly weathered
basalt rock formation was generally hard to very hard
The overall stability of the cut slopes appeared to be adequate However
significant erosion and sloughing of the residual and saprolite soils were observed at the
cut slopes The eroded and sloughed off materials extended about 0 5 to 4 5 meters
laterally from the toe of the cut slopes In areas where the existing roadway is narrow the
eroded and sloughed off material at the toe of the slopes encroached onto the roadway
Some of the cut slopes have eroded and or sloughed off at the middle and bottom portions
of the slopes leaving the top portion of the slopes overhanging and creating a potential for
instability The cut slopes in the weathered basalt formation and the moderately to slightly
weathered basalt have block type failures due to the jointing patterns of the basalt rock
formation

Cut Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated along the proposed roadway
alignment we believe that the planned cut slopes may expose stiff to very stiff
residual and saprolite soils as shown in the photographs on Plate A 1 In general it
is our opinion that a preliminary cut slope inclination of 1H 1V or flatter may be used
for design of the cut slopes planned along the roadway alignment It should be
noted that weathered and moderately to slightly weathered basalt formations were
observed along some portions of the roadway alignment The basalt formations
were observed in the low lying gulches We believe that the cut slope inclination
may be increased to in areas where weathered and or moderately to
slightly weathered basalt formations are encountered
Excessive surface run off over the slope face may cause erosion of the exposed
soils thus jeopardizing the long term stability and performance of the cut slopes
Therefore it is our opinion that cut slopes should be protected by appropriate slope
planting or by other means such as placement of geotextile fabrics on the slope
face immediately after the slope is excavated to reduce the potential for erosion of
the exposed soils For cut slopes with a vertical height greater than 9 meters we
recommend that a minimum 2 4 meter wide bench be provided at the mid point of
the slope Drainage ditches lined should also be provided on the benches to
collect and divert surface water from the slopes to appropriate discharge outlets
The drainage ditches and discharge outlets should be lined to reduce erosion from
the concentrated flows The 2 4 meter wide benches may be omitted for cut slopes
in the hard basalt formation
It should be noted that the planned cut slopes are steep Therefore in the event
that over cutting of a slope occurs or the cut slope is damaged by erosion
consideration should be given to leaving the slope as is rather than attempting to
backfill the slope to the design grade with sliver fills If backfilling of an over cut
slope is necessary the cut slope should be completely over cut and buttressed with
a fill slope by keying and benching the fill materials into the over cut slope

3 7 Pavement Drainage System
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Based on our experience with
projects having similar soil conditions there is a strong tendency for water to accumulate
at the interface between the granular subbase material and the underlying clayey
pavement subgrade soils In addition free water voids in the pavement section repeated
heavy loads and an erodible base can also cause pumping Unless the pavement
subgrade soils are properly prepared and adequate drainage is provided the accumulated
water will likely soften the silty and clayey subgrade soils and cause premature failure of
the new pavement Therefore paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients
maintained to carry surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be
allowed on the site during or after construction
In general the primary source of free water in the pavement section is surface
water infiltration through cracks and joints in the pavement A major source of infiltrated
moisture is the longitudinal pavement shoulder interface Water also enters the pavement
section from shallow ditches To reduce moisture infiltration into the pavement structure
pavement joints and cracks should be sealed to reduce water infiltration into the pavement
section and underlying subgrade soils Considering the high rainfall in this area
