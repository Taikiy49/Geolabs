SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the geotechnical information provided in the RFP documents and the 
plans and specifications for JFY11 Project J-200, we anticipate about 1 meter or less of 
compacted satisfactory materials overlying limestone (rock formation) at the project site. 
Groundwater was not encountered in the RFP borings and is not anticipated to be 
encountered in the relatively shallow excavations for this project. 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the MWSS and Armory facility 
locations for the project, the existing fills, new fills, and/or the limestone formation 
anticipated at the foundation subgrade level would provide suitable foundation support 
for the structures. Therefore, we recommend using a shallow foundation system 
consisting of spread footings and/or continuous strip footings to support the planned 
structures. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 200 kPa may be used for design of 
spread and/or continuous strip footings bearing on compacted fill or limestone 
formation. This bearing value is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be 
increased to 300 kPa when considering transient loads, such as those caused by wind 
or seismic forces. In general, footings designed using the allowable bearing pressure 
should be embedded a minimum of 500 mm below the lowest adjacent finished grade. 
Due to the potential for encountering cavities and/or voids in the limestone 
formation, a program of cavity probing and grouting at the foundation locations will be 
required during construction as stipulated in the Project Program of the RFP documents. 
Generally, a probe hole should be drilled at each isolated spread footing location. 
Additional probes should be drilled for every 10 square meters of spread footing. In 
addition, probe holes should be drilled at 3 meters on center along continuous wall/strip 
footings. The probe holes should be at least 75 mm in diameter and should extend to a 
depth of at least 4 meters below the planned bottom of footing elevation. Probe holes with 
detected cavities should be backfilled with cement grout pumped under pressure 
(minimum air pressure of 172 kPa). The volume of cement grout used to fill each probe 
hole should be equal to or greater than the volume of the probe hole backfilled. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical review and consultation 
performed for the MWSS Facilities at North Ramp project within Andersen Air Force 
Base on the Island of Guam. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations derived 
from available geotechnical information included in the RFP documents for the project 
and our engineering analyses. It is understood that verification borings will be performed 
by the Design-Build team upon receipt of the project site from the Government to 
confirm the subsurface conditions are as anticipated.  
The recommendations presented herein are intended for design of foundations, 
slabs-on-grades, pavements, earthwork requirements, and underground utilities only. 
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted 
at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located in the northwest area of the Aviation Combat Element 
(ACE) site at Andersen Air Force Base (AAFB). The site is bounded by vegetation and 
North Road to the north, vegetation and the continuation of North Road to the east, 
South Road and a POV parking lot to the south, and 34th Street and vegetation to the 
west. 
The site is to be graded and developed under the JFY11 Project J-200 as a part 
of the overall ACE area grading strategy. The J-200 project site includes areas for the 
proposed P-240 MWSS and Armory facilities, and the site slopes down from the north to 
south/southwest. It is anticipated that some grading may be required to achieve the 
design finished grades at the project site. The existing site is a bare lot with vegetative 
cover and includes an infiltration basin in the southwest corner, which will be 
constructed by the J-200 project. Prior to the J-200 project, the site was a former 
munitions and ordnance storage area, a portion of which had been used for a concrete 
batch plant.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
The Island of Guam is the largest and southernmost of the Mariana Island 
archipelago, which is an island arc parallel to the subduction zone forming the Mariana 
Trench. The Mariana Trench is part of a belt of trenches extending from Japan to the 
Palau Islands that also includes the Japan Trench, Izu-Bonin Trench, Yap Trench, and 
Palau Trench. 
The Island of Guam can be broadly divided into three geologic provinces: 
(1) the southern mountains; (2) the central mountains; and (3) the northern plateau. The 
island formation started during the Eocene Epoch by the eruption of basaltic rhyolite 
lavas. The mountainous central portion of the island is composed of these Eocene 
volcanic rocks and conglomerates, tuffs, and limestones of the Eocene and Oligocene 
Epochs. 
Lava flows and limestone formations of the Miocene Epoch make up the majority 
of the southern one-third of the island. The plateaus of the northern portion of the island 
were formed while this part of the island was still beneath the sea level. During the 
Oligocene and Miocene Epochs, a complex series of coral and algal reefs formed an 
elongated platform of limestones and related deposits. Tectonic activity has lifted these 
coral-algal limestones above sea level to form the northern Guam Plateau. The central 
portion of the island consists of the transition from the volcanic materials of the southern 
mountains to the limestone formations of the northern plateau. The project site is 
situated in a relatively level portion of the northern plateau, which forms the main portion 
of Andersen Air Force Base. A geology map for Andersen Air Force Base and the 
surrounding area is presented on Plate 3.   
The local geology is dominated by the limestone plateau. Weathering and 
solution working of the limestone has partially re-crystallized the calcium carbonate of 
the limestone forming massive limestones in some areas and poorly to well-cemented 
detrital limestones in other areas. Typically, the detrital limestones are highly variable in 
strength characteristics and are karstic or cavernous in nature. In addition, the 
limestone formations are generally porous. Except during heavy storms, the majority of

Seismic Design Considerations 
The project site is in the relatively high seismic area of the Mariana Islands. 
Therefore, the project site would be subjected to high seismic activity, and seismic 
design considerations will need to be addressed for the project. The following sections 
provide discussions on the seismicity of Guam and the soil profile for seismic design at 
the site. 
2.3.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
Guam is situated adjacent to the subduction zone that forms the Marianas 
Trench. The island is tectonically active and has experienced approximately 
20 earthquakes exceeding Magnitude 7.0 since 1902. Of these events, fifteen 
occurred prior to 1950 and five have occurred subsequent to that date. Several 
large faults and fault zones that traverse the Island of Guam include the 
following:  
1. 
Pago Bay - Adelup Point Fault

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, pavement, 
building slabs to be demolished, and other unsuitable materials should be 
removed and disposed properly to reduce the potential for contaminating the 
excavated materials to be re-used as fill materials. Utilities to be abandoned in-
place under the proposed pavements and structures (where appropriate) should 
be backfilled by pumping lean concrete (or Controlled Low Strength Material) 
under low pressure. 
After clearing and grubbing, areas designated to receive fills should be scarified 
to a depth of about 200 mm (8 inches), where possible, moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction, in this report, refers to the 
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. Mechanical compaction equipment should be used to 
compact the backfill materials. Compaction efforts by water tamping, jetting, or 
ponding should not be allowed. 
The subgrade in at-grade or cut areas below the pavement structural section 
should be recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. In the 
event that the natural limestone formation is exposed at the subgrade elevation, 
the subgrade should be proof rolled with a vibratory drum roller (9,000 kg 
minimum static weight) a minimum of six passes before placement of fill 
materials.  
Soft and/or loose, weak, or yielding areas (or cavities) disclosed during site 
preparation operations should be over-excavated to expose firm ground, and the 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with select fill materials (well graded 
75-mm minus materials) compacted to a minimum 95 percent relative 
compaction. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrades due

3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the excavated on-site granular limestone materials may be used as a 
source of select fill materials provided that deleterious materials such as 
vegetation are removed and over-sized materials greater than 75 mm (3 inches) 
in maximum dimension are screened. It should be noted that excavated soils 
classified as SC, GC, ML, MH, CL, CH, OL, or OH per the Unified Soil 
Classification System (USCS) should not be used as a source of select fill 
material due to the poor pavement support characteristics of the soils. However, 
soils classified as SC, GC, ML, MH, CL, or CH per the USCS may be reused as 
a source of general fill for the project construction. 
In the event that imported fill materials are needed for the project construction, 
imported fill materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no 
larger than 75 mm (3 inches) in largest dimension and should contain between 
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have 
a laboratory CBR value of 30 or more and should have a maximum swell of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. For materials 
within the upper 600 mm (2 feet) of the pavement structural section, the select fill 
material should have a CBR value of 40 or greater. Imported fill meeting these 
requirements may be considered select fill materials. 
Imported fill materials, if needed, should be tested for conformance with these 
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. An 
accredited testing laboratory should test the imported fill materials for 
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for 
the intended use.

3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements  
On-site and imported fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture, placed in level lifts of about 300 mm (12 inches) in loose 
thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The 
compaction requirement for fill materials placed in the upper 0.9 meters below 
the pavement grade should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. Aggregate base course materials should be moisture conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 200 mm 
(8 inches) in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 or 100 percent 
relative compaction for rigid and flexible pavements, respectively.  
3.2 
Structure Foundations 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions, the existing fills, new fills, or the 
limestone formation anticipated at the foundation subgrade level would provide suitable 
foundation support for the MWSS and Armory facilities. Therefore, we recommend 
using a shallow foundation system consisting of spread footings and/or continuous strip 
footings to support the new structures. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 200 kilopascals (kPa) may be used for 
design of spread and/or continuous strip footings bearing on compacted fill or limestone 
formation. This bearing value is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be 
increased to 300 kPa when considering transient loads, such as those caused by wind 
or seismic forces. In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 500 mm 
below the lowest adjacent finished grade. If foundations are located next to utility 
trenches, they should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending 
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench. 
Bottom of footing excavations should be re-compacted to at least 95 percent 
relative compaction. Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of footing 
excavations should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm materials. The 
over-excavation may be backfilled with the on-site soils compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction, or the bottom of footing may be extended down to bear 
directly on the underlying competent materials. In the event that the natural limestone

Based on the subsurface conditions and grading concept, we envision new 
concrete slabs-on-grade for the structures likely will bear on the on-site granular soils

subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor 
drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with

Pavement Design

UFC 3-201-01 Civil Engineering, UFC 3-250-01FA Pavement Design for Roads,

Based on the subsurface conditions presented in the RFP documents and the

California Bearing Ratio = 40

3.6 
Underground Utilities 
Generally, the excavated soils from the trench excavation for underground 
utilities should be stockpiled no closer than a horizontal distance equal to the depth of 
the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for appreciable 
ground movement toward the open trench. The trench excavation should comply with 
the applicable local, territorial, and federal safety requirements.

In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 150 mm of 
open-graded gravel (such as ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes to support

(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), also should be used for the initial trench backfill up to

critical to use the free-draining materials around the pipes to reduce the potential for 
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support around 
the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill around the pipe could result in backfill 
settlement and pipe damage. 
As an alternative, the granular bedding and the initial trench backfill (up to about 
300 mm above the top of the pipes) may consist of 25-mm minus, well-graded granular 
materials (such as aggregate base course or similar materials). Where the 25-mm 
minus, well-graded granular materials are used, the granular bedding and the initial 
trench backfill should be moisture-conditioned and compacted to no less than 
90 percent relative compaction. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 300 mm above the pipes to 
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on-site soils 
having particles less than 75 mm in largest dimension. The backfill should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in about 200 mm loose 
lifts, and mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Where trenches are located in the pavement areas, the upper 0.9 meters of the trench 
backfill should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. 
3.7 
Corrosion Potential and Chemical Attack to Concrete 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. 
Generally, the lower the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried 
metallic structure. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will 
contribute to corrosion of metallic objects. Based on our experience in the area, the 
subsurface materials may be classified as mildly to moderately corrosive for buried

recommendations on corrosion protection of metallic substructures, if appropriate. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in the 
soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. Based on our

cement and a minimum concrete cover of 75 mm over reinforcing steel should be
