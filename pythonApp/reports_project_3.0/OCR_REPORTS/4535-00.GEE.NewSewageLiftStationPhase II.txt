INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the New Sewage Lift Station project within the Kia i Kai Hale Housing Phase II area in the
District of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses for the
proposed project The recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of
foundations slabs on grade retaining structures site grading and pavements only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end
of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed New Sewage Lift Station site is located within the Kia i Kai Hale Housing
Phase I project in the Red Hill area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the
information provided it is desired to replace the existing sewage lift station by constructing a
new sewage lift station that provides a built in redundancy and increased wet weil capacity
The project will also include associated demolition of the existing sewage lift station and wet
well removal of the existing chain link fencing and installation of an above ground fuel tank
for the emergency generator
The proposed 1 100 square foot two story lift station building will provide for a ground
level control motor room emergency generator room and toilet facility The lower level will
consist of the dry well and pump room The below grade floors and walls will consist of
cast in place reinforced concrete construction The above grade walls will be constructed of
reinforced concrete masonry The fuel tank area will be enclosed with reinforced concrete
masonry block walls to match the building exterior
Based on our field observations we envision that only minor grading consisting of cuts
and fills on the order of about 3 feet or less would be required to achieve the design finished

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the western
third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern two thirds
of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and its
eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
The project site is located near the northwestern flank of the Aliamanu vent complex
of the Honolulu Volcanic Series As the molten magma ascended through this vent it reacted
with the groundwater generating steam The steam caused explosive eruptions which
deposited low cones of ash material that have consolidated to form tuff locally referred to as
mudrock
The Aliamanu tuff is a water laid ash deposit that was laid down when the sea level was
higher than the present level The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its material over
alluvium originating from Halawa Stream Man made developments in the last century have
brought the site to the existing conditions

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As previously indicated our field exploration generally encountered a dense silty sand
layer starting from the ground surface extending to approximately 5 to 10 feet below the
ground surface The silty sand layer was generally underlain by slightly to highly weathered
medium hard volcanic tuff formation mudrock extending to depths of about 19 5 to 23 feet
below the ground surface Stiff to very stiff silty and clayey soils were encountered below the
volcanic tuff formation extending to depths of about 29 to 34 feet below the ground surface
followed by moderately to highly weathered medium hard volcanic breccia formation
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 40 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our exploration
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe that a
shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used for
support of the new structures planned at the site In addition we anticipate that the concrete
slabs on grade at the site will be supported on the near surface sandy soils encountered in
the borings The near surface sandy soils may be considered to be generally non expansive
in nature Therefore the concrete slabs on grade may be supported on the cushion fill
materials placed on a prepared subgrade
It should be noted that volcanic tuff formation mudrock was encountered in the
borings within the proposed depth of the new sewage lift station Therefore the excavation
for the new sewage lift station about 215 feet deep may encounter some relatively hard
excavation conditions which should be anticipated by the contractor Detailed discussion of
these items and our geotechnical engineering recommendations for design are presented in
the following sections of this report
Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe that
shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used for support
of the new structures planned at the site We recommend that an allowable bearing pressure
of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf be used in the design of foundations bearing on

In general footings for the proposed structures should be embedded a minimum of
18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Where foundations are located next to
utility trenches or easements the foundations should be embedded below an imaginary
45 degree plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or the
foundations should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
The bottom of footing excavations should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction prior to the placement of reinforcing steel If soft and or loose materials
are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations the soft and or loose materials should
be removed to expose the underlying firm or dense materials The over excavation should be
backfilled with approved on site fill materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our recommendations
we estimate the total settlement of footings to be less than 1 inch Differential settlements
between adjacent footings supported on the recompacted soils should be on the order of
about 0 5 inches

Slabs On Grade
We understand that concrete siabs on grade will be used for the new sewage lift
station For the interior building slabs which will not be subjected to vehicular traffic we
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill be provided below the slabs to
provide for uniform support and to serve as a capillary moisture break The cushion fill
material should consist of open graded gravei such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent
damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane
is recommended on top of the cushion fill layer Placement of 2 inches of moist fine sand
above the plastic membrane may be considered to provide for protection of the membrane and
to aid in curing of the slab concrete Provision of the 2 inches of moist fine sand above the
vapor barrier will be left up to the discretion of the project structural engineer
Exterior concrete slabs such as walkways are also planned for the proposed project
In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion fill layer consisting of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be provided below the exterior concrete
slabs To reduce the potential for significant slab cracking due to concrete shrinkage crack
control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion
joints at right angle intersections

Concrete Driveway
We also envision that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the driveway areas For
slabs on grade that will be subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a 6 inch layer of
aggregate subbase be provided below the slab in lieu of the cushion fill layer for uniform
support The vapor barrier and the 2 inch moist fine sand layer may also be omitted for these
types of slabs The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basalt aggregate
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
In general we recommend that concrete slabs that will be subjected to vehicular traffic
be designed for the intended loads For design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade
reaction of 300 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the
compacted aggregate subbase Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across
the slab joints that will be subjected to vehicular traffic The subgrade soils beneath the
driveway slabs should be prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the
Site Grading subsection of this report

Site Grading
Based on the existing topography we anticipate that the proposed site grading may
consist of minor cuts and fills of about 3 feet or less relative to the existing ground surface in
order to achieve the design finished grades in addition an excavation of up to about 25 feet
in depth will be required for construction of the sewage lift station Based on the generally
limited grading anticipated for this project the following general guidelines may be used for
specifying the earthwork requirements for the project
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly
cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish concrete slabs asphaltic concrete
pavements and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly
off site or in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated
materials Existing utilities that are to be abandoned should be removed The resulting
excavation should be properly backfilled with general fill materials moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content placed in 6 to 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing should be over excavated to
expose firm natural materials and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
compacted fill In areas to receive fill and finished subgrades in cut areas the subgrade
should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
GEOLABS INC
W O 4535 00 Page 12
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding
to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the subgrade soil be re scarified moisture conditioned and recompacted as
recommended above Subgrade soils should be kept moist until covered by subsequent
layers of fill materials Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with compacted fill at no additional cost to the owner
In general we believe that the excavated on site soils may be re used as a source of
general fill material at the site provided that the materials are free of vegetation deleterious
materials and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension
Where imported materials are required as fill the material should consist of
non expansive select granular materials such as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder sand
Imported materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing
the No 200 sieve The imported fill materials should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or
more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1883 Imported materials should be tested for conformance with
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use
General fills should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction The compaction requirement for the last lift of fills placed
under the pavements should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed to compact the fills due to the nature
of the on site soils

Excavation
As previously mentioned construction of the new sewage lift station will require an
excavation of up to about 25 feet below the existing ground surface Based on our field
exploration the proposed project site is underlain by dense silty sands medium hard volcanic
tuff and stiff to very stiff silty clay materials It is anticipated that the near surface materials
consisting of dense silty sands may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment
such as excavating with a backhoe excavator However excavations into the volcanic tuff at
greater depths may require the use of hoerams or chipping
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based on
our visual observation of the existing site conditions and field data from the borings drilled at
the site We recommend that contractors proposing to bid on this proiect be encouraged to
examine the site conditions and the boring data to make their own interpretation

Drainage
The finished grades outside the new structure should be sloped to shed water away
from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundations and pavement areas Excessive landscape waiering near the foundations and
pavement areas should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration into
the subsurface
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce
the potential for significant water infiltration into the subsurface in addition drainage swales
should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water
runoff away from the foundations and pavement areas

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about 39 to
40 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings drilled are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD
Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses
a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit
is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring
attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs
of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be
50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical Handbook
of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The results of the
tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B 1
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed on
a bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
near surface soils The results of the test are presented on Plate B 2
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk sample
of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content relationships The
results of the test are presented on Plate B 3
