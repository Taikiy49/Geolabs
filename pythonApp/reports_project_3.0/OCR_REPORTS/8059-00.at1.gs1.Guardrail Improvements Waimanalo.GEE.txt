SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The project site is generally underlain by a 4.5 feet thick fill layer followed by 
alluvium extending to the maximum depth explored of 31.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The fill layer consisted of medium dense silty gravel with some sand and very 
stiff silty clay. The alluvium was composed of medium stiff to very stiff clayey silt with a 
little sand. We encountered groundwater in the drilled boring at a depth of 12 feet below 
the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. The groundwater level 
encountered generally corresponds to about Elevation +66 feet MSL. It should be noted 
that the groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, surface water runoff, 
and other factors. 
Several design alternatives consisting of installing a GRP slope, conventional 
retaining wall, and a soil nailed wall were considered for the project. Due to the limited 
space available within the right-of-way and the need to properly shore a near vertical 
excavation, the use of a soil nailed wall system was recommended. 
We understand that the soil nailed wall will have a height of about 13 feet and a 
length of about 27 feet. The soil nailed wall system consists of a series of individual 
reinforcing bars grouted into drilled holes to stabilize the steep slope. Based on our 
analysis, the installation of minimum 5.5-inch diameter soil nails with vertical and horizontal 
spacing of 5 feet is recommended. The first layer of soil nails should be about 2 feet below 
the top of the slope. The soil nails should have a minimum embedment length of 16 feet and 
be placed at an inclination of about 15 degrees from horizontal. Due to the very corrosive 
subsoil anticipated at the project site, a double corrosion protection system should be 
provided to protect the nails against corrosion. A soil nail testing program should be 
implemented, consisting of performing proof testing on a minimum of 10 percent of the 
production nails. 
The soil nailed wall should be well-drained to reduce the potential for buildup of 
hydrostatic pressures. A drainage system, consisting of 2-foot wide strips of a 
prefabricated drainage composite product, should be installed on the face of the slope 
before application of the shotcrete facing. The prefabricated drainage composite product 
should be installed extending from the top of the slope to the base of the slope and be 
hydraulically connected to weep holes at the base of the wall. The shotcrete facing should 
be installed by an experienced nozzleman.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Guardrail Improvements – Waimanalo project located in the Waimanalo 
area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown 
on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and recommendations presented herein are intended for the design 
of the wall system only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject 
to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project is located on the east side of Mahailua Street near the intersection of 
Kakaina Street and Mahailua Street in Waimanalo on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. Based on 
the information provided, we understand that a new retaining wall system with a height of 
about 13 feet and a length of about 27 feet is planned for the project.  We also understand 
that a 3-foot tall impact barrier will be constructed at the top of the retaining wall. 
An existing 18-inch drain line crosses the roadway and extends through the new wall 
area.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series 
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone eruptions. These later eruptions are referred 
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosion processes are dominated by 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope 
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once these materials reach 
the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become dominant, and the 
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. 
In general, stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the stream 
flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations. Because of this, transport 
of sediments is intermittent, and the bulk of the stream's hydraulic load consists of a 
poorly sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines. When the erosional 
base levels change, these sediment loads are left as deposits.

When deposits are left in place for long periods of time, chemical processes begin 
to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material. 
Chemical processes also cause induration, or cementation, of the coarse-grained portion 
of the sediment resulting in a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate. 
Simultaneously, erosion continues in areas above the valley floors and upstream in 
headwaters. This continued erosion generates material, which is transported downslope, 
covering the older alluvial deposits. 
The project site is located below the northeast flank of the Koolau Range. Basalt 
rock formation of the Koolau Volcanic Series forms the stratigraphic base of the region 
and is believed to underlie the near-surface deposits of alluvial and colluvial materials. 
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is along the east side of Mahailua Street near the intersection of 
Kakaina Street and Mahailua Street in Waimanalo on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
Mahailua Street is a two-lane roadway with a traffic lane in each direction. The roadway 
is covered by asphaltic concrete pavement with vegetation and trees on each side of the 
road.  
Based on the topographic survey plan provided, the existing elevation of the 
existing road surface is about +78 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). East of the roadway, the 
existing ground slopes down to about +68 feet MSL. 
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
one boring, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 31.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
The boring performed at the project site encountered 3-inch of asphaltic concrete 
overlain a fill layer consisting of medium dense silty gravel with some sand and stiff silty 
clay extending to a depth of 4.5 feet. The fill layer was underlain by alluvium extending to 
the maximum depth explored of 31.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The alluvium 
generally consists of medium stiff to very stiff clayey silt with a little sand.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by medium 
dense and stiff fill layers and medium stiff to very stiff alluvium soils extending to the 
maximum depth explored of 31.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Groundwater 
was encountered in the drilled boring at a depth of about 12 feet below the existing ground 
surface at the time of our field exploration. The groundwater depth corresponds to an 
elevation of about +66 feet MSL.  
Several design alternatives were considered. The soil nailed wall system was 
selected for the project. Detailed discussions and recommendations for these items and 
other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Design Considerations and Alternatives 
The project consists of the installation of a new guardrail system on the east side 
of the roadway that can resist the design vehicle impact loading. The City and County of 
Honolulu right-of-way is located about 11 feet beyond the edge of the existing roadway. 
In addition, an existing 18-inch drain line is located at the new guardrail location. 
Installation of a Grouted Riprap (GRP) slope was initially considered. Due to the 
limited space and the steep slope required to be within the right-of-way, it was determined 
that this alternative was not feasible. 
Another alternative considered was the use of a conventional retaining wall 
system. The conventional wall system would require a near vertical excavation at the 
edge of the existing roadway for installation. Therefore, proper shoring of the excavation 
would be needed. In addition, a large footing would be needed to resist the lateral earth 
loading and impact loading on the wall. Based on the above, this alternative was not 
selected.  
The use of a soil nailed wall was also considered. The soil nailed wall system 
consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars grouted into drilled holes to stabilize the 
slope. A shotcrete facing is generally applied to the face of the stabilized slope. The 
reinforcing bars provide both additional tensile and shear strength to the soil mass. This

Slope Preparation
At the on-set of soil nail installation, vegetation on the existing slope should be 
removed and disposed of properly off-site. The small trees should be cut down to 
the existing slope surface. The remaining roots and stumps should be sprayed and 
killed. 
 Soil Nailed Wall 
The soil nailed wall system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars 
grouted into drilled holes used to stabilize the steep slope. Design of the soil nailed 
wall system will need to consider both the internal and external stability of the 
reinforced mass. The design of the internal stability includes establishing the size,

spacing, orientation, and length of the grouted reinforcing bars. The external stability 
includes slope stability of the reinforced mass. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that impact barrier and soil nailed 
wall system must be designed to withstand 27 kips of impact load. In addition, a 
typical vehicular live load of 250 pounds per square foot (psf) was used in the 
analysis.  
The soil nails should be installed by drilling a minimum 5.5-inch diameter hole with 
inclination of approximately 15 degrees from horizontal. The soil nail bar should 
consist of ASTM A615 Grade 75 threaded bar with minimum bar diameter of 
1.250 inches. We anticipated that the existing subsoil at the project site may be very 
corrosive. Therefore, we recommend using a double corrosion protection system for 
the nails. Galvanized or epoxy coated bars surrounded by neat cement grout or 
sand-cement mixture with a minimum 28-day compressive strength of 4,000 pounds 
per square inch (psi) may be considered. 
Based on our soil nail analysis, we recommend using a design embedment nail 
length of 16 feet for the nails extending into the fill and alluvium materials 
encountered in our field exploration. The soil nails should be spaced 5 feet on-center 
horizontally and vertically. The first nail should be installed about 2 feet below the top 
of the slope. The soil nails and drainage strips should be positioned to avoid the 
existing 18-inch drain line. 
To reduce the potential for undermining the soil nailed wall system, the soil nailed 
wall should be embedded at least 4 feet below the existing ground surface at the 
bottom of the wall. 
Soil Nail Installation 
Potentially difficult drilling conditions may be encountered during the installation of 
the soil nails due to the presence of cobbles and boulders within the fill and alluvium. 
The contractor should anticipate the presence of cobbles, boulders, and tree roots at 
the site. Utilizing a temporary casing may be required to maintain an open hole for 
the soil nail installation.

Soil Nail Testing 
Due to the limited soil nails that will be installed, we believe the performance of 
pre-production verification testing on sacrificial soil nail is not needed. Proof tests 
should be performed on at least 10 percent of the production soil nails during 
construction to confirm the bond stresses used in the design. 
The proof tests should consist of subjecting the soil nail to at least 150 percent of the 
design loads, and the load should be held for at least 10 minutes (until stable). The 
proof test nails may be incorporated into the permanent soil nailed wall provided the 
nail satisfies the test criteria. Pullout tests on the soil nails are integral parts of the 
design of the soil nailed wall system. Therefore, a Geolabs representative should 
observe the pullout tests. 
Shotcrete Facing 
Shotcrete placement should be performed by an experienced nozzleman. Prior to 
production shotcreting, it is recommended that unreinforced test panels of 
shotcrete be constructed for inspection. 
Drainage 
The soil nailed wall should be well-drained to reduce the potential for buildup of 
hydrostatic pressures. A drainage system, consisting of 2-foot wide strips of a 
prefabricated drainage composite product, should be installed on the face of the 
slope before application of the shotcrete facing. The prefabricated drainage 
composite product should be installed extending from the top of the slope to the 
base of the slope and be hydraulically connected to weep holes at the base of the 
wall.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field boring. Variations of the subsurface conditions 
between and beyond the field boring may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then 
appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations presented 
herein. 
The field boring location indicated herein is approximate, having been taped from 
features shown on the Site Plan provided by KSF, Inc. on February 4, 2020. Elevation 
shown on the Log of Boring was obtained by interpolating between the spot elevations 
and contour lines shown on the same plan. The physical location and elevation of the 
boring should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used.  
The stratification breaks shown on the graphic representation of the boring depict 
the approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual 
transition. Water level data from the boring was measured at the time shown on the 
graphic representation and/or presented in the text of this report. These data have been 
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. It should be noted that 
the groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, seasonal precipitation, 
surface water runoff, and other factors.

application to the proposed Guardrail Improvements - Waimanalo project in accordance 
with generally accepted geotechnical engineering principles and practices. No warranty 
is expressed or implied. 
This report has been prepared solely for the purpose of assisting the design 
engineers in the design of the project. Therefore, this report may not contain sufficient 
data, or the proper information, to serve as a basis for detailed construction cost 
estimates.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
one boring, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 31.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The boring was drilled using a truck mounted drill rig with continuous flight augers.  
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representation of the materials encountered is 
presented on the Log of Boring, Plate A-1. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler 
using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second 
and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Log 
of Boring at the appropriate sample depth. The penetration resistance shown on the Log of 
Boring indicates the number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow 
counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on 
the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depth.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample 
depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test results 
is provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D 3080) were performed on selected soil samples to 
evaluate the shear strength parameters. The test results are presented on Plates B-2 and 
B-3.
Two Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D2166) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the soil. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-4 and B-5. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio Test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a 
bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-6.
