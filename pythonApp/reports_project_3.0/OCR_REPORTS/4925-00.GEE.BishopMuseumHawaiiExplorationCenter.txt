SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Hawaii Exploration Center at Bishop Museum in the Kalihi area of
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses for the subject project The recommendations presented herein are intended for
the design of foundations slabs on grade retaining structure site grading and pavements
only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is located within the Bishop Museum facility in the Kalihi area of
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project involves a new exploration center
located at the southwest corner of the Bishop Museum property
We understand that the exploration center will consist of a concrete steel and
glass finish structure with a vertical height of about 25 to 30 feet The proposed structure
will have two levels which includes a basement level on the east side The basement
level will have a proposed finished floor elevation of approximately 50 feet Mean Sea
Level MSL The proposed structure will have one level on the west side The single
level will have a finished floor elevation of approximately 64 feet MSL An elevator shaft
is also planned at the east end of the structure
In addition we understand that a parking area and driveways will be located to the
east of the proposed structure The parking area will consist of 25 parking spaces We
envision that flexible pavements consisting of asphaltic concrete will be used at the
parking area and driveways

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano became
extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and it was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank
The project site is located near the mouth of Kalihi Valley which was formed by
stream erosion of the Tertiary Age Koolau basalt flows This initial period of erosion and
valley carving was followed by several periods of relative rise and fall of sea level which
permitted valley filling by alluvium followed by subsequent periods of erosion During the
Quaternary Age Honolulu Volcanic Series lava flowed down the valley and was
subsequently partially covered by Quaternary Age alluvium

2 4 Soil Profile Type for Seismic Design
Our field exploration at the site generally encountered stiff to very stiff surface silty
and clayey soils of about 1 to 7 feet thick across the project site The surface soils were
generally underlain by very hard basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depths
explored
Based on the subsoil conditions at the project site we believe that the project site
may be classified as a Hard Rock Profile Therefore we believe that the seismic design
of the building structure at the project site may be designed based on a Soil Profile Type
Sa in accordance with Table 16 J of the Uniform Building Code 1997

with a finished floor elevation of approximately 64 feet MSL The east side of the

3 2 Foundation Probing
Based on our field exploration at the site basalt rock formation was encountered at
depths of about 1 to 7 feet below the existing ground surface We anticipate that the
basement level will bear on the basalt rock formation It should be noted that voids and or
cavities may be present in the basalt rock formation encountered at the project site
Due to the anticipated light loads imposed by the structure we believe that the
potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities below the
foundations is relatively small However if it is desired to further reduce the potential for
loss of foundation support then a program of cavity probing and grouting at the foundation
locations for the basement area should be conducted during construction

probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend at least 10 feet
below the planned bottom of footing elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or
suspected during the probing operation additional probe holes should be drilled at closer

3 3 Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for this project As
previously mentioned the proposed structure will consist of a basement level on the east
side and a single level on the west side Based on the subsurface conditions encountered
at the project site the basement level on the east side will bear on very hard basalt rock
formation and the single level on the west side will bear on the fills and alluvial deposits
Our laboratory test results indicated that the near surface silty clay soils encountered at
the site exhibit a moderate to high expansion potential when subjected to moisture
fluctuations Therefore special attention should be given to the silty clay soil subgrade
preparation procedures and slab on grade design at this site

subgrade soils underneath the single level slab areas on the west side of the structure

above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction In addition the slab subgrade area should be capped with at least

Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation

3 8 Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines will be required for the proposed project In
general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of
6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as No 3BFine gravel
No 67 gradation should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction
effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use
this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper
trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to

placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to a minimum of

3 9 Drainage
It should be noted that surface and subsurface drainage at the site are especially
critical considering the expansive nature of the near surface soils at the site Therefore
the finished grades outside the new building structure should be sloped to shed water
away from the slabs and foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding around the
structure It is also advised that gutter systems be installed around the building and that
the discharge be diverted away from the slab and foundation areas Excessive landscape
watering near the slabs and foundations should also be avoided Planters next to
foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the
potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete
to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface Drainage
swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface
water runoff away from the slabs and foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
eight borings to depths of about 8 to 17 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring
tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock formation and or boulder materials encountered at the
site were obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and classification

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would
be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
