SECTION 1.  
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The West Kauai Energy Project is generally located in the Waimea area on the Island of 
Kauai, Hawaii. Based on the information provided, we understand the intent of the project is to 
develop low-cost renewable energy projects on the island, including rehabilitation of the Puu Lua 
reservoirs in accordance with current dam safety regulatory standards. The project location is 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
Based on the agreed upon scope of services requested of Geolabs, Inc., our geotechnical 
field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling one boring extending to a depth of about 
142 feet below the existing ground surface at the location provided. As requested, we conducted 
constant head permeability tests at a depth of about 125 feet below the existing ground surface to 
evaluate the permeable characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered at the site.

SECTION 3.  
SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to geotechnical engineers for design are the subsurface materials encountered 
at the project site, the engineering properties of the materials encountered, and the variability 
of the subsurface conditions across the project site. Therefore, the following sections provide a 
description of the geologic setting of the project site, the surface and subsurface materials 
encountered at the site, and the groundwater conditions encountered. 
3.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai is essentially a dissected basaltic dome that was initially formed by a 
single large shield volcano, which has subsequently been deeply eroded and partially covered 
with later (younger) volcanic materials. The Island of Kauai is the oldest of the main Hawaiian 
Islands and today encompasses approximately 555 square miles in area with a maximum 
elevation of about 5,170 feet above Mean Sea Level (MSL) at Mt. Wai’ale’ale near the center of 
the island. It is believed that during the Tertiary Period (about 5 to 6 million years ago in the 
Pliocene Epoch), basaltic lavas belonging to the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series built a roughly 
circular island volcano from the ocean floor that was estimated to have been about 20,000 feet 
deep at the time. Near the end of the Pliocene Epoch, this main shield building volcanic phase 
came to a halt. 
Following a long period of erosion and island mass subsidence, volcanism was renewed 
during the early Pleistocene Epoch (about 1.5 million years ago) with the eruption of the Koloa 
Volcanic Series from multiple vents throughout the island. This post-erosional volcanic activity 
laid a veneer of fresh lava and interbedded volcanic sediments over the older deposits of the 
Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series. Because of the long period of erosion between the eruption of 
the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series and the Koloa Volcanic Series, deposits of the Koloa Volcanic 
Series typically filled the erosional valleys and depressions that existed at that time. 
Consequently, the basaltic rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are commonly found overlying thick 
residual soil and alluvial soil deposits. 
The dominant geologic processes of weathering, landform erosion, and sediment 
re-deposition followed during the late Pleistocene and Recent Epochs. Combined with the

erosional and depositional effects of the Pleistocene sea-level fluctuations, which occurred in 
response to the worldwide advance and retreat of the continental ice sheets, the Island of Kauai 
has evolved to its present form. 
The project site is underlain by basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lava of the Waimea 
Canyon Volcanic Series. These rocks are generally characterized by flows of smooth, vesicular 
basalt with thin, interbedded clinker layers. In-situ chemical weathering of the Waimea lava flows 
occurred in the last 2 to 4 million years, forming a mantle of residual soils, which grade to 
saprolite with increasing depth. In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is 
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The saprolite material grades with increasing 
depth to a basaltic rock formation. An embankment of man-made fill was placed over these 
materials to construct the existing Puu Lua Dam within the last century. 
3.2 
Subsurface Characteristics 
In general, we observed two main stratigraphic units at the West Kauai Energy Project, 
Puu Lua Dam and Reservoir project site. The stratigraphic units consist of soil materials of varying 
character and engineering properties. The two main stratigraphic units are listed below in the 
general order of geologic age, beginning with the younger unit and ending with the older unit: 
1. 
Recent Fill (Rf) 
2. 
Waimea Canyon Volcanics (Twnl) 
The following subsections briefly describe the general characteristics of each of the two 
primary stratigraphic units above based on our literature research, past experience with similar 
materials, and the field exploration and laboratory testing program.  
3.2.1 Recent Fill (Rf) 
This stratigraphic unit represents elevated areas and near-surface soils that were filled 
during the construction of the Puu Lua Dam. At the project site where the borehole was 
drilled and sampled, the fill materials generally consisted of medium stiff to stiff clayey 
and sandy silts extending down to a depth of about 111 feet below the existing ground 
surface.

The quality and composition of the fill materials are variable. These fills were frequently 
placed without proper compaction control. Therefore, it is difficult to make any general 
statements regarding their engineering properties or construction in these areas. 
3.2.2 Waimea Canyon Volcanics (Twnl) 
Waimea Canyon Volcanics is the oldest geologic unit observed in our test boring drilled at 
the Puu Lua Dam. Generally, it consists of highly weathered basalt, consisting of saprolite, 
overlying basalt formation anticipated at greater depths. In addition, it is anticipated that 
various types of volcanic rock sub-components, such as clinker layers and lava tubes, also 
may be encountered at greater depths within the basalt formation. The following 
subunits are used in this geotechnical data report. 
Saprolite 
Saprolite is essentially derived from the tropical weathering of volcanic 
formations, which may contain intrusive rock bodies of variable composition, such 
as basalt, andesite, etc. Saprolite is a form of residual soil that exhibits a relict 
structure from the parent rock. Structural features, such as vesicles and joints, are 
visible in saprolite. This subunit consists of predominantly silty and clayey soils 
(and, in some instances, sandy and gravelly soils) containing variations in color 
with varying degrees of mottling. 
3.2.3 Groundwater 
Groundwater level was not observed during our field exploration program during the 
advancement of the boreholes drilled for the project. However, it should be noted that 
perched groundwater and/or subterranean seepage have been known to exist at sites of 
high elevations. It should be noted that groundwater levels may vary depending on 
rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.

SECTION 4.  
GEOTECHNICAL EXPLORATION PROGRAM 
The geotechnical exploration program conducted for the proposed West Kauai Energy 
Project located at the Puu Lua Dam and Reservoir generally consisted of the following field and 
laboratory work components. A brief description of each work component is presented below. 
Detailed discussions, findings, and test results of the various components are provided in the 
respective appendices herein. 
4.1 
Test Boring and Associated Field Testing 
The test boring was drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig utilizing 4-inch diameter 
continuous-flight solid-stem augers (SSA), 8-inch diameter continuous-flight hollow-stem augers 
(HSA), and HQ coring tools to advance the boring. Our logger classified the materials encountered 
in the boring by visual and textural examination in the field and observed the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and 
laboratory testing. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard 
Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as 
shown on the Soil Log Legend (Plate A-0.1). Deviations made to the soil classification in 
accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log Key (Plate A-0.2). 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered in the drilled boring are presented on the 
Logs of Boring in Appendix A. 
Sampling of the soils generally was performed between about 1.5 and 5-foot intervals. 
Drive samples were obtained using the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) split-spoon sampler 
(ASTM D1586). Relatively “undisturbed” samples were obtained by pushing a 3-inch OD 
thin-walled steel Shelby tube (ASTM D1587) or by driving a tube-lined modified California 
sampler (ASTM D3550) into the soil formation. 
In-situ testing of the subsurface materials in the boring included the SPT, which was 
performed in accordance with ASTM D1586. Standard Penetration Test sampling provides a 
measure of the resistance or consistency of the soil and is reported as the uncorrected N-value,

4.2 
Laboratory Testing 
Laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples to determine the physical and 
engineering properties. Laboratory tests performed included moisture contents, unit weights, 
Atterberg Limits, grain size distribution, specific gravity, direct shear, Consolidated-Undrained 
Triaxial Compression (TXCU), Unconsolidated-Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU), 
consolidation, modified proctor, crumb, and pinhole tests. The laboratory testing was performed 
at our accredited laboratory in Oahu with the exception of select crumb and the pinhole tests, 
which were performed by GeoTesting Express, Inc. The laboratory test results performed by our 
laboratory and by GeoTesting Express, Inc. are presented in Appendices B and D, respectively. 
4.3 
Field Permeability Tests 
Two permeability tests were conducted in the borehole using the constant head test 
method. In general, the borehole had a diameter of about 3.5 inches and extended to a depth of 
about 125 feet below the ground surface at the time of the tests. 
A water truck was used to pump clear water into the borehole until establishing and 
maintaining a constant water level. The flow rate was recorded by timing and calculating the 
volume as a relative constant flow rate was achieved. Results of the permeability tests conducted 
in the boring are presented on Plates C-1 and C-2.

LIMITATIONS 
The geotechnical field and laboratory test data submitted in this geotechnical data report 
are based upon information obtained from the field exploration point. Variations of subsurface 
conditions beyond the field data point may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations 
may not become evident until construction is underway. 
The field boring indicated herein is approximate, having been staked out in the field using 
a hand-held Global Positioning System (GPS) device. The physical location of the boring should 
be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the field exploration point 
depict the approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual 
transition. We did not encounter groundwater in the boring at the time of our field exploration. 
However, it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in seasonal rainfall, surface 
water runoff and other factors.

for specific application to the West Kauai Energy Project in accordance with generally accepted 
geotechnical engineering principles and practices. No warranty is expressed or implied. This 
report has been prepared solely for the purpose of assisting the engineers in the evaluation and 
design of the project. 
This geotechnical data report was not intended to investigate the potential for the 
presence of hazardous materials existing at the project site. It should be noted that the 
equipment, techniques, and personnel used to conduct a geo-environmental exploration differ 
substantially from those applied in geotechnical engineering.

Test Boring Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling one 
boring, designated as Boring PL-1. The boring was extended to a depth of about 142 feet below 
the existing ground surface using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous-flight 
augers and coring tools. The drilling and sampling work was conducted between 
March 14 and 25, 2022. The boring location is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
The test boring was advanced with 4-inch diameter continuous-flight solid-stem augers 
(SSA), 8-inch diameter continuous-flight hollow-stem augers (HSA), and HQ coring tools from the 
ground surface. After the boring was advanced to the maximum drill depth, the bored hole 
generally was backfilled with a cement/bentonite/water grout mixture. 
Our field personnel classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for 
Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the 
laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for 
Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on 
the Soil Log Legend (Plate A-0.1). Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with 
ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil Classification Log Key (Plate A-0.2). Graphic representations 
of the materials encountered and other observations, such as groundwater levels, etc., are 
presented on the Logs of Boring, Plates A-1.1 through A-1.5. 
Soil samples were obtained from the boring drilled at the depths indicated in general 
accordance with ASTM D1586, Standard Test Method for Penetration Test and Split-Barrel 
Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound 
hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained 
from the boring drilled in general accordance with ASTM D3550, Standard Practice for Thick Wall, 
Ring-Lined, Split Barrel, Drive Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD modified California 
sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6-inch increments of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. Relatively 
“undisturbed” samples were obtained by pushing a 3-inch OD thin-walled stainless steel Shelby 
tube into the soil formation in general accordance with ASTM D1587, Standard Practice for 
Thin-Walled Tube Sampling of Soils for Geotechnical Purposes. The range of hydraulic pressures 
needed to advance the sampler through a 24-inch push is shown as the “Penetration Resistance” 
on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Samples recovered from our geotechnical field exploration program performed for the 
West Kauai Energy Project were visually examined and classified in our laboratory in Oahu, 
Hawaii. Selected samples were tested to evaluate the physical characteristics and engineering 
properties of the soils encountered. The laboratory testing program was formulated with the 
emphasis on evaluating the moisture, density, classification, strength, compressibility, and 
moisture-density relationship of the soils encountered. 
The laboratory tests performed included determination of dry density and natural 
moisture content, Atterberg Limits, grain size distribution, specific gravity, a direct shear test, 
Consolidated-Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXCU), Unconsolidated-Undrained Triaxial 
Compression (TXUU), consolidation, modified proctor, and crumb/pinhole tests. Results of the 
tests performed are presented on Plates B-1 through B-24. Detailed descriptions of the various 
tests performed for this exploration are presented below. 
Moisture and Density 
Moisture content and dry density tests were performed on selected soil samples to aid in 
defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered. The moisture content tests 
were performed in general accordance with ASTM D2216. The test results are presented on the 
Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. 
Atterberg Limits 
To evaluate the liquid and plastic limits of the soil encountered, 20 Atterberg Limits tests 
were performed in accordance with ASTM D4318. The test results are summarized on the Logs 
of Boring at the appropriate sample depths, and graphic presentations of the test results are 
provided on Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Sieve Analysis 
To provide information on the particle size distribution of the soils encountered, 20 Sieve 
Analysis tests, including four Hydrometer tests, were performed to evaluate the gradation 
characteristics and to aid in soil classification. The Sieve Analysis tests were performed in 
accordance with ASTM C117 and C136 and the Hydrometer tests were performed in accordance 
with ASTM D422. Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plates B-3 through 
B-6.

Specific Gravity 
Three Specific Gravity tests (ASTM D854) were performed on selected soil samples to 
evaluate the engineering properties of the in-situ soils.  Results of the tests are summarized on 
Plate B-7 and presented on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. 
Direct Shear 
To evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the in-situ soils, one strain-controlled, 
consolidated-drained Direct Shear test was performed on an in-situ soil sample in accordance 
with ASTM D3080. The test results are presented on Plate B-8.  
Consolidated-Undrained Triaxial Compression 
Three Consolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXCU) tests (ASTM D4767) were 
performed on selected in-situ soil samples to evaluate the drained and undrained shear strength 
of the silty/clayey soils encountered. The test results, stress-strain curves, and the Mohr Circles 
are presented on Plates B-9.1 through B-11.3. 
Unconsolidated-Undrained Triaxial Compression 
To evaluate the undrained shear strength of the silty/clayey soils, two Unconsolidated 
Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests were performed on selected in-situ soil samples in 
accordance with ASTM D2850. Confining pressures provided by the project designer engineer 
were applied to the “undisturbed” samples. The test results and the stress-strain curves are 
presented on Plates B-12 and B-13. 
Consolidation 
Four Consolidation tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the 
compressibility characteristics of the on-site soils. The tests were performed in accordance with 
ASTM D2435, and the test results are plotted on Plates B-14 through B-17. 
Modified Proctor Test 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557 Method A) was performed on a 
composite sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationship. The test results are presented on Plate B-18. 
Crumb Test 
Five Crumb tests (ASTM D6572) were performed by GeoTesting Express on selected soil 
samples to determine the dispersive characteristics of the in-situ soils. The test results are 
summarized on Plates B-19 through B-24, also presented in Appendix D.

Field Permeability Tests 
As part of our field exploration program, we conducted two permeability tests in the 
boring using the constant head test method. In general, the boring had a diameter of about 3.5 
inches and extended to a depth of about 125 feet below the ground surface at the time of the 
tests. 
A water truck was used to pump clear water into the borehole until established and 
maintained the constant water level. The flow rate was recorded by timing and calculating the 
volume as a relative constant flow rate was achieved. Results of the permeability tests conducted 
in the boring are presented on Plates C-1 and C-2.
