2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during recent geologic time
Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both are still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Mauna Kea which has
formed the west central portion of the island The project site appears to be underlain by
basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea which were
deposited during the Pleistocene Epoch Stearns and Macdonald 1946
The Hamakua Volcanic Series are typically composed of basaltic lava flows
which are mantled by a relatively thin layer of palagonitized ash locally referred to as
Pahala Ash The ash soil at the project site differs from Pahala Ash found in other
portions of the Island of Hawaii in that the in situ moisture content is generally lower
and the soil exists in a dry friable state The soils at the site have been subjected to
wind erosion resulting in the formation of a desert pavement on the surface This desert
pavement is a surficial layer of cobbles and gravel which were left behind as the finer
ash was blown away
The lava formation encountered at the site appears to be of both a a and
pahoehoe type flows which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean A a
lava is typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling
a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders Pahoehoe is
characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe type
lava Cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like

Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water

transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and_ inter flow
contacts cavities and along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities
is high therefore they serve as the primary water transmission features

2 4 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in
the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of

2 5 Site Specific Response Spectrum Analysis
In accordance with your request we have performed a seismic hazard analysis
and site response evaluation with respect to seismic design considerations for the
proposed project The purpose of our seismic hazard analysis and site response
evaluation is to establish appropriate design elastic response spectra taking into
consideration the proximity of the site to seismic sources regional seismicity and
site specific subsurface conditions A response spectrum is used by the structural
engineer to perform an analysis of the structure given the dynamic loading conditions
and the ground motions at the foundation elevations
The response spectrum is a graphical representation relating to the maximum
response of a single degree of freedom damped elastic system with different
fundamental periods to dynamic forces The anticipated earthquake ground motions and
dynamic response of the subsurface materials represented by site specific elastic
response spectra
Design Ground Motion
Based on the 1997 Uniform Building Code UBC the Island of Hawaii is within
Seismic Zone 4 with an associated peak ground acceleration of 0 40g
Alternatively site specific design ground motions may be developed based on a
site specific probabilistic seismic hazard analysis PSHA or determined based on
seismic hazard maps In any case the site specific design ground motions should
be based on a 10 percent probability of exceedance in 50 years This level of
seismic hazard is equivalent to an annual probability of occurrence of 0 002107
events per year or a return period of approximately 475 years
As previously mentioned the subsurface conditions generally consist of volcanic
ash and a a clinker materials overlying dense basalt formation Based on the
subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration the project site may be
classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as being a Very Dense Soil and Soft
Rock site corresponding to an Sc soil profile type based on the Uniform Building
Code Table No 16 J 1997 Edition

foundations during construction Foundation probing and grouting may be omitted for

3 4 Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the floors of the new

At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious materials and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site to
reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials used as

Finish subgrades in cut areas and areas to be filled should be proof rolled with a

further collapse under the load of heavy equipment The vibratory drum roller used
for the proof rolling operations should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per

Fills and Backfills
In general we recommend using structural fill materials to fill the project site from
the existing ground surface to achieve the design subgrades Structural fill
materials should consist of well graded granular materials 6 inches in largest
dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted
mass The excavated on site materials may be re used as a source of structural fill
materials provided that the materials are processed to meet the maximum particle
size and gradation requirements
Based on the limited height and extent of fill required for the project materials
larger than 6 inches should not be used as a source of fill for the proposed project
If the excavated on site materials do not contain sufficient fines to produce a
fragments or boulders should be considered to provide the required gradation and
particle sizes to develop a well graded material Imported material if necessary
should be observed and or tested by Geolabs for its suitability prior to being
transported to the site for the intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum
moisture content is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the

vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of rocks
and or cobbles Instead a Geolabs representative should conduct a testing
program to evaluate the number of passes needed by a compactor to achieve the
desired level of compaction at the start of the grading phase of the project Based
on this testing program the number of passes may then be used as the field
criterion for adequate compaction
For preliminary estimating purposes compaction may be accomplished by using a

speed of about 300 feet per minute The roller should pass over the fill surface at
least eight times for structural fill
Excavation
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by volcanic
ash and a a clinker over dense basalt formation It is anticipated the near surface
materials may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such as
excavators and ripped with large bulldozers However deeper excavations in the
dense basalt formations may require the use of hoe rams or chipping
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based
on our visual observation of the existing basalt formation and experience with
similar type subsurface conditions We recommend encouraging contractors to
examine the site conditions and boring data to make their own interpretation

The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling twelve borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 12 extending to a maximum depth of about
32 feet below the existing ground surface using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
coring tools The approximate boring locations for the project are shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer geologist who monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 12
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 determinations were performed on selected
soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The test
results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Four Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
rock core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the rock
formation encountered Results of the unconfined compression tests are presented on
Plate B 1
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a
bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The sample was remolded to near the optimum moisture content to perform
the test Results of the CBR test are presented on Plate B 2
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plate B 3
