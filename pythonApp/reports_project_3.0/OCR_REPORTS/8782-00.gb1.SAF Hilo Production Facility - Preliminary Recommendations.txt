This technical memorandum provides our preliminary geotechnical
recommendations for the above project. The following contains our preliminary findings and
geotechnical engineering recommendations for the project based on our recently completed
field exploration program. The laboratory testing program is currently on-going. A detailed
summary of our recommendations will be contained in our geotechnical engineering report.
The report should be consulted when it becomes available.
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located near Hilo Harbor and south of Kalanianaole Street in
Hilo on the Island of Hawaii. There are three existing tanks on the project site. We
understand that two of the existing tanks will be demolished for the construction of five
new tanks, a one-story office building, and a one-story processing building. The tanks
are generally about 48 feet high. A retaining wall is planned along the perimeter of the
tank site, separating the tank site from the parking and driveway areas.
Based on the available grading plans, cuts on the order of about 2 feet or less are
planned at the tank site. Fills on the order of about 3 feet or less are planned along the
parking and driveway areas.
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
13 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 13, extending to depths of about 21.5 to
54 feet below the existing ground surface.

between 3.5 and 8 feet below the existing ground surface. Below the basalt rock formation,
beach deposits consisting of medium dense gravelly sand were encountered in the borings
extending to depths of about 45 feet below the existing ground surface to the maximum
depth explored of about 54 feet below the existing ground surface. Some borings

TANK FOUNDATIONS 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we envision that 
shallow foundations consisting of a perimeter ring footing and concrete slab-on-grade may 
be used to support the new tank walls and interior floors. In order to provide uniform bearing 
for the foundations and concrete floor, we recommend that a 12-inch thick layer of 
compacted aggregate subbase be provided below the foundations and concrete floor of the 
tank structures. The 12-inch thick layer of aggregate subbase should also extend a minimum 
of 12 inches beyond the edges of the tank structures. The aggregate subbase materials 
should be moisture-conditioned to above optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum 
of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of 
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture content is the water content (percentage 
by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
As mentioned previously, hard to very hard basalt rock formations were encountered 
at relatively shallow depths at the locations of the tanks designated as TK-3001 through 
TK-3004. The tank location designated as TK-1601 was underlain by medium dense to 
dense gravelly sand fill extending to a depth of about 11 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the tank locations, we 
recommend using the following allowable bearing pressures for the design of shallow 
foundations.

underlying basalt rock.  
If the foundations for the tank structures are designed and constructed in accordance 
with the recommendations presented herein, the total settlement of foundations is estimated 
to be on the order of 1 inch with differential settlements on the order of about 0.5 inches. 
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance between 
the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure 
developed against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of foundations. 
A coefficient of friction of 0.45 may be used for foundations bearing on the compacted 
aggregate subbase. Resistance to lateral loads due to passive earth pressure may be 
calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 450 pounds per square foot per foot of 
depth (pcf) for TK-3001 through TK-3004 foundations and 350 pcf for TK-1601 foundations. 
These values assume that the soils around the foundations are well-compacted. Unless 
covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below the 
finished grade should be neglected.

SLABS-ON-GRADE 
We anticipate concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the ground floors of the new 
buildings. In addition, exterior concrete slabs-on-grade are planned in the tank site area.  
For the interior slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic), we recommend providing a 
minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill below the slabs for uniform support. The cushion 
fill should consist of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) to serve as a 
capillary moisture break.

To reduce the potential for excess moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to 
floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel cushion 
fill layer. Where provided, flexible floor coverings should be considered above the floor slab 
because they can better mask minor slab cracking. 
Where slabs are subjected to vehicular traffic or sustained vibrations, a minimum of 
6 inches of compacted aggregate subbase materials (3-inch minus granular materials) 
should be provided below the slabs in lieu of the 4-inch thick cushion fill layer. The aggregate 
subbase material layer should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. The impervious 
moisture barrier may be omitted for these slabs. 
For the exterior concrete slabs, we recommend providing a minimum 6-inch thick 
layer of aggregate subbase below exterior concrete slabs that will not be subjected to 
vehicular traffic. The subbase should be compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction. 
To reduce the potential for substantial shrinkage cracks in the concrete slabs, crack 
control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways (or slabs) 
with expansion joints at right-angle intersections. 
FOUNDATION PROBING AND GROUTING 
Based on our exploration and experience in the area, voids and/or cavities appear to 
be present in the basaltic lava flows at the project site. To reduce the potential for loss of 
foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities below foundations, we recommend 
implementing a program of cavity probing and grouting of the tank structure foundations 
during construction. Foundation probing and grouting should also be implemented for the 
processing building and office building. 
For estimating purposes, we recommend that probe holes for the tank structures be 
drilled at 10-foot on centers for the perimeter ring foundation. In addition, probe holes should 
be drilled at each isolated spread footing location. Probe holes also should be drilled within 
the tank floor area at about 10-foot on center, starting at the center of the tank and radiating 
out at 10-foot radial increments. We recommend that probe holes for the processing and 
office buildings be drilled at each isolated spread footing location (one probe hole for every 
50 square feet of footing area) and at 10 feet on-center along wall foundations or 
continuous strip footings. The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and 
should extend to a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation.  
If cavities and/or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation, 
additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacings to help delineate the vertical and 
lateral extent of the cavity and/or void. The probe holes and cavities discovered should be 
backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) with a slump of about 6 to 9 inches. 
The CLSM should be injected at low to moderate pressures. As an alternate to CLSM,

We understand that a retaining wall is planned around the tank site for grade 
separation at the site. The following sections include our recommendations for the retaining 
walls planned for the project. 
Retaining Structure Foundations 
In general, we believe that retaining structure foundations may be designed in 
accordance with the recommendations and parameters presented in the "Building 
Foundations" section herein. The retaining structure foundations may bear directly 
on the recompacted fills or basalt rock formation.  In addition, retaining wall 
foundations should be at least 18 inches wide and the bottom should be embedded 
a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades.

Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, line loads, and point loads within a

a rectangular distribution equal to 40 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure 
acting over the entire height of the structure may be used for the design. Additional 
analyses during the design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of 
point loads and line loads.

Drainage 
In general, retaining structures should be well-drained to reduce the potential for 
build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 
12-inch wide zone of permeable material, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, 
No. 67 gradation), placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) 
at the base of the retaining wall and along the length of the wall. The perforated pipe 
should discharge to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. The weepholes should be 
spaced not more than 6 feet apart. 
As an alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, 
may be used instead of the permeable drainage material. The prefabricated drainage 
product also should be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of 
the retaining wall. Unless covered by concrete or asphaltic concrete, the upper 
12 inches of the backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials, such as 
well-graded soils, to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration behind the 
walls. 
The backfill from the bottom of the wall to the bottom of the weephole should 
consist of relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for significant water 
infiltration into the subsurface. In addition, the upper 12 inches of the retaining wall 
backfill should consist of relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for 
significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure unless covered by 
concrete slabs at the surface.

driveway areas. In general, we anticipate vehicle loading for the new pavement will consist 
of passenger vehicles, light trucks, and some heavy fuel trucks.

the on-site granular fills or new structural fills.  Based on the anticipated traffic loading and 
the competent subgrade soils, we recommend using the following design pavement  section
