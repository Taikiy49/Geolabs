REMARKS The following sections contain our preliminary geotechnical engineering
recommendations for the planning and design of the proposed Puainako
Heights Subdivision Phase I project These recommendations are intended
to inform the owner about potential risks involved and the geotechnical
considerations that may need to be addressed for the proposed project
Subsurface Conditions
Seven borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 7 were drilled at the
project site Our borings indicate that the project site generally is underlain
by basalt formation at or near the ground surface extending down to the
maximum depth explored of approximately 30 feet below the ground
surface The basalt formations encountered in the borings drilled generally
were slightly to moderately weathered and hard to very hard
It should be noted that one of the seven borings Boring No 1 encountered
basalt formation from the ground surface extending down to about 5 5 feet
below the ground surface A layer of volcanic ash of about 9 5 feet in
thickness was encountered below the surface layer of basalt formation The
volcanic ash soils encountered in the boring generally consisted of soft to
medium stiff clayey silts The volcanic ash was underlain by another layer of
basalt formation extending down to the maximum depth explored in that
boring of approximately 17 5 feet below the ground surface
Based on the field exploration conducted at the project site and our
experience in the vicinity of the project site the thicknesses and depths of
the buried volcanic ash layer and the basalt formations are likely to vary at
the project site

General Discussion
Based on a review of available in house information and our field exploration
at the project site we believe that the proposed project generally is feasible
from a geotechnical engineering point of view However it should be noted
that several geotechnical considerations may have the potential for adverse
impacts on the design and construction of the proposed project Based on
our experience and the subsurface conditions encountered at this project
site the geotechnical considerations include but are not limited to the
following These items are further discussed in the following sections of this
memorandum
Site Grading
Storm Water Disposal
In addition it should be noted that the Island of Hawaii is located in an area
with active volcanoes Therefore based on our review of the regional
geology site location and existing ground surface conditions it appears that
some potential geologic hazards may exist at the project site These
potential hazards include but may not be limited to the following
Lava flow encroachment and pyroclastic fallout ash deposition from
possible future volcanic eruptions
Floodwater encroachment due to heavy storms
Although these potential geologic hazards exist at the project site we
believe that these potential geologic hazards do not represent a significant
higher potential on this project site than most of the other developments in
the Kaumana and Piihonua areas of Hilo
Site Grading
The project site generally is heavily vegetated with large trees and thick
shrubs Therefore we envision that significant efforts more than the typical
conditions will be required during clearing and grubbing of the project site
Based on our experience in the area cavities and or lava tubes are
encountered commonly in the basalt formations in the vicinity of the project
site Cavities and or lava tubes may vary significantly in size from small
blisters to large lava tubes of 20 feet in diameter or more In general the
majority of the small cavities are limited in lateral extent However the
potential for long lava tubes that may traverse across the project site exists
Therefore special attention should be given to the site preparation of the
ground surface prior to placement of fills and construction of improvements
on these types of materials

Ripping and proof rolling of the subgrades will be necessary to assist in
detecting and possibly collapsing near surface lava tubes and cavities
Ripping generally is conducted with the teeth of Caterpillar D 10 bulldozers
or similar size equipment The proof rolling process may be conducted
using a combination of Caterpillar D 10 bulldozers and large vibratory drum
rollers In addition the ripping and proof rolling operations should be
observed by a representative from Geolabs to evaluate the potential for the
presence of cavities and or lava tubes near the ground surface
In general soft loose weak or yielding areas or cavities disclosed during
site preparation operations should be over excavated to expose firm and or
dense ground The resulting excavation should be backfilled with
compacted fill materials It should be noted that backfilling of lava tubes may
be extensive due to the nature of these formations Therefore contract
documents for the mass grading should include additive and deductive unit
prices for over excavating the lava tube features and backfilling with
compacted fill to account for variations in the over excavation and backfill
quantities
In general the excavated on site materials except for the volcanic ash
materials may be used as a source of fill materials provided that they are
processed to meet the gradation requirements of the various fill types
recommended for grading of the project site In general structural fills
should consist of well graded granular materials less than 6 inches in
maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids
in the compacted mass General fills should consist of well graded granular
materials the majority of which are less than 12 inches in size with an
absolute maximum dimension of 18 inches
Boulders i e rock fragments larger than 12 inches in maximum size but
less than 3 feet in largest dimension encountered during site grading may
be used in rock fills provided that several provisions are followed In
general boulders should be placed at least 4 feet below the finished
subgrades and at least 6 feet laterally away from slope faces In addition
rock fills should be properly choked off at the surface to reduce the
potential for migration of fines through the voids between boulders
Where encountered in the excavations the volcanic ash materials should
not be re used as a source of fill materials The volcanic ash material should
be disposed of off site or used as a source of landscape materials if
appropriate

As mentioned previously a layer of buried volcanic ash was encountered in
Boring No 1
In general basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can
transmit water quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions
However it should be noted that the presence or absence of lava tubes and
large fractures would have a significant effect on the hydraulic conductivity
values of the basalt formations In addition presence or absence of volcanic
ash deposits will significantly affect the hydraulic conductivity values of
subsurface materials
Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project site the permeability
characteristics of the subsurface materials likely will vary significantly across
the project site We envision that relatively impermeable subsurface
conditions likely will be encountered in some areas of the project site
Because the project site is located in a relatively high rainfall area design of
a storm water disposal system should consider likely variations in the
permeability characteristics of the subsurface materials
The actual permeability characteristics of the subsurface materials will not
become evident until construction is underway Therefore adequate
contingency should be allocated to mitigate potential remediation work for
the storm water disposal system
