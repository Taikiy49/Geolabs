PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Auwaiolimu Street sewer improvement project is located along Auwaiolimu
Street in the Punchbowl area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii as shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1 We understand that the existing 6 inch diameter sewer line
has hydraulic and structural deficiencies and that it is planned to install a new 8 inch
diameter sewer line
The sewer line will begin on Puowaina Drive at a sewer easement located about
300 feet to the east of the intersection between Puowaina Drive and Whiting Street The
sewer line will continue north along the easement until reaching Auwaiolimu Street The
sewer line will then continue to the west about 450 feet along Auwaiolimu Street where it
will connect to an existing sewer line
REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in
age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep
valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed
by cinder and tuff cone eruptions These later eruptions are referred to as the Honolulu
Volcanic Series

During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
The project site is on the northern flank of Punchbowl Hill The Punchbowl Hill
Puowaina is a tuff cone near the center of Honolulu built against the end of a spur of
the Koolau Range The tuff is mostly brown palagonitized vitric ash and lapilli with
scattered fragments of coral limestone and Koolau basalt

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsoil conditions at the subject site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings to depths ranging from about 20 to 20 4 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Boring No 1 encountered 4 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by fill material
consisting of dense silty gravel extending to a depth of about 1 foot below the existing
ground surface The fill material was underlain by medium dense cinder sand extending to
a depth of about 3 feet below the existing ground surface The cinder sand was underlain
by residual soil consisting of stiff sandy silt extending to a depth of about 8 feet below the
existing ground surface The residual soil was underlain by friable volcanic tuff extending
to the maximum depth explored of about 20 1 feet below the existing ground surface The
volcanic tuff was generally medium hard
Boring No 2 encountered 5 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by fill material
consisting of dense silty sand extending to a depth of about 2 feet below the existing
ground surface The fill material was underlain by friable volcanic tuff extending to the
maximum depth explored of about 20 4 feet below the existing ground surface The
volcanic tuff was generally medium hard
Boring No 3 encountered 8 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by fill material
consisting of medium dense sandy gravel extending to a depth of about 2 5 feet below the
existing ground surface The fill material was underlain by medium dense cinder sand
extending to a depth of about 4 5 feet below the existing ground surface The cinder sand
was underlain by closely to slightly fractured volcanic tuff extending to the maximum depth
explored of about 20 feet below the existing ground surface The volcanic tuff was
generally medium hard
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings during the time of our exploration
However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to
rainfall time of year seasonal precipitation surface water runoff and other factors
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on
the Logs of Borings Plates 5 through 7 Results of the laboratory tests performed on
selected soil samples are presented on Plates 8 and 9

2 to 11 5 feet below the existing ground surface Our field exploration generally
encountered fill material cinder sand and residual soils extending to depths of about

Installation of the new pipe using trenchless methods and conventional
open trench method were evaluated for the project Based on our evaluation we
believe that both open trench method and or pipe bursting in conjunction with
open trench method are viable options for the installation of the new 8 inch sewer line
Detailed discussions of these items and our geotechnical engineering recommendations
are presented in the following sections of this report
Open Trench Method
Conventional open trench method is a viable option and may be used for the
installation of the proposed sewer line We envision that the invert depths of the new
sewer line will range from about 2 to 11 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Based on our field exploration groundwater was not encountered in our borings at the
time of our exploration
Excavation Method
It is anticipated that conventional excavation techniques may be used for the
proposed excavations However it should be noted that medium hard volcanic tuff
was encountered in our borings at depths ranging from 2 to 8 feet below the
existing ground surface Therefore excavation in the medium hard volcanic tuff
may require the use of hoerams or chipping In general the method and
equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the contractor
subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavated soils from the
trench excavation should be stockpiled no closer than a horizontal distance equal to
the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential
for ground movement
Excavation Support
As previously mentioned we envision that the new sewer line inverts will range
between about 2 to 11 5 feet below the existing ground surface Because of the
limited space sloping of the sides of the trenches may not be possible Based
on the relatively deep excavations anticipated at localized areas shoring of the
sides of the trenches will be necessary to facilitate the proposed trench
excavations and to protect personnel working in the trench
As previously mentioned medium hard volcanic tuff was encountered in our
borings Therefore we believe that it is not practical to use interlocking sheet piles
to shore the trench excavations Trench shoring or other methods may be
required The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with all
applicable safety requirements It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical

used The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole

Even if good construction procedures are followed some movement of the shoring
system and the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses
during excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to
estimate accurately the magnitude of ground movement The magnitude of ground
movements also depends greatly upon workmanship such as how quickly and
tightly the shoring supports are installed the subsoil conditions the size of the
excavation and the rate of excavation Therefore it is important to realize that the
excavation shoring should be installed properly and as early as practical and if
necessary the adjacent ground should be continuously monitored for cracks dips
and or other indications of movements with instruments such as inclinometers
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great effect upon the load
supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore pipe bedding is an important design
consideration In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or similar free draining material is
recommended under the pipes and manhole structures located over the anticipated
subsurface soil conditions The granular bedding should extend beyond the sides
of the pipe a minimum width of one fourth 1 4 the outside pipe diameter A typical
section of the trench detail is presented on Plate 3 Before the placement of
bedding material the excavated trench bottom should be observed by a
representative from Geolabs to confirm whether firm materials are exposed at the
bottom of the trench or if the bedding should be extended deeper as recommended
above
Backfill
In general trench backfill should be performed in accordance with the Standard
Specifications for Public Works Construction Department of Public Works City and
County of Honolulu September 1986 Free draining granular materials such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for the trench
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around
the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of
damaging the pipes Compaction by water ponding or jetting should not be allowed
for this project Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and
damage to the pipes

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site soils with particles
less than 6 inches in maximum dimension or select fill material As previously
mentioned volcanic tuff was encountered in our borings During trench excavation
in the volcanic tuff boulder size fragments may be generated Therefore boulders
size fragments generated during the trench excavations should not be used for
trench backfill
The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in maximum 8 inch horizontal loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential
for future ground subsidence Where trenches are located below pavement areas
the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
Trenchless Methods
Two categories of trenchless methods are available rehabilitation and conventional
trenchless Trenchless rehabilitation consists of reusing the existing pipe or pipe
alignment and includes pipe bursting slip lining and Cured In Place Pipe CIPP methods
Conventional trenchless methods consist of installing a replacement pipe generally parallel
to the existing pipe that includes horizontal directional drilling and microtunneling
Pipe Bursting
Pipe bursting consists of winching a pneumatic powered bursting tool through an
old section of pipe breaking and cracking the old pipe as it progresses A new pipe
section is pulled into place behind the bursting tool replacing the old pipe section
High Density Polyethylene HDPE pipe is typically used with pipe bursting
Conventional open trench method was used for the installation of the existing
6 inch sewer line The open trench method consists of excavating a trench wide
enough for the sewer line installation placing the sewer pipe and backfilling the
open trench with backfill material During pipe bursting the soil surrounding the
bursting tool will be compressed In a backfilled trench compaction of the backfill
material will generally occur
Damage to existing utilities in close proximity of the bursting tool may occur for
existing utilities generally within 3 feet of the bursting tool In areas where the
existing utilities are close to the sewer line the utility crossings may be exposed
and the crossing utility moved further away from the bursting tool during the pipe
installation or the utility crossing may be supported in place to reduce the potential
for damage

For conditions where the bursting tool is close to the existing ground surface
surface heave may occur We anticipate that a 10 inch HDPE pipe will be required
to obtain an inside diameter of at least 8 inches For this HDPE pipe size
approximately 4 feet of ground cover should be provided to reduce the potential for
ground heave Based on the topographic survey map provided the sewer line has
less than 4 feet of cover along the sidewalk area on Auwaiolimu Street and along
the easement Pipe bursting may be used in areas where the existing sewer line
has less than 4 feet of ground cover provided that pavements and or driveways are
not present such as the easement area
Entry and exit pits will be required to accommodate the bursting tool and the
replacement pipe and excavation pits will be required to connect the laterals with
the main sewer line The excavation pits may encounter fill material cinder sand
residual soils and the medium hard volcanic tuff formation Discussions regarding
excavation method excavation support pipe bedding and backfill are presented in
the Open Trench Method section of this report Electrofusion methods may be
used to connect the HDPE pipes at the joints Since the sewer line will probably
need to keep active a bypassing system will probably be required
In summary we believe that pipe bursting is a viable option for the proposed sewer
line rehabilitation provided that the concerns above are addressed
Slip Lining
Slip lining consists of inserting a new pipe of smaller diameter into the deteriorated
host pipe The new pipe is inserted either by pushing or pulling the pipe into the
deteriorated pipe The annular space between the existing pipe and the new pipe
is filled with grout
Excavation pits may be required to connect laterals with the main sewer line
Based on our exploration the excavation pits may encounter fill material cinder
sand residual soils and the medium hard volcanic tuff formation Discussions
regarding excavation method excavation support pipe bedding and backfill are
presented in the Open Trench Method section of this report
We believe that slip lining method may be a viable option provided that the host
pipe does not have joint excessive settlements severe misalignment and large
deformations

Horizontal Directional Drilling
This trenchless steerable installation method uses a multi axis drilling machine to
bore a small pilot hole The pilot hole is then reamed to a larger size to
accommodate the new pipe The new pipe is pulled back into the reamed hole by
the drilling machine
It is generally more difficult to maintain an accurate alignment with horizontal
directional drilling as compared to pipe bursting Since the sewer line is a gravity
type sewer line accurate alignment and grade are critical If the existing sewer line
is planned to be used as a pilot hole deviation in grade of the new sewer line may
occur The diameter of the drilling rods are much smaller than the diameter of the
existing pipe and therefore it may be difficult to maintain the center of the drill rods
at the center of the existing sewer line
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions along Auwaiolimu Street
consisted of fill material cinder sand and residual soils underlain by medium hard
volcanic tuff formation The volcanic tuff encountered in our borings along
Auwaiolimu Street was friable broke down to gravel and sand The friable
volcanic tuff encountered contain high gravel content about 50 percent or more
and may be problematic and is expected to cause difficulty in drilling and steering
the pilot hole as well as hole stability problems
Another consideration in the above method is that an entry pit of significant size will
be required to accommodate the drilling machine required for the size of pipe being
considered or large excavation pits will be required to tie in the installed sewer line
to the manhole structure
Because of the above mentioned reasons we believe that installation of the new
sewer line using horizontal directional drilling may not be a viable option

Microtunneling
Microtunneling is an installation method where a pipe is jacked behind a remotely
controlled steerable laser guided tunnel boring machine Entry and exit pits will be
required These shallow pits are generally rectangular in shape to accommodate
the length about three to four tunnel diameters and width about one tunnel
diameter of the tunnel boring machine the jacking frame reaction block and pipe
sections to be jacked into place During the microtunneling process the excavated
material is removed by using a mixture of water and slurry pumped through a pipe
for disposal balanced screw auger or a screw conveyor system
Microtunneling is performed with specialized equipment that is very costly and is
generally limited to the smallest pipe size of 18 inches
In microtunneling selecting the right machine cutter head for the ground condition
is critical Different types of cutter heads are used for various types of soil rock
Based on our field exploration fill material cinder sand and residual soils
underlain by medium hard volcanic tuff formation The volcanic tuff encountered
in our borings along Auwaiolimu Street was friable broke down to gravel and
sand We anticipate that the cutter head will bore through different types of
soil rock in the same run creating a mixed face condition which may impede
tunneling Should slurry be used in the microtunneling work significant loss of
slurry may occur within the cinder deposit encountered at the site
Because of the above mentioned reasons we believe that installation of the new
sewer line by microtunneling may not be a viable option
