shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1

are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

new bike and pedestrian path along the shoreline from Lihi Park to Ahihi Point as shown

pedestrian path from the north end of Kealia Beach to Ahihi Point The project will also
include bridge improvements at Waikaea and Moikeha Canal Bridges new bridge
structures at Kapaa Stream Bridge and at Kumukumu Stream crossing new comfort
stations new pavements at Kealia Lookout Parking Area and site grading

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lava of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lava of
the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation
The majority of the Island of Kauai is covered by lava of the Waimea Canyon
Volcanic Series These lavas consist of four distinct formations
and Makaweli These formations are comprised of thin bedded a a and pahoehoe flows to
massive basalt flows that ponded in calderas and graben
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of
Kauai These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded
from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various locales Associated with the
vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at the vent

During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global
sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Kauai remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and
lower relative to the present sea level of Kauai
The basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lavas of the Koloa Volcanic Series are
generally characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt inter bedded with thin
clinker layers The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils which grade
to saprolite with depth In general saprolite is composed of mainly silty material and is
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock
formation with depth
Erosion of the upper Koloa and Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series has deposited
alluvial sediments along streams drainageways and low lying areas These sediments
are generally unconsolidated to moderately consolidated non calcareous soil deposits
Agricultural residential and commercial developments within the last century have
brought the project site to its present conditions
ANTICIPATED SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Based on our review of available subsurface information in the project area and
geologic maps we anticipate that the project site is generally underlain by unconsolidated
calcareous sediments including beach sand deposits recent alluvial deposits and residual
soil derived from in situ weathering of the basaltic lavas of the Koloa Volcanic Series
Deep deposits of loose soft reef detritus and lagoonal deposits are anticipated at the
Waikaea Moikeha and Kapaa Bridges Recent alluvial deposits that include gravel
cobbles and boulders are anticipated along the stream areas

Bridge Structure Foundations
New bridge structures are planned at Waikaea Moikeha and Kapaa Bridges The
new bridge structures at Waikaea and Moikeha Bridges will span over the existing bridge
structure
Based on available subsurface information for the State of Hawaii Department of
Transportation bridges on Kuhio Highway the subsoil conditions at the new bridges were
estimated The estimated subsoil conditions at Waikaea and Moikeha Bridges consist of
very loose soft reef detritus and lagoonal deposits to Elevation 100 feet Mean Sea Level
MSL underlain by stiff clays and dense sand At Kapaa Bridge the estimated subsoil
conditions generally consist of very loose soft reef detritus and lagoonal deposits to
Elevation 90 feet MSL underlain by stiff clays and dense sand to Elevation 120 feet
MSL followed by a basalt rock formation
Because of the anticipated deep deposit of loose soft subsoils we recommend that
a deep foundation system consisting of driven piles be used to support the proposed
bridge structures We recommend that 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete
piles bearing on or embedded within the stiff alluvial deposit or basalt formation be used to
support the proposed structures

Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic

the walls

Site Grading
Site grading along most of the alignment will involve relatively small cuts and fills
Thicker fills are anticipated at the Kumukumu Bridge structure comfort station near
Kealia Beach Park and Kealia Lookout Parking Area
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris deleterious materials and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site or stockpiled in a
designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft soils may be used as
fill provided that it meets the requirements for fill material
Prior to filling the existing ground should be scarified to a depth of 10 inches and
moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Where shrinkage cracks are observed after
compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close
cracks or prepared again as recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of
the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill
The excavated soft soils may be used as fill provided that it meets the requirements for fill
material Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1557
In general the excavated residual soils beach sand and alluvial soils may be used
as a source of general fill materials provided they are properly moisture conditioned to
about 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent of the maximum dry density established by ASTM Test Designation D 1557

Some amounts of cobbles and boulders will be generated from the cuts into the residual
and alluvial soils
Select granular fill and imported fill should consist of non expansive granular
material such as crushed coral basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded
from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of
less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883
Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs Inc for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use
Fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
Base course and select borrow subbase materials should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test
procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Fill slopes should be designed with a maximum inclination of 2H 1V For fill slopes
comprised of beach sand a maximum inclination of 3H 1V should be used For
preliminary design purposes cut slopes in stiff residual and alluvial soils may be designed
with inclinations of up to 2H 1V
Excavation into the residual soils beach sand and alluvial soils may be achieved
by conventional excavation equipment however remnant boulders should be expected in
the residual and alluvial formations
