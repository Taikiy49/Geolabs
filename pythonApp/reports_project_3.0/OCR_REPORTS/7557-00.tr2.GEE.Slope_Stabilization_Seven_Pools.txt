SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered fill materials and saprolite soils over 
weathered basalt formation. In general, the fill materials consisted of soft to stiff clayey soils 
extending to about 15 feet below the pavement surface. The fill materials were underlain by 
hard silty and clayey saprolitic soils extending to a depth of about 30 feet below the 
pavement surface. Beneath the saprolite soils, medium hard to hard basalt formation was 
encountered and extended to the maximum depth drilled of about 42 feet below the 
pavement surface. The basalt was interbedded with layers of clinkers. 
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration; however, the subsurface soils had relatively high moisture contents likely due 
to the high rainfall environment. It should be noted that groundwater levels can significantly 
vary depending on rainfall, temperature, surface runoff, groundwater withdrawal, and other 
factors. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site and our slope stability 
evaluation, we believe that the critical slope failure wedge may be likely located through the 
fill materials overlying the saprolite soils and weathered basalt formation. Based on the slope 
geometry and the anticipated subsurface profile at the site, we recommend considering the 
following repair alternatives for stabilizing the slope. 
Tieback Anchor Retaining Wall 
Soil Nail Retaining Wall 
Anchored Wire Mesh System 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific design 
recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Hana Highway Emergency Landslide Repair project, located 
in Hana near the Pools of 'Ohe'o (Seven Pools) on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the proposed project. These recommendations are intended for the design of the ground 
anchor system only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to 
the limitations noted at the end of this report.  
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project is located at approximately 1,700 feet north of the Seven Pools National 
Park along Hana Highway. The highway is a winding, narrow, two-lane undivided road with 
very steep cut slopes on one side and steep fill and/or natural slope on the downhill side. 
The road is heavily used by residents and tourists. Based on the information provided, we 
understand a section of the roadway about 50 feet long had experienced slope instability. 
It is desired to stabilize the roadway section for public safety.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui (Tertiary 
Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene Epoch). 
The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain between these two volcanoes. The 
project site is located on the northeastern portion of the island. 
The project site is on the southeastern flank of the Haleakala Shield Volcano in the 
District of Hana. The East Maui Volcano is represented by the older rocks of Honomanu 
Volcanic Series, buried beneath younger volcanic deposits. This lower unit volcanic series 
(late Pliocene, early Pleistocene Epoch) consists of thinly-bedded tholeiitic basaltic 
pahoehoe and a’a lava flows. These lavas tend to be very permeable and carry basal 
water. The Honomanu Series is overlain by the Kula Volcanic Series (late Pleistocene 
Epoch), which is primarily composed of thick lava flows with appreciable embedded 
pyroclastic (ash and cinders) materials. Rocks of the Kula Series cover much of the 
present-day surface of the East Maui Volcano. 
A long period of deep erosion and sediment deposition occurred between the end 
of the Kula Volcanic Series and the beginning of the Hana Volcanic Series. The Hana 
Volcanic Series (Holocene Epoch) generally consists of the youngest lava flows and 
cinder cones presently encountered at the East Maui ground surface. The lavas from this 
volcanic series range in composition from ultra-basic olivine porphyries to non-porphyritic, 
alkalic basalts. Although the Hana lavas were generally more fluid and the lava flows 
tended to be thinner and less massive than the preceding Kula lavas, thick accumulation 
of lava did occur in the valleys and valley heads. 
The erosional process of volcanic islands is dominated by the detachment of 
weathered soil and rock materials from the interior steep mountain slopes, and the 
movement of the eroded materials down slope by gravity toward the lowland mountain 
perimeter areas. Once these eroded slope materials (colluvium) reach a valley stream, 
alluvial processes become dominant, and the sediments are further transported by 
flowing water and deposited as alluvium.

In general, mountain stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that 
the stream flows may transmit large volumes of water for very short duration. Because of 
this, the transport of sediment is intermittent, and the bulk of a stream's hydraulic load 
may consist of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fine soil 
particles. When the erosional base level of a land mass changes, through long-term 
changes in sea-level or the sinking/raising of the island mass, sedimentary deposits may 
either be reactivated by renewed incision and erosion or remain deposited in-place as 
unconsolidated alluvial and colluvial deposits. 
When unconsolidated sedimentary deposits are buried in-place for long periods of 
time, chemical processes begin to alter the materials, simultaneously causing additional 
breakdown and weathering of the materials. The continued upland erosion generates new 
eroded materials, which are transported downslope and may cover the older alluvial and 
colluvial soil deposits.  Depending on the erosional base level and the rate of sediment 
transport, these newer sediments may be generally transient in terms of geologic time. 
The project site is along Hana Highway traversing steep slopes on the sides of 
large drainage ravines. In-situ chemical weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of 
saprolitic soils. In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of 
the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. It should be noted that spheroidally weathered 
boulders are commonly encountered in saprolite. In recent times, grading for Hana 
Highway has altered the site to its present form.

2.3.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic 
activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island 
of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small 
that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of the 
earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) 
are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground 
shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii. The Island of 
Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); 
however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii. 
It should be noted that some earthquakes in Hawaii are caused by the burden of the 
island masses overlying the lithosphere, which causes the lithosphere to sag beneath 
the immense weight. The isostatic adjustment of the Pacific lithosphere beneath the 
islands’ overburden causes some moderate to strong intensity earthquakes. The 
1973 Honomu Earthquake (Magnitude 6.2) and the 2006 Kiholo Bay/Hawi 
earthquakes (Magnitude 6.7/6.0) were the result of lithosphere adjustment. Both of 
these earthquakes were widely felt throughout the Hawaiian Islands.  
It should be noted that the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes 
greater than M5+. In addition, other lithospheric origin earthquakes have occurred 
within the Island of Maui in the past, such as the Maui Earthquake of 1938 (~M6.5) 
centered north of the Island of Maui and the 1871 Lanai Earthquake (~M6.8).

In general, it is common that some large boulders or cobbles may be encountered 
in the fill materials and saprolite soils. Dense basalt rock would likely be encountered in 
the drilling depths. Therefore, hard drilling conditions should be anticipated by the 
contractor. To reduce the potential for caving-in of the drilled holes, temporary casing of 
the drilled holes may be required during the ground anchor system installation. 
Drilling fluids including mineral and polymer slurry should not be allowed for the 
ground anchor installation. If utilizing air pressure during the drilling, the contractor should 
be cautious when drilling is advanced into the loose soil materials to prevent undermining 
the roadway subgrade. Undermining the roadway subgrade will further cause ground 
settlement and loss of foundation support. 
The contractor should fully explore the project site and make his/her own 
interpretation. It is the contractor’s responsibility to bid the project accordingly with 
sufficient contingency taking into account potential caving-in, hard drilling, and difficult 
access conditions.

Tieback Anchor Retaining Walls 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site and our slope stability 
evaluation, we recommend constructing a new tieback anchor retaining wall to restrain 
the potential slope failure wedge and to support the highway traffic loads.

In general, a permanent tieback anchor system is constructed by drilling a 
borehole, installing a steel tendon or reinforcing bar into the borehole, and grouting the 
borehole. The basic components of a grouted permanent tieback anchor consist of the 
anchorage and the bonded and unbonded lengths of the steel tendon or reinforcing bar. 
In general, the unbonded length of the tieback anchor is the portion of the steel tendon 
(or reinforcing bar) that does not have direct contact with the grout, allowing the tendon 
(or reinforcing bar) to elongate elastically and transfer the resisting force from the bonded 
length to the retaining structure. 
The resistance of the tieback anchor should be developed from the stable ground 
condition beyond the slope critical failure wedge, typically referring as bonded zone. We 
envision that the permanent tieback anchors would derive support principally from the 
bond between the grout and the saprolitic soils anticipated at the project site. The bond 
stress, which is the pullout resistance per unit area of the grout/soil interface contact, is 
variable depending on the type of soil and grout, the overburden stress, and the 
construction procedures used in installing the permanent tieback anchors. An average 
bond stress of 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the saprolitic soils. 
The bond stress used in the design will need to be confirmed during construction by load 
testing. 
Based on our analysis, we believe that a 15-foot high tieback anchor retaining wall 
may be considered to restrain the potential slope failure wedge and to support the 
highway traffic loads. Based on the geometry of the slope and anticipated critical slope 
failure wedge, we envision the minimum unbonded length to be approximately 10 feet. 
The tieback anchors should have a minimum length of 20 feet with a minimum 
diameter of 6 inches and vertical and horizontal spacing of about 5 feet center-to-center. 
The following guidelines may be used in designing the permanent tieback system for this 
project. 
Due to the specialized nature of the permanent tieback anchor retaining wall 
construction, a Geolabs representative should be present to observe the geotechnical 
aspects of the tieback anchor retaining wall construction and testing of tieback anchors.

3.1.1 Construction Considerations 
The performance of the permanent tieback anchors depends significantly upon the 
contractor's method of construction and construction procedures. As a result of the 
potential variations in the subsurface materials encountered at the project site, we 
recommend that a Geolabs representative is present to observe the installation of 
the tieback anchors during construction. A specialty contractor experienced in the 
construction of permanent tieback anchors should perform construction of the 
tieback anchors. The following may have a significant impact on the effectiveness 
and cost of the tieback anchors. 
Drilling of the tieback anchor holes may encounter hard basalt rock formation, in 
addition to abundant hard cobbles and boulders.  Therefore, special drilling tools 
for drilling into and through the hard rock will be required.  Temporary casing of the 
drilled holes for the permanent tieback anchors will be required if caving-in 
conditions occur during drilling of the tieback anchor holes especially in the 
loose/soft pockets and cobble/boulder. 
Grouting of the tieback anchors has a major impact on the load carrying capacities 
of the tieback anchors. A neat-cement grout or sand-cement grout mix should be 
used. Placement of grout should be by tremie methods beginning from the bottom 
of the hole. The grout should be placed through the tremie pipe by pressure 
methods. The grouting operations should be conducted promptly (within 8 hours) 
after drilling to reduce the potential for significant caving-in of the sides of the drilled 
holes. 
Due to the nature of the basalt rock formation, it is common that grout loss from 
the drilled hole exceeds a few times the volume of the annular space between the 
drilled hole and tieback. The contractor should anticipate the potential grout loss 
and incorporate the necessary costs into the unit prices during the bidding process.

3.2 
Soil Nail Retaining Wall 
As an alternative, a soil nail retaining wall system may be used to repair the slope 
failure and to support the highway traffic loads. The soil nail retaining wall system would 
save construction cost and reduce construction duration, versus the permanent tieback 
anchor retaining wall system. The soil nail retaining wall could be constructed in top-down 
technique, serving both as temporary shoring of the excavation and the permanent 
retaining wall. 
A soil nail retaining wall system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars 
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize an excavation or slope. A shotcrete facing is 
generally applied to the face of the excavation providing the appearance of a conventional 
concrete retaining wall. To provide the natural rock appearance, the soil nail installed may 
be capped with sculpture shotcrete. The reinforcing bars provide both additional tensile 
and shear strengths to the soil and reinforce the sidewall of the excavation. The reinforced 
soil mass behaves in a manner similar to a gravity retaining wall but lacks a footing. In 
general, the face of the soil nail retaining wall system is slightly battered at a slope 
inclination of about one horizontal to twelve vertical (1H:12V).

3.2.2 Design Considerations 
For corrosion protection, the soil nails will require some form of corrosion 
protection, such as a fully encapsulated double corrosion protection system, due 
to coastal front environment. 
Due to the nature of the basalt rock formation, similar to the tieback anchor 
retaining wall system, grout loss from the drilled hole may exceed a few times the 
volume of the annular space between the drilled hole and soil nail. The contractor 
should anticipate the potential grout loss and incorporate the necessary costs into 
the unit prices during the bidding process. 
3.2.3 Soil Nail Testing 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated behind the soil nail retaining walls, 
we recommend performing a minimum of two verification tests on the first and 
second rows of the production soil nails at selected locations. In addition, proof 
tests should be performed on at least 10 percent of the production soil nails during 
construction to confirm the bond stresses used in the design. 
The verification tests should be loaded gradually to 150 percent of the design load 
for the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of about 200 percent 
of the design load. In addition, the load at every loading increment should be held 
for at least 10 minutes, and creep tests should be held for at least 60 minutes. The 
proof tests should consist of subjecting the soil nail to at least 150 percent of the 
design loads and the load should be held for at least 10 minutes (until stable). The 
test nails may be incorporated into the permanent soil nail retaining wall provided 
they satisfy the test criteria. Pullout tests and creep tests on the soil nails are 
integral components of the design for the soil nail retaining wall system. Therefore, 
we recommend conducting pullout tests and creep tests in the presence of a 
Geolabs representative.

3.3 
Anchor Wired Mesh System 
One of the considerations is to accommodate the highway traffic during the 
construction. The existing highway near the collapsed slope is narrow. To keep the 
highway open to traffic, majority of the construction equipment will have to situate on the 
slope or guided suspension cable. To meet the above objective, we recommend installing 
an anchored wire mesh system, to mitigate the slope failure. 
Anchored wire mesh system is a slope treatment that involves the installation of 
steel wire mesh panels anchored onto the slope face with soil/rock nails in an effort to 
prevent the soils and rocks from moving/falling under the highway.  The primary intent of 
this stabilization system is to closely pin the wire mesh to the slope face as a retention 
system. 
The slope to be anchored with steel wire mesh must be trimmed of vegetation and 
scaled of loose rocks prior to the installation of the anchored wire mesh system. The 
anchored wire mesh system and all associated hardware, such as the high strength steel 
wire mesh, compression claws, and spike plates should be powder coated with a black 
pigment or other color as directed by the County.  Boundary wire ropes should be 
polyvinyl chloride (PVC) coated in black unless directed otherwise by the County.  All 
other exposed steel elements should be painted with a black colored rubberized paint. 
The following provides a brief outline of the anchored wire mesh installation procedures. 
1. 
Trim slope face of vegetation, loose materials, and protruding rock outcrops. 
2. 
Stakeout grouted soil/rock anchors in general accordance with the project 
spacing requirements considering the low spots. 
3. 
Excavate the dell for pre-tensioning of the anchored wire mesh before 
drilling the grouted soil/rock anchors.

Based on our evaluation of the slope, we recommend that the soil/rock anchor be 
a minimum of 20 feet long with a spacing of 5 feet center-to-center in a staggered pattern. 
The drilled hole for the soil/rock anchor should be a minimum of 6 inches in diameter. The 
grout should have a minimum 28-day unconfined compressive strength of 4,000 psi or 
greater in 28 days. The grouted soil/rock anchors should have an ultimate pullout capacity 
of 30 kips. 
We recommend performing the proof tests on at least 10 percent of the grouted 
anchors during construction to confirm the ultimate pullout capacity used in the design. 
The proof tests should consist of subjecting the grouted anchor to the design ultimate 
pullout capacity of 30 kips and the load should be held for at least 10 minutes (until stable). 
The test anchors may be incorporated into the permanent grouted anchors supporting the 
anchored wire mesh system, provided they satisfy the test criteria. Pullout tests on the 
grouted anchors are integral components of the design for the anchored wire mesh 
system. Therefore, we recommend conducting pullout tests in the presence of a Geolabs 
representative.

the drilled hole exceeds a few times the volume of the annular space between the drilled 
hole and soil/rock anchor due to the nature of the rock formation. Therefore, the contractor 
should include sufficient contingency in his/her bid to account for additional grout in the 
event cavities/voids are encountered during soil/rock anchor installation. The contractor 
should employ means and methods reducing excessive grout loss, such as grout sock, 
grout admixture, etc.

The soil/rock anchors should be set in low spots, where possible, in order to pull

Slope scaling is the manual sweep or removal of loose surface rocks in addition to 
extensive vegetation removal, including in-situ fractured rock outcroppings and loose 
boulders, from the hill slope surfaces located within the identified problem area. Slope 
scaling typically is accomplished by manual labor consisting of specialized slope scaling 
teams using pry bars to remove precarious loose rock materials while suspended on the 
slope face. The hill slope typically is scaled from top to bottom such that the generated 
debris is progressively moved toward the bottom of the slope for collection and proper 
disposal. The hill slope may be scaled by several passes through the area to scale at 
progressively greater detail starting with larger blocks of unstable rock and ending with 
sweeps for the remaining “hung-up” and smaller size rock materials. 
The contractor’s proven prior successful experience with slope scaling techniques 
is absolutely required. Scaling teams should be certified in high angle rescue techniques 
because the workers likely will be using rappel equipment to work on the steep slopes. 
The scaling teams also require team foremen on the ground to watch for hazards and 
direct the effective use of manpower in a safe and orderly manner. Temporary roadway 
protection may be necessary using devices such as steel roadway plates, jersey barriers, 
etc.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
two exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 
42.7 and 6.5 feet below the existing ground surface, respectively. The borings were drilled 
with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch solid-stem augers and PQ coring tools. 
The borings were drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing 
in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard 
Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification 
System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil 
classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log 
Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on 
the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard 
Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond 
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow 
bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is 
mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test results are 
provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B-2. 
Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) were 
performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the 
materials. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressures were used as the 
applied confining pressures for the relatively “undisturbed” soil samples. The test results 
and stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-3 and B-4.

Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Tests 
In order to evaluate the consistency or relative density of the near-surface soils 
and depict the top rock surface contour, Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) tests were 
conducted at nine locations, designated as P-1 through P-9, extending to depths of about 
1 to 12 feet below the existing ground surface along the edge of Hana Highway. The 
approximate DCP locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
A portable Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) was used for probing by driving an 
8-mm diameter 60° steel cone tip with an 8-kg hammer falling 575-mm in vertical height. 
The blow counts were recorded for approximately every 75-mm (3-inch) interval of 
penetration and converted to standard penetration resistance (SPT, ASTM D1586) using 
correlations between Average Penetration (AP) and SPT (developed by Sowers and 
Hedges and later Livneh and Ishai). Both AP and SPT blow counts are shown on the Logs 
of DCP at the appropriate depths, presented on the Logs of DCP, Plates C-1.1 through 
C-9.1. 
The procedures used in our DCP tests are in general accordance with 
ASTM D6951, Standard Test Method for Use of the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer in 
Shallow Pavement Applications. Correlations between DCP penetration and CBR are 
based on the equation [CBR = 292 / (DCP1.12)] developed by the U.S. Army Corps of 
Engineers and recommended in ASTM D6951. Correlations between DCP penetration 
and SPT blow count was derived from the publication entitled “Application of Dynamic 
Cone Penetrometer (DCP) for determination of the engineering parameters of sandy 
soils” prepared by S.D. Mohammadi, et al, and is summarized in the table below.
