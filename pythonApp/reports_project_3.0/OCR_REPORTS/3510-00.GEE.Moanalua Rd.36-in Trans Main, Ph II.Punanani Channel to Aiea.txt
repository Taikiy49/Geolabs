REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which erosion incised
deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows
followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu
Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are then transported
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3510 00 Page 6
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream
flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this transport
of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a
poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional
base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport the newer sediments are
generally transient in terms of geologic time In addition their consistency and density
are generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits

Bridge Crossing at Waimalu Stream
A shallow foundation system for support of the bridge crossing is not suitable for
the proposed bridge structure at Waimalu Stream due to the proximity of the bridge
supports to the edge of the existing concrete wall and presence of soft loose alluvial soils
Therefore we recommend a foundation system consisting of 24 inch diameter cast in
place drilled shafts be used for support of the proposed water main pipeline at the
Waimalu Stream crossing

The cast in place drilled shafts would derive vertical support primarily from
adhesion between the drilled shaft and the surrounding soils The end bearing capacity
of the drilled shafts has been discounted due to the potential for caving in of the
soft loose soils within the planned depths of the drilled shafts Based on the structural
loads provided and the subsurface soil conditions encountered at the site the following
are our preliminary recommendations for the drilled shaft foundation for the bridge
crossing at Waimalu Stream
Shaft Diameter 24 inches
Allowable Compression Load Capacity 60 kips
Bottom of Footing Elevation 12 feet MSL
Estimated Shaft Length 45 feet
Estimated Tip Elevation 33 feet MSL
The allowable compression load capacity recommended above is for dead plus
live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by
wind or seismic forces Drilled shafts should have a minimum center to center spacing of
three times the diameter to avoid capacity reduction due to group action
Lateral loads imposed on the foundations can be resisted by a combination of the
following 1 resistance of the drilled shaft soil system to deflect 2 passive resistance
on the vertical faces of drilled shaft footings where applicable and 3 side shear
between the footings and soils Passive resistance on the footings may be calculated
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f
for footings embedded in the near surface in situ stiff soils For side shear between the
sides of the footings and adjacent soils a shear value of 150 p s f may be used Lateral
load resistance of a drilled shaft is a function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the
stiffness of the drilled shaft allowable deflection at the top of drilled shaft and induced
moment in the shaft We have estimated the lateral load capacity of the 24 inch diameter

Excavation Method
In general the method and equipment to be used for excavation should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
As previously indicated a large portion of the water main alignment eastern
portion is within the stiff residual soils The stiff residual soils may grade to basalt
rock formation with increasing depth Therefore some portions of the eastern
water main alignment consisting of deeper excavations may encounter rocky
materials The contractor should be made aware of this potential condition The
excavated soils from the trench excavation should be stockpiled no closer than a
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the
excavation to reduce the potential for ground movement

Dewatering
We anticipate that the trenches will generally be about 10 feet deep along the
proposed water main alignment and may extend up to approximately 20 to 28 feet deep
where the water main crosses Punanani Channel and Kaonohi Stream Groundwater
was encountered within the anticipated excavation depths in our borings drilled near the
streams Therefore dewatering of the proposed trenches will be necessary to facilitate
the proposed construction in these areas
The dewatering operation should be conducted in such a manner that the
dewatering will not cause areal ground subsidence which may cause potential damage to
the existing structures Therefore consideration should be given to a dewatering system
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3510 00 Page 17
that includes a deep cut off wall to reduce the volume of water to be removed within the
excavation and to reduce the areal extent of groundwater drawdown outside of the trench
excavation
Since the excavation dewatering will involve discharge of groundwater from the
dewatering operation into the adjacent drainage system a National Pollutant Discharge
Elimination System NPDES permit will be necessary The contractor should consult
their independent consultant or the State of Hawaii Department of Health for the latest
regulations and information pertaining to the NPDES permit application
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and he she
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on the conditions encountered The dewatering method selected
should have minimal impact on the existing groundwater levels surrounding the proposed
excavation Dewatering operations should be performed such that only clear water is
pumped Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in a blowing or
heaving of the excavation bottom or sides considering the soft loose soils encountered
near the streams
In addition special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility
trenches connected to the excavations If this occurs the granular bedding and or backfill
in the utility trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater
drawdown resulting in settlements and potential damage to utility lines and or other
existing adjacent structures

We believe that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents should be
written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater table

2 The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide safe

3 The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modifications

Pipe Bedding

load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important design
consideration For pipes bearing on the stiff and medium dense to dense soils a

placement of the granular bedding material the bottom of the trench should be firm and
unyielding For pipes with inverts in the soft loose soils an additional stabilization layer
consisting of at least 18 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
wrapped in a filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be provided below the

Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Standard
Specifications for Public Works Construction September 1986 The first zone of backfill
extending from the bedding material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes
should consist of select free draining granular material to reduce the compaction effort
required and resulting stresses on the pipe The granular material should be well graded
less than 1 inch in maximum size and should have less than 10 percent passing the
No 200 sieve Compaction by water ponding or jetting may be allowed provided the
granular material is free draining such as No 3B Fine gravel
The trench backfill from 12 inches above the top of the pipes to within 2 feet below
the finished subgrade may consist of the approved excavated on site soils It should be
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3510 00 Page 20
noted that the excavated on site soils in some areas may be wet therefore it may be
necessary to aerate the wet excavated soils prior to placement to permit compaction of
the backfill material to the specified requirements In addition the soft loose soils
encountered in our borings near the streams should not be used as trench backfill
In general the backfill should be placed in thin lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness with each lift mechanically compacted to a minimum 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 The trench backfill from 2 feet below the finished
subgrade elevation up to the subgrade elevation should consist of select borrow
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction A typical section of the
trench is presented on Plate 6
Trenches should be kept free of water during backfilling operations Should
backfilling below the groundwater level be necessary free draining granular material
such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be utilized to reduce
the potential of future settlement of the trench backfill Where the free draining granular
materials are used a filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be used to
wrap the free draining materials
Thrust Blocks and Earth Pressure on Pipes
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide
resistance to the thrust forces due to the pressurized pipes The thrust force acting on
the pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of the thrust
block Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f above the groundwater
table and 150 p c f below the groundwater for thrust blocks embedded in the in situ soil
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3510 00 Page 21
To estimate the frictional resistance between the thrust block and surrounding soils a
coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used These values assume that the soil around the
block is well compacted and the top of the thrust block is embedded a minimum of 3 feet
below the ground surface
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the
pipes the unit weight of the backfill material and the frictional resistance between the
backfill material and the trench walls For a preliminary estimate of the vertical loads on
the buried water main pipes an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot p c f
for the backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0 3 between the backfill and trench
sidewalls may be used Earth pressure forces upon the pipe increase rapidly with
increased width of the trench Therefore the width of the water line trench should be kept
to a minimum Traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be considered where the
proposed water line alignment is located within traveled ways
Settlements
Primary settlement of the new sewer line construction is normally caused by the
difference in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier
compacted backfill material placed over the water pipes In addition dewatering will also
cause a net increase in earth stresses on the underlying soils below the pipe invert The
net increase in loading will cause settlement of the underlying subsoils below the pipe
invert Based on the results of our preliminary exploration and engineering analyses
primary settlement on the order of 1 to 1 inches may be anticipated for this project
The above settlement estimate is based on the results of our exploration the
geological information in the area and our engineering analyses Proper construction
procedures and good workmanship should be engaged during construction to reduce
ground disturbance and potential for ground settlements

APPENDIX B
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the near surface soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits Graphic presentation of the
test results is provided on Plate B 1
One sieve analysis test ASTM D 422 was performed on a selected sample to
evaluate the grain size distribution of the soil encountered Results of the sieve analysis
test are summarized on Plate B 2
One unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression test ASTM D 2850 was
performed on a selected sample of the soil to evaluate its undrained shear strength
characteristics Results of the test are presented on Plate B 3
Three direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate their drained shear strength characteristics Results of the tests are
presented on Plates B 4 through B 6
Three sets of Chloride and Sulfate tests were performed by Environmental
Laboratory of the Pacific on selected samples of the soils to evaluate their corrosion
characteristics of the soils Results of the tests are summarized on Plate B 7
