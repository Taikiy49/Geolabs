SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration indicates that the existing pavement structure along 
Lilipuna Road generally consisted of an AC surface course with multiple overlays over a 
relatively thin layer of base course/subbase materials underlain by in-situ subgrade soils.  
The base/subbase materials encountered consisted of sandy gravel and are generally 
dense in relative density. It should be noted that the base course encountered at the 
majority of the boring/coring locations is relatively thin, on the order of less than about 
4 inches in most cases. The subgrade materials encountered below the pavement 
section generally consist of stiff silty clays/clayey silts with moisture contents in excess 
of 40 percent. 
Our visual pavement condition survey indicates that a substantial portion of 
Lilipuna Road exhibits distressed pavements with medium to high severity.  Ideally, we 
recommend reconstructing the entire Lilipuna Road with a new pavement section.  If 
budgetary cost is a consideration, we recommend reconstructing only pavements with 
distresses of medium to high severity with a new pavement section consisting of 
2 inches of AC on 4 inches of ACB.  After removal of the existing asphaltic concrete 
layer to accommodate the new pavement structural section, the exposed base materials 
or asphaltic concrete remnants should be proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton (static 
weight) drum roller for a minimum of eight passes to compact the exposed base 
materials. If broken up asphaltic concrete is exposed after the milling process, the 
broken up asphaltic concrete should be removed to expose the underlying intact 
asphaltic concrete or aggregate base materials before proof-rolling the AC and/or base 
materials. 
If soft soils are exposed after the milling process, we recommend providing a 
stabilization layer in lieu of proof-rolling the soft subgrade, which may induce pumping 
condition.  The stabilization layer should consist of 8 inches of aggregate base course 
over a layer of geogrid (such as Tensar Triax TX 160 or equivalent) underlain by a 
woven geotextile fabric (such as Mirafi HP370 or equivalent). Distressed pavement 
areas with low severity may be rehabilitated by cold-planing 2.0 inches of the existing 
AC and resurfacing with 2.0 inches of AC overlay.  Due to the poor bonding of the 
existing multiple AC overlays, a contingency fund should be allocated in the 
construction budget for extending the cold-planing thickness to the underlying intact 
asphaltic concrete.

Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering analyses 
performed for the Rehabilitation of City Streets, Unit 69 Lilipuna Road project in the 
Kaneohe area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our 
analyses for the roadway rehabilitation project. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The Rehabilitation of City Streets, Unit 69 project is part of an overall Pavement 
Survey Project covering various locations across the City and County of Honolulu. 
Generally, the Unit 69 project will involve rehabilitation of approximately 12,000 lineal 
feet of Lilipuna Road from Kamehameha Highway to Kamehameha Highway in the 
Kaneohe area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The roadway alignment of the pavement 
rehabilitation project is shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The existing Lilipuna Road is a two-way street with a single travel lane in each 
direction, except towards Kamehameha Highway where the roadway widens to 
accommodate parking lane, additional travel lane, and/or turn lanes.  Based on our field 
observations, it appears that the roadway has received multiple AC overlays in the past.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu, encompassing approximately 604 square miles of land area, 
may be divided into four basic geographic provinces.  The provinces include the 
Wai’anae Mountains, Ko’olau Mountains, Leilehua Plateau, and Coastal Plains.  The 
Island of Oahu was formed by the merging of basaltic lavas erupted from the 
two prominent volcanoes, the Wai’anae and Ko’olau Volcanoes. Of the two, the 
Wai’anae Volcano is the older having breached the ocean surface nearly 4 million years 
ago.  The Ko’olau Volcano rose above the sea level approximately 2.7 million years ago 
making it the younger of the two volcanoes. 
In general, the two volcanoes were built by the long-term eruption of thinly 
bedded a’a and pahoehoe lava flows, which erupted from elongated and linear trending 
volcanic vents associated with the prominent rift zones of each of the volcanoes.  The 
Ko’olau lavas banked against the lava flows of the Wai’anae Volcano to form the 
Leilehua Plateau of the central interior portion of Oahu.  The Wai’anae Mountains, 
which form the western third of the Island of Oahu, contain the oldest basalt rock 
formations on the Island of Oahu.  The Ko’olau Mountains form the eastern two-thirds of 
the island and include the rock layers exposed along the Ko’olau Pali extending from 
Waimanalo to Kahuku. 
The project area is located on the southern flank of the Koolau shield and 
composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of the lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series.  
These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt with 
interbedded thin clinker layers.  In-situ chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows has 
occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years.  The weathering process has formed a mantle of 
residual soils which grade to saprolite with depth.  In general, saprolite is composed mainly 
of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks.  The saprolite 
grades to basaltic rock formation with depth.

2.2 
Visual Pavement Condition Survey 
A pavement condition survey of the project area was performed on July 12, 2012. 
During the field survey, our engineer walked on the sides of the street, recorded 
approximate pavement distress locations, and determined pavement distress types 
through visual classification. 
The visual classification was performed in general accordance with the definitions 
described in the “Asphalt Distress Manual” dated June 1997 prepared by the Maintenance 
and Preservation Division (FL-P) of the Facilities Technology Laboratory (FL), U.S. Army 
Construction Engineering Research Laboratories (USACERL). Some of the pavement 
distress definitions are presented in the table below. 
ASPHALT PAVEMENT DISTRESSES  
Alligator Cracking 
(Fatigue) 
Alligator cracks are a series of interconnecting cracks caused by 
fatigue failure of the asphaltic concrete surface under repeated 
traffic loading. 
Block Cracking 
Block cracks are inter-connected cracks that divide the pavement 
into approximately rectangular pieces. 
Depression 
Depressions are localized pavement surface areas with elevations 
slightly lower than those of the surrounding pavement. 
Edge Cracking 
Edge cracks are cracks parallel to and usually within 1 to 1.5 feet of 
the outer edge of the pavement. 
Longitudinal 
Cracking 
Longitudinal cracks are cracks parallel to the pavement’s centerline 
or laydown direction. 
Transverse 
Cracking 
Transverse cracks are cracks extending across the pavement at 
approximately right angles to the pavement centerline or direction of 
laydown. 
Patch 
Patch is an area of pavement that has been replaced with new 
material to repair the existing pavement. 
Pothole 
Potholes are small bowl-shaped (usually less than 30 inches in 
diameter) depressions in the pavement surface.

stabilization layer in lieu of proof-rolling the soft subgrade, which may induce pumping 
condition.  The stabilization layer should consist of 8 inches of aggregate base course 
over a layer of geogrid (such as Tensar Triax TX 160 or equivalent) underlain by a 
woven geotextile fabric (such as Mirafi HP370 or equivalent) as shown on Plate 5. The 
aggregate base course should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T-180 
(ASTM D 1557). Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density.

and resurfacing with 2.0 inches of AC overlay. It should be noted that there may be 
localized pavement areas with medium to high severity that will require spot pavement 
reconstruction. Due to the poor bonding of the existing multiple AC overlays, a 
contingency fund should be allocated in the construction budget for extending the 
cold-planing thickness to the underlying intact asphaltic concrete.  
In general, the contractor should vacuum-sweep the pavement areas 
immediately after cold-planing to remove gravel-sized materials and dust that was not 
picked up by the cold-planing operation. It should be noted that the vacuum-sweep 
should not preclude the dust control operation during cold-planing. In general, the 
contractor should begin paving no later than three working days after cold-planing 
begins. Finally, vacuum sweeping of cold-planed surfaces should also be performed 
immediately prior to paving. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and 
scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar 
to those encountered in our field exploration. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D 2041. Asphalt concrete (AC) 
material should be constructed in accordance with Section 401 Asphalt Concrete 
Pavement of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road, Bridge, and Public Works 
Construction and its amendments. 
It should be noted that additional areas requiring reconstruction with new 
pavement section may develop between the time the construction plans are prepared 
and the actual commencement of the project construction. Therefore, the road should 
be re-surveyed to evaluate whether new distressed areas have developed that may

3.5 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

earthwork recommendations provided herein. If this review is not made, Geolabs cannot 
be responsible for misinterpretation of the recommendations presented. 
3.7 
Construction Monitoring 
Geolabs should be retained to provide geotechnical engineering services during 
construction. The critical items of construction monitoring include observation of the 
excavation of the pavement reconstruction areas and subgrade preparation. This is to 
observe compliance with the design concepts, specifications, or recommendations and 
to expedite suggestions for design changes that may be required in the event that 
subsurface conditions differ from those anticipated at the time this report was prepared. 
The recommendations provided herein are contingent upon such observations. If the 
actual exposed subsurface conditions encountered during construction are different 
from those assumed or considered in this report, then appropriate modifications to the 
design should be made.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway rehabilitation by drilling 
and sampling ten borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 10, extending to depths 
ranging from about 5.5 to 11.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. In addition, the 
boring exploration was supplemented by coring the existing pavement section to depths of 
about 1 to 1.5 feet below the existing pavement surface at 18 selected locations, identified 
as C-1 through C-20 (excluding C-4 and C-6).  The approximate boring and coring 
locations for this project are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.12. The borings 
were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and 
coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Rock 
formations were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as 
shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. The Rock Description System is based on 
the publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in 
Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).  Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-28. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-2 through B-6.

characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-7 and B-8.
