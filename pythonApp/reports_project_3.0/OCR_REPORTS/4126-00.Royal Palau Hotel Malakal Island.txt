GEOLOGY OF KOROR AND MALAKAL ISLANDS
The Palau Islands form a curved chain of islands of approximately 100 miles in
length extending from north to southwest The Palau Islands are the exposed crest of the
Palau Ridge expressed as a series of long arcuate submarine ridges separating the
Philippine Sea and the Pacific Ocean There are about 350 larger islands that comprise
the Palau Island chain The Palau barrier fringing reef encloses most of the Palau Islands
forming a broad shallow water lagoon occasionally separated by deep water passages
Babeldaob Island is the largest island of the Palau chain and is located to the north of
Koror Island A floating bridge structure connects the two islands across a deep water
passage
In general the islands of Koror and Malakal in the Republic of Palau are composed
of the accumulation of Tertiary Epoch volcanic materials and the growth of organic
limestone The geomorphology of the islands is controlled by many factors including
faulting repeated historic land submergence and emergence episodes as a result of sea
level fluctuation submarine erosion and subaerial deep tropical weathering and erosion
of landforms
The volcanic islands are a result of the accumulation of volcanic deposits along the
crest of the Palau Ridge The limestone islands were once formed in shallow waters and
have experienced a complex history of tectonic movements in addition to repeated
submergence and emergence related to sea level fluctuations affecting the growth and
distribution of limestone deposits
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4126 00 Page 6
Malakal Island is situated at the mapped transition zone between volcanic and
limestone island geology As a result Malakal Island contains both volcanic and limestone
rock and their derived soils Malakal Island is separated from the volcanic island of
Arakebesang located to the north Koror Island is primarily of volcanic origin with a small
area along the east side composed of a limestone rock ridge Causeways constructed on
the shallow water reef platforms between the individual islands connect the islands of
Koror Malakal and Arakebesang
Based on the Geology Map of Babelthuap and Adjacent Islands Palau Islands
prepared by the U S Army and the U S Geological Survey the geologic materials mapped
on Babeldaob Koror and Malakal Islands are mainly volcanic rock deposits However
based on our previous field exploration the volcanic deposits are locally overlain by layers
of weathered rock materials and younger alluvial estuarine lagoonal and coralline beach
deposits
Volcanic breccia may be defined as a coarse grained volcanic rock composed of
various broken rock fragments called clasts that are welded together by heat and
pressure with a fine grained mineral rock matrix Breccia rock is typically formed by
volcanic eruptions and or volcanic debris flows consisting of super heated rock masses
The emerged limestone islands are generally steeply sloping and densely vegetated
with little surface soil cover The coastal areas often exhibit mangrove swamp underlain
by soft organic soils and lagoonal deposits of reef formation and coralline fragments and
sands It should be noted that the limestone frequently exhibits signs of rock dissolution
producing cave and shoreline overhanging structures
The volcanic islands typically contain some soil cover grading to volcanic rock
formation at depth The soil thickness may vary depending on location The islands are
typically incised by multiple streams and contain steep slopes covered by dense
vegetation often obscuring the ground surface Broad areas of mangrove swamp are
GEOLABS HAWAII

more typical of the volcanic islands where larger volumes of alluvial sediment are
generated and deposited along the coast and at the mouths of streams
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located on the northeastern side of Malakal Island in the Republic
of Palau The subject parcel is roughly rectangular in shape and encompasses an area
of approximately 6 2 acres Malakal Lagoon borders the project site on the northeast and
east and the Palau Fisheries dock bounds the project site on the south We understand
that a future marina facility will be located to the north of the project site Ernguul Road
traverses the west perimeter of the subject parcel and provides the main access to the
proposed hotel facility The project site is located within 0 5 miles west of the existing
Malakal Quarry where rock is visible at the ground surface
It is our understanding that the currently vacant project site was once an industrial
facility which has subsequently been demolished The project site is relatively flat with
overgrown shrubs Three pieces of concrete slabs were noted on the subject site with the
largest piece covering roughly the area of the proposed L shaped mid rise hotel building
footprint
Based on the available topographic map the existing ground surface elevations at
the project site range from approximately 3 to 5 feet MSL with the southeastern portion
of the parcel having the highest elevation and the northeastern and western portions of the
parcel being the lowest At the time of our field exploration the northeastern corner of the
property bordering Malakal Lagoon was inundated during high tide The current grade
provides very poor drainage The poor drainage condition was aggravated by the presence
of surficial clayey soils on the western portion of the site which often time ponds water and
is difficult to maneuver vehicles after rain storms

Pile Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the anticipated heavy structural
loads we recommend that driven pile foundations be used to support the proposed 6 story
hotel tower structure We believe that 16 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4126 00 Page 12
may be used to support the proposed hotel tower building The piles would derive support
principally from end bearing in the lower portions of the extremely weathered breccia
formation or on the moderately weathered breccia rock formation underlying the site at
greater depths as encountered in our borings drilled for the project An allowable
compressive load of up to about 75 tons 150 kips may be used for the recommended pile
foundation design The allowable compressive load for the piles is for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by up to 50 percent for transient loads such as for wind or seismic
forces Piles should be spaced a minimum of 4 feet center to center to avoid reduction in
vertical capacity due to group action and to facilitate driving of piles in a group
We understand that precast prestressed concrete piles can be produced locally in the
Republic of Palau The pre casting yard in the Republic of Palau is capable of manufacturing
16 and 18 inch octagonal piles up to 160 feet in length In addition the local pre casting yard
can also manufacture 12 inch square piles up to only 30 feet long We envision that the
precast piles most likely will be transported on barge from the pre casting yard to the project
site
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site the anticipated
lengths of the piles would be approximately 65 and 75 feet below the existing ground surface
We anticipate that the western portion of the L shaped tower structure to require pile lengths
of about 75 feet long with the eastern portion of the tower structure to require piles on the
order of about 65 feet long The above estimated pile lengths are provided for initial cost
estimation purposes The actual production pile lerigths should be estimated after
completion of a test pile program Due to possible variation in the length of the piles line
items for add ons shorter piles etc should be included in the contract documents
Considering the relatively short pile lengths of up to about 75 feet from a pile handling
point of view pile splicing should be avoided in order to utilize the bending and uplift
capacities of the precast concrete piles Therefore we recommend that the proposed piles
be cast in one piece for the proposed pile installation

Predrilling
Our borings indicated that the project site is underlain by a relatively thick layer of
undocumented fill that contains cobble to boulder size particles The presence of
these over sized particles will likely impede the pile driving process and may cause
the piles to be damaged during driving In order to facilitate the pile driving operations
through these potential obstructions predrilling at the proposed pile locations to a
depth of about 15 to 20 feet below the existing ground surface would be required prior
to pile installation
We wish to emphasize that the diameter of the predrilled holes should be limited to
about 18 inches in order to provide the 16 inch octagonal pile with sufficient soil
contact for lateral load resistance If an over sized predrilled hole is encountered
during construction the annular space between the piles and predrilled holes should
be backfilled with tremie concrete to provide the recommended lateral load capacity
Detailed recommendations pertaining to predrilling would be presented after
completion of the test pile program
Uplift Load Resistance
Uplift loads imposed on the pile foundations may be resisted by a combination of the
dead load of the driven pile and by shear along the pile surface and the adjacent soils
An ultimate uplift load capacity of 25 kips may be used in design of the pile
foundation The uplift load capacity includes the weight of the pile The structural
capacity of the pile member in tension should be checked by the project structural
engineer
Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on the building tower structure may be resisted by a
combination of the lateral load capacity of the driven piles and the passive pressure
acting against the vertical faces of the pile caps grade beams Passive earth
pressure against the vertical faces of the pile cap grade beam may be estimated

using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth
pcf
Lateral load resistance for driven piles is a function of the stiffness of the surrounding
soil the stiffness of the pile allowable deflection at the top of the pile and the induced

The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a minimum energy

Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils may
be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations should
be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the excavation
bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be performed without
pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and should be coordinated with
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4126 00 Page 33
shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not adversely affected
Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in a blowing or heaving
of the excavation bottom or sides
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches connected
to excavations If this occurs the granular bedding and or backfill in the utility
trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater drawdown
resulting in settlements and potential damage to utility lines and or other existing
adjacent structures
Pavement Design
Flexible and or rigid pavements may be required for the proposed hotel development
Detailed information pertaining to the traffic loads was not available at the time this report
was prepared For preliminary design purposes we have assumed that the new pavements
at the parking lot will generally be subjected to passenger vehicles and light pick up trucks
However we anticipate that the access service roads and loading docks will be subjected
to heavier vehicles such as maintenance vehicles and delivery trucks
Our preliminary pavement design assumed that the pavement subgrade soil will be
similar to the near surface soils encountered in the borings with a minimum CBR of about 10
and a swell value of less than 1 percent If imported fills are used for the proposed
pavements the subgrade on which the pavement structural section is to be placed will need
to have a minimum CBR of 12 Based on the above assumptions we recommend that the

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the hotel site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 extending to depths ranging from about
16 5 to 93 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with augers and coring tools The approximate locations of
the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD split barrel sampler with a
140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last
12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would
be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical
Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Six Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentation of the test results is
provided on Plate B 1
Thirty four sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 including thirty three hydrometer tests
ASTM D 422 were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification Graphic presentation of the test
results are provided on Plates B 2 and B 8
One direct shear test ASTM D 2166 was performed on a selected soil sample to
evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plate B 9
Two Modified Proctor tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on the bulk samples of
the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content relationships The
results of the tests are presented on Plates B 10 and B 11
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests were performed on the bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soils The results of the tests are presented on Plates B 12 and B 13
