SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
In accordance with the scope of service outlined in our fee proposal dated 
August 14, 2013, we researched existing available subsurface information in the vicinity 
of the proposed GCL Kalaeloa Development project site.  This report summarizes the 
findings from our research performed for the GCL Kalaeloa Development Site project in 
the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii and presents generalized subsurface 
maps, including generalized soil properties. The project location and general vicinity are 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This information is to assist in the due diligence phase of the project; in the 
preliminary planning of the development scheme; and, to assist in the development of 
initial project cost estimates. The findings presented herein are subject to the limitations 
noted at the end of this report.   
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is situated in the northeastern portion of Kalaeloa, formerly 
known as Naval Air Station Barbers Point.  Based on the information provided, it is 
proposed to develop over 100 acres of former military land as a mixed use campus with 
several multi-story buildings providing an estimated 1.35 million square feet of floor 
space, a 1,500 car parking structure and several open parking lots.  It is anticipated that 
the structures will have moderate to high column loads. 
The geology in the area is dominated by hard coral formation with overlying 
alluvium. Some of the considerations in the design of the facility include: site 
preparation and grading; foundation support for supporting high capacity column loads; 
excavation difficulties; and, possible expansive soil issues. 
Due to the preliminary nature of this study and time constraints, field exploration 
consisting of drilling and sampling of borings was not included in this due-diligence 
study.  Based on our field observations and experience in the area, the primary 
geotechnical considerations for the development of the site include the following items:

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Existing Geotechnical Engineering Data 
The site is situated in a portion of the former NAS Barbers Point that was 
developed in the earlier portion of the 20th century and subsequently fell into disuse 
after World War II.  There has been little or no development within the area in recent 
times.  Therefore, available information is largely from nearby developments, both on 
and off the Kalaeloa or former NAS Barbers Point facility. However, few of these 
projects involved relatively large structures and the information is generally limited due 
to the shallow depths of the borings for these existing explorations.   
2.2 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of weathered remnants of the extinct 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae volcano forms the bulk of 
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau shield forms the majority of the 
eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active 
and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu. 
The mixed use campus site is located on the Ewa Coastal Plain off the southern 
flank of Waianae and its geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related 
to glacio-eustatic fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis 
of the coastal plain.  The subsurface conditions are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age 
coral/algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea during 
the Pleistocene Epoch which are intercalated with terrigenous sediments.  The base of 
the stratigraphic section in this area is Waianae Basalt. 
The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late Pliocene 
Epoch and the early Pleistocene Epoch by thin bedded basaltic lava flows.  The main 
shield-building activities ceased approximately 2.5 – 3.5 million years ago.  Evidence 
from drilled wells indicates that the island of Oahu has subsided as much as 1,200 feet 
since the cessation of this early volcanic activity (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During that period of subsidence, coral-algal reefs began to grow on the southern 
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across the mouth of the bays.  The

growth of the reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence.  A series of 
lagoons formed behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments 
accumulated in the lagoons (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as a 
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world.  As the great 
continental glaciers accumulated, the level of the ocean fell since there was less water 
available to fill the oceanic basins.  Conversely, as the glaciers receded, or melted, 
global sea levels rose because more water was available.  The land mass of Oahu 
remained essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in 
nature.  These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both 
higher and lower relative to present sea level on Oahu. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quit waters 
protected by fringing reefs. 
The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal sediments may have also resulted 
in deposits of coral-algal limestone and marls within the predominantly lagoonal 
sedimentary unit. 
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef 
deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused 
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials.  In addition, renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the 
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy 
conditions.  
During periods of no significant sea level changes, continued stream action 
extended the alluvial channels, deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over 
the reefs and lagoonal sediments.

Land development projects within the last 125 years have brought the area to its

2.3 
Geological Conditions 
The geology of Oahu was mapped by the US Geological Survey (2007).  This 
map indicates that the geology within the project region may be grouped into two major 
stratigraphic units.  The engineering characteristics of these units appear to be 
reasonably consistent.  These soil units were used on the maps in the body of this 
report and are identified as follows: 
Coral-Algal Deposits (Qls) 
Older Alluvium (Qa) 
Based on our review of the available data, and previous experience in the area, 
the following discussions summarize the general characteristics of the materials that 
may be encountered within the development area. 
2.3.1 Coral-Algal Deposits (Qls) 
This unit consists of fossil remnants or coral-algal reefs and their related detrital 
debris deposits.  In Hawaii, the detrital deposits tend to be the dominant portion of 
any coral rock assemblage. 
The detrital deposits are weakly to strongly cemented coral debris consisting of 
gravel, sand and silt sized clastic fragments.  Some of the coral fragments were 
dissolved and the solution cavities filled with cemented and/or uncemented fine 
sands and silts.

Due to the granular nature of the majority of the coral-algal deposits and the degree 
of cementation frequently present, this formation generally has high in-situ strength 
characteristics.  Structurally, the coral-algal reef deposits are capable of supporting 
heavy foundation loads.  Difficult excavation can be encountered in areas with a 
high degree of cementation. 
The competent nature of the material may reduce the need for elaborate trench 
shoring systems but poorly cemented areas and lagoonal deposits may be less 
stable. 
Again, due to the granular nature of the coralline materials, they are generally 
highly permeable and capable of transmitting large volumes of water.  The coralline 
materials generally present good conditions for the use of infiltration systems, such 
as wastewater disposal units or storm water management systems. 
The following subunits of coral-algal deposits are typically encountered: 
2.3.2 Coral-Algal Detritus 
This subunit consists of granular detritus broken from reef formations.  The soils are 
generally medium dense to dense sandy gravels and gravelly sands.  Silts and 
clays are only present in minor or trace amounts.  Detritus may also be weakly 
cemented. 
2.3.3 Coral-Algal Reef Formation 
This formation is composed of corals and calcareous algae in their original growth 
positions or detrital deposits which have become moderately to well cemented.  
Excavation through massive reef deposits may be difficult. 
2.3.3.a Lagoonal Reef Formation 
This subunit consists of dendritic or branching corals in their original 
growth forms.  The interstices between the branches of coral have been 
filled with a fine sandy organic silt.  Conventional excavation methods may 
be used in lagoonal deposit; however, shoring may be required due to the 
weak structure of the formation.

2.3.3.b Filled Cavity 
This term is used to describe what appears to be a cavity in a reef 
formation which has been filled by loose sand and soft silts. 
2.3.3.c Beachrock 
During lower stands of the sea, beach sands and related littoral deposits 
were exposed at elevations below current sea level.  Frequently, when 
calcareous littoral deposits remain relatively stable for extended periods of 
time, they will become cemented into a compact sandstone material. 
2.3.4 Older Alluvium (Qa) 
The Older Alluvium is assigned the position of being the second oldest unit in the 
stratigraphic column of the project region even though some of the coral-algal reef 
deposits may be intercalated with the alluvial deposits. 
This unit generally overlies the coral reef deposits and consists of terrigenous 
sediments which have been transported by stream action.  The materials consist 
mainly of very stiff to hard brown silty clays.  Experience in the Ewa – Kapolei and 
Kalaeloa region indicates that there may be buried channels traversing the Ewa 
Plain.  Gravel lenses representing buried stream channels may be encountered.  
Portions of the Older Alluvium unit contain some weathered basalt boulders and 
cobbles.  These coarse grained parts of the unit may also contain artesian water 
from the basal fresh water system or as a sub-aquifer. 
The old lagoonal deposits of the Older Alluvium unit consist of marine or lagoonal 
sediments which have been desiccated and consolidated into very stiff to hard gray 
clayey silts. 
The Older Alluvium materials generally exhibit moderate to high strength which 
leads to a relatively high bearing capacity. Frequently, these materials show a 
moderate to high shrink/well potential. Conventional excavation methods may 
generally applied to these materials.

2.4 
Soil Conditions 
Soil conditions for Oahu have been mapped by the US Department of Agriculture 
Natural Resources Conservation Service and are available at the USDA NRCS website.  
Although this mapping focuses primarily on agronomic information regarding the soils, 
there are some engineering properties that can be gleaned from the mapping.  In 
addition, the NRCS mapping is valuable in that it generally delineates areas where the 
soil properties are consistent. 
The NRCS mapping indicates that the GCL Kalaeloa site is underlain by the 
following soil groups:  
Coral Outcrop (CR) 
Ewa Silty Clay Loam (EmA) 
Filled Land, Mixed (FL) 
Mamala Stony Silty Clay Loam (MnC) 
2.4.1 Coral Outcrop (CR) 
This soil group consists of exposed coral-algal limestone and their related detrital 
debris deposits.  As with the geologic units above, the detrital deposits are weakly 
to strongly cemented coral debris consisting of gravel, sand and silt sized clastic 
fragments.  Some of the coral fragments were dissolved and the solution cavities 
filled with cemented and/or uncemented fine sands and silts. 
The infiltration capacity of the coralline soils can be very rapid.

2.4.2 Ewa Silty Clay Loam (EmA) 
This soil group consists of brown hard silty clay deposited as alluvial soils over the 
coralline deposits.  Our experience with these soils indicates that they have a high 
shrink-swell potential when allowed to dry and are subsequently rewetted. 
As mapped, the EmA soils have relatively flat natural slopes, ranging from about 
0 to 2 percent.  Their infiltration capacity is low and they would be limiting for the 
application of infiltration systems.  However, according to the NRCS, coralline 
bedrock is encountered at relatively shallow depths, on the order of about 20 to 
40 inches below the existing ground surface. 
2.4.3 Filled Land, Mixed (FL) 
As applied to the GCL Kalaeloa site, this soil group consists of man-made fill soils 
over coralline deposits.  Our experience with these soils indicates that they are 
mixed with respect to the material, source and consistency. 
As mapped, the FL soils have relatively flat natural slopes, ranging from about 0 to 
3 percent.  Their infiltration capacity is low to moderately low and they may be 
limiting for the application of infiltration systems.  However, based on the NRCS 
information, coralline bedrock is encountered at relatively shallow depths, on the 
order of about 40 to 60 inches below the existing ground surface. 
2.4.4 Mamala Silty Clay Loam (MnC) 
This soil group consists of brown hard silty clay deposited as alluvial soils over the 
coralline deposits.  Our experience with these soils indicates that they have a high 
shrink-swell potential when allowed to dry and are subsequently rewetted. 
As mapped, the MnC soils have flat to moderate natural slopes, ranging from about 
0 to 12 percent.  Their infiltration capacity is low and they would be limiting for the 
application of infiltration systems.  However, according to the NRCS information, 
coralline bedrock is encountered at relatively shallow depths, on the order of about 
8 to 20 inches below the existing ground surface.

3.2 
Undocumented Fills 
It appears that a relatively large portion of the site has been mapped as filled 
land.  Although historical information indicates that most of this fill was associated with a 
former military airfield, MCAS Ewa Field, it should be noted that there was a large 
expansion of the NAS Barbers Point and MCAS Ewa Field complex during World War II.  
It has been our experience that in much of the civil work that was done for military 
facilities during the war, execution of the work in an expedient manner tended to take 
precedence over quality.  Therefore, much of the area mapped as fill may consist of 
what would be considered uncontrolled fills or grading work.  In addition, construction 
standards in the early 20th century greatly differed from those in use today.  So, the 
grading work may not comply with current standards.  Such areas of undocumented or 
lower quality fills may not be exposed until additional exploration and/or construction is 
underway.

3.3 
Cavities and Voids 
It has been our experience in other projects on the Ewa Coastal Plain that coral 
formations are generally highly variable in terms of hardness, weathering, and 
fracturing.  In addition, cavities and/or voids are commonly encountered in coral 
formations.  In many cases, cavities and voids in the coral formation may be filled with 
loose coralline deposits.  In areas where natural coralline deposits are anticipated near 
the ground surface, special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation to 
reduce the potential for distress to the future improvements, such as foundations, floor 
slabs and pavements. 
3.4 
Coral Excavation 
Based on our review of available data, some areas of the development site may 
be underlain by coral formation at or near the existing ground surface.  The coral 
formation in the project area is believed to be moderately weathered and medium hard 
to hard in nature. We anticipate that the majority of the coral formation may be 
excavated with heavy excavation equipment, such as the large backhoe excavator. 
However, coral formations typically contain localized hard and crystallized zones. 
Therefore, we anticipate that some difficult excavation conditions may arise in localized 
areas during construction.  It is possible that the excavation for a portion of the utility 
line trenches will require the use of hoerams or chipping. The contractor must exercise 
care to avoid over-ripping, which would disrupt the structure of the coral formation 
resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for footings in its vicinity.

3.5 
Expansive Soil Conditions 
As indicated previously, the available mapping indicates that portions of the 
development site may be underlain by alluvial deposits consisting of moderately to 
highly expansive clayey soils.  Unless foundations, pavements and slabs-on-grade 
constructed above these expansive soils are properly designed, there is a potential for 
future distress to the structures and pavements resulting from shrinking and swelling of 
the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content.  To reduce the potential for 
structural distress resulting from swelling of the subgrade soils, we recommend properly 
preparing the subgrade soils prior to fill placement. 
The lateral extent and thickness of the alluvial soils should be delineated during 
the design phase by conducting a detailed field exploration program.  Furthermore, the 
expansive potential of the alluvial soils should also be evaluated during the design 
phase.  Where expansive soils are encountered, over-excavation of the alluvial soils 
may be necessary under foundations, slabs-on-grade, and pavements.  We estimate 
that an over-excavation depth on the order of about 12 to 24 inches may be required in 
areas underlain by the expansive soils.  The non-expansive, select granular fill should 
extend beyond the perimeter of the building a minimum of 24 inches (or extend to the 
edge of the concrete sidewalk surrounding the building, if applicable).  The resulting 
over-excavation would need to be backfilled with non-expansive select granular fill, such 
as coral fill. 
3.6 
Deep Shrinkage Cracks 
Our experience in the area indicates that shrinkage cracks are commonly present 
at the existing ground surface underlain by alluvial soils.  In general, shrinkage cracks 
will develop in the ground due to the lack of maintaining the soil moisture content as the 
surface soils start to dry out under the sun and dry weather conditions.  The 
development of shrinkage cracks in the ground is apparently intensified by the

prolonged dry climatic conditions that are characteristic of the Kapolei area, resulting in 
large fluctuations of the soil moisture content. 
If the existing ground cracks are left unattended for an extended period of time 
and are allowed to penetrate deeper below the surface, the ground cracks could 
adversely affect the performance of the future building foundations near the areas with 
large shrinkage cracks.  The ground cracks could serve as conduits for water to 
percolate into the foundation soils and cause loss of strength of the foundation soils 
resulting in potential settlement of the building structure.  In addition, migration of the 
foundation soils into the openings of the shrinkage cracks could undermine the 
foundation system and result in potential settlement of the building structure.  Therefore, 
remedial work to correct or mitigate the observed ground cracks should be implemented 
prior to fill placement and construction of the proposed structures. 
3.7 
Foundations 
We understand that the development will generally consist of a variety of 
multi-story buildings.  Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site, 
we believe that most of the proposed structures may be supported on a shallow 
foundation system consisting of spread and/or continuous footings provided that several 
provisions are followed. 
As mentioned previously, cavities and/or voids may be present in the coralline 
deposits at the project site.  To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support 
resulting from collapse of the cavities and/or voids below foundations, a program of 
cavity probing and grouting of the foundation locations may be necessary during 
construction.  In addition, due to the variable nature of the fill materials and potentially 
expansive nature of the alluvial soils, over-excavation of these materials may be 
required during construction.  Furthermore, column loads for typical structures 
supported on shallow foundations should be limited to about 200 to 300 kips.

providing the following preliminary foundation recommendations for use in the planning 
of the project.

we recommend evaluating the subsurface conditions by conducting a field exploration 
program consisting of drilling and sampling borings during the design phase when the 
type and distribution of the proposed structures are better defined. 
3.8 
Slabs-On-Grade 
In addition to the over-excavation of the expansive alluvial soils and replacement 
with non-expansive, select granular fill materials, the building slabs-on-grades underlain 
by the expansive alluvial soils should be at least 5 inches thick and should be reinforced 
with reinforcing bars (spaced at least 18 inches in each direction) in lieu of the standard 
welded wire fabric.  This recommendation is provided to reduce the potential for 
structural distress from differential movements of the slabs due to the expansive nature 
of the on-site alluvial soils.

For interior building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic or machine vibration), 
we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs.  The open-graded 
gravel cushion fill would provide uniform support of the slabs and would serve as a 
capillary moisture break.  To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through 
the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier, 
such as a plastic membrane, is recommended on top of the open-graded gravel cushion 
fill layer.  Flexible floor coverings, such as carpet or sheet vinyl, should be considered 
because they can better mask minor slab cracking.  We also recommend that interior 
walls be designed to incorporate some flexibility to accommodate a small amount of 
possible ground movements. 
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery vibration, we 
recommend providing a 6-inch layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs in lieu of the 
4-inch thick gravel cushion fill layer.  The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed 
basaltic aggregates compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  Where 
slabs are intended to function as rigid pavements for trucks, a minimum slab thickness 
of 6 inches may be used for preliminary design purposes.  Provisions should be made 
for proper load transfer across the slab joints that will be subjected to vehicular traffic.  
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at least 
12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade. 
To further reduce the potential for distress to the building slabs-on-grade and 
foundations resulting from water infiltration into the subsurface from areas immediately 
adjacent to the building foundations, we recommend considering a concrete sidewalk 
(or pavements) around the perimeter of the building structures.  The non-expansive, 
select granular fill recommended below the building slabs, if applicable, should extend 
out to the edges of the concrete sidewalk and should be compacted to at least 
90 percent relative compaction.  The concrete sidewalk slab may be supported directly 
on the non-expansive select granular fill.  Construction joints should be provided at 
intervals equal to the width of the sidewalk with expansion joints at right-angle 
intersections.

Based on our experience in the Kapolei area, minor differential slab movements 
between the building slab and the abutting sidewalk slabs have been observed on 
several occasions.  We believe that this may be attributed to the lack of maintenance of 
the sidewalk subgrade moisture content after the initial subgrade preparation.  It should 
be noted that the moisture content requirement of the clayey subgrades (at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture) is an important requirement considering the 
moderately to highly expansive nature of the on-site alluvial clayey soils.  Therefore, the 
subgrade soils underneath the sidewalk should be properly moisture-conditioned and 
maintained moist until the placement of the select granular fill and concrete.  In addition, 
consideration may be given to structurally connecting the two abutting slabs with dowels 
or other structural connections, especially at the entrances to the building and other 
openings in the walls. 
It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled 
tightly against the slab edges with low expansion, relatively impervious soils.  These 
areas should also be graded to divert water away from the slabs and to reduce the 
potential for water ponding around the slabs and foundations.
