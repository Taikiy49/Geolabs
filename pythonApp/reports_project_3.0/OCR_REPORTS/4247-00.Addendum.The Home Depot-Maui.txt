REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes the older West Maui Tertiary
Epoch and the more recent East Maui also known as Haleakala Pleistocene Epoch The
Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain located between these two volcanoes The
project site is located at the northern area of this gently sloping plain
The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows from Haleakala ponding on West Maui
It is comprised of alluvium washed from the slopes of West Maui and East Maui Haleakala
The erosional processes are dominated by the detachment of soil and rock masses from the
mountain walls and the soil materials are transported downslope toward the Isthmus primarily
by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a
valley alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited
as alluvium In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the
stream flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this the
transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of
a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional
base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin to
alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the materials
Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion of
the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate Simultaneously
GEOLABS INC
W O 4247 00 Page 5
erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in headwaters This
continued erosion generates material which is transported downslope covering the older
alluvial deposits Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer
sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time In addition their consistency and
density are generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits
Underlying the alluvial deposits are overlapping lavas from the West Maui and East
Maui volcanoes The bulk of the Haleakala shield was built during the late Pliocene and early
Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows of the Honomanu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch the characteristics of the lavas changed to very hard thickly
bedded flows of andesitic composition These lavas have been grouped as the Kula Volcanic
Series Typically the basalt consists of thinly to thickly bedded a a and pahoehoe type flows

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The project site is generally underlain by fill from previous agricultural use consisting
of very stiff to very hard silty clays and clayey silts The fill is underlain by alluvial deposits
consisting of very stiff to very hard silty clays and clayey silts Moderately to extremely
weathered basalt boulders were encountered in several of the borings at about 15 feet below
the existing ground surface Based on our field exploration and laboratory tests we believe
that the main building may be supported on a shallow foundation system consisting of spread
and or continuous footings bearing on the in situ silty clays and clayey silts The surficial silty
clays and clayey silts encountered during our field exploration were generally dry Therefore
we recommend that the silty clays and clayey silts encountered at the footing subgrade level
be thoroughly moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture content and recompacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Our laboratory tests indicate that the in situ clayey soils exhibit a low to moderate
potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations We recommend
that concrete slabs on grade be supported on a minimum of 12 inches of non expansive
imported select granular fill materials to reduce the potential for structural distress resulting
from the shrinking and swelling of the in situ soils In addition the subgrade soils below the
non expansive select granular fill materials should be properly prepared and kept moist until
covered by the concrete slab

Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe that
the in situ silty clays and clayey silts would provide suitable foundation support for the
proposed main building We recommend that a shallow foundation system consisting of
spread and or continuous footings be used to support the proposed main building
For the design of foundations bearing on the in situ silty clays and clayey silts an
allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used to support the main building
This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should
be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility tench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
Our field exploration indicated that the surficial soils are generally dry Therefore the
subgrade soils at the bottom of footing elevations should be scarified to a depth of at least
8 inches moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test
procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding
to the maximum dry density
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations
the soft and or loose materials should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials The over excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill materials

We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed building
structure Based on the existing topography we anticipate that about 3 feet or less of cuts and
fills will be required to achieve the design finished grades
The near surface soils encountered at the proposed site consist of silty clays and
clayey silts which exhibit a low to moderate potential for shrinking and swelling when
subjected to moisture fluctuations Therefore to reduce the potential for structural distress to
the slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the silty clays and clayey silts we

minimum of 12 inches of non expansive select borrow fill material below the concrete slab

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site In addition the
metal objects and asphalt concrete embedded within the soil located at the
northwest portion of the site should be removed and disposed of properly The
near surface soils encountered in cut areas may be stockpiled and re used as a
source of general fill material provided that they meet the requirements for general
fill presented in the Fill and Backfill Materials subsection of this report
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm material and the
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered fills The
excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site and or used
in landscape areas where appropriate Contract documents should include additive
and deductive unit prices for over excavation and compacted fill placement to account
for variations in the over excavation quantities

should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction

for areas subjected to vehicular traffic Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The non expansive select
granular fill materials should be placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to at least
95 percent relative compaction Select borrow subbase and base course materials
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level
lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density

critical elements of the earthwork observations and soil density tests should be
performed by Geolabs during the site grading operations to assist the contractor in
obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on each
fill lift Where compaction is less than required additional compactive effort should be

Seismic Site Coefficient
The Island of Maui is designated as Seismic Zone 2B based on the Uniform Building
Code 1994 Based on our field exploration the project site is underlain predominantly by
alluvial deposits consisting of very stiff to very hard silty clays and clayey silts Based on this
information the project site may be classified as being an S soil profile with a soil site
coefficient of 1 0 Table No 16 J of the Uniform Building Code 1994

The 1997 Uniform Building Code has not yet been adopted by the county The site
would be classified as Soil Profile Type Sc for the Uniform Building Code 1997
Liquefaction Potential
Liquefaction occurs when a build up of excess pore water pressure is induced on
saturated cohesionless soils resulting in a substantial loss of strength The most common
cause of this phenomenon is the cyclic loading due to earthquakes This process may cause
both vertical and horizontal movements in the soil and if not confined can result in significant
deformations Loose uniformly graded fine grained sands and loose silts with little cohesion
are most susceptible to liquefaction Generally liquefaction is not likely to occur if the loose
deposit is deeper than 50 feet below the ground surface The greater overburden pressure
can be sufficient to prevent liquefaction in deeper deposits
Based on our field exploration the project site is underlain by very stiff to very hard
cohesive soils These near surface soils are underlain by weathered volcanics Therefore
we believe that potential for liquefaction at this project site is minimal
Corrosion Potential of Soils
Fill material will be imported to the site to raise the grades to the design finished
elevations The exact location of the material source is unknown at this time however we
anticipate that the source will be close to the project site The soils in the project vicinity are
classified as Puelu Clay Loam by the USDA Soil Conservation Service and have been
determined to have low corrosivity
Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems be
installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation and
pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also
be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils

These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and

properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the
potential for water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and
maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slabs and footing foundations
Percolation Testing
Eight percolation tests were performed in Boring 111 and 112 in the
proposed parking area to evaluate the subsurface permeability conditions at the site The
falling head method was used for the percolation testing After the boring was drilled water
was injected into the boring To obtain a steady state percolation rate the soil was allowed
to soak in the water for at least four hours After the initial soaking period water was
readjusted and drop of the water level in the boring was measured along with time Results
of the falling head percolation tests are presented in Appendix C
The test indicated that the subsurface silty clay and clayey silt soils have coefficient of
permeability k in the range of 3 x 10 to 7 x 10 centimeters per second

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 29 borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 15 Boring Nos 101 through 113 and Boring No 201
to depths of about 11 5 to 30 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate
locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill
rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials encountered are
provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 29
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 2 inch O D standard penetration sampler and ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84
Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with
a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the
Logs of Borings as REC 74

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Six one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the near surface soils The results are summarized on Plate B 1
Six Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of these
tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
representation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Three unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength characteristics The results of these
tests are presented on Plates B 3 through B 5
Four direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics Results of the direct shear tests are
presented on Plates B 6 through B 9
Three Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on the near
surface soils to determine the dry density and moisture content relationship Results of the
tests are presented on Plate B 10 through B 12
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on the near surface soil to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soil Results
of the tests are presented on Plate B 13 through B 15

APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
Percolation tests were performed by using the falling head method in four of the borings
drilled Boring 111 112 The test borings have or had a diameter of 4 inches
and depths of 6 to 11 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of testing Water was
injected into the test borings and was allowed to soak for at least 4 hours to achieve a
steady state percolation rate Water was again injected into the test borings and the drop in
water level with respect to time was recorded The water levels within the test borings were
monitored by an electric water level sounder The results of the percolation tests are
presented on Plates C 1 through C 8
