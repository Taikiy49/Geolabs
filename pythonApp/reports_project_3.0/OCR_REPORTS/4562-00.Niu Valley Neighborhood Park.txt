INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Niu Valley Neighborhood Park Improvements project located at 5510 Kanau
Street in the Honolulu area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity
of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the design of foundations slabs on grade site preparation
and pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
volcano Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age Ice Age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau volcano reached the end
of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Koolau Range and its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio eustatic
fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age The base of the stratigraphic
section in this area is Koolau Basalt The Koolau volcanic shield was built by thinly bedded
lava flows during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epochs The main shield building
activities ceased approximately 2 million years ago In the mountainous regions of Hawaii
the erosional processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock mass from the slopes
and movement of the material downslope toward the axis of the valley by gravity to form
colluvial deposits Once the colluvium reaches streams in the valley alluvial processes
become dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium Large areas
of alluvium can be found in the lowland areas and areas bordering streams
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the
oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above the present sea level
GEOLABS INC
W O 4562 00 Page 5
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of accumulated
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at correspondingly
higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters protected
by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and
reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials Land development and reclamation projects within the last century
have brought the project site to its present form

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the generally competent materials encountered at the project site we
recommend that the proposed comfort station be supported on a shallow foundation system
consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the engineered fills
Based on our laboratory tests the near surface clayey soils exhibit a high shrink swell
potential when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents To reduce the potential
GEOLABS INC
W O 4562 00 Page 7
for distress to the future lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from the swelling of the
near surface soils we recommend that the on site clayey soils be capped with a minimum of
36 inches of non expansive select granular fill capping fill material below all slab areas In
general the subgrade soils should be scarified moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above
the optimum moisture and compacted to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing
surface prior to the placement of fill material The slab subgrade should be kept moist and
free of shrinkage cracks prior to placement of capping fill cushion fill and or slab concrete
Our geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of foundations
slabs on grade site preparation and pavements are further discussed in the following
sections of this report
Building Foundations
Based on the generally stiff subsoil conditions encountered at the site we recommend
that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used
for support of the proposed comfort station An allowable bearing pressure of up to
2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing
on the engineered fills This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased
by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements the
foundations should be embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement
is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural
loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 3
may be used for footings bearing on the engineered fills Resistance due to passive earth
GEOLABS INC
W O 4562 00 Page 8
pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot
per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted Unless
covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should
be neglected
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate the total settlements to be less than 1 inch Differential
settlements between adjacent footings supported on similar materials should be on the
order of about 0 5 inches
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative from Geolabs
prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing
conditions and the required embedment depths
Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the comfort station As
previously indicated our laboratory tests indicate that the clayey soils encountered in our
borings exhibit a high potential for shrink swell when subjected to moisture fluctuations To
reduce the potential for structural distress of the slabs on grade resulting from the shrinking
and swelling of the clayey soils we recommend that the subgrade soils underneath the slab
areas be properly prepared and capped with at least 36 inches of non expansive select
granular fill material below the slab cushion The non expansive select granular fill capping
fill should also extend laterally beyond the slab on grade perimeter a minimum of 18 inches
The non expansive select granular fill should be placed as soon as possible after the
subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the Site
Preparation section of this report The capping fill and the underlying subgrade soils should
be wetted and kept moist prior to final placement of concrete Where shrinkage cracks are
noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils be thoroughly
moistened to close all cracks or prepared again Saturation and subsequent yielding of the
GEOLABS INC
W O 4562 00 Page 9
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation
of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer consisting of free draining
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below the
slab above the capping fill to provide more uniform support of the slab To reduce the
potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the
cushion layer Two inches of moist fine sand may be considered above the plastic membrane
to provide protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Provision of
the 2 inches of moist fine sand above the vapor barrier will be left up to the discretion of the
project structural engineer
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project Since these
exterior concrete walkways can tolerate some movement we recommend that a minimum
12 inch cushion layer consisting of the non expansive select granular fill material be
provided below the concrete slab The cushion layer should be compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Contraction joints should be provided at intervals equal
to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at least
12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab should be
backfilled tightly against the slab with relatively impervious soils on site soils and graded to
divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the
foundations
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the proposed finished floor elevation
16 5 feet MSL we anticipate site grading work for the proposed project will consist of fills
on the order of about 2 feet thick and cuts on the order of about 3 feet deep At the on set
GEOLABS INC
W O 4562 00 Page 10
of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared
Debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of off site
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill
or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm material and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic
soils should be properly disposed of off site Contract documents should include additive
and deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement to account for
variations in the over excavation quantities
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation
should be properly backfilled with select granular fill material moisture conditioned to above
optimum moisture content placed in 6 to 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities to be abandoned in place under the proposed
Structure should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure
After clearing and grubbing the areas within the future slab on grade limits should be
over excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 36 inch thick capping layer of
non expansive select granular fill material The non expansive select granular fill material
should also extend a minimum of 18 inches laterally beyond the perimeter of the proposed
slab on grade areas The over excavated subgrades should then be scarified to a minimum
depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The compaction
requirement should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction for areas
subjected to vehicular traffic It should be noted that proper moisture conditioning and
preparation of the clayey subgrade soils is critical to the performance of the foundation and
slab system
The on site clayey soils generated from the cut areas may be reused as a source
of general fill material up to the bottom of the non expansive select granular capping fill and
aggregate subbase layer provided that deleterious materials vegetation are removed and
GEOLABS INC
W O 4562 00 Page 11
the materials are screened for over sized materials 3 inches in maximum dimension
Imported general fill materials needed for the site filling should consist of materials with a
low expansion potential Imported general fill materials should consist of materials with
particle sizes of 3 inches or less in maximum dimension The material should have a
laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 12 or higher and a swell potential of not
more than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883
The non expansive select granular fill required under the building and walkway slabs
should consist of non expansive select granular material such as crushed coral or basalt
The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles
passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more
and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1883
Aggregate base and subbase course materials required for the pavement sections
should consist of crushed basalt aggregate and should conform to Sections 703 06 and
703 17 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works
Construction 1994 Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use
General fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The non expansive select
granular fill materials under the building and walkway slabs should be placed in level lifts of
about 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Aggregate base and subbase
course materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
GEOLABS INC
W O 4562 00 Page 12
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction should
be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable
compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed to compact
the fill materials
Cut and fill slopes planned for the subject project should be constructed at an inclination
of 2H 1V or flatter Fills should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide
stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest point and
continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the fill placement
recommendations presented above Excessive water should be diverted away from the tops
of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as the fill slopes are completed to
reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative from Geolabs It is
important that a representative from our office observe the site grading operation to evaluate
whether undesirable materials are encountered during the scarification process and whether
the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our field exploration

the driveway and parking stall In general we anticipate the vehicle loading will consist
primarily of passenger vehicles and light pick up trucks Based on our field exploration and
laboratory testing the on site clayey soils appear to have a CBR value of about 4 9 with an
expansion value of about 6 4 percent Based on the above assumptions we recommend that
the following flexible pavement section be used for preliminary design purposes

water into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to
collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation For long term performance we
recommend that a subdrain system be constructed adjacent to paved landscaped areas
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines will be required for the proposed project In general
for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches
of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the pipes Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also
be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate
support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the
possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the
potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support
GEOLABS INC
W O 4562 00 Page 14
for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and
damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils or select fill material The
backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in maximum
8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below
pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should
be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems be
installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation and
pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also
be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils Drainage swales should
be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface run off away from the slabs
and foundations
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface clayey soils The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings to depths ranging from about 11 to 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Three one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of the tests are summarized on
Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil sample to
evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results are
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic representation
of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on the
near surface clayey soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils The
test results are presented on Plate B 3
