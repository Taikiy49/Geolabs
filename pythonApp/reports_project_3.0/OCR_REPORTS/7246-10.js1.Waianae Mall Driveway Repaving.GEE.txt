REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be 
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. The rocks of the Waianae Volcanic Series are 
divided into Lower, Middle and Upper Members.  The lavas of the Lower Member 
consist largely of thinly bedded pahoehoe flows, whereas the Middle Member consists 
of interbedded pahoehoe and a`a flows with a higher occurrence of the a`a form.  The 
Upper Member consists primarily of massive a`a lava flows. 
Erosion deeply dissected the Waianae Shield removing much of the western 
slope of the dome resulting in broad amphitheater headed valleys. During the 
Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of continental 
glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill the 
oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.  When the 
glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of 
the calcareous reef materials. 
The project site is at the foot of Puu Paheehee which is an elevational 
prominence on an erosional remnant ridge from the western flank of the Waianae 
Range.  Chemical and mechanical weathering of the volcanic rocks of the ridge has 
resulted in the deposition of an apron of colluvium and alluvium along the pediment of 
the ridge.  These deposits generally consist of remnant boulders and highly expansive 
clay.  In the lower areas where the topographic gradient decreases, the clay soils 
frequently mantle the underlying coral formation.

The existing Waianae Mall is located at the northeastern corner of the intersection 
of Farrington Highway and Leihoku Street in the Waianae area on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii.  The project site is located along the southern portion of the mall. The driveway

At the time of our exploration, the existing pavement surface at the Farrington 
Highway driveway fronting Burger King and the mall’s southern commercial buildings

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration program for the project consisted of drilling and sampling a 
total of five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, to depths of about 4.67 to 
5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) testing 
was also performed at five locations adjacent to the borings to depths of approximately 
0.9 to 5.65 feet below the existing pavement surface.  Three bulk samples of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-3, were collected at boring 
locations B-2, B-4, and B-5, respectively. The bulk samples were composite samples of 
the near-surface soil cuttings generated from supplemental holes drilled adjacent to the 
borings. The approximate boring, DCP, and bulk sample locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Our borings generally encountered a pavement section consisting of 
1 to 4 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by 13 to 32 inches of fill material. Below 
the fill, we generally encountered alluvial soils consisting of medium stiff to stiff clay and 
coralline detritus consisting of very dense sandy gravel and sandstone to the maximum 
depth explored of about 5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on factors such as 
seasonal rainfall, storm surge conditions, and other factors.

Fatigue cracks are a series of interconnecting cracks caused by 
fatigue failure of the asphaltic concrete surface under repeated 
traffic loading. 
Block Cracking 
Block cracks are inter-connected cracks that divide the pavement 
into approximately rectangular pieces. 
Pothole 
Potholes are small bowl-shaped (usually less than 30 inches in 
diameter) depressions in the pavement surface.

A rut is a surface depression in the wheel paths.

Raveling is wearing away of the pavement surface due to a loss of

Subgrade Strength Characterization 
Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) tests were performed adjacent to all five 
boring locations in general accordance with ASTM D6951 to obtain indicative subsoil 
strength characteristics such as California Bearing Ratio (CBR). The tests were 
generally performed through the subgrade between depths of about 0.9 and 5.65 feet 
below the existing ground surface. 
The Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) device consists of two 16mm-diameter 
stainless steel rods with the lower rod containing an anvil, a replaceable 60° pointed tip 
and depth markings. The upper rod holds a 17.6-pound drop hammer with a 22.6-inch 
drop distance. At the beginning of a test, the cone is “seated” by dropping the hammer 
until the widest part of the cone is just below the testing surface at which point the initial 
penetration is recorded as 0. The operator then lifts and drops the hammer one or more 
times depending on the strength of the soil at the test location. Following each 
sequence of hammer drops, a penetration reading is taken. The process is continued 
until the desired depth of testing is reached, the full length of the lower rod is buried, or 
refusal conditions are encountered. 
Data from our DCP testing is presented as the penetration index which is the 
penetration distance of the cone for each drop of the hammer expressed in inches per 
blow. The penetration indices obtained from our tests were correlated with a common 
pavement design parameter CBR. The penetration indices are presented on the DCP 
field logs shown in Appendix B.

subgrade soils. As mentioned above, ponding water was observed in the vicinity of the

The existing subgrade soils under the new pavement structural section should be 
scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to near the optimum 
moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T180 or 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
In general, the aggregate base course should be moisture-conditioned to above 
the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction in 
accordance with AASHTO T180 or ASTM D1557. Geolabs should test imported 
aggregate base course for conformance with the recommendations presented herein 
prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
Aggregate base course as required should meet the material requirements for 
Base Course as specified in Section 31 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works 
Construction, City & County of Honolulu, September 1986.  
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basaltic 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to at least 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity determined 
in accordance with AASHTO T209 (ASTM D2041).

Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction.

LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field borings and DCP testing. Variations of the subsurface 
conditions between and beyond the field borings and DCP testing may occur, and the 
nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is 
underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the 
recommendations presented herein.

Vista HCx. The elevations shown on the borings were obtained from the Grading Plan 
contained in the Waianae Mall Parking Lot & Site Repair Rehab Work Bid Set, dated 
August 30, 2013. The physical locations of the boring points should be considered 
accurate only to the degree implied by the method used.  
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. These data 
have been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, 
it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in tides, rainfall, perched 
groundwater conditions, groundwater withdrawal, and other factors.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to depths of about 
4.67 to 5.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The approximate boring locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill 
rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties.  The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two One-Inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed 
samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils.  The test results are 
summarized on Plate C-1. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits.  The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.  Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plate C-2. 
Four Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification.  Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions are provided on Plate 
C-3. 
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed 
on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils.  The test results are presented on Plates C-4 through C-6.
