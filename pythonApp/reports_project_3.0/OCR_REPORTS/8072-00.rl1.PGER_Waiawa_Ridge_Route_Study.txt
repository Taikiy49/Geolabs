Based on the information provided, it is desired to identify a preferred route and general 
requirements for the proposed Waiawa Ridge Route Study project. We understand that two 
routes, designated as Alignments A and G, are selected for detailed study. The approximate 
alignments of the two routes are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. The following 
descriptions of the two route alternatives are provided for information and reference: 
Alignment A 
Alignment A generally consists of approximately 10,700 linear feet of 48-inch and 60-inch 
diameter pipe beginning at the Waiawa Ridge by Gentry development, following Waihona 
Street to Kamehameha Highway, then intercepting Lehua Avenue to the Pearl City

upon completion of the construction of Alignment A.

exceeded 25 feet, microtunneling is the suggested construction method.

SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to our geotechnical analysis are the subsurface materials anticipated along the 
proposed microtunneling/deep tunnel boring alignments, the engineering properties of the 
materials anticipated, and the variability of the subsurface conditions along the project 
alignments considering the long, linear nature of the project site. Therefore, the following

3.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu generally is composed of the weathered and dissected remnants of 
two extinct shield (domed) volcanoes – Waianae and Koolau. The older Waianae Volcano forms 
the bulk of the western third of Oahu’s landmass while the younger Koolau Volcano forms a major 
portion of the eastern two-thirds of Oahu’s landmass. Generally, it is believed that the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes began erupting on the order of about 20 to 25 million years ago as 
deep ocean submarine volcanoes. Subaerial volcanic eruptions on the Island of Oahu ended 
approximately 2.2 to 3.5 million years ago in the Pleistocene Epoch. Furthermore, the Waianae 
Volcano became extinct while the Koolau Volcano remained active; therefore, the eastern flank 
of Waianae Volcano is partially overlapped and buried below the younger Koolau Volcano lavas 
near the central interior portions of the Island of Oahu. 
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly-bedded 
basaltic lava flows. The main shield-building activities ceased approximately 2.5 million years ago. 
Evidence from drilled wells indicates that the Island of Oahu has subsided by as much as 
1,200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During that period of subsidence, coral-algal reefs began to grow on the southern coast 
of the Island of Oahu, forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth. The growth of the 
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence. A series of lagoons formed behind the

During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as a result 
of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great continental glaciers 
advanced, the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill the oceanic basins. 
Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global sea levels rose because more water was 
available. The land mass of Oahu remained essentially stable during these changes, and the 
fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the 
sea that were both higher and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of terrigenous 
sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at correspondingly higher 
elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters protected by 
fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef 
deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused desiccation of the 
soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials. In 
addition, renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited 
terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high-energy conditions. 
During periods of no significant sea level changes, continued stream action extended the 
alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal sediments. During 
these stable periods, the growth of fringing coral/algal reefs formed distinct ledges of reef 
limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea during that period. About 
15,000 years ago, a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred. In the last 10,000 years or so, the 
sea level has adjusted to its present stand. Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to 
accumulate in low-energy estuarine or lagoonal environments, resulting in thick deposits of soft 
and/or loose sediments along the coast in areas that were formerly valleys and drainage ways. 
The coastal plain is composed of interbedded alluvial, marine, and post-erosional volcanic 
deposits. Extensive development of the plain and reclamation of the low-lying areas have given 
the land its present shape. There is evidence that basaltic lava flows of the Honolulu Volcanic

The proposed Alignment A generally traverses lowland coastal alluvial plains along the 
southern distal flank of the Koolau Mountain Range. The coastal plain surfaces traversed by 
Alignment A are relatively flat to gently undulating in the easterly to the westerly direction. There 
are five primary stream channels that discharge at the East Loch of Pearl Harbor. The meandering 
streams have contributed significantly to the erosional evolution and deposition of the coastal 
alluvial plains by incising into the underlying basaltic rock (Koolau Basalt) comprising the 
basement rock formation of this portion of the Island of Oahu. Land reclamation projects in the 
last 100 years have brought the area to its current form. Many of the resulting fills at the ground 
surface are of poor quality in terms of supporting large structural loads. 
The proposed Alignment G is along the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano. The 
geomorphology and subsurface conditions at the site are related directly to the glacio-eustatic 
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of Pearl Harbor and 
the Honolulu Coastal Plain. The alignment is situated within a portion of the Schofield Plateau 
geographic region that is comprised of an old alluvial terrace. Subsurface conditions along the 
alignment are dominated by older (Pleistocene age) silty and clayey alluvial soil deposits. These 
older alluvial soil deposits typically are locally intercalated with some other terrigenous deposits, 
such as stream sands and gravel deposits and cobble and boulder deposits.

3.3.1 Recent Fill (Rf) 
This stratigraphic unit represents low-lying areas that were filled during urbanization. The 
majority of these low-lying areas is underlain by recent alluvium at relatively shallow 
depths that were originally marsh areas. Some of the filled lands along the sewer 
alignment are underlain by older alluvial soil deposits. In areas underlain by the recent 
alluvium, the fill materials generally consist of clayey silts and silty clays with stiff to hard 
consistencies. The fills only form a thin crust over the soft recent alluvial soils. 
The quality and composition of the fill materials are variable. These fills were frequently 
placed without proper compaction control. Therefore, it is difficult to make any general 
statement regarding their engineering properties or construction in these areas. 
3.3.2 Recent Alluvium (Ra) 
These soils generally are the result of sedimentation in a very low energy environment, 
such as an estuary or bay. Frequently, the soils assigned to this unit accumulated very 
rapidly underwater and might not have been exposed to air. Therefore, there is a 
tendency to encounter under-consolidated (OCR [Over Consolidation Ratio] < 1.0) 
sediments, which settle under the weight of minimal overburden or structural loading. 
Recent alluvium generally is found within some stream channels and bordering some 
stream banks, at the mouths (outlets) of streams, and within broad valley floors and basin 
features surrounded by hills. These soils also may be found in proximity to the swamp and 
tidal flat environments. The soils assigned to this unit generally are considered to have 
low in-situ permeability and have low strength characteristics and the tendency for

3.3.3 Older Alluvium (Qa) 
Older alluvial deposits are generally very stiff to hard silty and clayey soils that were 
originally deposited in alluvial environments, buried over long periods of time by more 
recent deposits to become consolidated, and then subsequently uncovered at the ground 
surface by erosion or other geologic processes. This unit generally overlies the Koolau 
Basalt and consists of terrigenous sediments, which have been transported by stream 
action from the upper reaches of the streams.  
Gravel lenses representing buried stream channels sometimes are encountered. Portions 
of the Older Alluvium unit may contain some weathered basaltic cobbles and boulders. 
These coarse-grained parts of the unit also may contain artesian groundwater from the 
basal freshwater system or as a sub-aquifer. The deposits may resemble saprolite soil

3.3.4 Tertiary Koolau Basalt (Tkb) 
Koolau Basalt is the oldest geologic unit along the two route alternatives. Generally, it 
consists of a’a and pahoehoe flows of basaltic lavas and may have a mantle of cobbles 
and boulders, or residual soils, overlying the top of the basalt formation. In addition, it is 
anticipated that various types of volcanic rock subcomponents, such as clinker layers and 
lava tubes, also may be encountered within the basalt formation. The following subunits 
are used in this preliminary geotechnical engineering report, where applicable. 
Residual Soil 
This subunit consists of soils produced from the in-situ chemical weathering of the 
basic rock originating from Koolau Basalt. These soils generally consist of stiff to 
very stiff, brownish red or reddish brown clayey silts and silty clays. The soils are 
usually “structureless” and may have a blocky texture.

Saprolite 
Saprolite essentially is derived from the tropical weathering of volcanic 
formations, which may contain intrusive rock bodies of variable composition, such 
as basalt, andesite, etc. Saprolite is a form of residual soil that exhibits a relict 
structure from the parent rock. Structural features, such as vesicles and joints, are 
visible in saprolite. This subunit consists of predominantly clayey silt and silty clay 
soils containing variations in color with varying degrees of mottling. 
Clinker 
Clinker is the fragmental basalt found on the top, and sometimes at the bottom, 
of a’a lava flows. Clinker generally is gravel to cobble-sized fragments of 
scoriaceous rock in a tightly packed but open-graded structure. 
Basalt Formation 
This subunit is used to designate the in-situ lava formation. This subunit occurs as 
red, brown, or gray, severely fractured to massive, medium hard to very hard rock 
formation. 
3.3.5 Groundwater 
In general, the groundwater level along the two proposed sewer alignments occurs as a 
basal water table at or near sea level, typically ranging from about -1 to +2 feet MSL with 
occasional fluctuations to +3 feet MSL due to extreme high tide conditions. This 
groundwater is usually brackish or saline. In some isolated areas, perched groundwater 
and/or subartesian groundwater may be encountered. It should be noted that portions 
of the alignments may be located within the Pearl Harbor artesian aquifer basins. The 
potentiometric head of this aquifer varies spatially, temporarily and in response to 
pumpage. 
Due to the proximity of portions of the proposed alignments to the Pearl Harbor West

MICROTUNNELING/DEEP TUNNEL BORING 
Microtunneling/deep tunnel boring is a trenchless process for installing underground 
pipelines, ducts, culverts, and utilities. The technique involves the use of powerful hydraulic jacks 
that push or jack successive pipe sections through the ground behind a remotely controlled 
excavating unit or cutting disk head at the same time as the excavation is taking place within the 
excavating unit. The following sections briefly describe the advantages and methods of 
construction for microtunneling/deep tunneling boring. 
4.1 
Major Advantages or Benefits 
In general, the use of microtunneling/deep tunnel boring techniques may offer the 
following major advantages or benefits as compared to the conventional open trench 
construction method. 
1. 
Reduced disturbance and potential damage to adjacent utilities, existing roads, 
and surrounding structures 
2. 
Improved public and job site safety 
3. 
Reduced surface disruption to vehicular and pedestrian traffic 
4. 
Reduced disturbance to local residents and businesses 
5. 
Reduced air and noise pollution and vibration 
6. 
Greater flexibility in alignment selection and reduced need for utility relocations 
7. 
Reduced extensive shoring system 
8. 
Reduced need for significant dewatering 
9. 
Smaller volumes of spoils that require transportation and disposal 
10. 
Reduced potential for treatment of contaminated soil and groundwater 
4.2

4.4 
Ground Behavior 
Ground behavior for soft ground tunneling may be described according to six categories, 
as summarized on Plate 7, Categories of Ground Conditions for Soft Ground Tunnels. Coarse-
grained, granular cohesionless or predominantly sandy soils can be characterized as firm, 
running, or flowing ground depending on the degree of cementation and on the groundwater 
conditions. Above the groundwater level, these soils will not stand unsupported but tend to ravel 
or run until a stable slope is formed that buttresses the tunnel face. If there are sufficient fines 
present to give the soil some apparent cohesion, these soils may be able to stand unsupported 
in the heading for short periods of time. Below the groundwater level, these soils may be flowing 
ground unless the cohesion or cementation of the soil is high enough to resist the seepage forces.  
Both running or flowing ground behavior indicates potentially unstable ground conditions with 
low stand uptime. 
An assessment of the ground conditions likely to be encountered along the 
microtunneling/deep tunnel boring excavations should be conducted to evaluate the stability of 
the excavations and to provide information on the selection of appropriate equipment and 
methods for the tunnel and shaft construction. Based on our review of the subsurface conditions 
anticipated along the proposed microtunneling/deep tunnel boring alignment, the significant 
geotechnical engineering consideration is the presence of soft clayey silt soils at about the pipe 
invert elevations along localized areas of the proposed sewer alignments. Under such conditions, 
significant loss of ground due to flowing or squeezing ground behavior may occur at the heading 
resulting in potential surface settlement unless the soils are stabilized or an appropriate 
tunneling method is used. Therefore, the tunneling method selected should provide a face 
support pressure high enough to resist the hydrostatic head associated with groundwater levels. 
Special attention should also be given to the vertical and lateral extents and hardness of the 
basalt rock formation anticipated along a significant portion of the sewer alignments.

4.7.3 Thrust Block Resistance 
Thrust block provides the reaction for the main jacks when pushing the pipe forward. The 
allowable passive resistance of the soil, which is a function of the strength of the soil, will 
control the thrust resistance that can be developed.  It may seem reasonable to allow the 
thrust block to develop the full passive resistance because this is a temporary 
construction loading condition.  However, it must be recognized that relatively large 
strains are required to develop the full maximum passive resistance. At jacking loads that 
impose stresses approaching the maximum passive resistance, there will be large 
movements of the thrust block in response to the strains required to develop full passive 
resistance. These movements may result in damage to buried utilities or other facilities 
behind the thrust block. 
In addition, large movements of the thrust block are not desirable for pipe jacking because 
the movements are usually not uniform, and it becomes difficult to maintain the thrust 
block perpendicular to the pipe alignment. If the thrust block does not remain square, the 
force applied to the pipe may not be uniform, resulting in eccentric loading, which could 
damage the pipe. The design of the thrust block for this project should take into 
consideration the presence of soft soils adjacent to the shaft excavations. These soft soil 
deposits may limit the jacking forces that can be mobilized unless a rigid reinforced 
concrete thrust block is constructed to distribute the jacking forces over a large enough 
area to avoid overstressing these soils.   
Movements of the thrust block can also be controlled by limiting the allowable passive 
resistance to about one-half of the maximum passive resistance.  This corresponds to a 
much lower level of strain and smaller soil movements. If the project requires higher 
jacking forces than can be safely transferred to the soil mass behind the thrust block, then

5.1 
Earth Pressure Loads on Pipes 
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench, the size of the pipes, the 
unit weight of backfill material, and the friction resistance between the backfill material and the 
trench walls. To calculate the vertical loads on the buried utility pipes, we recommend using an 
average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot (pcf) for the backfill material and a coefficient 
of friction of 0.3. Earth forces acting upon the pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of 
the trench. Therefore, the width of the trench should be kept to a minimum. Traffic loads on the 
buried pipes should also be considered for the portion of the pipes located in roadway areas. 
5.2 
Pipe Bedding 
In general, the first zone of backfill extending from the bedding material to at least 
12 inches above the top of the pipes (or groundwater level) should consist of select free-draining 
granular material (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) to reduce the compaction effort required and 
resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce 
the potential for the formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate 
support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and 
pipe damage. 
We envision that soft lagoonal/marsh deposits will be encountered at or near the invert 
elevations in localized areas of the selected sewer alignment. Therefore, we recommend 
providing a subgrade stabilization layer consisting of at least 24 inches of No. 2 Rock (ASTM C33, 
No. 4 gradation) wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) below the 
bedding layer for uniform support. The stabilization layer should extend beyond the sides of the

Settlements 
The primary settlement of the new waterlines is normally caused by the difference in unit 
weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill material placed 
over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the underlying subsoils below 
the trench invert. Based on our engineering analyses, the primary settlement on the order of less 
than 0.5 inches is anticipated for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good workmanship 
will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could occur if improper trench 
support is used.

6.1 
Soil Borings, Rock Coring, and Associated Field Testing 
Based on the length of the proposed sewer alignments, the number of jacking/receiving 
shafts, and the anticipated subsurface conditions, we believe a geotechnical exploration program 
consisting of a minimum of 30 and 15 borings should be implemented for Alignments A and G, 
respectively. Based on the plan and profile drawings provided, we envision that the borings 
should extend to at least two tunnel diameters below the tunnel invert at the proposed boring 
locations, which could vary from about 30 to over 100 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Typically, soil borings should be advanced with 4-inch diameter continuous-flight 
solid-stem augers (SSA) to the first encounter with groundwater and then continued with PQ-size 
wireline coring methods to advance the PQ-size casing from the ground surface. In general, auger 
drilling should be used in areas where stiff to hard cohesive soils (older alluvium) exist and in 
locations where rock coring is not anticipated. Additionally, SSA drilling should be switched over 
to the wireline coring method when hard clays, boulders, and/or rock formation are encountered 
or when soft and/or loose soils that caved in are encountered.

be obtained using the modified California sampler (ASTM D3550) and the Standard Penetration 
Test (SPT) split-spoon sampler (ASTM D1586). In the softer soils, relatively “undisturbed” samples 
should be obtained by driving a ring-lined modified California sampler (ASTM D3550) and/or 
hydraulically pushing a thin-walled Shelby tube (ASTM D1587) into the soil formation.

is performed in accordance with ASTM D1586. Standard Penetration Test (SPT) sampling provides

Categories of Ground Conditions for Soft Ground Tunnels 
(After Terzaghi, 1950) 
Firm Ground - A heading may be advanced several feet or more without immediate support. Hard 
clays and cemented sand or gravel generally fall into this category. 
Raveling Ground - After excavation, the material above the tunnel or in the upper part of the 
working face tends to flake off and fall into the heading.  Slightly cohesive sands, silts, and fine 
sands gaining their strength from apparent cohesion typically exhibit this type of behavior. Very 
stiff fissured clays may be raveling materials also. 
Running Ground - Cohesionless, dry soils run from any unsupported vertical face until a stable 
slope forms at the natural angle of repose (.i.e., approximately 3 degrees to 35 degrees). Running 
ground consists of dry, cohesionless materials, such as clean loose sand or gravel. 
Flowing Ground - If seepage develops at the working face, raveling or running ground is 
transformed into flowing ground, which advances like a viscous fluid into the heading. Silt, sand, 
or gravel below the water table without a high enough clay content to develop significant 
cohesion will be flowing-type soils. 
Swelling Ground - A condition where the ground absorbs water, increases in volume, and expands 
slowly into the tunnel.  This may occur in highly over consolidated clays that exhibit high volume 
change characteristics upon wetting. 
Squeezing Ground - Squeezing ground conditions are analogous to plastic flow, and the soil is 
observed to advance slowly into the tunnel excavation without any signs of fracturing. Squeezing 
occurs without an increase in the water content or a volume change in the soil and is governed 
by the soil strength in comparison to the overburden pressure. Squeezing ground may include 
soft to medium stiff or stiff clays depending on the overburden pressure at the tunnel depth.
