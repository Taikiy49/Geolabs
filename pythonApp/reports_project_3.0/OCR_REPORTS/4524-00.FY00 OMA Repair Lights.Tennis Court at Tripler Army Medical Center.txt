INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed FY00 OMA Repair Lights of Tennis Court project within the Tripler Army
Medical Center in the District of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input only and are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the Tripler Army Medical Center in the District of
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii It is proposed to improve the existing lighting
system for the Tennis Court as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Based on the information
provided we understand that a total of 15 existing light poles will be replaced and new light
poles will be installed adjacent to the existing light poles around the tennis court
The new light poles will be of steel construction approximately 25 feet in height
500 pounds in weight and 8 inches in diameter at the base Based on a bolt circle diameter
of 11 inches and anticipated structural loading we envision that a minimum diameter of
24 inches should be used in our engineering analyses for the anticipated drilled shaft
foundations The project structural engineer provided the following information pertaining to
the foundation design of the new light poles for our engineering analysis

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by
thinly bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased approximately
2 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicates that the Island of Oahu has
subsided by as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity
Macdonald and Abbot 1970
During that period of subsidence coral aigal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed behind
the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the lagoons
Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available
to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially stable
during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to the
present sea level of Oahu
GEOLABS INC
W O 4524 00 Page 5
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of terrigenous
sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at correspondingly higher
elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters protected by
fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and
reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action extended
the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal sediments
During these stable periods the growth of fringing coral algal reefs formed distinct ledges of
reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea during that period
About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that rise the
valleys in the project area were submerged In the last 10 000 years or so the sea level has
adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to
accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits of
soft loose sediments along the coast in areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways
The project site is geologically situated on the northeastern flank of the Aliamanu vent
and Salt Lake vent complex of the Honolulu Volcanic Series As the molten magma
ascended through these vents it reacted with the groundwater generating steam This
steam caused explosive eruptions which deposited low cones of ash material that have
consolidated to form tuff locally referred to as mudrock
The Aliamanu tuff is an older water laid ash deposit that was laid down when the sea
level was higher than its present level The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its material
over alluvium originating from Halawa Stream After this phase of volcanic activity ceased
sedimentary deposits accumulated over the Aliamanu tuff before the next phase began
Then the Salt Lake eruptions began when the sea was at about 40 to 60 feet below its
present level The Salt Lake eruptive event deposited its material over earlier tuffs from the
GEOLABS INC

older Aliamanu events The water that accumulated in the crater was believed to originate
from depressional springs and rainwater During periods of little rainfall the shallow ponded
water was reduced by evaporation and over the years the mineral salt content of the ponded
water increased resulting in the name Salt Lake
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located at the existing tennis court within Tripler Army Medical
Center in the District of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project site is situated
to the north of the intersection of Krukowski and Patterson Roads The existing tennis court
is located across the roadway of Building 102
The existing tennis court is paved with asphaltic concrete and is surrounded by a
fence The existing light poles are located outside of the fence On the north side of the
tennis court and about 15 feet away from the fence the ground slopes down toward the
valley at a ratio of about two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V The ground slopes upward
at about 2H 1V on the east side of the tennis court with about 20 feet clearance from the toe
of the slope to the fence Some utility manholes were observed along south side of the site
Several large trees were located on the west side of the tennis court Some minor cracks
were observed within the asphaltic concrete pavement

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicates that the proposed light pole locations are generally
underlain by hard silty and clayey soils overlying hard residual soils throughout the depths
explored In consideration of the lateral loads and moments acting on the foundations of the
proposed light pole foundations we recommend that drilled shafts embedded in the silty soils
be used to support the proposed lighting system We recommend that the drilled shafts be
embedded a minimum of 4 feet below the lowest adjacent finished grades for the proposed
light pole foundations located on level grounds Detailed discussion of these items and our
geotechnical engineering recommendations are presented in the following sections of this
report
Drilled Shaft Foundations
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances the proposed
new light pole structures may be supported by a foundation system consisting of
cast in place concrete drilled shafts Based on the anticipated base plate dimensions for the
new light pole structures we envision that a minimum diameter of 24 inches should be used
for the proposed drilled shaft foundations Based on the anticipated structural loads and the
subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we recommend that drilled shaft
GEOLABS INC
W O 4524 00 Page 8
foundations with an embedment length of not less than 4 feet below the lowest adjacent
grades be used for the new light pole structures located on level ground conditions
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally from
skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable compressive load
capacity of up to 25 kips per shaft may be used for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces
Resistance of the shaft soil system deflection may resist lateral loads imposed on the
light pole foundations Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is a function of the stiffness
of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of shaft and
induced moment in the shaft Based on the information provided each drilled shaft will be
subjected to a lateral load of 750 pounds and an overturning moment of about
14 000 pound feet at the ground surface Based on our foundation analyses the maximum
moment induced in the drilled shaft increases slightly to 14 750 pound feet at a depth of
about 1 foot below the top of the shaft Our lateral load analyses indicates that the estimated
deflection at the top of the drilled shaft should be less than 0 25 inches
Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the light pole foundations because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to that of shallow foundations
However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the following conditions
would have a substantial effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the contact
between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper construction
techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft holes and
placing concrete into the holes
GEOLABS INC
W O 4524 00 Page 9
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is not recommended Placement of concrete
by wet construction methods using a tremie pipe will be required where groundwater is
encountered in the drilled holes Concrete should be placed in a suitable manner by
displacing the water in an upward fashion from the bottom of the drilled hole
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch range should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Concrete should
be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for segregation of the aggregates from
the concrete mix If precast concrete pole bases are used the gap between the pole base
and the surrounding soils should be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete A
representative from Geolabs should be present to observe the installation of drilled shafts
during construction to confirm the subsoil conditions and embedment depths
Utility Trench
We envision that new electrical duct lines will be required to service the proposed new
lighting system In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or S4C is
recommended under the duct lines Free draining granular materials such as clean sand
or S4C should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 6 inches above the duct
lines to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of
the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this
free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper backfill material
and placement procedures could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the duct lines
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site soils or imported select
granular fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 6 inch level loose lifts
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and mechanically compacted
to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground
subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench
backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative

compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content corresponding
to the maximum dry density

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about
16 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled at the approximate
locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted
drill rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Boring Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates
A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
