PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct an infiltration basin for storm water disposal in the area
south of the proposed secondary effluent reuse facility near Geiger Road The existing
ground elevations at the proposed infiltration basin site ranges from about 33 feet Mean
Sea Level MSL to 37 feet MSL The infiltration basin is anticipated to be about 4 to 12 feet
in depth

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau banked
against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the Schofield
Plateau or Central Oahu
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating sediments
which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and further south these
alluvial sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and coral algal reef
formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from zero feet in the area of
H 1 Freeway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves
as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu
Deposition of these sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
growth and retreat of the great continental glaciers As the glaciers advanced less water
was available to fill the ocean basins and sea levels fell below its present stand As the
glaciers melted and retreated more water became available and sea levels rose to and often
above the current level When the stand of the sea was high and prolonged marine deposits
accumulated at elevations above the current sea level
At the project site the near surface materials are primarily coral algal deposits which
are composed of medium hard limestone with an open lattice like structure It is believed that
these coral deposits were laid during the Kaena 95 feet Mean Sea Level MSL stand of
GEOLABS HAWAII

the sea and subsequently eroded to near their current elevation during the Waimanalo
25 feet MSL stand The coral formation has also been subjected to subaerial weathering
Coral algal materials are chemically composed of calcium carbonate During
subaerial weathering slightly acidic rain and groundwater can slowly attack the carbonate
dissolving the weaker portions of the formation This creates solution cavities ranging from
small pores to large caverns
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is situated within the existing Honouliuli Wastewater Treatment Plant
in the District of Ewa on the island of Oahu Hawaii It is bounded by North Hanson Road
to the west Geiger Road to the south and the Coral Creek Golf Course to the east and
north
Based on our field reconnaissance of the area the existing primary treatment facilities
in the plant are located primarily in the central and eastern portions of the plant site The
existing primary treatment facilities include primary clarifier tanks pre aeration tanks pump
stations small buildings and other minor structures The existing secondary treatment
facilities in the plant are located primarily in the northwestern portion of the plant site A
complex network of underground utilities inter connects the existing facilities
The proposed storm water infiltration basin is located at the southwestern corner of
the plant site The area was previously used as a nursery area for potted plants and
vegetation At the southern end a portion of the area is currently being used as the Ewa
Refuse Convenience Center Based on the grading plans provided the topography at the
project site is relatively level except for a 2 foot high slope with an inclination of about
5 percent located near the middle of the proposed basin area The ground surface
elevations range between 33 feet MSL at the eastern end and 40 feet MSL at the western
end

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were recently explored by drilling three
probes designated as Probe Nos P 1 through P 3 The approximate locations of these
probes and borings drilled previously for the Secondary Effluent Reuse Facility are shown

Field Percolation Testing
Seven falling head percolation tests were performed in the three probes at various
depths to evaluate the permeability of the subsurface materials The results of our
percolation tests are presented in Appendix C of this report
Based on the results of our field percolation tests we believe that a permeability rate

permeability rate corresponds to a disposal rate of about 8 5 gallons per square foot per day
For the purpose of design we recommend that only the bottom area of the infiltration basin
be used to determine the capacity of the infiltration basin The design depth of the infiltration
basin should be based on the anticipated amount of water retained prior to it percolating into
the ground

It should be noted that some siltation may occur and therefore lower the permeability
of the soil We therefore recommend that this occurrence be accounted for in the design of
the infiltration basin Otherwise some type of maintenance program should be implemented
Slopes
Our field explorations indicate that the site is generally underlain by competent coral
formation and coral detritus deposits Therefore it is our opinion that excavations into the
dense to very dense coral formations for the infiltration basins may be constructed with slope
inclinations up to one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V However we recommend that the
tops of the slopes be set back a minimum distance of 10 feet from the proposed R1 Storage
Tanks
The excavated coral material may be suitable for fill material for other portions of the
project provided that the largest rock fragments in the material is less than 3 inches in
maximum dimension and that the materials are free of vegetation and deleterious materials
In addition the material should be relatively well graded and should have a minimum of
10 percent passing the No 200 sieve size
Excavation Method
The contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations should
determine the method and equipment to be used for excavation Generally the in situ
coral formation encountered in our explorations should be rippable by a large bulldozer or
other normal heavy excavation equipment However due to the nature of the coral
formation hoerams and or chipping may be required in localized areas of hard excavation
Therefore allowances should be made for the additional time and expense that may be
required by the hard excavation

APPENDIX B
Field Percolation Testing
The falling head percolation testing was performed in general accordance with the
Auger Hole method This method is a variation of the borehole method in which water is
added to fill an auger hole at a given level and then the fall of the water level is observed
with time The permeability is based on the assumption that a unit hydraulic head gradient
causes the flow through the bottom and sides of the hole This method is expected to give
only a very approximate indication of the actual permeability of the soil
Prior to conducting the falling head percolation tests the hole was filled with water in
an attempt to saturate the surrounding soils The saturation phase was continued until the
amount of head drop per a unit of time became fairly constant
