INTRODUCTION

for the proposed Royal Kunia Site 5 located in the District of Waipahu on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii The site is located south of Anonui Street west of Kupuohi Street east of Kunia
Road and north of a drainageway The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended to assist in the design of the mass grading construction
single family house structure foundations retaining wall foundations road pavements utilities
excavation and fill placement only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger shield
Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its
eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the
Schofield Plateau
The Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded
against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch The
dips of the lava beds are near horizontal between 3 to 5 degrees from horizontal The lava
flows on the plateau have undergone in situ weathering to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are
characterized by the red colors of the soil The deep narrow canyons such as Waikakalua
Kipapa and Kaukonahua are the result of this in situ weathering
GEOLABS INC
W O 4355 00 Page 5
This in situ chemical weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils that
grade to saprolite with depth In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks Spheroidally weathered boulders are
typical in the Schofield Plateau The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with depth
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The proposed project is located south of Anonui Street west of Kupuohi Street east

In general the site consists of a relatively flat area elevated above an adjacent
drainageway with two major amphitheater shaped gullies The site gently slopes downward
to the southeast corner of the project site at an average slope inclination of about 3 percent
The ground elevations not including the amphitheater shaped gullies range from 337 feet
Mean Sea Level MSL at the southeastern corner of the project site to about 392 feet MSL
near the northwest corner of the project site
The amphitheater shaped gullies have perimeter slope inclinations ranging between
1 H 1V Within the amphitheater shaped gullies the slope inclinations range between about
20H 1V and 10H 1V The gullies are about 15 to 20 feet deep with bottom elevations ranging
from 337 to 345 feet MSL
The majority of the project site was overgrown with brush and weeds Considerable
amounts of household and construction debris were observed throughout the site An old
rock lined drainage ditch was observed to be traversing the center of the project site Several
dirt roads were observed at the eastern half of the project site Reddish brown residual soil
was exposed at the ground surface

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our exploration we believe that the proposed single family
house structures may be supported on shallow foundations The shallow foundations should
bear on properly compacted fill or in situ material
GEOLABS INC
W O 4355 00 Page 7

along the tops of the existing gullies we believe that these lots should be over excavated by
3 feet below the finish grade and replaced with properly compacted on site or import material
A large quantity of boulders will likely be generated from the earthwork operations
Only a limited amount of boulders can be incorporated into the fill Therefore we believe that
a boulder crushing operation may be necessary Otherwise hauling boulders off site and
hauling import fill material onto the site will be necessary
Uncontrolled or discarded fill material was observed within the two amphitheater
shaped gullies This material generally consists of silty cobbles and boulders and covers the
majority of the gullies This material should be removed during the site preparation phase of
construction prior to fill placement
Our laboratory testing on the silty and sandy clay materials indicated a low expansion
potential Conventional slab on grade design may be utilized for the proposed structures
Laboratory testing on the silty and sandy clay materials indicate a significant collapse
potential from water infiltration when the soil was compacted to a low density at a moisture
content below optimum Also our laboratory testing on the silty clay and sandy clay material
indicated a moderate shrinkage potential Therefore proper moisture conditioning and
compaction are very important site grading considerations

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly
cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials
should be removed and disposed of properly off site or stockpiled in a designated area
to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
Uncontrolled or discarded fill material was observed within the two amphitheater
shaped gullies This material generally consists of silty cobbles and boulders and
covers the majority of the gullies The soft loose fill material within the gullies should
be removed to allow proper subgrade preparation and compaction Embedded
boulders and cobbles into firm natural ground should be removed if it extends more
than 12 inches above the ground surface
GEOLABS INC
W O 4355 00 Page 9
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft soils may be used
as fill provided that it meets the requirements for fill material
Prior to filling the existing ground should be scarified to a depth of 10 inches and
moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction For pavement subgrades the
minimum relative compaction should be 95 percent Where shrinkage cracks are noted
after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened
to close cracks or prepared again as recommended above Saturation and subsequent
yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may
require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted
engineered fill The excavated soft soils may be used as fill provided that it meets the
requirements for fill material Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Fills and Backfills
The fill material should consist of low expansive materials less than 3 inches in largest
dimension with a CBR swell less than 1 percent The fill material should also be free
of vegetation and deleterious materials The fill material below 5 feet from finished
subgrade may consist of larger sized material 12 inches in largest dimension
provided that the material is well graded In general the on site silty clay material
should be suitable for fill material
Base course and select borrow subbase material should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate and should conform to the City and County of Honolulu Department of
Public Works Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction dated
September 1986
GEOLABS INC
W O 4355 00 Page 10
Select granular fill and imported fill should consist of non expansive granular material
such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be
well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and
should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1883 Imported fill materials should be tested by
Geolabs Inc for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the
project site for its intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
All fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
Base course and select borrow subbase materials should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage
by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils are critical
elements of earthwork observations and soil density tests should be performed by
Geolabs Inc during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required
GEOLABS INC

degree of compaction and the proper moisture content Where compaction is less than
required additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture
content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction It should be noted that the
moisture requirement of the fills and subgrades about 2 percent above the optimum
moisture is an important requirement for the use of the on site clayey soils

Scour Protection
The master drainage plan by ParEn Inc indicated that the storm water level in the
drainageway could reach 10 feet above the bottom of the slope and the water velocity will
be relatively slow Based on the low plasticity and small particle size of the on site soils
we believe that the slope has the potential to scour from storm water flows within the
drainageway To reduce the potential for scour of the slope we recommend that the lower
GEOLABS INC

10 feet of the slope be lined with an erosion control blanket such as Bon Terra SFB or
equivalent Alternatively discarded rip rap boulders underlain by a geotextile fabric
AMOCO 4551 or equivalent could be utilized
The erosion control blanket or geotextile fabric should be placed such that there
is close contact with the soil and such that void spaces are nearly non existent between the
underlying soil and the geotextile fabric Note also that the erosion control blanket or

imported select granular fill material compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative
compaction Over compaction of the retaining wall backfill should be avoided
Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
A typical drainage system consists of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable material
such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation immediately around the
perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall discharging to an
GEOLABS INC
W O 4355 00 Page 18
appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product
such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used in lieu of the drainage material The
prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated
pipe at the base of the wall Unless covered by concrete the upper 12 inches of
backfill should consist of impervious material such as the on site silty clay or the
on site sandy clay material to reduce water infiltration behind the walls
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Inc prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation
bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Slabs On Grade
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by
near surface clayey soils with low swelling potential and moderate shrinking potential Unless
floor slabs are properly designed and constructed there is a potential for future distress to the
lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of these soils due to
changes in the moisture content To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from
the shrinking and swelling of the subgrade soils we recommend that the subgrade soils be
kept within about 2 percent above the optimum moisture content prior to final placement of
concrete Conventional slab on grade design may be utilized above the properly prepared
subgrade soils
Consideration should be given to construction of a 3 foot wide sidewalk adjacent and
surrounding the entire house structure A sidewalk with a 8 inch deep thickened edge should
help to stabilize the subgrade soil moisture below the house structure and therefore reduce
the potential for swelling shrinking and softening of the subgrade soils
The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab and graded
to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential of water ponding around the
foundation
GEOLABS INC
W O 4355 00 Page 19
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill be placed below the
slab The cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or
Basaltic Termite Barrier BTB should provide more uniform support of the slabs and should
serve as a capillary break To reduce future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to
floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane may be
considered on top of the gravel or BTB cushion layer Flexible floor coverings such as carpet
or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking It is also
recommended that interior walls be designed to incorporate some flexibility to accommodate
small amount of possible ground movements
For slabs on grade that will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as single family
residential driveways the 4 inch gravel BTB cushion layer and the vapor barrier may be
omitted For the design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 100 pounds per
square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted subgrade material A
minimum slab thickness of 4 5 inches may be used for preliminary design purposes provided
that rebar reinforcement is provided For garage slabs we recommend that rebar
reinforcement be utilized For driveway slabs we recommend that as a minimum heavy duty
welded wire fabric be utilized Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the
slab joints which are subjected to vehicular traffic The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to
any unpaved area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
Exterior concrete walkways and street sidewalks are currently planned for this project
For these concrete slabs we recommend a minimum 4 inch thick slab thickness The 4 inch
gravel BTB cushion layer and the vapor barrier may also be omitted for these slabs
Control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways or sidewalks
and at right angle intersections
Subgrade soils should be prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented
in the Site Grading section of this report Compaction requirements for the subgrade soils
and select granular fill are also discussed in the same section of this report

Utilities

In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the
pipes The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of free draining
backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from
compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material be used to reduce
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes The use of on site soils as backfill immediately around utility
pipes is not recommended
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the finish
subgrade should consist of the approved on site silty sand or gravel fill soils or select granular
material The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future

and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength of the near surface soils The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce
water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain surface run off away from walls and foundations

APPENDIX B

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations ASTM D 2937 were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Two seis of sulfates and chlorides tests were performed by
Environmental Laboratory of the Pacific on selected samples to evaluate the corrosion
potential of the soil The results of these tests are presented on Plate B 1
Eight sets of resistivity ASTM G 57 and pH tests ASTM G 51 were performed on
selected samples to evaluate the corrosion potential of the soil The results of these tests are
presented on Plates B 1 and B 2
Fifteen one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils under surcharge loads These
tests were run on air dried samples Results of these swell tests are presented on Plates B 3
and B 4
Five one inch ring volumetric shrinkage tests were performed on remolded samples to
evaluate the volumetric shrinkage potential of the soils from near optimum moistures to
air dried moistures Results of these volumetric shrinkage tests are presented on Plate B 5
Four Atterberg Limits Tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
these tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic representations of the test results are provided on Plate B 6
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample to evaluate
the shear strength characteristics The test results and shear stress versus normal stress
plots are presented on Plate B 7
One unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression test TXUU ASTM D 2850 was
performed on a selected sample to evaluate the undrained shear strength characteristics The
approximate in situ effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure
Results of the test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
The test results and stress strain curves are presented on Plate B 8

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
One one inch ring compression collapse test was performed on a relatively undisturbed
sample to evaluate the compression potential under surcharge pressures with no saturation
and to evaluate the collapse potential under surcharge pressures with saturation Results of
this compression collapse test are presented on Plate B 9
Five California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on buik
samples of near surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement subgrade
support Results of these CBR tests are presented on Plates B 10 through B 14
Three Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on the
near surface soil to determine the maximum dry densities and optimum moistures of the
on site soils Results of these tests are presented on Plates B 15 through B 17
