the reclamation of low lying areas and the creation of new land by

springs entering it and a complex series of sea level fluctuations which
influenced erosional and depositional processes in the area
Development of the Honolulu waterfront area is feasible from a

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is felt that there is a need to develop and revitalize the
waterfront portions of Honolulu Harbor downtown Honolulu and the
Kewalo Ala Moana areas In order to coordinate the revitalization and
development efforts it has been proposed to formulate a master plan for
these areas which would interface with the existing master plans for the
Aloha Tower area Chinatown Downtown and Kakaako This Honolulu
Waterfront Master Plan is currently under development at the time of
preparation of this report When completed this Honolulu Waterfront
Master Plan will represent the combined efforts of disciplines such as
planners engineers and architects interacting with agencies of the
local state and federal governments
This preliminary report was prepared solely for project planning
purposes and therefore may neither contain sufficient data nor
appropriate recommendations for detailed design

The purpose of our reconnaissance was to map and provide a

EXISTING CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of
two extinct shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano
Waianae forms the bulk of the western third of the island while the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2080 00 Page 3
younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of
the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and its
eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project area is located along the southwestern flank of Koolau
and its geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to
glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene and the
genesis of Honolulu Harbor The subsurface conditions in the majority of
the area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene age coral algal reefs and
related sediments laid during high stands of the sea which are
intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic
section in the area is Koolau Basalt There are no known surface
exposures of Koolau Basalt with the project limits
The stratigraphic history of Honolulu Harbor is quite complex due to
its large areal extent the number of streams drainageways and springs
entering it and a complex series of sea level fluctuations influencing
erosional and depositional processes in the area
The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late
Pliocene and early Pleistocene by thin bedded basaltic lava flows The
main shield building activities ceased approximately 2 5 to 3 5 million
years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicates that the island of Oahu
has subsided as much as 1200 feet since the cessation of this early
volcanic activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on
the southern coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs between
the mouths of the major streams The growth of the reefs essentially kept
pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed behind the
barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2080 00 Page 4
During the Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the
world As the great continental glaciers accumulated the level of the
ocean fell since there was less water available to fill the oceanic
basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic
in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the
sea which were both higher and lower relative to present sea level on
Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and
fans of terrigenous sediments in the head of the old bays accumulation of
reef deposits at corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine
sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs
The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal sediments may have also
resulted in deposits of coral algal reef limestones and marls within the
predominantly lagoonal sedimentary units
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments
and reef deposits Sub aerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition
renewed sub aerial erosion of upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited
terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream
action extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited
alluvium over the lagoonal sediments
The geologic history of the Honolulu Harbor area has been further
complicated by the deposition of recent pyroclastic materials from
explosive eruptions of the vents of the Punchbowl and
members of the Honolulu Volcanic Series
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2080 00 Page 5
These post erosional i e following the cessation of shield
building events were contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level
fluctuations
The eruptions were rather explosive in nature owing to the
interaction of groundwater with the rising magma resulting in steam
explosions which expelled large quantities of pyroclastic material
predominantly ash and cinder The finer grained ash units of these
deposits of pyroclastics have consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally
referred to as mudrock The coarser grained units cinder generally
blanket the central portion of the Honolulu Coastal Plain and is locally
referred to as black sand The black sand is sometimes locally cemented
with the cementation ranging from weak to rocklike
About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level
occurred During that rise the deep valleys in the area were drowned In
the last 10 000 years or so sea level has adjusted to its present stand
Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in the
low energy estuarine or lagoonal environment of Honolulu Harbor and its
vicinity resulting in thick deposits of soft harbor sediments along the
coast in the areas which were formerly valleys and drainageways
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years
have brought the project area to its present form Since the beginning of
urbanization of the Honolulu area there has been a seaward progression of
land reclamation This seaward expansion was accomplished by the
infilling of marshy areas fishponds shallow offshore reefs and by the
placement of dredge spoils from harbor expansion projects A great many
of the filling or reclamation projects were done for small scale
commercial maritime uses municipal disposal residential and or military
development Many of the older fills are probably not capable of
supporting large structural loads on shallow spread footing foundations

Subsurface Conditions
The research conducted for the project indicates that the subsurface
materials consisted mainly of a thick sequence of interbedded alluvial and
marine sedimentary deposits including coral algal reefs that are
underlain at relatively great depths by Koolau basalt rock
The soils may be grouped into several major stratigraphic units The
engineering characteristics of these units appear to be reasonably
consistent for planning purposes These soil units were used on the maps
and in the body of this report and are identified as follows

mapped for this report as there are no known exposures within the project
limits and it is believed that the occurrence of these units within the

Koolau Basalt Tkb
The Koolau basalt is the oldest geologic unit within the project
area It generally consists of interbedded pahoehoe and a a
flows of basaltic lavas and may have a mantle of cobbles and
boulders or residual soil overlying the top of the rock
It is anticipated that various types of volcanic rock
sub components such as clinker layers and lava tubes could also
be encountered
The Koolau basalt is the aquifer material of the Honolulu
Isopiestic Areas which is one of the major developed groundwater
bodies supplying fresh water for the Island of Oahu
The aquifer is the permeable lava rock while the upper confining
member caprock is the relatively impermeable alluvial and
lagoonal deposits There is no lower confining member as is the
usual case in most aquifers At the bottom the fresh water
level is confined by salt water according to the Ghyben Herzherg
Principle which is based on observations that fresh groundwater
floats on saline groundwater in coastal regions The ratio of
densities between fresh and salt water causes the salt water to
be displaced downward about 40 feet below sea level for every
foot that the fresh basal water table or potentiometric head
rises above sea level

Structurally the Koolau basalt and its residual soils is one of
the strongest units within the study area Therefore the basalt
and related residual soils saprolites make excellent bearing
formations due to their high strength characteristics in an
undisturbed state However its use for foundation support in
the project area is severely limited by the considerable depths

Older Alluvium Qa
The older alluvium is assigned the position of being the second
oldest unit in the stratigraphic colum of the project area even
though some of the coral algal reef deposits may be intercalated
with the alluvial deposits
This unit generally overlies the Koolau basalt and consists of
terrigenous sediments which have been transported by stream
action from the upper reaches of the streams The materials
consist mainly of very stiff to hard brown clayey silts and silty
clays Gravel lenses representing buried stream channels are
sometimes encountered Portions of the older alluvium unit
contain some weathered basalt boulders and cobbles These coarse
grained parts of the unit may also contain artesian water of the
basal fresh water system or as a separate aquifer
The old lagoonal deposits of the older alluvium unit consist of
marine or lagoonal sediments which have been desiccated and
consolidated into very stiff to hard gray clayey silts
The old lagoonal deposits consist almost exclusively of clayey
silts of rather uniform texture and grain size with occasional
shell fragments

The older alluvium materials generally exhibit high strength
values which leads to a relatively high bearing capacity
Frequently these materials show a moderate to high shrink swell
potential however this is not considered a constraint for the
type of structures proposed on this project Structurally the
older alluvium materials are considered to be sufficiently
competent to support relatively large structural loads with

Coral Algal Deposits Q1s
This unit consists of fossil remnants of coral algal reefs and
their related detrital debris deposits In Hawaii the detrital
deposits tend to be the dominant portion of any coral rock
assemblage
The detrital deposits are weakly to strongly cemented coral
debris consisting of gravel sand and silt sized clastic
fragments Some of the coral fragments were dissolved and the
solution cavities filled with cemented and or uncemented fine
sands and silts
Due to the granular nature of the majority of the coral algal
deposits and the degree of cementation frequently present this
formation generally has high in situ strength characteristics
Structurally the coral algal reef deposits are capable of
supporting heavy foundation loads
As previously stated the areas mapped as coral algal deposits
may have a thin layer 5 feet of fill resulting from grading
activities during urbanization of the area The coral algal
deposits may also have a thin mantle of natural beach sand
These features have not been differentiated in the mapping

4
Honolulu Tuff and Cinder Qht
Rocks from vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series are the second
youngest of the natural materials in the project area
The tuff subunits consist of volcanic ash which has consolidated
or hardened to form a medium hard rock called tuff that resembles
siltstone or sandstone locally called mudrock Within the
project limits the tuff originated from volcanic vents in
Punchbowl Crater Puowaina and its occurrence is generally
limited to that area The tuff is usually found overlying
coralline deposits or alluvium units
Since the tuff formation is a medium hard rock it has very high
strength characteristics Structurally the tuff is very
competent and capable of supporting heavy foundation loads on
either spread foundations or end bearing piles However the
tuff thins out with distance from Punchbowl Crater and is not
generally encountered in borings or wells drilled near the
waterfront areas
Slightly younger than and often mantling the Punchbowl Tuff
coral algal materials and or the Older Alluvium are deposits of
sand sized volcanic cinder originating from the Roundtop
Tantalus and Sugarloaf vents above Makiki This cinder was
formed during fire fountain eruptions and was air lain over
most of the central Honolulu Coastal Plain It is locally
referred to as black sand or the Honolulu black sand
These cinder deposits are generally near uniform poorly graded
sands ranging from brown to gray or black in color Weathering
has some effect upon grain size as the cinder breaks down forming
silt sized weathering products The cinder is usually found ina
medium dense to dense state However weathering can result in
the development of cementation sometimes to the extent of
forming a weak to moderate strength sandstone like material

Recent Alluvium Ra
The recent alluvium consists mainly of very soft to medium stiff
brown to dark gray clayey silt and organic silt with varying
amounts of sand and infrequent pockets of gravel and cobbles
These soils are generally the result of sedimentation in a very
low energy environment such as an estuary or bay Frequently
these soils assigned to this unit accumulated very rapidly under
water and have never been exposed Therefore there is a
tendency to encounter underconsolidated sediments which settle
under the weight of a minimal overburden
Included in this unit are subunits of localized thin layers
about 10 feet of lagoonal marine sediments consisting mostly of
very soft to medium stiff gray clayey silts These gray silts
are identified as lagoonal facies subunits of the young alluvial
unit
The soils assigned to this unit are generally considered to he
poorly to very poorly suited for use as support for foundation
loads due to their low in situ strength characteristics and the
tendency for settlements to occur in structures overlying soils
of this unit

FUTURE CONDITIONS
Geology and subsurface conditions generally do not alter or follow
dynamic trends on a real time basis as do the elements examined by other
disciplines such as traffic water usage or cargo handling The major
trend with respect to geotechnical engineering has been the seaward
expansion of the land area by fill placement or reclamation
It is most likely that this trend will continue whether the Master
Plan developed from these studies is implemented or not However it is
almost certain that this trend will slow due to current regulatory
restrictions
The implementation of the Master Plan will most likely result in
improvements in the geotechnical and geoenvironmental aspects of areas
within the development area The following discussions and recommendations
present some of the probable constraints or impacts of the development and
some solutions or mitigating measures to the constraints or impacts
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based upon the above presentation of the regional geology and
anticipated subsurface conditions of the Honolulu Waterfront Master Plan
district it may be seen that conditions within the area are highly
complex and that it is difficult to develop cut and tried generic
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2080 00 Page 14
discussions or recommendations for the area Generally site specific
geotechnical explorations will have to be performed for each major element
of the redevelopment The following discussions and recommendations are
intended to present an overview of the geotechnical engineering
considerations and constraints which may affect elements of the overall
Plan such as cost factors and general constructability
Development of the Honolulu waterfront area is feasible from a
geotechnical engineering viewpoint provided that the plans for the
development take into consideration the complex geological conditions
existing within the Master Plan district and the constraints which may be
imposed by these subsurface conditions
Much of the existing waterfront area is previously reclaimed or
filled land Throughout history the expansion of the Honolulu waterfront
area has continued to progress seaward by the filling of marshy areas
fishponds and shallow offshore areas Therefore the land reclamation
efforts proposed in the Master Plan are a natural continuation of the
previous trend in expansion and redevelopment
The historical record clearly indicates that the reclamation was
done however the record does not clearly provide data regarding
engineering supervision of the efforts or the quality of the work In
addition acceptable engineering standards have changed drastically over
the decades
For example in the early 1900 s it was acceptable to place an
end dumped underwater fine grained soil fill to reclaim land and to
support a one story wooden shed or warehouse where supplies would be laded
by hand and drayed by horse and carriage In the 1980 s when merchandise
is handled in heavy bulk containers moved by large trucks and forklifts
the reclamation fill must be of a substantially higher quality to
provide the desired performance
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2080 00 Page 15
Likewise up until the middle of this century it was acceptable to
dispose of municipal solid waste in almost any locale that was not
desirable for development and in a relatively uncontrolled manner Now
some of these formerly undesirable areas have become highly prized real
estate and the handling or removal of the solid waste could potentially be
a serious environmental concern
Foundation Support
Based on our review of the available information it is most likely
that new structures which will be constructed as part of the development
may need to be supported by deep foundations such as piles or caissons
It may be possible to support small lightly loaded or flexible structures
on spread footing foundations if the individual site conditions permit
Proposed Fill Reclamation
Landfilling or reclamation may require special fill placement
techniques and may require bulkheading e g sheetpiling of the
boundaries of the fill Generally the underwater portions of the fills
to a level of about three 3 feet above the mean water level should
consist of a clean granular self compacting material such as 3B Fine
gravel or surge rock This granular fill will need to be choked at the
top by the placement and compaction of lifts of successively finer grained
materials or by geotextiles to reduce the loss by downward migration of
fine grained general fill at finish grade
Depending upon the subsoil conditions in areas to reclaimed special
fill placement procedures or preparation may be needed If the offshore
bottom consists of a thin layer of soft material over a competent
substrate it may be advantageous to remove the soft material by dredging
prior to the placement of the granular fill to avoid entrapment of the
soft soils and or contamination of the new fill Either of these
situations could result in future settlements of the new fill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2080 00 Page 16
If the soft soils are moderately thick it may be necessary to place
the fill in a staged manner across the fill area to displace the soft soil
in a mudwave as construction progresses The resulting mudwave may also
need to be removed Care must be exercised to avoid trapping the mudwave
below the new fill
Where the soft subsoils are thicker it may be necessary to construct
the new fill behind a bulkhead or cofferdam However the thickness and
strength of the subsoils may also limit the use of bulkheads or
cofferdams The toe of the bulkheads must develop sufficient passive
resistance to the loads imposed by the new fills to prevent a deep seated
failure of the bulkhead Also the bulkheads may require tie backs or
anchoring to prevent overturning These limitations must be examined on a
site specific basis during actual design
It may also be necessary to apply surcharge loads to the completed
fills in order to accelerate the settlement of the new fill so that
settlements will have reached a tolerable level prior to the construction
of any proposed development or site improvements
The current Honolulu Waterfront Master Plan indicates the proposed
placement of major reclamation fills in Keehi Lagoon and at the Diamond
Head side of the main entrance of Honolulu Harbor fronting Fort
Armstrong There is also a smaller fill area proposed along the seaward
side of Kewalo Basin No specific data is available on the major two
sites however based on geologic evidence and examination of aerial
photographs we estimate that these two sites are probably underlain by
recent shallow water coral reefs and related deposits The Kewalo site is
probably underlain by recent dredged coral sand and gravel material above
the underlying reef deposits
Waterways and Shoreline Improvements
The proposed Master Plan shows a series of canals or waterways in the
makai Kakaako area between Honolulu Harbor near Pier 4 and Kewalo basin
The Master Plan also shows shoreline improvements between the harbor
entrance and the Kewalo Channel
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2080 00 Page 17
Excavation for the canals and similar water features will probably
encounter man made fills of relatively good quality However the
available information indicates that these fills are most likely underlain
by relatively soft or loose lagoonal deposits Therefore the waterways
may require bulkheading or the use of sloped banks to minimize sloughing
and or erosion of the banks The bulkheads will be subject to the same
limitations discussed above Previous experience in the makai Kakaako
area indicates that sheetpiling for bulkheads will probably encounter
coral deposits at depths ranging from about twenty to thirty feet below
the existing surface and will have to penetrate into the coral formation
to develop stability Due to the anticipated subsoil conditions we
recommend that sloped banks be graded at 2 Horizontal to 1 Vertical or
flatter and that some form of slope protection be used
The shoreline improvements may have either a hardened bulkhead
boundary or an armor stone protection Based on the anticipated subsoil
conditions either method will probably work with proper design
Infrastructure
It is anticipated that some infrastructure e g sewer water and
other utilities improvements will need to be made as part of the
Waterfront development Based on the available information and previous
experience in Kakaako there will be some difficulties in constructing
these improvements due to the variable soil conditions and shallow depths
to groundwater in the Master Plan area
Excavated trenches will probably require shoring and or bracing and
dewatering This may result in somewhat higher construction costs
Barbers Point Deep Draft Harbor
The Barbers Point Deep Draft Harbor BPDDH is also included as a
part of the Master Plan study Geologically the entire BPDDH area is
underlain by coral algal deposits

Previous experience in the BPDDH area indicates that the subsoils
generally consist of relatively competent coral materials These coral
materials are generally capable of supporting structural loads on spread

Geoenvironmmental Concerns
It is our opinion that there may be potential geoenvironmental
impacts which may occur as a result of the Development of the Master Plan
area due to the heavy industrial nature of portions of the area and the
possible nature of the materials placed in the old Kewalo Landfill
Included in these potential impacts are the occurrence and potential
contact with groundwater and soils contaminated with hazardous or toxic
substances such as heavy metals hydrocarbons radioactive materials and
volatile organic substances There is also a potential of encountering
petroleum products in vapor dissolved and or free product phases
Petroleum products have been encountered in excavations and borings
in Kakaako and in the vicinity of the old refineries and the bulk
