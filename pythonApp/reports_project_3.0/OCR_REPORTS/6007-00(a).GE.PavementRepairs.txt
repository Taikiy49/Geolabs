PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is on the northern side of Kalanianaole Highway across from
Wailupe Beach Park on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
A pavement survey was performed to evaluate the existing pavement structure.
The evaluation was used to determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction is needed
for the existing pavement structure. Based on drawings provided to us, the existing
pavement structure consists of 2 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by 6 inches of
aggregate base course material. We understand that the existing finished grades need to
be maintained for the project.
Although drilling of borings for the pavement design was recommended, we
understand we have been directed not to drill borings for the pavement design. Therefore,
the existing pavement composition and thicknesses, and the subgrade condition were not
available for determining the new pavement structural section design. Conservative
assumptions of the subgrade conditions and existing pavement section were used for the
new pavement design. In addition, we understand that we will not be held accountable for
the long-term performance of the new pavement.

OBSERVED PAVEMENT DISTRESS
Types of Distress and Severity Levels
The types of asphalt pavement distress observed includes alligator cracking, block
cracking, and patching. The types of distress and severity levels used in this report and
shown below are based on the Paver Asphalt Distress Manual by the U.S. Army Corps of
Engineers,
Alligator cracking is a series of interconnecting cracks within the asphalt concrete
(AC) surface caused by the fatigue failure of the surfacing under repeated traffic loading.
The cracking initially appears as a series of parallel longitudinal cracks. Then, as the
cracking increases, the cracks connect and form many sided, sharp-angled pieces.
Alligator cracking can be formed from reflective cracks from the underlying AC surface.

Pavement Survey Results
A pavement survey was performed along the roadways and parking area at the
project site. The types of distress and the severity of the distress based on the Asphalt
Distress Manual by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers were used in the survey.
The observed distress types observed along the roadways and parking area
included alligator and block cracking. The severity levels for these distress types ranged
from low to high severity. Asphaltic concrete patches with low severity level were observed
near the existing bath house structure. In several pavement areas, heaving of the
pavement surface caused by tree roots were observed. We understand that removal of
tree roots for the project is prohibited. The pavement survey results are summarized on
the Pavement Survey Plan, Plate 2.
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Pavement Design Methodology and Parameters
The type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement areas and the
traffic loading would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing
pavements should be performed. Distresses consisting of alligator cracking and block
cracking are failures of the asphalt concrete surface. In addition, AC patches with low
severity level were included in this category. These distressed pavement areas are
generally rehabilitated with an AC overlay or AC replacement. Since the existing finished
grades need to be maintained for the project, only AC replacement was considered. The
procedures used in determining the required AC thickness generally follow those of the
publication entitled, “Asphalt Overlays and Pavement Rehabilitation” prepared by the
Asphalt Institute.
Reconstruction of the existing pavements is usually performed for those areas
where the observed distresses are likely caused by failures in the underlying base,
subbase, and/or subgrade materials. These distresses include depression, swelling, edge
cracking and potholes. These distresses were not observed during the pavement survey
performed at the project site. Therefore, only pavement rehabilitation is recommended for
the project.
Traffic information on the roadways was not available for this report. In addition,
subsurface information for the pavement design was not available for this project. The
subgrade soil for this project was assumed to consist of clayey soils with a California
Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 5. Based on available drawings, it was assumed that the
existing pavement section consisted of 2 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by
6 inches of aggregate base course material.

Rehabilitation with AC Replacement
Based on our pavement survey, the distresses observed along the roadways and
parking area indicates that the pavement repair may consist of pavement rehabilitation
with AC replacement. Asphalt overlay analysis was performed to determine the required
AC overlay thickness and the minimum thickness of replacement AC required. The
following assumptions were used in the analysis:
Subgrade CBR = 5
Maximum Single-Axle Load = 18,000 pounds
Average Gross Mass of Heavy Truck = 25,000 pounds
Number of Heavy Trucks = 2 Daily
Design Period = 10 Years
Annual Growth Rate = 2 Percent
Based on the analysis, the calculated thickness of AC overlay required is 0 inches
and the minimum thickness of replacement AC required is 2 inches. The asphalt concrete
overlay analysis is presented on Plate A-1 in Appendix A.
Based on available drawings, the existing pavement consists of 2 inches of
asphaltic concrete underlain by 6 inches of aggregate base course material. Thicker
asphaltic concrete (about 5 inches) was encountered in the borings drilled for the design of
the first vehicular bridge structure.
Based on the above, we recommend performing pavement rehabilitation consisting
of AC replacement on the existing pavement structure. The pavement repair was
separated into two areas designated as Areas “A” and “B”. We anticipate heavier vehicular
traffic in Area “A” compared to Area “B”. For Area “A”, the existing 2 inches of asphaltic
concrete should be removed and replaced with 2 inches of new asphaltic concrete. The
existing aggregate base course layer should be re-compacted to at least 95 percent
relative compaction prior to placing the new asphaltic concrete. Should the existing
asphaltic concrete layer be thicker than 2 inches and cracks are present at the surface of
the asphaltic concrete at 2-inch depth, we recommend using a pavement reinforcing
system consisting of GlasGrid 8501 to reduce the potential for reflective cracking through
the replacement asphalt concrete. Prior to placing the GlasGrid, the existing pavement
surface should be clean, dry, and relatively even surface. In addition, cracks exceeding
Y%-inch in width should be filled with a sealant. We recommend that an allowance of
providing GlasGrid for half of the total area in Area “A” be used. For Area “B”, 1-1/2 inches
of the existing asphaltic concrete surface should be removed and replaced with
1-1/2 inches of new asphaltic concrete.
