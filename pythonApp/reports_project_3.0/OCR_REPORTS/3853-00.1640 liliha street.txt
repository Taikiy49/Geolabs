INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed new church building at 1640 Liliha Street in Honolulu on the
island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses of the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is desired to construct a three story church building and a parking lot at
1640 Liliha Street in Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The proposed structures
will be located on 34 parcel numbers 37 partial and 38 for the church building
and parcel 30 for the parking lot We understand that the proposed structure will be
supported on slab on grade and spread footings Details pertaining to the proposed
construction were not available at the time this proposal was prepared We anticipate
that minor site grading consisting of cuts and fills on the order less than 3 feet will be
needed to achieve the design grades
Special attention was given to the depth of the basalt rock formation as well as the
bearing capacity anticipated settlements and swelling potential of the near surface
adobe soils and its effects on the foundation and slab on grade construction for the
proposed project

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our geotechnical exploration was to obtain an overview of the
surface and subsurface conditions at the site to develop a soil rock data set for the
formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the design of the
building foundation and pavement sections for the proposed project In order to
accomplish this our exploration program consisted of the following tasks and work efforts
1 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig and two
operators to and from the site
2 Drilling and sampling of seven borings to depths of about 10 to 20 feet
below the existing ground surface
3 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the boring by an
Engineer Geologist from our firm
4 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field exploration
as an aid in classifying the materials encountered and evaluating their
engineering properties
5 Analyses of the field and laboratory data for the formulation of geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertinent to the proposed building and
parking lot
6 Preparation of six copies of a formal report summarizing our work on the
project and presenting our findings and recommendations
7 Coordination of our overall work on the project by a Project Engineer from
our firm
8 Quality assurance of our work and client design team consultation by a
Principal Engineer of our firm
9 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing and clerical
support
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration and Logs of Borings are presented in
Appendix A of this report Results of the laboratory tests are presented in Appendix B

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age Ice Age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava
banking against its eastern flank
The site is located on the southern flank of the elongated Koolau Volcanic Range
which forms the southeastern portion of the island of Oahu Hawaii The range was
formed from basalt lava flows originating from a rift zone roughly paralleling the existing
mountain trends During the late Tertiary and Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age deep valleys
including the Nuuanu Valley were eroded by streams During higher stands of the sea
level in the Pleistocene Epoch the valleys were infilled with alluvial sediments to the high
sea level stands Underlying these alluvial sediments are the older volcanic lava rock
formation
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located at 1640 Liliha Street in Honolulu on the island of Oahu
Hawaii The subject lots encompass a total area of approximately 17 500 square feet It
is bounded to the southeast by Liliha Street to the northwest by a residential house and
to the northeast and southwest by commercial buildings
The project site is presently used as a parking lot for the commercial buildings
adjacent to the lot Based on our field observations the project site is nearly level A
small stream runs northeast to southwest between the proposed church building and

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration at the project site consisted of drilling and sampling seven
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 7 to depths of about 10 to 20 feet below
the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration generally encountered surficial fills consisting of medium
dense sandy gravels and clayey gravels to 1 5 to 4 feet below the existing ground
surface The fills were underlain by a relatively thick deposit of medium stiff adobe clay
to about 11 feet below the ground surface Below the adobe clay hard basalt formation
was encountered and extended to the maximum depth explored of approximately 20 feet
below the ground surface
Groundwater was encountered in Boring Nos 2 and 4 at 9 and 6 feet below the
existing ground surface respectively The presence of nearly impervious clayey soils
made measurement of groundwater difficult due to the very long response time
necessary Although groundwater was not encountered in the remaining five borings
groundwater may be present from influence of the nearby stream It should be noted that
groundwater levels are subject to change due to seasonal precipitation run off and other
factors
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in our exploration are presented
on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 7 of Appendix A Laboratory testing was
performed on selected samples obtained and the test results are presented in
Appendix B

conditions encountered it is our opinion that shallow foundations consisting of spread

Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site it is our
opinion that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings
may be used to support the proposed new building An allowable bearing pressure of up
to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f may be used for the design of footings bearing on
the medium stiff clayey soils encountered at shallow depths This allowable bearing
pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient
loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Laboratory tests performed on the on site clayey soils indicate that the material
generally has a high shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations In
order to reduce the effects of the expansive clay soils resulting from moisture fluctuations
we recommend that the footing bottoms be embedded a minimum of 3 feet below the
lowest adjacent finish grade The bottom of the footing excavations should be lightly
compacted to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3853 00 Page 7
reinforcing steel and concrete Over compaction of the adobe clay soils at the footing
subgrade level should be avoided to reduce the potential for subsequent heaving of the
expansive clay soils If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of
footing excavations they should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials The over excavation should be backfilled with select granular material
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom may be
extended down to the underlying competent material
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement If foundations are designed in accordance with our recommendations we
estimate total settlements to be less than 1 inch with differential settlements on the order
of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that the soil around
footings is well compacted A coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used for footings
bearing on the in situ clay soils Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths

Slab On Grade
The project site is generally underlain by a deposit of adobe clay soils which are
considered to be highly expansive when subjected to moisture fluctuations Unless
concrete slabs supported by these soils are properly designed there is a potential for
future distress to the lightly loaded slab on grade construction resulting from the shrinking
and swelling of these soils due to changes in moisture content To reduce the potential
for structural distress resulting from the swelling of the on site expansive soils we
recommend that non expansive select capping fill material be provided for a minimum
depth of 36 inches below the bottom of the slab cushion The capping fill should also
extend beyond the slab perimeter a minimum of 12 inches
The non expansive select capping fill should be placed as soon as possible after
the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in
the Site Preparation section of this report The prepared capping fill and the underlying
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to final placement of concrete The
select capping fill material should provide a capping layer of moist soil above the
underlying potentially expansive subgrade soil thereby reducing the loss of moisture from
the clayey subgrade soils In our opinion the reduction in potential drying and wetting of
the subgrade soils should reduce the impact of shrink swell effects on the slab
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM
C 33 No 67 gradation be incorporated below the concrete slab as a cushion layer To
reduce future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to the floor coverings an
impervious moisture vapor barrier membrane is recommended on top of the gravel
cushion layer Two inches of moist fine sand may be placed above the membrane to
provide protection for the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Flexible
floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better
mask minor slab cracking The thickened edges of slab adjacent to any unpaved area
should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade Areas adjacent
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3853 00 Page 9
to the slab should be backfilled tightly against the slab with non expansive impervious soil
and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding
around the foundations
Exterior concrete sidewalks around the structure may be required for the proposed
project If sidewalks are planned around the building the capping fill required under the
slab should also extend to the outside edge of the sidewalk slabs The 36 inch capping
layer recommended under the building slab may be reduced to 18 inches for the
sidewalks if some amount of slab cracking can be tolerated Contraction joints should be
provided at intervals equal to the width of the sidewalk with expansion joints at right angle
intersections The subgrade soils beneath the sidewalks should be prepared in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the Site Preparation section of this
report
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and current grading concept we anticipate that
site preparation work for the proposed construction will consist of minor cuts and fills on
the order of about 3 feet
At the on set of earthwork the areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below any areas designated to receive fill
should be over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation
should be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or
organic soils should be properly disposed of off site or used in landscaping areas if
appropriate
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3853 00 Page 10
Existing pavement slabs or foundations that are to be demolished should be
completely removed Any over excavation resulting from the demolition should be
backfilled with compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should
be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with select
granular fill material placed in 6 to 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Utilities that are to be abandoned in place under the
proposed structure should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure
After demolition clearing and grubbing all areas beneath future slabs should be
over excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 36 inch thick capping layer of
non expansive select granular fill material below the slab subgrade level i e bottom of
gravel cushion or base course The select granular fill should extend a minimum of
12 inches beyond the perimeter of the proposed building slab on grade If sidewalks are
planned around the building the capping fill should extend to the outside edge of the
sidewalk slabs The 36 inch capping fill required may be reduced to 18 inches for the
sidewalks if some amount of slab cracking can be tolerated
Prior to placement of select capping fill the clayey subgrade should be moisture
conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and lightly compacted to
about 85 to 90 percent relative compaction Recompaction of clayey subgrades to over
90 percent relative compaction should be avoided in order to reduce the potential for
future slab distress resulting from the swelling shrinkage of the expansive adobe soils
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after preparation of the subgrade we recommend that
the soil be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks Saturation and subsequent yielding
of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill at
no additional cost to the owner Contract documents should include additive and
deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill placement to account for
variations in the over excavation quantities
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3853 00 Page 11
The on site expansive clayey soils generated from the cut areas should not be
used as fill materials below the building footprint and pavement areas They should be
disposed of properly off site or used in landscaping areas if appropriate Select granular
fill materials needed for the 36 inch capping layer should consist of non expansive
imported granular material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and
should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent Imported fill material should be
tested by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery
to the project site for its intended use
Fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office observe the site
grading operation to evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the
excavation process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those
encountered in our field exploration
Pavements
New flexible pavements are anticipated for the driveway and parking areas for the
proposed project We anticipate the vehicle loading for this project will consist primarily of
passenger vehicles light pick up trucks and occasional heavy trucks We have made

the adobe clay soils encountered in the borings Based on the above assumptions we
recommend the following pavement section be used for preliminary design purposes
Asphaltic Concrete Pavement
2 0 Inch Asphalt Concrete
6 0 Inch Basalt Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
18 0 Inch Basalt Subbase Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
26 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on a Moist Compacted Subgrade
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction If pumping of the subgrade
occurs during construction the subgrade should be proof rolled to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction After proof rolling a layer of filter fabric such as
Mirafi 500X or equivalent should be placed on the subgrade prior to placement of the
subbase and base course The subbase and base course should consist of crushed
basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Laboratory CBR tests should be performed on samples of the actual subgrade
soils encountered during construction to confirm the above recommended design section
The recommended pavement section assumes that good drainage will be provided for
areas adjacent to the pavements
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere on the
site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in
or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs extend a minimum of
2 inches into the subgrade below the base and or subbase course to reduce migration of
landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system could be
constructed to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation For long term

Utility Trench
New utility connections will be required for the proposed new building A granular
bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is
recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the trench backfill up to
about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to
reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the
pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential of formation of
voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the
pipes Improper backfill material and placement could result in settlement of the backfill
and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select fill material The backfill
should be placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not
less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground
subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet of the trench
backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the new structure should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that
gutter systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away
from the foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs
should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete
bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils

These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil

possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and
footing foundations

The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer geologist who monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 7
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Spilt Barrel Sampling of Soils
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of
borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of
the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals

Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths
Three 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples to
evaluate the swelling potential of the soils in the natural state The results of these tests
are summarized on Plates B 1
One Atterberg Limits test was performed on a selected sample of the soils to
evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presenta
tion of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One California Bearing Ratio CBR test was performed on a bulk sample of the
near surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement subgrade support
Results of the CBR test are presented on Plate B 3
