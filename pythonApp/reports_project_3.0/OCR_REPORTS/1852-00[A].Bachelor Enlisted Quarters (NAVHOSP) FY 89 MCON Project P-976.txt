INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of a seismic ground response
study for the proposed Bachelor Enlisted Quarters
Project P 976 U S Naval Base at Subic Bay on the island of Iuzon in
the Republic of the Philippines It incorporates the findings from the
geotechnical engineering exploration performed by Geolabs Hawaii for
the referenced project
Attached to this report are three appendices Appendix A presents
the method of evaluating the predominant period of an earthquake
motion In Appendix B details of the computer program PC SHAKE which
was used for the ground response study are discussed Appendix C
presents the development of the proposed ground acceleration response
spectra
The general location of the project site and its vicinity are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The layout of the proposed
building and development site is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of this study was to develop a suitable set of ground
acceleration response spectrum for the earthquake resistance design of
the proposed Bachelor Enlisted Quarters Building The scope of work
for this study which was carried out in general accordance with our
proposal of included literature review of the
geology and seismicity of the Subic Bay area interpretation of
sub soil data at the site and the development of the proposed ground
response spectra for design use
GEOLOGY AND SEISMICITY OF SUBIC BAY
General
The following sections constitute an assessment of the geologic
and seismic characteristics of Subic Bay They include an evaluation
of the regional geology seismicity of Subic Bay local geology and
subsoil conditions of the proposed building site
As the study of the seismicity of Subic Bay was not included in
our scope of work we have utilized existing reports obtained from the
Naval Civil Engineering Laboratory NCEL in California References 1
and 12 as reference for the assessment
Regional Geology
The geology of Iuzon are well published by various geological
journals in the Philippines The geologic information obtained from
these sources indicates that Central Luzon may be divided into seven

The project site is located in the western Ultramaphic Metamorphic

Major structural features of central Luzon can be divided into two

Smaller scale structures are related to individual processes of

Central Iuzon is composed of two mountain ranges separated by a
deep intermontane basin The southern Sierra Madre Range forms the
eastern segment consisting of metamorphic and sedimentary rocks
Faulting is common throughout the range The margins of the Central
Valley consist of folded Tertiary sedimentary units The entire basin
fill has been folded and faulted into broad synclines and anticlines
Faulting is also found along the boundary of the Central Valley and the
southern Sierra Madre Range The Central Valley is bounded to the
north by the Philippine Fault a major tectonic feature that forms the
southern boundary of the Cordillera Central and Carballo Mountains and
separates the northern and southern segments of the Sierra Madre
Central Luzon is mainly the product of volcanic constructional
processes Many major structural stratigraphic and lithographic
features of Central Luzon are typical of subduction zone volcanic arc
systems The eastern margin of central Luzon is formed by the Sierra

Local Geology
The project site is located within the Western Volcanic Intrusive
Province of Luzon and comprised of Recent to Pliocene Quaternary
Volcanic deposits The E W Trending San Antonio Fracture Zone is
probably the closest identified major fault zone situated about 4
miles northerly of the site at the closest point
At the project site the volcanic deposits are primrily of
pyroclastic origin and consist of lithic tuffs volcanic breccia
agglomerate and volcanic mudflows lahar believed to have been
generated by explosive eruptions from Mout Natib Mount Santa Rita and
Mount Balakibok three of the major volcanic centers which comprises
the Zambales Bataan Volcanic Highlands
Examination of the geomorphology of the area leads to the
conjecture that the site is located on a marine deltaic flat located at
the toe of a rugged mountain terrain The lowlying area was cut during
low stands of the sea by streams originating in the mountainous areas
to the east of the site
Numerous glacial eustatic sea level variations caused the
emergence and submergence of the land mass which subsequently produced
soil deposits at various levels both above and below the present sea
level
This field exploration revealed that the subsurface materials in
the study area are mainly of a low energy depositional estuarine or
lagoonal envirorment which would be encountered in a sheltered bay
area such as Subic Bay

Owing to the low energy estuarine or deltaic environment of
deposition these soft and highly compressible sediments would tend to
be coaser sand and gravel deposits close to the hillside and grade to
poorly consolidated clays and silts containing traces of organic
matter away from the hillside The loose deposits are underlain at
depths by deposits of ash flow tuffs and mudflow debris from the
eruption of Mount Natib The upper soft deposits were probably
contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea level changes
Subsoil Conditions
Based on the drilled borings the subsoil conditions at the
proposed building site can be generalized as follows
The subsoils consist of very loose silty sand which grades with
more fines with increasing distance from the hillside This
compressible layer is as deep as about 38 feet near Binictican Drive
and thins out to about 24 feet at the hillside end of the proposed
building site The loose and soft soils were underlain by medium stiff
to stiff saprolite tuff and medium dense to very dense weathered tuff
to about 28 to 53 foot depths Below this was rock formation
consisting of dense volcanic tuff
Generalized cross sections illustrating the subsurface conditions
below the proposed building site are presented on Plates 6 and 7
SETSMIC GROUND RESPONSE ANALYSTS
General
Subic Bay is located in one of the most active seismic zones of
the world It has been categorized in the Navy Design Manual NAVFAC
P 355 as a Seismic Zone 4 area on a 0 to 4 zone scale indicating the
potential for great damage due to earthquake Therefore seismic
response of the proposed building during anticipated earthquake s
should be analyzed so that appropriate strengthening measures can be
included in the design of the building
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1852 00 A Page 8
Dk No 35
The purpose of our analysis was to develop a suitable set of
ground acceleration response spectrum for use in the structural
earthquake resistant design of the building
A detailed analysis of the soil structure interaction problem
would involve the determination of the response of a structure when it
is subjected to earthquake ground motions which vary from point to
point in the soil around and underlying the structure and travel in
some unknown ways across the base of the structure This admittedly
complex problem is usually idealized for purpose of analysis so that
motions in the vicinity of the structure are considered to be due to
vertical propagation of body waves from the underlying stiffer
formation to the ground surface Reference 9
The procedure which was followed for our ground response study is
illustrated on the flow charge on Plate 8 and can he further
generalized into four general tasks as described below
i A Design Earthquake Level must be selected Based on
historical earthquake data and empirical relationships between
the length of fault and the maximm possible earthquake
generated a statistical analysis can be performed to
determine the risk probabilities for the occurrence of various
sizes of earthquakes within 200 miles from the site and their
attenuation to the site From this analysis a specific size
of earthquake with a corresponding risk level e g 50
probability of excedence in 50 years can be selected by the
user The design earthquake level can be represented by three
major parameters to be determined which include the
magnitude intensity maximum ground acceleration and
predominant period of the design earthquake

Design Earthquake Level
According to the proposed NAVFAC P 355 1 Seismic Design for
Essential Buildings in draft Reference 13 the seismic design for
new construction of facilities located in Seismic Zones 3 and 4 should
be based on a maximum earthquake occurring at the site with a 50
probability of excedence in a 50 year period The design earthquake
level meeting this requirement was obtained from previous seismicity
studies performed by the Naval Civil Engineering Laboratory in
California References 1 and 12
The intensity of the design earthquake was determined
probabilistically with the selected risk level that is 50 probability
of excedence in a 50 year period based on the NCEL total seismic risk
evaluation on Plate 9 the development of which has been discussed
earlier in the Seismicity section The result indicates that in
order to fulfill the design criteria the design earthquake would have
an intensity maximm ground acceleration on rock of 0 2g with a
return period of about 70 years at the project site
The magnitude of the design earthquake was chosen as equivalent to
the NCEL estimated 50 year maximum earthquake magnitude for the San
Antonio Fracture Zone The result indicates that the design earthquake
would have a magnitude of about 6 5 on the Richter Scale
By projecting the intensity of 0 2g and the magnitude of 6 5 onto
the acceleration attenuation curves it can be estimated that the
design earthquake may occur along the San Antonio Fracture Zone at a
distance of about 10 to 20 miles

The predominant period of the design earthquake at the site

Based on the above the design earthquake with a 50 probability
of excedence in 50 years would have a magnitude of 6 5 on the Richter
Scale a maximm acceleration of 0 2g on rock at the site and a
predominant period of 0 16 second
Earthquake Acceleration Time Record
In order to carry out the ground response study by the use of the
computer program PC the acceleration time history of the
design earthquake is required Ideally if the design earthquake had
previously occurred and the acceleration time history was recorded at
the project site then that record would provide a highly realistic
representation of the earthquake for the study However neither such
hypothetical record nor any other strong motion earthquake record was
available for earthquakes having occurred in the Philippines for the
Current study Therefore records of representative earthquakes with
similar characteristics from other countries had to be obtained and
modified with scaling factors to match the Design Earthquake

One of the most important factors controlling the characteristics
of earthquakes is the geological structure of the earthquake zone
Unlike those earthquakes occurring in the West Coast of the United
States which are of a spreading zone type the earthquakes that will
occur in the project site or in the Philippines as a whole are
believed to be of the subduction zone type which bears close
resemblance to those occurring in Japan
The representative earthquake should be comparable to the design
earthquake in the following aspects
1 Type of earthquake Subduction Type
2 Magnitude 6 5 on the Richter Scale
3 Maximum Ground Acceleration on Rock 0 2g
4 Distance from Causative Fault 10 to 20 Miles

history which was recorded at a soil site on the island of Hokkaido
Japan with a magnitude of 6 7 and maximum horizontal ground
acceleration of 0 22g at a distance of about 29 miles from the
causative fault was selected Reference 2
The records of acceleration velocity and displacement time
history of this earthquake are plotted on Plate 11
The predominant period for the above earthquake was calculated by

Scaling Factors
Two scaling factors one for the maximm acceleration and the
other for the predominant period were used to modify the
acceleration time history of the representative earthquake to
match the Design Earthquake

The acceleration scaling factor is used to scale the amplitude of
the acceleration function of the representative earthquake up or
down to produce the design maximm acceleration in rock outcrop
to match the design earthquake The predominant period scaling
factor is used to increase or decrease the time interval of the
recorded acceleration data of the representative earthquake so
that an exact match of predominant periods between the design and
the representative earthquakes can be obtained
The modified acceleration time history of the representative
earthquake which has a duration of 29 4 seconds is shown on

magnitude 6 5 occurring near the site and producing a maximm
acceleration in rock outcrop at the project site of 0 20g with a
predominant period of about 0 16 second

Development of Response Spectra
Definition of Response Spectrum
Because of its geometry and composition each structure possesses
unique values of fundamental period and damping factor If we
consider a group of different structures with different
fundamental periods and damping factors excited by the same
ground motion during an earthquake the combined influence of the
amplitudes of ground acceleration and their frequency components
will create different responses in tems of accelerations
velocities displacement stresses strains bending moments
shears and other parameters on each of these structures Graphs
showing the maximum responses induced in the entire range of such
structures i e different fundamental periods and damping
factors are called response spectra
In the present study much of the attention was directed to the
development of the acceleration response spectra at the ground
surface of the proposed building site
Computer Program PC SHAKE
The computer program PC SHAKE computes the responses in a system
of homogeneous visco elastic and horizontal layers subjected to
vertically travelling shear waves The program incorporates the
average non linear relationships between the dynamic shear moduli
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1852 00 A Page 15
Dk No 35
and damping ratios of various soils as functions of shear strain
and static properties Thus by inputting the static properties
of the soils the program can determine the dynamic properties of
the soils by using these functions with a sufficient degree of
accuracy
The program can compute the responses anywhere in the system from
a selected design motion given also anywhere in the system In
the current study the selected ground motion of the design
earthquake was assigned at the outcropping rock of the project
site and the responses are computed for the final finished grade
of the proposed building site
A more detailed description of the program PC SHAKE and an output
from a typical program run are shown in Appendix B

CONCLUSIONS
For the design earthquake of magnitude of 6 5 occurring at 10 to
20 miles from the project site it is estimated that the motion in the
outcropping rock at the site will have a maximm acceleration with a
50 probability of excedence in 50 years of 0 2g and a predominant
period of 0 16 second Modifications of a subduction zone type
earthquake from Japan gave these desired characteristics to the motion
of the design earthquake in an outcropping rock formation at the Subic
Bay site
The ground response associated with vertical propagation of the
shear waves of the design earthquake through the linear viscoelastic
soil system at the building site was analyzed by using a
one dimensional wave propagation method computer program PC SHAKE
The computed ground acceleration responses are presented
We recommend that the proposed ground acceleration response
spectra as provided herein on Plate 14 be used for the earthquake
resistant design of the proposed Bachelor Enlisted Quarters

APPENDIX A
Evaluation of Predominant Period of Earthquake Motion
From Existing Acceleration Time Records
The predominant period of a recorded earthquake motion can be
determined by performing a Response Spectrum Analysis on the recorded
data
The response spectrum analyses were performed with the subsoil
conditions at the recording stations which were approximately known for
the Japanese Earthquakes used for this project Based on the
corresponding subsoil profile the recorded motion either on rock or on
soil was deconvolved using PC SHAKE down to the base rock and the
response spectrum of the base rock was plotted The fundamental period of
the peak or the maximum spectral acceleration response was then taken as
the predominant period of the earthquake record in rock
Generally this approximate procedure is applicable for earthquake
motions recorded on relatively thin and stiff dense soil sites and the
response spectrum of the base rock will usually show a distinct easily
identified peak However for motions recorded on relatively deep and
soft loose soil sites the frequency components of the deconvolved motions
at base rock may involve considerable error and the base rock response
spectrum may show multiple and indistinct peaks not easily identified
The predominant period of the recorded motion may then be taken as a range
or a certain value within the range depending on the distance of the
recording station from the causative fault In the present analysis the
lowest values of the ranges were used as the predominant periods of the
recorded motions since the earthquake records used were recorded close to
the earthquake sources distance from causative fault less than about 25
miles
Predominant Period Amplification with Distance
The relationship between distance from causative fault and
predominant period for different magnitudes of earthquakes in rock as
shown on Plate A 1 is available for earthquakes occurring in the Western
Unites States Reference 11 Such a relationship however is not
readily available for the Japanese or Philippine earthquakes which are
typically of the subduction type
In the present study an attempt was made to plot the calculated
predominant periods of 5 recorded Japanese earthquakes using the method
mentioned above and to fit similar curves for different earthquake
magnitudes onto the graph shown on Plate A 1 The plotting as shown by
the points on Plate A 1 reveals that for distant earthquakes distance
from causative fault in excess of 25 miles there is a considerable

APPENDIX B
Computer Program PC SHAKE
The computer program SHAKE was developed by Schnabel Lysmer and Seed
at the University of California at Berkeley The PC version of this
program PC SHAKE developed and distributed by Geotechnical Software
Services was used to perform the current seismic ground response study
PC SHAKE offers basically the same options of the main frame program
SHAKE which includes the built in relationships between the dynamic
moduli and damping ratios of soils as a function of shear strain and
static properties The built in relationship for sand and saturated clay
are shown on Plates B 1 through B 4 Using these established
relationships PC SHAKE can be used to perform the ground response
analysis with an in put of the static properties of the soil layers at the
project site The strain dependence of moduli and damping ratios is
accounted for by an equivalent linear procedure based on the average
effective strain level camputed for each layer in the soil profiles
PC SHAKE computes the responses anywhere in a system of homogeneous
viscoelastic layer of infinite horizontal extent which is subjected to
vertically travelling shear waves The method is based on the continuous
solution to the wave equation adapted for use with transient motions
through the Fast Fourier Transform Algorithym The design motion can be
given anywhere in the system and new motions can be computed in any other
layer
An output from a typical PC SHAKE run for the ground response
analysis is presented from Plates B 5 through B 13
