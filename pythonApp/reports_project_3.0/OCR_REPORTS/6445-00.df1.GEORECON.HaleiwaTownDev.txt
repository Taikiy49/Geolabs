SECTION 1 GENERAL

Introduction
This geological reconnaissance and feasibility study report presents the findings
and recommendations from our reconnaissance and literature search pertaining to the
use of injection wells for the disposal of treated wastewater effluent for the proposed
Haleiwa Town Development on the North Shore of the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our literature search and field
reconnaissance and presents our geotechnical recommendations The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
1 2
Project Considerations
The proposed project involves a master plan development on about 56 acres
located on the western side of the town of Haleiwa on the North Shore of the Island of
Oahu Hawaii It is proposed to develop multi and single family dwelling units along with
commercial and community areas
Part of the development will include a community wastewater treatment facility At
present consideration is being given to using landscape irrigation as the primary method
of effluent disposal with one or more injection wells as a backup system
It is desired to map locations with potential for installation of injection wells within
the project site An in house literature search will be conducted to identify geologically
favorable areas and an on ground reconnaissance will be performed by our geologists to
identify where feasible areas might be located

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu encompassing approximately 604 square miles of land area
may be divided into 4 basic geographic provinces The provinces include
Mountains Mountains 3 Schofield Plateau and 4 Coastal Plain The
Island of Oahu was formed by the merging of basalt lavas erupted from the
two prominent Volcanoes Wai anae and Ko olau Of the two the Wai anae Volcano is
the older having breached the ocean surface nearly 4 million years ago The Ko olau
Volcano rose above sea level approximately 2 7 million years ago making it the younger
of the two volcanoes
In general the two volcanoes were built by the long term eruption of thinly
bedded a a and pahoehoe lava flows which were erupted from elongate and linear
trending volcanic vents associated with the prominent rift zones of each of the
volcanoes The Ko olau lavas banked against the lava flows of the Wai anae Volcano to
form the Schofield Plateau of the central interior portion of Oahu The Wai anae
Mountains which form the western third of the Island of Oahu contain the oldest basalt
rock formations on the Island of Oahu The Ko olau Mountains form the eastern
two thirds of the island and include the rock layers exposed along the
extending from Waimanalo to Kahuku
During the final stages of eruption and continuing well after the cessation of the
Wai anae and Ko olau eruptions the island mass began to subside thousands of feet
under the tremendous weight of the rock In addition to the subsidence of the island
the force of stream erosion worked in conjunction with the large scale fracture and
slumping of portions of the land mass to form the topography of today Between
850 000 to 70 000 years ago volcanic activity returned to the Island of Oahu with the
eruption of ash cinder and basalt lavas from vents located throughout the eastern and
southeastern portions of the island These most recent eruptions are collectively known
as the Honolulu Volcanic Series Subsequently erosion by streams and ocean waves
has continued to work on the exposed land surfaces to form the topography of the
present day

The Island of Oahu is no longer sinking beneath the sea however the effects of
the early whole island subsidence combined with the added effect of major Pleistocene
age sea level fluctuations worked to create drowned river valley systems and the
development of coral reefs The coral reefs and lithified sand dunes may be observed
to be exposed subaerially on Oahu and also buried in submarine deposits well offshore
of the island The coral and sand dune deposits were formed in shallow offshore seas
during periods of higher and lower sea levels associated with the effects of the advance
and retreat of glaciers which controlled the volume of water contained in the seas
Today some of the shallow sea coralline deposits are visible on the flanks of mountains
or buried beneath terrestrial sediments evidence of the higher and lower stands of the
sea in prehistoric time
The project site is located in the Waialua Haleiwa area The area geology is
typified by a section of recent alluvial deposits overlying older alluvial deposits and the
Koolau basalt basement rock Along the shoreline coralline reef sand dune and beach
sand deposits can be found interbedded into the alluvial deposits

Existing Site Conditions
The majority of the site is currently agricultural land Formerly the area was
used for the cultivation of sugar cane but now other crops such as corn are being
grown At the time of our field reconnaissance no crops were being cultivated
The ground surface slopes gently down in a northerly direction with ground
surface elevations ranging from about 5 feet to 20 feet above Mean Sea Level

3 1
Injection Wells
Injection wells are used for the disposal of waste fluids into subsurface
formations that are capable of accepting and transmitting sufficient volumes of fluids
For the purposes of a legal definition an injection well is considered to be a bored
drilled driven or dug hole whose depth is greater than its greatest horizontal dimension
and is used for the disposal of waste fluids
In Hawaii the construction and operation of injection wells is governed by the
Hawaii Department of Health DOH under Hawaii Administrative Rules HAR Chapter
11 23 Underground Injection Control the UIC Rules
Legal Requirements
The UIC Rules only allow for the construction of new wastewater injection wells in
exempted aquifers i e makai of the UIC Line An Approval to Construct from DOH
is required before construction may commence After the well is constructed
information must be submitted to DOH in order to obtain the final Permit to Operate
which is generally valid for a period of 5 years
Our review indicates that the project site does overlie an exempted aquifer
however the Rules have a further caveat that if there are artesian conditions
present there must be a minimum separation of 50 feet from the bottom of the
injection well to the artesian formation Our review indicates that the project site
does overlie the Haleiwa Kawailoa aquifer complex which is considered to be an
artesian system Therefore there will be restrictions on the maximum depths of
injection wells for the project Our present estimate of the depth to the top of the
artesian aquifer is on the order of about 100 feet below the existing ground surface
in the vicinity of the proposed wastewater treatment facility

Koolau Basalt QTkl
This unit consists of the flank and caldera flows of the original Koolau Volcano
Flank flows tend to be thinly bedded flows of tholeiitic basalt in both the pahoehoe
and a a forms Caldera filling flows tend to be more thickly bedded and more
massive in structure Included in this unit are areas that were mapped previously as
members of the Kailua Volcanic Series It may have a mantle of cobbles and
boulders or residual soils overlying the top of the basalt rock formation In addition
it is anticipated that various types of volcanic rock sub components such as clinker
layers and lava tubes also may be encountered within the basalt rock formation
In terms of permeability this unit has the highest potential for the disposal of waste
water however since the area is considered to be an artesian basin construction
of injection wells using this formation would be prohibited
Coral Reef Formation Qcrs
Being in a subtropical region Hawaii does not have the extensive coral reefs as
found in more tropical regions Reefs here are built of a combination of corals and
calcareous algae and the growth pattern is discontinuous Coral reef limestone
usually is encountered only on the forereef areas or where an emerged reef has
become consolidated or cemented through chemical weathering Locally the term
coralline formation is used to describe reef materials that appear to be in their
original growth or deposition position

Wave action and biological activity can break coralline deposits into clastic
materials When these materials are encountered usually as a sandy gravel or
gravelly sand they are referred to as coralline detritus because the deposit is
largely detrital fragments of coral and calcareous alga
In general coral formations can be highly permeable and are capable of
transmitting large volumes of water The existing
Treatment Plant located to the south of the site uses coral formation for the
disposal of effluent
Our field reconnaissance indicates that the coralline formation within the project
limits have been mantled by about 4 feet of what appears to be alluvial or fill soil
Numerous fragments of coralline materials were observed in and along the existing
corn field in the southern part of the site and in the bottoms of the ditch along
Achiu Lane and the ditch crossing the old cane haul road
Coral fragments and possible formation were also observed along Paalaa Road
however this was felt to be too far from the proposed wastewater treatment plant
location to be practical
Dune Sand Qd
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea level was lower than it is at present and
expanses of coral reefs were exposed to the prevailing winds This resulted in the
formation of sand dunes in coastal areas
Although sand dunes are relatively permeable the fine grained nature of the sand
makes the formation susceptible to clogging from suspended solids in the effluent
stream In addition the locations of dune sands in the project vicinity are a
considerable distance from the proposed treatment plant location Therefore dune
sands are not considered to be practical for locating injection wells
Recent Beach Sand Qbd
This unit represents beach sands along the present coastline Similar to dune
sands these sands are relatively permeable but are also susceptible to clogging

Recent Alluvium Qa
These soils are generally the result of sedimentation in a very low energy
environment such as an estuary or bay Frequently the soils assigned to this unit
accumulated very rapidly under water and might not have been exposed to air
Recent alluvium generally is found within some stream channels and bordering
some stream banks at the mouths outlets of streams and within broad valley
floors and basin features surrounded by hills These soils also may be found in
proximity to swamp and tidal flat environments
Recent alluvial soils have low permeability and are poorly suited for effluent
disposal through injection wells

Injection Well Siting
Based on our review it appears that the most feasible locations for siting
injection wells would be the areas mapped as Coral Formation on the Generalized
Geological Map Plate 2 In particular the area between the old cane haul road and the
existing ditch We recommend focusing the exploration for injection well locations in
that area as shown by the locations on Plate 2 In general we recommend conducting
a series of short term injection tests in test borings in order to develop an estimate of
the range of permeability within the coral areas
Once a potential well site has been selected more detailed testing should be
conducted to further refine the design of the proposed effluent disposal well Based on
a telephone conversation with of the Hawaii Department of Health
Safe Drinking Water Branch they would most likely require physical proof of the depth
to the underlying basalt formation i e drilling to the basalt
