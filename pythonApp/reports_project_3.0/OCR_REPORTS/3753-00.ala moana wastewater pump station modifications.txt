2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
The Ala Moana WWPS property is located on the southern flank of the Koolau
Shield Volcano and its geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to
glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of
the Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions in the Kaka ako area are
dominated by fossil Pleistocene age coral algal reef deposits laid during high stands of the
sea that were intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic
section in this area is Koolau Basalt
About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that
sea level rise the valleys in the project area were drowned or submerged In the last
10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and
marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft lagoonal sediments along the coast in the
areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways including the alluvial channels that cut
through the coral reef ledges
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought
the Kaka ako area to its present form It should be noted that many of the resulting fills are
of poor quality in terms of supporting large structural loads

Based on our field exploration the project site is generally blanketed by a surface
fill layer extending to a depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface Below the
surface fill layer loose to very loose lagoonal deposits consisting of silty and clayey sands
and gravel interbedded with occasional layers of very soft silty clays were encountered to
depths of about 25 to 30 feet below the existing ground surface It should be noted that
the loose to very loose lagoonal deposits encountered at the site are highly sensitive to
vibrations and other disturbances The loose to very loose lagoonal deposits would turn
into a viscous fluid temporarily known as liquefaction when subjected to vibrations
and or disturbed and would result in appreciable settlements at the ground surface The
highly compressible lagoonal deposits were underlain by coralline materials of varying
densities with occasional dense or cemented layers extending to the maximum depth
explored of approximately 61 5 feet below the existing ground surface

3 1 Jet Grouting
Jet grouting is a technique utilizing a special drill bit and injection monitor with radial
horizontal nozzles to produce stabilized soil cement columns The jet grouting technique
is a process that produces soil cement columns by pumping neat cement grout slurry
through horizontal jets injected at high pressures The horizontal jets of cement grout
slurry cuts and mixes the surrounding in situ materials with the neat cement slurry grout as
the drill bit is slowly rotated and withdrawn to form a soil cement column

Jet Grouted Columns
Based on experience with similar projects in the area the jet grouted column
foundations should have a minimum diameter of 36 inches In addition the grout
mix should have a specific gravity of at least 1 5 and should be able to produce
jet grouted columns with a 7 day unconfined compressive strength of at least
200 pounds per square inch psi and a 28 day unconfined compressive strength of
at least 500 psi The final mix design and jet grout parameters of the jet grouted
column foundations should be determined by a specialty contractor experienced in
soil stabilization by jet grouting in similar subsurface conditions The specialty
contractor will be required to produce jet grouted column foundations meeting the
minimum diameter unconfined compressive strength and minimum embedment

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of about 41 5 and
61 5 feet below the existing ground surface for our current field exploration The
approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with hollow stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX E
Field Permeability Testing
APPENDIX E
Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the in situ soils was evaluated by performing a constant head
permeability test in Boring No 2 The test was performed at a depth of about 15 feet
below the existing ground surface
The constant head permeability test was performed by pumping water into the
borehole until the water surface stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient
period of time The water height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide
data upon which to base the calculation of the subsoil permeability The test result of the
field permeability test is presented on Plate E 1
