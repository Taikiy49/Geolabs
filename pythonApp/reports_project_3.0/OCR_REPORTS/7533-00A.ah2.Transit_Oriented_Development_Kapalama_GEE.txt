SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering 
exploration performed in support of the feasibility study and Rough Order of Magnitude 
(ROM) cost estimate for the transit-oriented development at the Kapalama site in the 
Honolulu area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general site vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes the findings from our limited field exploration and 
laboratory testing, and presents our preliminary geotechnical recommendations derived 
from our analyses for the proposed development. The findings and preliminary 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is generally bounded by the Kapalama Canal to the south, Hart Street 
to the west, City Square Shopping Center to the east, and various commercial warehouse 
buildings to the north. The mauka portion of the project site consists of one and two-story 
commercial buildings. The portion of the project site makai of Kalani Street consists of an 
industrial warehouse and two-story commercial offices.  
Based on the information provided, we understand it is desired to evaluate the 
feasibility of the transit-oriented development within the Department of Hawaiian Home 
Lands’ (DHHL) properties near the future Kapalama Transit Station. We understand that the 
project site will consist of the development of two 18 to 21-story mixed-use buildings. 
We understand the purpose of the current study is to provide baseline information for 
project feasibility and ROM cost estimate. Due to preliminary nature of the feasibility study, 
a very limited field exploration was conducted at the project site. It should be noted that the 
subsurface profile interpreted by a single boring will likely yield significant uncertainty due to 
the anticipated ground variation across the project site. Therefore, a detailed field 
exploration program should be conducted during the project design phase to obtain

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Koolau Volcano reached 
the end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. When the building of 
the Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes came to an end, only a slight embayment 
existed on the southern coast of Oahu. 
The project site is generally located on the coastal plain of Southern Oahu. The 
coastal plain was built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano, which forms the eastern 
two-thirds of the Island of Oahu. The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of 
alluvium derived from erosion of the volcano, interbedded with coral reefs and associated 
deposits. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. Most of the coastal plains were developed during the 
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly. As the glaciers grew and 
advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below 
the present stands of the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water 
became available such that the sea levels rose to above its present level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition caused the top of the basaltic lava flows to 
be highly weathered and mantled by residual soils. During the Kaena stand of the sea (at 
approximately +90 feet Mean Sea Level [MSL]) of the Pleistocene Epoch, a delta of silt, 
sand, and gravel built out into the embayment near the project site. Therefore, the project 
site is generally underlain by deposits consisting of calcareous sediments and lagoonal 
deposits.  
With the urbanization of the area, man-made fills were placed to reclaim marshy 
areas and lagoons. The fill was placed upon lagoonal deposits consisting of silty sands

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our preliminary field exploration and engineering analyses, the following

3.1 
Deep Foundation Design Alternatives 
Based on the information provided, we understand the proposed project will 
consist of developing two mixed-use buildings ranging from 18 to 21 stories in height. 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions described above and the moderately high 
building loads of the mixed-use buildings, we recommend considering the following deep 
foundation alternatives for the foundation support of the planned development. 
1. 
Augered Cast-In-Place (ACIP) Piles 
2. 
Precast, Prestressed Concrete Driven Piles 
3. 
Cast-in-Place Concrete Drilled Shafts 
Lateral load analyses for the deep foundation systems should be performed during 
the project design phase when detailed foundation design layout information is available. 
As mentioned above, near-surface soils with potential contamination were encountered 
at depths between about 2.5 and 7 feet below the ground surface. An environmental 
engineer should be engaged for detailed assessment, mitigation, and treatment. Our 
recommendations herein do not consider for contaminated soil impact to the foundation 
construction of the selected alternative. 
3.1.1 Augered Cast-In-Place (ACIP) Piles 
To support the moderately high axial structural loads anticipated for the mixed-use 
buildings, we recommend using a deep foundation system, such as augered 
cast-in-place (ACIP) piles. Augered cast-in-place piles are constructed by rotating a 
continuous flight hollow-stem auger (generally 10 to 24 inches in diameter) into the 
ground until either the planned pile tip depth or termination (refusal) criterion is 
achieved. At the termination depth, grout of a specified consistency is pumped into

the augered hole through an orifice located in the drilling bit at the tip of the 
hollow-stem auger. The auger is slowly withdrawn from the hole and the pile shaft is 
formed as a continuous flow of grout replaces the soil removed by the auger. After 
the auger stem is removed, the reinforcing steel cage is then inserted into the grouted 
hole (through the fluid grout) within the set-time window allowed by the grout. 
The ACIP pile system can be installed with relatively higher production rate and less 
noise/vibration that may have potential impact to the adjacent existing structures 
supported on a shallow foundation system. In addition, it does not require temporary 
steel casing to protect the borehole from caving. 
The ACIP piles would derive vertical support primarily from skin friction between the 
foundation element and the medium dense coralline detritus, soft coral formation, 
and stiff alluvial clay deposits present at the site. In general, the end-bearing 
component of ACIP piles is a small component of the total compression load capacity 
and has been discounted in our analyses. 
Based on our engineering analyses, the new mid-rise buildings may be supported on 
18 and/or 24-inch diameter ACIP piles. In general, ACIP piles installed in groups 
should have a minimum center-to-center spacing of three times the pile diameter to 
avoid reduction in the vertical load capacity due to group action and to facilitate 
drilling of the ACIP piles. Our preliminary recommendations pertaining to the ACIP 
pile capacities are presented in the following table. It should be noted that pile lengths 
are based on an assumed bottom of cap elevation at about 5 feet below the existing 
ground surface.

3.1.2 Precast, Prestressed Concrete Driven Piles 
As a deep foundation design alternative, we believe precast, prestressed concrete 
driven piles may be considered to support the new building structures. It should be 
noted that the use of driven pile foundation may pose some significant design and 
construction issues for the project development due to the presence of relatively thin, 
hard coral ledges anticipated at various depths and the moderately high structural 
load demand. The presence of relatively thin, hard coral ledges anticipated at various 
depths across the project site would result in the potential for driven piles to “hang 
up” on the relatively thin, hard coral ledges and the potential need to increase the 
size of the pile caps substantially to accommodate the additional replacement piles. 
The concrete driven pile system can also be installed with relatively higher production 
rate and minimal spoil disposal as they are considered displacement piles. However, 
the pile driving operation may generate excessive noise and vibration that may have 
an impact to the adjacent existing structures supported on a shallow foundation 
system.

3.1.3 Cast-in-Place Concrete Drilled Shafts 
Cast-in-place concrete drilled shaft foundation system may be also considered as a 
deep foundation design alternative. Drilled shaft foundations are highly effective in 
soil formations that can stay open after drilling until concrete placement. 
Unfortunately, the loose sands under water at the project site likely will not stay open 
following drilling. Therefore, partial-depth temporary casing and/or the use of drilling 
fluids (polymer slurry) likely will be necessary to maintain the integrity of the drilled 
hole during drilled shaft installation. This would increase the construction complexity 
and costs for the foundations. In addition, the anticipated shallow groundwater level 
at the project site also may pose some construction difficulties because proper 
observation of the sides and bottoms of the drilled shafts may not be possible. 
One of the primary advantages of the drilled shaft foundation system is that 
construction would not generate appreciable noise and vibrations normally 
associated with the other deep foundation systems, such as driven piles. In addition, 
construction of the drilled shafts will involve generating spoils that will need to be 
disposed of. 
Therefore, the drilled shaft foundation system can be installed with higher 
compressive load capacity and less noise/vibration disturbance to adjacent existing 
structures, but slower construction speed and significant spoil disposal. The use of 
temporary steel casing or drilling fluids (polymer slurry) through intermittent loose 
zones in the compressible lagoonal deposit may be required to prevent the drilled 
hole from caving. 
The cast in-place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support primarily from 
skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding coralline and alluvial deposits 
present at the site. The end-bearing component of the drilled shafts has been

Field Exploration 
The subsurface condition at the project site was explored by drilling and sampling 
one exploratory boring, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 102 feet 
below the existing pavement surface. The boring was drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with 4-inch O.D. solid-stem augers and PQ coring tools. The boring was drilled at 
the approximate location shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and textural 
examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for 
Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Boring, Plates A-1.1 through A-1.3. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch O.D. modified 
California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the logs of boring indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
It should be noted that the efficiency of the safety hammer used for this project was 
measured in May 2015 and the resulting energy transfer ratio is shown on the Logs of 
Boring. The soil consistencies and densities presented in the boring logs have been 
adjusted based on the energy transfer ratio, as appropriate.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the 
field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive 
strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on the Logs 
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses.  RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting 
drilling-induced fractures or breaks.  If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs 
of Boring as RQD = 50%.  Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality 
of the rock, based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and 
Minerals."

Seismic Shear Wave Velocity Test 
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling of the subsurface materials at the project site 
was performed using Seismic Cone Penetration Testing (SCPT) equipment. The purpose 
of the seismic shear wave velocity profiling of the subsurface materials was to analyze the 
seismic design considerations more closely for the project. Shear wave velocity testing using 
seismic cone penetration test equipment was performed at the boring location, designated 
as Boring No. 1, on the Site Plan (Plate 2). The seismic shear wave velocity profiling was 
performed at various depths extending to a maximum depth of about 103.1 feet below the 
existing ground surface. 
To conduct the seismic shear wave velocity test in the boring, the test boring was 
advanced utilizing rotary coring methods to the maximum depth of the boring. Log of the 
materials encountered in the boring are presented in the Logs of Boring in Appendix A. After 
the boring was advanced to the maximum depth of the borehole, the bored hole was 
backfilled with 0.25-inch diameter coated bentonite pellets. The temporary casing from the 
coring operations was used as a tremie pipe to place the bentonite pellets starting from the 
bottom and advancing upward. When the bentonite pellets are in contact with the water in 
the borehole, the pellets start to hydrate slowly. As the bentonite pellets hydrate, they swell 
and soften. The probe was then pushed through the softened bentonite extending to a depth 
of about 103.1 feet below the existing ground surface using seismic cone testing equipment 
(SCPT). 
The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10-ton steel 
electronic subtraction cone with an apex angle of 60 degrees and a projected surface area 
of 1.55 square inches (10 square centimeters) into the bored hole. The cone carries a 
uniaxial horizontal accelerator geophone to detect the arrival of a shear wave generated and 
propagated from the ground surface. The seismic measurements were made when the 
SCPT had stopped and a shear wave was sent into the subsurface. A shear wave was 
generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a switched hammer. The 
propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone was measured at each 
discrete depth interval. The vector difference of these depths divided by the time difference 
for the shear wave to arrive at various depths given provided the average shear wave 
velocity over the depth interval. 
The shear wave velocities measured and the weighted average seismic shear wave 
velocity calculated for the top 100 feet of the soil profile at the boring location are presented 
on Plates B-1.1 through B-1.4 in Appendix B.

APPENDIX C  
APPENDIX C
Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate
sample depths.
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths, and
graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate C-1.
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) including one hydrometer test
(ASTM D422) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Graphic presentations of the
grain size distributions are provided on Plate C-2.
Three Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) were
performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the in-situ
soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the applied
confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test results and the
stress-strain curves are presented on Plates C-3 through C-5.
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected soil sample to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are
presented on Plate C-6.
One Consolidation test with time rates (ASTM D2435) was performed on a sample
of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the
materials encountered. Results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate C-7.
