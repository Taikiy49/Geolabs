REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
KAI Hawaii, Inc. 
Page 3

Hawaii • California 
early Pleistocene in age.  After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations.  These series are referred 
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series.  The project site is located at the southern flank of the 
Koolau Mountain Range. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea.  When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations.  When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level.  
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and in the heads of valleys, the erosional 
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls 
and are transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as 
colluvium.  Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial 
processes become dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as 
alluvium. 
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The Wailupe Family Housing site is located on the northern side of Kalanianaole 
Highway across from Wailupe Beach Park on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The existing 
Wailupe Bridge 4 (culvert) runs beneath the road in front of Quarters A and into the 
existing drainage canal located in the middle of the property. A grated drain inlet structure 
is located at the eastern end of the culvert behind the roadway curb. At the western end 
of the culvert, an underground utility pipe crossing the culvert is supported on a concrete 
pillar. The culvert is located approximately 100 feet north of the existing First Vehicular 
Bridge.  
The culvert is constructed of a reinforced concrete top slab, rock masonry walls 
and a concrete bottom slab. The culvert is paved over with asphaltic concrete within the 
roadway. It is our understanding that the culvert was damaged and large spalls with 
exposed reinforcement were observed. Steel plates were placed on top of the asphaltic 
concrete pavement to protect the damaged culvert.  
Based on the topographic survey map provided and our field observation, the site 
is relatively flat with existing ground surface elevations ranging from about +9.2 to 
+9.5 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) within the area of the existing Wailupe Bridge 4 (culvert).

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the project site are assumed to be similar to those 
encountered in our borings presented in our Geotechnical Engineering Exploration Report 
entitled “First Vehicular Bridge, USCG Wailupe Family Housing, Honolulu, Oahu, Hawaii”, 
dated February 29, 2008. The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the borings encountered a surface fill layer, about 6 to 7 feet thick, 
underlain by alluvial deposits to about 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface, the 
maximum depth explored. The fill layer consisted of medium stiff to very stiff silty clay with 
sand and gravel, and medium dense silty coral sand with gravel. The alluvial deposits were 
composed of very soft to hard clays and silts with sand and gravel, and medium dense to 
dense clayey/silty gravel with sand.  
Groundwater levels were encountered in the borings at depths of about 7.0 and 
7.7 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of the field exploration. The 
groundwater levels measured generally correspond to approximately +1.5 and +1.3 feet 
MSL, respectively. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to tidal fluctuation, rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and 
other factors.

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the information provided, the proposed project will realign the existing 
Wailupe Bridge 4 (culvert) with a new box culvert and a new drain inlet structure to replace 
the existing culvert and drain inlet structures. Based on the subsurface soil conditions 
encountered during our previous field exploration conducted for the adjacent First Vehicular 
Bridge, the proposed box culvert and drain inlet structures may be supported on a shallow 
foundation system. Detailed discussions and recommendations for foundation design and 
earthworks are presented in the following subsections. 
Drainage Structure Foundations 
Based on the available subsurface information, an allowable bearing pressure of 
up to 2,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may be utilized for design of the drainage 
structures bearing on the medium stiff and medium dense fill materials anticipated at the 
project site. To provide uniform bearing support for the new drainage structures, we 
recommend providing a minimum 6-inch thick layer of aggregate base course compacted 
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction below the bottom of the drainage 
structures.  
Foundation subgrades should be recompacted to a firm and unyielding surface 
prior to the placement of the aggregate base course materials.  If soft/loose subgrade

Site Grading 
We anticipate that the site grading work for the project will generally consist of 
demolition and removal of the existing box culvert and drain inlet structures and cuts and fills 
for the installation of the new box culvert and drain inlet structures. At the on-set of earthwork, 
the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared. Vegetation, 
construction debris, deleterious materials, and other unsuitable materials should be removed 
and disposed of properly off-site or in a designated area to reduce the potential for 
contamination of the excavated materials with the spoils.  
After clearing and grubbing, the exposed subgrades should be scarified to a depth of 
about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
The drainage structure excavations will need to be properly backfilled to reduce the 
potential for settlement at the ground surface. The excavated on-site soils or imported select 
granular fill materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and clay lumps and 
rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension may be used as backfill up to 
the finished subgrades.  
The select granular fill materials should be well-graded from coarse to fine, with 
particles no larger than 3 inches in their largest dimension. The material should also contain 
between 10 and 30 percent fines (particles passing the No. 200 sieve). Select granular fill 
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
Backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. In areas where there will be pavements, the 
upper 3 feet of the backfill below the pavement finished grade should be compacted to no 
less than 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry 
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and discussions submitted herein are based, in part, upon information 
obtained from the available field borings. Variations of subsurface conditions beyond the 
test borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become

transition. Water level data from the test borings were measured at the times shown on the 
graphic representations and/or presented in the text herein. These data have been 
reviewed, and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, it must be 
noted that groundwater level changes may occur due to seasonal precipitation, surface 
water runoff, and other factors.
