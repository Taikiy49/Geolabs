This memorandum summarizes our understanding of the project and presents
our preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations for the design of the
proposed BWS Kalihi Corporation Yard, Replacement of Underground Storage Tanks
project in the Kalihi area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.
It should be noted that our preliminary recommendations presented herein are
based on our recently completed borings. Laboratory testing of the recovered samples
is currently being performed. A detailed summary of our findings and recommendations
will be contained in our geotechnical engineering report. The final report should be
consulted when it becomes available.
Project Considerations
Based on the information provided, the project site is located at the Board of
Water Supply (BWS) Kalihi Corporation Yard in the Kalihi area in Honolulu on the Island
of Oahu, Hawaii. We understand that the project consists of the replacement of the
existing 6,000 gallons and 2,500 gallons underground storage tanks (USTs) with a new
aboveground storage tank with enclosure. The existing USTs are located in the central
portion of the corporation yard. The new tank site will be located to the south of the
existing tanks.
Based on available drawings, the bottoms of the holding concrete pads and tanks
are about 13 and 11 feet below the existing ground surface for the 6,000-gallon
unleaded fuel and 2,500 diesel fuel tanks, respectively.
Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to a maximum depth of about
26.5 feet below the existing ground surface.

In general, our borings encountered a surface fill layer about 5.5 feet thick 
consisting of medium dense to loose sandy gravel underlain by residual soils and saprolite 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 26.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The residual soils encountered generally consisted of stiff to very stiff silty clay 
and clayey silt. The saprolite encountered varied from medium dense gravelly and silty 
sand to very stiff silty clay with some highly weathered basalt rock. 
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. It should be noted that the groundwater level will fluctuate based on rainfall, 
surface water runoff, seasonal precipitation, and other factors.  
Underground Fuel Tank Removal 
Based on the as-built information provided, the existing 6,000-gallon and 
2,500-gallon USTs are double wall fiberglass tanks and are tied down to reinforced 
concrete base slabs at depths of about 13 and 11 feet below the existing ground surface, 
respectively. We understand that the existing USTs will be removed and replaced with a 
new aboveground storage tank. We recommend the UST removal activities should 
follow the guidance presented in the “Technical Guidance Manual for UST Closure & 
Release Response,” prepared by the State of Hawaii, Department of Health. All Federal 
and State regulations pertaining to UST removal should be followed and monitored by 
an environmental consultant retained by the owner.  
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered during our field exploration, we 
believe that a temporary cut slope for open-cut excavation may be practical provided 
the space for the cut slope is available. For the relatively thin surface fill layer consisted 
of medium dense silty gravel, a temporary cut slope may be designed with a slope 
inclination of one and one-half horizontal to one vertical (1.5H:1V) or flatter.  For the 
underlying residual soils and saprolite consisted of stiff to very stiff silty clay and clayey 
silt, a temporary cut slope inclination of 0.5H:1V to near vertical may be used for a short 
duration, provided the slope is protected from moisture variations. If the excavation has 
to remain open for a longer duration or if the excavation has to be vertical cuts, the 
excavation walls will have to be protected with the use of proper shoring during 
construction.  
One possible method of shoring would be to use interlocking sheet pile shoring. 
The sheet piles should be driven with a suitable hammer to a sufficient depth. Due to 
stiff to very stiff clayey soils encountered in the drilled borings and the frequent 
presence of cobbles and boulders in residual soils and saprolite, it should be noted that 
installation of the sheet pile shoring to the desired depths at the site may require 
predrilling of the sheet pile locations.  Another possible shoring method would be to use 
a soldier piles and lagging system.

The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for the pit excavation 
and the adequacy and safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s site 
superintendent, who should be required to continuously present on-site during 
excavation and construction works, will have the best opportunity to promptly observe 
changing conditions during construction, such as unforeseen subsoils, unexpectedly 
high groundwater table, inappropriate construction sequence or techniques, etc., which 
may affect shoring stability. 
Upon removal of the USTs, pea gravel backfill, concrete slabs, sand bedding 
layer, and trench liner, the exposed subgrade should be properly recompacted prior to 
backfilling with select granular fill materials. Select granular fill should consist of 
non-expansive granular material such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. 
The material should be well-graded from coarse to fine, with particles no larger than 
3 inches in their largest dimension. The material should also contain between 10 and 
30 percent fines (particles passing the No. 200 sieve). Select granular fill should have a 
laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or more and should have a 
maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.  
The select granular fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum water content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
In areas where there will be pavements, the upper 3 feet of the backfill below the 
pavement finished grade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative 
compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum 
dry density. Compaction should be accomplished by rollers without vibratory actions or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed to compact the fills. 
Shallow Footing Foundations 
We understand that the proposed project consists of an aboveground storage 
tank with an enclosure. Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions encountered at 
the site, we recommend using shallow spread footing/concrete slabs-on-grade with 
thickened-edge (reinforced concrete pad) foundations to support the new fuel tank. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used for the design of the concrete pad bearing on recompacted on-site soils and/or new 
compacted fills needed to achieve the design finished grades. The bearing value 
recommended is for dead-plus-live-loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for 
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.

In general, the bottom of the pad should be embedded a maximum of 18 inches 
below the lowest adjacent exterior grade. Foundations located next to retaining walls, 
other foundations, utility trenches, or easements should be embedded below a 45-degree 
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench (or retaining 
wall). Alternatively, the footings should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of 
the utility lines or the bottom of the retaining wall. 
The footing/slab-on-grade finished subgrades should be compacted to a minimum 
of 90 percent relative compaction. Soft or yielding ground conditions, if encountered, at the 
exposed subgrade level should be over-excavated until firm materials are encountered 
and backfilled with compacted select granular fill materials for uniform support.  
If the foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the foundations to be less than 
1 inch.  
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by a combination of frictional 
resistance between the base of the foundation and the bearing material and by passive 
earth pressure developed against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of the 
footings. A coefficient of friction of 0.35 may be used for footings bearing on compacted 
aggregate base course materials. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be 
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 330 pounds per square foot per foot of 
depth (pcf). The passive earth pressure value assumes the concrete footings will be cast 
neatly against the medium dense on-site soils or new compacted fills. Otherwise, the soils 
around the footings should be well-compacted (minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction). Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive pressure resistance in 
the upper 12 inches below the finished grade should be neglected. 
It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the footings should be backfilled 
tightly against the edges of the slabs with relatively impervious soils as soon as the 
concrete is cured or practical. These areas should also be graded to divert water away 
from the slabs to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs. 
Pavement Design 
We understand that both flexible (asphaltic concrete) and rigid (Portland cement 
concrete) pavements may be utilized for the proposed project.  In general, we anticipate 
the vehicle loading will primarily consist of passenger vehicles, pick-up trucks, and 
maintenance trucks.  
We have assumed the pavement subgrade soils will be similar to the surface 
soils generally encountered during our field exploration. On this basis and the assumed 
traffic loading, we recommend using the following pavement sections for preliminary 
design purposes.

The pavement subgrades should be prepared in accordance with the 
recommendations presented in the “Site Grading” section herein. Where minor 
shrinkage cracks are observed after subgrade preparation, we recommend thoroughly 
moistening the soil to close the cracks prior to recompacting. Saturation and 
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor 
drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
well-compacted fill. 
Paved areas should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to 
carry surface water off the site. Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site 
during or after construction. 
Site Grading 
We anticipate that the site grading work for the project will generally consist of 
minimal cuts and fills except for the excavation and backfill for the removal of the 
underground fuel tanks. At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading 
limits should be thoroughly cleared. Vegetation, construction debris, deleterious materials, 
and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site or in a 
designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials with 
the spoils.  
After clearing, the exposed subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 
8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, 
and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing should be over-excavated to 
expose firm natural ground, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
imported fill materials. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly 
disposed of off-site. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices 
for over-excavation and engineered fill placement to account for variations in the 
over-excavation quantities.

Imported fill and backfill materials, if required, should consist of non-expansive 
select granular fill material such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The 
materials should be well-graded from coarse to fine, with particles no larger than 3 inches 
in their largest dimension. The material should also contain between 10 and 30 percent 
fines (particles passing the No. 200 sieve). Select granular fill should have a laboratory 
CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when 
tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
Fill and backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. In areas where there will be 
pavements, the upper 3 feet of the fill/backfill below the pavement finished grade should 
be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers 
to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density 
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.
