INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Kona Liberty House at the Liliuokalani Trust Subdivision
in the area of Kailua Kona of the District of North Kona on the island of Hawaii The
general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed
project These recommendations are intended for design input of foundations site
grading excavation and backfill and pavements only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of
this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central part of the island The lava formation encountered at the
site appears to be of pahoehoe flow which spread and ponded as it approached the
ocean Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3371 10 Page 6
internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in
pahoehoe lavas These cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state
and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as
blisters and pockets Lava tubes intra flow cavities are formed when molten lava
drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for
a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller
in horizontal extent
Basalts are considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions Water is transmitted through
the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and
along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they
serve as the major water transmission features

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling seven borings to
depths ranging between about 20 and 25 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered a very thin surficial layer of gravel
over volcanic rock consisting of hard to very hard basalt with occasional weak layers
from the ground surface to depths ranging from about 20 to 25 feet below the existing
ground surface The basalt encountered ranged from slightly to severely fractured and
ranged from unweathered to moderately weathered The basalt formation encountered
included dense and vesicular porous basalt
Cavities were encountered at different depths within the basalt formation in five
of the seven borings drilled at the site As previously mentioned cavities are commonly
encountered in the pahoehoe flows In general the cavities encountered were located
at various depths ranging from about 1 to 13 feet below the existing ground surface
The vertical heights of the cavities encountered were on the order of about to 1 feet
GEOLABS HAWAI

It should be emphasized that the vertical and lateral extent of these cavities are highly
variable
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field
exploration However groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on factors such as
seasonal rainfall groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other factors
For detailed description of the soil and rock conditions encountered please refer
to the Logs of Borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 7 presented on Plates A 1
through A 7 of Appendix A
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions at the project site
consist generally of basalt rock formation with frequent cavities Based on our
evaluation of the subsurface conditions with respect to the proposed development the
principal geotechnical engineering considerations for the proposed retail store
development include the following
1 foundation support of the proposed building structure
2 probing and grouting of voids and cavities beneath foundations for the
proposed building and
3 grading of the proposed project site and pavement design for the access
roads and parking areas
Based on the grading plan for the project we understand that fills on the order
of about 4 feet will be required to raise the existing ground to the finish grade at the
GEOLABS HAWAIi

proposed building location Therefore we believe that a shallow foundation system
bearing in the compacted fills may be used to support the proposed building
Alternatively due to the relatively shallow depth to the basalt rock surface footings may
be extended down to bear directly on the basalt rock formation to utilize the higher
bearing pressures
Due to the presence of cavities as encountered in our borings we recommend
that a program of probing and grouting the footing locations be implemented during

cut slope during construction to evaluate if portions of the cut slope need to be flattened
to accommodate the exposed conditions
Based on the grading plan provided we believe that the parking area will be
located on fill areas We understand that the fill materials will be generated from the

Building Foundations
Based on the grading plan provided we understand that the entire building will
be located on fills of about 4 feet thick Therefore we believe that shallow spread
and or continuous footings bearing in the compacted fills may be used to support the
proposed Liberty House store and related facilities Alternatively due to the relatively
shallow depth to basalt rock the footings may be extended down to bear directly on the
in situ basalt formation to utilize the higher bearing pressure of the basalt rock
formation
For footings bearing in the compacted fill materials an allowable bearing
pressure of up to 4 000 p s f may be used for design An allowable bearing pressure
of up to 6 000 p s f may be used for footings bearing directly on the basalt rock
formation These bearing values are for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
A program of probing and grouting the footing locations will need to be
implemented during construction This is further discussed in the Foundation Probing
section of this report

Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade Where footings are to bear directly on the basalt rock
formation an embedment depth on the order of 4 to 6 feet may be required for the
footing to bear on the basalt rock formation Where footings are to be located adjacent
to below grade structures or utilities the footings should extend to a depth below an
imaginary 45 degree plane projected upward from the bottom of the below grade
structure If the footings are designed in accordance with the recommendations
presented herein total settlement of footings is estimated to be on the order of less
than 1 inch with differential settlements on the order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
For footings bearing directly on basalt rock a coefficient of friction of 0 6 may be used
The coefficient of friction should be reduced to 0 4 for footings bearing on compacted
fill materials Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f for
footings embedded in compacted fill Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be neglected
Slab on Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed Liberty
House store In general slabs on grades which are not subjected to vehicular traffic
such as the interior floor slab should be supported on a cushion fill layer consisting of
4 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation The cushion fill layer
would provide more uniform support of the slab and would also serve as a capillary
moisture break A vapor membrane should be incorporated above the capillary break
to reduce potential damage to moisture sensitive floor coverings by infiltration of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3371 10 Page 12
moisture It is advisable to place a 2 inch layer of moist fine sand above the vapor
membrane to protect the membrane and to aid in concrete curing
Where slabs on grade will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the loading
dock slab a 6 inch layer of compacted base course is recommended for more uniform
support in lieu of the 4 inch cushion fill and 2 inch sand The base course should
consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 For design of structural slabs a
modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per cubic inch p c i may be used for the
compacted base course Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across
slab joints which are subjected to vehicular traffic The thickened edges of slabs
adjacent to any unpaved area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade

Foundation Probing
As previously indicated our field exploration encountered cavities in five of the
seven borings drilled at this site The cavities encountered in the borings ranged from
about to 1 feet in height To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support
resulting from collapse of cavities below a footing we recommend that a program of
cavity probing and grouting be conducted during construction We recommend that the
probing and grouting program be conducted for all footings located in cut areas and for
footings located in fill areas where the fill thickness is less than 5 feet below the bottom
of footing
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3371 10 Page 15
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each spread
footing location and on 10 foot centers for continuous footings The probe holes should
be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned
bottom of footing If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the
probing operation additional probes should be drilled at closer spacings to help
delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and
any cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to
moderate pressures A typical sand cement grout would consist of 1 part sand 1 part
cement and 0 7 parts water
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at this site
we recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and
grouting operation is complete Since there is a potential for encountering cavities
and or voids at this site the contractor should be made aware that a longer lag time
between probing grouting operations and foundation construction should be allocated
in the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation
of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the
presence of cavities and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess
grout take and or changed conditions are observed

Fill and Backfill Materials
The fill and backfill materials to be used as general fill should consist of well
graded granular materials less than 12 inches in largest dimension The on site
cut material if less than 12 inches in maximum dimension may be used as
general fill or backfill materials Fill materials with boulders greater than
12 inches in diameter may be used as rock fill provided that they are placed at
least 4 feet below the finish subgrades In general placement of boulders in
fills should be in accordance with the procedures recommended in the Boulder
Disposal subsection of this report
As previously mentioned we recommend that the upper 4 feet of fill below the
finish subgrade consist of a choker fill layer In addition the engineered fill
recommended beneath footings and slabs on grades should also conform to the
requirements of the choker fill layer The choker fill should consist of well
graded granular material of 6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines
to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass If the excavated
rock material does not contain sufficient fines to produce a well graded material
off site cinders or non expansive soil may be mixed into the excavated material
to increase the percentage of fines to provide a well graded material for
compaction and increase the stability of the fills Any imported material if
necessary should be observed and or tested by Geolabs Hawaii for its
suitability prior to being transported to the site

Geophysical Survey
A geophysical survey was conducted using a ground penetrating radar GPR
system along generated grid lines of the proposed connecting road and building pad
location on The GPR system was manufactured by Geophysical
Survey Systems Inc consisting of an SIR System 3 control unit with a 500 Megahertz
antenna set The grid lines were generally spaced about 20 and 30 feet apart
Ground penetrating radar utilizes impulse radar technology to obtain real time
profiles of the subsurface materials A radar frequency pulse is transmitted into the
subsurface and when it encounters an interface between two materials with differing
electrical properties it is reflected back to a receiver on the surface In a relatively
homogeneous medium this reflection is noted as an anomaly
The reflected pulse is intercepted by the receiver and then electronically
processed and printed on a graphic recorder producing a record similar to a marine
depth recorder chart As the transmitter and receiver antenna array is moved across
the ground surface a real time graphic record of the subsurface is generated Markers
can be inserted into the data print to reference distances as the array is moved
providing a means to physically locate anomalies
Ground penetrating radar is generally considered applicable to the location of
subsurface voids and buried structures Depth of penetration and data resolution are
dependent upon soil type moisture content electrical properties antenna frequency
and power
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3371 10 Page 24
The use of ground penetrating radar is limited by the presence of conductive
materials within the profile being surveyed This is particularly true if the conductive
materials are in the upper or surface portions of the subsurface profile Conductive
materials attenuate the electromagnetic signal and interfere with penetration of the
radar pulses
Based on the results of this survey no clear indications of major lava tubes and
cavities were not found at the test locations at the time of our field survey Based on
these results it is our opinion that major lava tubes and cavities do not appear to be
present within the proposed road alignment and building pad area However it should
be noted that the survey results may be influenced by the site conditions subsurface
soil conditions weather conditions and the operation of the GPR system at the time
the survey was conducted

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
seven borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 7 at the approximate locations
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of our borings range from about 20 to
25 feet below the existing ground surface They were drilled using a truck mounted
drill rig equipped with rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 7
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
