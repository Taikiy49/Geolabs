2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The project site is located in an area dominated by recent stream and lagoonal
deposits overlying formations of volcanic origin During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age
sea levels fluctuated and reached as much as 300 feet below the present sea level
Stream transported sediments consisting mainly of boulders cobbles and gravel were
deposited at a lower sea level As the sea level began to rise the capacity of the streams
was reduced and finer sands and silts were subsequently deposited At some point the
accumulation of sediments could not keep up with the rising sea level and the area
became flooded As a result very fine grained sediments were then deposited in marine
or estuarine environments Eventually the sea level stabilized while the sedimentation
process of fine grained sediments and shell beds continued Sediments with shell beds
are characteristic of near shore deposits Subsequently organic sediments which are
peaty and contain plant fibers and fragments accumulated and are indicative of marshy
conditions Land development within the last 100 years has brought the site to its present
form Many of the resulting fills over the marsh deposits are of poor quality in terms of
supporting heavy structural loads

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
seven borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 7 extending to depths ranging
between about 25 5 and 31 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate
locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

approximately 4 to 5 feet of surface fills consisting of very stiff to hard silty clays Below
the upper fill crust our borings generally encountered a relatively thick marsh deposit
consisting of soft to very soft organic clays extending to the maximum depth explored of
approximately 31 feet below the existing ground surface
Shallow groundwater conditions were encountered in the drilled borings at depths
between about 4 5 and 12 5 feet below the existing ground surface These depths
generally correspond to approximately Elevation 2 5 to 4 0 feet MSL Due to the
proximity of the project site to Wailani Stream and Pearl Harbor it is likely that the water
levels measured in the borings during our field exploration were affected by seasonal
precipitation storm surge conditions and tidal influences

3 1 Drilled Shaft Foundations
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances the proposed
new light pole structures may be supported by a foundation system consisting of
cast in place concrete drilled shafts Based on the anticipated structural loads and the
subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we recommend that drilled shafts with a
minimum diameter of 60 inches and an embedment length of not less than 26 feet below
the bottom of the concrete pedestal be used for the 60 foot high new light poles planned
for the softball field area The new backstops at the softball and baseball fields should
also be supported by drilled shafts with a minimum diameter of 60 inches and an
embedment length of not less than 5 and 8 feet below the design finished grades
respectively
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally
from skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable compressive
load capacity of up to 15 kips per shaft may be used for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces
Lateral loads imposed on the light pole foundations generally may be resisted by
resistance of the shaft soil system to deflect Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is
a function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable
deflection at the top of the shaft and induced moment in the shaft Based on the
information provided each drilled shaft for the 60 foot high light pole will be subjected to a
lateral load of 8 98 kips and an overturning moment of about 484 kip feet at the top of the

3 3 Concrete Walkways
We understand that walkways compliant with the Americans with Disabilities Act
ADA are planned for the proposed ballfield renovation project Based on our field
exploration and laboratory test results the walkway alignment may be underlain by a
surface layer of moderately expansive clayey soils Unless slabs on grade constructed
above these expansive soils are properly designed there is a potential for future distress
to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils
due to changes in the moisture content To reduce the potential for structural distress
resulting from swelling of the subgrade soils we recommend that a minimum 12 inch thick
layer of non expansive select granular material or aggregate subbase be placed below
the new concrete walkways

3 4 Utility Trench
We envision that new electrical duct lines will be required to service the proposed
new lighting system In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or
S4C is recommended under the electrical duct lines Free draining granular materials
such as clean sand or S4C should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about
6 inches above the duct lines to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce
the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It
is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper backfill material and placement procedures could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
seven borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 7 extending to depths ranging
between about 25 5 and 31 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were
drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers The
approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 7
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are

Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples to aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Two one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples of
the near surface clayey soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the subsurface materials
The results of the tests are summarized on Plate B 1
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct
shear test are presented on Plate B 2
