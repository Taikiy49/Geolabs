INTRODUCTION
Maintenance and repair work for the pavement at portions of North
Nohili Road and the nearby parking apron at the Pacific Missile Range
Facility at Barking Sands Kauai are planned In order to facilitate
the proposed repair work the existing pavement distress condition and
the appropriate methods of restoration have been evaluated
This report presents the results of our soil engineering
investigation performed for the above mentioned project The general
location of the project site and its vicinity are shown on the Project
location Map Plate l
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1544 00 Page 2
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of this investigation was to explore the general soil
conditions below the pavement areas in order to identify and categorize
the various distress conditions of the asphalt surfaced pavement in order
to provide reconmendations for general repair procedures of the
pavement It is anticipated that only localized repairs would be made at
this time and therefore a detailed pavement design analysis to
determine an overlay or new pavement is not included in our analysis
The scope of our investigation which was performed in general
accordance with our proposal of March 25th included Grilling and
sampling of twenty one 21 borings field inspection of the pavement
surface laboratory soil testing engineering analysis utilizing the
field and laboratory data and report preparation
The boring logs and laboratory test results are presented in the
appendices of the report
SITE CONDITION
The project site is located at the Pacific Missile Range Facility
Barking Sands on the island of Kauai Hawaii
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 3
The portion of the existing North Nohili Road under investigation
is approximately 10 400 feet long and paved with asphaltic concrete
Pavement cracks were encountered in most parts of the roadway especially
at road intersections where severe cracking conditions were generally
observed In general the cracks run parallel and perpendicular to the
direction of the roadway
The subject aircraft parking apron is trapezoidal in shape
measured approximately 500 feet long with widths of 350 and 500 feet It
is presently covered with both asphalt and concrete pavements The
parking apron normally serve helicopters and cargo planes with occasional
use by jet fighters Cracks and other forms of distress on the pavenent
surface were observed during our investigation
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface soil conditions below the asphalt pavement at North
Nohili Road and the parking apron were explored by drilling and sampling
twenty one 21 shallow borings to depths of about 5 feet below the
existing ground surface The borings were drilled both in areas which
indicated distress as well as in the good areas The approximate
locations of these borings are shown on the Site Plan Plates 2 through
8
In general our borings encountered 1 5 to 3 5 and about 4 inches
of asphaltic concrete at the North Nohili Road and the parking apron
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 4
respectively Underneath the asphaltic concrete 6 to 8 inches of dense
well compacted base course consisting of crushed basalt rock was
encountered The subgrade soil which was encountered to the full depth of
all borings consists of dense to very dense silty sand
Groundwater was not encountered during our exploration
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATION
Based on the results of our field exploration and the laboratory
testing the subgrade soil consists mainly of dense to very dense silty
sand which appears to be competent for the support of the pavement for
both the roadway and the parking apron area The base course material
which consists mainly of well compacted crushed basalt rock is also
considered to be in good condition Therefore it is our opinion that
the current pavement distress is a surface phenomenon which is probably
caused by aging and weathering of the existing asphaltic surface
TYPES OF PAVEMENT DISTRESS
Based on the results of our field inspection most of the pavement
distress conditions at North Nohili Road and the parking apron can be
classified into five 5 different types These types of pavement
distress together with their possible causes are presented as follows

1 Block Cracking
Interconnected cracks that divide the pavement into approximately
rectangular blocks were encountered at both project locations The
blocks range in size from approximately 1 by 1 foot to 10 by 10
feet In most of the blocks the asphalt was found to have
hardened significantly The block cracking is normally caused by
shrinkage of the asphalt concrete and daily temperature cycling
which results in daily stress strain cycling
Some typical block cracks at the parking apron and the roadway are
shown on Photos 1 and 2 in Appendix C The cracking condition is
normally in low to medium severity level
2 Longitudinal and Transverse Cracking
Longitudinal cracks which run parallel to the pavenent s
centerline and transverse cracks which extend across the pavement
at approximately right angles to the pavement centerline were also
encountered These cracks may be caused by shrinkage of the
asphaltic surface due to hardening of the asphalt In the case of
longitudinal cracks it may also be caused by a poorly constructed
paving lane joint
Although seme spalled cracks were encountered during our
inspection the severity level of the distress condition at both
project locations is in general low to medium as shown on Photos
3 and 4

Oil Spillage
Spilling of oil fuel or other solvents which cause deterioration
or softening of the pavement surface is evident at the parking
apron area Along the North Nohili Road this form of distress was
not encountered
Photos 5 and 6 show two locations at the parking apron where
possible oil spillage occurred
Ravelling and Weathering
Ravelling and weathering which are the wearing away of the
pavement surface caused by the dislodging of aggregate particles
and loss of asphalt or tar binder can be seen in most parts of the
project locations Photos 6 and 7
The severity level is in general low along the roadway However
probably because of the combined effect of oil spillage the
severity level of the pavement distress at the parking apron is
higher

REPAIR RECOMMENDATIONS
The subgrade and base course are both in good condition There
fore repair work will probably be limited to those performed on the
bituminous layer of the pavement
Both methods and materials for the repair of bituminous surfaces
vary considerably with local conditions such as weather and availability
of material and equipment But in general the principle of bituminous
work should remain the same Presented below are some general
recommendations regarding the repair work at the project site
1 All bituminous repairs should be undertaken in warm and dry
weather
2 For cracks of less than 1 8 inch width and surface weathering one
of the following three basic types of seal coats can be used
a Fog Seal
A fog seal can be a light application of asphalt or tar
emulsion diluted with water or a light application of a
liquid asphalt When a diluted emulsion is used it will
flow into the fine cracks and form a thin coating on the
pavement surface
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 8
Slurry Seal
A slurry seal is a mixture of slow setting asphalt emulsion
crushed fine aggregate about 1 8 inch mineral filler and
water The materials are combined in proportions to produce
a mixture of slurry consistency and applied to the pavement
surface
Sand and Aggregate Seal
Sand and aggregate seals may be used to rejuvenate and seal
the surface of weathered asphalt pavements and to seal newly
constructed porous asphalt pavement These seals are
constructed in the same manner as single course bituminous
treatments with the selected bituminous material applied at
the rate of 0 1 to 0 4 gallon per square yard depending on
the aggregate size and condition of the surface to which it
is being applied
Cracks 1 8 inch or wider may be sealed individually with a prepared
joint sealer Large cracks may also be filled with a bitumincus
sand mixture using a slurry seal mixture or a hot mix sand asphalt
Permanent repair of ravelling surfaces can usually be accomplished
by the application of a sand or aggregate seal coat

5 Permanent repair for areas subjected to continuous fuel spillage
consists of removal of the damaged pavement and replacement with
portland cement concrete or a tar rubber overlay
CONCLUSION
Our investigation indicates that the subgrade and base course
material at the study portion of North Nohili Road and the nearby
parking apron are in general in good condition The current pavement
distress conditions are due to the deterioration of the bituminous
surface caused by hardening of the material weathering and possible oil
spillage Repair work can therefore be limited to those performed on
the pavement surface
