EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is located at the driving range for Klipper Golf Course at Marine 
Corps Base Hawaii in Kaneohe on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
Based on our field observations and the preliminary plan provided, the replacement 
of the poles and netting area is relatively flat with existing ground surface elevations 
ranging between about +4 and +5 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) according to Google Earth. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling two borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 20 and 20.3 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by surface 
fill material consisting of very stiff clayey silt extending to depths of about 1.5 to 4.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface.  The fill layer was underlain by saprolite, consisting of 
medium stiff to very stiff sandy silt and extending to 14.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. Below the saprolite, basalt rock formation was encountered and extended to the 
maximum depth explored of approximately 20.3 feet below the existing ground surface. 
We encountered groundwater in the drilled borings at depths of about 5.0 to 
6.0 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration.  The 
groundwater levels measured generally correspond to about Elevations +4.0 to +5.0 feet 
MSL. It should be noted that groundwater levels could fluctuate depending on tidal 
fluctuations (due to the proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean), seasonal 
precipitation, groundwater withdrawal and/or injection, and other factors.   
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration methodology and the Logs of Borings 
are presented in Appendix A. Results of the laboratory tests performed on selected soil 
samples obtained from our field exploration are presented in Appendix B. 
FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Nine 50-foot high poles with three guy anchor blocks will be installed to replace the 
existing pole and netting system at the project site. Based on the loading demands and 
timber pole dimensions provided by the project structural engineer and subsurface soil 
conditions encountered during our field exploration, we recommend embedding the poles 
a minimum of 10.5 feet. Detailed discussions and recommendations for foundation design 
are presented in the following sections.

Pole Foundations 
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances, we 
recommend using a pole embedment depth of no less than 10.5 feet below the design 
finished grades. Ideally, a hole larger than the pole diameter should be drilled to the design 
embedment depth. Then, the pole should be placed into the hole with grout or CLSM used 
to fill the annular space. However, we understand from the pole/netting designer that this 
type of backfill would accelerate deterioration of the wood poles. Therefore, the poles 
should be placed in holes or excavations large enough to properly compact the backfill 
around the poles.  
The pole foundation would derive vertical support principally from skin friction 
between the pole foundation and the surrounding soils.  A net allowable compressive load 
capacity of up to 4.5 kips per pole may be used for dead-plus-live loads and may be 
increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as wind or seismic forces. 
Lateral Load Resistance 
The lateral load resistance of pole foundation is a function of the stiffness of the 
surrounding soil, the stiffness of the pole, allowable deflection at the top of the pole 
foundation, and the induced moment in the pole foundation. The lateral load 
analyses were performed using the program LPILE-plus for Windows, which is a 
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference, laterally loaded deep foundation 
program originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin. The program 
solves for deflection and bending moment along a deep foundation under lateral 
loads as a function of depth. The analysis was carried out with the use of non-linear 
“p-y” curves to represent soil moduli. The lateral deflection was then computed 
using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths.

our analyses are summarized in the following table.

Foundation Settlement 
Settlement of the pole foundation will result from elastic compression of the pole 
and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the soils encountered at the 
site. We anticipate that the total settlements of the pole are estimated to be less 
than 0.5 inch. We believe that a significant portion of the settlement will be elastic 
and should occur as the loads are applied. 
Construction Considerations 
Pole foundations consist of 15.75-inch diameter wood poles with a minimum 
embedment of 10.5 feet in order to accommodate the lateral load and overturning 
moments.  However, the performance of the pole foundation will depend significantly upon 
the contractor's method of construction and construction procedures.  In our opinion, the 
following conditions would have an impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft 
foundations. 
Medium Stiff to Very Stiff Clayey/Sandy Silt Soils 
Based on our field exploration, the proposed new pole locations are generally 
underlain by medium stiff to very stiff sandy/clayey silt extending to depths of about 
14.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  Based on our laboratory test results, 
the surface clayey/sandy silt soils encountered at the site exhibit a high natural 
moisture content which would prohibit proper compaction. Therefore, we 
recommend that non-expansive select granular fill materials be used to fill in the 
hole around the 15.75-inch wood pole. The non-expansive select granular fill 
recommended for the pole foundation should consist of crushed basalt or coral. The 
materials should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain less than 20 percent particles 
passing the No. 200 sieve.  The materials should have a laboratory CBR value of 
20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in 
accordance with ASTM D1883. The non-expansive select granular fill materials 
should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these recommendations prior to 
delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
Workmanship 
The lateral load resistances of the pole foundations depend, to a large extent, on 
the contact between the wood poles, select granular fill and the surrounding soils.  
Therefore, proper construction techniques are important. The contractor should 
exercise care in drilling or excavating the holes and in placing select granular fill 
into the holes. The non-expansive select granular fill materials should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts 
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction.

must be noted that groundwater level changes may occur due to seasonal precipitation, 
surface run-off, and other factors.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling two borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 20 and 20.3 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Rock 
cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown 
on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in 
general accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification.  Results of 
the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth.  
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Three Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested.  The test results are 
presented on Plates B-2 through B-4. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-5. 
One Modified Proctor (ASTM D1557) was performed on the near-surface soil to 
determine the maximum dry density and optimum moisture content of the soil.  The test 
results are presented on Plate B-6. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) test (ASTM D1883) was performed 
on a bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils.  The results of the test are presented on Plate B-7.
