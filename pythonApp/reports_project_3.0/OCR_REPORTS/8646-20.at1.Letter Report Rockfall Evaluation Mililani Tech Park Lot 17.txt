REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age. As volcanic activity at Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows from 
Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming the Schofield Plateau. 
The project site is located within the Schofield Plateau. The Schofield Plateau 
was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against the already eroded 
slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch. The dips of the lava beds

plateau have undergone in-situ weathering to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are 
characterized by the reddish color of the soil. 
This in-situ weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils that 
grade to saprolite with increasing depth. In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty 
material, while residual soils are more clayey. Both residual and saprolitic soils are 
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The residual and saprolitic soils 
grade to a basaltic rock formation with increased depth. Spheroidally weathered 
boulders within the residual and saprolitic soils are typical in the Schofield Plateau.  
GENERAL SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is generally located on a plateau at the southern end of Kahelu 
Avenue within Mililani Technology Park in Mililani on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The 
plateau is covered with heavy vegetation consisting of Albizia trees, lower-lying trees, 
and California grass. Based on the grading plan provided by Castle & Cook Homes 
Hawaii, Inc. dated April 9, 2023, the plateau is generally level, approximately 1,600 feet 
long by 400 feet wide. Existing elevations on the plateau range from about +858 feet 
Mean Sea Level (MSL) on the northeastern end to about +800 feet MSL on the 
southwestern end of the site.  
There are relatively steep slopes to the east, west, and south of the plateau; 
however, it should be noted that a detailed topographic survey map of the slopes was 
not available at the time of this study. Therefore, we used an available topographic map 
of the area created by the United States Geological Survey (USGS). Based on the 
USGS general topographic map, the east and west ends of the project site slope down 
into natural gullies with inclinations ranging from one-and-a-half horizontal to one 
vertical (1.5H:1V) to about 1H:1V. The south end of the plateau generally slopes down 
towards existing residential structures and the existing Wikao Street at inclinations 
ranging from about 2H:1V to 3H:1V. These slopes surrounding the plateau are generally 
covered by medium to large shrubs and medium size trees, such as Ironwood. The 
general site layout is presented on the Site Plan, Plate 2. In addition, the USGS General 
Topographic map used during this study is presented on Plate 3. 
Based on our field observations, the slopes surrounding the project site are 
heavily vegetated with a few saprolitic outcrops and boulders. Photograph No. 1 
(Plate 4.1) shows the overall condition of the slopes surrounding the Lot 17 plateau.

firm on June 2, 2023 to evaluate the existing site conditions. It should be noted that due 
to the heavy slope vegetation and steepness of portions of the slopes, we were unable 
to access and observe some portions of the slopes. However, some of the existing 
slope conditions were observable via drone. Site reconnaissance photographs depicting

Access to the project site was gained from Kahelu Avenue through a previously 
cleared path along the plateau. Our reconnaissance began along the western-facing 
slope at the project site. Based on our observations and the USGS topographic map of 
the area, the western slope has an inclination of about 1H:1V and slopes down toward 
the undeveloped valley on the west side of Lot 17. As shown in Photograph No. 2 
(Plate 4.1), the western-facing slope is heavily vegetated, which made on-the-ground 
observations of the slope conditions difficult. Therefore, a drone was used to capture 
aerial views of the slope conditions. Based on aerial views and on-the-ground 
reconnaissance, we did not observe rock outcrops or boulders that could be potential 
rockfall hazards to the existing residential structures. In addition, based on the geometry 
of the slope, potential rockfall hazards would likely fall away from the residential 
structures and into the gully.  
In general, the south-facing slope was more accessible due to relatively less 
vegetation density and a flatter slope inclination of about 2H:1V to 3H:1V. On the 
western edge of the south-facing slope, we encountered a saprolitic rock outcrop and 
well-embedded boulders. As shown in Photograph Nos. 3 through 5 (Plates 4.2 and 
4.3), the observed boulders in this area were generally about 2 to 6 feet in diameter, 
embedded, and on a section of the slope that is facing away from the existing 
residential structures. In addition, the saprolitic outcrop encountered in the area 
appeared to be well-embedded, and potential rock blocks that could be broken away 
from the outcrop would be about 6 inches in diameter or less (Photograph Nos. 6 and 7, 
Plates 4.3 and 4.4). Therefore, we believe that these boulders and outcrops present low 
rockfall hazard potentials. It should be noted that based on the property boundaries 
indicated on the General Plan dated April 9, 2023, the boulders and outcrops 
encountered on the south-facing slope are located within the adjacent property owned 
by Launani Valley Community Association (TMK No.:  9-5-002: 56). 
Moving further down the slope towards Wikao Street, the slope face becomes 
highly vegetated with ironwood trees down to near the existing residential structures. In 
addition, we encountered several benches on the slope extending down to the street 
level (Photograph Nos. 8 and 9, Plates 4.4 and 4.5). The benches along the slope were 
heavily vegetated and appeared to be uniform, with approximate dimensions of about 
10 feet tall and 5 feet wide. Based on our on-the-ground reconnaissance and aerial 
views from the drone, we did not observe rockfall hazards along the benched areas of 
the slope.  
An overall photograph of the existing conditions of the eastern slope face 
(captured via drone) is presented in Photograph No. 10, Plate 4.5. In general, the 
eastern-facing slope is vegetated with California grass and Albizia trees near the top of 
the slope and medium to large size trees and shrubs further down the slope. Based on 
the general USGS topographic map of the area, the eastern-facing slope inclines down 
into the valley at an inclination of about 1.5H:1V. Generally, the ground surface

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our site reconnaissance, we believe that most of the eastern and 
western-facing slopes below the project site incline toward the undeveloped valleys, 
which minimizes the risk to lives and properties during a potential rockfall event. In 
addition, the southern-facing slope was observed to have a flatter inclination angle of 
about 2H:1V at the upper sections of the slope and previously graded benches at the 
lower slope sections that extend down to Wikao Street. We believe that flatter slope 
inclination coupled with benches that are created along the slope reduces the potential 
of rockfall hazards along the southern-facing slope. 
During our field observations, we encountered a few core stone boulders along 
the southern and eastern-facing slopes that appear to be partially embedded and facing 
away from existing residential structures. Therefore, we believe that the boulders and 
outcrops encountered during our site reconnaissance present a low rockfall hazard 
potential. In addition, based on our field observations and the project plans provided, we 
believe that the observed potential rockfall hazards are located outside the Castle & 
Cook property boundaries and within the properties for Launani Valley Community 
Association (TMK No.: 9-5-002: 056) and Hawaii Technology Development Corp. 
(TMK No.:  9-5-002: 057). 
In general, we did not observe potential rockfall hazards within the project and 
Castle & Cooke property boundaries. The few outcrops and boulders observed with low 
to moderate rockfall hazard potential are located within the boundaries of adjacent 
properties owned by others. Therefore, we believe it may be appropriate to inform the 
property owners of the potential hazards within their properties.
