REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be of the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of the late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age When the building of the Waianae and Koolau
volcanoes came to an end only a slight embayment existed on the southern coast of
Oahu
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3365 10 Page 7
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition caused the top of the basaltic lava
flows to be highly weathered and mantled by residual soils During the Pleistocene
Epoch the Kaena stand of the sea level at approximately 90 feet MSL a delta of silt
sand and gravel built out into the embayment near the project site Agricultural land
development and reclamation projects within the last century have brought the project
site to its present form
The project site is located at the southern juncture of the Koolau and Waianae
Volcanic Ranges and has a complex geologic history Based on our previous
exploration at the project site the basic stratigraphic sequence consists of Tertiary Age
Koolau Basalt at depths ranging from about 40 to 100 feet below the surface Partially
consolidated older alluvium consisting of silts and clays with sands and gravels overlie
the basalt Recent unconsolidated stream deposits harbor sediments marine marshy
silts and clays extend from 2 to 9 feet above the older alluvium up to near the
groundwater level Fill materials were placed on the recent stream deposits up to the
existing ground surface
SITE DESCRIPTION
The Kolea Cove at Pearl Harbor project is located in the Waipio area in the
District of Ewa on the island of Oahu Hawaii The project site is an arc shaped strip
of land encompassing an area of about 18 acres adjacent to and north upslope of

the Middle Loch Shoreline The project site is bounded by Waipahu High School to the
north undeveloped lands to the east Middle Loch Shoreline to the south and Waipio
Point Access Road to the west
In general the project site is accessible via an existing cane haul road and dirt
road along the Middle Loch shoreline extending from Waipio Point Access Road to the
east side of the project During our exploration the northern portion of the site was
vegetated with tall trees thick brush and tall grasses Along the eastern perimeter
relatively deep erosional gullies and near vertical bluffs were noted during our field
exploration To the south and west of the project site sparse vegetation with huge
stockpiles of off site borrow soils were noted
Based on our observations the site generally slopes down toward Middle Loch
on the south with about 50 feet of vertical relief The overall slopes at the site range
from near vertical bluffs at the north to near level ground at the proposed building sites
along the shoreline on the south side The site generally may be described by three
distinct areas
1 the Terraces to the north
2 a Slope Break Transition zone and
3 undocumented fills over swampy low lying areas
The Terraces are river delta deposits which were deposited by a higher sea level
stand and have now been cut by erosion to form bluffs facing Middle Loch The
Terraces are generally located near the northern perimeter of the site and range in
elevations from about 50 to 59 feet MSL
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3365 10 Page 9
The Slope Break Transition zone is located between the Terraces and
undocumented fills This zone generally consists of in place river delta deposits and
some fills on the upper half of the site Based on our observation and records the sites
for Building Nos 1 through 4 9 through 12 and 17 through 19 appear to be in the
Slope Break Transition zone The parking areas behind Building Nos 9 through 11 and
17 through 19 appear to be in the Terraces and Slope Break Transition areas where
cuts on the order of about 20 feet are anticipated
The area of undocumented fills is generally located on the lower half of the site
and is formed by placement of fills over previous swampy lowlands These fills were
placed over soft and marshy soils in the low lying areas which were generally located
within the areas bounded by the sites for Building Nos 5 through 8 and 13 through 16
Prior to the undocumented fill placement a working platform consisting of about 18
inches of No 3B Coarse gravel wrapped in a filter fabric Mirafi 140N was placed
above the marshy soils Following the construction of the working platform to support
construction equipment fill placement continued through to the present Based on our
records we believe that the fill materials generally consist of various off site borrow
materials generated from different project sites The off site borrow materials were
generally not properly moisture conditioned and were placed with minimal compaction
effort without the observation and testing by a geotechnical engineer Therefore these
fills are termed undocumented fills The sites for Building Nos 5 through 8 and 13
through 16 appear to be in the undocumented fill area

Based on our field exploration and laboratory testing the significant geotechnical
engineering considerations for the proposed project are the vertical and lateral extent
strength characteristics and compressibility of the undocumented fill soils Based on
the preliminary site plan it is likely that the site for Building Nos 5 through 8 and 13
through 16 are underlain by undocumented fills The thickness of the undocumented
fills may vary from about 4 to 12 feet and consist of silts and clays with some sands

unconfined compressive strengths and are considered to be slightly to moderately
expansive Because of the potentially variable nature of the undocumented fill soils

and replaced with compacted engineered fills Therefore footings should be supported
on at least 4 feet of compacted engineered fill to reduce potential differential
settlements between footings The existing fill soils may be reused as compacted

Over Excavation and Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation trees debris rubbish
construction wastes and other unsuitable materials should be removed and
disposed of properly off site to reduce potential contamination of the excavated
materials
As previously indicated over excavation of the existing fills of at least 4 feet
below the existing grades or footing subgrades whichever is deeper will be
required beneath the site of Building Nos 5 through 8 and 13 through 16
including related parking areas The excavated material may be reused as fill
material provided it contains no organic or other unsuitable materials with particle
sizes exceeding 3 inches in maximum dimension In general we recommend
that the excavated materials be tested and approved for its intended use by our
office Unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
The exposed surface following stripping and or over excavation should be
scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2
percent above optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density

W O 3365 10 Page 14
If zones of soft loose soils are encountered at the subgrade levels of the over
excavation during grading further over excavation may be required to expose
firm soils This should be further evaluated by Geolabs Hawaii in the field
Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over
excavation and engineered fill construction to account for variations in the over
excavation quantities Since there is a potential for additional over excavation

Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade soils we

areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill
Fill and Backfill Materials
In general the on site materials may be used as fill materials provided that they
contain no organic material or other unsuitable material with particle sizes
exceeding 3 inches in maximum dimension Fill and backfill materials should be
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above optimum moisture placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction Any boulders or cobbles larger than 3 inches
in maximum dimension should be separated from the excavated material and
disposed of off site
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3365 10 Page 15
Backfills behind retaining walls should consist of non expansive granular
materials compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction Over
compaction of the retaining wall backfill would exert additional earth pressures
on the walls and should be avoided
Where required import materials should consist of non expansive materials
Import fill materials should consist of granular materials such as crushed coral
basalt crusher waste or cinder and should not contain organic or other
deleterious materials In general the import fill should be well graded with no
particles greater than 3 inches in size and should have a laboratory CBR value
exceeding 12 with swell value not exceeding 1 percent when prepared near the
optinum moisture Import fill should be moisture conditioned to above the
optinum moisture content and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction Import fill materials should be tested and approved for its intended
use prior to hauling to the project site
Cut Slope
In general cuts on the order of about 20 feet or less are anticipated during the
grading work Based on our exploration a cut slope ratio of two horizontal to
one vertical 2H 1V or flatter may be used Cut slopes exposing the expansive
clayey soils should be protected from drying to reduce the potential for instability
due to shrinkage cracks Therefore the slope face should be adequately
protected from development of shrinkage cracks It may be prudent to flatten cut
slopes to 2 H 1V or flatter In the event over cutting occurs consideration
should be given to leaving the slope rather than attempting to backfill the slope
to the design grade with sliver fills If backfilling of an over cut slope is
necessary keying and benching requirements should be implemented

top of slopes and geotextile fabrics be placed on the slope to reduce the
potential for erosion

Set Back Distance
In general for cut or fill slopes greater than 10 feet in vertical height a minimum
horizontal set back distance of 5 feet should be maintained between the limits
of the structure and the top or toe of slopes for structures planned near the edge
of slopes For cut or fill slopes less than 10 feet a prudent set back distance
should be considered
Fill Settlements
Due to the presence of soft compressible soils below Building Nos 5 through
8 and 13 through 16 we estimate that placement of up to 11 feet of new fills in this
area wouid induce ground settlements on the order of about 2 to 4 inches due to

12 months would be adequate to achieve approximately 70 to 80 percent of
consolidation The new fills should be placed as early as possible The construction
of buildings utility lines and other improvements should not commence until tolerable
rates of settlement are achieved

Surcharge Program
We understand that it may be desired to reduce the waiting period for the fill
ground settlements Therefore it is our opinion that implementation of a
surcharge program may be considered at the proposed building sites The
lateral extent of the recommended surcharge area is presented on the Site Plan
Plate 2 A surcharge height of approximately 5 feet in addition to the permanent
fills may be used The top of the surcharge fill should extend approximately
5 feet away from the building limits and should slope down at a ratio of 1H 1V
In general the surcharge materials should be rolled with a minimum 10 ton roller
for a minimum of 3 passes Based on our engineering analyses we believe that
a settlement period of about 4 to 6 months would be adequate to achieve
approximately 80 to 90 percent consolidation with the recommended surcharge
fill The new fills and surcharge fills should be placed as early as possible to
induce the anticipated settlement Settlement gauges should be installed prior
to fill placement to monitor the settlement rate and evaluate when building and
utility construction can proceed
Settlement Monitoring
It should be recognized that it is very difficult to accurately predict the exact time
required for the filled ground to settle Settlement rates are greatly affected by
small details of the subsoil structures and the history of the subsoil deposition
Based on our consolidation data and previous exploration data we believe that
a 4 to 6 month surcharge period will be required for the project The actual
settlement rate should be monitored during construction and provisions should

To monitor the actual settlement rate we recommend that settlement gauges
and markers be installed in the areas of Building Nos 5 through 8 and 13

areas where fill thickness of 5 feet or more are planned The settlement gauges

Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site and our
previous experience we believe that the proposed compacted engineered fills and or
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3365 10 Page 20
cut subgrades would provide suitable foundation support for the proposed buildings
Shallow isolated spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the
proposed buildings Specific recommendations for design of foundations of the
proposed structures are discussed below
In general an allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 p s f may be used for
design of footings bearing on compacted engineered fill and or stiff native soils The
bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 for
transient loads such as wind or seismic forces
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grade Where footings are to be located adjacent to below grade
structures or utilities the footings should be extended to a depth below an imaginary
45 degree plane projected upward from the bottom of the below grade structure
Where footings are located adjacent to slopes or on slopes the footings should be
embedded to a sufficient depth to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of at
least 8 feet measured from the edge of the footing to the face of the slope
If the footings are designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total settlements of footings are estimated to be on the order of about 1 inch
with differential settlements on the order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth

We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of
a suitable method of dewatering
1 The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or damage
to existing facilities utilities and the environment
2 The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide safe
and dry working conditions in the excavation

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Four California Bearing Ratio CBR tests were performed on bulk samples of
the near surface soils collected for pavement design Results of the CBR tests are
summarized on Plate B 1
Two compaction tests ASTM D 1557 91 were performed on bulk samples of
the near surface soils to evaluate the maximum dry density and optimum moisture
content relationship Results of the compaction tests are also summarized on
Plate B 1 Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plates B 4 and B 5
Five one inch ring swell tests were performed on the near surface soils and the
on site stockpile materials hauled from Moanalua Road and Sand Island to evaluate the
swelling potential of the soils The test results are summarized on Plate B 2
Two Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Graphic
presentation of the test results is on Plate B 3 Results of the tests are also
summarized on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two consolidation tests with time rates were performed on the potentially
compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the materials
encountered Results of the consolidation tests are presented on Plates B 6 and B 7
One direct shear test was performed to evaluate the shear strength of the soils
sampled Results of the direct shear tests are presented on Plate B 8
