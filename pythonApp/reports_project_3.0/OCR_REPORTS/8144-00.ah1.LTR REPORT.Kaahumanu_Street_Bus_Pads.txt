PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located along Kaahumanu Street between the intersections of 
Hookanike Street and Komo Mai Drive in Pearl City on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that two concrete bus pads 
experienced cracking. The cracks occurred on the initial concrete pads and reoccurred 
on the replacement pads. It is desired to investigate the cracking bus pads subgrade 
conditions in support of the assessment and mitigation. Based on the construction plans 
provided, we understand that the concrete bus pads were installed in accordance with 
the City and County’s standard design details, consisting of 10 inches of Portland 
Cement Concrete (PCC) supporting directly on the recompacted existing subgrade.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two main 
shield volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau.  The older shield volcano (Waianae Volcano) is 
estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of 
the island.  The younger shield volcano (Koolau Volcano) is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and forms the majority of the eastern 
two-thirds of the island.  The Waianae Volcano became extinct while the Koolau 
Volcano remained active.  Therefore, the older Waianae Volcano’s eastern flank was 
partially buried below the younger Koolau Volcano lavas that banked against the 
Waianae’s eastern flank.  These banked and ponded lava flows formed a broad upland 
plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau of Central Oahu. 
The project site is located along the distal southern flank of the Koolau Volcano, 
as shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  The project site traverses some hilly 
terrain that is underlain by weathered volcanic deposits, which were erupted from the 
Koolau Volcano.  The volcanic deposits have been mapped previously and are referred 
to as the Tertiary Period Koolau Volcanic Series (H.T. Stearns).  The thick volcanic 
deposits consist of mainly thinly bedded, sequential layers of basaltic lava flows 
consisting of hard basalt rock and clinker.  Much of the near-surface basaltic rock is 
extremely weathered and has been reduced to clayey and silty residual soils containing 
decomposed rock fragments. 
Based on the subsurface exploration conducted at the project site, the bus pads 
are generally underlain residual soils extending to the maximum depth explored of 
about 11.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  We anticipate that hard basalt 
rock would be encountered at greater depths.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to a depth of about 
11.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  In addition, two bulk samples of the 
near-surface soils were collected at selected locations to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics. The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site 
Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2.  
Our field exploration suggests that the bus pads at the project site are generally 
underlain by 10-inch concrete pavement and a thin layer of leveling course over residual 
soils. The residual soils encountered in our borings generally consist of medium stiff to 
very stiff silty and clayey soils extending to the maximum depth explored of about 11.5 feet 
below the existing pavement surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater fluctuations may occur due to 
variation in rainfall, runoff, seepage, temperature, and other factors.

samples obtained from our field exploration are presented in Appendix B. 
SITE RECONNAISSANCE 
A site reconnaissance was performed on June 29, 2020 to mark the proposed 
boring locations and observe the existing site conditions. Details of our site 
reconnaissance are provided in the following sections.  
Traffic Loading 
Based on observations during our site reconnaissance, we observed relatively low 
traffic volume and infrequent bus passenger pick-ups during the non-peak hours. When 
passenger pick-ups/drop-offs are not required, buses will remain along the left side of the 
right lane passing the bus stop. With the exception of vehicles parking, most of the traffic 
(including pedestrian vehicles, large trucks, etc.) will occupy the left lane and the left half of 
the right lane. Based on these observations, we believe the left half of the bus pads (closer 
to the centerline of the road) is significantly more loaded from vehicular traffic than the right 
half of the bus pads (closer to the curb). 
Transverse Cracking in Bus Pads 
Transverse cracks were observed only in the left half of the bus pads (closer to the 
centerline of the road). Each bus pad is approximately 84 feet long and 20 feet wide. Each 
bus pad was sawcut into 14 sections spaced at seven sections longitudinally and two

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered residual soils extending to the maximum depth 
explored of about 11.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. We did not encounter 
groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. However, it should be 
noted that groundwater levels can vary significantly depending on rainfall, temperature, 
surface runoff, and other factors. 
Detailed discussions and recommendations for these items and other geotechnical 
aspects of the project are presented in the following sections. 
Bus Pad Transverse Cracks 
Based on the results of our field exploration and laboratory testing, the in-situ 
silty/clayey soils exhibit moderate shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to 
fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. No subbase material was encountered in the 
borings drilled beneath the bus pad. The shrink/swell subgrade may result in concrete bus 
pad distress for cracking when subjecting traffic loads. In addition, the depths of the 
transverse sawcut joints about 1 to 1.75 inches are not enough to prevent concrete 
shrinkage cracking. It should be noted that the sawcut joint is typically at least ¼ of 
concrete slab thickness. 
Based on the above discussion, we believe that the concrete slab transverse 
cracks may be caused by a combination of insufficient pavement subgrade and insufficient 
concrete shrinkage control. 
Repairs of Bus Pads 
To mitigate the moderately expansive clayey/silty soil subgrade condition, we 
believe the concrete bus pad should be supported with a minimum 6 inches of aggregate 
subbase course. Prior to placement of the subbase materials, the in-situ subgrade should 
be scarified to at least 12 inches deep, moisture-conditioned to a minimum of 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to no less than 95 percent relative 
compaction. The subbase course should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content and compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
Grace Pacific, LLC 
Page 6 
W.O. 8144-00

maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
Where minor shrinkage cracks are observed after subgrade preparation, we 
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks prior to recompacting. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather 
and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
well-compacted fill. 
Aggregate subbase course should meet the material requirements for subbase 
course as specified in Section 30 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works 
Construction, City & County of Honolulu, September 1986. Geolabs should test imported 
fill materials for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project 
site for the intended use. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation and 
subbase course placement to observe whether undesirable materials are encountered 
during the excavation, observe the scarification process, and confirm whether the exposed 
soil conditions are similar to those encountered in our field exploration.  The proctor tests 
and/or field observations should be performed on the actual subgrade soils during 
construction to confirm that the subgrade preparation is adequate. 
As an additional effort for stability and uniform compaction, we recommend 
proof-rolling the finished aggregate subbase course prior to placing the concrete pavement 
using a pneumatic-tired vehicle with a gross vehicle weight of at least 30,000 pounds, such 
as a fully-loaded water truck. The equipment used for proof-rolling should be operated at a 
speed of about 300 feet per minute and make at least two passes over each area 
designated for proof-rolling. Proof-rolling should also be performed on successive lifts of 
aggregate base course or subbase course materials. Areas with excessive rutting and/or 
pumping should be over-excavated to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation 
should be backfilled with well-compacted aggregate subbase material. 
The traverse joints should be cut to a depth of at least t/4 (t = actual constructed 
thickness of the concrete slab) using a blade thickness of 1/8 inches as soon as 
achievable with little reveling or other damage to the concrete finish. To enhance 
performance, the replacement of bus pads should be doweled into adjacent concrete slab 
using 5/8 inches smooth steel rod at 18-inch center-to-center spacing placed in the “Speed 
Dowel” sleeve filled with grease.

information obtained from the borings drilled and bulk samples collected. Variations of

Field Exploration 
The subsurface condition beneath the selected bus pads along Kaahumanu Street 
were explored by drilling and sampling four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 
4, extending to a depth of about 11.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2. The borings 
were drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch OD solid-stem augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation 
of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. Graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) were 
performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the 
in-situ soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the 
applied confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test results 
and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed 
on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-4 through B-6. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557 Method A) was performed 
on a selected bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and 
moisture content relationships. The test results are presented on Plate B-7.
