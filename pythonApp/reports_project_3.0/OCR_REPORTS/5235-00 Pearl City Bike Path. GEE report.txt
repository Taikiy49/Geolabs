Dear Mr. Hare:
In accordance with your authorization letter dated March 8, 2004, we have
conducted a geotechnical engineering exploration for the proposed HECO (Hawaiian
Electric Company) 8-Inch Pipeline along the Pearl City Bike Path in the Waiau area on the
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. This letter report summarizes the findings from our field
exploration conducted at the project site and presents our findings and
recommendations for the design of the pipeline foundations for the above project.
These findings and recommendations are intended for design input only and are subject
to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report.
Project Considerations
The project site is located along a portion of the Pearl City Bike Path near the
Waiau Power Plant on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
Based on the information provided, it is planned to install an 8-inch diameter pipe
along the bike path. In general, the majority of the pipeline will be installed below grade.
However, a portion of the pipeline will be installed above grade. The portion of the pipeline
to be installed above grade is located between Station 671+60 and Station 674+00. The
above grade pipeline will be located across a watercress farm area near the bottom of the
embankment for the bike path. We understand that 22 H-piles with a center-to-center
spacing of approximately 20 feet will be used to support the above grade portion of the
pipeline. The project plans require that the pipeline be supported by H-piles (HP8 x 36)
having an ultimate vertical capacity of 10 kips per pile.
We understand that it is planned to use HP10 x 42 H-piles in-lieu of the HP8 x 36
H-piles due to the availability of materials. Based on the information provided, the vertical
and horizontal loads acting on a single H-pile (based on 20 feet maximum spacing
between H-piles) are approximately 2.2 kips and 0.6 kips, respectively.

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling two test borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of
about 42.5 and 61.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The test borings were
drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary
coring tools. The approximate locations of the borings drilled for the project are shown
on the Site Plans (Plates 2.1 and 2.2).
The materials encountered in the borings drilled were classified by visual and
textural examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations
on a near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate 3.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling
30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel
Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
Torvane shear tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples retrieved in
the field. The torvane shear test provides a quick estimate of the undrained shear strength
of the soil. Results of the torvane shear tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths.
Subsurface Conditions
Boring Nos. 1 and 2 were drilled on the bike path, which is about 5 to 7 feet higher
than the existing ground surface along the pipeline alignment. Boring No. 1 generally
encountered fill materials consisting of silty and clayey soils with boulders and cobbles
extending to a depth of about 7 feet below the existing ground surface. Below the fill
materials, lagoonal deposits consisting of very soft organic silts were encountered to a
depth of about 14 feet below the existing ground surface. Alluvial deposits were
encountered below the lagoonal deposits extending to a depth of about 36 feet below the
existing ground surface. The alluvial deposits encountered consisted of soft silty clays
with gravel. The alluvial deposits grade to medium stiff at about 24 feet below the existing
ground surface and grade with boulders and cobbles at about 28 feet below the existing
ground surface. The alluvial deposits were underlain by basalt formation that was
generally highly weathered and medium hard. The basalt formation in Boring No. 1
extended to the maximum depth explored of about 42.5 feet below the existing ground
surface. * |

Existing Site Conditions
As mentioned previously, the project site is located along a portion of the bike
path near the Waiau Power Plant on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The location of the
project site is shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2.
In general, the area where the pipeline alignment will be supported by H-piles is
located between Station 671+60 and Station 674+00. The alignment of the pipeline
supported by H-piles is located at the bottom of the bike path embankment. The
pile-supported pipeline will begin just east of the Waimano Long’s Stream and terminate
before the entry into the HECO power plant.
The bike path embankment generally consists of sandy and gravelly soils with
some boulders and cobbles exposed at the slope face. In addition, a watercress farm
was observed at the bottom of the bike path embankment. The watercress farm
consisted of ponded water of about 6 to 12 inches high (above the ground level).
It should be noted that several large underground utility lines were observed to
traverse below the bike path embankment. In addition, some overhead communication
and power lines were observed above the proposed alignment of the pipeline.
Discussion and Recommendations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings drilled, we
believe that the pipeline supports (H-piles) will need to extend through the soft and
compressible lagoonal and alluvial deposits encountered in the borings drilled in order
to support the pipeline without appreciable settlement of the supports. The soft and
compressible lagoonal and alluvial deposits generally extend to depths of about 30 to
45 feet below the bike path ground surface. Therefore, we believe that the H-piies
needed for support of the pipeline likely will need to extend to the bottom of the highly
compressible alluvial deposits (80 to 45 feet deep in the borings) in order to provide the
H-piles with adequate capacity to support the pipeline. Detailed discussions and
geotechnical recommendations for design of the pile foundations are presented in the
following sections of this letter report.

Ground Settlements
In general, the borings drilled at the project site indicate that highly compressible
lagoonal and alluvial soil deposits are present along the proposed pipeline alignment in
the areas explored. The borings drilled generally indicate that the thickness of the
compressible soil deposits range from about 18 to 38 feet.
The borings drilled for this exploration did not encounter artesian groundwater
conditions at the project site; however, artesian groundwater conditions may be present
at the project site based on our experience in the area. Due to the potential for artesian
groundwater conditions in the Waiau area, we understand that wells have been installed
in the area and groundwater has been drawn down from the original groundwater
condition.
It should be noted that changes in the level of the groundwater level would cause
consolidation of the underlying highly compressible lagoonal and alluvial deposits at the
project site. When the groundwater level is brought down to a lower level, the effective
unit weight of the soils located between the original (higher) groundwater level and the
lowered groundwater level increases from the effective unit weight (buoyant unit weight)
to the total unit weight (total weight of soil and water). The increase in unit weight of the
soils above the lowered groundwater level results in additional load on the compressible
deposits below the groundwater table.
Due to the drawdown of the groundwater levels in the Waiau area, it should be
noted that there is continuing areal ground settlements in the Waiau area. Since the
groundwater level drawdown occurs continuously over time, it is difficult to determine
the magnitude of the ground settlements that will occur at the project site in the future.
Pipe Foundation Support
Based on the anticipated structural loading and the subsurface materials
encountered at the project site, we recommend that the H-piles (HP10 x 42) be utilized
for support of the pipeline. Piles for the pipeline support should extend through the very
soft and very loose lagoonal and alluvial deposits and into the stiff or medium dense
alluvial deposits consisting of silty clay and weathered boulders and cobbles to provide
an ultimate vertical load capacity of 10 kips. The stiff or medium dense alluvial deposits
were encountered at depths ranging from about 25 to 40 feet below the existing grade
along the proposed pipeline alignment.
The H-piles would derive vertical support from both friction and end bearing
between the H-pile and underlying stiff or medium dense alluvial deposits consisting of
silty clay and weathered boulders and cobbles. Based on the depths to the stiff or
medium dense alluvial soil deposits and the maximum pile stick-up of approximately
2.5 feet above the finished grades along the proposed pipeline alignment, the H-pile
lengths would range between about 30 and 45 feet (based on the borings drilled). Due

the pile driving operations.
To obtain more accurate depths to the stiff and/or medium dense alluvial
deposits along the pipeline alignment, consideration should be given to probing at the
pile locations prior to installing the H-piles. The probing operations may reduce the
potential for encountering obstructions that may hinder the driving of the H-piles to the
bearing layer consisting of the stiff or medium dense alluvial deposits. Probing at the
pile location may consist of driving or vibrating an H-pile or similar size piles into the
ground until a highly resistant layer is encountered in the subsurface.
Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on the H-piles may be resisted by the lateral load capacity of
the H-piles. Lateral load resistance of the H-piles is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil, the stiffness of the pile, allowable deflection at the top of the pile,
and the induced moment in the pile. The lateral load analyses were performed
using the program LPILE-plus for Windows, which is a microcomputer adaptation of
a finite difference, laterally loaded pile program originally developed at the
University of Texas at Austin. The program solves for deflection and bending
moment along a pile under lateral loads as a function of depth. The analysis was
carried out with the use of non-linear “p-y” curves to represent soil moduli. The
lateral deflection was then computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various
depths.

induced moment, the depth at which the maximum moment occurs for the piles,
and the maximum horizontal deflection of the H-pile are presented in the following
table. The values provided in the table are based on an HP10 x 42 piles. Because
the H-piles are spaced at approximately 20 feet on center, the effect of group action
was not been taken into consideration in our lateral load analyses.

Settlements of the pile foundations will result from both elastic compression of the
pile member. Based on our analyses, we estimate the total settlement of the piles
to be less than 1 inch with differential settlements between piles not exceeding
about 0.5 inches. As mentioned previously, areal ground settlements are occurring
in the Waiau area due to the drawdown of the groundwater surface. The estimated
pile settlements do not include settlements resulting from consolidation of the
compressible lagoonal and alluvial deposits due to drawdown of the groundwater
surface (unable to quantify because groundwater drawdown is time dependent).
Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering energies of about
10,000 foot-pounds or greater in the field. Determination of driving equipment to be
used for this project should take into consideration the "matching" of the pile
hammer with the weight of the piles to be driven. This will result in the pile hammer
efficiently delivering the required energy to the top of the pile and also reducing
potential damage to the piles. Geolabs should review the pile driving equipment
proposed to evaluate whether the equipment is capable of installing the piles to the
recommended capacity.
The contractor should forward pile and driving equipment data forms to Geolabs
for review prior to construction. Geolabs should review the submittal and assess
the driveability of the piles with the use of the selected hammer. A wave
equation analysis should be performed after selection of the pile driving
equipment to assess the driveability of the piles and to establish the pile driving
criteria.

Proof Testing
We understand that the project plans and specifications require that proof testing
of the H-piles be performed. In general, we recommend that proof tests be
performed on at least two H-piles installed for foundation support of the pipeline.
The proof tests should consist of subjecting the H-piles to at least 10 kips of
vertical load (ultimate compressive capacity), and the load should be held for at
least 1 hour. We recommend that the proof tests be conducted under the
observation of a representative from Geolabs.
Corrosion Evaluation
Several of the subsurface soil samples retrieved from our field exploration at the
project site were subjected to corrosivity tests. Laboratory pH tests and minimum
resistivity tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the corrosivity
of the soils. The results of these tests are provided on Plate 6 of this letter report.
Based on the test results, the pH values of the subsurface soils range between
7.2 and 8.3, and the minimum resistivity values of the subsurface soils range
between about 900 and 2,000 ohm-cm. The test results indicate that the
subsurface soils encountered along the proposed pipeline alignment may be
considered “highly corrosive.”
Based on the FHWA manual entitled “Durability/Corrosion of Soil Reinforced
Structures” (FHWA-RD-89-186), we estimate that the steel loss of the H-piles due
to corrosion in a highly aggressive environment would be between about 20 to
25 mils (600 to 650 uum loss of steel) over a period of 50 years. We believe that the
estimated amount of steel loss would not adversely affect the performance of the
H-piles.
