SOIL INVESTIGATION REPORT
CAMPBELL BUS MAINTENANCE FACILITY
CAMPBELL INDUSTRIAL PARK
BARBERS POINT OAHU HAWAII
This report presents the results of our soil investigation
performed for the proposed Heavy Maintenance Facility of the Honolulu
Bus at Campbell Industrial Park Barbers Point Oahu Hawaii The
general location of the site and its vicinity are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
PROJECT CONSIDERATION
It is proposed to develop a design for heavy maintenance facilities
at the site where an existing large warehouse type building is now
situated It is our understanding that the preliminary consideration is
to refurbish the interior and add new pavements outside the building for
bus repair and maintenance A preliminary layout of the proposed
project site is presented on the Site Plan Plate 2
W Page 2
In addition the following are also planned
l New concrete slabs for various functions replacing the old ones
in the rear one third about 210 feet of the existing
warehouse building
2 bus lifts
3 a large pit 12 feet deep for a paint booth drop table
4 underground fuel tanks and storage tanks
5 a new depressed loading ramp on the southern side of the
existing building and
6 a new paved parking area for the buses
Other than the above mentioned most of the existing facilities
such as the office area visitor and employee parking and the sewage
disposal facility are planned to remain
PURPOSE AND SCOPE OF SERVICES
The purpose of the soil investigation is to provide information
related to soils and foundation engineering for the above design The
scope of services rendered is in general conformance with our proposal
dated In general the following were performed

1 A field exploration and testing program consisting of a site

The site has been developed previously and is occupied by a large
metal framed warehouse building with concrete floor Slabs asphalt
concrete paved perimeter driveways and parking areas and a large

Existing Warehouse Concrete Slabs

A visual examination of the existing warehouse slabs were made by
one of our engineers In general the slab appeared to be in fair
condition The majority of the slabs were 24 by 36 feet panels
except along the middle strip of the warehouse where 3 buried rail
tracks ran along the length of the warehouse The warehouse slabs
displayed various types of cracks most of the which appeared to be
shrinkage cracks Only a small number of the slab panels showed
crack patterns that appeared to have been caused by heaving Many
of the slabs in the central strip of the warehouse had cracked into
several pieces which appeared to have been caused by over stressing
At the time of our field exploration the rear one third of the
warehouse was being used to store empty soft drink cans Since
this portion of the slabs are planned to be replaced no special
effort was made to remove the stockpile for a comprehensive visual
inspection of the condition of the underlying floor slab Visual
observations of the exposed portion of this slab indicated
conditions similar to those at the front two thirds of the
warehouse

The unpaved yard area in the back of the warehouse had been graded
to a fairly level surface and covered with a mixture of gray

Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at this site consisted mainly of various
thickness of fill overlying emerged coral reef rock The fill is
thicker under and around the warehouse than in the yard area
In the warehouse area our borings indicated the concrete slabs
were about 4 5 inches thick underlain mainly by stiff reddish brown
clayey silt mixed with various amount of gravel The granular base
course layer under the concrete slab was generally absent in three of
the five boring locations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1340 00 Page 7
Boring 1 encountered 3 to 4 inches of gray silty gravel and Boring
4 encountered 2 5 inches of white coral gravel and gray basalt rock
fragments Our borings indicated that the thickness of the clayey silt
layer varied from 1 5 to 2 5 feet The CBR values of the subgrade
material just below the concrete slabs varied from 36 to 76 Under the
reddish brown clayey silt fill were various mixtures of coral sand and
gravel materials which were probably generated from the original site
grading operation This coral fill material overlies the coral reef
rock typical of the Barbers Point area
Outside the warehouse the existing fill was relatively thin with
thicker wedge of fill along the warehouse perimeter This fill
consisted mainly of mixtures of coral sand and gravel which probably
were generated from the original site grading operation Other type of
mixtures such as brown sandy silt and gray basalt rock fragments also
were observed on the yard surface
The existing A C pavement sections in the perimeter driveway
consisted of 1 2 to 1 3 inches of asphalt surfacing and about 2 inches
of crushed basalt rock base course
Under the surface fill and or pavement section was the white and
tan coral reef rock which consists mainly of cemented coral reef
deposits of sand and gravel to 15 feet the maximum depth drilled The
coral reef rock may extend a few hundred feet deep at the site

Occasional large cavities in the coral formation have been reported
in the Barbers Point area One of our test pits Test Pit D
encountered a small cavity at a depth of 5 feet which could not be
filled with one 55 gallon drum of water Underground cavities of
various sizes could be expected under the site
Groundwater level was not encountered in our borings It is likely
to be encountered at a depth of 1 to 2 feet above the mean sea level at
the site
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our findings we believe that the site will provide
suitable footing support for the proposed structures In general the
existing concrete slabs are in a fair condition However these slabs
are only about 4 5 inches thick and may provide only a limited service
life for forklift truck operations
Existing Subgrade Support
Concrete Slab Subgrade

Based on our field CBR test values of 38 to over 70 good
subgrade support condition is anticipated under the existing slab
In view of a low expansive nature of the clayey silt at subgrade
and the occasional crack patterns indicative of Slight heaving we
believe that the modulus of subgrade reaction of the existing
subgrade should be assumed at k 250 p c i for rigid pavement
design
GEOLABS HAWAII

Because the existing subgrade materials consisted mainly of reddish
brown clayey silt excess water in the subgrade may Cause pumping
under wheel loading on the slabs Therefore washing of the
existing slab should be avoided as much as possible
It is our understanding that slabs in the rear one third of the
area will be removed and a thicker concrete slab will be
constructed to support heavier wheel loading due to tow trucks
forklift trucks etc
For the new concrete floor slab design where wheel traffic is
anticipated we believe that the existing subgrade material of
reddish brown clayey silt should be removed to at least 4 inches
below the bottom of the slab and replaced with aggregate subbase
material meeting Section 30 Select Borrow for Subbase Course
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction
For coral type subbase material the maximum amount of fines
passing the 200 sieve may be increased to 20 The subgrade and
subbase course material should be compacted to 95 of the maximum
ASTM D 1557 78 density The design value of modulus of subgrade
reaction should not be more than 260 p c i for rigid pavement
design

Although the coral reef rock at shallow depths will provide
excellent subgrade support its surface elevation is expected to
vary and the new pavement design would be dictated by the existing
coral fill from the original site grading operation For the new
flexible pavement design we recommend a design CBR value of 35 be
used for the coral fill material at the subgrade level
If clayey silt soils from the excavation under the warehouse slabs
are used at subgrade level the design subgrade support under the
new flexible pavement should be lowered to CBR 10 Thus it
appears that a much thicker pavement section would be required It
is recommended that this clayey silt be used as fill at a level at
least 2 feet below the finished grade or in the landscaped area
Site Grading
It is our understanding that the site grading will be kept to a
ninimum The majority of the new excavation work will be for the new
deep pit in the body paint work area underground fuel tanks and storage
tanks and the new depressed loading ramp on the southern side of the
facility The filling work will include the existing depressed loading
ramps The removal work will include the rear one third portion of the
existing concrete slabs and probably removing a portion of the subgrade
material under these slabs
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1340 00 Page 11
In general the deeper excavations will likely encounter the coral
reef rock which will probably require heavy ripping or hoe ramming The
resulting excavated material will generally be suitable for use as fill
provided that any over sized chunks of coral are broken down to 6 inch
size or smaller and that these particles are fully embedded in the
silty sand matrix when compacted The top 6 inches of the subgrade
material under the slabs or pavements should not contain particles in
excess of 3 inches The excavated on site materials should be examined
and approved by a soils engineer before being used as fill
All i11 and backfill should be placed in horizontal lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a density of not
less than 95 of the maximum ASTM D 1557 78 density within the top 2 5
feet below the finished grade The compaction may be reduced to 90
minimum below this depth
Where the existing A C is in a poor condition as discussed under
the SITE CONDITIONS section the existing subgrades should be
over excavated to firm ground These areas should be verified in the
field by a qualified soils engineer The bottom of the excavation
should be recompacted to 95 relative compaction and backfilled with
granular fill material If over excavation encounters coral rock at
shallow depths such over excavation may be terminated and the bottom of
the excavation leveled and backfilled with compacted granular fill

Pit for Paint Booth Drop Table
It is our understanding that a large pit measuring about 10 by 62
feet in plan and about 12 feet deep would be required for a paint booth
drop table for the body paint work It is anticipated that the
excavation will mostly be in the coral rock after penetrating through
the floor slabs and 2 to 3 feet of reddish brown clayey silt and or
coral sand and gravel fill In general we believe that the upper
portion of the excavation in fill may need to be shored or Sloped back
on a H V ratio to minimize sloughing The lower portion of
excavation in coral may be cut in a near vertical sides except where
cavities exist The excavation should be shored according to government
regulations
If the pit is to be over excavated formed concreted and
backfilled we recomended that the walls of the pit be designed to
resist lateral earth pressures of 35 p s f per foot of depth along the
long axis of the pit and 55 p s f per foot of depth for the short
axis We believe that build up of hydrostatic pressures in the material
behind the wall is not likely due to relatively pervious nature of the
coral formation
Footings
It is our understanding that the new footings required are for the
new CMU firewall and various machinery foundations No new structural
footings are planned
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1340 00 Page 13
For footings bearing directly on the coral reef rock an allowable
net bearing pressure of up to 7000 p s f may be considered For wall
footings bearing on fill an allowable soil bearing pressure of 3500
p s f may be used when the footing bottom is embedded at least 18
inches below the lowest adjacent grade A one third increase in the
above soil bearing pressures may be allowed for wind or seismic loads
For construction of the new depressed loading ramp the existing
footing should be properly protected
It is possible that cavities may be encountered during actual
footing excavations When encountered these cavities should be cleaned
o soils or other debris and backfilled with lean concrete
We also recommend that all footing excavations be inspected by a
soils engineer to confirm the conditions upon which our design
recommendations were based
Absorption Field
If absorption fields are required to handle effluent from a septic
tank they should be placed at least 2 to 3 feet into the coral rock
Based on our field percolation test results we believe that the
absorption field design should be based on a percolation rate of 1 to 2
minutes per inch
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1340 00 Page 14
Plans and specifications for site grading and foundation
construction should be forwarded to the soils engineer for review and
written comments prior to construction to see that the intent of our
recommendations is included
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the site grading pit and foundation
excavations absorption field excavations and subgrade preparation for
slabs and pavements be observed by the soils engineer during
construction This is to see that adequate compaction has been
obtained and that the subsurface conditions are consistent with the
findings of our investigation
Tf conditions encountered are different from those presented in
this report then appropriate modifications to the design should be
made

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
Se ee ens
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling 8
respectively below the existing ground surface at the approximate
locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 A truck mounted drilling
rig was used to drill the borings and the test pits The borings and
the test pits were drilled with 4 5 inch and 10 inch diameter augers
respectively
The soils rock encountered were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by one of our engineers who continuously
monitored the drilling operations The classifications were further
reviewed by visual inspection and testing in the laboratory The soils
were Classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Boring and Test Pit Logs Plates A 1
through A 12
Soil samples were obtained by driving a 2 inch standard split spoon
free falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive the sampler for 12
inches are shown on the log of borings at the appropriate sample depths
Four inch I D drilled core samples of the concrete slabs were
obtained by using a rotary wash diamond bit core barrel for laboratory
colipression testing in 4 and 5
Two field percolation tests were conducted in Test Pits A and C
After the test pits were drilled and filled with water for about 10
minutes the pits were filled again with water and the fall of water
levels in the pits with time were recorded The test results are
summarized on Plate A 13
Eight field California Bearing Ratio CBR tests were carried out
in our borings to obtain data that would aid to evaluate the
characteristics of the in situ base course and subgrade soils for
pavement design The results of the field CBR tests are summarized on
Plate A 14

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Se ee
Four compression tests were performed on drilled concrete cores from
the existing slabs The heights of the cores were measured in
accordance with ASTM C 174 procedure The compression tests were
performed in accordance with ASTM C 39 procedure except the cores were
tested in an air dried condition The normal maximum aggregate size
measured on sawed core surface was three fourths of an inch The
compressive strengths were corrected according to height and diameter
ratio in accordance with ASTM C 42 procedure The test results are
presented on Plate B l
Moisture content unit weight and Atterberg Limits determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented
on the boring logs at the appropriate sample depths The results of the
Atterberg Limits are summarized on Plate B 2
Three moisture density tests modified Proctor were performed on
bulk samples of the existing subgrade and base course soils obtained
from the borings to determine their compaction characteristics Because
of the limited amount of soil sample obtained from each boring similar
types of soil were combined and reused in the tests The test results
are presented on Plates B 2 through B 4
One laboratory CBR test was performed on bulk samples of the
existing subgrade soils to evaluate their suitability as pavement
support The test results are summarized on Plate B 3
One sieve analysis test was performed on samples of the base course
material to evaluate its grain size distribution The test results are
summarized on Plate B 3
Three one inch ring swell tests were performed on remolded samples
to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils in compacted
conditions The test results are summarized on Plate B 5
