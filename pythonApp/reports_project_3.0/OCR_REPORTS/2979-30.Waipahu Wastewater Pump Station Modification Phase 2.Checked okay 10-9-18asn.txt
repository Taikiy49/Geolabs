REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The Waianae and Koolau Volcanic Shields were
built during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epochs by thinly bedded basaltic lava
flows The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the western third of the island while
the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The
main shieid building activities ceased approximately 3 5 to 2 5 million years ago It is
believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank is
partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
The project site is located along the southern flank of Koolau The geomorphology
and subsurface conditions at the site are directly related to the glacio eustatic fluctuations
of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of Pearl Harbor and the
Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions at the project site are dominated by
fossil Pleistocene age alluvial soil deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of
the sea that are intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic
section in this area is Koolau Basalt Evidence from wells drilled indicate that the Island of
GEOLABS INC
W O 2979 30 Page 6
Oahu has subsided by as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic
activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed behind
the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the lagoons
Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill the
oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded and melted global sea levels rose
because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially stable
during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to the
present sea level on Oahu
The higher sea level stands resulted in the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal
sediments may have also resulted in deposits of coral algal limestone and marls within the
predominantly lagoonal sedimentary units
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
GEOLABS INC
W O 2979 30 Page 7
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During
that rise the deep valleys in the project area were submerged Within the last 10 000
years the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments
have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting
in thick deposits of soft harbor sediments along the coast in areas that were formerly
valleys and drainage ways Along the northern fringe of Pearl Harbor brackish water
marshes developed resulting in thick layers of organic sediments in a lacustrine
environment Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have
brought the area to its present form These projects involved the placement of man made
fills over the thick lagoonal and marsh deposits

The project site is generally underlain by surface fills overlying a thick layer of soft
compressible soils recent alluvium Medium stiff to very stiff older alluvium was
encountered below the recent alluvium followed by saprolitic soils partially weathered rock
materials Basalt bedrock formation was not encountered within the depths of our
borings which extended to a maximum depth of about 238 5 feet below the existing

ground settlements anticipated at the site we recommend that a deep foundation system

site In general we recommend that pile foundations be used to support the pump station
addition new emergency generator building underground fuel storage tank electrical
transformer pad and new pipelines connected to the new and existing structures In
addition we recommend that the floor slabs of the new structures be designed as
structural slabs capable of spanning between the pile foundations to reduce the potential
impacts of loss of ground support below the slabs due to ground settlement
Based on the grading plans for the site we understand that new fills on the order of
about 1 5 to 2 feet will be needed to raise the existing ground surface to achieve the
design finished grades at the project site Because the thick layer of soft recent alluvium is
still undergoing the process of primary consolidation from the previous fill placed at the
site we believe that ground settlements on the order of about 15 to 20 inches may be
experienced over the lifetime of the pump station The estimated ground settlements are

Pile Foundation
As previously indicated the project site is underlain by a thick layer of recent
alluvium consisting of soft compressible soils extending to about 85 to 99 feet below the
existing ground surface The soft recent alluvium is underlain by medium stiff to hard older
alluvium and very stiff to hard saprolitic soils extending to the maximum depth explored of

believe that the stiff older alluvium encountered below the soft recent alluvium would be
capable of providing suitable foundation support for the deep foundation system
recommended at the site

emergency generator building underground fuel storage tank and electrical transformer

known as downdrag forces on the pile shaft The magnitude of the downdrag force

and the pile perimeter area Due to the anticipated consolidation of the soft recent

extending through the thick layer of soft recent alluvium Therefore the allowable

Pile Foundation Settlements
Settlement of the pile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression of
the pile member and the subgrade response We estimate that the total settlement
of the pile supported foundation to be less than 0 5 inches with differential
settlements between piles not exceeding about one half of that amount We
believe that these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads
are applied
Predrilling
Our borings at the site encountered very stiff to hard surface fill materials In
addition new compacted fills of up to 2 feet thick are planned to raise the existing
ground to the design finished grades Therefore we recommend that the pile
locations be predrilled to approximately 10 feet below the design finished grades to
reduce the potential for hard driving conditions near the ground surface during pile
installation In addition predrilling the pile locations will also aid in reducing the
vibrations generated during the pile driving operations on the adjacent existing
structures The diameter of the predrilled holes should be limited to approximately
20 inches for the 16 5 inch octagonal piles to provide the pile with sufficient soil
contact for lateral load resistance The predrilled hole for the 12 inch square pile
should be limited to 14 inches in diameter If an over sized predrilled hole is
encountered during construction the annular space between the piles and
predrilled holes should be backfilled by jetting clean sand into the void to provide
the recommended lateral load capacity

variation in the length of the piles unit prices should be obtained during bidding for
add ons shorter piles etc
Pile Driving Criteria
For preliminary design of the 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete
piles pile driving may be terminated when the aggregated blow counts are at least
3 blows per inch for the last 12 inches or 8 blows per inch for the last 4 inches The
preliminary driving criteria should be confirmed and or modified after selection of
the pile driving equipment and performance of a wave equation analysis In
addition the pile driving criteria may be modified during the test pile driving to
accommodate variations of soil conditions in the field
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption A pile may be rejected
or its design capacity may be reduced when the driving resistance is interrupted for
more than 4 hours and the pile cannot be driven to the required depth During soft
driving such as when the pile tip is within soft soils the hammer stroke or energy
GEOLABS INC
W O 2979 30 Page 17
should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile stresses do not develop
within the pile
Because variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the bearing
soils the supporting capacity of each pile should be confirmed in the field by a
representative from our office during the driving operation It should be noted that
the pile design was developed from our pile analysis using the field exploration
data A test pile program should be carried out to confirm our preliminary pile
design and establish the driving criteria The production pile driving criteria may be
revised based on the test pile program
Test Pile Program
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we recommend that a test pile
program be conducted for the recommended pile foundation system In general
we recommend that the test pile program consist of a minimum of eight test piles
which includes at least one 12 inch square test pile The test piles may be
incorporated into the actual foundation system upon the evaluation of the dynamic
load test results The test pile program should be undertaken to fulfill the following
principal objectives
1 To examine the ability of the selected hammer to drive the piles to the
recommended depths and driving resistance
2 To assess if the pre drilling depth needs to be modified or revised
3 To evaluate and modify if necessary the preliminary driving criteria from the
wave equation analysis
4 To more accurately determine the probable production pile tip elevations
The length of the test piles should be at least 160 feet for the 16 5 inch octagonal
piles The test piles should be at least 125 feet for the 12 inch square piles Due to
the significant pile lengths required at the site splice cans should be provided at the
GEOLABS INC

site during the test pile driving As mentioned previously splice cans may be
fabricated with 0 25 inch thick high grade steel and should be at least 6 feet long
Each test pile should be instrumented for monitoring by a pile driving analyzer
PDA to observe the stresses developed in the test piles and to evaluate the pile
bearing capacity Instrumentation and monitoring of a pile by a pile driving analyzer
is also known as dynamic load testing In addition the test pile program should
provide flexibility to allow for addition or relocation of the test piles as may be
deemed necessary by our office to suit the conditions encountered during the test

Floor Slabs
Based on our field reconnaissance of the site we noted that ground settlements on
the order of about 1 foot or more might have occurred at the existing sewage pump station
since its construction in the early 1970s Based on our analyses it appears that the soft
compressible deposits encountered below the surface fill layer is still undergoing the
process of primary consolidation under the influence of the existing fill loads and due to the
lowering of the regional artesian head As we mentioned earlier our analyses indicate that
settlements on the order of about 15 to 20 inches or more may occur over the lifetime of
this project due to continued consolidation of the underlying soft compressible soils In
addition because of the organic nature of the soft compressible deposits we believe that
large magnitude long term secondary settlements may occur and should be anticipated
Additional settlement may occur as a result of significant lowering of the regional artesian
head in the general area
Due to significant long term settlements anticipated over the lifetime of this project
we recommend that the floor slabs of the new structures be designed as structural slabs
capable of spanning between the pile foundations to reduce the adverse effects of the loss
of ground support below the slabs The sliding resistance between the floor slab pile caps
and the supporting subgrade should be neglected due to loss of ground contact resulting
from the anticipated ground settlement

Fills and Backfills
In general the excavated on site surface fills may be re used as a source of

backfill materials and should be disposed of properly off site
Where imported fill materials are required as general fill and backfill materials the

30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve Imported fill material should be
tested by Geolabs for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to
the project site for the intended use

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
The fill and backfill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches

Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Relatively undisturbed samples of the soft soil deposits were retrieved by
hydraulically pushing a 3 inch OD thin wall Shelby tube into the soil formation The
hydraulic pressures needed to advance the sampler are recorded as the Penetration
Resistance in pounds per square inch psi per foot of penetration on the Logs of Borings
Pocket penetrometer and torvane shear tests were performed on selected cohesive
soil samples retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of
the unconfined compressive strength of the sample The torvane shear test provides a
quick estimate of the undrained shear strength of the soil Results of the pocket
penetrometer and torvane shear tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The results
of this test are summarized on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
One sieve analysis test D 1140 was performed on a
selected sample of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid
in soil classification Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Two unconsolidated undrained triaxial TXUU compression tests ASTM D 2850
were performed on selected samples of the fine grained soils to evaluate the undrained
shear strength of the materials The samples were confined at the estimated overburden
pressures and sheared to failure Results of the TXUU compression tests are summarized
on Plates B 3 and B 4
Five one dimensional consolidation tests ASTM D 2435 were performed on
selected samples of the soils encountered to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of
the materials Results of the consolidation tests are presented on Plates B 5 through B 9
Three direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials The results of the
tests are presented on Plates B 10 through B 12
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a
bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the materials The test results are presented on Plate B 13
