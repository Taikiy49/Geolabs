Regional Geology
The island of Oahu is composed of two 2 shield volcanoes Waianae
and Koolau built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas during the late
Pliocene and Pleistocene epochs Preliminary reconnaissance of the site
and its vicinity indicates that the area is underlain by thin residual and
transported soils derived from the weathering of Koolau lavas
Koolau basalts are typically thin bedded primitive lavas with a large
percentage of pahoehoe flows Cavities or voids such as lava tubes
are common in pahoehoe lava flows and are frequently encountered in cuts
or excavations
Kamiloiki Valley is typical of the steeply sided valleys of the
leeward side of the eastern portion of the Koolau Range Erosion of the
windward slopes of the Koolau range has truncated the headwaters of the
valley removing the source of the streams which originally carved the
valley After the initial period of erosion alluvium and colluvium
accumulated in the floor of the valley followed by the formation and or
deposition of a surficial clay soil derived from the chemical weathering
of the basic igneous rocks forming the Koolau Range
Soil Conditions
Based upon our field exploration the subsurface conditions may be
categorized into three 3 general types of material as described below
1 ADOBE a dark brown to gray highly expansive clay soil
containing some organics roots and a few cobbles and boulders
This soil is characterized by a very high shrink swell potential
and low shear strength It is not considered suitable for use as
fill material or for foundation support
GEOCLABS HAWAIi
W O 1753 00 Page 6
Dk No 50
2 COLLUVIUM a weakly to moderately cemented conglomerate of
boulders and cobbles with a matrix of grayish brown to brown
clay silty clay or silty sand between the larger clasts The
colluvium represents material which has been eroded from the
adjacent upper valleys and transported by gravity to the lower
slopes and valley floor This material generally has good
strength both in place and as fill although it often exhibits a
moderate shrink swell potential when excavated and placed as
fill It is generally considered suitable for foundation support
and as a source of fill material if provisions are made to limit
the swelling potential of the soil
3 KOOLAU BASALT slightly to highly weathered basaltic bedrock
deposited as lava flows originating from the southeast rift of
the Koolau volcano and subsequently exposed by erosion This
material has excellent strength and little or no shrink swell
potential when excavated and placed as fill It is considered
excellent for foundation support and is a good source of fill if
reduced to manageable size Due to the hardness of portions of
the rock it may present difficulties in excavation
In general the lower slopes and the valley floor are mantled by a
surface layer of adobe clay This layer varies in thickness from 10
feet in the lowest portion of the site along the intermittent streams to
zero where rock outcrops become the dominant surface feature In the
southern part of the site the adobe forms a very thin layer overlying the
colluvium or basalt downslope of the proposed roadway
The colluvium layer overlies Koolau Basalt and varies considerably in
thickness throughout the site but generally decreases in thickness with
increasing elevation In the upper portions of the project site the
colluvium tapers out and the basalt is exposed at the surface

excavated and recompacted as fill Therefore the excavated

rock formation

may be used as general fill material Most of the fills will be
placed along sloping areas which will require the use of these
smaller size materials which can be well compacted to provide the
necessary stability of the fills

Rock falls are a general phenomena occurring on all ridge slopes in
Oahu Various methods of boulder barriers or catchments can be used to
reduce the hazard of falling boulders For this site some of the methods

1 Boulders sweeps along the upper boundaries of the site could be

Natural Hillside Stability
In Hawaii one of predominant forces in weathering and erosion is the
geologic process known as mass transfer This process is the movement
of material down the side slopes of valleys primarily under the influence
of gravity The process of mass transfer ranges from very slow movement
of less than one inch per year soil creep to very rapid movement such
as the landslides which occurred on New Year s Eve of 1987 88 in Kuliouou
Niu and Hahaione Valleys The small slide along the western valley slope
in Kamiloiki is an example of the rapid form of mass transfer and is
referred to as a soil avalanche
Soil avalanches are part of the normal weathering and erosion
processes in Hawaii These avalanches are generally small surficial
slupms or skin tears of the thin soil cover which occurs on steep slopes
and are generally not indicative of mass instability of the Slope itself
They are also much more prevalent in the wetter windward side of the
island than a dryer area like Hawaii Kai
The preponderance of slides and slumps which have occurred during
this winter of 1987 1988 are most likely the result of having had a few
years of abnormally dry weather followed by the prolonged and the
torrential rains of the current wet season During the dry periods the
soil cover on the steep slopes desiccated and began to crack forming
fissures for water to enter and accumulate This season s long periods of
rainfall allowed for deep saturation of the soils and the heavy rainfall
events added water faster than the soils allowed percolation and runoff
This caused portions of the saturated soils to become overloaded and to
fail or slide

It should be noted that the recent New Year s Eve storm was an
extremely rare occurrence and the probability of reoccurrence is generally
believed to be very small
It can be seen from the above discussion that it is not possible to
predict the occurrence and extent of these soil avalanches as they are
primarily dependent on weather conditions
However due to the dryer weather conditions and recent performance
of the site during the storm it is our opinion that Kamiloiki Valley may
be considered less susceptible to soil avalanches than many of the other
valleys

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content determinations were performed on selected samples as
an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The
results of the moisture contents are presented on the Test Pit and Boring
Logs Plates A 1 through A 17
Four Atterberg Limit determinations were performed on selected
samples to aid in the classification of the soils The test results are
summarized on Plate B 1
Seven direct shear tests were performed on remolded samples of clay

STANDARD GRADING SPECIFICATIONS
KAMILO ESTATES
W O 1753 00 MARCH 18 1988
The work under this section includes clearing and grubbing preparation of
fill areas placement and compaction of fill and removal and backfilling
of underground structures These specifications are of a general nature
and may be superseded by specific requirements or recommendations within
the soils report
II
Clearing
All areas within contract limit lines shall be cleared or trash
debris organic matter and all other deleterious materials These
materials shall be removed and disposed outside of Hawaii Kali
Preparation of Fill Areas
A Iocose surface soils shall be removed to expose competent natural
soils Localized soft pockets shall be undercut and backfilled with
compacted select materials
B All surface shall be inspected and approved by soils engineer
prior to the placement of any fill
C After approval the surface shall be scarified to a depth of
twelve 12 inches brought to near optimum moisture content and
compacted as herein specified
D Where the existing surface exceeds a slope of 5 horizontal to 1
vertical 5 1 the ground surface shall be keyed and benched into
competent soil to bond the fill to the existing ground surface
GEOLABS HAWAII
IIT
Page 2
E Subsurface drains may be required as directed by the soils
engineer where springs or zones of seepage are encountered
Fill Materials
Material for fill shall consist of on site soils or approved import
material Import material shall be tested and approved a minimum of
seven 7 working days prior to transportation to the site Fill
material shall contain less than 5 percent organic matter and other
deleterious material and shall not contain rocks or lumps in excess
of twelve inches 12 in diameter
Placement and Compaction of Fill
A The fill shall be placed in horizontal loose layers not to
exceed eighteen inches 18 in thickness Each layer shall be
spread uniformly and blade mixed to attain uniformity of material
and water content within each layer
B Rock fragments or boulders less than twelve inches 12 in
diameter may be utilized provided they are not concentrated in ay
one area and that there is sufficient fine grained material to fill
the voids between the rocks
C Rocks greater than twelve inches 12 in diameter shall be
removed from the fill unless specifically approved for placement in
the fill by the soils engineer
D All fill shall be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of the
maximum dry density established by ASTM Test Designation D 1557
unless otherwise specified in the soils report
GEOLABS HAWAI
Vi
Page 3
E The compaction equipment may be sheepsfoot vibratory or
pneumatic tire rollers suited to the soil type and capable of
producing the specified results in an expedient and efficient
manner The equipment shall be in good working condition fully
ballasted and self cleaning
F The compaction shall be accomplished in a systematic and uniform
manner over the entire fill area to produce a uniformly compacted
fill
G Fill material placed in an unsatisfactory condition and not in
accordance with these specifications shall not be approved and shall
be removed or reworked until the required density is obtained
H The soils engineer shall be unrestricted access to the fill to
perform the necessary inspections and testing
Removal and Backfilling of Underground Structures
Underground structures such as cesspool cisterns lava tubes
septic tanks wells pipe lines etc shall be demolished and
removed as directed by the soils engineer Backfilling of the
excavations shall be in accordance with these specifications
Seasonal Requirements
A No fill shall be placed during unfavorable weather conditions
When interruption of work is due to heavy rain the previous fill
surface shall be approved before resumption of earthmoving
operations
B The contractor shall maintain the surface of the fill so that it
will be free draining and will not pond surface water
