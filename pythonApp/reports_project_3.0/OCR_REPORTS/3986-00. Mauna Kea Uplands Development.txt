INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Water System Improvements as part of the Mauna Kea
Uplands Development for Mauna Kea Beach Hotel in Ouli South Kohala on the island of
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea some activity during recent
geologic times Hualalai last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both still
active
The project site is underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series
of Mauna Kea which were deposited during the Pleistocene Epoch Stearns and
Macdonald 1946
The Hamakua series is typically composed of basaltic lava flows which are
mantled by a relatively thin layer of palagonitized ash locally referred to as Pahala Ash
The ash soil at the project site differs from Pahala Ash found in other portions of the
island of Hawaii in that the in situ moisture content is generally lower and the soil exists
in a dry friable state
The soils at the site have been subjected to wind erosion which has resulted in the
formation of a desert pavement on the surface of the site This desert pavement is a
surficial layer of cobbles and gravel which were left behind as the finer ash was blown
away
In the area of the project site the lava flows are in the A a form The Ava flow is
generally characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly surface of fragmental clinker underlain
by a dense core of massive basalt which frequently has a thinner less continuous layer of
clinker below the massive core The clinker surface of the lava flows at the project site
has been mantled by the Pahala Ash as discussed above

SITE DESCRIPTION
The proposed project site is located in the vicinity of the Mauna Kea Beach Hotel in
Ouli of South Kohala on the island of Hawaii The locations for the proposed
development are sited to the east of Queen Kaahumanu Highway with ground elevations
ranging from 400 feet to 780 feet above Mean Sea Level MSL In general the
proposed project locations are either undulating or gently sloping and covered with low

proposed booster stations control buildings and reservoirs generally consist of relatively
thin surficial layers of ash with sand gravel and cobbles overlying weathered basalt rock
and clinker formations Based on our evaluation of the subsurface conditions with
respect to the proposed development the principal geotechnical engineering
considerations for the proposed water system improvement project include the following
Foundation support for the proposed reservoirs and control buildings
e Probing and grouting for potential cavities beneath the foundations and
e Site grading
The new reservoir foundations may bear directly on the hard basalt rock formation
or compacted granular fill
Due to the potential presence of cavities as commonly encountered in basaltic lava
flows we recommend that a program of probing and grouting of footing locations be
implemented during construction A representative of Geolabs Hawaii should observe
the probing and grouting operation to evaluate the extent of cavities if encountered
Special attention should be given to the Foundation Probing section of this report
Based on our borings drilled at the proposed cut slopes the areas are generally
underlain by relatively thin layer of ash over weathered basalt and clinker formations

Foundation Probing
Based on our previous experience in the area voids and or cavities may be
present in the basaltic lava flows encountered at the project site To reduce the potential
for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities and or voids below a
footing we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be conducted
during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each spread
footing location and 10 foot on centers for continuous footings The probe holes should
be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned
bottom of footing If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the
probing operation additional probes should be drilled at closer spacings to help delineate
the vertical and lateral extent of the cavities and or voids The probe holes and cavities
discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate
pressures A typical sand cement grout would consist of 1 part sand 1 part cement and
0 7 part water
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and
grouting operation is complete The Contractor should also be made aware that a longer
lag time between probing grouting operations and foundation construction may be
required in the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3986 00 Page 12
cavities and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or
changed conditions are observed
Concrete Slab On Grade
Concrete slabs on grade are planned for both the 618 tank and the 780 tank
Based on the soil conditions encountered in our field exploration and proposed finish floor
elevations it is anticipated the tank subgrade at both tank locations may straddle across
weathered rock and ash In order to provide uniform foundation support beneath the slab
and to minimize the potential for differential settlements it is recommended that the slab
subgrade be over excavated to a minimum of 2 feet and replaced with granular fill
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction A minimum 4 inch thick
compacted base course is recommended underneath the concrete slab on grade
For design of the structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per
square inch per inch of deflection p c i may be used for the compacted base course
A perimeter drain around the outside edge of the slab on grade is recommended to
reduce the potential for water pressure buildup underneath the concrete slab

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site After clearing and
grubbing the reservoir areas should be over excavated to a minimum depth of
24 inches below the future footing and slab subgrade levels to provide the granular
fill layer The granular fill layer should extend a minimum of 24 inches laterally
beyond the limits of the reservoir structure
Fill and Backfill Materials
If required fill materials should consist of well graded granular material with no
particles greater than 6 inches in largest dimension The material should have a
laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell value
of not more than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883 It should contain between 10 and 30 percent passing the
No 200 sieve
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to above the optinum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Excavation

ripping with large bulldozers where the rock is near the existing ground surface
However excavations into the deeper formations may require the use of hoerams
or chipping

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling eleven borings designated as Boring Nos 1 to 11 to depths ranging from
about 10 to 30 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer Geologist who monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 thru A 11
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock materia left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound _
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Very Poot
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD
would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of
excavation
