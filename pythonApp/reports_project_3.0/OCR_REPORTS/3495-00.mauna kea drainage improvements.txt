INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Mauna Kea Drainage Improvements project within the
Mauna Kea Beach Hotel in the Ouli area of South Kohala on the island of Hawaii The
general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed
project These recommendations are intended for design input only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of
this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the Mauna Kea Beach Hotel in the Ouli area of
South Kohala on the island of Hawaii It is proposed to construct a new drainage
channel of either reinforced concrete or concrete rubble masonry rip rap design The
alignment of the proposed drainage channel is situated approximately between the
south wing of the Mauna Kea Beach Hotel and the 10th Hole of the Mauna Kea Golf
Course
Based on the preliminary site plan provided the proposed open drainage
channel is approximately 300 feet long and 15 feet wide We understand that inlet and
outlet structures with wing walls are planned at the ends of the drainage channel
GEOLABS HAWAII

Detailed information pertaining to the design finish grades of the proposed drainage
channel were not available at the time this report was prepared However we
anticipate that a majority of the proposed drainage channel alignment invert will be
below the existing ground surface therefore excavations below the existing ground
surface will be required for the project construction
Our previous experience in the area suggest that the proposed drainage channel
alignment may be underlain by surficial clinkers over basalt formation at greater depths
Special attention was given to the excavation stability and site grading provisions for
the proposed drainage improvements

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic
Series of Mauna Kea deposited during the Pleistocene Epoch Stearns and Macdonald
1946 The Hamakua Volcanic Series is typically composed of basaltic lava flows
which are mantled by a surface layer of palagonitized ash locally referred to as Pahala
Ash
Based on previous experience in the area the lava flows underlying the site are
characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly surface of fragmental clinker underlain by a
dense core of massive basalt which frequently has a thinner less continuous layer of
clinker below the massive core Our experience in the vicinity of the project site
indicates that the upper clinker layers are generally cemented and generally have good
strength characteristics
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located within the Mauna Kea Beach Resort Hotel in the Ouli
area of South Kohala on the island of Hawaii The proposed drainage channel
alignment is situated approximately between the south wing of the Mauna Kea Beach
Hotel and the 10th Hole of the Mauna Kea Golf Course The project site may be
accessed through the existing access road leading to the beach
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3495 00 Page 6
The mauka side of the proposed drainage channel is situated in an existing gully
sloping toward the ocean with steep side slopes on both sides of the gully The bottom
of the gully is approximately 15 to 18 feet below the access road level with ground
cover consisting of tree stumps weathered clinkers and large size boulders up to 6
feet in diameter Retaining walls constructed of basalt cobbles and boulders were
observed at several locations of the existing gully The makai side of the proposed
drainage channel alignment is heavily vegetated with Kiawe trees up to about 20 feet
in height
The existing ground surface elevations at the site range from about 50 feet
Mean Sea Level MSL at the east mauka side to about 5 feet MSL near the south
wing of the hotel makai side
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling one boring to a
depth of about 26 feet below the existing ground surface This was supplemented with
a field reconnaissance and shallow hand dug test pits at selected locations along the
proposed drainage channel alignment The approximate location of the drilled boring
is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our boring encountered weathered clinker materials consisting of
dense sandy gravels and silty sands extending to a depth of about 10 feet below the
ground surface The clinker materials were underlain by hard basalt rock formation
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 26 feet below the existing
ground surface Our site reconnaissance and shallow test pit excavations indicated that
the proposed drainage channel alignment is heavily vegetated along with basalt cobbles

Foundation Support
The dense clinkers and underlying hard basalt rock formation encountered in the
boring should provide adequate support for the proposed reinforced concrete concrete
rubble masonry drainage channel and its appurtenances at the project site An
allowable bearing value of up to 4 000 p s f may be used for the design of the
reinforced concrete drainage channel structure bearing on a 12 inch gravel cushion
layer The gravel cushion layer is intended to provide more uniform bearing support
and should consist of free draining material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C33
No 67 gradation or similar material A schematic detail for the placement of the gravel
cushion layer is shown on Plate 3 For the concrete rubble masonry design the gravel
cushion layer may be eliminated and the drainage channel may bear directly on the
dense clinkers or basalt rock formation The bearing value provided is for dead plus
live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as those
caused by wind and or seismic forces
Footings for the headwall structures may bear directly on the dense clinkers or
basalt rock formation in lieu of the 12 inch gravel cushion layer Footings should be
embedded a minimum of 2 feet below the lowest adjacent finish grade If soft and or
loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations they should
be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation should
be backfilled with compacted select granular fill material
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 400 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that
the soil around foundations is well compacted or undisturbed A coefficient of friction

The backfill immediately behind the drainage channel structure should consist
of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of free draining granular materials with sizes generally
between inch and inch such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation to reduce the potential build up of hydrostatic pressures The remainder of
the backfill should consist of on site and or imported granular materials moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to between 90 and
95 percent relative compaction Over compaction of the channel wall backfill should
be avoided The upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of non expansive
impervious material to reduce water infiltration behind the wall thus reducing the
potential build up of hydrostatic pressures
Since the project site is located in Seismic Zone 3 in accordance with the

the design of retaining structures Dynamic lateral earth pressures due to seismic

Site Grading
Detailed information pertaining to the design grades were not available to us at
the time this report was prepared In general site grading consisting of minor fills and
relatively deep cuts are anticipated for the project construction
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3495 00 Page 11
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce potential
contamination of the excavated materials Soft and or loose soils encountered during
the clearing operations should be over excavated and disposed of properly off site
After clearing and grubbing the areas within the drainage channel limits should
be excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 12 inch thick gravel cushion
below the reinforced concrete channel slab The bottom of the excavation should be
proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of four passes to
aid in detecting and possibly collapsing near surface cavities Any loose or soft spots
encountered during the proof rolling process should be removed and replaced with
compacted granular fill material
The on site clinkers and basalt rock materials generated from the cut areas may
be stockpiled and reused as a source of fill material provided they are properly
processed as recommended below Generally the on site soils should be suitable for
use as general fill provided they are processed and reduced to a relatively well graded
select granular material with a maximum size of about 6 inches Excavated materials
greater than 6 inches in diameter may be broken down for use as fill material or should
be disposed of off site properly
Any imported material if needed should be well graded from coarse to fine with
no particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a
laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 12 or more and should have a
maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883 In addition it should contain between 10 and 30 percent passing
the No 200 sieve Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3495 00 Page 12
conformance with our recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use
In general fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12
inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Backfills behind retaining
walls should be compacted to between 90 and 95 relative compaction Over
compaction of the retaining wall backfill would exert additional lateral earth pressures
on the walls and should be avoided Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures
The finished grades should be sloped to shed water away from the existing
structures and to reduce the potential for ponding In general measures should be
incorporated into the grading of the subject project to reduce the potential for erosion
and to prevent concentration of water around the existing structures
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs
Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office observe the site grading to
evaluate whether any undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation
process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered
in our exploration and assumed in this report

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling one
boring to a depth of about 26 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate
location shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The boring was drilled using a truck mounted
drill rig
The materials encountered in the boring were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
materials encountered are provided on the Log of Boring Plate A 1
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts need
ed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Log of
Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a
rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock
material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and
description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Log of Boring as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Log of
Boring as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
