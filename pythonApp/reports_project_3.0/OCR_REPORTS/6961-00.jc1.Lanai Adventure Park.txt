SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration at the proposed adventure park development, the 
project site is generally underlain by stiff to very stiff residual and/or saprolite soils 
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 41.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface.  We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our 
field exploration. 
Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered in our field exploration, we 
believe that the project site may be classified as Site Class D – Stiff Soil Profile for seismic 
design consideration.  We recommend shallow foundations to support the new one-story 
adventure center building.  Allowable bearing pressures of up to 3,000 psf may be used to 
design the shallow foundations. This bearing value is for dead–plus-live loads and may be 
increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces. 
We recommend installing the proposed cross-plate anchors at least 2 feet below 
the ground surface to develop resistance to support the guy wires of the poles. A ratio of 
the embedment depth and height of the anchorage should be maintained no less than 
1.5. Each cross-plate anchor should be tested for pull-out resistance, to verity the 
design load capacity. 
Based on results of the percolation test conducted for designing disposal of 
wastewater effluent system in accordance with the Department of Health (DOH) testing 
guidelines, the proposed site is considered unsuitable for leach field. 
Site grading operations, consisting of scarification, moisture conditioning and 
compaction, are critical elements of the earthwork.  A Geolabs representative should 
monitor the site grading operations to observe whether undesirable materials are 
encountered during the excavation and scarification process and to confirm whether the 
exposed soil conditions are similar to those encountered in our field exploration. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.4 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed adventure park development in the mountain range near 
Palawai Basin on the Island of Lanai, Hawaii.  The project location and general vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our 
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses pertaining to the design of 
foundations, site grading, and pavements only.  The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.5 
Project Considerations 
The project involves design and construction of an adventure park by Synergo, 
which is based in Portland, Oregon. We understand that the proposed adventure park 
consists of eight ziplines, an adventure center, and mountain bike trails with other 
adventure activity courses. Based on the information provided, we understand that the 
ziplines are planned to be installed through the mountain terrain, supported by power 
poles (Class 1 and Class 2) about 12 to 14 feet and/or 40 to 50 feet tall. Three of eleven 
pole locations do not have vehicle access and are planned to be constructed via helicopter 
lifting for equipment and material. 
The proposed adventure center consists of a one-story building with shop, locker 
room and restroom. Based on the information provided, we understand that a leaching 
field system is planned at the adventure center site. A percolation test was conducted 
near the planned location for design of the wastewater disposal system in accordance 
with the Department of Health (DOH) testing guidelines. 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling at each pole location for 
the zipline tour where vehicle access was permitted, using a truck-mounted drill rig. 
Where vehicle access was not available, the subsurface soil conditions were examined 
using a portable Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP).

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.4 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Lanai is a shield volcano built by eruptions at the summit and along 
three rift zones more than 1.20 to 1.46 million years ago. The principal rift zone trends 
northwestward as a broad ridge and is responsible for the conspicuous elongation of the 
island in that direction. A less conspicuous bulge on the southern side of the island is 
the result of volcanic extrusion on the southwest rift zone. The rift zones intersect at the 
island summit. The summit of the shield volcano collapsed to form a caldera from which 
a shallow graben, bordered by an echelon of step faults, extends south-southeast 
toward Manele Bay. Numerous dikes exposed in the sea cliff indicate that this Manele 
Graben lies along another rift zone. 
The caldera was largely, but not completely, filled by lava flows and the present 
Palawai Basin is a remnant of the caldera. Just to the west of it, Miki Basin (with an 
average diameter of about 0.9 miles) is a nearly filled pit crater. The top of the ridge 
between these basins is about 140 feet above the floor of the Palawai Basin. On the 
south, the floor of Palawai Basin merges with the floor of the Manele Graben, where the 
most recent lava flows in the caldera overflowed onto the outer slope of the volcano. 
The Island of Lanai was built by the extrusion of thin-bedded a’a and pahoehoe 
tholeiitic basaltic flows that are generally inclined at about 6 to 15 degrees from 
horizontal (where not disturbed by faulting). Volcanic rocks on the Island of Lanai are 
grouped as the Lanai Volcanic Series. The near-surface soils generally consist of 
well-drained, fine-textured and moderately fine-textured soils derived from volcanic ash 
and the in-situ weathering of the igneous rocks. The formation of the Island of Lanai 
was also the result of large changes in sea-level that caused submergence and 
re-emergence of the land mass through geologic time. Faulting and stream erosion in 
conjunction with changes in sea-level are also responsible for the high sea cliffs along 
the southern and western coastlines. 
The project site is northerly from the Palawai Basin and traverse across 
Kapano Gulch in the mountain range. Most of the soils in the project area are

2.5 
Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
nine (9) borings, designated as Boring Nos. 901 through 909, extending to depths of 16.5 to 
41.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The approximate boring locations are shown on 
the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the subsurface conditions at the site consisted of stiff to very stiff 
clayey residual and/or saprolite soils extending to the maximum depth explored of 
approximately 41.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
In addition, fourteen (14) dynamic cone penetrometer (DCP) tests were 
conducted, designated as P-1 through P-14, at the proposed zipline pole locations and 
adventure center building site. The DCP tests were extended to depths of about 6 to 11 
feet below the ground surface. The approximate DCP locations are also shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the subsurface conditions at the site consisted of 3 to 4 feet of medium 
stiff to very stiff clayey soils overlying hard saprolitic soils. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field 
exploration.  It should be noted that groundwater levels can significantly vary depending 
on rainfall, temperature, surface runoff, groundwater withdrawal, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration methodology and the Logs of 
Borings are presented in Appendix A.  Results of the laboratory tests performed on 
selected soil samples retrieved from our field exploration are presented in Appendix B. 
Results of DCP tests are presented in Appendix C.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration for the proposed adventure park development generally 
encountered stiff to very stiff clayey residual and/or saprolite soils to the maximum depth 
drilled of about 41.5 feet below the ground surface.  Based on the subsurface conditions 
encountered at the site, we envision using shallow continuous footings to support the 
new one-story adventure center building planned at the site. An allowable bearing 
pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design of 
shallow foundation system. 
We recommend installing the proposed cross-plate anchors within the very stiff 
in-situ clayey soils to develop resistance to support the guy wires of the poles. The 
cross-plate anchor should be embedded at least 2 feet below the ground surface or 
deeper. Each cross-plate anchor should be tested for pull-out resistance, to verify the 
design load capacity. 
Detailed discussions and specific recommendations for design of foundations, 
retaining structures, site grading, pavements, and other geotechnical aspects of the 
project are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Shallow Foundations 
We envision that the proposed one-story adventure center building subgrade will 
consist of stiff in-situ clayey soils.  Therefore, we recommend using shallow spread 
and/or continuous footings to support the proposed structure at the project site.  As an 
alternative, the foundations for support of the buildings may also consist of 
thickened-edge slab footings. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of 
shallow foundations. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be 
increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces.

The foundation subgrades should be scarified by at least 8 inches and 
re-compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  The scarification should 
extend at least 2 feet laterally beyond the edges of footings. 
Where soft clayey soils are encountered at the foundation subgrade, we 
recommend over-excavating the soft clayey soils below the building footing footprint to 
expose the underlying stiff in-situ clayey soils or over-excavating to a maximum depth of 
24 inches below the subgrade layer.  The subgrade over-excavation should extend at 
least 3 feet beyond the edge of the footings.  The over-excavation should be backfilled 
with compacted structural fill materials. 
The structural fill materials should consist of well-graded granular materials 
generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the 
occurrence of voids in the compacted mass.  The structural fill materials should be 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed 
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
In general, the building footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finished grade.  Footings constructed adjacent to slopes or 
on slopes should be embedded to a sufficient depth to provide a minimum horizontal 
setback distance of at least 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footing to the 
face of the slope.  Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be 
embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge 
of the utility trench, or the footings should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts 
of the utility lines. 
In order to reduce the potential for appreciable differential settlement, the new 
building structure should be situated entirely over cuts and should not straddle across 
cut and fill transitions.  If the building structure is to straddle a cut and fill transition,

pressure value assumes that the soils around the foundations are well compacted.  
Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches 
below the finished grade should be neglected.

over-excavated to expose the underlying firm materials and replaced with compacted 
fill.  A Geolabs representative should observe the footing excavations prior to placing 
the reinforcing steel and/or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions and 
the required embedment depths.  Observation of the foundation excavations should be 
designated a “Special Inspection” item in accordance with Section 1704 of the 
International Building Code (2006).

Retaining Structures 
We envision that retaining walls would likely be required at the site for grade 
separation.  Based on the subsurface conditions, the following general guidelines may 
be used for design of the retaining walls. 
3.4.1 Wall Foundations 
We believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed in accordance with 
the recommendations and parameters presented in the “Shallow Foundations” 
section herein.  In addition, retaining wall foundations should be at least 
18 inches wide and should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the 
lowest adjacent finished grades.  For sloping ground conditions, the footing 
should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 6-foot setback distance measured 
horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope.  Wall 
footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in 
stepped footings.

3.4.3 Drainage 
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures.  A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of 
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), 
placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of 
the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes.  As an alternative, a 
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used 
instead of the drainage material.  The prefabricated drainage product should also 
be connected hydraulically to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
The backfill from the bottom of the wall to the bottom of the weephole should 
consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for significant water 
infiltration into the subsurface.  In addition, the upper 12 inches of the retaining wall 
backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for 
significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure unless covered by 
concrete slabs at the surface.

and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the over-excavation

After clearing and grubbing, the exposed subgrades should be scarified to a depth

moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.

3.5.2 Fill Materials 
The excavated on-site soils may be re-used as a source of general fill and backfill 
materials, provided that the materials are screened of over-sized materials (greater 
than 6 inches in maximum dimension) and are free of vegetation, deleterious 
materials, and clay lumps.  Where used as fill and backfill materials, the on-site 
soils should be properly moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Remnant boulders may be encountered within the residual and saprolite soils.  The 
boulders should be crushed or hauled off-site.  Imported general fill materials 
needed to fill the site may consist of materials with a low to moderate expansion 
potential.  Imported general fill materials should consist of soil materials with a 
maximum particle size of 3 inches or less with sufficient fines (between 10 and 
60 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve) to prevent the occurrence of voids 
in the compacted mass.  In addition, general fill materials should have a CBR value 
of 8 or greater and a swell of 2 percent or less when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D1883.  It should be noted that the general fill requirements presented 
herein are intended as guidelines only and may be modified based on additional 
laboratory testing and field observations on the available fill materials conducted by 
Geolabs.  
The select granular fill should consist of non-expansive select granular materials, 
such as crushed coral or basalt.  The material should be well graded from coarse to 
fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension.  The material also 
should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. In 
addition, the material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should 
have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D1883.

3.5.4 Cut and Fill Slopes 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, we believe 
the planned cut slopes will expose stiff clayey soils.  The cut slopes planned at the 
site may be designed with a slope inclination of one and one-half horizontal to 
one vertical (1.5H:1V) or flatter.  In areas where soft clayey soils, unstable, and/or 
friable materials are exposed at the cut slope faces, the cut slopes may need to be 
flattened.  Therefore, provisions to flatten the cut slopes and/or slope stabilization 
alternatives should be considered in the design of the project.  A Geolabs 
representative should observe and map the cut slopes during construction to 
evaluate whether the exposed cut slope materials are consistent with our 
assumptions. 
Permanent fill slopes constructed using the on-site materials (or imported fill 
materials) may also be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.  Fills 
placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the 
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding.  The filling operations 
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers 
in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations.  Fill slopes should 
be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a 
well-compacted slope face.  In addition, slope planting should be provided as soon 
as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes. 
Areas to receive fill should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches, moisture 
conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to at 
least 90 percent relative compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry 
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same 
soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557.  Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

Drainage 
It should be noted that surface and subsurface drainage are especially critical 
considering the potentially expansive nature of the clayey soils. Therefore, the finished 
grades outside the new buildings should be sloped to shed water away from the slabs 
and foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding around the structure.  Gutter 
systems should be installed around the building and discharge should be diverted away 
from the slab and foundation areas.  Excessive landscape watering near the slabs and 
foundations also should be avoided.  Planters next to foundations should be avoided or 
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration 
into the subsurface. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
above foundation recommendations because ponded water could cause subsurface soil 
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength.  The foundation excavations 
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of 
the concrete to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface.  
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to 
drain surface water runoff away from the slabs and foundations.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling nine boreholes, designated as Boring Nos. 901 through 901, extending to 
depths of about 16.5 to 41.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary wash 
drilling/coring tools. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous basis.  These classifications were further reviewed visually and by 
testing in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  Deviations made 
to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-9. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content and Unit Weight tests were performed on selected soil samples 
to aid in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and 
correlating the layers between the borings.  The moisture content tests were performed 
in general accordance with ASTM D2216.  The unit weight tests were performed in 
general accordance with ASTM D2937.  The test results are presented on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths in Appendix A. 
Twenty-two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification.  The 
test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B-1 through B-3. 
Fifteen 
Unconsolidated 
Undrained 
Triaxial 
Compression 
(TXUU) 
tests 
(ASTM D2850) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained 
shear strength of the silty and clayey soils encountered.  The approximate in-situ 
effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the 
relatively “undisturbed” soil sample.  The test results and the stress-strain curves are 
presented on Plates B-4 through B-18. 
Three one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed 
samples of the near-surface clayey soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the 
subsurface soils. The test results are summarized on Plate B-19. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a 
bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils.  The test results are presented on Plate B-20. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the relationship between the moisture 
content and the dry density of the near-surface soils as fill materials. The test results are 
presented on Plate B-21.

Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Tests 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were also explored by conducting 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) testing at fourteen locations, designated as P-1 
through P-14, extending to depths of about 6 to 11 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The approximate DCP locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Portable Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) was used for probing by driving an 
8-mm diameter 60° steel cone tip with an 8-kg hammer falling 575-mm in vertical height. 
The blow counts were recorded per every or near 75-mm (3-inch) penetration and 
converted to standard penetration resistance (SPT, ASTM D1586) using correlation 
between Penetration Index (PI) and SPT, developed by Sowers and Hedges and later 
Livneh and Ishai. Both PI and SPT blow counts are shown on the Logs of DCP at the 
appropriate depths, presented on the Logs of DCP, Plates C-1.1 through C-14.3.
