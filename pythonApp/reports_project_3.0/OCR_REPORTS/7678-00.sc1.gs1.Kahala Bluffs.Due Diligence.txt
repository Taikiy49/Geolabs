Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age.  After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone eruptions.  These later eruptions 
are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea.  When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations.  When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level.

When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosional processes are dominated by 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported 
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium.  Once these 
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become 
dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. 
When alluvial and colluvial deposits are left in place for long periods of time, 
chemical processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously, causing a breakdown or 
further weathering of the material.  Chemical processes also cause induration, or 
cementation, of the coarse-grained portion of the sediment, resulting in a poorly 
consolidated sedimentary rock, or conglomerate.  Simultaneously, erosion continues in 
areas above the valley floors and upstream in headwaters.  This continued erosion 
generates material, which is transported downslope, covering the older alluvial deposits.  
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport, these newer sediments are 
generally transient in terms of geologic time.  In addition, their consistency and density 
are generally less than those of the older, partially consolidated deposits.   
The project site is located near the foot of Mauumae Ridge near the confluence 
of the Waialae-Nui Stream Channel.  The geologic maps indicated that the site is 
underlain mainly by basalt rock formation of the Koolau Volcanic Series with alluvial 
deposits near the makai side of the project site.  We anticipate that the basalt rock 
formation is overlain by fill and colluvial deposits in the gently sloping area of the site. 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is on the north side of Keanu Street, which is accessed via Hunakai 
Street in the Waialae-Kahala area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. Based on 
the information provided, the property encompasses about 4.25 acres of partly developed 
and undeveloped land and is bounded by the existing Waialae Gardens condominium 
complex on the eastern boundary and other existing homes on the western boundary of 
the project site. In addition, portion of the project site bounded by the Nahona Kahala 
residential development at the southern boundary. The site is accessed via Hunakai and 
Keanu Streets at the southern boundary. A second site access is from a locked chain link 
gate at the western end of the site via Keanu Street.  
The parcel is generally square in shape and encompasses relatively flat to gently 
sloping graded terrain at the southern half of the lot. The southern portion of the lot is 
elevated above Keanu Street and appears to have been previously cleared and graded. 
An existing Hawaiian Electric Company (HECO) utility easement crosses through the 
property in a general easterly to westerly orientation. Existing overhead power poles reside 
within this easement.

Site Reconnaissance 
Our geologist conducted a general ground surface reconnaissance of the 
accessible flatter portions of the site during dry weather conditions on February 9, 2018.  
We observed the following existing site conditions at the time of our site visit: 
The southern flatter portion of the lot appears to have been previously filled 
and graded to achieve the existing flat to gently sloping ground surface. 
Some limited exposure of the embankment indicates that the fills are 
generally rocky containing mixed cobbles and boulders with variable soils 
and some construction waste debris (concrete). The existing fill appears to 
be at least medium dense and as thick as about 10 to 15 feet at the front 
portion of the lot adjacent to the lower Keanu Street entrance. 
The ground surface at the southern portion of the lot is mostly covered with 
tall grass and shrubbery with limited exposure of the ground surface. Large 
boulders residing at the ground surface were not observed at this portion of 
the lot. However, we observed some abandoned vehicles, refuse and local 
areas containing mounds of green waste. 
We observed an area of the ground surface containing concrete material 
that appears to have been spread for disposal. The thickness of the 
concrete could not be determined by visual observation. 
The surface soils at the southern half of the site appear to be variable with 
mainly granular (sandy and gravelly) soil materials including cobbles 
observed at the ground surface in the limited areas that are devoid of 
vegetation (existing foot trails).

The northern half of the property encompasses a hillside composed of basalt 
rock formation standing at overall inclinations of about one and one half 
horizontal to one vertical (1.5H:1V) to 1H:1V. The hillside is irregular and 
contains some higher relief and fractured rock outcroppings that could 
present potential rockfall hazards to the lower portion of the lot. 
The rocky hillside is mostly covered by ground surface vegetation such as 
tall grass, Haole Koa and Bougainvillea shrubbery. Several large Kiawe 
trees were observed on the hillside in the vicinity of some larger rock 
outcroppings protruding from the surrounding ground surfaces. We observed 
some very widely scattered void structures in the rock face representing 
small lava tube structures on the face of the hillside. 
At the toe of the rock slope comprising the northern half of the property, we 
observed many anomalous loose basalt boulders and clusters/piles of 
boulders that appear to have placed or pushed towards the toe of the slope. 
The deposits appear to extend outward from the slope toe within the tall 
grass for distances of about 20 to 30 feet. These boulders appear to 
represent man-made deposits. 
We also observed some boulders and blocks of basalt rock that appear to 
represent fallen boulder deposits located within about 5 to 15 feet of the 
slope toe. The boulders exhibit a more natural and irregular fractured 
appearance without markings resulting from machine handling. These rocks 
appear to represent fallen boulders (rockfall) from the adjacent hillslope. 
We did not observe visual evidence of mass slope instability or rock 
instability associated with potential rockslide conditions. 
Based on our observations, the hillside appears capable of producing 
potential rockfall hazards with block sizes generally on the order of about 
1 to 3 feet in dimension. Some larger blocks of fallen rock observed at the 
slope toe approach about 4 feet in larger dimension. There are a number of 
boulders on the slope that appear to be precarious with limited support and 
exhibit the potential to produce falling rock hazards. Therefore, rockfall 
mitigation should be implemented to protect the proposed down slope 
development.

Conceptual Rockfall Mitigation Alternatives 
Based on our visual observation of the existing hillside conditions with respect to 
the proposed home development scheme, it appears that some form of rockfall mitigation 
control(s) may be necessary to protect the down slope dwellings and infrastructure. 
Rockfall mitigation may consider the options of 1) Avoidance, or 2) Containment. 
Avoidance alternatives may consist of providing a designated and maintained 
setback or clear-area buffer zone between the slope toe and the proposed development. 
Avoidance alternatives are favorable where open space may allow. Avoidance alternatives 
are generally of lower construction and lower maintenance cost than containment. It 
should be noted that even buffer zones and catchment ditches require periodic 
maintenance to remove accumulations of fallen rock to maintain the system effectiveness. 
Some conceptual mitigation schemes involving avoidance may include: 
1. 
Setback of the proposed development to create a designated rock 
deposition zone and buffer area along the toe of the slope. 
2. 
Excavation of a catchment ditch or graded buffer terrain at the slope toe to 
contain falling rock.

Containment alternatives may consist of the construction of specialized rock 
retention or containment devices/structures on the hillside or along the toe of the hillside. 
The alternatives should be considered if avoidance alternatives (buffer zone or catchment 
ditch) are not possible due to limited space or other factors. Containment alternatives are 
of generally higher construction cost and have the additional cost of periodic component 
maintenance and eventual replacement of the structures as the life cycle is affected by 
environmental corrosion and potential damage from rock impact or containment. Some 
conceptual mitigation schemes involving containment may include: 
1. 
Ring net or wire mesh drapery covering the face of the rock slope to control 
the fall of rock toward the slope toe at a designated collection point. 
2. 
Rockfall impact barrier fencing constructed along the slope toe to catch 
falling rock. 
Uncontrolled Fills 
Based on our site reconnaissance, uncontrolled fill materials up to about 10 to 
15 feet in height were observed at the project site. In general, we recommend that the 
existing uncontrolled fills disclosed during additional exploration and/or site grading be 
over-excavated, where necessary, to expose the underlying firm ground.  The resulting 
over-excavation should be backfilled with compacted fill.  The areal extent and depth of 
over-excavation should be determined in the field during construction by the project 
geotechnical engineer.  Environmental testing of the existing uncontrolled fill materials will 
be required. 
Rock Excavation & Lava Tubes 
We anticipate that the underlying basalt rock formation may be encountered at 
relatively shallow depths in the mauka portion of the site.  Hard rock excavation using 
hoerams may be required in this area for site grading and utility installation. 
Where basalt rock formation is encountered near the finished grades, proof-rolling 
the rocky subgrade should be performed to collapse near-surface cavities.

Existing MSE Wall 
The existing wall along the Nahona Kahala development appears to be an MSE 
wall system.  In general, an MSE wall system is a composite wall system, which utilizes 
high-density polyethylene, galvanized metal strips, or other reinforcing elements, to 
reinforce the backfill zone and improve the shear strength of the reinforced soil zone. This 
composite system essentially forms a gravity wall structure.  
Because of the high wall heights of up to about 9 feet, we believe that soil 
reinforcement elements would be needed. Typically, the reinforcing elements are about 
0.7 to 1 times the wall height.  Therefore, 6.5 to 9 feet long reinforcing elements are 
anticipated for the 9-foot high wall. 
We recommend that as-built drawings for the existing wall be obtained to review the 
wall construction and to verify that the wall and reinforcing elements are not within the 
project property.
