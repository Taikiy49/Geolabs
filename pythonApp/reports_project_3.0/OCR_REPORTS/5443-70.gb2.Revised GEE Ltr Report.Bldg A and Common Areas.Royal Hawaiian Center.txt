SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Based on the boring information contained in the above mentioned report the
subsurface conditions at the proposed improvement and expansion areas of
Building A generally consisted of fill materials extending to an elevation of about 1 to
1 foot Mean Sea Level MSL It should be noted that Portland cement concrete was
encountered in the fill layer Soft loose lagoonal deposits extending to an elevation of
about 30 to 34 feet MSL were encountered below the fill materials The lagoonal
deposits consisted of silty sand gravel and sandy silts Below the lagoonal deposits
medium dense and stiff alluvial deposits silty sands and sandy silts were encountered
in the borings extending to elevations of about 36 to 39 feet MSL The alluvial deposits
were underlain by medium dense to dense coralline deposits that extended to
elevations of about 41 to 53 feet MSL Stiff alluvial deposits consisting of sandy silts
were encountered below the coralline deposits extending to elevations of about 60 to
63 feet MSL The alluvial deposits were underlain by medium dense coralline detritus
extending to the maximum depth explored of 101 feet
Groundwater was encountered at the project site at elevations ranging between
0 7 and 0 5 feet MSL It should be noted that groundwater levels could fluctuate
depending on tidal fluctuations due to the proximity of the project site to the
Pacific Ocean seasonal precipitation groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other
factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the generally poor subsurface conditions encountered below the fill
layer we recommend using a deep foundation system to support the new structural
elements planned for the improvements and expansion project Due to the limited space
for the construction work and noise considerations we recommend utilizing micropiles to
support the new structural elements planned Detailed discussion of our geotechnical
recommendations for the project foundation design are presented in the following
sections
Micropiles
In general the micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter usually
less than 12 inches drilled and grouted pile with steel reinforcing The micropile
foundation typically is constructed by drilling a hole with or without casing placing
reinforcing steel in the hole and grouting the hole Micropiles are desirable because they
can be installed readily in access restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and
ground conditions In addition installation of the micropiles generally causes minimal
disturbance to adjacent structures the adjacent soils and the environment
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site we estimate that
a micropile system with a grout bulb diameter of 7 5 inches may be used to support the
new structures Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site we
recommend that each micropile be designed based on an allowable compressive load
capacity of 50 tons 100 kips The allowable compressive load capacity of the micropiles

To provide an allowable compressive load capacity of 50 tons 100 kips we
recommend extending the micropiles through the lagoonal deposits and embedding the
grout bulb a minimum of 30 feet into the bearing layer consisting of stiff alluvial deposits
and medium dense to dense coralline deposits Based on the boring information we

therefore the tip elevation of the micropile would extend to approximately 65 feet MSL
The micropiles will derive its vertical support primarily from skin friction between the grout
and the surrounding alluvial and coralline deposits
To reduce the potential for caving in or necking of the lagoonal deposits which
would result in less grout cover around the steel reinforcement potential corrosion issue
we recommend using a cased micropile system permanent casing The outside
diameter OD of the permanent casing should be about 7 5 inches same as the grout
bulb size The permanent casing should extend 3 feet into the bearing layer or to
approximately 38 feet MSL
Lateral Load Resistance
It should be noted that the micropile foundation system provides low lateral load
resistance due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile Therefore the
lateral load resistance contributed by the micropiles should be neglected Lateral
loads imposed on the new structure foundations may be resisted primarily by
passive pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and grade
beams and by shear acting along the sides of the pile caps and grade beams
Passive pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and grade
beams may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per
square foot per foot of depth pcf above the groundwater level and 175 pcf below
the groundwater level Additional resistance to lateral loads may be provided by
shear resistance between the sides of the pile caps and grade beams and the
adjacent soils A side shear resistance of up to 150 pounds per square feet psf
may be used for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces only
Micropile Foundation Settlements
Settlements of the micropile foundations will result primarily from elastic
compression of the pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total
settlement of the pile supported foundations to be 0 5 inches or less with differential
settlements between columns supported on micropiles not exceeding about
one half of the total settlement We believe that these settlements are essentially
elastic and should occur as the loads are applied

Micropile Load Test Program
It should be noted that the compressive load capacity of the micropiles is highly
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the
contractor to install the micropile Therefore the compressive load capacity of the
micropile may vary considerably between different contractors and micropile
foundation systems In order to determine whether the contractor s methods of
micropile installation are adequate and to determine the ultimate compressive load
capacity we recommend performing at least one pre production compressive load
test on a sacrificial micropile In general the purpose of the pre production load
test on a sacrificial micropile is to fulfill the following objectives
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed
by the contractor to install the micropiles to the depths required
To assess the contractor s method of drilling and grout injection
In general the pre production load test should be performed in accordance with
ASTM D1143 Standard Loading Procedure Based on experience we believe
that the load test should be conducted no earlier than 7 days after completion of the
micropile installation to allow the grout adequate time to cure Additional micropiles
may be used for reaction during the compressive load testing of the pre production
load test micropile The reaction micropiles may be installed to depths as deep as
the load test micropile to provide adequate reaction in uplift to be determined by
the contractor Alternately the pre production micropile may be tested in pullout
The use of reaction micropiles may not be required for the testing
The load test micropile should be loaded gradually to at least 200 percent of the
allowable design load in compression We recommend holding the maximum test
load 200 percent of the design load for a minimum of 4 to 8 hours depending on
the recorded movements of the load test micropile The pre production load test is
an integral part of the design of the micropile foundation system
In addition to the pre production load test we also recommend performing pullout

Slabs On Grade
For the interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend
providing a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of open graded gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below the slabs The open graded gravel cushion fill
would serve as a capillary moisture break and would provide for uniform support of the
slabs To reduce the potential for excessive future moisture infiltration through the slab
and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier is
recommended on top of the open graded gravel cushion layer
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at
least 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade It should be emphasized that the areas
adjacent to the slab edges should be backfilled tightly against the edges of the slabs with
relatively impervious soils These areas also should be graded to divert water away from
the slabs and to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs
Slab subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and recompacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by
ASTM D1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Soft and or yielding areas encountered at the slab subgrades should be
over excavated to expose firm and or dense materials and the resulting excavation
should be backfilled with aggregate subbase compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed
off site
