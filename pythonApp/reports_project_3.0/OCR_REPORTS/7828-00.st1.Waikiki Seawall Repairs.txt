PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
It is proposed to repair the existing compromised seawalls at Queen’s Surf of 
Waikiki Beach in Honolulu on Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The general vicinity of the project 
site is shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that sections of the existing 
rock seawalls about 460 feet long were compromised by the wave action and erosion. 
Based on the current design concept, a GFRW system is planned to be installed behind 
the existing seawall to reduce the lateral earth pressure on the existing seawall. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Based on field explorations conducted by others, the subsurface conditions behind 
the existing seawall is anticipated to be fill materials and beach deposits.  The fill materials 
may consist of stiff sandy silts with some gravel.  We understand that various amounts of 
concrete debris, cobbles and boulders may also be anticipated in the fill materials behind 
the existing seawall. Beach deposits may consist of very loose to dense silty sands with 
traces of gravel. 
Based on the proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean, groundwater should 
generally be encountered near mean sea level and vary with tidal fluctuations. In addition,

GEOTEXTILE FABRIC RETAINING WALL 
In order to reduce the lateral earth pressures acting against the existing seawall, 
we understand that it is desired to construct a GFRW behind the existing seawall. The 
following contains our recommendations for the design of the GFRW planned at the 
project site. 
The GFRW system is a composite wall system, which utilizes the woven geotextile 
fabric to reinforce the backfill zone and to improve the shear strength of the reinforced 
soil zone. This composite wall system essentially forms a gravity wall structure with an 
ability to carry appreciable structural loads. In addition, geotextile fabric retaining walls 
are desirable due to the flexibility of the wall, ease of construction, high load carrying 
capacity, and economy. 
In general, the reinforced zone should extend laterally behind the wall face to a 
distance of approximately 80 percent of the wall height. The woven geotextile fabric 
should be placed at about every 24 inches of fill height (maximum vertical spacing).  The 
front face of the woven geotextile fabric should be wrapped at least 3 feet from the face 
of the wall. 
The reinforced fill material should consist of select granular fill materials with a 
maximum particle size of 3 inches compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction (ASTM D1557). The reinforced fill material should have an angle of internal 
friction of at least 38 degrees when compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. 
Unit weight of 130 pounds per cubic foot (pcf) may be used for the compacted select 
granular materials. A coefficient of friction of 0.45 may be used to evaluate the resistance 
to sliding between the wall and the on-site granular materials. 
Geotextile fabric retaining wall (GFRW) required behind the existing seawall may 
be constructed with a vertical face inclination. In order to prevent compaction of the 
backfill directly against the existing seawall and to reduce the transfer of lateral loads onto 
the seawall, backfill within 12 inches from the face of the existing seawall should consist 
of free-draining materials, such as 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) or similar 
material. The existing seawall also should be checked to resist limited lateral earth 
pressures due to the free-draining open-graded gravel.  In general, the existing seawall 
should be designed for active earth pressure of 10 pounds per square foot per foot of 
depth (pcf). 
In addition, the GFRW structure should be well-drained to reduce the potential for 
buildup of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of 24-inch wide 
strips of prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, placed at 
10 feet on centers placed behind the reinforced soil zone.

We understand that it is desired to plug the existing seawall weep holes to reduce 
the potential for migration of fine-grained soils through the weep holes from wave action.  
Therefore, the drainage system for the GFRW may not have adequate daylighting to 
relieve the potential buildup of hydrostatic pressures.  Because the seawall will not have 
weep holes, we believe that the existing seawall should be designed to withstand buildup 
of hydrostatic pressures.  For the design of seawall without weep holes, the normal high 
groundwater level may be assumed at an elevation of +3 feet Mean Sea Level. 
In addition, we recommend that the 12-inch thick 3B Fine gravel immediately 
behind the seawall extend to the bottom of the seawall to help dissipate the potential 
buildup of hydrostatic pressures naturally into the ground.  In addition, a filtration trench 
12 inches wide by 12 inches deep be installed at the back of the GFRW.  The filtration 
trench should be backfilled with 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) or similar 
material wrapped on all sides with filter fabric. 
LIMITATIONS 
The recommendations submitted in this letter report are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from subsurface investigations conducted by others. Variations of 
assumed subsurface conditions may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations 
may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, 
Geolabs should be contacted to re-evaluate the recommendations presented in this letter 
report.
