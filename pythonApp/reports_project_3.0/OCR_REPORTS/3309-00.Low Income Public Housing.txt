INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Low Income Public Housing project in Waikoloa in the
district of South Kohala on the island of Hawaii The general location of the project
site and its vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering findings and recommendations
derived from our analyses These recommendations are intended for design input only
and may not be appropriate to form the basis for construction cost estimates nor for
construction bidding purposes

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These include the following Kohala which has been long extinct Mauna
Kea which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted
in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The native ground at the project site is underlain by A a basalt rock formation
mixed with some relatively thin layers of volcanic wind blown ash derived from the
volcanic activity of Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa The ash generally has high in situ
moisture contents and low in situ densities The ash is characterized as having
thixotropic properties i e itis susceptible to significant loss of strength when remolded
under equipment vehicular or seismic loads

SEISMIC_CONSIDERATIONS
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near
the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes
In this process when the loose saturated sand and or cohesionless silt deposit is
subjected to vibration such as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and
decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable
to occur rapidly enough due to a large areal extent of the deposit to dissipate the
build up of pore pressure the effective stress internal strength of the soils is reduced
Under sustained vibration the pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden
pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave
as a viscous fluid
During liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal
and vertical movements if not confined resulting in significant deformations Soils
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3309 00 Page 10
most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained sands and
loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that liquefaction will not
occur if the deposit is greater than 50 feet below the ground surface In deeper
deposits the greater overburden pressure is sufficient to prevent liquefaction from
occurring The major factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of a soil include
the following
1 Grain Size Distribution Fine grained and uniform sands are generally
more susceptible to liquefaction than coarse grained or well graded
sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose fine sands and silts are most susceptible
to liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
3 Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake
In general the in situ moisture contents of the near surface silty gravel soils
appear to be relatively low Considering the relatively thin ash layer encountered and
the absence of near surface groundwater it appears that the potential for seismically
induced liquefaction at the site is low

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration of the uncontrolled fill generally encountered very loose to
medium dense rocky material consisting of well graded gravelly cobbles and boulders
typically less than 24 inches in size with sands and silts Based on the field data and
topographic analyses the uncontrolled fills are estimated to range from near zero to
about 21 feet in thickness The thickness of fills encountered in the test pits are shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 and generally confirm the topographic analyses We would
like to emphasize that the fill thicknesses shown on the Site Plan are based on test pit
data and may not represent the actual fill thickness across the site
Based on field observations large boulders of 4 to 6 feet in diameter were
observed in the area outlined as Zone A on the Site Plan and along the toe of the
uncontrolled fill slope It should be noted that large boulders may be buried within the
fil mass at depths greater than those penetrated during the field exploration and
between test pits Test pit penetration was restricted to depths of about 6 to 9 feet
below the existing ground surface due to the very loose caving nature of the
uncontrolled fills Boulders in excess of about 2 feet in size were not encountered in
the test pits with the exception of Test Pit No 1 located near Zone A where refusal
was met at a depth of about 3 feet due to a large boulder
Due to the loose and variable nature of the uncontrolled fill mass we recommend
that the entire uncontrolled fill mass be removed to expose firm natural ground The
uncontrolled fill material may be reused as engineered fill material In general the
removed fill mass need not be segregated when replaced as engineered fill provided
that boulders in excess of 2 feet in dimension are not encountered Larger boulders
should be segregated and be placed in the deeper portions of the proposed fill as
recommended herein Because of the silty nature of the fines in the uncontrolled fill

material we recommend that the general engineered fill be capped with 2 feet of select
granular fill material
Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results we believe that the

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
During clearing and grubbing if significant ash materials and or loose to soft
soils are encountered these materials should be over excavated to expose firm
soil and the resulting over excavation should be backfilled with engineered fill
The over excavated wet ash materials should be disposed of properly off site or
stockpiled for later use as topsoil
In areas to receive fill the subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at
least 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM
D 1557 91 test procedures

Foundations

wood framed structures with slab on grade construction Based on the subsoil
conditions encountered at the site shallow foundations bearing on the native materials
and or compacted engineered fill may be used to support the proposed structures For
design of shallow footing foundations an allowable bearing pressure of up to

caused by wind or seismic forces
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grade If foundations are located next to utility trenches or below grade
structures they should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or below
grade structure
It is recommended that the bottom of all footing excavations in soil be compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel
and concrete If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing
excavations they should be over excavated to expose firm materials The over
excavation should either be backfilled with granular material compacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom be extended down to firm
materials

Asphaltic concrete pavements are anticipated for the parking areas and Portland

primarily of passenger vehicles with occasional light trucks Therefore we have
assumed light traffic loading conditions for design purposes We have made our
preliminary pavement designs assuming the pavement subgrade soil will consist of the
compacted near surface material encountered in our test pits or import fill with a
California Bearing Ratio CBR of at least 20 Based on the above assumptions we
believe the following pavement sections may be used for preliminary design purposes

The subgrade soils for pavements should be scarified to a depth of at least

Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere on
the site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate
landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend the curbs extend a
minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soil below the base course to reduce migration
of landscaping water into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system could
be constructed to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation For long term
performance we recommend a subdrain system be constructed adjacent to
paved landscaped areas

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines will be required for the project For support of
the utility pipes we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the pipes Free
draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to
provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the potential for damaging
the pipes from compaction of the backfill
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site excavated
materials with maximum size not exceeding 6 inches in maximum dimension The
trench backfill should be moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture content
placed in maximum 12 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less
than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground
subsidence Where the utility lines are below pavements the top 2 feet of the backfill
for the utility trench below the subgrade should be compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction
Surface and Subsurface Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the
foundations and pavement should be avoided
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil

saturation and erosion and subsequent loss of strength The foundation excavation
should be properly backfilled against the walls immediately after setting of the concrete
to reduce potential water infiltration
Consideration should be given to the siting of seepage pits septic systems and
drywells such that their discharge be located within native ground rather than within the
engineered fills Discharge into the engineered fill may cause saturation or a migration
of fines within the fill leading to potential future instability and settlement We
recommend the use of solid rings to support the drywell excavation through the surficial
fills to extend the discharge depths of the drywells into the underlying native material
