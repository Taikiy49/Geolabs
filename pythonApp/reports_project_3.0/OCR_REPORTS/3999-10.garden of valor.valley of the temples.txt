PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site currently exists as roughly 4 acres of undeveloped forested land
located at the southern boundary of the Valley of the Temples Memorial Park
The subject site generally encompasses a relatively steep ridgeline hill bounded to the
east by a gully and to the west by a stream tributary
It is our understanding that the site will be developed for additional burial plots as
part of the cemetery expansion Based on the information provided the site grading will
generally consist of cutting down the existing ridgeline and filling of the eastern bounding
drainage gully Cuts and fills ranging up to about 30 feet are planned at the site Based
on the available grading plan the fill materials will originate from the onsite sources

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
W O 3999 10 Page 4
be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island
The younger shield volcano Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
in age Ice Age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae
became extinct while Koolau was still active After a long period of volcanic inactivity
during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls which are then transported
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium In general
stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream flows transmit
large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this transport of sediments is
intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture
of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional base levels change
these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin
to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material
Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion
of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate
Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in
headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is transported downslope
covering the older alluvial deposits Depending on the local base level and rate of
transport these new sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time
In addition their consistency and density are generally less than those of older partially
consolidated deposits
The project site is situated at the base of the Koolau Pali a high cliff which extends
along most of the windward side of the Koolau Range on the island of Oahu The climate
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3999 10 Page 5
along the Pali is typified by a high to very high average annual rainfall Following the
main shield building stage of the Koolau volcano in the late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Epoch stream erosion landsliding and deep weathering of the basalt rock became the
dominant agents affecting the morphology of the area
The project site is located on the large colluvial apron deposited near the base of
the Koolau Range The colluvial apron formed as a result of the accumulation of soil and
rock materials derived from large scale erosion and landsliding along the Koolau Pali
Subsequently stream erosion incised these deposits to create the present day
topography of the region as evidenced by the occurrence of multiple hill and gully terrain
The project site generally exists as a prominent ridgeline composed of alluvial and
colluvial soil deposits The ridge is separated by an incised drainage gully to the east and
a well developed stream channel located to the west of the ridge The ridgeline merges
northerly into the developed landscape of the existing Garden of Patriotism of the Valley
of the Temples Memorial Park
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The proposed project site is located at the southern boundary of the existing Valley
of the Temples Memorial Park The site is located at the southern termination of Avenue
of the Temples Road which provides access to the existing Garden of Patriotism burial
grounds The site exists as a north to south oriented hill bordered by drainage channels
The site is densely forested with fern and hao tree growth scattered guava plum and
lauhala The ground surface of the ridgetop and hill sides are generally obscured by a
dense growth of fern vegetation Access to the central portion of the site is provided by
an existing dirt road located along the top of the ridgeline
The ground surface elevations range between about 260 feet Mean Sea Level
MSL at the northern portion of the site to about 390 feet MSL at the top of the hill on
the southern boundary of the site Regionally the site slopes to the north However
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3999 10 Page 6
the ridgeline produces hillslopes trending down toward both the east and the west
The gully located to the east of the ridgeline contains an intermittent stream channel
The well developed stream channel to the west of the ridgeline appears to be a tributary
of Ahuimanu Stream The Ahuimanu Stream tributary is situated in a relatively broad
stream gully with associated flood terraces bordering the banks
The gully located on the eastern side of the ridgeline is essentially a narrow ravine
incised by intermittent stream activity The western and eastern slope bordering the gully
ranges from about one and one half horizontal to one vertical 172H 1V to two and one
half horizontal to one vertical 2 H 1V and slopes down toward the intermittent stream
The slopes bordering the gully show some potential indication of the occurrence of old
slope failure events now covered by vegetation The indications include some irregular
topographic features combined with lack of mature tree growth in localized areas
The gully to the east of the ridgeline is generally confined and scoured as a narrow
ravine ranging in depths from about 3 to 5 feet below the adjacent banks at the gully
bottom In some places the stream banks remain in an overhanging condition as a result
of stream undercutting Additionally some erosional features consisting of scoured
depressions ranging in depths from 4 to 10 feet exist at localized segments of the gully
apparently as a result of localized runoff convergence or small waterfalls
The depressions were noted to contain soft deposits of clay and extremely weathered
rock fragments with a few boulders Test Pit No 3 was excavated at the location of a
significant depression In general the gully bottom was observed to contain soft to
medium stiff alluvial and colluvial deposits
The slope on the western side of the ridgeline exists at about and trends
down toward the Ahuimanu Stream tributary Some steeper slopes exist at the toe of the
slope bordering a relatively flat area on the eastern side of the stream The flat area
appears to represent an old stream terrace that may have been subsequently
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3999 10 Page 7
widened by some grading to utilize the site for storage Recent cut slopes located at the
toe of the hillside show good exposure of the colluvial soils comprising the hill
Based on our site reconnaissance and boring information the ridgeline comprising
the central portion of the site is believed to be generally composed of stiff to very stiff
older alluvial and colluvial deposits A few moderately weathered basalt boulders
generally less than about 3 feet in diameter were observed at the ground surface
therefore it may be inferred that some boulders may be encountered in the subsurface
Based on our site reconnaissance the intermittent stream gully appears to be
underlain by some recent alluvium with organics overlying the older alluvial colluvial soils
of the site The stream appears to be generally active downcutting into the older colluvial
deposits as opposed to aggrading or filling upwards Based on our observation of the wet
condition of the soils at the stream invert the area may be experiencing some continual
slow seepage of groundwater Furthermore the condition of the stream gully and areal
drainage suggests that rapid flash flood type discharges may be experienced during
periods of moderate or extended rainfall at the site
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions in the vicinity of significant cut areas at the project site
were explored by drilling and sampling of borings to depths of 30 and 35 feet below the
existing ground level The subsurface conditions in the vicinity of the significant fill
areas were explored by the excavation of four shallow hand dug test pit excavations
and hand auger borings to depths of about 3 to 5 feet below the existing ground
elevation The approximate location of the borings and test pits are shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration borings generally encountered stiff to very stiff older alluvial
and colluvial materials consisting of silty clay and clayey silt soils comprising the hill
areas of the site The alluvial colluvial deposits generally consist of extremely
GEOLABS HAWAII

weathered gravel and cobble clasts supported in a clayey matrix Scattered
moderately weathered boulders may be buried in the subsurface however our borings
did not encounter their presence Rock formation was not encountered in the borings
drilled at the site
Our exploratory test pits and site reconnaissance generally encountered soft to
stiff recent alluvial and older alluvial colluvial deposits consisting of clayey silt soils
underlying the stream gully at the eastern half of the site The deposits generally
consisted of clayey silt composed of extremely weathered gravel cobbles and scattered
boulders in a clayey matrix In general the deposits ranged from soft at the ground
surface to medium stiff and stiff at depths of about 4 to 5 feet below ground level
Test Pit No 1 encountered interbedded layers of decomposing organic debris such as
leaves and tree branches The remainder of the test pits generally encountered alluvial

Multiple ground surface probings along the stream gully invert with a steel rod
indicate that significant soft soil deposits may exist Probe penetration ranged from about
18 inches to greater than 36 inches with only slight to moderate hand pressure
Groundwater levels were not encountered in the borings or test pits at the time of

bottom than the adjacent stream bank and hillslope areas It should be noted that the
Kaneohe and Ahuimanu areas tend to exhibit variable groundwater levels related to
seepage and perched water table conditions Furthermore the water level at the project

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
It is our opinion that the proposed project is feasible from a geotechnical
engineering stand point provided that our recommendations are followed The wet soil
conditions and high to very high average annual rainfall at the project site will require
some special earthwork construction methodology In addition due to the thickness of
the fills to be placed in the eastern gully some ground settlements are anticipated and
should be accounted for in the design and construction of the drainage structure
planned for the gully bottom
Based on the results of our exploration the proposed 10 to 30 feet of fill may be
supported on the in situ clayey alluvial and colluvial soil materials provided a ground
settlement program is adhered to We believe that the new fills planned would induce
ground settlements as a result of the consolidation of the underlying soils Settlements on
the order of about 8 to 12 inches may be anticipated for fills overlying the soft to stiff
alluvial colluvial stream deposits Therefore special attention should be given to the
recommendations provided in the Settlement section of this report
Based on our field exploration we anticipate the clay and silt soils comprising the
ridgeline hill may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment Because the
soil deposits are alluvial and colluvial in origin the potential for encountering some
moderately or slightly weathered boulders exists The on site clayey silt materials
generated from the excavations may be used as a source of fill or backfill materials
provided that they are screened of the over sized materials greater than 6 inches in
largest dimension depending on the usage and properly moisture conditioned
Special attention should be given to the recommendations provided in the Site Grading
section of this report

Fill Settlement
Due to the presence of potentially soft compressible soils at the existing gully
location we estimate that the placement of up to 30 feet of new fills in this area would
induce some ground settlements on the order of about 8 to 12 inches due to the
consolidation of the underlying deposits beneath the new fills Based on our engineering
analyses we believe that a settlement period of about 3 months would be adequate to
achieve approximately 90 percent consolidation The new fills should be placed as early
as possible Subsequent sections of this report will discuss in greater detail the ground
settlement monitoring program recommended and installation of the proposed drainline
beneath the fills
Site Gradin
Based on the existing terrain at the subject site and the preliminary grading plans
provided we anticipate that site grading work for the proposed project will generally
consist of maximum cuts ranging up to about 30 feet and maximum fills ranging
between about 20 and 30 feet thick Items of grading that are addressed in the
following subsections include 1 Site Preparation 2 Fills and Backfills 3 Fill
Placement and Compaction Requirements 4 Excavation 5 Cut and Fill Slopes
Ground and Settlement Monitoring and 8 Surcharge
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office observe the site
grading operations to evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the
site preparation process and to confirm that the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to
those encountered in our field exploration

Generally the on site soils should be suitable for use as engineered fill provided
the high in situ moisture contents can be substantially lowered to achieve 85 percent
compaction Since the project site is located in a high rainfall area the on site materials
have very high in situ moisture contents of about 15 to 30 percent above optimum
Therefore significant drying of excavated materials will be required prior to their use as fill
and or backfill
Due to the high in situ moisture content of the on site soils and the high volume of
rainfall that occurs in the area it is our opinion that the compaction requirement for this
project may be reduced from the standard of 90 percent to a minimum of 85 percent
maximum dry density as established by ASTM D 1557 78 Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Even with this reduced 85 percent minimum compaction requirement we believe
substantial moisture reduction would still be required along with the use of light
compaction construction equipment The required aerating to lower the soil moisture and
more intense compaction effort would generally reduce the rate of fill placement at the
site
Based on previous experience in the area a shrinkage factor of about 15 percent
due to the differences between the natural and compacted soil densities may be
considered for on site cut and fill quantities
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site or in a designated
area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3999 10 Page 12
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural material
and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered
fill
After clearing and grubbing all areas at grade or designated to receive fill should
be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative
compaction Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the
subgrade we recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close cracks
or the subgrade should be re scarified moisture conditioned and recompacted
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement
weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted engineered fill
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the subgrade
soils should be proof rolled using a minimum 10 ton smooth drum roller and a
woven geotextile such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent should be placed on the
subgrade prior to placement of fill material Alternately the pumping areas may
be over excavated a minimum of 6 inches below the finish subgrade to expose
firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted engineered fill Contract documents should include additive and
deductive unit prices for over excavation geotextile and engineered fill
placement to account for variations in the over excavation quantities
Fills and Backfills
The on site silty clay and clayey silt materials generated from excavations may be
used as a source of general fill or backfill materials provided that they are
screened of the over sized materials greater than 6 inches in largest dimension
depending on the usage and properly moisture conditioned
W O 3999 10 Page 13
If imported material is required the material should be non expansive granular
material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 6 inches
in largest dimension The material should have a laboratory California Bearing
Ratio CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell value of
1 percent or less Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance
with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended
use Aggregate base course and select borrow subbase materials should meet
the material requirements for Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in
Sections 30 and 31 respectively of the Standard Specifications for Public Work
Construction Department of Public Works City and County of Honolulu
September 1986 Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for
its intended use
General fill material should be free of organic material and highly expansive clay
and follow the above maximum size recommendations
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction Imported fill should
be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with test procedures
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding
to the maximum dry density

W O 3999 10 Page 14
Oversized material greater than 8 inches in maximum dimension may be placed
within deep fills below 10 feet from finish grade that are set back away from the
fill slope Oversized material should also be set apart from other large material to
avoid nesting and allow finer material into the interstices to create a dense mass
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of soils are critical elements of
earthwork observations and soil density tests should be performed by
Geolabs Hawaii during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the
required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on each fill lift
Where compaction is less than required additional compactive effort should be
applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified
compaction

Cut and Fill Slopes

designed with a maximum inclination of 172H 1V or flatter for the proposed
project Fill slopes may be designed with a maximum inclination of 2H 1V or
flatter Fills placed on existing slopes having slope ratios steeper than 5H 1V
should be keyed and benched into the firm and stable materials to enhance the

placement Fill slopes should be constructed by over filling past the design limits

of the slope and cutting back to the design slope to provide adequate

Subdrainage
Considering the wet climate of the area seepage conditions may be present at
localized areas at the site Therefore we recommend that additive and
deductive provisions be incorporated into the construction documents to provide
for subdrain construction in areas of the site where groundwater seepage is
observed during and after grading or as recommended by the Engineer during
construction
In particular we recommend the installation of subdrains in the eastern gully
bottom Because the proposed new drainline will be constructed adjacent to the
existing channel the potential exists for residual seepage to occur along the
alignment of the old channel The subdrain should be made to daylight near the
location of the proposed downstream headwali It is possible that additional
subdrains may be necessary if significant seepage areas are encountered during
the site preparation
In general subdrains should consist of 4 inch diameter perforated pipes with
perforations facing down Subdrains should be surrounded and underlain by at
GEOLABS HAWAII

least 6 inches of drainage material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation or equivalent A non woven filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or
equivalent should wrap around the drainage material
Ground and Settlement Monitoring
Based on our exploration data we believe that a 3 month waiting period will be
required for this project The actual settlement rate should be monitored during
construction and provisions should be made for potential delay in the project
construction schedule based on the estimated duration if a longer settlement
period is required
To monitor the actual settlement rate we recommend that several settlement
gauges be installed at the locations of deepest fill in the eastern gully
The settlement gauges should be installed on the existing ground surface at the
future fill areas and on the finish grade level of fill areas
The settlement gauges should be installed by Geolabs Hawaii A typical detail for
settlement gauges is presented on Plate 4 The gauges should be read optically
by a professional land surveyor and the readings should be transmitted to our
office for review The readings should be transmitted on a weekly basis during the
duration of the settlement period and or until the settlement readings indicate that
the settlement rate has stabilized
It should be recognized that it is difficult to accurately predict the time required for
the filled ground to settle Settlement rates are greatly affected by minute qualities
of the subsoil structure and the history of the subsoil deposition
A proper contingency plan should be devised to accommodate the potential for
delay in the construction schedule and possible extra costs involved with a delay

Surcharge
To reduce the waiting period for the fill ground settlements to occur it is our
opinion that implementation of a surcharge program should be considered for the
site especially since surplus soils may be available from the planned cuts at the
site The lateral extent of the recommended surcharge area should generally
conform to the location of the axis of the buried gully feature The excavated
surplus soils generated from the on site cuts may be used as surcharge
The surcharge height is not critical and may extend from 5 to 10 feet above finish
grade depending on the availability of the surplus soils In general the surcharge
materials may be placed without compaction effort for this project
Drainline Construction
Based on the information provided a new 36 inch diameter pipe drain is planned
to intercept the flow in the gully for transmittal beneath the new fills and offsite
Consideration should be given to the anticipated settlement of the subgrade and new fills
in relation to bending of the new drainline when selecting the pipe materials for the
project
Temporary diversion of the gully stormwater flow will be required for the
construction of the new drainage structure The diversion may consist of the excavation
of a temporary ditch at the margin of the new pipe drain or other action as deemed
appropriate by the contractor The design impact and safety of the diversion operations
should be made the sole responsibility of the contractor including the application for any

Pavement Design
Flexible pavements are anticipated for the proposed Garden of Valor Expansion
project The design traffic for the proposed roadways was not available at the time this
report was prepared However we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the proposed
roadways will consist primarily of passenger vehicles and light trucks with occasional
heavy truck traffic We have made our preliminary pavement designs assuming the
pavement subgrade soil will generally consist of engineered fill or the near surface
clayey silts as described in our test pits and borings with a minimum laboratory
CBR value of 8 and swell value of less than 1 percent If site grading exposes soils
other than those assumed additional tests should be performed to confirm or revise the
recommended pavement sections for actual field conditions Based on the above
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3999 10 Page 19
assumptions we recommend that the following pavement section be used for
preliminary design and budgeting purposes
Asphaltic Concrete Pavement
2 0 Inch Asphalt Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
6 0 Inch Select Borrow Subbase Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
14 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on a Compacted Subgrade
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less
than 90 percent relative compaction The base course and subbase course materials
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO
Test Method T 180 ASTM D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
We recommend that CBR and density tests be performed on the actual pavement
subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the
recommended section The recommended section assumes that good drainage will be
provided for the paved areas
Paved roadways should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry
surface water off the pavement areas Surface water ponding should not be allowed
during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or
adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs extend a minimum of
2 inches into the subgrade soils below the aggregate base course to reduce the potential
for migration of landscape water into the pavement section For long term performance
we recommend that a subdrain system be used along the sides of the roadway

Setback from Slope
During our site reconnaissance it was observed that some of the existing slopes
located along the northern boundary of the site near the southern limits of the Garden of
Patriotism appear steeper than those shown on the topographic plan The area of
concern is located adjacent to a flat ground area within the flood limits and shown on the
topographic plan as Garden of Patriotism Section 2 The localized over steepened
slopes may be the result of the cutting back of the slope for use as borrow or erosion after
the topographic survey was completed Some portions of the slope were observed as
fresh cuts standing nearly vertical As a result we recommend that no structures be
planned within a horizontal distance of 20 feet from the proposed top of slope to reduce
the potential for damage due to slope instability and natural weathering of the existing
slope condition
Should it be desired to include the grading of these slopes in the project grading
plan the recommendations provided in the Site Grading should be followed
The setback recommendation may be negated if the slopes are regraded to meet the cut
and fill slope recommendations contained herein
Utility Trench
In general for support of the utilities and proposed drain pipe we recommend that
a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation be used under the pipe The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the
pipe should consist of free draining backfill material such as No 3B Fine gravel to
reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical
that a free draining granular material be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
The use of on site soils as backfill immediately around utility pipes is not recommended
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils or select fill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3999 10 Page 21
material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 85 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet
of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades should be sloped to shed water away from pavements to
reduce the potential for ponding Excessive landscape watering near pavements should
also be avoided These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance
of the above pavement recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface
soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength
