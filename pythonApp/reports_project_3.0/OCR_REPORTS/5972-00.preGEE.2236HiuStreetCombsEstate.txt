REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age
and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano became
extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and it was partially buried below Koolau
lavas banking against its eastern flank
The project site is in the lower part of Kalihi Valley which was formed by stream
erosion of Tertiary Age Koolau basalt flows This initial period of erosion and valley
carving was followed by several periods of relative rise and fall of sea levels which
permitted valley filling by alluvium followed by subsequent periods of erosion During the
Quaternary Age the Honolulu Volcanic Series lava flowed down the valley and was
subsequently partially covered by Quaternary Age alluvium Continued recent alluvial
processes have covered part of the site with black to gray adobe clay

Building Foundations
In general we believe that the proposed new construction may be supported on
a shallow foundation support system bearing on the in situ hard basalt rock formation
anticipated at shallow depths or on new compacted fills An allowable bearing pressure
on the order of about 4 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of
shallow foundations extending through the upper clayey soils and bearing directly on
the underlying hard basalt rock formation or on compacted fills This bearing value is for
those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 to
24 inches below the lowest adjacent grade Depending on the depth of the basalt rock
formation below the finished grades deeper footing embedment depths may be
required at localized areas to provide a competent bearing surface for the footings
Based on the available information and our field exploration results basalt rock
formation may be anticipated at depths on the order of about 2 to 3 feet below the
existing ground surface For footings bearing directly on the basalt rock formation the
footing bottom should be relatively level and clear of sharp points that may result in
concentrated loading
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a
45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench
or the footing should be embedded to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures
with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement
If the foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with our
recommendations we estimate the total settlements of the foundations to be less than
0 5 inches Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on similar
materials may be on the order of about 0 25 inches or less
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction between the
bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure acting
against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 50
may be used for footings bearing directly on the basalt rock formation Resistance due
to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf assuming that the soils around the
footings are well compacted The passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of the soil
should be neglected unless covered by pavements or slabs

Cavities and or Voids
Based on our field exploration and experience in the project area cavities and or
voids are commonly encountered in the pahoehoe lava flows that characterize the project
site To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from the collapse of
cavities below foundations we recommend implementing a cavity probing and grouting
program for the new structure foundations during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend drilling probe holes at 10 foot on centers
for the continuous strip footings In addition probe holes should be drilled at each isolated
spread footing location one probe per 50 square feet of foundation bearing area The
probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to a depth of at
least 10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation
If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation
additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the vertical and
lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be
backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material CLSM with a slump of about 6 to
9 inches The CLSM should be injected at low to moderate pump pressures As an
alternate to CLSM sand cement grout with a slump of about 6 to 9 inches also may be
used for the grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the project site
we recommend obtaining unit prices for additional probing and grouting during bidding In
addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting
operations are completed The contractor also should be made aware that a longer lag
time between probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in
the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
Geolabs representative This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of cavities
and or voids and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take
and or changed conditions are observed
In addition special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation to reduce
the potential for distress to the future improvements such as floor slabs and pavements
The excavated subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches where
possible moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and proof rolled
with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller to detect the presence of potential
near surface cavities or loose zones The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a
speed of about 200 to 300 feet per minute If the proof rolling operations detect a
near surface cavity or loose zones the area should be ripped open cleaned of the debris
and then backfilled with compacted fill materials CLSM or lean concrete

Expansive Soil Conditions
As previously indicated the project site generally is underlain by a surface layer
of clayey soils Our experience in the area suggests that the near surface clayey soils
may consist of adobe clay which is highly expansive when subjected to moisture
fluctuations Unless slabs on grade constructed above these expansive soils are
properly designed there is a potential for future distress to the lightly loaded
slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes
in the moisture content
To reduce the potential for appreciable structural distress resulting from swelling
of the subgrade soils we recommend properly preparing the subgrade soils prior to
capping layer of non expansive select granular fill material imported fill material
should be provided below the building slab areas The non expansive select granular
fill should extend beyond the perimeter of the building a minimum of 24 inches or
extend to the edge of the concrete sidewalk surrounding the building if applicable
We also recommend the building slabs on grades be at least 5 inches thick and
be reinforced with No 3 reinforcing bars spaced at about 12 inches in each direction in
lieu of the standard welded wire fabric This recommendation is provided to reduce the
potential for appreciable structural distress due to differential movements of the slabs
due to the nature of the near surface expansive soils
For interior building slabs we recommend placing a minimum 4 inch thick layer
of cushion fill consisting of open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below
the slabs The open graded gravel cushion fill would provide uniform support of the
slabs and would serve as a capillary moisture break To reduce the potential for future
moisture infiltration through the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the open graded gravel cushion
fill layer Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered
because they can better mask minor slab cracking We also recommend designing
interior walls to incorporate some flexibility to accommodate a small amount of possible
ground movements
In order to reduce the potential for appreciable distress due to differential
movements between the more heavily loaded foundations and the lightly loaded
building slabs we recommend using free floating slabs on grade with no structural
connections to the wall and column foundations Joint filler and sealant may be used to
fill the openings between the edges of the slabs and other structural elements To
further reduce the potential for distress to the building slabs on grade and foundations
resulting from water infiltration into the subsurface from areas immediately adjacent to
the building foundations we recommend considering a concrete sidewalk around the
perimeter of the building structures The non expansive select granular fill
recommended below the building slabs should extend out to the edges of the concrete

Rock Excavation
Based on the limited field exploration conducted for the project it appears that the
project site is underlain by basalt rock formation near the existing ground surface
Unconfined compression tests were performed on selected rock cores obtained from our

Appendix B Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results the basalt formation

that some of the basalt formation may be excavated with heavy excavation equipment
such as the large backhoe excavator However the majority of the rock formation is
generally hard and may require hard ripping with bulldozers and possibly pneumatic tools
such as hoe rams
Because basaltic rock formation was encountered near the existing ground surface
during our field exploration it appears that the trench work for utility installation will likely
involve excavation in the basaltic rock formation and will require hard ripping or the use of
hoerams The contractor must exercise care to avoid over ripping which would disrupt the

Lot Drainage
It should be emphasized that proper surface drainage is critical to the performance
of foundations and slabs constructed on expansive soil conditions Therefore the finished
grades outside the building structures should be sloped adequately to shed water away
from the building foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also
advised that gutter systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be
diverted away from the foundation and pavement areas
Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be
avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to reduce the potential for appreciable water infiltration into the subsurface soils
Drainage swales with positive drainage should be provided as soon as possible and
maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs

Field Exploration
Our limited field exploration program for the preliminary geotechnical engineering
exploration consisted of drilling and sampling two borings designated as Boring Nos 1
and 2 extending to depths of about 20 5 and 22 feet below the existing ground surface
We drilled the borings using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight
augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on the Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch outside diameter OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
We obtained core samples of the basalt formations encountered at the site using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a
hollow bit to cut into the basalt formation The material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
