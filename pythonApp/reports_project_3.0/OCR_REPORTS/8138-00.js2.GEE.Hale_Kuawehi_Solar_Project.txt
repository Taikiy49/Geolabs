SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered volcanic ash deposits underlain by 
clinker materials and basalt formation. The thickness of the volcanic ash deposits ranged 
from about 2.25 to 5 feet at the boring locations. The volcanic ash deposits generally 
consisted of hard sandy and gravelly silts and dense to very dense silty gravel. The 
volcanic ash deposits generally were underlain by clinkers and basalt formation extending 
to the maximum depth explored of about 16.6 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
clinker materials generally consisted of dense to very dense silty and sandy gravel. 
The basalt formation at the project site generally ranged from medium hard to hard 
and slightly weathered to unweathered. The basalt formation was encountered from 
depths of about 2.25 to 6 feet below the ground surface. Based on the field exploration 
conducted, it appears that the depth to basalt formation varies across the project site. We 
did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using 
shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new structures planned. 
As an alternative, foundations for support of the new structures planned also may consist 
of thickened-edge slab footings. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be 
used for the design of footings bearing on the recompacted on-site materials or basalt 
formation. Bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the 
lowest adjacent exterior grade. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
design recommendations.

 SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to our geotechnical analysis are the subsurface materials encountered 
at the project site, the engineering properties of the materials encountered, and the 
variability of the subsurface conditions across the project site. Therefore, the following 
subsections provide a description of the geologic setting of the project site, the surface 
and subsurface conditions encountered at the site, and a discussion of the items needed 
for seismic design, such as the seismicity, soil liquefaction, and the soil profile 
characteristics for seismic analysis. 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers 
an area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during the 
recent geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea 
(both still active). 
The project site is situated on the western flank of Mauna Kea Mountain, which 
makes up the north-central portion of the island. Based on available geologic information 
and geomorphological interpretation, the project site appears to be underlain by basaltic 
lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea Volcano, which were deposited 
during the Pleistocene Epoch (Stearns and Macdonald, 1946). 
Following the cessation of volcanic activity in Kohala and Mauna Kea, activity from 
the other shields resulted in widespread aerial fall of ash over much of the island. This 
ash has weathered rapidly into a fine silty soil, which is typified by low in-situ densities 
and high field moisture contents. In some locales, when the field moisture content is 
sufficiently high, the ash soil exhibits thixotropic properties (i.e., the soil temporarily loses 
strength when remolded and regains its strength after internal pore pressures dissipate). 
In more arid areas, the ash soil exists in a dry friable state even though field 
moisture contents can approach 100 percent of the dry unit weight. Our exploration

The lava formation underlying the site generally consists of a’a and pahoehoe 
basalt type flows, which spread and ponded as they flowed toward the ocean. A'a lava 
typically is characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface resembling a 
jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders. Typically, a 
denser and more layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core.  
Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an 
internal structure of vesicular (porous) rock. Cavities are commonly encountered in 
pahoehoe lavas. Cavities form when lavas are still in a molten state and represent both 
lava tubes (intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes 
form when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube-like structure, 
which may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets 
(inter-flow cavities) are generally smaller in horizontal extent.

2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to 
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before 
or during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the 
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not 
erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but 
most are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. 
However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause 
minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes 
on the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 
90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree 
of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the active volcanoes 
located in the southern portion of the Island of Hawaii. 
The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 6 (M6+) including the October 15, 2006 earthquake. Based on

2.4.2 Liquefaction 
Based on the International Building Code (2012 Edition), the project site should be 
evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction. Soil liquefaction is a condition where 
saturated cohesionless soils near the ground surface undergo a substantial loss 
of strength due to the build-up of excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic 
stress applications induced by earthquakes. In this process, when the loose 
saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration (such as during an earthquake), 
the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water 
pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of 
pore water pressure, the effective stress (internal strength) of the soil is reduced.  
Under sustained vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could equal the 
overburden pressure, essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and 
causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil acquires a 
mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements, and if not 
confined, will result in significant deformations.

 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the project site generally is underlain by volcanic 
ash deposits, on the order of about 2.25 to 5 feet thick, consisting of hard and/or dense 
sandy silts, gravelly silts, and silty gravel. The volcanic ash deposits generally were 
underlain by clinkers and basalt formation extending to the maximum depth explored of 
about 16.6 feet below the existing ground surface. The clinker materials generally 
consisted of dense to very dense silty and sandy gravel. The basalt formation was 
encountered from depths of about 2.25 to 6 feet below the ground surface and generally 
ranged from medium hard to hard and slightly weathered to unweathered. We did not 
encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using 
shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new equipment and 
structures planned. As an alternative, thickened-edge slab footings also may be 
considered for support of the new equipment and structures planned. Detailed 
discussions and recommendations for the design of foundations, slabs-on-grade, site 
grading, roadways, and other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the 
following sections. 
Equipment and Structure Foundations 
We understand that new structures to support various equipment and 
appurtenances may be required for the project. Details of the new equipment and 
structural loading information were not available at the time this report was prepared. 
Generally, we anticipate these structures to be one story in height and lightly loaded. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using 
shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new structures planned. 
As an alternative, thickened-edge slab footings also may be considered for support of the 
new structures planned.

Foundation Proof-Rolling 
Based on the relatively light structural loads anticipated for the new structures, we 
believe the risk of potential collapse of cavities below the foundations would be relatively 
low. Therefore, we believe the foundation probing and grouting program generally 
recommended for new structures planned at the site may be omitted for this project.  
To provide a higher degree of assurance against potential collapse of near-surface 
cavities below foundations, we recommend proof-rolling within the structure footprint (and 
a minimum of 5 feet beyond the perimeter of the structure) using a 10-ton (minimum static 
weight) vibratory drum roller a minimum of eight times to help detect and collapse near-
surface cavities and/or voids. The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed of 
about 300 feet per minute (3.5 miles per hour). 
The proof-rolling operations should be performed in the presence of a Geolabs 
representative. Yielding areas, loose areas, or cavities disclosed during the proof-rolling 
operations should be over-excavated and backfilled with compacted fill materials. 
Concrete Slabs-On-Grade 
Concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the ground floors of the new structures. 
We anticipate the floor slabs to be like industrial slabs and likely will be subjected to 
vibrations and/or wheel loads. Therefore, we recommend providing a minimum of 
6 inches of compacted aggregate subbase below the slabs in lieu of the typical 4-inch 
thick cushion fill layer. The aggregate subbase material layer should be moisture-
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. An impervious moisture barrier may be considered on top 
of the compacted aggregate subbase if moisture infiltration through the slab is of concern. 
To reduce the potential for substantial shrinkage cracks in the concrete slabs, 
crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways (or 
slabs) with expansion joints at right-angle intersections.

3.4.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the top 2 feet of fill materials should consist of structural fill materials. 
Structural fill materials should consist of well-graded granular materials generally 
less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the 
occurrence of voids in the compacted mass. In addition, structural fill materials 
should contain less than 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve by weight. 
In general, the surface materials at the project site consist of volcanic ash 
materials. We envision that site grading required at the site will involve placement 
of fills consisting of volcanic ash materials. In addition, volcanic ash materials may 
be present at the subgrades of new structures and pavements required for the 
development of the project. 
Volcanic ash materials generally are difficult to compact because the strength of 
the materials is very sensitive to moisture content. Volcanic ash materials at the 
site generally exist in a drier condition. Therefore, some moisture-conditioning may 
be required to facilitate compaction of the materials. However, it should be noted 
that excessive moisture in the volcanic ash materials may create pumping ground 
conditions. Therefore, the contractor should use caution not to overwater when 
moisture-conditioning volcanic ash materials. Where soft or pumping ground 
conditions are encountered during construction, over-excavation and replacement 
with suitable fills may be required. 
Consideration may be given to mixing the volcanic ash materials with crushed rock 
and/or clinker to provide a well-graded structural fill. Mixing volcanic ash materials 
with crushed rock would reduce the moisture sensitivity of the material and may 
facilitate easier compaction. 
Fill and backfill materials below the minimum 2-foot thick structural fill layer may 
consist of general fill materials. General fill and backfill materials should consist of 
well-graded granular materials, the majority of which are less than 12 inches with

Corrosion Potential 
Design of metallic substructures, such as electrical conduits or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects. Items of corrosion potential that are addressed in the subsequent 
subsections include the following:

3.8.2 Corrosivity Laboratory Testing 
Two sets of corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), 
were performed by our office and Eurofins TestAmerica on selected near-surface 
soil samples obtained from our field exploration. A summary of the corrosivity tests 
is presented in Plate B-3 of Appendix B. 
Based on the results of corrosivity laboratory tests, the near-surface soils at the 
project site exhibit minimum resistivity values ranging from approximately 5,500 to 
6,300 ohm-cm, indicating that the near-surface soils are moderately corrosive 
(Corrosion Rating of 4) to buried metallic structures. Therefore, we recommend 
properly designing near-surface metallic substructures (such as piping) for 
protection against the potential for corrosion. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete structures is 
dependent on the pH, chloride content, and sulfate content found in the soil. In 
general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be 
considered "non corrosive" to underground concrete pipelines and structures.

Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.

encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4.

ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock 
Log Legend, Plate A-0.3.  The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

Field Resistivity Surveys 
Geophysical survey equipment (i.e. resistance meter) was mobilized to the project 
site on July 9, 2020 for the field resistivity survey. Sixteen (16) field resistivity surveys 
were conducted at two locations, designated as R-1 and R-2, using the in-situ 
Wenner 4-Pin Method and an AEMC Model 4630 resistance meter manufactured by 
AEMC Instruments. The method utilizes four surface probes or pins (electrodes) to 
evaluate the average resistivity of the underlying soil mass. The average resistivity values 
of the soil mass, extending from the existing ground surface to the test depth, was 
assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations shown on the 
Site Plan (Plate 2). 
The soil resistivity surveys were performed along the existing ground surface at 
the approximate survey locations shown on the site plan. The average soil resistance 
along the proposed alignment was evaluated at each survey location with the electrodes 
aligned at equal spacing of generally 2.5, 5, 10, and 20 feet. The depth of penetration of 
a resistivity sounding is a function of the electrode spacing. 
The resistance meter (AEMC Model 4630) provides a voltage between the outer 
electrodes, causing an electrical current to flow between them. The voltage drop between 
the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive, built-in voltmeter. As a result, the 
soil resistance (in ohms) is obtained from the voltmeter dial reading. The soil resistivity 
(in ohm-cm), which is directly related to both resistance and spacing, can then be 
computed. The evaluated soil resistances and computed resistivity values are presented 
on Plate D-1.  
In addition, the computed resistivity values with pin spacing at the test locations 
are presented in the graph on Plate D-2. At the time of resistivity surveying, the 
temperature was about 80º F to 85º F, and the existing ground surface was dry. It should 
be noted that the soil resistivity may be influenced by temperature, weather conditions, 
existing underground utilities, and other factors.
