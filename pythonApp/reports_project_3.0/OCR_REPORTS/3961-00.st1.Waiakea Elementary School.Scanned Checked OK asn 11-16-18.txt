INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed open playcourt at Waiakea Elementary School in South Hilo
on the island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses of the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design of
foundations site grading and pavements only and are subject to the limitations noted at
the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a new open playcourt at Waiakea Elementary School in
South Hilo on the island of Hawaii Based on the available information the playcourt will
be striped for basketball volleyball and badminton courts We understand that the
proposed playcourt will be approximately 72 feet by 100 feet in dimensions The
proposed playcourt is oriented lengthwise toward the edge of the existing slope with the
existing baseball diamond to the east A 10 foot high chain link fence will be erected
around the perimeter of the playcourt
We also understand that construction of a playcourt roof is planned in the future
Installation of 6 inch diameter PVC pipes under the playcourt pavement is currently
planned to accommodate future tie beams which will be used to provide additional lateral
and uplift support for the future playcourt cover In addition a modified drywell is planned
at the northern end of the playcourt The modified drywell will be approximately 8 feet in
diameter and about 7 5 feet in depth
Minor site grading consisting of approximately 1 to 2 feet of cuts and fills are
anticipated for this project In addition excavation of up to about 7 5 feet in depth will be

playcourt were not available at the time this report was prepared

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the eruptive activity of five
major shield volcanoes Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna Kea which has had
some activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea which are both considered to be active
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield
Volcano which had been built up by the successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows
and pyroclastic materials The most recent lava flows which comprise the ground surface
and shallow subsurface of the project site are believed to be Pliocene to Pleistocene in
age approximately 12 million years to 2 million years before present time and belong to
the upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series
The lava formation at the site appears to be pahoehoe flow which is characterized
by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous
rock Cavities and or voids are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava These cavities
are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes are
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are
generally smaller in horizontal extent

Future Playcourt Roof Foundations
We understand that a roof may be installed in the future for the playcourt Based
on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site it is our opinion that a
shallow foundation system consisting of spread footings may be used to support the
proposed future playcourt roof An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per
square foot p s f may be used for the design of footings bearing on the basalt cobbles
and gravels intermixed with volcanic ash encountered at shallow depths This bearing
value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient
loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
The bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below
the lowest adjacent finish grade For footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on
slopes the footings should be embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance
from the outside edge at the base of the footing to the slope face is not less than 8 feet
Where foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should
be embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of
the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural
loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
The bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3961 00 Page 8
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM
D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density If soft and or loose materials are
encountered at the bottom of footing excavations they should be over excavated to
expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation should be backfilled with
select granular material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the
footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying competent material
If foundations are designed in accordance with our recommendations we estimate
total settlements to be less than 1 inch with differential settlements on the order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that the soil around
footings is well compacted A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings
bearing on the in situ basalt cobbles and gravels intermixed with volcanic ash soils
Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of
soil should be neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Playcourt Pavement
New flexible pavements are anticipated for the proposed playcourt We anticipate
that the loading for the playcourt will not consist of vehicular traffic We have made our
preliminary pavement design assuming the pavement subgrade soil will be similar to the
basalt cobbles and gravels intermixed with ash soils encountered in the borings with a
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3961 00 Page 9
minimum California Bearing Ratio CBR of 10 5 and swell of less than 1 8 percent
Based on the above assumptions we recommend the following pavement section be
used
1 5 Inch Asphalt Concrete
4 0 Inch Basalt Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
5 5 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on a Moist Compacted Subgrade
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction The base course should
consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
Laboratory CBR tests should be performed on samples of the actual subgrade
soils encountered during construction to confirm the above recommended design section
The recommended pavement section assumes that good drainage will be provided for
areas adjacent to the pavements
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere on the
site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in
or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs extend a minimum of
2 inches into the subgrade below the base course to reduce migration of landscape water
into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to
collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation For long term performance we
recommend a subdrain system be constructed adjacent to paved landscape areas

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
After clearing and grubbing the areas within the proposed future playcourt should
be excavated where necessary to the design finish subgrades The excavated
materials may be reused as a source of fill materials provided that the materials
meet the gradation requirements presented in the Fills and Backfills subsection
of this report Loose or soft spots encountered at the excavated subgrade levels
should be removed and replaced with structural fill compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction
The finished subgrades should be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory
drum roller or similar heavy construction equipment for a minimum of 6 passes
prior to constructing improvements and placing fill to assist in detecting and
collapsing near surface cavities Loose areas or cavities disclosed during clearing
and proof rolling operations should be excavated to expose firm materials and the
over excavation should be backfilled with structural fill compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Contract documents should include additive and

variations in the over excavation and backfill quantities The proof rolling
operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii to evaluate
the presence of voids and or cavities below the subgrade level
Fills and Backfills
Fill and backfill materials used as general and structural fill up to the pavement
subgrade level should consist of well graded granular materials less than 6 inches
in largest dimension The on site cut materials may be used as fill or backfill
materials provided they are processed and reduced to a relatively well graded
select granular material with a maximum size of about 6 inches Any imported
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles greater than
6 inches in largest dimension Any imported material should be observed and or
tested by Geolabs Hawaii for its suitability prior to being transported to the site
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to
the maximum dry density
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rock boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number
of passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction
should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3961 00 Page 12
observation of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii On the basis of this testing
program the number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for
adequate compaction
Excavation
Based on our field exploration the proposed site is underlain by basalt cobbles
and gravels intermixed with varying amounts of volcanic ash soils at shallow
depths It is anticipated that the near surface materials may be excavated with
normal heavy excavation equipment such as ripping with large bulldozers
However excavations into the deeper basalt formations may require the use of
hoerams or chipping
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based
on our visual observation of the existing soil and rock formations and field data
from the borings performed at the site We recommend that all contractors
proposing to bid on this project be encouraged to examine the site conditions and
the boring data to make their own interpretation
Cut and Fill Slopes
In general we believe that cut and fill slopes may be designed with a slope
inclination of 2H 1V or flatter All fills placed on slopes with inclinations steeper
than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide
stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the
lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance
with the above fill placement recommendations Fill slopes should be constructed
by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted
slope face Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope
planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion
of the finished grades

Retaining Structures
The following general guidelines may be used for preliminary design of retaining

Retaining Structure Foundations
In general we believe that retaining structure foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the Future Playcourt Roof
Foundations section of this report The footings should have a minimum width of
18 inches In addition wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be
embedded a minimum depth of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished
grade For sloping ground conditions the footing should extend deeper to obtain a
minimum 8 foot set back distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of
the footing to the face of the slope Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction
of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings

PVC Pipe Installation
We understand that the installation of 6 inch diameter PVC pipes under the
playcourt pavement is currently planned to accommodate for future tie beams It is
anticipated that most of the trenches for the PVC pipes will be excavated in surficial fills

the pipes

located in paved areas the upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the playcourt
subgrade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling three
borings to depths of approximately 6 5 to 25 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings
were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Spilt Barrel Sampling of Soils
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of
borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
One sieve analysis test ASTM C 136 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Results of the test are presented on Plate B 1
One California Bearing Ratio CBR test was performed on a bulk sample of the
near surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement subgrade support
Results of the CBR test are presented on Plate B 2
