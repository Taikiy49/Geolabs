INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Mohouli Street Extension project in the Punahoa area in the
District of South Hilo on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of pavements retaining
structures and site grading only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

We understand that a new roadway connecting Ainako Avenue to Mohouli Street
Mohouli Street Extension is planned in the Punahoa area of South Hilo on the Island of
Hawaii as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The proposed alignment commences near
the intersection of Ainako Avenue with Kaumana Drive proceeds east traverses Alenaio
Stream and Kukuau Street and terminates at the intersection of Mohouli and Komohana

Minor cuts of up to about 5 feet deep may be required in localized areas to achieve the
design finished grades for the proposed roadway construction
Based on the plan and profile drawings provided the new Mohouli Street

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes which include the following Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna Kea
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3553 00 Page 5
which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801
and Mauna Loa and Kilauea which are still active
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield
Volcano which has been built up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials The near surface lava flows are from the Mauna Loa Volcano
overlying the Mauna Kea shield The lava flows of the Mauna Kea shield are believed to
be Pliocene to Pleistocene in age and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua
Volcanic Series The near surface lava flows of the Mauna Loa shield may be on the
order of only 750 to 1 500 years old
The lava formation at the site appears to be pahoehoe flow which is characterized
by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous
rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava These cavities were formed
when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities
and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes are formed when molten
lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend
for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller
in horizontal extent

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration along the proposed roadway extension alignment generally
encountered basalt rock formation and dense clinker materials near the existing ground
surface extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 40 5 feet below the
existing ground surface Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the traffic
data provided we recommend that a pavement structural section consisting of 3 inches of
asphalt concrete on 6 inches of asphalt concrete base over 6 inches of untreated
permeable base over 6 inches of aggregate subbase be used for the travelway and the
shoulder areas of the road For the widening of the existing pavements along Ainako
Street between about and we recommend that the existing
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3553 00 Page 9
pavements be removed and replaced with the above new pavement section across the
entire roadway prism
In general the hard basalt rock formation encountered at the new arch and box
culvert sites should provide adequate support for the proposed drainage structures To
reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities
below the foundations we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be
implemented at the arch and box culvert foundation locations during the project
construction An 8 inch gravel cushion layer is recommended below the box culvert slab
to provide more uniform bearing support for the drainage structure
Based on our site reconnaissance a thin surficial layer of alluvial deposit and
pockets of soft and wet soils may be anticipated along localized areas of the proposed
roadway extension alignment It is our opinion that these wet and soft soil deposits
where encountered at the pavement or fill subgrade levels should be removed to expose
the underlying dense ground The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with
compacted fill materials
In addition special attention should be given to the proper surface drainage of the
roadway pavements to reduce the potential for ponding water as observed along a
significant portion of the roadway alignment during our field exploration program

The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
where possible moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction The aggregate subbase
materials should also be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO
Test Method T 180 ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage
by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

aggregate compacted to a density of more than 91 percent of the maximum theoretical

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the aggregate subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement
surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface
water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should
not be allowed on the site during or after construction Where landscaping is planned
adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that a subdrain system be constructed
to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation and to reduce migration of
landscape water into the pavement section
Considering the high rainfall in the Hilo area we envision that an edge drain
system for the permeable base layer will be required to collect infiltrated water and to
drain the excess water out of the pavement section The edge drain may consist of a
6 inch PVC perforated pipe with perforations facing down surrounded by untreated
permeable base material A filter fabric permeable separator such as Mirafi 180N or
equivalent should be placed around the permeable base to reduce the potential for
migration of fines into the drainage material Care should be exercised when placing the
base subbase and or construction of the surface course to avoid damaging the edge
drain collector pipe A typical edge drain detail is shown on Plate 3 The edge drains
should be daylighted into appropriate drainage structures for proper discharge

Foundations
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered in our drilled borings we believe that
the foundations for these retaining structures may be supported on shallow
foundations bearing on the basalt rock formation encountered at shallow depths
GEOLABS HAWAIl

An ultimate bearing pressure of up to 40 000 pounds per square foot psf may be
used to evaluate the extreme event limit state of the foundations bearing on the
basalt rock formation To evaluate the strength limit state of the foundations a
resistance factor of 0 6 or a bearing pressure of up to may be used
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing
excavations they should be over excavated to expose the underlying dense hard
basalt rock formation The over excavation should be backfilled with concrete or
lean concrete In addition we recommend that the foundations be probed and
grouted to detect voids and or cavities as discussed in the Foundation Probing
section of this report
Wall foundations should have a minimum width of 24 inches In addition wall
foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade or deep enough to bear
directly on the basalt rock formation whichever is greater For sloping ground
conditions the foundations should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 6 foot
set back distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of the foundation to
the face of the slope Wall foundations oriented parallel to the direction of the
slope should be constructed in stepped foundations
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the
inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the
potential for foundation settlement
Based on a service limit state bearing pressure of we estimate that
foundation settlements under the anticipated design loads for foundations bearing
on the basalt rock formation to be less than 0 5 inches total with differential

settlements along the wall face to be less than about 1 inch per 40 feet of wall
length
Lateral loads acting on the retaining structures may be resisted by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing
material and by passive earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the
foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 52 and 0 65 may be used for
foundations bearing on the basalt rock formation for the strength limit state and
extreme event limit state respectively Resistance due to passive earth pressure
may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 225 and 450 pounds per

basalt rock formation or the space around the footings is backfilled with concrete or

Dynamic Lateral Earth Pressures
Since the project site is located on the Island of Hawaii seismically active
dynamic lateral earth pressures will need to be considered in the design of
retaining structures Dynamic lateral earth pressures due to seismic loading
may be estimated by using 24H psf rectangular pressure
distribution for level backfill conditions where H is the height of the wall in feet
The dynamic lateral earth pressures presented above are in addition to the static
lateral earth pressures An appropriately reduced factor of safety may be used
when dynamic lateral earth pressures are accounted for in the design of the
retaining structure
Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately adjacent to the wall with a perforated pipe perforations facing down
at the base of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an
alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain
may be used instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product
should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall

Foundation Probing
Based on the borings drilled and our experience in the area voids and or cavities
may exist in the basalt rock formation encountered at the project site To reduce the
potential for loss of foundation support resulting from the collapse of cavities below
foundations we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting for the arch
culvert and box culvert foundations be implemented during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each spread
footing location and at 10 feet on center for continuous footings For mat foundations or
large spread footings we recommend that a probe hole be provided for every 50 square
feet of foundation footprint area The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter
and should extend at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of footing If cavities and or
voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probes
should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the vertical and lateral extent of
the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with
sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures We recommend that a low
strength sand cement grout mixture with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the
grouting operations

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fills and backfills up to 3 feet below the finish subgrade of the pavement
areas should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in
level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness and compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction Fill materials within 3 feet of the finish subgrade of
pavement areas should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with AASHTO T 180 ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction should be accomplished
by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or
other types of acceptable compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or
ponding should not be allowed for compaction of the fills
Rock or boulder fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum
dimension of the rocks or 3 feet whichever is less Placement should be by haul
trucks traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the currently
placed lift Spreading of the rock fill should be performed by a Caterpillar D 9
bulldozer or similar heavy equipment to facilitate seating of the rock The rock fill
should be watered heavily during placement by water trucks traversing in front of
the current rock lift face and spraying water continuously during placement
Compaction equipment such as a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or other similar
compaction equipment providing suitable energy to achieve the required
compaction should be utilized The number of passes to be made to achieve
proper compaction should be determined at the on set of site grading by the
performance of a trial compaction test and by plate bearing tests
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3553 00 Page 24
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rocks boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the
number of passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of
compaction should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project
under the observation of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii On the basis of
this testing program the number of passes may then be used as the field criterion
for adequate compaction

depths

Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Three sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on Plate B 1
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils Results of the test are presented on Plates B 2 and B 3
Two Standard Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
