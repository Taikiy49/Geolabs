REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kaho olawe is located about 11 kilometers to the south of Maui and
about 27 kilometers to the southeast of Lana i as shown on the General Site Plan
Plate is approximately 117 square kilometers in land area with a
maximum elevation of about 450 meters above sea level at the Lua Makika summit on
the eastern portion of the island
Kaho olawe is believed to be composed of a single shield volcano formed by the
extrusion of successive layered thinly bedded pahoehoe and a a basalt lava flows that
emanated from the rift zones and caldera at Lua Makika The primary eruptive rift zones
of the former volcano extend westerly from the summit and to a lesser degree to the
north and east from the summit Two cinder cone remnants can be seen located at
Puu Moiwi and Puu Kamama The area at Lua Kealialalo is believed to represent the
weathered remains of a collapsed crater at the crest of the broad lava shield Some of
the more recent aged rocks composing the upper caldera filling layers of the island have
been dated at about 1 million years old
Intermittent stream flow has incised gullies and some deep gulches into the
residual soil and weathered rock surface of the island The climate is generally arid due
to the island s leeward location with respect to the island of Maui Most rainfall occurs
during large Kona storm events and as light misting showers carried by the trade winds
The geomorphology of the island is most significantly affected by wind erosion which has
created a generally barren hard pan soil surface The lower slopes of the island show
exposure of bare rock with a thin soil cover suggesting that some ocean wave erosion
had occurred during higher stands of the sea during the Pleistocene Epoch It is believed
that much of the soil cover existing below about Elevation 244 meters Mean Sea Level
MSL was historically swept into the ocean by a large tsunami created by a huge slice of
the southwestern Lana i cliff line falling into the ocean Similar large scale soil erosion
and coral fragment deposition have been found on the islands of Maui and Lana

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project area is located in the vicinity of Lua Makika which is situated at the
central portion of the eastern half of the island of Kaho olawe as shown on the General
Site Plan Plate comprises the summit of the central upland backbone of
the island The project site generally consists of flat to very gently sloping terrain
underlain by tan and reddish brown stiff silty and sandy soils representing residual and
saprolite soil products derived from the complete weathering of basalt rock
A horizontal distance of about 220 meters separates the center of the two
proposed helicopter landing pads as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Based on the
available grading plan the existing ground surface elevations of the proposed western
helicopter pad LZ 1A ranges from about 423 meters MSL to about 425 meters MSL
The existing ground surface elevations of the proposed eastern helicopter pad LZ 1B
ranges from about 431 meters MSL to 435 meters MSL
K 1 Road traverses the area in a northeast to southwest direction just south of the
proposed helicopter landing pad site The proposed Pad site is located
down slope and west of the summit of Lua Makika near the head of Kaukamoku Gulch a
significant drainage feature of the area Based on the available topographic plan the
head of the drainage feature passes between the two proposed helicopter landing pads
as a low and gently sloping basin Due to the drainage feature s close proximity to the
summit significant concentration of stormwater flows are not anticipated to occur
In general the project site consists of gently sloping to flat ground vegetated with
widely scattered Kiawe and ironwood trees and tufts of dryland grass Much of the
windswept ground surface is barren and devoid of vegetation with a hardpan soil surface
The ground surface is covered sporadically with a thin scatter of loose friable gravel
chips believed to represent mineral concretions created by the weathering of basalt
materials

SURFACE SOIL CONDITIONS
The soils observed at the project site appear to be relatively uniform in appearance
and may be classified as silty sand with traces of low plasticity clay representing the
in situ residual and saprolitic soil derived from the complete weathering of basalt rock
Based on our site reconnaissance the soils appear to be very stiff though friable due in
part to the arid environment The soils appear to have a low shrink swell potential Most
of the flat to gently sloping terrain has developed a hardpan crust of soil which is
relatively resistant to the initiation of erosion Abrasion and scouring by wind appears to
be responsible for the formation of the surficial hardpan crust However once the surficial
crust is exposed or broken through resistance to erosion from stormwater runoff is
lowered forming gullies
Significant topsoil development and occurrence of loosely deposited alluvial and or
windblown materials were not observed likely due to exposure to the stripping action of
wind When excavated the on site soils were observed to have a blocky and friable
texture that promotes the formation of soil clumps The soil clumps may be broken down
to fine particles of silt sand and some gravel It is anticipated that some moderately
weathered cobbles or boulders may be encountered buried in the near surface soil
horizon
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field reconnaissance and laboratory test results we believe that the
principal geotechnical engineering considerations for construction of the proposed
helicopter pads and access roads include the following
e The near surface soils are relatively dry and will require a significant amount
of water to moisture condition the soils for site preparation grading and
soil cement mixing
Excavation of the near surface soils may encounter soil clumps cobbles
and boulders which would affect the mixing of the on site soils to form a
soil cement pavement structure especially in cut subgrades

As previously mentioned the on site near surface soils exist in a relatively dry
condition about 10 to 20 percent below the optimum moisture content Therefore in
order to prepare the on site soils from the existing conditions to near the optimum
moisture content for site preparation grading and soil cement mixing a large amount of
water will be needed at the site to facilitate the site grading work Based on our
estimates we believe that approximately 220 to 300 liters of water would be needed for
each cubic meter of controlled fill for the grading operations Therefore an adequate
water supply and number of water trucks should be available at the site to
moisture condition the on site soils to the specified moisture contents to prepare the
subgrades and work the on site soils as controlled fills
It should be noted that the soil cement pavement mixture is quite sensitive to the
moisture content of the on site soils and during the mixing procedures The contractor
should avoid over watering of the soil cement pavement mixture which would reduce the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil cement product Conversely if too little
water is used in the mixing of the soil cement the specified unconfined compressive
strength may not be achieved Therefore care should be exercised by the contractor to
work with the on site materials in a relatively small range of moisture contents for the
soil cement pavement structure Based on our soil cement testing performed for the
project we believe that the range of moisture contents for soil cement mixing should be
between about 5 percent below the optimum moisture and the optimum moisture content
based on ASTM Test Designation D 698

Helicopter Pads
As previously mentioned the two helicopter landing pads are to be designed to
accommodate CH 53 helicopters Based on the plans provided we understand that the

mats placed over a firm base Based on the anticipated site conditions we recommend
that the base below the PSP landing mats consist of at least 150 mm of aggregate base
course in order to provide a firm base on which to construct the PSP landing mats and
to withstand the repeated loading from the helicopter take offs and landing
We understand that a plastic membrane is to be provided below the aggregate
base course to reduce the potential for migration of POL deeper into the subsurface
We recommend that the plastic membrane below the aggregate base course be
cushioned by 50 mm of fine sand above the plastic membrane to aid in protecting the

base course The plastic membrane should be placed on 50 mm of fine sand The fine
sand should be moistened and densified with a vibratory plate prior to placement of the
aggregate base course The 150 mm thick aggregate base course may be placed in
one lift and should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction based on
ASTM Test Designation D 1557
Because the base of the PSP landing mais will consist of aggregate base
course there may be a concern with Foreign Object Debris FOD from the potentially
loose gravel exposed at the Heli Pad areas Therefore consideration may be given to
the use of some cement treatment in the top 75 to 100 mm of the aggregate base
course to reduce the potential for FOD at the surface of the Heli Pads For preliminary
estimating purposes the cement content for the cement treated surface may be
assumed to be about 4 percent by weight to provide some cohesion and to reduce the
hazard associated with FOD

We understand that the access roads leading to the two Heli Pads from K 1 Road
are planned to be surfaced with soil cement using the on site fine grained soils We also

Based on the relatively light traffic loading conditions anticipated for these access
road pavements we believe that a 200 mm thick soil cement pavement may be used for
the access road pavement section Based on the soil conditions at the site and the
soil cement testing performed for the overall project we recommend that a cement
content of 23 percent by weight may be used for the soil cement pavement structure
The 23 percent cement content for the soil cement pavement structure is based on the
soil cement product achieving a minimum unconfined compressive strength of at least
based on a 7 day curing period The relationship between
unconfined compressive strength and cement content for the various soil samples
conducted for the overall project is presented on Plate 3 for reference
The soil cement pavement should be moisture conditioned to between 5 percent
below the optimum moisture and the optimum moisture content based on ASTM Test

accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 558
Prior to placement of the soil cement pavement structure the subgrade soils
should be scarified to a depth of about 200 mm moisture conditioned to between

Cut and Fill Slopes
We anticipate that some minor cut and fill slopes on the order of 2 meters or less
will be constructed at the site to provide the level pads for the proposed
construction We envision that the cut slopes would generally expose the residual
and saprolite soils described in the Surface Soil Conditions section of this report
In general we believe that cut slopes exposing the very stiff residual and saprolite
soils may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical
2H 1V or flatter As previously indicated graded surfaces exposing the on site
materials are highly susceptible to erosion by wind and surface water run off
Therefore freshly graded cut slopes should be protected by appropriate slope
planting or other means to reduce the potential for erosion of the exposed
materials as soon as possible after the slope faces are exposed
Permanent fill slopes constructed using compacted general fill materials or
controlled fill materials may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or
flatter Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched
into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling
operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal
compacted layers in accordance with the fill placement recommendations
presented herein n general fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and
cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face In
addition slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the
potential for erosion of the finished slopes

Drainage
The finished grades adjacent to and beyond the Heli Pad and soil cement
pavement areas should be sloped to shed water away from the pads and pavements to
reduce the potential for ponding Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the
site during or after construction These drainage requirements are essential for the
proper performance of the Heli Pads and soil cement pavements since ponded water
could cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The
edges of the Heli Pad excavations should be properly backfilled to reduce water
infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and
should be maintained to drain surface water run off away from the Heli Pads and
soil cement pavements

Laboratory Testing
One bulk sample designated as S 39 was retrieved from the location of the
proposed Pad as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Bulk sample S 39 was
retrieved specifically for design of the Pad work package WP 5 Other bulk
samples were retrieved from the vicinity of the Pad as shown on the General
Site Plan Plate 1 in support of design of other work packages Test information from
the other bulk samples retrieved in the vicinity were used to formulate the
recommendations presented in this report where appropriate
A moisture content ASTM D 2216 test was performed on the bulk soil sample
S 39 as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The result of the
test is presented on the Summary of Index Property Tests Table A 1
One Atterberg Limits Test ASTM D 4318 was performed on the bulk soil sample
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The results of this
test are presented on the Summary of Index Property Tests Table A 1 Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate A 1
One sieve analysis test with hydrometer ASTM D 422 was performed on the bulk
soil sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil
classification The results of this test are presented on the Summary of Index Property
Tests Table A 1 Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate A 2
One California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed on the
bulk soil sample retrieved to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement subgrade
support The results of the CBR test are presented on the Summary of Index Property
Tests Table A 1 Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate A 3
One Standard Proctor compaction test ASTM D 698 was performed on the bulk
soil sample to evaluate the moisture density relationship Results of the test are
presented on the Summary of Index Property Tests Table A 1 and graphic presentation
of the test results is provided on Plate A 4
