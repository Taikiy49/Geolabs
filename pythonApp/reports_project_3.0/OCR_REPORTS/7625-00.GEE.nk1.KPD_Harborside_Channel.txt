SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a thin layer of surface fill material about 
1 to 4.5 feet thick. Beneath the western half of the site (makai), the fill materials were 
generally underlain by soft to medium hard coral formation and coralline sandstone 
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 26.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Towards the eastern half of the site (mauka), the fill material was generally 
underlain by medium dense coralline detritus extending to about 9 to 24 feet below the 
existing ground surface overlying the coral formation. We encountered groundwater in the 
borings at depths between about 2.5 and 4.5 feet below the ground surface at the time of 
our field exploration, corresponding to about Elevations +1.8 to +1.0 feet MSL.  
Based on the design embankment sections for the proposed retention basin and our 
slope stability analyses, we recommend reinforcing the 1H:1V embankment slope with 
woven geotextile fabric. The woven geotextile fabric reinforcing will provide resistance 
against raveling slope failure on the embankment faces. 
The woven geotextile fabric (Tencate Mirafi PET 70/70 or equivalent) should be 
placed at about every 24 inches of fill height. The front face of the woven geotextile fabric 
should be wrapped at least 3 feet from the front slope faces of the reinforced embankment. 
The reinforced embankment fill material should consist of select granular fill materials. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
geotechnical recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the KPD Harborside Retention Basin project in the Kapolei area on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of 
embankment and site grading only. The findings and recommendations presented herein 
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The proposed KPD Harborside Retention Basin project is located between the 
shoreline and Malakole Street in the Campbell Industrial Park area of Kapolei on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The existing drainage channel is bordered by the Island Energy 
Services oil refinery facility to the south and multiple private business complexes to the 
north. Generally, the channel is approximately 1,500 feet long and is relatively flat with 
top of the channel bank elevations ranging from about +3.5 to +6 feet Mean Sea Level 
(MSL) and bottom of the channel elevations ranging from about +1.5 to +4 feet MSL.  
Based on the information provided, we understand that it is desired to convert the 
existing drainage channel into an unlined retention basin approximately 1,400 feet long. 
The retention basin banks will be built up to an elevation of approximately +12 feet MSL 
with a back-slope inclination of approximately one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) and 
a front-slope inclination of approximately 2H:1V. The bottom of the existing channel will 
be excavated down to an elevation of approximately -10 feet MSL.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age.  As volcanic activity in the 
Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded 
eastern slope forming a broad plateau, now known as the Schofield Plateau.  The project 
site is located on the southwestern flank of the Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering followed by the erosion of Schofield Plateau 
generated sediments, which were transported to the coast.  In the project vicinity and to 
the south, these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments and 
coral/algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge.  The thickness of the 
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to 
over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach.  This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu.  
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.  When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials.  Placement of man-made fills and commercial development of 
the area within the last 20 years have brought the project area to its present form.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling six borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending to depths of approximately 21.3 to 
26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, two bulk samples of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, were obtained to evaluate the 
embankment support characteristics of the near-surface soils. We also conducted two 
field percolation tests in each borehole at various depths for a total of 12 percolation tests 
using constant head and/or falling head methods, as appropriate. The approximate boring 
and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the project site is underlain by a thin layer of surface fill material about 
1 to 4.5 feet thick. The fill material generally consisted of medium stiff sandy silt or medium 
dense gravelly sand. Beneath the western half of the site (makai), the fill materials were 
generally underlain by soft to medium hard coral formation and coralline sandstone 
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 26.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. On the eastern half of the site (mauka), the fill material was generally 
underlain by medium dense coralline detritus extending to about 19 to 24 feet below the 
existing ground surface overlying the coral formation. 
We encountered groundwater in the borings at depths between about 2.5 and 
4.5 feet below the ground surface at the time of our field exploration.  The groundwater 
levels measured corresponds to about Elevations +1.8 to +1.0 feet MSL.  It should be 
noted that the groundwater levels are subject to change due to tidal fluctuations, seasonal 
precipitation, and other factors. 
2.4 
Seismic Design Considerations 
The project site may be subjected to seismic activity. Therefore, seismic design 
considerations will need to be addressed for the project. Based on the subsurface 
materials encountered at the project site and the geologic setting of the area, we believe 
that the project site may be classified as “Stiff Soil Profile” from a seismic analysis 
standpoint. Therefore, we believe the seismic design of the retention basin channel 
embankment may be classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as “Stiff Soil” 
corresponding to a Site Class “D” soil profile based on Chapter 20 of ASCE 7-10.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by a thin layer 
of surface fill material, coral formation, coralline sandstone, and/or coralline detritus, 
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 26.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface.  We encountered groundwater in the borings at depths between about 
2.5 and 4.5 feet below the ground surface at the time of our field exploration, 
corresponding to about Elevations +1.8 to +1.0 feet MSL. 
Based on the proposed retention basin embankment geometry and our slope 
stability analyses, we recommend reinforcing the approximately 10-foot high 1H:1V fill 
slope embankments with woven geotextile fabric. The woven geotextile fabric reinforcing 
will provide resistance against raveling slope failure on the embankment faces.  
In general, the woven geotextile fabric (Tencate Mirafi PET 70/70 or equivalent) 
should be placed at about every 24 inches of fill height. The front face of the woven 
geotextile fabric should be wrapped at least 3 feet from the front face of the reinforced 
embankment. The reinforced embankment fill material should consist of select granular 
fill materials with a maximum particle size of 3 inches compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction (ASTM D1557). 
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical recommendations for the 
design of embankment and site grading are further discussed in the following sections.

Woven Geotextile Fabric Embankments 
It was proposed to construct retention basin embankments along the existing 
drainage channel with a front slope inclination of 2H:1V and a back slope inclination of 
1H:1V. The retention basin will be graded up to an elevation of about +12 feet MSL and 
an invert elevation of about -10 feet MSL. Due to the total height of the front embankment 
slope exceeding 20 feet, the retention basin will be constructed with a 5-foot wide bench 
in the middle of the front slope. Due to the restriction of the property limits, the relatively 
steep back slope will be constructed about 10 feet high.

To reduce potential localized slope failure, we recommend constructing the 1H:1V 
back slope with woven geotextile fabric reinforcing. In general, the woven geotextile fabric 
(Tencate Mirafi PET 70/70 or equivalent) should be placed at about every 24 inches of fill 
height. The front face of the woven geotextile fabric should be wrapped at least 3 feet 
from the front slope faces of the embankment, as illustrated on Plates 3 and 4. 
The reinforced fill material should consist of select granular fill materials with a 
maximum particle size of 3 inches compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction (ASTM D1557). Within 3 feet from the embankment back slope faces, the 
compaction requirement may be reduced to 90 percent relative compaction. The select 
granular fill materials should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger 
than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 
20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in 
accordance with ASTM D1883. The material should consist of crushed coral or basalt. 
Because the woven geotextile fabric is designed to provide reinforcement to the 
slope embankment, installation of utilities within the reinforced soil zone should not be 
permitted after construction of the geotextile fabric embankment system. 
Our slope stability analyses indicate that the above slope embankment specified 
herein will provide sufficient factor of safety against global slope failure under the static 
and the rapid drawdown conditions. However, localized shallow failure mechanism may 
occur under the seismic condition. Details of our slope stability analyses are presented in 
Appendix E. 
To minimize localized shallow slope failure and/or erosional failure, we recommend 
installing the erosion control matting, such as North American Green C350, or equivalent, 
on the face of the hydromulch seeded slope embankment. Anchor pins and trenches 
should be provided for installation of the erosion control matting in accordance with the 
manufacturer’s guidelines.

 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce 
the potential for contamination of the excavated materials. Soft and yielding areas 
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill 
and/or future improvements should be over-excavated to expose firm material, and 
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill. The excavated 
soft soils should not be reused as fill materials and should be properly disposed of 
off-site or in landscaped areas, if appropriate. 
After clearing, the exposed subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 
8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture,

and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction 
requirements for the woven geotextile fabric embankment (except within 3 feet of the 
embankment slope faces) and areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be 
increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum 
dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
If coral formation is exposed at the subgrade level, the subgrade should be 
proof-rolled with a large vibratory drum roller (minimum 10 tons static weight) a 
minimum of eight passes to help detect and collapse near-surface cavities and/or 
voids. The vibratory drum roller or heavy equipment should be operated at a speed 
of about 300 feet per minute. Yielding areas, loose areas, or cavities disclosed during 
clearing and proof-rolling operations should be over-excavated and backfilled with 
compacted fill materials.  
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after the subgrade compaction, we 
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above. Saturation 
and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor 
drainage may require over-excavating the soft areas and replacing these areas with 
engineered fill. A Geolabs field representative should evaluate the need for 
over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil conditions. 
 Fills and Backfills 
Generally, we anticipate the earthwork would likely involve select granular fill 
materials. Select granular fill should consist of non-expansive granular material, such 
as crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The material should be well-graded 
from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and 
should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The 
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a 
maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.

Geolabs should test imported fill materials for conformance with these

On-site soils generated from the retention basin excavation may be reused as select 
granular material, provided that it has a maximum particle size of 3 inches and is free

 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at 
least 90 percent relative compaction (or 95 percent relative compaction for the woven 
geotextile fabric embankment and finished subgrades subjected to vehicular traffic).  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory smooth drum 
rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, 
or ponding should not be allowed to compact the fills.  Where compaction is less than 
required, additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture 
content, as necessary, to obtain the specified compaction. 
 Cavities and Voids 
Coral formations generally are highly variable in terms of hardness, weathering, and 
fracturing. In addition, cavities and/or voids are commonly encountered in coral 
formations within the project vicinity. In many cases, cavities and voids in the coral 
formation may be filled with loose coralline deposits and/or clayey alluvial soils. In 
areas where coralline formation is anticipated near the ground surface, special 
attention should be given to the subgrade preparation to reduce the potential for 
distress to the future improvements.

In areas where the excavated subgrade exposes coral formation, the excavated

 Excavations 
Our field exploration indicated that the majority of the project site may be underlain 
by coral formation and/or coralline sandstone at relatively shallow depths. The coral 
formation and coralline sandstone in the project area appear to be slightly to 
moderately weathered and soft to medium hard in nature. It is anticipated that the 
majority of the coral formation and coralline sandstone may be excavated with heavy 
excavation equipment, such as the large backhoe excavator. However, coral 
formations and coralline sandstone typically contain localized hard and crystallized 
zones. Therefore, we anticipate that some difficult excavation conditions may arise 
in localized areas during construction. It is important to note that the coral formation 
and coralline sandstone encountered in our field exploration exhibited laboratory 
unconfined compressive strengths of up to about 3,200 pounds per square inch (psi). 
Therefore, it is possible that excavations will require the use of hoerams or chipping. 
The contractor must exercise care to avoid over-ripping, which would disrupt the 
structure of the coral formation, resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for 
improvements in the vicinity.

 Cut and Fill Slopes 
We envision the cut slopes at the project site generally will expose competent coral 
formation and/or coralline sandstone along the makai half of the retention basin 
embankment and medium dense coralline detritus (soil-like material) along the 
mauka half of the retention basin embankment. In general, cut slopes and permanent 
fill slopes constructed of the soils may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V 
or flatter. The cut slope in the competent coral formation may be steepened up to 
1H:1V. Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into 
the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill embankment against sliding. The 
filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal 
compacted layers. 
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Surface water should be diverted away 
from the tops of slopes, and slope planting should be provided as soon as possible 
to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
six borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending to depths of about 21.3 to 
26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, two bulk samples of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, were obtained to evaluate the 
compressive and compactibility characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate 
boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were 
drilled by using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and/or rotary 
coring tools 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual length 
of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For 
example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 
74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality 
of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling, 
mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is 
recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe 
the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of 
Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths.

to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentation of the test results is 
provided on Plate B-1. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic representations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-2.

selected rock cores to evaluate their unconfined compressive strength of the coral 
formation and coralline sandstone encountered.  Results of the unconfined compression 
tests are presented on Plate B-3. 
Three Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-4 through B-6. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557 Method B) were performed 
on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-7 and B-8. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the compressive characteristics of the 
soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-9 and B-10.
