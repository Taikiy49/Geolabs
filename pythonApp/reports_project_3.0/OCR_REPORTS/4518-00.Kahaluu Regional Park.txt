2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two separate submarine shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano
Waianae Shield is estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in age 2 7 to 3 4 million years
ago and Koolau Shield is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age 2 2 to
2 5 million years ago After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These more recent volcanic eruptions
are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the worldwide glaciers grew and advanced less water
was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded a larger volume of water became
available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level The larger
glacio eustatic sea level fluctuations ranged from about 250 feet above the present sea
level to about 400 feet below the present sea level Therefore the island shorelines have
transgressed and regressed in response to the sea level fluctuations
The processes of terrestrial erosion and the associated depositional environments
were affected by these glacio eustatic sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low
the erosional base level was correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths
below the present sea level As a result terrestrial sediments were carried farther
seaward and deposited in what had been marine environments When the sea level was
high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher
elevations In addition the higher sea level allowed for the deposition of marine deposits
such as coral and limestone in environments that had been covered with terrestrial soils
and rock

In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock mass from the slopes and movement of the material
downslope toward the axis of the valley by gravity to form colluvial soil deposits Once the
colluvium reaches streams in the valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium Large areas of alluvium can be
found in the lowland areas and areas bordering streams
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the voicanic dome of the
Koolau Shield The headward erosion of these valleys reached the easily eroded
materials in the caldera complex and carved broad amphitheater headed valleys The
heads of these valleys coalesced to form a long steep cliff or pali When the sea level rose
again sediments accumulated in the valleys In the vicinity of the project site these
sediments accumulated in a marshy low energy environment along the shores of
Kaneohe Bay and Kahaluu Pond
The project site is situated in the lower portions of Waihee Valley When this area
was developed man made fills including stockpiles of dredged soils were placed over
the soft marshy sediments to reciaim the land In many cases the fill materials used to fill
the area especially the dredged soil stockpile were derived from the nearby marshy
areas Therefore the composition of the fill materials appears to be the same as that of
the marshy sediments of the area

3 3 Surface and Subsurface Drainage
The infiltration characteristics of the compacted on site soils appear to be poor and
ponding water would cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of
strength of the compacted soils Therefore we wish to emphasize the importance of
surface drainage at the site Due to the relatively flat slope gradients planned for the
surface of the field we recommend that a surface water runoff collection system be
considered and be incorporated into the project construction to provide for proper surface
drainage
To provide for better surface drainage and to reduce the potential for excessive
ponding at the surface of the field we recommend that a series of subsurface drainage
consisting of shallow subsurface trench drains be provided across the field In general the
shallow subsurface trench drains would be at least 18 inches wide and at least 2 feet
deep Each shallow subsurface trench drain would consist of 4 inch or 6 inch diameter
perforated pipes with perforations facing down The shallow subsurface trench drains
should be surrounded and underlain by at least 6 inches of drainage material such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or equivalent A non woven filter
fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent should wrap around the drainage material In
general the 18 inch wide 2 foot deep subdrain trenches should be capped with granular
fill The shallow subsurface trench drains should be spaced at about 50 foot intervals and
should daylight into appropriate drainage structures for proper discharge

structural distress due to differential movements of the slabs due to the nature of the
on site soils
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 000 pounds per square foot psf may be
used for the design of the thickened edge footings bearing on a minimum of 3 feet of
compacted aggregate subbase course This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and
may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below
the lowest adjacent grade Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements
should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility tench As an alternative the footings should extend to a depth
as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce
the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate the total settiements of footings for the structure to be on
the order of about 1 inch Differential settlements between adjacent footings should be on
the order of about 0 5 inches or less

The subgrade soils at the bottom of footing excavations should be moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test
procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density Observation and testing of the footing
excavations should be designated a Special Inspection item Therefore we recommend
that footing excavations be observed by a representative from Geolabs prior to the
placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the competency of the foundation
bearing materials and the required embedment depths
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 45 may be used for footings bearing on the compacted aggregate subbase
course Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent
fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf assuming that the soils
around the footings are well compacted The passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of
the soil should be neglected unless covered by pavements or slabs
We also recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion fill layer consisting of

placed below the slab to provide for uniform support of the slab To reduce the potential
for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious
moisture barrier is recommended on top of the cushion fill layer Two inches of moist fine
sand may be considered above the moisture barrier to provide protection of the membrane
and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Provision of the 2 inches of moist fine sand
above the moisture barrier is optional and will be left up to the discretion of the project

3 5 Leach Fields
We understand that wastewater effluent generated from the future comfort station
at the site will be disposed of on site Therefore we envision that the disposal of
wastewater effluent will be by the leach field method Field percolation tests were
conducted during our field exploration to obtain data pertaining to the preliminary design of
the wastewater disposal system Two field percolation tests designated as P 1 and P 2
were performed at selected locations as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The two field percolation tests were performed in general accordance with the State
Systems and Chapter 10 of the Ten State s Standards to evaluate the permeability of the
subsurface soils for wastewater disposal The tests were performed by drilling 4 inch
diameter boreholes extending to depths of about 5 to 9 feet below the existing ground
surface Approximately 6 inches of gravel was then placed at the bottom of the boreholes
to protect the bottom of the holes from scouring

granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should
also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes or
12 inches above the groundwater level to provide adequate support around the pipes It
is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes or
groundwater level to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the
excavated on site soils The backfill materials should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content placed in about 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are located below pavement
areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
nine borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 9 extending to depths of about 16 5 to
21 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled at the approximate
locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted
drill rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 9
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testin
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
this test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Three sets of sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 D 1140 were performed on
selected samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to
aid in soil classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on
Plate B 2
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to determine the moisture content versus the dry density
relationship Results of the test are presented on Plates B 3 and B 4
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR ASTM D 1883 tests were
performed on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The results of the test are presented on Plates B 5 through
B 7
