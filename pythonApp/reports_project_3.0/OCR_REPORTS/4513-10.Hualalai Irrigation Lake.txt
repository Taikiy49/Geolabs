REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the northern flank of Hualalai mountain which makes up
the west central portion of the island and consists partly of the prehistoric basaltic lava flows
of the Hualalai Volcanic Series which were most likely deposited during the late Pleistocene
Epoch Stearns and MacDonald 1946
Ava lavas are typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface that
resembles a jagged accumulation of loose cobbles boulders and tabular shaped rock
fragments The interior portion of the a a lava flow may contain a very dense thick and
non porous core of harder rock material The a a lava is more viscous when molten and
flows slower than the more liquid and rapid flowing pahoehoe lava Therefore pahoehoe
lava rock is typically characterized by a smoother undulating and billowy flow surface
characterized by an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basalt lavas flows These cavities were
performed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow
cavities and blisters or pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes intra flow cavities are
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are
generally smaller in horizontal extent

Basalt rock is considered to be relatively permeable and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted through
the porous rock matrix along joints fractures inter flow contacts cavities and along clinker
seams The permeability of the clinker seams and cavities is higher therefore they serve as
the major water transmission features
SITE RECONNAISSANCE
The proposed irrigation lake is located to the north of an existing irrigation lake The
proposed irrigation lake is located about 4 000 feet south of Queen Kaahumanu Highway on
the mauka side of the Kaupulehu Resort Development in the District of North Kona on the
Island of Hawaii
The existing ground surface elevations at the proposed irrigation lake site range from
about 440 to 460 feet MSL Based on our observations and the topographic map
provided the project site generally slopes downward from north to south Based on our
observations the project site consists of both a a and pahoehoe lava The a a lava was
observed along the west side of the project site
The surficial expression of the pahoehoe lava flow appeared to be hard and relatively
smooth Large scale subsurface voids were not evident at the surface The surficial
expression of the a a lava flow was irregular jagged and loose Beneath this surficial mantle
of loose clinker was a solid core of very hard basalt material
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The shallow subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by means of
seismic refraction surveys A 12 channel seismograph was utilized in combination with a
12 geophone array to evaluate the relative velocity of an induced compressional wave
P wave signal travelling through the subsurface strata The seismic wave source was
generated with a 12 pound sledge hammer impacting a metallic plate coupled with the
ground surface Two seismic refraction survey lines identified as SR 1 and SR 2 were
performed at the selected locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on the results of the seismic refraction surveys the waveform first arrival
velocities of the subsurface strata were observed to range between about 1 100 to
10 300 feet per second These P wave velocities are considered to be relatively low for the
character of the basalt rock observed at the surface therefore it is suspected that the
near surface rock formation may contain a significant volume of small voids and open
fractures buried in the subsurface Based on our observations and the seismic refraction
surveys performed we believe that the basalt rock formation underlying the project site is
generally medium hard to hard with frequent small voids and is slightly weathered The
following table provides a summary of the results of our seismic refraction surveys

FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS
Based on our site reconnaissance the project site is underlain by a a and pahoehoe
lava flows at the ground surface The a a lava flows at the project site are porous in nature
and may collapse under proposed fill embankments Therefore we recommend that the
a a lava flows located within the footprint of the proposed irrigation lake be removed to the
underlying dense basalt
We anticipate that excavation in the hard basalt formation will result in very jagged
and rough surface which may puncture the impervious liner Therefore we recommend that
a minimum 12 inch thick layer of structural fill material be provided in the areas that will be
covered by the impervious liner

Finished subgrades in cut areas and areas designated to receive fills should be
proof rolled with a vibratory drum roller 15 ton minimum static weight and a D 9
bulldozer or similar heavy equipment a minimum of eight passes to help in detecting
and collapsing near surface cavities and or voids The proof rolling operations should
be performed in the presence of a representative from Geolabs Yielding areas loose
areas or cavities disclosed during clearing and proof rolling operations should be

cavities and or voids contract documents should include additive and deductive unit

the over excavation and backfill quantities
Soft and or loose areas encountered at the excavated subgrade levels should be
removed and replaced with structural fill materials compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction It is recommended that a representative from
Geolabs be present to observe the subgrades prior to the placement of compacted
fills
The sides and bottom of the proposed irrigation lake should be a relatively smooth
surface free of rocky materials and particles which may puncture the impervious liner
We recommend that a minimum 12 inch thick layer of structural fill material
6 inch minus be provided on the sides and bottom of the irrigation lake below the
liner system The finished subgrades of the slope face and bottom of the lake should
be proof rolled with a vibratory smooth drum roller with a minimum weight of 15 tons
for a minimum of 6 passes to provide a relatively smooth surface for the placement of
the underlining and impervious liner

Fill Material
Fills and backfills to be used as structural fills should consist of well graded granular
materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to
prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass In general the excavated
materials a a lava and pahoehoe basalt may be re used as a source of structural fill
materials provided that the materials are processed to meet the maximum size and
gradation requirements presented above
Imported fill materials should be free of organics and deleterious materials and should
be suitable for the intended use based on the recommendations for fill materials
provided in this report In addition imported fill materials should be observed and or
tested by Geolabs prior to being transported to the site for the intended use
Ell PI Te ion Reau
In general fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum
dry density of the soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to
the maximum dry density
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of rock
and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of passes of a
compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be conducted at
the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a representative
from Geolabs On the basis of this testing program the number of passes may then
be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction

We understand that a synthetic liner will be provided for the irrigation lake to reduce
the potential for water infiltration through the underlying material It is our opinion that the
material type performance installation and protection details of the impervious liner should
be designed in accordance with the recommendations by the liner manufacturer Itis critical
that the liner maintain its integrity to prevent saturation and build up of seepage water
pressures within the fill embankment
Our experience with similar synthetic liner installations indicate that there is sometimes
a tendency for the liners to become detached at the top of the water impoundment It
appears that this may be the result of inadequate anchoring of the top of the liner and
insufficient flexibility of the liner material We recommend that the liner manufacturer be
consulted regarding proper construction detailing and installation of the liner with particular
attention to proper anchoring to reduce the potential of liner failure Effects of fluctuating
water levels on the performance of the impervious liner near the top anchoring should also
be considered The anchorage trench should be backfilled with base course material
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

An underlining consisting of a heavy duty geotextile fabric will be required beneath the

Based on the soil rock conditions encountered in our field reconnaissance and the

slopes for the irrigation lake should have adequate factors of safety against sliding and
rotational failures during seismic activity

The pavement subgrades in the fill should be scarified to a minimum depth of about
8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction If scarification is not possible such as in basalt
rock formation the pavement subgrades should be proof rolled with a minimum 15 ton
vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes prior to fill placement to assist in
detecting and collapsing near surface cavities
The aggregate base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction CBR and field density tests should be
performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the
adequacy of the above section
In general paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed
on the site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate
landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be
extended a minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soil below the aggregate base course to
reduce the potential for migration of significant landscape water into the pavement section
Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect the excess water from
