INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the alternative pile foundation and pipe support system for the Kahekili Highway 42 Inch
Water Main Kaneohe to Waihee Line Booster Phase IVA project located in Kaneohe on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our additional field exploration and
laboratory testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of
foundations trench excavation and trench backfill only and are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Kahekili Highway 42 inch Water Main Kaneohe to Waihee Booster Phase IVA
project site is located along Kahekili Highway between Ahuimanu Road and the Valley of The
Temples Memorial Park and Mortuary in Kaneohe on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
approximate limits of the project are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The project
consists of approximately 6 600 linear feet of trench excavation water line installation and
subsequent trench backfill The proposed construction also includes crossings beneath the
existing Ahuimanu Channel and a 75 foot by 7 5 foot box culvert structure
The planned construction consists of the installation of 42 inch diameter ductile iron
pipes at approximately 7 to 16 feet below the existing ground surface These depths
generally correspond to invert elevations ranging from about 28 to 100 feet Mean Sea
Level MSL The new water main alignment will be generally constructed along the mauka
west shoulder of Kahekili Highway
In the current plans the new water main is supported by 12 inch square precast
prestressed concrete piles Both vertical and battered piles are utilized for foundation
support with pile lengths of about 60 feet At the horizontal and vertical bends the pipe is
encased with a reinforced concrete jacket Individual pipe support pile caps constructed of
GEOLABS INC

reinforced concrete are used to support the new water line along the length of the pipe with
no vertical or horizontal bends The pile spacing shown on the plans range from 2 to 8 5 feet
on centers
In the alternative pile foundation and pipe support system we recommend that the
foundation system consist of only vertical 12 inch square precast prestressed concrete piles
for support At the horizontal and vertical bends we recommend that the pipe be encased
in a reinforced concrete jacket and supported on vertical piles Lateral loads imposed on the
foundations at the horizontal bends of the water main are resisted by the lateral load
resistance of the vertical piles supporting the reinforced concrete jacket In addition we also
recommend that the water pipe be supported on a continuous reinforced concrete pile cap
4 feet wide by 16 inches thick to serve as a concrete cradle Pipe cushion material is
recommended on top of the concrete cradle for pipe support

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available
GEOLABS INC
W O 4429 00 Page 5
to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When
the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was correspondingly
lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level When the sea level
was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher
elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reach the
stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream
flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this the transport
of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a poorly
sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional base
levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin to
alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material
Chemical processes also causes induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion
of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate
Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in
headwaters This continued erosion generates material that is transported downslope
covering the older alluvial deposits
GEOLABS INC
W O 4429 00 Page 6
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer sediments are
generally transient in terms of geologic time In addition their consistency and density are
generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits
SITE DESCRIPTION
The alignment of the new 42 inch water main extends along the portion of Kahekili
Highway between Ahuimanu Road and the Valley of the Temples Memorial Park and
Mortuary in Kaneohe on the Island of Oahu Hawaii In general the water main alignment
is located along the mauka west shoulder of Kahekili Highway as shown on the General
Site Plan Plate 2 The shoulder area is relatively level and is covered with grass The water
main also crosses several streets at the edges of the intersection with Kahekili Highway The
existing ground surface along the water main alignment slopes upward gently from the north
toward the south from approximately Elevation 35 feet MSL up to approximately Elevation
120 feet MSL
The water main alignment also traverses an existing concrete lined channel
Ahuimanu Channel from about Station 13 70 to Station 14 50 The channel invert is at
about Elevation 26 feet MSL The water main alignment crosses below the existing
concrete channel at this location Therefore the concrete channel will need to be partially
removed and restored in order to construct the water main
The water main alignment also traverses a concrete box culvert structure below
Hui lwa Street At this location Station 49 85 to Station 50 30 the water main will be
installed by microtunneling methods as specified in the original contract drawings
Therefore driven piles will not be provided at this segment of the water main alignment

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions at the project site generally
consist of surface fills comprised of medium stiff to very stiff silty clay of about 5 to 15 feet
thick The surface fills were underlain by very soft and or loose alluvial deposits ranging in
GEOLABS INC
W O 4429 00 Page 8
thickness from about 10 to 39 feet followed by stiffer and denser alluvial deposits extending
to the maximum depth explored of about 81 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was encountered at depths of about 7 to 36 7 feet below the existing ground
surface at the time of our field exploration
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered during our field exploration we
recommend that the water main and appurtenant structures be supported on a deep
foundation system consisting of driven piles due to the moderate structural loads for the new
water main The driven piles would extend through the very soft and or loose alluvial
deposits and derive support primarily from side resistance within the stiffer and denser
alluvial deposits underlying the weaker alluvial deposits Based on the anticipated structural
loads provided we recommend that 12 inch square precast prestressed concrete piles be
used for support of the water main and appurtenant structures In addition we recommend
that the driven piles be structurally connected with a reinforced concrete beam pile cap to
take advantage cf the lateral load resistance of the piles and recommended capacity of the
driven piles With the use of a reinforced concrete pile cap the spacing between piles may
be increased accordingly based on the overburden soils above the pipe
Based on our field exploration and field exploration by others cobbles and boulders
were encountered within the surface fill and weaker alluvial deposits To facilitate pile
installation through these potential obstructions cobbles and boulders we recommend
predrilling of the pile locations along portions of the water main alignment In general we
believe that the depth of predrilling should extend to about 30 feet below the existing ground
surface especially where the cobbles and boulders may be anticipated
As previously mentioned groundwater was encountered in our borings at depths of
about 7 to 36 7 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration
Therefore provisions for dewatering should be considered for the new water main
excavations that are located below the groundwater level Detailed discussions of these
items and other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following sections

Pile Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site and the moderate
structural loads imposed on the pipe foundations we recommend that a deep foundation
system consisting of driven piles vertical only be used to support the water main and
appurtenant structures Based on the structural load demands we recommend that 12 inch
square precast prestressed concrete piles driven into the stiff to dense alluvial deposits be
used to support the water main and appurtenant structures The driven piles would derive
support primarily from side resistance within the stiff to dense alluvial deposits underlying the
weaker alluvial deposits

facilitate pile driving in groups

Uplift Load Resistance
We anticipate that the driven piles supporting the vertical pipe bends will be subjected
to some uplift loads In general uplift loads are produced by the internal water
pressure in the pipeline at the vertical pipe bends The uplift loads imposed on the
foundation may be resisted by a combination of the dead load of the driven pile and

engineer should check to confirm that the pile member and the foundation system

Pile Foundation Settlements
Settlements of the pile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression of the
pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total settlement of the
pile supported foundations to be less than 0 75 inches with differential settlements
between the piles to be about 0 5 inches or less We believe that these settlements
are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering energies in the range
of at least 25 000 to 30 000 foot pounds in the field Significantly higher energy
hammers hammers with energies greater than 50 000 foot pounds are not
recommended for this project due to the smaller 12 inch square piles The hammer
should be equipped with an energy level control such that driving within the very soft
and or loose soils may be controlled at a lower energy to reduce tensile stress
development which could damage or cause cracking of the piles
GEOLABS INC
W O 4429 00 Page 13
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy to
the top of the pile and also reducing damage to the piles Geolabs should review the
pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate if the equipment is capable of installing
the piles to the recommended capacity The contractor should forward pile and
driving equipment data forms to Geolabs prior to construction for evaluation Geolabs
should review the submittal and assess the driveability of piles with the use of the
selected hammer

Test Pile Program
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we recommend that a test pile
program be undertaken to fuifill the following objectives
1 To examine the driveability of piles with the use of the selected hammer
2 To estimate the production pile lengths
3 To assess the test pile driving criteria which may be modified for use as the
production pile driving criteria
4 To confirm whether predrilling will be necessary at the recommended pile
locations and to confirm the lateral limits of the project requiring predrilling
To achieve these objectives we recommend that the test pile program consist of a
minimum of 8 to 10 percent of the total number of piles for the project The test piles
may be incorporated into the actual foundation system after evaluation of the pile
driving behavior and the pile driving criteria A representative from Geolabs should
select the test pile locations prior to construction Test piles should be cast at least
10 feet longer than the estimated pile lengths to account for potential variations in the
subsurface conditions In addition the test pile program should provide flexibility to
allow for addition or relocation of the test piles as may be deemed necessary by our
office to suit the conditions encountered during the test pile driving Re striking of all
the test piles may be required as part of the test pile program
Due to the relatively low capacity of the piles 45 tons we believe that static load

Dewatering
As previously mentioned groundwater levels higher than the bottom of the planned
excavations are anticipated at several of the utility line crossings Therefore dewatering
of these excavations will be necessary for the water main installation In general the
dewatering operation should be conducted in such a manner that the dewatering will not
cause significant areal ground subsidence which may cause potential damage to the
nearby existing structures Therefore consideration should be given to a dewatering
system that includes a deep cut off wall to reduce the volume of water to be removed
within the excavation and to reduce the areal extent of groundwater drawdown outside of
the trench excavation
Since the excavation dewatering may involve discharge of groundwater from the
dewatering operation into adjacent drainage systems a national pollutant Discharge
Elimination System NPDES permit may be necessary The contractor should consult
their independent consultant or the State of Hawaii Department of Health for the latest
regulations and information pertaining to the NPDES permit application

equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and
he she should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be
required during construction depending on the conditions encountered The
dewatering method selected should have minimal impact on the groundwater level
surrounding the proposed excavation
Because the project site is located in a semi developed area the dewatering
operation should be conducted in such a manner that the dewatering will not cause
significant areal ground subsidence which may cause potential damage to existing
structures and utilities Therefore consideration should be given to a dewatering
system that includes a cut off wall where appropriate
Dewatering Considerations
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of a
suitable method of dewatering
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or
damage to existing structures roads and environment
b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and provide safe
and dry working conditions in the excavation
C The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexibie to allow
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be the sole responsibility
of the contractor His her qualified representative who should be required to be
GEOLABS INC
W O 4429 00 Page 18
continuousiy present on site during dewatering activities will have the best
opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and
to implement as soon as possible necessary precautionary or remedial measures
including but not limited to slowing down or stopping the dewatering operations
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils may
be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations should
be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the excavation
bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be performed without
pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and should be coordinated with
shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not adversely affected
Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in a blowing or heaving
of the excavation bottom or sides
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches that may
be hydraulically connected to the planned excavations If this occurs the granular
bedding and or backfill in the utility trenches could act as subdrains and cause
significant areal groundwater drawdown resulting in settlements and potential
damage to utility lines and or other existing adjacent structures
Trench Backfills
After the installation of the water main foundation support driven piles and reinforced
concrete pile cap we recommend that pipe bedding material be placed above the
completed pile cap prior to pipe installation In general the granular bedding should consist
of about 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel should also be used for the
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes or about 12 inches above the
groundwater level to provide adequate support around the pipes Itis critical that the
free draining materials be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support around the sides of the pipes Based
on the borings very soft and or loose deposits may be encountered within the trench

excavation To reduce the potential for appreciable intrusion of the very soft and or loose
soils into the pipe cushion material a non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent
wrapped around the pipe cushion material may be utilized in these areas
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes or
groundwater levei to the top of the subgrade may consist of the excavated on site soils

backfill materials
The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed
in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where
trenches will be located below areas subjected to vehicular traffic the upper 3 feet of the
trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the water main alignment were further explored by
drilling and sampling ten borings extending to depths ranging from about 74 4 to 81 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings drilled are
shown on the Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 3 of this report The borings were drilled using
a truck mounted drill rig equipped with rotary wash drilling and rotary coring equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 10
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer and torvane shear tests were performed on selected cohesive
soil samples retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample tested The torvane shear test provides a
quick estimate of the undrained shear strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer and torvane shear tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses
a hollow bit to cut into the coral rock formation Tne rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the RQD would be
50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical
Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its

RQD would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The results
of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
Four unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression tests ASTM D 2850 were
performed on selected in situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strengths of the
soils The approximate in situ effective overburden pressures were used as the applied
confining pressured for the relatively undisturbed soil samples The test results and the
stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 2 through B 5
Five consolidation tests ASTM D 2435 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the soft materials encountered The
results of the consolidation tests are presented on Plates B 6 thru B 10

APPENDIX C
Pressuremeter Testing
APPENDIX C
Pressuremeter Testing
The pressuremeter test basically consists of placing an inflatable cylindrical probe in
a predrilled hole and expanding the probe while measuring the changes in volume and
pressure in the probe The purpose of pressuremeter tests is to characterize the
stress strain relationship of the in situ soil
The pressuremeter tests for this project were performed in general conformance with
Procedure A of ASTM Test Designation D 4719 87 An NX size single cell pressuremeter
manufactured by Roctest was used in the tests The pressuremeter probe has a length to
diameter ratio of above 6 0 and the volume of the uninflated probe Vo is 75 cubic
inches in The borehole was prepared by driving a 3 inch OD split barrel sampler with a
140 pound hammer free falling 18 inches Approximately equal pressure steps were used
to inflate the pressuremeter probe in such a way that approximately 9 to 27 increments were
obtained for the test For every pressure step the probe volume readings were taken at
30 and 60 seconds after the pressure increment was applied The volume difference
between the 30 second and 60 second readings is referred to as the creep volume Vc
The test results were corrected for pressure loss due to the stiffness of the probe
membrane and hydraulic head The test curve is based on the 60 second volume readings
The creep curve is a plot of Vc versus the pressure applied to the borehole wall P The P
reading is believed to be associated with the total in situ lateral stress The creep pressure
Pe is related to the in situ yield stress Above Pe plastic deformations become prevalent
