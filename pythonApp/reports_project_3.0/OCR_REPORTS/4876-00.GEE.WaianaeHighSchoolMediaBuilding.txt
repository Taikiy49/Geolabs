SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Waianae High School Media Building project located in the Waianae
area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of
foundations slabs on grade and _ site preparation only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is located within the Waianae High School campus along
Farrington Highway in the Waianae area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The site is an
approximately square shaped vacant area as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on the information provided the proposed building will be a single story
structure with approximately 3 500 square feet of floor area We understand that the site
will require approximately 3 feet of fill to attain the design finish grade Structural details
for the proposed structure were not available during preparation of this report
1 3 Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our field exploration was to obtain an overview of the surface and
subsurface conditions at the project site to develop an idealized soil rock data set for the
formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of
foundations slabs on grade and site preparation for the subject project The work was

2 1 Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age Ice Age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava
banking against its eastern flank
The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late Pliocene and
early Pleistocene Epoch by thin bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building
activities ceased approximately 3 5 to 2 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells
indicates that the island of Oahu has subsided as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation
of this early volcanic activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed behind
the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the lagoons
Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the late Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global
sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to the present sea level on Oahu

The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal sediments may
have also resulted in deposits of coral algal limestone and marls within the predominantly
lagoonal sedimentary units The project site is located near the shoreline in the Waianae
area and is underlain by calcareous reef deposits

3 1 Foundations
Based on the current design concept and the generally competent subsoil
conditions encountered at the site we believe that spread and or continuous footings may
be used to support the proposed new facility building An allowable bearing pressure of up
to may be used for design of footing foundations bearing on the recompacted in
situ soils or new compacted fills This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
For footings bearing on the in situ stiff dense soils the bottom of the footing
excavations should be recompacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction to provide a
relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing steel or
concrete Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations
should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation
may be backfilled with on site granular soils compacted to a minimum of 95 percent

relative compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying
competent materials For footings bearing on new compacted fills the bottom of the
footing excavations should also be recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum
dry density
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades Footings constructed near tops of fill slopes should be embedded
deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back distance of 5 feet measured from
the outside edge of the footing bottom to the slope face If foundations are located next to
utility trenches or easements they should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary
plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts
of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent
below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for
foundation settlement
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings
bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new compacted fills as recommended herein to
be less than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the order of one half 2 inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 4 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new
compacted fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This
assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted Unless covered by pavements
or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected

3 2 Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be required for the construction of
the new facility building Based on our field and laboratory test results we believe that
new building slabs may be constructed on grade provided that the slab subgrade is
properly prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented below and in the
Site Preparation section of this report
In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer consisting of
No 3 Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below the slabs to provide
more uniform support of the slabs and to serve as a capillary moisture break To reduce
the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel cushion layer
Slab subgrades should be prepared by scarifying the subgrade soils to a depth of
about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction The compaction requirement should be increased to 95 percent
relative compaction for slab subgrades that will be subjected to future vehicular traffic
Slab subgrades should be kept moist until covered by concrete Where shrinkage cracks
are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils be thoroughly
moistened to close the cracks and recompacted to provide a firm base prior to concrete
placement Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement
weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement
with well compacted fill

3 3 Site Preparation
We anticipate that the majority of the site preparation work for the proposed project
will consist of fills of approximately 3 feet to achieve the design finish grades
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation organic debris construction debris
deleterious material and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of
properly off site or in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials with the cleared and grubbed spoils Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing should be over excavated to expose firm
natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill
The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site Contract
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and
engineered fill placement to account for variations in the over excavation quantities
After clearing and demolition the excavated subgrades beneath future slab and
pavement areas should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content and proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton
vibratory drum roller to a minimum of 90 and 95 percent relative compaction
respectively to detect the presence of potential near surface cavities sinkholes and or
loose zones The proof rolling operations should extend a minimum of 5 feet beyond
the building footprint of the future building If the proof rolling operations detect a near
surface cavity or loose zone the area should be ripped open cleaned of debris and
then backfilled with compacted fill material or lean concrete
Fill materials required for the project construction should consist of non expansive
select granular material such as crushed coral and basalt The material should be
well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension
and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum
swell of one percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test

A granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such
as No 3B Fine gravel should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the
compaction effort on the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is
critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site soils or select granular
fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

3 6 Drainage
The finished grades outside the new facility building should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that
gutter systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away
from the foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs
should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete
bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the concrete to
reduce the potential for water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain surface runoff away from the slab and footing
foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the new media building site were explored by
drilling and sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to
depths ranging from about 15 9 to 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of these borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight
augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
