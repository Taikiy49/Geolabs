SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The subsurface conditions encountered at the site consisted of about 7 to 8 inches 
of AC over fill material, alternating layers of residual and saprolitic soils, and basalt 
formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about 35.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. We encountered seepage groundwater in the drilled borings at depths of 
about 13.5 and 24 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field 
exploration, corresponding to elevations of about +551 and +568 feet MSL. It should be 
noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, perched groundwater, and other factors. 
Based on the geometry of the roadway and slope conditions, we recommend 
utilizing the soil nail retaining wall system to mitigate the slope instability and restore the 
Nuuanu Pali Drive roadway. We recommend a minimum length of the soil nails to be at 
least 15 feet long to be installed with a staggered grid pattern in a spacing of 5 feet 
center-to-center for horizontal and vertical spacing. The soil nails should be installed by 
drilling a minimum of 8-inch diameter holes with inclinations of approximately 20 degrees 
from horizontal. 
Based on the results of the global slope stability analyses, we believe the proposed 
slope mitigation scheme, consisting of three tiers of soil nail retaining walls, is capable of 
providing minimum factors of safety of greater than 1.5 for the static loading condition and 
1.2 for the seismic loading condition.

recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed in support of the Emergency Repairs in the Vicinity of 3708 Nuuanu Pali Drive 
project in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general site 
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. These findings 
and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the evaluation of the slope stability 
in support of the design of slope mitigation only. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.  
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located in the vicinity of 3708 Nuuanu Pali Drive in the District 
of Honolulu, on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. We understand that the existing Concrete 
Rubble Masonry (CRM) retaining wall along Nuuanu Pali Drive collapsed. The collapsed 
retaining wall was 6.5 feet high and 40 linear feet long and served as grade separation 
between the roadway and Nuuanu Stream Bank. 
Based on the information provided and our site observation, a Banyan Tree fell 
over Nuuanu Stream Bank, where a portion of the tree roots extended under the existing 
retaining wall footing and resulted in destabilizing the footing subgrade. The large tree 
roots lifted the wall section, causing adverse structural damage and collapsing the 
existing CRM retaining wall. To provide safety to the public motorists and prevent soft 
ground conditions from extending into the roadway, it is desired to repair the collapsed 
retaining wall.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield 
volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle 
to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series 
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone eruptions. These later eruptions are referred 
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins, such that sea levels fell below the present sea level. 
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
Following the extrusion of lavas in the early Pleistocene Epoch, the island 
underwent a long cycle of erosion and weathering, forming the prominent ridgelines and 
summits as we know today. During the erosion period, the interfluvial ridges and areas 
such as the Schofield Plateau were subjected to deep chemical weathering, forming a 
mantle of residual soil and saprolite, which grades with depth to saprolite and weathered 
basalt rock formation. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, renewal of volcanic activity occurred with the 
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series. Along the crest of the ridge along the 
westerly side of Manoa Valley are the Tantalus, Roundtop, and Sugarloaf vents. The 
project site is situated on an interfluvial ridge on the southeastern flank of Koolau.

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located along the downslope side of the roadway fronting 3708 
Nuuanu Pali Drive in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. Prior to the heavy storm 
event, the slope face appeared to be heavily vegetated based on our site observation in 
October 2022. A Banyan Tree fell over Nuuanu Stream Bank, and its roots caused 
damage to the existing retaining wall footing, resulting in the collapse of the wall. The 
tree's roots lifted the wall section, causing structural damage and creating unstable 
ground conditions near the roadway. 
The project site is generally less than about 35 feet wide and extends down from 
the roadway to the Nuuanu Stream at about a one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) slope 
inclination for a horizontal distance of about 50 feet. Based on our field observations and 
the topography survey map provided to us, the project site generally slopes downward to 
the west with existing ground surface elevations between about +575 and +589 feet Mean 
Sea Level (MSL). 
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling three borings, designated 
as Boring Nos. 1, 2 and 3, extending to depths of about 26.5 to 35.5 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. 
Based on the results obtained from our field exploration, the project site is 
generally underlain by fill placed over residual/saprolitic soils overlying weathered basalt 
formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about 35.5 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. The fill generally consisted of very dense sandy gravel and very stiff 
clayey silt extending to depths of about 1.5 to 3.5 feet below the existing pavement 
surface. Underlying the fill, our borings encountered residual/saprolitic soils generally 
consisting of stiff clayey silt and medium dense silty gravel extending to depths of about 
19.5 to 25.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. Beneath the residual/saprolite 
soils, our borings encountered weathered basalt formation extending to the maximum 
depth explored of about 35.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. 
Groundwater was encountered at depths varying from about 13.5 to 24 feet below 
the existing pavement surface at the time of our exploration. The groundwater levels

of asphaltic concrete (AC) over fill material, alternating layers of residual and saprolitic

below the existing ground surface. We encountered seepage groundwater in the drilled 
borings at depths of about 13.5 and 24 feet below the existing ground surface at the time

should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of

Based on the geometry of the roadway and slope conditions, we recommend 
utilizing the soil nail retaining wall system to mitigate the slope instability and restore the 
Nuuanu Pali Drive roadway. We recommend a minimum length of the soil nails to be at 
least 15 feet long to be installed with a staggered grid pattern in a spacing of 5 feet 
center-to-center for horizontal and vertical spacing. The soil nails should be installed by 
drilling a minimum of 8-inch diameter holes with inclinations of approximately 20 degrees

3.1 
Soil Nail Retaining Walls 
In general, we believe the downslope of the roadway may be stabilized by utilizing 
tiers of soil nail retaining walls using the top-down construction method. The following 
discussion provides a general stabilization mechanism and recommended design 
parameters.

A soil nail retaining wall system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars 
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize the steep cut slope. Shotcrete facing is generally 
applied to the face of the cut slope, providing either temporary support or the permanent 
retaining wall facing. The shotcrete mixture may be stained with a preferred color tone, 
providing a soft aesthetic appearance. The reinforcing bars provide both additional tensile 
and shear strengths to the soil and reinforce the sidewall of the steep cut slope. The 
reinforced soil mass behaves in a manner similar to a gravity retaining wall but lacks a 
footing. In general, the face of the soil nail retaining wall system is slightly battered at a 
slope inclination of about 1H:12V for aesthetic purposes. 
Soil nail retaining walls are normally installed in a cut condition only. In the event 
that the proposed retaining walls may require partial fills, we recommend that the fills be 
placed first using shotcrete or flowable fill to allow construction of the soil nail retaining 
wall entirely in a cut condition. 
3.1.1 Design Soil Parameters 
The design of the soil nail retaining wall system will need to consider both the internal 
and external stability of the reinforced soil mass. The design of the internal stability 
includes establishing the size, spacing, orientation, and length of the grouted 
reinforcing bars. The external stability includes the overall slope stability of the 
reinforced slope. The geotechnical design parameters needed for evaluating the 
internal and external stability of the soil nail retaining wall system are provided in the 
following table:

3.1.2 Soil Nail Anchors 
Based on our global slope stability analyses, we recommend a minimum length of 
the soil nails to be 15 feet with a staggered grid pattern in a spacing of 5 feet 
center-to-center for horizontal and vertical spacing. It should be noted that the soil 
nail anchor drilling may encounter denser saprolite consisting of cobbles/boulders 
material. The contractor should be notified of having suitable drilling equipment to 
advance through the hard soil/rock material and achieve the depth required. 
The soil nails should be installed by drilling a minimum of 8-inch diameter holes with 
inclinations of approximately 20 degrees from horizontal. Based on the above design 
parameters, we believe that the soil nail anchors are capable of achieving a design 
load capacity of 30 kips with a factor of safety of at least 2. Based on our soil nail 
analyses, the specified soil nail design load capacity is sufficient to support the load 
demand transmitted from the soil nail retaining wall for static and seismic loading 
conditions with 150 psf of surcharge and 0.215g of ground acceleration, respectively.

the near-surface soils are moderately corrosive (Corrosion Rating of 4) to buried 
metallic structures. Based on the relatively low values of chloride content and sulfate 
content tested on the in-situ materials, we believe that the near-surface soils at the

3.1.3 Drainage 
The design of the soil nail retaining wall system does not include hydrostatic 
pressures that might be caused by groundwater or water trapped behind the wall. 
Therefore, the soil nail retaining wall should be well-drained to reduce the build-up of 
hydrostatic pressure. A typical drainage system would consist of 2-foot wide strips of 
a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, placed on the 
face of the excavation between the soil nails. The prefabricated drainage product 
should extend from the top to the base of the wall and should be hydraulically 
connected to a weephole at the base of the wall. The weepholes should discharge 
to appropriate outlets. 
3.1.4 Soil Nail Testing 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated behind the soil nail retaining wall, we 
recommend performing a pre-production verification test on a sacrificial soil nail. In 
addition, proof tests should be performed on the selected production soil nails, at 
least 10 percent of the production soil nails, during construction to confirm the bond 
stresses used in the design. 
The verification test should be loaded gradually to the maximum test load of about 
200 percent of the allowable design load and held for creep testing. In addition, the 
load at every loading increment should be held for at least 10 minutes, and the creep 
test should be held for at least 60 minutes. The proof tests should consist of 
subjecting the soil nail to at least 150 percent of the allowable design loads, and the

3.1.5 Workmanship 
The construction of soil nail retaining walls should be performed by a specialty 
contractor experienced in the construction of soil nail retaining walls. Due to the 
specialized nature of the soil nail retaining wall construction, a Geolabs 
representative should be present to observe the geotechnical aspects of the soil nail 
retaining wall construction and testing of soil nails. 
Due to the soft/loose consistency of the near-surface materials behind the damaged 
slope embankment, caving in and/or sloughing may occur during the soil nail drilling. 
To reduce the potential for caving-in of the drilled holes, temporary casing of the 
drilled holes would be required during the soil nail installation as a casing method. 
Some hard drilling conditions should be anticipated to be encountered during the 
installation of the soil nails due to the presence of the cobbles/boulders and basalt 
formation. In addition, soft/loose zones of sand and gravel may be encountered in 
the saprolitic materials. 
Drilling fluids, including mineral and polymer slurry, should not be allowed for the soil 
nail installation. If utilizing air pressure during the drilling, the contractor should be 
cautious when drilling is advanced into the loose soil materials to prevent 
undermining the roadway subgrade. Undermining the roadway subgrade will further 
cause ground settlement and loss of foundation support. Excessive air pressure may 
also cause enlarging the borehole and result in greater grout taken. 
The contractor should fully investigate the project site and make his/her own 
interpretation. It is the contractor’s responsibility to bid the project accordingly with

3.2 
Slope Stability Evaluation 
Slope stability analyses of the soil nail retaining wall were evaluated using the 
computer program Slide2 from Rocscience Inc. The slope stability analyses were 
performed at a representative slope section of the project site. 
Bishop’s simplified method of limit equilibrium was used to evaluate the balance of 
forces and moments acting within the slope sections to determine the factors of safety for 
the soil nail retaining wall based on the generalized subsurface profile and estimated 
shear strength parameters. The Bishop’s simplified method of slope stability analysis 
assumes a circular slip surface, which is divided into slices with finite mass. The following 
parameters were used in our global slope stability analyses to evaluate the rotational 
slope failure mechanism: 
1. 
Slope geometry. 
2. 
Density and shear strength of the in-situ fill, residual/saprolitic soils, and 
basalt rock formation based on the borings drilled at the project site and 
empirical correlations. 
3. 
Inclusion of construction traffic surcharge and seismic excitation. 
To evaluate the long-term stability of the selected slope areas, effective stress soil 
parameters were determined and utilized in our analyses. Generally, a minimum safety

exterior loads, such as seismic conditions. 
To evaluate the potential for slope instability due to seismic ground motion, a

generally are widely acceptable by various agencies, including Federal, State and City.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1, 2 and 3, extending to depths of 
approximately 26.5 to 35.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools.  
Our geologist/logger classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual 
and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3.2. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch O.D. standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. 
In addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general 
accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch O.D. modified California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number 
of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be 
factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock 
Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs 
of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality 
of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and 
Minerals."

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture content (ASTM D2216) and unit weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and a 
graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Five Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the peak and/or residual shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. 
The test results are presented on Plates B-3 through B-8. 
To evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on-site clayey soils, 
one unconfined compression test was performed on a selected soil sample in accordance 
with ASTM D2166. The test result is shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depth. The individual stress-strain curve of the unconfined compression test is 
presented on Plate B-9. 
Four Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D7012 Method C) were 
performed on selected rock cores to evaluate the uniaxial compressive strength of the 
rock cores obtained. Results of the unconfined compression tests are presented on 
Plate B-10. 
One set of Corrosion test, including pH (ASTM G51) Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), was 
performed by our office and Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on a selected soil 
sample obtained from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on 
Plate-B-11.
