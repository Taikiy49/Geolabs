INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering reconnaissance
performed for the Hualalai Irrigation Well Nos 5 and 6 project located in the District of
North Kona on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field reconnaissance and performance
of seismic refraction surveys at the site and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our analyses for the proposed water system project These
recommendations are intended to assist in the design of site grading and the water
transmission pipeline and access road construction only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes which include the following Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has
had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa
and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the northern flank of Hualalai which makes up the
west central portion of the Island of Hawaii The project site is located on gently sloping
terrain at about the 900 foot elevation In general the near surface lava rock formation
encountered at the site consists of layered and thinly bedded pahoehoe with some relatively
minor a a type flows The lava flows at the site appear to have emanated from the higher
elevations located southerly of the project site flowing toward the ocean north and northwest
of the site
General Lava Characteristics
Ava lavas are typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface that
resembles a jagged accumulation of loose cobbles boulders and tabular shaped rock
fragments The interior portion of the a a lava flow may contain a very dense thick and
non porous core of harder rock material The a a lava is more viscous when molten and
flows slower than the more liquid and rapid flowing pahoehoe lava Therefore pahoehoe
lava rock is typically characterized by a smoother undulating and billowy flow surface
characterized by an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Individual pahoehoe lava flows are generally thinner than their a a lava counterpart
The pahoehoe and a a lava rocks may range from medium hard to very hard depending upon
the degree of internal fracturing and the amount of vesicles solidified gas bubbles contained
in the lava rock mass
Basalt rock is considered to be relatively permeable and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted through
GEOLABS INC

the porous rock matrix along joints fractures inter flow contacts cavities and along clinker
seams The permeability of the clinker seams and cavities is higher therefore they serve
as the major water transmission features
Lava Tube Characteristics
Buried cavities and voids ranging from large caves lava tubes to localized blisters
are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe lava These cavities are
created when the flowing molten lava suddenly ceases in the interior core of the lava flow
The cavities that are created by molten lava represent both lava tubes and caves intra flow
cavities and localized blisters and void pockets inter flow cavities Blisters and void
pockets inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent and are typically
isolated closed void structures formed by volcanic gas that was trapped within the flow
interior Some blisters may extend a short distance downhill before pinching out and
terminating
Lava tubes are formed when the molten lava drains from the cooling flow interior
leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally
along the flow length While the lava flow is active the feeding river of magma crusts over
to develop a continuous roof leaving the interior lava stream flowing through a tunnel of
its own making Once the molten lava source ceases the flow tunnel drains and becomes
a hollow lava tube The lava tube may branch converge plunge and rise in a general
downhill direction to feed and maintain the advancing lava flow front The main lava tube
may extend downslope for several miles occasionally reaching all the way to the coastline
The lava tube cavities resemble smooth sided tunnels consisting of arched roofs
and nearly flat floors that may be in excess of about 40 feet in diameter Over time large
blocks of rock may fracture and fall from the roof to create a boulder strewn floor Where
the lava tube roof breaches the ground surface large pits skylights form that allow
surface observation of the underground lava tube structure In some cases the roof of the
lava tube may be composed of only a few feet of fractured rock located below the ground
surface Subsequent ground disturbance or weathering of the thin roof structure may

W O 4513 00 Page 6
cause the roof to collapse and create a new open pit Thus the collapse of large
near surface lava tubes can be a significant hazard to land use
SITE DESCRIPTION
As previously mentioned the project site is located toward the southeast of the
Hualalai Resort in the Kaupulehu area of the District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii
The proposed Hualalai Irrigation Well Nos 5 and 6 project site is located on the mauka side
of Queen Kaahumanu Highway as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The project
site is located in the vicinity of the existing Well and 4 and associated access road
as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The project site is located on relatively undeveloped gently sloping terrain located
adjacent to the existing land conservation boundary At the time of our field work the site
was accessed via a series of connecting access roads and trails from the Queen
Kaahumanu Highway located toward the north The project site contains an existing rough
graded trail which traverses generally along the elevation contour from the existing Well
No 2 site to the east toward the proposed Well No 6 site The approximate distance
between Well No 2 and Well No 6 is about 5 600 lineal feet as shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
The existing access trail crosses gently undulating terrain consisting of pahoehoe lava
flow surfaces with minimal visible soil cover It appears that during the construction of the
access trail some thin rock fills were spread at topographic low elevations depressions to
distribute the road grade more evenly and to allow for access water well drilling vehicles
Minor rock cuts of about 1 to 2 feet in depth were observed at localized hillcrests located at
the western end of the project site The existing access trail crosses what appears to be a
historic footpath intersection located at about Station 20 45
The ground surfaces at the project site range from about Elevation 860 feet Mean
Sea Level MSL at the western end of the site to about Elevation 955 feet MSL at the
eastern end of the site The ungraded portions of the project site are vegetated with dryland

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The shallow subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by means of
seismic refraction surveys A 12 channel seismograph was utilized in combination with a
12 geophone array to evaluate the relative velocity of an induced compressional wave
P wave signal travelling through the subsurface strata The seismic wave source was
generated with a 12 pound sledge hammer impacting a metallic plate coupled with the
ground surface Five seismic refraction survey lines identified as SR 1 through SR 5 were
performed at the selected locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on the results of the seismic refraction surveys the waveform first arrival
velocities of the subsurface strata were found to range between about 1 600 to 3 700 feet
per second These P wave velocities are considered to be relatively low for the character of
the basalt rock observed at the site therefore it is suspected that the rock formation may
contain a significant volume of small voids and open fractures buried in the subsurface
Based on our observations and the seismic refraction surveys performed we believe that the
basalt rock formation underlying the project site is generally medium hard with frequent small
voids and is slightly weathered The following table provides a summary of the results of our
seismic refraction surveys

Based on our field reconnaissance the project site is generally underlain by medium
hard to hard basalt rock with numerous small voids As discussed previously a portion of
the proposed new access road traverses a zone of relatively high hazard for encountering
potentially large lava tubes and caves Based on our evaluation of the subsurface conditions

The portion of the proposed access road located between about Station 47 50
through Station 50 50 traverses ground that is suspected to contain large lava tubes buried
at variable depths in the subsurface The partially collapsed lava tubes and cave structures
were observed to range between about 10 and 40 feet in diameter with estimated roof
thickness ranging from less than 5 feet to over 15 feet depending upon location The lava
tube exposures are located toward the north and south of the access road and are believed
to cross beneath the access road
It is possible that the trench excavation work for the new pipeline may encounter the
large cavities especially between Station 47 50 and Station 50 50 In the event that the
excavation work encounters a cavity the exposed void located beneath the pipeline should
be collapsed to expose the lava tube and or cave and should be backfilled with appropriate
materials depending on the size of the cavity For this portion of the roadway we
recommend that the access road subgrades be proof rolled with a Caterpillar D 10 bulldozer
a minimum of eight passes followed by eight passes with a minimum 15 ton vibratory drum
roller to assist in detecting and possibly collapsing the potential near surface cavities

Fill and Backfill Materials
In general we recommend that structural fill materials be used to fill the areas from
the existing ground surface to achieve the design subgrades Structural fill materials
should consist of well graded granular materials of 6 inches in largest dimension with
sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass The
excavated on site materials may be re used as a source of structural fill materials
provided that the materials are processed to meet the above gradation requirements
lf the excavated on site materials do not contain sufficient fines to produce a
fragments or boulders should be considered to provide the required gradation and
particle sizes to develop a well graded material Imported material if necessary
should be observed and or tested by Geolabs for its suitability prior to being
transported to the site for the intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general structural fills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction The aggregate base course layer should also be
moisture conditioned and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
GEOLABS INC
W O 4513 00 Page 13
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Compaction should be accomplished by
sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment
Where fills are composed of rock and or cobbles conventional compaction testing
would not be practicable Therefore a testing program to evaluate the number of
passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs On the basis of this testing program the number of
passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction For
preliminary estimating purposes compaction may be accomplished by using a large
vibratory drum roller with a static weight of at least 15 tons traveling at a speed of
about 200 to 300 feet per minute The roller should pass over the fill surface at
least eight times for structural fill
Fill Slopes
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the structural fill materials recommended herein
may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or
flatter Fills that are to be placed on existing ground steeper than 5H 1V should be
benched The fill slope face should be finished to a relatively smooth and
well compacted surface The fill slope face should be free of large boulders greater
than 18 inches in largest dimension and should also be free of voids which would
allow erosion and migration of fines to occur In addition slope planting or other
means of slope protection should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the
potential for erosion of the finished slopes

Road Subgrade and Surfacing
Based on the information provided it is desired to utilize compacted aggregate base
course treated with an asphaltic emulsion seal coat for the proposed access road finished
surface In general we anticipate that the vehicle loading of the proposed service roadway
for this project would consist primarily of passenger vehicles and light pick up trucks
Therefore we have assumed generally light traffic loading conditions for the road surface
design purposes
Based on our review of the Soil Sement product information and previous test
sections performed for other projects we believe that this product may not be appropriate
for the coating of aggregate base course wearing surfaces as proposed for this project
The aggregate base course materials that are proposed for use on this project may not
contain an adequate amount of fine soil particles to permit acceptable sealing of the road
surface and protection from erosion If it is desired to pursue the use of this product test
sections should be performed to evaluate the product performance with respect to
treatment of aggregate base course surfaces We believe an asphaltic emulsion sealant
may be more appropriate for use on this project
The subgrade below the roadway areas should be prepared in accordance with the
recommendations provided in the Site Preparation subsection of this report The
aggregate base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

Water Transmission Line
The new underground water line trenches will be excavated in the medium hard to
hard basalt rock formation Therefore the trench excavation work for the water line
installation will likely involve excavation in the basalt formation and will require hard ripping
or hoerams
For the new water lines granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or 1 inch minus well graded materials is recommended
below the pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation or 1 inch minus well graded materials should be used for the initial trench
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material
to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper backfill material around the pipes and
improper placement could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
Where the 1 inch minus well graded granular materials are used for bedding and or initial
trench backfill the 1 inch minus well graded granular materials should be compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well graded granular materials less
than 6 inches in maximum size The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction Where trenches are located in asphaltic concrete paved
surfaces the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
