FIELD EXPLORATION
The geophysical surveys were conducted using a ground penetrating radar system
manufactured by Geophysical Survey Systems Incorporated The system consisted of
a SIR System 3 control unit with 500 Megahertz and 100 Megahertz antenna sets
Ground penetrating radar utilizes impulse radar technology to obtain real time
profiles of the subsurface materials A radar frequency pulse is transmitted into the
GEOLABS HAWAITi
W O 2688 00
subsurface and when it encounters an interface between two materials with differing
electrical properties it is reflected back to a receiver on the surface In a relatively
homogeneous medium this reflection is noted as an anomaly
The reflected pulse is intercepted by the receiver and then electronically processed
and printed on a graphic recorder producing a record similar to a marine depth recorder
chart As the transmitter and receiver antenna array is moved across the ground surface
a real time graphic record of the subsurface is generated Markers can be inserted into
the data print to reference distances as the array is moved providing a means to
physically locate anomalies
Ground penetrating radar is generally considered applicable to the location of
subsurface voids and buried structures Depth of penetration and data resolution are
dependent upon soil type moisture content electrical properties antenna frequency and
power
The use of ground penetrating radar is limited by the presence of conductive
materials within the profile being surveyed This is particularly true if the conductive
materials are in the upper or surface portions of the subsurface profile Conductive
materials attenuate the electromagnetic signal and interfere with penetration of the radar
pulses Generally ground penetrating radar will not function with conductivities higher
than about meters Mho meters

DISCUSSION OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our geophysical surveys of the old landfill site as located in the field indicate that
there are ground penetrating radar anomalies at shallow depths within the areas
traversed These anomalies appear to be located at depths of about three 3 feet or less
below the ground surface as it existed at the time of our field exploration
Based on observations of materials exposed during the site clearing operations
it is likely that many of these anomalies are indicative of buried metal objects However
it is not possible to conclusively identify those anomalies as drums Furthermore since
penetration was limited due to the nature of the surface clay soils it was not possible to
conclusively determine the natural subsurface conditions at the site
It is our opinion that consideration should be given to conducting gradient
magnetometer surveys at the site Various geophysical operator consulted generally
