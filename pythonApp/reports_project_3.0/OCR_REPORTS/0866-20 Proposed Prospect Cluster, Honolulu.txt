SITE CONDITIONS
Surface
The project site is located on the northern slope of Punch
bowl Crater above Prospect Street near the intersection eee
Prospect and Puowaina Streets See Plate 1 The site is
presently heavily overgrown with trees shrubs and grasses
Located in the northeast corner of the site is a burned out
house and associated concrete slab two loose rock walls and
a sidewalk and stairway leading to Prospect Street
The site is fairly steep sloping up from the street level
at a gradient of 3 1 H V Surveyed elevations range from
about 195 at the street to 264 feet at the southeast boundary

The probings were concentrated along the rear property line

Geology and Soil Conditions
Punchbowl Crater is composed of a vitric volcanic tuff formed
during a violent phreatomagmatic eruption of ash similar to
Salt Lake Crater and Diamond Head Crater Volcanic tuff
forms a brown hard glassy rock known locally as mudrock
The site is underlain entirely by the Punchbowl tuff
Exposed rock outcrops can be found along Prospect Street in
a 10 to 20 foot nearby vertical cliff Outcrops are also
present in a 3 to 5 foot ledge behind the derelict dwelling
and on the ground surface around the dwelling The tuff is
highly fractured and jointed Bedding planes are evident
dipping approximately 40 degrees in Test Pit No 2 to seven
degrees in the existing steep rock outcrop at the street level
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 866 20
Over most of the site the tuff is highly weathered up to a
depth of about 8 5 feet Test Pit No 2 and easily excavated
with a backhoe The weathered tuff is very highly fractured
and breaks easily into a platy gravel sand and silt mixture
when excavated Overlying the tuff over most of the site
is a relatively thin layer of recent alluvium and slope wash
material consisting of a 1 to 3 foot layer of brown clayey
silt
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our test pits hand probing and geo
logic reconnaissance the site appears to be underlain by
competent subsoils and it is our opinion that the site can
be developed for the proposed residential development pro
vided the soil engineering recommendations are followed
Site Grading
Based on the test pit data the soils at the site can be
generalized as follows
1 Brown clayey silt loose recent slope wash
one to three feet thick
2 Highly weathered mudrock sandy silt and gravel
three to six feet thick
3 Dense mudrock five to eight feet deep
The upper portions of the cuts less than five to eight feet
should generally be easily excavated however some hard
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 866 20
ripping may be required in the deeper cuts through the dense
mudrock formation
The proposed cut slopes are generally from about 5 to 10 feet
high and therefore would be in the highly weathered mudrock
and recent slope wash deposit Most of the highly weathered
mudrock has essentially decomposed to a stiff soil
Geologic reconnaissance indicated that the bedding planes in
the weathered mudrock may dip as steep as 40 degrees approx
1 in portions of the site and therefore cuts
should be flatter than H V in order to reduce the
instability along the bedding planes For these reasons cut
slope ratios should be limited to 14 1 H V maximum
Fill slopes of 2 1 H V are recommended
The site should be cleared of vegetation and debris then
scarified and recompacted to 90 of its maximum dry density
established by ASTM D 1557
Fills should be placed in 9 inch loose lifts compacted to the
above specified density
Where the existing ground is steeper than 5 1 H V benching
and keying are required to properly bond the new fill to the
slope The filling operation should start at the lowest point

the above structural fill requirements For construction
details refer to Plate 3
The on site upper slope wash material is generally expansive
and should not be used for fill The underlying weathered
mudrock soils less than 8 inch size can be used as a source
of fill material However they may have moderate swell
potential and should be compacted wet of optimum moisture
content to reduce the swelling potential
Building Foundations
It appears that most of the building foundations will be in
cut Spread or continuous footing foundations extending
through the upper slope wash deposit and bearing on the
underlying weathered mudrock formation is recommended for
the structures
An allowable bearing value of 4000 p s f can be utilized
for foundations bearing on the dense weathered mudrock for
mation The above values are for total dead plus live loads
and may be increased by one third when temporary loads are
considered
On level areas the bottom of the above designed footings
should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the outside

finished grade to resist erosion develop the bearing capacity
of the subsoils and to reduce the effects of the moderately
expansive soils
Footings located on or near the tops of steep slopes will be
subject to erosion and slope creep of the surface soils
Therefore these footings should be embedded an additional
amount to help resist downhill movement Footings on the
sloping areas of the site should be embedded a sufficient
depth to obtain a 6 foot minimum horizontal set back distance
which is measured from the outer edge of the footing out to
the slope face
The site should be graded to drain water away from the build
ing foundations
Retaining and Basement Walls
Retaining and basement walls up to 10 feet high are planned
Well graded granular material such as black sand coral or
base course should be used as backfill material behind the
walls Impervious material should be used for the top 2 feet
of the backfill
The recommendations given under foundations should be followed
for the design of the wall foundations

For lateral earth pressures the following can be used
Walls unrestrained at the top 35 p c f equivalent
fluid pressure
Walls restrained at the top 55 p c f equivalent
fluid pressure
Additional loads due to traffic or surcharge
should be included where applicable
A friction factor of 0 4 can be utilized to determine the
sliding resistance of the wall foundation Additionally a
passive soil resistance of 300 p c f may be used
A subsurface drainage system should be used to prevent the
build up of hydrostatic pressure behind the walls
Slab on Grade
The laboratory soil tests indicate that the on site soils
have low to moderate swell potential therefore slab on
grade should have deeper perimeter footings extending down
a mimimum of 12 inches below the finished grade to reduce
ground movement due to drying and wetting around the slab
All slab subgrade should be kept adequately moist prior to
concrete placement to minimize future heaving
It is recommended that slab on grade should be placed over
a 4 inch layer of base course compacted to 95 of its maximum
dry density
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 866 20
The subgrade should be protected from drying and kept moist
during construction It should be compacted to a minimum of
90 of maximum dry density
To protect against moisture damage of the flooring material
a polyethylene moisture barrier should be used
Where the slab is located below the outside ground surface a
subdrainage system around the perimeter of the walls will
help to prevent seepage water
Access Road
The proposed access road will be mostly in cut A portion
of the road will be situated at the top of the existing
near vertical mudrock slope facing Prospect Street Our
geologic reconnaissance of this slope indicates that the mud
rock exposed is hard and quite massive and the mudrock has
relatively flat bedding planes Based on the above observa
tions it is our opinion that this steep natural slope is
presently stable
Concrete pavement is planned for the road Based on the CBR
test results the following is the recommended pavement sec
tion
6 inch Concrete
6 inch Select Borrow
12 inch Total Pavement Thickness

FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface conditions were previously explored by
excavating seven 7 test pits with a backhoe to depths of
3 5 to 8 0 feet Four 4 continuous sampling probings were
made to depths of 1 0 to 2 5 feet and two 2 hand probes
were dug to depths of 5 feet The approximate locations of
the test pits and probes are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The soils were classified by visual and textural exami
nations in the field by our engineer who continuously moni
tored the excavation and drilling operations The classifi
cations were verified by visual inspection and testing in the
laboratory The soils were classified in accordance with the
Unified Soil Classification System
In the continuous sampling probings soil samples were
obtained by driving either a 2 4 inch I D ring sampler or
a standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer free
falling from a height of 30 inches The blow counts to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the Logs of Prob
ings at the appropriate sample depths
Disturbed samples were obtained from test pits and hand
probings
