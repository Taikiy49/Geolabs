SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration along the proposed roadway rehabilitation project generally 
encountered medium dense sands along the majority of the alignments extending to the 
maximum depth explored of approximately 5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  
Groundwater was encountered at depths of about 4 and 4.5 feet in the two borings drilled 
on Kamehameha Avenue towards Puunene Avenue at the time of our field exploration. 
Because the project will involve the reconstruction of the existing roadways, we 
have performed a detailed economic comparison involving both flexible and rigid 
pavement sections for the project in accordance with the latest guidelines provided by the 
State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing and 
Research Branch. 
Based on our field exploration program and the current design concept, we envision 
that the new pavements for the project will be constructed over the in-situ sandy soils in 
the shallow subsurface.  Based on the anticipated subgrade conditions, the traffic data 
provided for our pavement analyses, the economic analysis performed, and construction 
considerations, it is our opinion that new pavements for the roadway rehabilitation project 
may consist of (A) 3 inches of AC over 6 inches of ACB on 4 inches of ASB for 
Kamehameha Avenue extending from Puunene Avenue to Lono Avenue; (B) 3.5 inches of 
AC over 6 inches of ACB on 4 inches of ASB for Kamehameha Avenue to the west of 
Lono Avenue; and (C) 3 inches of AC over 4.5 inches of ACB on 4 inches of ASB for 
Hina Avenue.  It should be noted that CBR tests and/or field observations should be 
performed on the actual subgrade soils during construction to confirm that the above 
pavement design sections are adequate. 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface 
should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off 
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
pavement design recommendations.

Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering analyses performed for the Kamehameha Avenue and Hina Avenue 
Pavement Rehabilitation project in Kahului on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the design of new pavements only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The roadway rehabilitation project is along Kamehameha Avenue from 
Puunene Avenue to South Papa Avenue, and along Hina Avenue from Wakea Avenue 
to Niihau Street, and from Molokai Akau Street to Ani Street for a total distance of 
2.1 miles. The approximate limits of the roadway rehabilitation project are shown on the 
General Site Plan, Plate 2.

the existing pavements with a new pavement structural section.  We understand that the 
proposed construction will be a federally funded project. Therefore, a pavement

- Department of Transportation will be required. The new pavements for the project will 
be designed based on the methodology described in the Pavement Design Manual 
dated March 2002 prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, 
Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research Branch. 
Special attention was given to the potential presence of near-surface soft and/or 
loose soils, which may be a contributing factor to the pavement failures observed along 
portions of the roadways under consideration. The findings obtained from our field 
exploration and laboratory testing programs will be used as a basis to formulate

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene 
Epoch).  The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located between these 
two volcanoes.  The project site is situated on the isthmus connecting the two volcanic 
domes. 
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts 
of the two volcanoes.  During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fell to stands lower than 
present sea level exposing large areas of coralline sand on what was the ocean bottom.  
Wind action transported these coralline sands inland forming extensive areas of sand 
dunes.  In some areas, these dunes have been mantled by man-made fill which was 
apparently placed during the period that the area was used for the cultivation of sugar 
cane. 
2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The existing Kamehameha Avenue within the project limits is generally a 
four-lane undivided roadway between Puunene Avenue and Lono Avenue except for 
the additional turn lanes at the intersection areas.  The roadway transitions to a 
two-lane undivided roadway between Lono Avenue and South Papa Avenue.  The 
existing Hina Avenue within the project limits is generally a two-lane undivided roadway 
from Wakea Avenue to Niihau Street and from Molokai Akau Street to Ani Street.  The 
approximate limits of the two roadways are shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The shoulder areas beyond the edge of pavement generally consist of short cut 
grass, sidewalks at various locations, traffic signs and utility posts. The surface soils 
along the shoulder areas below the vegetation generally consist of brown sandy silts to 
tan coralline sands with silt. The existing Kamehameha Avenue roadway alignment 
generally slopes down from south to north, between the limits of South Papa Avenue 
and Lono Avenue, and slopes up from west to east as the alignment traverses from 
Lono Avenue to Puunene Avenue.

Our office conducted a site reconnaissance in January 2012 to collect general

2.2.1 Areas with Minor to Moderate Distresses and Severity 
Based on our site reconnaissance, the portion of Kamehameha Avenue extending 
from Puunene Avenue to Kaulawahine Street falls into this category.  The existing 
pavements within this 550-foot long roadway segment appear to be generally fair 
with minor signs of structural defects observed. Overall pavement deterioration is 
restricted to light alligator cracking of low to moderate severity. The ride 
characteristic of the pavement was generally fair.  In addition, the pavement 
condition at the intersection and approaches of Kamehameha and Hina Avenues 
were recently reconstructed by the County of Maui; therefore, the roadway within 
these limits was observed to be in good condition.  
2.2.2 Areas with Base/Subgrade Failures 
In general, the majority of Kamehameha Avenue and Hina Avenue, with the 
exception as noted above, falls under this category.  The existing asphaltic 
concrete pavements were observed to be in poor condition with moderately to 
highly distressed areas. Alligator and alligator block cracking were observed to be 
widespread and generally moderate to high in severity. The distressed pavement 
areas generally consist of elongated, severely distressed zones of alligator block 
cracking with depressions of 1 to 2 inches, rutting and some shoving of the 
pavement within the tire lanes. Potholes patched with AC material were observed at 
various locations along the alignments. The ride characteristic of the roadways was 
observed to be generally fair to irregular and bumpy in the areas of poor 
pavements.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling twelve borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 12, at selected locations along the roadway 
rehabilitation project extending to depths ranging from about 4 to 5.5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface. In addition, four bulk samples of the subgrade soils, 
designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-4, were collected for laboratory Resistance (R) Value 
tests.  The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plans, 
Plates 3.1 and 3.2. 
In general, our borings drilled for the roadway rehabilitation project encountered 
a pavement section consisting of approximately 2 to 5 inches of AC over medium dense 
sandy gravel base course materials.  Below the existing pavement section, the borings 
generally encountered medium dense poorly-graded sands and silty sands along the 
majority of the roadway alignments under consideration for this project.  In 
Boring No. 12, very stiff sandy silts overlying dense silty sands were encountered below 
the pavement section and extended to the maximum depth explored of approximately 
5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow drilled borings at the time of our 
field exploration except towards the intersection of Kamehameha Avenue and 
Puunene Avenue.  In Boring Nos. 1 and 2, groundwater was encountered at depths of 
about 4 and 4.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  These groundwater levels 
generally correspond to Elevation +2.5 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  It should be noted 
that water level may vary with seasonal rainfall, time of the year, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in the borings are presented 
on the Logs of Borings in Appendix A. Results of the laboratory tests performed on 
selected soil samples are presented in Appendix B.

3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
The pavement structural section presented herein was generally determined 
based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual 
dated March 2002. The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii 
- Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research 
Branch. The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer 
method developed and used by the California Department of Transportation (Caltrans). 
The design procedures for rigid pavements are based on the design procedures of the 
Portland Cement Association “Thickness Design for Concrete Highway and Street 
Pavements.”

A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to

Aggregate subbase materials required for the project should consist of crushed 
basaltic aggregates and should conform to Subsection 703.17 of the State of Hawaii, 
Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction (2005).  Aggregate subbase 
material should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers 
to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T-180 or 
ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D 2041. Asphaltic concrete 
(AC) material should be constructed in accordance with Section 401 Asphalt Concrete 
Pavement of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge 
Construction (2005) and its amendments. 
3.5 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry all surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage 
structures. Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. 
Development of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in 
the drainage structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement 
deterioration or premature failure of the pavements.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignments by drilling 
and sampling twelve borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 12. The borings 
extended to depths of about 4 to 5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 and 3.2. The borings 
were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-12. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits of the near-surface fine-grained soils. 
The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Five Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Four laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D 2844) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-3 through B-6.
