SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the site generally consist of 1 inch of asphaltic concrete 
underlain by fill consisting of medium dense to dense silty sand with varying amounts of 
cobbles and stiff to very stiff clayey silt extending to depths of about 2 to 5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface.  Talus deposits (medium dense to dense basaltic boulders and 
cobbles) were encountered below the fill in Boring Nos. 1 and 2 extending to depths of about 
30 and 9 feet below the existing pavement surface, respectively.  Residual soil, weathered 
clinker, and basalt rock interbedded with clinker were encountered below the talus deposits.  
In Boring No. 3, the fill was underlain by medium hard to very hard basalt rock interbedded 
with clinker extending to the maximum depth explored.  We encountered groundwater in the 
borings at depths ranging from about 17 to 21.1 feet below the existing pavement surface, 
which corresponds to about +3.9 to +8 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). 
We understand that three options are being considered to repair the damaged 
shoreline and to reduce the potential for erosion of the roadway and shoreline due to wave 
action of the ocean waters.  The three options being considered include the following: 
1. 
Rock rubblemound revetment with a berm toe 
2. 
Hybrid rock revetment and Concrete Rubble Masonry (CRM) gravity 
retaining wall 
3. 
Hybrid rock revetment and reinforced concrete wall with tie-backs 
The revetment generally consists of an armor layer with an underlayer and geotextile 
fabric below.  The armor layer consists of larger stone to protect the existing slope from wave 
action.  The underlayer and geotextile fabric placed below the armor layer reduces the 
potential for migration of the existing soil material through the voids in the larger stone of the 
armor layer.  The underlayer generally consists of smaller stone but provides sufficient void 
space to allow water to pass through.  To provide stability to the rubblemound revetment, a 
berm consisting of large stone (generally similar size to the armor stone) is placed at the toe 
of the revetment. 
Based on the existing topography at the site, we believe that the rock rubblemound 
revetment is a viable option for Area “C.”  The existing ground surface along the shoreline 
at Area “B” and the CRM Reach generally have very steep slope inclinations; therefore, 
constructing a revetment along the shoreline in these areas is likely not viable.  A rock 
revetment in conjunction with a retaining wall may be more feasible for Area “B” and the 
CRM Reach locations.  The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion 
and specific design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL

performed for the proposed Kalepa Point Shoreline Damage Repairs project in the 
Kipahulu area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and general site vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the proposed project. These recommendations are intended for the design of shoreline 
revetment, retaining walls, site preparation, and pavements only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report.  
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located on Piilani Highway at Kalepa Point, which is around 
Mile Post 38.2 in the Kipahulu area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii.  We understand that, 
in October 2014, Tropical Storm Iselle caused damage to the existing roadway and 
seawall around Mile Post 38.2.  The damages consist of eroded areas of the roadway 
edge, washed out portions of the existing wall facing, undermining of the roadway and 
metal guardrails at localized areas of the site.    
Based on the information provided, several options are being considered to extend 
the width of the roadway and reduce the potential of erosion of the road and shoreline 
from wave action from the ocean waters.  We believe that the subsurface conditions at 
the site may consist of shallow surface granular fills over boulders and/or rock formation.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui is the second largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago with 
an area of approximately 728 square miles. Two major volcanoes built the Island of Maui. 
The older volcano is West Maui (Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent volcano is 
East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene Epoch). The Isthmus of Maui is a 
narrow, gently sloping plain between these two volcanoes. 
The project site is on the southeastern flank of the Haleakala Shield Volcano in the 
District of Hana. Haleakala was generally built in three distinguishable series of lava flows. 
The lower unit is the Honomanu Volcanic Series (late Pliocene, early Pleistocene Epoch), 
which consists of thinly-bedded tholeiitic basaltic pahoehoe and a’a lava flows. These 
lavas tend to be very permeable and carry basal water. Overlying this unit is the Kula 
Volcanic Series (late Pleistocene Epoch), which is primarily composed of thicker alkalic 
a’a flows that may contain many inter-stratified, thin ash-soil layers. 
A long quiescent period with few eruptions followed.  During which time, canyons 
were carved in the volcano due to eustatic changes (higher and lower stands of the sea 
level). At the end of the resting period, copious flows erupted along the southwest and 
east rifts. This is the third and upper rock unit, known as the Hana Volcanic Series 
(Holocene Epoch). The lavas from this volcanic series range in composition from 
ultra-basic olivine porphyries to non-porphyritic, alkalic basalts.

3.1 
Rock Rubblemound Revetment 
The revetment generally consists of an armor layer with an underlayer and 
geotextile fabric below.  The armor layer consists of larger stone to protect the existing 
slope from wave action.  The underlayer and geotextile fabric placed below the armor 
layer reduces the potential for migration of the existing soil material through the voids in 
the larger stone of the armor layer.  The underlayer generally consists of smaller stone 
but provides sufficient void space to allow water to pass through.  To provide stability to 
the rubblemound revetment, a berm consisting of large stone (generally similar size to 
the armor stone) is placed at the toe of the revetment.

Based on the existing topography at the site, we believe that the rock rubblemound

3.2 
Concrete Rubble Masonry (CRM) Retaining Wall 
In areas with a steep slope inclination, such as Area “B” and the CRM Reach, the 
rock revetment in conjunction with a CRM gravity retaining wall may be a viable option.  
The CRM gravity retaining wall will need to be designed to resist the static lateral earth 
pressures acting on the wall.  Since the wall will be located along a sloping shoreline that 
is subjected to wave action, resistance from passive earth pressures should be neglected.  
This would require a larger and heavier CRM wall to resist lateral earth pressures acting 
on the wall.  The following general guidelines may be used for design of the CRM gravity 
retaining wall. 
 Retaining Wall Foundations 
For design of the gravity retaining wall foundation, an allowable bearing pressure of 
up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design of shallow 
foundations bearing on the boulders/cobbles (talus deposits or stone underlayer) or 
basalt rock formation. This bearing value is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and 
may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind 
or seismic forces.   
It should be noted that the stone underlayer below the revetment would likely move 
over time due to erosion from wave action.  If the wall bears on the stone underlayer, 
movement of the stone underlayer could result in structural distress to the wall.  To 
reduce potential for structural distress to the wall from movement of the stone 
underlayer, we recommend placing a minimum 2-foot thick layer of reinforced 
concrete below the full width of the wall to evenly distribute the load over the stone

underlayer and to act as a “bridging” layer if movement of the stone underlayer 
occurs. 
Generally, wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches. In addition, wall 
footings should be embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest 
adjacent finished grade of the new revetment.  
Lateral loads may be resisted by friction developed between the bottom of the 
foundation and the bearing soil. A coefficient of friction of 0.5 may be used for footings 
bearing on the 2-foot thick layer of reinforced concrete. Resistance due to passive 
earth pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation should be 
neglected since the wall is located in an area that is prone to erosion by wave action. 
 Static Lateral Earth Pressures 
For design of the retaining wall, we recommend using equivalent fluid pressures of 
34 pcf and 53 pcf for the active and at-rest conditions, respectively. Surcharge and 
hydrostatic pressures are not accounted for in the above values. Surcharge stresses 
due to areal surcharges, live loads, and point loads should be considered in the wall 
design. 
For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall, a rectangular 
distribution with uniform pressure equal to 28 and 44 percent of the vertical surcharge 
pressure acting on the entire height of the wall may be used for the active and at-rest 
conditions, respectively. Additional analyses during design may be needed to 
evaluate the surcharge effects of point loads and line loads. 
 Dynamic Lateral Earth Pressures 
Dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading will need to be considered in the 
design of the retaining wall. Forces caused by dynamic lateral earth pressures due 
to seismic loading (amax= 0.26g) within the upper soil layer above the basalt rock 
formation may be estimated using 5.0H2 pounds per linear foot of wall length for level 
backfill conditions, where H is the height of the wall within the upper soil layer in feet. 
The resultant force should be assumed to act through the mid-height of the wall within

 Drainage 
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures. A typical drainage system for the retaining walls should consist of about 
1 cubic foot of permeable material, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 
gradation), wrapped with non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) at each 
of the weep hole locations. The weep holes should be spaced no more than 6 feet 
apart. 
Alternatively, a 1 to 2-foot wide strip of geocomposite drainage material extending 
the full height of the retaining wall may be used. The geocomposite drainage material 
should be connected hydraulically to a weep hole or drainage outlet. Unless covered 
by concrete, the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of impervious material to 
reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration behind the walls. 
3.3 
Reinforced Concrete Wall with Tie-Backs 
As mentioned previously, resistance from passive earth pressures should be 
neglected for the design of the retaining wall since the wall will be located along a sloping 
shoreline that is subjected to wave action.  A larger and heavier gravity retaining wall may 
be required to resist the lateral earth pressures.  However, it may not be feasible to use 
a larger and heavier gravity retaining wall; therefore, a reinforced concrete wall with a 
permanent tieback anchor system may be used in lieu of a gravity retaining wall to provide 
the lateral support.

In general, the permanent tieback anchor system is constructed by drilling a 
borehole, installing a reinforcing bar into the borehole, and grouting the borehole.  The 
basic components of a grouted permanent tieback anchor consist of the anchorage and 
the bonded and unbonded lengths of the reinforcing bar. In general, the unbonded length 
of the tieback anchor is the portion of the reinforcing bar that does not have direct contact 
with the grout, allowing the reinforcing bar to elongate elastically and transfer the resisting 
force from the structure to the bonded length.  The bonded length of the tieback anchor 
is the portion of the reinforcing bar that is directly bonded to the grout allowing it to transfer 
the applied load into the ground.  Based on the depths of the boulders and cobbles 
encountered in the borings, we recommend that the unbonded length of the tieback 
anchor extend at least 25 feet behind the new concrete retaining wall so that the bonded 
length extends beyond the boulder and cobble layer and into the basalt rock formation.  
Due to the presence of boulders and cobbles at the site, there is a high potential for caving 
in of the borehole in the boulder and cobble layer.  To reduce the potential for caving in 
of the borehole, we recommend using a permanent steel casing for the unbonded length 
of the tieback anchor. The permanent steel casing would also reduce the potential grout 
loss that could occur in the boulder and cobble layer if not cased.   
We envision that the permanent tieback anchors would derive support principally 
from the bond between the grout and the basalt rock anticipated at the project site.  The 
bond stress, which is the pullout resistance per unit area of the grout/soil interface contact, 
is variable depending on the type of soil/rock and grout, the overburden stress, and the 
construction procedures used in installing the permanent tieback anchors.  An average 
bond stress of 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the basalt rock.  The 
bond stress used in the design will need to be confirmed during construction by load 
testing.   
In general, we believe the reinforced concrete retaining wall foundations may be 
designed in accordance with the recommendations and parameters presented in the 
"Concrete Rubble Masonry (CRM) Retaining Wall" section herein. 
The permanent tieback anchor system will be designed to provide resistance to 
the lateral earth pressures and lateral loads imposed from the traffic loading on the

3.4 
Site Preparation 
We believe that only minor site grading work will be required to achieve the finished 
grades at the site.  In general, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed at the on-set of earthwork.  Vegetation, debris, 
deleterious materials, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of 
properly off-site.  Soft, loose, and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas 
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over-excavated to expose firm 
or dense material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted 
fill.  The excavated soft, loose, and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site.  
In addition, contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for 
over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variation in the 
over-excavation quantities. 
Walls and foundations of the existing structures and pavements to be demolished 
should be completely removed. Over-excavations resulting from the demolition operations 
should be backfilled with compacted granular fill material. Existing underground utilities to 
be abandoned should be removed, and the resulting excavation should be properly 
backfilled with the excavated on-site materials. The on-site materials should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in 6 to 8-inch level 
loose lifts, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Utilities to be 
abandoned in-place under the proposed addition should be backfilled by pumping lean 
concrete or CLSM (Controlled Low Strength Material) under low pressure. 
After clearing, grubbing and demolition, areas to receive fill and finished subgrades 
in cut areas should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture conditioned to at 
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction.  The compaction requirement for the finished subgrades 
in areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in

Scarification would not be possible in subgrades consisting mainly of boulders or 
basalt rock.  Therefore, subgrades consisting mainly of boulders or basalt rock should be 
proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton (static weight) vibratory drum roller for a minimum of 
eight passes to provide a firm and unyielding surface and to help detect and collapse 
near-surface cavities and/or voids.  A Geolabs representative should observe the proof-
rolling operations. Yielding areas, loose areas, or cavities disclosed during proof-rolling 
operations should be over-excavated and backfilled with compacted fill materials.  
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft and/or loose areas 
and replacement with well-compacted fill.  A Geolabs representative should evaluate the 
need for over-excavation in the field. 
In general, the excavated on-site soils may be re-used as a source of general fill 
or backfill materials, provided that they are free of deleterious materials (vegetation) and 
are screened of particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension.  Imported fill 
materials needed for site filling should consist of non-expansive, select granular materials, 
such as crushed coral or basalt.  The materials should be well graded from coarse to fine 
with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve.  The materials should have a 
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or 
less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.   
Fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, 
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  In general, compaction 
should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other similar types of 
acceptable compaction equipment.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to depths of about 35 to 
37.8 feet below the existing pavement surface. The borings were drilled with a truck-
mounted drill rig equipped with augers and rotary coring tools. The borings were drilled at 
the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing 
in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard 
Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification 
System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil 
classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log 
Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on 
the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01 (2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock 
Log Legend, Plate A-0.3.   
The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods for 
the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs 
of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality 
of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and 
Minerals."

~25 blows per inch with bounce.
Example: Porous rock such as clinker, cinder, and coral reef
Can be indented by one hammer blow. Can be scraped or peeled by knife. SPT can penetrate by
~100 blows per foot.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentations of the test results are 
provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B-2. 
Seven Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were 
performed on selected core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of 
the boulders and basalt rock encountered. Uniaxial compressive strength test results are 
presented on Plate B-3. 
One laboratory soaked California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D1883) was performed 
on a bulk soil sample to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils. The test 
results are presented on Plate B-4.
