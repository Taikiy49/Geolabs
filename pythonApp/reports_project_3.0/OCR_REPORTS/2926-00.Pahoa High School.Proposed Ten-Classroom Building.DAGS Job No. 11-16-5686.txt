INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed new ten classroom building at Pahoa High School in the town
of Pahoa on the island of Hawaii The general location of the project site and its vicinity
is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2926 00 Page 2
This report summarizes the findings resulting from our field exploration and
laboratory testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived
from our analyses based on the data obtained through the field exploration and laboratory
testing
These recommendations are intended for design input only and may not be
appropriate to form the basis for construction cost estimates or for construction bidding
purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a new ten classroom building for the Pahoa High School
in Pahoa on the island of Hawaii
Based on the information provided to us a three story classroom building is
proposed for the project The new building will measure approximately one hundred and
fifty five feet 155 by sixty five feet 65 in overall plan dimensions and will accommodate
ten 10 classrooms a teachers workroom and toilet facilities
Based on the information provided to us the estimated column and wall loads will
be approximately two hundred seventy 270 kips and fifteen 15 kips per foot
respectively It is our understanding that concrete columns with CMU walls and concrete
floor and steel ceiling construction will be utilized for the proposed building
It is our understanding that a 51 stall parking lot and an on site sewage disposal
system are also being included in the proposed project

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface
conditions at the project site in order to develop a generalized subsurface data set for the
formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to foundation site
grading and pavement design of the project Information regarding the infiltration
characteristics of the material at the site of the proposed leaching field was also obtained
The work was done in accordance with our proposal dated March 19 1992 to the
Department of Accounting and General Services Division of Public Works
The scope of work consisted of the following
1 Review of available soils and geologic information pertinent to the project
site and its vicinity
2 Recoraissance of the project site and its vicinity to make visual
observations of the surface conditions
3 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drilling rig and operators
to and from the project site
4 Drilling and sampling eight 8 borings to depths ranging from 9 to 26 feet
below the existing ground surface
5 Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the drilled borings by a
geologist of our firm
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2926 00 Page 4
6 Performance of five 5 in situ percolation tests for evaluation of the
infiltration characteristics of the subsurface materials
7 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field exploration
to determine the classification and engineering properties of the materials
encountered
8 Engineering analyses of the field and laboratory data for the purpose of
developing geotechnical engineering design recommendations
9 Preparation of this report summarizing our work in the project and
presenting our findings and geotechnical engineering design recommen
dations
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration and laboratory testing are presented
in the appendices of this report
SITE DESCRIPTION
The proposed project site is located between the existing Administration Building
F the existing Agricultural Building G and Kalapana Road in the Pahoa High School
campus
The building site is open grassy land presently occupied by a girls restroom and
a small building approximately thirty feet 30 by thirty feet 30 in plan dimension with
an access concrete ramp Portion of the future parking lot area is currently occupied by
an old Auto Shop Building H The site and its development are shown on the Site Plan

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling eight 8 borings to
depths ranging between 9 and 26 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our exploration encountered a surficial layer of silty sand sandy gravel
and clayey silt materials with various consistency This was followed by a competent
deposit of sandy gravel saprolite material down to approximately 6 to 17 feet below the
existing ground surface Hard weathered basalt formation was then encountered down
to 26 feet below the existing ground surface the maximum depth drilled
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered are presented on the Boring
Logs Plates A 1 through A 8 Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples
obtained The test results are presented in Appendix B

Site Grading
The currently proposed finish floor elevation for the classroom building is at
elevation 675 feet above MSL within approximately 2 feet of the existing ground surface
Minimal cuts and fills are anticipated to be required for the proposed construction
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
All areas to receive slabs and pavements should after clearing and grubbing be
scarified to a minimum depth of six inches 6 moisture conditioned and recompacted
to a minimum of 90 percent of its maximum dry density as determined in accordance with
procedures described in ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 Any loose or soft spots
encountered should be removed and replaced with compacted non expansive select
material Our field exploration indicated that soft clayey silt material were encountered
near the existing ground surface in some of the drilled borings It is recommended that
these soft clayey silt material within the building areas should be over excavated down to
a minimum two feet 2 below the subgrade elevation The material resulting from over
excavation should be removed and properly disposed of off site
Imported fill material to replace the over excavated material and raise the ground
surface to the proposed finish subgrade should consist of non expansive select granular
material less than three inches 3 in maximum size with less than 15 percent passing the
No 200 sieve The select granular material should be placed in compacted level lifts not
exceeding ten inches 10 in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent

The site grading should be designed to divert surface water away from the building
foundations
Foundations
According to the information provided to us by the project structural engineer
structural loads on the order of 270 kips per column and 15 kips per foot of bearing wall
are anticipated
In consideration of the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the
site we believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used to support
the proposed building
An allowable bearing capacity of up to 3 000 p s f may be utilized for the design
of footing foundations bearing on the recompacted in situ sandy gravel material
encountered at shallow depths This bearing value is for total dead plus live loads only
and may be increased by up to one third 1 3 for loads of short duration such as those
caused by wind or seismic loads
The bottoms of the footing excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of
95 percent of the maximum dry density as determined in accordance with procedures
described in ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 If soft loose pockets are encountered at the
bottom of the footing excavation they should be over excavated to competent in situ
material and replaced with select granular material compacted to a minimum of 95
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 2926 00 Page 8
percent of its maximum dry density as determined in accordance with procedures
described in ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
The footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grade
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines
Footing settlements under the anticipated design loadings are estimated to be less
than one inch total and one half inch differential
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
developed against the buried vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 p c f per foot of depth This assumes that the soil around the foundation
is well compacted A coefficient of friction of 0 85 may be used Unless covered by
pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 18 inches of soil should be
neglected
Footing excavations should be observed by the project geotechnical engineer prior
to placement of reir forcing steel or concrete to allow verification of the bearing material
and the embedment depths

Concrete slab on grade construction are anticipated for the ground floor of the new
ten classroom building

following siab section may be used for preliminary design purposes

In order to protect against future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to
the floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier such as a plastic membrane on top
of the basaltic termite barrier layer is recommended
In accordance with D A G S Technical Memorandum No 61 dated
the above six inch 6 thick basaltic barrier would replace the cushion fill normally used
beneath the moisture barrier Two inch 2 layer of BTB should be provided over the
moisture barrier to help concrete curing
The slab thickened edges adjacent to any unpaved area should be embedded at
least twelve inches 12 below the lowest adjacent grade

Sewage Disposal System
A septic tank system with a leaching field is also planned as part of the proposed
project for the disposal of the sanitary waste generated by the facility Currently the
proposed sewage disposal system is located at the northern end of the project site
Four 4 field percolation tests at various depths were performed at two locations
Boring Nos P 1 and P 2 in the area of the proposed leaching field The test data are
presented in Appendix C
Based on our exploration and percolation test results it appears that the rate of
percolation at both locations is relatively high It is our opinion that a shallow leachfield
design is feasible for the areas in the vicinity of Boring Nos P 1 and P 2 A maximum
rate of sewage application of four 4 gallons per square feet per day may be used for the
design of the proposed leaching field
However due to the variability of the percolation characteristics of the subsurface
materials the absorption capacity of the disposal system should be further verified by
additional field percolation tests performed during construction
Subsurface Runoff Disposal
it is our understanding that dry wells are required for surface runoff disposal at the
project area At the time of our field exploration the locations of the proposed dry wells
had not been determined
Our field exploration disclosed that the site is underlain by sandy gravel over
weathered basalt formation Based on our field percolation test performed in Boring B 6

he measured coefficient of permeability is on the order of 1 5 x It is our
opinion that the in situ materials are considered to be sufficiently permeable to accept the

it is recommended that the dry wells should be at least 20 feet in depth below the
existing ground surface The actual size of the subsurface disposal system will depend
on the design quantity of the future surface runoff The details of the field percolation test

It should also be pointed out that loss of infiltration capacity due to saturation and
soil clogging by combined physical chemical and biological factors may occur in the
future The latter is the most important factor since an organic mat of several millimeters
thick may form at the soil liquid interface Other factors such as rainfall etc may also
affect the permeability to some extent Therefore the leaching field and the subsurface
runoff disposal systems will probably require periodic maintenance
All abandoned cesspools and drywells should be properly backfilled with non
expansive granular material and adequately compacted to avoid future ground
subsidence

Utility Trench
New utility lines may be required for the project A granular bedding consisting of
six inches 6 of select borrow or ASTM C 33 No 67 is recommended under the
pipes
Granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel should be used for the trench backfill
up to about one foot above the pipes to reduce the possibility of damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the subsequent trench backfill from the level one foot above
the pipes to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular
material and placed in maximum ten inch 10 horizontal loose lifts mechanically
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of the maximum dry density as determined in
accordance with procedures described in ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence
Drainage
The finished grade outside the building should be shaped to shed water away from
foundations and to avoid ponding conditions Also it is advised that gutter systems be
installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation
and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs
should also be avoided
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2926 00 Page 13
Pianters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to prevent water from entering the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause excessive subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled tight against the walls or slab footings immediately after
setting of the concrete to prevent ponding conditions
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain
all surface run off away from the slab and footing foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of
eight 8 borings to depths ranging between 9 to 26 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Pian Plate 2 The borings were
drilled using truck mounted equipment
The material encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who continuously monitored the drilling
operations These classifications were further reviewed by visual inspection and testing
in the laboratory Al soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered are presented
on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 8
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer free falling 30
inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the
Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D
2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of tie bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 and would be shown on the Boring Logs as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
