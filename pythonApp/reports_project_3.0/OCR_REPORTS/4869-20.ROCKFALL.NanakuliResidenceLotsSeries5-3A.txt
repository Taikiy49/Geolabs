SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Geolabs conducted an evaluation of the natural hillside located upslope of the
proposed Nanakuli Residence Lots Series 5 3A project site in an effort to study the
potential for rockfall hazards at the project site Based on our geological reconnaissance
and rockfall hazard analyses we believe that there are potential rockfall hazards present
at the project site that are in need of mitigation for the development of the new residential
lots Based on the results of our rockfall hazard analysis of the existing hillside about
22 percent of the total boulders may roll past the top of existing cut slopes along the
mauka side of the proposed lots in the event of a rockfall emanating from the hillside Our
analyses indicate that the majority of the boulders passing the analysis point likely will
consist of boulders of 3 to 4 feet in diameter with some contributions from boulders of
about 4 to 5 feet in diameter Boulders less than 3 feet in diameter generally do not
contribute to the rockfall hazards at the project site due to the relatively gentle terrain
In order to reduce the potential for future rockfall hazards to the new development
at the bottom of the hillside we recommend that a rockfall catchment area be provided
near the bottom of the existing hillside along the top of the existing cut slope at the project
development site Our rockfall hazard analyses using the computer program CRSP
Version 4 0 indicate that a statistical confidence of over 95 percent probability of reducing
the potential for rockfall hazard from affecting the residential lots may be achieved with the
implementation of a boulder catchment area The boulder catchment area generally
consists of a level catchment zone of about 10 feet wide for maintenance considerations
followed by a fill embankment of about 6 feet in height to contain the potential rockfall
debris
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific
recommendations for the design of the catchment area

1 1 Introduction
This report presents the findings and recommendations derived from our
evaluation of the potential for rockfall hazards in the vicinity of the proposed
Nanakuli Residence Lots Series 5 3A project located in the Nanakuli area on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii This report summarizes the findings from our geological
reconnaissance and engineering analyses of the field information and presents our
recommendations for reducing the potential for rockfall hazards at the project site The
findings and recommendations are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

The project site is located within the existing Nanakuli Residence Lots
Series 5 3A project in the Nanakuli area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii We
understand that the Nanakuli Residence Lots project site was developed originally in the
early 1990s Of the 52 residential lots that were mass graded previously we
understand that construction of dwellings on 16 lots was deferred in order to allow the

hillside area above the 16 residential lots Therefore it is desired to perform a
geological reconnaissance of the hillside to evaluate the existing slope conditions with
respect to the potential for rockfall hazards at the project site and to provide for some
mitigation of the situation to reduce the potential hazard associated with rockfall at the
project site if appropriate

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middie to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae
Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and the eastern flank of
Waianae Volcano was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern
flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the
Schofield Plateau
The Waianae Mountain Range Waianae Volcano is composed of layered
basaltic lava flows and pyroclastic materials which are grouped and classified as the
Waianae Volcanic Series The Waianae Volcanic Series is divided into the lower
middle and upper volcanic members The lower member comprises of lava flows and
associated pyroclastic rocks that built the main mass of the Waianae Shield Volcano
The middle member consists of rock that accumulated and gradually filled the vast
volcanic caldera The upper member is a relatively thin capping layer that covered the
entire top of the shield volcano late in its history of evolution
Once the Waianae Shield Volcano formed a long period of deep erosion
sedimentation and subsidence of the Island of Oahu occurred which produced the
large valleys of the western side of the shield volcano These erosional valleys
gradually were filled with enormous accumulations of alluvium and colluvium deposited
as a combined result of stream erosion base level rise sea level rise and subsidence
of the island mass Over time and as a result of sea level fluctuations some of the
older alluvial and colluvial deposits were buried and through consolidation and
cementation formed relatively dense deposits However the current surface soil
deposits above these older deposits typically are unconsolidated

2 4 Recent Rockfall Activity
The present day rockfall events typically involve individual boulders and localized
erosion of weathered outcrops composed of in situ rock formation materials The rock
materials are loosened by chemical and or physical decomposition occurring along
existing rock formation fractures and discontinuities The loose rocks then begin
moving via the influence of a triggering mechanism rockfall initiation and roll or bounce
under the influence of gravity until the potential energy has been expended Rockfalls
commonly are triggered by surface water runoff high winds or the levering action of tall
vegetation imparting stresses on the root system The present rockfall events
commonly are associated with ongoing landform erosion and mass transfer processes
that continue to very slowly shape the valley topography Therefore it is anticipated
that rockfall may continue to occur though not with the close frequency and the scale of
the pre historic rockfall events which likely contributed to the majority of the existing
talus deposits
By observing the general pattern of boulder distribution and character dimension
and shape of the surface boulder deposits at the project site pertinent information was
obtained that was used to assist in the modeling of future rockfall activity at the project
site In general the initiation of rockfall movement is not anticipated to occur on slopes
flatter than about 3H 1V because of the inertial resistance to movement imparted by the
gentle grades

Catchment Area Dimensions
In general we recommend that the base of the catchment area be at least 10 feet
wide horizontally In order to contain the potential rockfall debris we recommend
that a fill embankment of at least 6 feet in height with a 5 foot crest width be
provided
The front face of the fill embankment side facing upslope should have a slope
inclination of 1H 1V In order to reduce the need to design for impact forces acting
on the fill embankment from the potential boulders entering the catchment area a
steeper slope inclination should not be used along the front face of the fill
embankment A slope inclination flatter than 1H 1V is not desirable for the front
face of the fill embankment because the flatter slope would allow the rockfall
boulder to roll past the catchment area In order to reduce the size of the fill
embankment the back face of the embankment also should have a slope

Geotextile Fabric Reinforcement
Due to the relatively steep fill slope inclinations 1H 1V we recommend that the fill
embankment be reinforced with a woven geotextile fabric The woven geotextile
fabric would reinforce the backfill zone and to improve the shear strength of the
reinforced soil zone due to the relatively steep fill slope inclination This composite
system essentially forms a stabilized structure with an ability to stand at relatively
steep slope inclinations In addition geotextile fabric reinforcements are desirable
due to the flexibility ease of construction high load carrying capacity and
economy
In general the top layer of the geotextile fabric should be placed about 1 foot below
the crest of the fill embankment Subsequent layers of the geotextile fabric should
be spaced at a maximum of 2 feet vertically The woven geotextile fabric should
consist of Mirafi GOOX or equivalent

Embankment Fill Materials
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris deleterious materials and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to
reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
After clearing and grubbing finished subgrades in cut areas and areas designated
to receive fills should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches where possible
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
The excavated on site materials may be re used as a source of general fill
materials provided that the materials are free of vegetation deleterious materials
clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension
Please note that there is a substantial amount of cobbles and boulders embedded
in the soil matrix at the project site Because of the appreciable amount of cobbles
and boulders embedded in the soil matrix at the project site we recommend that
excavation into the existing ground to generate fill materials be limited We
envision that the majority of the new embankment fill materials would need to be
constructed with imported granular fill materials with a maximum particle size of
3 inches

Imported fill materials for the construction of the steepened fill embankment should
consist of non expansive select granular material such as crushed coralline or
basaltic materials The materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with no
particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The materials should have
a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of
1 percent or less Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance with
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use
Fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to
at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to
the maximum dry density Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot
rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Due to the sloping condition of the existing ground surface the new fill materials
should be keyed and benched into the existing ground surface prior to and during
placement of the fill materials
