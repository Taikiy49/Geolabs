INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Stockpile Movement Facilities Modifications Project in
Johnston Island Atoll The general location of the site is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1 The exploration was performed in general accordance with
our revised fee proposal dated March 19 1990
This report summarizes the findings from the exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses based on the
data obtained during the exploration These recommendations are intended for
design input considerations and are not intended for construction bidding estimation
purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed Stockpile Movement Facilities Modification project is situated
within the Red Hat area of Johnston island and involves three 3 general locations
within the Red Hat area The area is currently used for the storage of chemical
agents in concrete igloos The sites are at relatively low elevations with nearly flat
topography and poor drainage
lt is proposed to prepare the three 3 locations for the storage of 20 ton
containers Details of the proposed site grading were not available for the preparation
of this report However based on observations of the site conditions and discussions
with design team personnel it is anticipated that the site preparation will involve
placement of fills to raise the existing grade about one 1 foot to improve the
drainage and thereby reduce the potential of flooding and water damage to the
containers

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated within the Red Hat area in the southern part of
Johnston Island The Red Hat area is approximately 600 by 1050 feet in plan
dimension and is currently used for storing chemical materials in concrete igloos
The topography of the site is relatively flat Existing ground surface elevations
range from about seven 7 to about 9 feet above the Mean Lower Low Water
datum MLLW

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The field exploration disclosed that the site is underlain by dredged fill
consisting of coral sand and gravel It appears that this fill was placed by hydraulic
filling since the upper portion of the fill has densified to form a medium dense to
dense surface crust which grades to loose with depth particularly near the
groundwater level
The existing fill crust if properly proof rolled and densified should provide
adequate bearing support for the proposed site improvements
The coral fill material available on Johnston Island if placed and compacted
in accordance with the recommendations given herein should provide adequate
support for the proposed container handling and storage facilities
Site Grading
In general it is anticipated that the proposed site grading will consist of the
placement of about one 1 foot of new fill to raise the existing grade to improve
GEOLABS HAWAII
Lo
W O 2435 00 Page 6
surface drainage and to reduce the potential of flooding and water damage to the
containers which are to be stored on the ground surface
It is anticipated that the fill material required for the proposed site grading will
be locally available dredged coral materials Based on our laboratory testing which
indicates high CBR California Bearing Ratio ASTM Test Method D 1883 test values
and previous experience with similar coral materials it is our opinion that these
dredged coral sands and gravels are a good source of fill material
Previous experience with coral fill indicates that due to the calcareous nature
of the material there is generally some cementation of the coral if the fill is placed and
compacted at moisture contents slightly above the optimum moisture content This
cementation effect generally increases the bearing strength of the fill
Therefore it is our opinion that the coral fill material available on Johnston
Island if placed and compacted in accordance with the recommendations given
herein should provide adequate support for the proposed container handling and
storage facilities
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should
be cleared of vegetation debris rubbish and other deleterious materials
These materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
Fill areas should be scarified to a depth of six 6 inches moisture conditioned
to about 3 to 5 percent above optimum moisture content and recompacted to
95 of its maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 Any
GEOLABS HAWAII

loose pumping yielding or soft spots encountered during this
proof roiled recompaction operation should be over excavated to firm ground
and then replaced with compacted select granular material such as the coral
material available on Johnston Island
Fill Placement
All fills should be placed in maximum ten 10 inch thick loose level lifts and
compacted to a minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density per ASTM Test
Method D 1557 78
Due to the calcareous nature of the fill material it is anticipated that some
cementation will form within the compacted fill if the material is at the proper
moisture content when compacted We recommend that the moisture content
of the fill material be 3 to 5 percent on the wet side of optimum moisture
content at the time of compaction to facilitate the cementation effect
Due to the granular nature of the on site soils and the available fill materials
the use of vibratory compaction equipment which is highly suited to this type
of material is recommended
It is Our anticipation that the ground settlements induced by the placement of
one 1 foot of new fill will be minimal

Existing Utility Lines
lt is our understanding that there are existing buried utility lines below the site
and that many of these lines are asbestos cement pipe with soil covers ranging from
unknown to about four 4 feet above the top of pipe
Asbestos cement pipes are relatively brittle and may be damaged by excessive
stresses imposed by earth pressures and or traffic loads
For our analyses of traffic loads we have used the Rough Terrain Container
Handler with a 40 foot top handler attachment as the design vehicle Based on the
available information on this vehicle we have assumed a maximum axle load of 135
kips with an internal tire pressure of 70 p s i in our analyses
Based on these preliminary analyses it appears that for existing
asbestos cement utility lines having a minimum soil cover thickness of two 2 feet
the loads imposed on the pipes by the vehicles would generally be tolerable
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2435 00 Page 9
It is recommended that the actual thickness of the soil cover over the existing
utilities should be verified in the field i e the pipes should be exposed and the actual
depth to their tops should be verified
For existing utility lines with soil compacted coral sand and gravel covers less
than two 2 feet thick it is recommended that the trenches be covered with either
steel plates or concrete aprons to help distribute the vehicle loads and reduce the
potential of damage to the lines We feel that standard road plates may be used
The steel plates should be of minimum one half 1 2 of an inch thick and should be
wide enough to extend a minimum 18 inches each side beyond the estimated trench
width The concrete apron should be adequately designed per structural engineering
requirements
RCP Drain Lines
New drainlines are planned as part of the site drainage improvements Based
on the information provided to us reinforced concrete pipes RCP drain lines of 18
24 and 30 inch diameters will be installed RCP s of Class Ill Wall C per ASTM C
76 are being considered
For RCP installation a granular bedding consisting of minimum six 6 inches
of ASTM C 33 No 67B gravel 3B Fine should be provided beneath the pipes If
8B Fine is not available the bedding should consist of a clean coral gravel between
3 8 and 3 4 of an inch in size
f
Granular materials such as dredge coral sand and gravel material less than
2 inch in size should be used for trench backfill The backfill up to about one 1 foot
above the pipes should first be water tamped and let drained and then followed by
light tamping to reduce the possibility of damaging the pipes

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level one foot above the pipes
to finished grade should be placed in maximum 10 inch horizontal loose lifts
mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 maximum dry density per ASTM Test
Method D 1557 78 to protect against potential future ground subsidence The upper
six 6 inches of backfill below finished subgrade level should be compacted to a
minimum of 95 maximum dry density
Based on our preliminary analyses it appears that the additional loads
imposed by the design vehicle exceed the allowable supporting strength of Class III
RCP with two 2 feet of compacted granular soil cover
It is recommended that the designer adopt an appropriate factor of safety for
the overall design Concrete jacketing should be used to protect the pipes from
damage under the anticipated traffic loads
Concrete Jacketing
Use of a reinforced concrete jacket as part of the RCP installation is
recommended to help distribute the heavy axle loads from the design vehicle when
the soil cover is thin The concrete jacket should be properly designed to resist the
induced shear and moment loads from the vehicle The trench bottom should be
compacted to 90 maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 prior to
pipe and jacket installation
One analytical approach is to model the jacket as a beam on an elastic
foundation The idealized model assumes that the deflection of the foundation
supporting medium may be correlated to the stress applied by a coefficient k the
modulus of subgrade reaction For the design of the reinforced concrete jacket a
value of 140 k c f may be used for the modulus of subgrade reaction

Miscellaneous Structures
It is our understanding that relatively light miscellaneous service structures
may be needed for the project It is our opinion that the upper coral sand and gravel
crust may provide adequate foundation support for these structures if properly
recompacted
An allowable bearing value of up to 3500 p s f may be used for foundation
design We believe a factor of safety of 2 5 against ultimate bearing failure is
obtained when the coral material is properly compacted A minimum footing
embedment of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finish grade is recommended
The bearing value may be increased by one third when temporary wind and seismic
loads are considered
The bottom of footing excavations should be scarified to a minimum depth of
six 6 inches moisture conditioned to 3 to 5 percent above optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 95 maximum dry density per ASTM Test
Method D 1557 78
Estimated settlements are on the order of less than one half 1 2 of an inch
total and a quarter 1 4 of an inch differential provided that the fills and individual
footing bottoms are prepared in accordance with the recommendations given herein
Lateral Earth Pressures
Lateral loads acting on structures may be resisted by passive earth pressures
developed against the buried vertical faces of the foundation system and by friction
resistance between the bottom of the tanks foundations and the bearing soil

friction factor of 0 4 may also be used
Light Pole Foundations
It is our understanding that high light poles may be constructed as part of the
project It is our recommendation that drilled pier foundations be used to support the
light poles
Since the piers would be subject to repeated wind loads a critical
consideration would be to limit permanent lateral deflection of the piers This may be
achieved by sufficient embedment of the piers
The vertical compressive load on the poles is relatively light and may be
supported partially by frictional and partially by end bearing resistance between the
piers and the subsoils
It is our opinion that for a 36 inch diameter drilled pier embedded a minimum
of five 5 feet below the existing ground surface into the on site fill material would
provide adequate support for the proposed light poles

Drilled Pier Construction
The drilled pier construction should be closely monitored by the geotechnical
engineer in order to see that adequate foundation support is achieved Since a
portion of the loading capacities come from the frictional resistance between the shaft
and the in situ soils extra care should be applied to the drilling of the pier hole
selection of a non shrink concrete mix design and placement of the concrete
Prompt placement of concrete in the prepared holes i e as soon as they are
completed is recommended to reduce caving in of the hole The concrete mix
should be designed to minimize shrinkage in order to maintain high frictional contact
between the in situ soils and pier shafts The concrete should be placed in a suitable
manner to avoid segregation of the aggregates

Plans and specifications for the proposed construction should be forwarded
