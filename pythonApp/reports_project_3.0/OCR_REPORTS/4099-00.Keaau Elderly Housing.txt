for the proposed Keaau Elderly Housing project in the Keaau area within the District of Puna
on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations based on our
review and analyses of the site conditions the grading plan and the subsurface information
obtained for this project These geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design
of foundations site grading retaining walls and pavements only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is located on the eastern flank of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano
which has been built up by basaltic lava flows These flows are of the Pleistocene Epoch
Ice Age and of the Recent Age and belong to the Ka u Volcanic Series The lava flows
were subsequently covered by a surface layer of palagonitized ash locally referred to as
Pahala Ash The volcanic ash soil at the project site differs from the Pahala Ash found in
other locations on the Island of Hawaii in that the in situ moisture content is generally lower
The lava formation at the site appears to be of pahoehoe flow which is characterized
bya smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava Many of these cavities were formed
when the still molten lava created the structures by means of flow and ponding of the lava
The cavities represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as
blisters and pockets Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow
leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally
along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our subsurface exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four borings
to depths of about 15 to 15 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate
locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered loose to medium dense silty sands
volcanic ash to depths of about 0 2 to 6 5 feet below the existing ground surface The silty
sands were generally underlain by volcanic rock consisting of medium hard to hard basalt
formations extending to the maximum depths explored of about 15 5 feet below the existing
ground surface The basalt formations ranged from slightly to severely fractured and from
relatively unweathered to moderately weathered
Voids and or cavities were not encountered in our borings drilled at the project site
However it should be noted that lava tubes and cavities are commonly encountered in basalt
formations Therefore special attention should be given to the recommendations presented
in the Site Preparation subsection of this report to help detect the near surface cavities and
voids during construction
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of the field exploration
However groundwater levels may fluctuate depending on seasonal precipitation time of the
year and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site is underlain by a surficial layer of loose
to medium dense silty sands with some gravel volcanic ash Our borings encountered
medium hard to hard basalt formations from depths of about 0 2 to 6 5 feet extending to the
maximum depths explored of about 15 5 feet below the existing ground surface The basalt
formations were typically slightly to severely fractured and relatively unweathered to
moderately weathered
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
that an 18 inch layer of structural fill material be provided below the building footprints of
the proposed structures to provide a choking layer above the larger sized general fill
material and to facilitate construction of the buildings The structural fill layer should be
extended beyond the edges of the proposed structures by a minimum of 2 feet laterally
With the structural fill layer we believe that the proposed structures may be supported on
shallow spread and or continuous footings bearing on the structural fill or compacted
on site soils
it should be noted that the site is heavily vegetated and is blanketed with a layer of
volcanic ash soils Therefore clearing and grubbing of the site will likely consist of
removing a large amount of unsuitable materials for disposal off site to avoid contaminating
the on site excavated materials which may be re used as a source of fill materials

Fills and Backfills
For building areas we recommend that a minimum of 18 inches of structural fill
material be provided below the building footprints to serve as a choking layer for the
larger sized general fill below and also to facilitate construction of the houses The
structural fill layer should extend a minimum of 2 feet laterally beyond the building
footprints
Structural fill materials should consist of well graded granular materials less than
6 inches in largest dimension The on site cut materials with the exception of the
volcanic ash soils may be used as structural fill or backfill materials provided that they
are processed and reduced to a relatively well graded select granular material with
a maximum size of about 6 inches The organic laden soils and other unsuitable
materials should not be used as structural or general fill or backfill materials
Imported materials to be used as structural fill should be well graded from coarse to
fine with no particles greater than 6 inches in largest dimension

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum
dry density as determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of rock
boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of
passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii On the basis of this testing program the
number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered permanent cut and fill slopes may
be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter
Fills to be placed on slopes with inclinations steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and
benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The
filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal
compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations Fill
slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio
to obtain a well compacted slope face In addition boulders should not be exposed
at the fill slope face Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and
slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for
erosion of the finished slopes
Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
that shallow spread and or continuous footings be used to support the proposed new
buildings planned at the site As previously indicated we recommend that a minimum of
18 inches of structural fill be provided below the house structure within the building footprint
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for
the design of footings bearing on structural fill materials or the recompacted on site materials
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4099 00 Page 11
This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces In general footings should
be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade
Where foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate the total settlements to be less than 1 inch with differential
settlements on the order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 4 may be used for footings bearing on the structural fill material or compacted
on site granular soils Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This
assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted Unless covered by pavements or
slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soi should be neglected
It is important that a representative of Geolabs Hawaii observe the footing excavations
to evaluate the competency of the bearing materials and the embedment depths If
unsuitable materials are encountered in the footing excavations these materials should be
over excavated to expose firm materials and replaced with compacted structural fill

Since the project site is located on the Island of Hawaii seismically active dynamic

Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the retaining
structures discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the retaining structures
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of granular fill material
less than 3 inches in maximum dimension Unless covered by concrete slabs the
upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious material to reduce
water infiltration behind the retaining structures

APPENDIX A

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 at the approximate locations shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of our borings extended to about 15 to 15 5 feet below
the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped
with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler
the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would
be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical
Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
The field percolation rates of the on site materials were estimated by performing six
falling head percolation tests designated as PT 1 through PT 6 at the locations shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 The field percolation tests were performed in general accordance with
Wastewater Systems and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards to evaluate the
permeability of the subsurface soils for wastewater disposal The test results and the Site
Evaluation Percolation Test forms are presented on Plates C 1 through C 6
The percolation tests were performed by drilling 4 inch diameter boreholes to depths
of about 1 8 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface Approximately one inch of sands
and gravel were then placed at the bottom of the boreholes to protect the bottom of the holes
from scouring and sediments
During testing the holes were carefully filled with water to a depth of 6 inches over the
sand and gravel layer The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide
data upon which to base calculation of the percolation rates
