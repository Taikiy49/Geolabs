PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site comprises the Ka u High and Pahala Elementary School campus
located in the town of Pahala in the District of Ka u on the Island of Hawaii Some
significant sized below ground lava tubes and caves have been previously discovered at the
school site Previous subsurface exploration and construction projects had encountered lava
tubes and caves at the school campus site Most of the caves encountered during previous
construction projects were left as is without disturbance to the features with the exception of
those cavities located directly below the buildings to be constructed We understand that
portions of the lava tubes below the buildings had been collapsed where they were
encountered and backfilled with compacted fill material Based on the previous reports the
cave features appeared to be relatively large and extensive across the school site The recent
discoveries have prompted the initiation of further study to evaluate the campus project site
with respect to the occurrence and stability of the subsurface lava tubes and caves present
at the school site
In 1998 Geolabs performed a geophysical evaluation of the campus and adjacent park
facilities using a non intrusive method of electrical subsurface profiling The open grounds of
the campus were surveyed with ground penetrating radar in an effort to map the probable
locations of buried anomalous features that were believed to represent lava tube and cave
features significant sized voids in the basalt rock Based on our interpretation of the
geophysical data obtained a map was produced showing the approximate trends of
anomalous features detected in the subsurface
The intrusive test boring exploration was undertaken to support the preliminary findings
derived from the GPR survey The borings were performed in an effort to validate whether the
electrical anomalies detected by GPR in fact represent buried cave features Furthermore the
GEOLABS INC
W O 4003 10 Page 4
intent of the test borings was to evaluate the depth and quality of the overburden material
comprising the roof of the potential lava tube and or cave feature The number and locations
of the test borings were based on our recommendation and were made with the concurrence
of representatives of the Department of Accounting and General Services D A G S in
accordance with the priority of the school needs
Ground penetrating radar is an electromagnetic geophysical method that uses
high frequency radio waves which are transmitted into the subsurface and reflected back
where they are received by a recording instrument The recording instrument generates
a near continuous cross sectional profile of the reflected signals Variation in the reflected
signal is caused by the radar wave reflection at subsurface interfaces of materials having
contrasting electrical properties Because of the large contrast in electrical properties
between open void space air and the surrounding rock material the reflected signal
indicates an anomalous signature within the profile at the location of the below ground void
space

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes including the following Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea some activity during
recent geologic times Hualalai last erupted in 1801 and is considered dormant and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea both still active The project site is situated on the southeast flank
of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano
The volume of Mauna Loa extending from the ocean floor is estimated to be on the
order of about 10 000 cubic miles making it possibly the largest volcanic mountain on earth
Furthermore it is estimated from current lava production that Mauna Loa may have been
forming for the past 1 to 2 million years Mauna Loa is actually composed of two separate
shield volcanoes Mauna Loa and Ninole The latter having been covered by Mauna Loa
lavas left only a few localized surface expressions visible at present The Mauna Loa
shield has been built by volcanic eruptions along two principal rift zones that extend
southwestward and east northeastward from the summit caldera
The majority of the surface rock exposures at Mauna Loa consists of lavas and
volcanic deposits belonging to the Ka u Volcanic Series Pleistocene and Holocene age
and the older Kahuku Volcanic Series Pleistocene age In addition a regional ash
deposit identified as Pahala Ash Pleistocene age forms a soil mantle overlying and
interbedded within the rocks of the Ka u Volcanic Series The ash deposits typically occur
GEOLABS INC
W O 4003 10 Page 7
as kipukas isolated deposits with some significant accumulations ranging up to about
30 feet in thickness
The project site is located on land that has been mapped by others as Pahala Ash
covering most of the ground surface Beneath the surface ash layer rocks of the Ka u
Volcanic Series may be found The Ka u Volcanic Series essentially consists of tholeiitic
basalt Based on our observation and a review of previous geotechnical data for the school
campus the basalt rock underlying the project site generally consists of pahoehoe type
lava with a high incidence of embedded lava tubes
Pahoehoe basalt rock is generally the result of thinner more fluid lava flows The
feeding river lobes of a pahoehoe flow crust over to develop a continuous roof leaving the
interior lava stream to flow through a tunnel of its own making These remnant flow tunnels
or voids are known as lava tubes The lava tubes feed the advancing lava flow front and
margins As a result the lava tube may branch or converge generally in a downhill
direction to maintain the lava flow In the waning stages of an eruption the lava may drain
from the main tube system leaving open tunnels ranging from several feet to over 40 feet
in diameter The open tubes resemble tunnels with arched roofs and nearly flat floors The
main lava tubes may extend downslope for several miles occasionally reaching all the way
to the coastline Some of these large tube structures are known to exist beneath the
campus site and have been explored by limited underground traverse
Another type of void space that is typically encountered in pahoehoe rock is a pocket
blister These blisters are typically isolated closed void structures formed by trapped
volcanic gases within the flow interior Some blisters may extend a short distance downhill
before pinching out and terminating
The surface of pahoehoe flows are generally smoother than their rough and irregular
counterpart the a a flow A a flows form from generally more viscous slower moving lavas
and are expressed by a solid rock core sandwiched between an upper and lower less
dense clinker layer The pahoehoe flow surface may be expressed by broad
GEOLABS INC
W O 4003 10 Page 8
dome shaped hillocks created by the heaving and buckling of the flow crust as it
approaches obstructions
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located in the town of Pahala in the District of Ka u on the
southeast side of the Island of Hawaii The school campus is located just below the
1 000 foot elevation mauka of the Hawaii Belt Road on the southeastern slope of Mauna
Loa Puahala Street bounds the school campus to the east The main driveway entrance
to the campus is from the easterly direction via Kamani Street The general location of the
project site is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The approximately 33 acre campus consists of classroom greenhouse sports and
administration buildings some vacant cottages a library building and a swimming pool
The campus also contains open fields playground areas and a large soccer field as
shown on the Site Plan Key Map Plate 2 In general the campus slopes gently down
toward the south and southeasterly direction with the buildings and playgrounds situated
on generally flat ground surfaces The ground surface ranges from about Elevation
980 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the northwestern corner to about Elevation
915 feet MSL at the southeastern corner of the campus
The areas surveyed by GPR geophysical survey generally consisted of open ground
comprising of lawn and asphalt concrete pavement The boring locations were situated on
both lawn and asphalt concrete pavement surfaces The open ground surface was covered
mostly by tan clayey silt soils representing weathered volcanic ash materials Localized
areas of the ground surface consist of spotty basalt rock exposures representing the
protruding smooth surface of pahoehoe lava rock

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The discussion of the intrusive exploration is organized by study area to include our
findings and recommendations for the nine study areas separately The Site Plan Key Map
Plate 2 presents an overview of the nine study areas and the 26 boring locations with
respect to the target GPR anomalies located at the project site The Site Plans Plates 3 1
through 3 9 show the boring locations with respect to the fixed reference features present
at the site The Logs of Borings drilled are presented in Appendix A
In general the lava tube and cave features encountered in the drilled borings appear
to correlate well with the estimated depths determined by the GPR geophysical survey
Where the test borings did not encounter a lava tube or cave feature the GPR data was
reviewed and in some cases some refinement to the estimated size of the potential lava
tube feature was made Upon further review of the GPR data and boring information it is
our opinion that some of the anomalous features detected by the GPR survey cannot be
completely discounted based on the findings derived from the single 4 inch diameter test
boring This is especially the case for Area No 3 where some significant sized lava tube
GEOLABS INC
W O 4003 10 Page 11
features mapped at the soccer field are believed to be connected to the multiple smaller
anomalous features detected at the Administration Building parking lot
Furthermore two borings were drilled within each study area designated as Area

a lava tube that was believed to be inter connected as the same feature although they are

Area No 1
The purpose for Boring 18 and 19 at Area No 1 was to evaluate the
shallow subsurface conditions near some localized areas of observed surface soil erosion
Based on our observations there are three general areas of scattered holes in the ground
surface that penetrate the underlying rocky fill materials The general location of the areas
containing the small diameter holes and the location of the borings are shown on the Site
Plan Plate 3 1
Based on our observations the holes in the ground surface range from about 2 to
4 inches in diameter The eroded holes appear to pass through a thin surface silty soil
GEOLABS INC
W O 4003 10 Page 12
layer and into a shallow buried fill layer consisting of poorly graded basalt cobbles and
boulders containing a lack of finer soil rock matrix materials The surface holes are
irregular in shape therefore probing of the depth from the surface is difficult to perform
Based on the boring data and observation of the open holes the underlying cobbles and
boulders appear to be loosely nested together with some significant void spaces between
the generally angular cobble and boulder materials The rock fills appear to extend to
depths of about 2 to 2 feet below the existing ground surface
Beneath the rock fill stiff tan colored clayey silt soils were encountered extending
to depths ranging from about 4 feet to in excess of about 10 feet the maximum depth
explored below the existing ground surface Based on the boring information obtained
the soils underlying the rock fill may represent the original ground surface of volcanic ash
soils overlying deeper buried basalt rock formation The rock fill material may have been
spread over the original clayey soil surface to raise the grade and provide a stable base for
construction at the site
The erosional holes are believed to be a result of the piping of the thin surface soils
into the underlying poorly graded rock fill containing appreciable void space between the
rocky fill materials The piping of soil is believed to be initiated by surface water runoff
forming puddles and percolating into the ground Once a conduit into the underlying rocky
fill is formed by the percolating water the conduit may be enlarged by continued erosion
and piping of the surface soils
Due to the widely scattered condition of the shallow surface holes in the vicinity of
Area No 1 it is believed that much of the area may be underlain by the rock fill that may
not have been properly choked with finer materials and compacted The rock fill is
considered to be of poor quality in this respect Based on the subsurface information
obtained the eroded holes do not appear to be related to lava tube or cave features in this
area Furthermore the grounds in this area do not appear to be susceptible to collapse
due to the presence of subsurface lava tubes and or caves However it may be anticipated
GEOLABS INC
W O 4003 10 Page 13
that additional surface holes will develop over time and the existing holes have the potential
to enlarge thus producing a potential tripping hazard to persons traversing the area
As a precautionary measure to reduce the potential tripping hazard we recommend
that the existing surface holes be backfilled with fine gravel tamped firmly and capped with
a minimum of 6 inches of compacted soil to reduce the potential for enlargement of the
existing holes If a permanent solution is desired then the area should be scarified to a
depth of about 2 to 3 feet below the existing ground surface using heavy equipment The
earth materials resulting from the scarification effort should be mixed with imported fill
materials to produce well graded materials suitable to fill the interstices between the larger
cobble and boulder fragments The choking of the rocky fill with finer materials would serve
to reduce the potential for piping of the capping topsoil layer Due to the widely scattered
extent of the surface holes the injection of grout into the subsurface to fill the voids is
probably not cost effective

the backfill Specifications pertaining to the type of backfill and methods of placement
should be provided with respect to the size and accessibility of the cave Finally the safety

Area No 4
The purpose for Boring Nos 11 and 20 located at Area No 4 was to explore the
subsurface where some locally occurring small erosional holes were observed at the
ground surface In addition the GPR survey detected the presence of an anomalous
feature at the same location The area explored is located adjacent to some existing
playground equipment as shown on the Site Plan Plate 3 4
The test borings were drilled in the vicinity of the observed surface soil erosion A
set of playground climbing bars was recently removed from the site Boring No 11
encountered approximately 2 5 feet of loose sandy cinder fill overlying hard basalt
boulders A void of approximately 2 feet in diameter was encountered located in between
the basalt boulder fill The boulders extended to a depth of about 8 5 feet below the ground
level where a silty ash soil horizon was encountered Basalt rock formation was
encountered below the soil horizon located at a depth of about 11 5 feet below the ground
level Based on the information obtained from Boring No 11 it appears that the void exists
within the boulder fill The loose sandy and gravelly surface fill materials overlying the
boulder layer may be eroding by surface water percolation into the underlying boulder fill
containing void pockets in between the boulders
Based on conversation with some long time residents of the area the playground
area was filled to the existing level grade from a pre existing natural sloping ground
condition During the earthwork fill placement a variety of materials may have been placed
as fill including the possibility that some large trees pre existing at the site were buried
Over time the organic matter may have decomposed to form voids in the fill or the boulders
used as fill were poorly placed and contained large voids between the blocks of rock
Boring No 20 was drilled to aid in the confirmation of findings obtained from
Boring No 11 Both borings encountered similar stratigraphy that indicate soil and boulder
GEOLABS INC

fills of approximately 5 to 8 feet in thickness overlying buried ash soils The ash soils may
represent the pre existing natural ground surface with underlying basalt rock formation
encountered at about 9 to 11 feet below the existing ground surface The boring
information suggests that the observed localized surface soil erosion may be caused by
piping of the near surface fill soils into generally shallow buried voids present in the boulder
fill layer Based on the boring information we anticipate that the potential for sudden
ground collapse is low at the location of the removed jungle gym playground equipment
However it appears that the soil erosion may persist due to the loose and permeable
nature of the near surface fill materials overlying the boulders containing void space
As a precautionary measure we recommend that continued observation for signs
of soil erosion and the formation of holes at the ground surface be conducted Should
recurrence of the erosional holes be observed at the same location we recommend
recording the location of the holes by a sketch diagram and filling the holes immediately
with soil material to reduce potential hazards associated with tripping If the problem
persists or grows in magnitude it may be necessary to excavate the fills in the vicinity of
the problem area to evaluate the extent of the problem to develop recommendations for
proper backfilling of the site If the problem area is generally limited in size the injection
of cement grout into the subsurface may be a viable solution Otherwise the potentially
poor quality fill may require removal and replacement with better quality fill materials

In general an unconfined compressive strength for basalt rock that is less than
about 2 000 psi is considered to be relatively weak rock material The weaker rock
materials at the project site are typically a result of the strongly vesicular character which
shows a porous and honeycomb character of small holes derived from the exsolving gases
within the cooling lava flow Unconfined compressive strengths ranging from about
2 000 psi to greater than 4 000 psi generally represent fair to good quality denser rock
materials with fewer vesicles
The unconfined compressive strength of the rock cores obtained from the cave roof
encountered in Boring No 10 generally indicate fair to good rock strength despite a
marginal rock roof thickness of about 6 feet in height Therefore the potential for collapse
of the roof structure is considered to be low to moderate However due to the repeated
traffic exposure and marginal thickness of the roof material some distress to the cave roof
may be imparted over the passage of time by traffic loading
Ground Surface Structure Monitoring Program
As a precautionary measure we recommend that the school implement a monitoring
program to record observations of the condition of the ground surface and building structures
with respect to possible earth movement The program would serve as a means to compare
observations recorded over the passage of time The comparison could be used to evaluate
general trends in the condition of school grounds and some buildings Furthermore the
monitoring program would serve to heighten the awareness of the potential presence of
subsurface caves and their possible effects on the ground surface and overlying buildings
We have prepared a set of guidelines for the proposed monitoring program which
includes a map of the campus and log form that may be used to implement the monitoring
program In addition some baseline observations were recorded as an example of how to
implement the program The guidelines and instruction including a blank map and log form
are provided in Appendix B The map and log form may be reproduced and the copies used
for the bi annual monitoring survey An initial baseline survey was performed by Geolabs and
the results are presented in Appendix C

LIMITATIONS

re evaluate the findings provided in this report
The locations of the mapped ground penetrating radar anomalies and drilled borings

location of the marked anomalies and drilled borings should be considered accurate only to
the degree implied by the methods used
The ground penetrating radar data have been reviewed and interpretations of the radar
signatures were made in the formulation of this report However it must be noted that
geophysical surveys such as ground penetrating radar are subject to interpretation of the data
by the operator s to form judgmental conclusions The geophysical survey conducted for this
project was performed in conformance with generally accepted protocol for this type of
exploration

High and Pahala Elementary school site in accordance with generally accepted geotechnical

PROGRAM GUIDELINE
GROUND SURFACE STRUCTURE MONITORING
KA U HIGH AND PAHALA ELEMENTARY SCHOOL
KA U ISLAND OF HAWAII
This document describes the ground surface and structure monitoring program to be
performed at the campus by school maintenance personnel The intent of this document
is to serve as a guide for the implementation of the monitoring program
PURPOSE
Recent investigation of the school campus has identified the presence of some
buried lava tube and cave features The buried lava tubes and overlying grounds are
believed to be generally stable However there is a possibility that over time the lava tube
could be subjected to activity that may weaken the structural integrity of the feature
creating some possible ground instability
This monitoring program is recommended as a precautionary measure for the
observation of the school grounds and buildings for potential signs of distress that may be
related to possible ground movement The program should also serve to heighten
awareness of the presence of lava tube and cave features and their potential effects on
the ground surface
PROGRAM DESCRIPTION
The monitoring program essentially consists of recording visual observations of
selected features noted at the ground surface The monitoring program should also
include visual observation pertaining to the performance of the existing building
foundations Abnormalities such as expanding cracks deepening depressions and the
formation of open holes may indicate possible ground movement
The monitoring program is designed for non technical personnel to conduct the
observations in a systematic manner to allow for comparison of subsequent observations
to identify possible trends associated with the features under observation It is
recommended that the same personnel conduct the monitoring program to maintain a
level of continuity and familiarity with the program objectives and observe changes that
may occur with the passage of time
W O INC PLATE B 1
Monitoring Schedule
The monitoring program ground survey should be conducted at 6 month periodic
intervals The 6 month interval schedule is intended to provide the school officials with up
to date information to identify basic trends or patterns in the occurrence of the observed
features
The initial baseline monitoring survey was conducted on August 10 1999 by a
project geologist from Geolabs Inc Therefore the next monitoring date should be
conducted on or about and about every 6 months thereafter
Materials Needed
The following is a list of some basic items that should be available for the personnel
preparing the survey report
e This program description document which may be used for reference
The attached map titled General Site Plan Exhibit A which may be used
to reference the campus layout and approximate location of observed
features
e The attached log form titled Monitoring for Ground and Building
Abnormalities Exhibit B to record the observations made in the field
e Camera to record observations where appropriate
e Notepad to record detailed sketch drawings of observed features where
appropriate
e Measuring tape to assist with reference measurements where appropriate
Record Keeping
The bi annual monitoring reports should be kept for reference and comparison to
previously made observations In the event that certain areas of the campus experience
recurring observation of abnormalities the records may assist in showing a trend or
pattern associated with the potential problem area
Furthermore documentation of maintenance work related to the backfilling of holes
and cracks in addition to minor foundation or building repair should be filed with the
monitoring reports to assist in the awareness of potential trouble areas In addition all
school personnel should be encouraged to report their personal observations of ground or
building distress at the campus The additional reporting should aid in heightening the
awareness of the condition of the school grounds and buildings
Recommended Actions
Should an abnormality be discovered subsequent more detailed observation of the
surrounding area should be made If the ground surface abnormality is judged to be
relatively minor such as slight cracking or slight depressions heavy vehicle traffic over the
vicinity should be minimized Should the conditions persist become increasingly evident
or traffic cannot be rerouted a geotechnical engineer should be consulted to conduct an
evaluation of the potential problem
W O INC PLATE B 2
If the observed abnormality is related to a building foundation or other structure and
is determined to be of concern for stability a structural engineer should be consulted to
evaluate the possible cause s of the problem Some subsurface exploration may be
necessary to assist in the evaluation of the observed distress
The location of small holes in the ground surface should be recorded on a sketch for
future reference and evaluation of the possible pattern The holes should be backfilled
with soil to reduce potential trip hazards The discovery of large holes or collapsed
features should be reported immediately to school officials Access to the site and vicinity
should be restricted until the feature is examined by qualified personnel
