Subsoil Contamination
It was not in our scope of work to determine whether the subsoils at the site are
contaminated by petroleum products nor to quantify the level of contamination if it exists
However a strong fuel odor was encountered immediately upon drilling through the
existing asphalt pavement indicating possible contamination of the subsoils
With an H Nu photo ionization detector we measured a headspace reading of
approximately 80 parts per million ppm of organic vapor from the soil sample taken at
approximately 6 inches below the existing ground when enclosed in a plastic sample bag
Petroleum odors were also noted in all other samples taken from the boring
At a height of about 18 inches above the top of the boring H Nu readings of 2 to
5 ppm were also measured during our field exploration Readings fluctuated depending
upon the wind direction and intensity Our field work was conducted in a day of strong
and gusty tradewinds

Excavation
The proposed excavation is anticipated to be up to about 12 feet deep below the
existing ground surface It is imperative that adequate shoring support be provided to
reduce the potential of ground movement that may jeopardize existing adjacent
structures particularly the Burger King restaurant
Excavation Method
Based on the subsoil conditions we believe that excavation utilizing conventional
earth moving equipment should not be difficult

Shoring Support
Based on the anticipated subsoil conditions at the tank site we recommend that
the proposed excavation be supported with horizontally braced continuous
interlocking steel sheetpile walls
Since the excavation is not going to be dewatered it appears that only one level
of horizontal bracing may be installed above the anticipated shallow groundwater
table Assuming that the bracing will be installed at the existing ground level we
recommend that the apparent earth pressure diagram shown on Plate 3 may be
used for the sheeting and bracing design
Based on our estimate we recommend that the steel sheeting be driven to a
minimum embedment depth of 12 feet below the excavation bottom or a minimum
total depth of 24 feet below the existing ground surface The horizontal bracing
should be designed with a minimum safety factor of 2 to support the sheetpile
walls tightly against the excavation faces
It is essential that the interlocking sheeting be driven properly with a suitable
sheetpile hammer to the required embedment depth prior to excavation and be
maintained tight against the excavation faces by the horizontal bracing at the
ground level The shoring system should be maintained until completion of the
excavation backfilling

Backfilling
It is our understanding that the tank excavation will be backfilled on the same day
after the tanks are removed
Due to the potential contamination of the subsoils we recommend that the project
environmental consultant be consulted regarding proper disposal of excavated
material from the tank site and also backfilling of the excavation since it was not
within our scope of work to provide this type of environmental consultation
From the geotechnical engineering standpoint of view we recommend that No 3B
Fine ASTM C 33 No 67 gravel be used for backfilling the proposed excavation
due to the anticipated high groundwater level at the site The gravel is self
compacting in nature and no mechanical compaction is required under water It
may be compacted in maximum 12 inch thick layers with a minimum of three 3
passes of a vibratory plate above the groundwater level
Construction Precaution
We recommend that the project environmental consultant be contacted for the
environmental and health aspects which should be considered for this project
From the geotechnical engineering standpoint of view we believe that the sheeting
and bracing system used must comply with all applicable safety requirements

It is our opinion that the adequacy and safety of the design and installation of the
sheeting and bracing system used should be made the sole responsibility of the
contractor His qualified representative who should be required to be continuously
present on site will have the best opportunity to promptly observe any changing
conditions during construction such as unforseen subsoils unexpected high
groundwater table unsuitable construction sequence or techniques etc which
might affect the excavation support stability
However it must be noted that even with good construction procedure some
movement of the shoring support might still occur because of changes in earth
stresses which will normally occur during excavation The magnitude of movement
will depend greatly on workmanship for example how quickly and tightly the
trench support is installed It will also depend on the type of subsoils the depth
and width of excavation and also the rate of excavation
In view of the complexity of the problem it is difficult to accurately predict the
actual movement of a shoring support system and the adjacent ground
Therefore it is important that the shoring support in particular the horizontal struts
be installed properly and as early as practicable and that the adjacent ground be
continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of movements
such as excavation face sloughing If such movements occur remedial measures
such as but not limited to stopping the excavation should be implemented at the
judgement of the contractor s full time on site representative

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling one 1 boring to
a depth of 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 A truck mounted drill rig was used to drill the boring
The material encountered in the boring was classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who continuously monitored the drilling
operations The classifications were further reviewed by visual inspection and testing in
the laboratory The soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soils
Classification System A graphic presentation of the material encountered is presented
on the Boring Log Plate A 1
Soil samples were obtained from the boring by driving a 2 inch O D standard
penetration sampler or a 2 4 inch D split barrel sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12 inches are shown on
the Boring Log at the appropriate sample depths
