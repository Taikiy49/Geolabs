SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration at the proposed residential development, the project 
site is generally underlain by stiff to very stiff saprolite soil and/or weathered volcanic tuff 
overlying weathered basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depth explored of 
approximately 101 feet below the existing ground surface.  We did not encounter 
groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered in our field exploration, we 
believe that the project site may be classified as Site Class C – Very Dense Soil and Soft 
Rock Profile for seismic design consideration.  We recommend drilled shaft foundations to 
support the proposed mid-rise residential buildings.  We recommend utilizing drilled shaft 
diameters between 3 and 5 feet with embedment depths ranging from about 20 to 
45 feet below the anticipated bottom of footing elevations for the drilled shaft 
foundations, to achieve allowable compressive load capacities from 750 to 1,500 kips. 
As an alternative, a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and/or strip 
footings may be installed to support the proposed mid-rise buildings, provided probing and 
grouting operation is implemented at each column location. Allowable bearing pressures of 
up to 6,000 psf may be used to design the shallow spread/strip foundation. This bearing 
value is for dead–plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient 
loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Single and/or two-story buildings may be supported on shallow foundations 
consisting of spread and/or continuous footings.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 
3,000 psf may be used for design of the foundations bearing on the recompacted 
on-site soils with a minimum of 18 inches embedment below the lowest adjacent 
finished grade. 
To reduce the potential for structural distress to the lightly loaded slabs due to 
the moderately expansive soils, we recommend capping the on-site silty and clayey 
soils below the slab cushion with a minimum of 12 inches of non-expansive select 
granular fill material. Prior to placement and compaction of the capping layer, we 
recommend scarifying the subgrade soils to a depth of about 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioning to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, and 
compacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction (ASTM D1557). 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct 
while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below 
Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank.  These banked or ponded lavas formed 
a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau of Central Oahu. 
The project site is along the distal southern flank of the Koolau Volcano; 
therefore, the project site traverses terrain that is mostly underlain by deeply weathered 
volcanic deposits erupted from the Koolau Volcano.  The volcanic deposits have been 
mapped and are referred to as the Tertiary Period of the Koolau Volcanic Series.  The 
thick volcanic deposits consist of mainly thin individual layers of basaltic lava flows.  The 
volcanic rock appears to be extremely weathered to form clayey and silty saprolitic soils.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling eleven borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 11, extending to depths of 20 to 101 feet below the 
existing ground surface.  The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
In general, 2 to 4 inches of asphaltic concrete over 6 to 10 inches of gravel base 
course were encountered at the site.  Below the pavement section, the borings 
encountered weathered volcanic tuff and/or saprolitic soils followed by weathered basalt 
rock formation extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 101 feet 
below the existing ground surface. 
The weathered volcanic tuff encountered in the borings along the southwestern 
boundary of the property was very dense and extended to depths of 15 to 41 feet below 
the ground surface.  The saprolitic soils consisted of stiff to hard clayey and silty soils 
either at a shallow depth or underlying the weathered volcanic tuff.  It should be noted 
that medium stiff to stiff alluvium soil was encountered along the middle portion of the 
northern property, saddling between Boring Nos. 1 and 4.  Based on the laboratory test 
results, the silty and clayey alluvium soils may exhibit moderate expansion potential 
when subjected to moisture fluctuations. 
The basalt rock formation encountered in the borings ranged from severely to 
moderately fractured, highly to slightly weathered, and medium hard to very hard.  
Because of the mountain range sloping terrain, the depths of the basalt rock formation 
varied significantly, ranging from near the existing ground surface to a depth of 40 feet 
or greater. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field 
exploration.  It should be noted that groundwater levels can significantly vary depending 
on rainfall, temperature, surface runoff, groundwater withdrawal, and other factors.

2.4 
Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code, 2006 Edition (IBC 2006), the project 
site may be subjected to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to 
be addressed for the project.  The following sections provide discussions on the 
seismicity of the Island of Oahu and the soil profile for seismic design. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts 
in the tectonic plates.  In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to 
volcanic activity.  Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before 
or during volcanic eruptions.  In addition, earthquakes may result from the 
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not 
erupt.  The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but 
most of the earthquakes are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive 
instruments.  However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and 
a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii.  Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii.  Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of high seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of 
Hawaii.  The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of 
Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5.  Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have

some areas on the Island of Oahu, including the Tantalus, Iwilei, and Tripler areas, 
reported more intense ground shaking, severe enough to have cracked reinforced 
concrete.

should be evaluated for potential soil liquefaction.  Based on the subsurface 
conditions encountered, it is our opinion that the liquefaction hazard at the site may 
be ignored due to the stiff cohesive and dense granular soils overlying weathered 
basalt rock formation at the site.  Therefore, the potential for liquefaction is not a 
design consideration at this project site.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration for the proposed residential towers at the existing apartment 
complex generally encountered weathered volcanic tuff and/or stiff to very stiff saprolite 
soils overlying weathered basalt rock to the maximum depth drilled of about 101 feet 
below the ground surface.  Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site 
and relatively large column loads anticipated, we recommend utilizing drilled shaft 
foundations to support the proposed mid-rise residential buildings.  As an alternative, 
shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or strip footings bearing on the stiff silty and 
clayey soils may provide sufficient compressive load capacities to the new mid-rise 
buildings, provided probing and grouting operation is implemented at each column 
location. 
For single and/or two-story buildings, a shallow foundation system consisting of 
spread and/or continuous footings bearing on the near-surface stiff to very stiff soils may 
be used.  Soft and/or loose soils encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations 
should be over-excavated to expose firm materials.  The over-excavated areas should 
be backfilled with granular material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction or the footing may extend down to bear on the underlying firm material. 
To reduce the potential for structural distress to the lightly loaded slabs resulting 
from shrinking and swelling of the near-surface silty and clayey soils due to changes in 
the moisture content, we recommend capping the on-site silty and clayey soils below 
the slab cushion with a minimum of 12 inches of non-expansive select granular fill 
material.

3.1.2 Lateral Load Resistance 
Lateral loads imposed on the building structures may be resisted by a combination 
of the lateral load capacity of the drilled shafts and the passive pressure acting 
against the near-vertical faces of the foundation caps and/or grade beams.  Passive 
earth pressure against the near-vertical faces of the foundation caps and/or grade 
beams may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per 
square foot per foot of depth (pcf).  Additional lateral load resistance may be 
provided by friction developed between the floor slabs (when engaged to act as a 
diaphragm) and the underlying cushion fill materials.  A coefficient of friction of 0.4 
may be used between the concrete floor slabs and the cushion fill materials. 
In general, lateral load resistance of the drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of 
the surrounding soil, the stiffness of the shaft, allowable deflection at the top of 
shaft, and induced moment in the shaft.  In general, we recommend spacing the 
drilled shafts a minimum of 2.5 times the diameter of the shaft from 
center-to-center.  Therefore, the effect of group action was considered in our lateral 
load analyses by including an efficiency factor in the direction of loading. 
The lateral load analyses were performed using the program LPILE-plus for 
Windows, which is a microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference, laterally 
loaded drilled shaft program originally developed at the University of Texas at 
Austin.  The program solves for deflection and bending moment along a drilled

3.1.3 Foundation Settlements 
Total settlements of the drilled shafts are estimated to be on the order of less than 
0.5 inches with differential settlements between the drilled shafts to be 
approximately half of the total settlements.  We believe that a significant portion of 
the settlement is elastic and should occur as the loads are applied. 
3.1.4 Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations 
In general, the performance of drilled shafts depends significantly upon the 
contractor's method of installation and construction procedures.  The following 
conditions would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled 
shaft foundations. 
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend, to a significant extent, on the 
frictional resistance between the shaft and the surrounding soils.  Therefore, proper 
construction techniques, especially during the drilling operations, are important.  
The contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft holes and in placing

drilled shafts.  The load test shaft should be structurally reinforced and 
instrumented with embedment strain gages for load testing purposes.  As a 
minimum, two embedment strain gages should be placed at each level, starting 
near the load cell location at an elevation of about 5 feet above and below the load

resistance and end-bearing components of the drilled shaft by loading the shaft in 
two directions (upward for shear resistance, and downward for end-bearing and 
shear resistance).

3.7.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, 
asphaltic concrete, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and 
disposed properly off-site.  Soft and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing 
and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to 
expose firm materials, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted fills.  The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly 
disposed off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate.  Contract 
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation 
and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the over-excavation 
quantities. 
After clearing and grubbing, the exposed subgrades should be scarified to a depth 
of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557.  Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Where shrinkage cracks develop in the subgrade soil after compaction, we 
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above.  Saturation 
and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and 
poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
well-compacted fill.  The need for over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil 
conditions should be evaluated in the field by our representative.

compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  Aggregate base 
course and subbase materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils are 
critical elements of the earthwork, Geolabs should conduct observations and 
perform soil density tests during the site grading operations to assist the contractor 
in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on 
the native subgrade soils and each fill lift.  Where compaction is less than required, 
additional compaction effort should be applied with adjustment in the moisture 
content, as necessary, to obtain the specified compaction.  It should be noted that 
the moisture content requirement of the general fills and subgrades (at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture for the clayey soils) is an important 
requirement considering the moderately expansive nature of the clayey soils. 
3.7.4 Cut and Fill Slopes 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, we believe 
the planned cut slopes will expose stiff clayey soils and/or weathered volcanic tuff 
and weathered basalt rock.  In general, the cut slopes exposed in soil conditions 
may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or 
flatter.  Slope inclination for the cut sloped exposing in weathered volcanic tuff 
and/or weathered basalt rock condition may increased to 1H:1V or flatter. 
Permanent fill slopes constructed using the on-site materials (or imported fill 
materials) may also be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.  Fills 
placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the 
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding.  The filling operations 
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers 
in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations.  Fill slopes should

3.8 
Excavation 
We anticipate excavations on the order of up to about 10 feet below the existing 
ground surface may be required for the construction of the residential development.  
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by stiff to very stiff 
saprolite soil and weathered volcanic tuff and/or weathered basalt rock within the 
anticipated depths of the excavation. Therefore, we believe that conventional 
excavation equipment may be used for the excavation; however, some hard excavation 
should be expected in the weathered volcanic tuff and/or weathered basalt rock and 
remnant boulders in the saprolitic soils.   
The contractor should develop and implement a monitoring program to detect 
ground movement and/or subsidence adjacent to deeper excavations, which may result 
in damage to nearby structures and pavements.   
3.8.1 Excavation Method 
In general, the contractor should determine the method and equipment used for 
excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations.  Based on our field 
explorations, the in-situ stiff to very stiff silty and clayey soils and weathered 
volcanic tuff and/or basalt rock encountered in our borings should be rippable by a 
bulldozer or other heavy equipment. 
The excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance 
equal to the depth of the excavation measured from the edge of the excavation in 
order to reduce the potential for ground movement.  In addition, the excavated soils 
should be stockpiled at least 15 feet away from underground utilities to reduce the 
potential for ground movement or subsidence, which may damage the underground 
utility lines.

3.8.2 Excavation Support 
Excavation for construction of the mat foundation and basement parking 
(if needed) may require adequate shoring to reduce the potential for adjacent 
ground movement. The excavation support and shoring system used are the 
responsibility of the contractor and must comply with all applicable safety 
requirements. 
The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and safety of the 
shoring installation.  His/her representative, who should be required to be 
continuously present on site during excavation and construction work, will have the 
best opportunity to promptly observe changing conditions during construction, such 
as unforeseen subsoils, unexpectedly high groundwater table, inappropriate 
construction sequence or techniques, etc., which may affect shoring stability. 
However, it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and 
the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during 
excavation.  Due to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately 
estimate the magnitude of movement.  The magnitude also depends greatly upon 
workmanship, such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports 
are installed, on the subsoil conditions, the size of the excavation, and the rate of 
excavation. 
Therefore, it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed 
properly and as early as practical, and if necessary, the adjacent ground should be 
continuously monitored for cracks, dips, and/or other indications of movements 
with instruments.

Pavement Design 
We envision that flexible pavements will be used for the driveways and parking 
areas.  In general, we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the driveways and parking 
areas would primarily consist of passenger vehicles with some light trucks and 
occasional heavy trucks.

We have assumed that the pavement subgrade soils will be similar to the clayey

Flexible Pavements

The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, and compacted 
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  The aggregate base course and 
aggregate subbase (if needed) materials should consist of crushed basalt aggregate 
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction.  CBR and field density tests

Paved areas should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to 
carry surface water off-site.  Surface water ponding should not be allowed on-site during 
or after construction.  Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or 
adjacent to the pavement areas, we recommend extending the curbs a minimum of 
2 inches into the soils below the aggregate subbase course layer to reduce the potential 
for migration of landscape water into the pavement section.  Alternatively, a subdrain 
system could be constructed to collect excess water from landscaping irrigation.  For 
long-term performance, we recommend constructing a subdrain system adjacent to the 
paved/landscaped areas.

We anticipate that the utilities for the new residential development will primarily 
consist of water, sewer, and drain lines.  In general, good construction practices should 
be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities.  The 
contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used for trench 
excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations.  In addition, the 
excavations should comply with the applicable federal, state, and local safety

requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design and

As mentioned above, the stiff to very stiff silty and clayey soils and weathered 
volcanic tuff and/or basalt rock were encountered in the borings drilled.  Based on the 
results of our field exploration, it appears that conventional excavation methods 
generally may be utilized for utility line excavation with occasionally hoeram operation 
for hard zone, such as hard rocks and bounders.  We encourage the contractor

In general, we recommend providing a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes for support.  
Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), also should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above 
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes.  It is critical to use this 
free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches 
of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes.  Improper trench 
backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on-site soils (with a 
maximum particle size of 6 inches) or select granular fill material.  The backfill should be 
placed in maximum 8-inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground 
subsidence.  Where trenches are below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench 
backfill below the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. 
3.11 Drainage 
It should be noted that surface and subsurface drainage are especially critical 
considering the moderately expansive nature of the soils. Therefore, the finished grades 
outside the new buildings should be sloped to shed water away from the slabs and 
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding around the structure.  Gutter

systems should be installed around the building and discharge should be diverted away 
from the slab and foundation areas.  Excessive landscape watering near the slabs and 
foundations also should be avoided.  Planters next to foundations should be avoided or 
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration 
into the subsurface. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
above foundation recommendations because ponded water could cause subsurface soil 
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength.  The foundation excavations

the concrete to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface.  
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to 
drain surface water runoff away from the slabs and foundations.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling eleven boreholes, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 11, extending to 
depths of about 20 to 101 feet below the existing ground surface.  The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  The borings were drilled using a 
track-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary wash 
drilling/coring tools. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous basis.  These classifications were further reviewed visually and by 
testing in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
and other observations, such as groundwater levels, are presented on the Logs of 
Borings, Plates A-1 through A-11. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond 
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond Core 
Drilling for Site Investigation.  Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow 
bit to cut into the soil materials.  The soil material left in the hollow core of the bit is 
mechanically recovered for examination and description.

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of soil and/or rock masses.  Recovery is defined as the actual length of material 
recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.  For example, if 
3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 
74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses.  RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting 
drilling-induced fractures or breaks.  If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5-foot core run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of 
Borings as RQD = 50%.  Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality 
of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and 
Minerals."

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content and Unit Weight tests were performed on selected soil samples 
to aid in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and 
correlating the layers between the borings.  The moisture content tests were performed 
in general accordance with ASTM D2216.  The unit weight tests were performed in 
general accordance with ASTM D2937.  The test results are presented on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths in Appendix A. 
Eight Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification.  The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Five Sieve Analysis tests were performed to evaluate the gradation 
characteristics and to aid in soil classification. The tests were performed in accordance 
with ASTM C117 and C136. Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Six Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested.  Direct shear test 
results are presented on Plates B-3 through B-8. 
Four 
Unconsolidated 
Undrained 
Triaxial 
Compression 
(TXUU) 
tests 
(ASTM D2850) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained 
shear strength of the silty and clayey soils encountered.  The approximate in-situ 
effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the 
relatively “undisturbed” soil sample.  The test results and the stress-strain curves are 
presented on Plates B-9 through B-12. 
Four one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples 
of the near-surface clayey soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the subsurface 
soils. The test results are summarized on Plate B-13.
