SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Hawaii Kai Water System Improvements project in the Hawaii Kai area
of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the
proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of the new water
system improvements and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is located along Kealahou Street Mokuhano Street Honokahua
Street Kepaniwai Street Kaeleku Street and the Kalama 170 Reservoir access road in
the Hawaii Kai area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the information
provided it is proposed to install about 2 800 lineal feet of 16 inch diameter
waterline along the streets mentioned above The waterline will connect to an existing
16 inch diameter waterline at Kealahou Street and a stub out line at the Kalama
170 Reservoir
Based on the available plans the majority of the waterline inverts range between
about 4 5 and 6 feet below the existing ground surface Deeper inverts are planned
where the waterline traverses existing utility lines The deeper waterline inverts range
between about 7 and 12 feet below the existing ground surface The waterline will also
traverse an existing drainage channel along Mokuhano Street
Several methods to install the waterline across the drainage channel were
evaluated for feasibility These methods included fastening the waterline to the existing
bridge traversing the drainage channel construction of a separate waterline bridge
crossing conventional cut and cover methods and horizontal directional drilling methods

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and the younger shield Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which
time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series The project site is located at the southern flank of the
Koolau Mountain Range
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and in the heads of valleys the erosional
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and
are transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium
Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes
become dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium

3 1 Trench Excavation
Based on the information provided we understand that the inverts of the proposed
16 inch diameter waterline will range from about 4 5 to 6 feet below the existing ground
surface with deeper inverts planned in areas where the waterline will traverse existing
utilities Our field exploration generally encountered sand and gravel fills silty clay cinder
material alluvial deposits consisting of silty sand and clay clinker materials and medium
hard basalt rock formation at the project site
We envision that most of the trenches for the new waterline will likely be excavated
in the fill materials consisting of sand gravel and silty clay cinder material and alluvial
deposits consisting of silty sand and clay It should be noted that some difficult excavation
conditions may be in encountered during the trench excavation due to the presence of
boulders in the fill materials and cemented zones in the cinder materials We envision that
the trenches near the reservoir will likely encounter clinker materials and medium hard
basalt formation Therefore difficult excavation in the medium hard basalt rock should be
anticipated near the reservoir and may require the use of hoerams or chipping
In general the method and equipment to be used for the trench excavation should
be determined and selected by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety
considerations The excavated materials from the trench excavation should be stockpiled
no closer than a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the excavation from the edge of
the excavation to reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement The trench
excavation should comply with all applicable local state and federal safety requirements
3 2 Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important design
consideration For pipes bearing on the sand and gravel fills cinder material silty clay
clinker material and basalt rock a minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular bedding
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be provided
below the pipe A typical section of the trench backfill is presented on Plate 4

3 3 Trench Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Water
System Standards State of Hawaii 1985 Volume 1 The first zone of backfill extending
from the bedding material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes initial trench
backfill should consist of free draining granular materials to reduce the compaction effort
required and resulting stresses on the pipe
The granular material should have a maximum particle size of 1 inch and should
have less than 10 percent passing the No 200 sieve It is critical that the initial trench
backfill be properly placed and compacted to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper trench backfill around the pipes could result in settlement of the backfill and
damage to the pipes
The trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the top of the pipes to about
2 feet below the pavement subgrade may consist of the excavated on site clayey gravelly
and sandy soils provided that they are free of deleterious materials vegetation and are
screened of particles greater than 6 inches in largest dimension The backfill should be
placed in thin lifts normally 6 to 8 inches in loose lift thickness with each lift mechanically
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 A typical
section of the trench backfill is presented on Plate 4 Boulders rock fragments larger than
12 inches in dimension may be encountered in the excavated materials therefore
boulders encountered in the excavated materials should not be re used as trench backfill

The trench backfill from about 2 feet below the pavement subgrade elevation up to
the pavement subgrade elevation should consist of select borrow subbase material
granular materials with a maximum particle size of 3 inches compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Because the waterline trench is located within paved
areas the upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Trenches should be kept free of water during backfilling operations If backfilling
below the groundwater level is necessary free draining granular materials such as No 3B
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used to facilitate fill placement in the
wet trench with minimal compaction requirements and to reduce the potential of appreciable
future settlement of the trench backfill Where the free draining granular materials are used a
filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be used to wrap the free draining
materials
3 4 Thrust Blocks
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at selected locations of the waterline
to provide resistance to the thrust forces due to bends in the pressurized pipes In
general thrust forces acting on the pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting
against the near vertical faces of the thrust block Based on the available information the
planned inverts are anticipated at about 4 5 to 6 feet below the existing ground surface
with deeper inverts planned at about 7 to 12 feet below the existing ground surface
Based on our field exploration medium stiff to stiff silty clay dense cinder materials
and medium hard basalt rock were encountered near the planned waterline invert depths
Based on the materials encountered at the planned invert depths we recommend that a
lateral bearing pressure of at least 2 000 pounds per square foot psf be used for the
design of thrust blocks

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths of about 20 to
55 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted
drill rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers and rotary coring tools The
approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer or geologist who monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the hollow
core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would
be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Three unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
rock core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the underlying
basalt rock The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths and are summarized on Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The results
of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One sieve analysis test ASTM C 117 C 136 and one sieve analysis test with a
hydrometer test ASTM D 422 were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification Graphic
presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on Plate B 3
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics Results of the direct shear test are
presented on Plate B 4
