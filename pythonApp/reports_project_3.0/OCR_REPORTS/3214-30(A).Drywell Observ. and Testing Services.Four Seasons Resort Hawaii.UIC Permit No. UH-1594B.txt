INTRODUCTION
In accordance with our Master Service Agreement with Kaupulehu Makai Venture
and the Underground Injection Control UIC permit application No UH 1594B we have
completed our visual observations made during construction of the 11 drywells planned
for the Four Seasons Resort Hawaii development In addition we have also performed
three injection tests for the new drywells This engineering report has been prepared in
support of Underground Injection Control permit application No UH 1594B The general
location of the project site and vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes findings resulting from our field observations and testing of a
portion of the new drywells at the project site In addition the as built information of the
11 drywells are summarized and provided in the Appendices of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Four Seasons Resort Hawaii project site is located in Kaupulehu in the
District of North Kona on the island of Hawaii The resort development consists of 37
hotel bungalow units lobby and golf clubhouse buildings a restaurant fitness center
swimming pools and associated access roadways and parking lots The hotel resort site
encompasses an area of approximately 33 acres of previously partially developed land
GEOLABS HAWAII

The County of Hawaii requires that new development create no net increase in
discharge of surface or storm water runoff The general practice on the island of Hawaii
is to utilize drywells as a method of disposal of surface runoff
As part of the resort construction a total of 13 drywells were planned for
construction for the disposal of storm water runoff under UIC permit application
No UH 1594B However only 11 of the 13 planned drywells were constructed at the
site Of the 11 drywells six drywells were pre existing and have been modified in depth
to meet the new finish elevations of the site The remaining five drywells were
constructed as new structures
The six pre existing drywells were originally constructed as part of an earlier
development planned for the site which was never completed Underground Injection
Control permit No UH 1594 was the original permit covering the six pre existing drywells
Under the conditions set forth in the approval to construct the new drywells the
Department of Health DOH has required that a minimum of three drywell excavations be
logged during construction for geologic lithology In addition three drywells were required
to undergo short term injection testing to evaluate their operational status after
construction A list of drywells which were lithologically logged and injection tested are as

SITE DESCRIPTION
The Four Seasons Resort Hawaii project at Hualalai is located on the northwestern
coastline of the island of Hawaii The site is bounded by the Pacific Ocean to the
northwest and to the west south and east by the proposed Hualalai residential
subdivisions and golf course The resort is in the lee of Mauna Kea Mauna Loa and
Hualalai mountains in an area of low rainfall quantity and intensities
The climate of the area is generally dry and warm with average daily temperatures
ranging between about 70 to 85 F Annual rainfall in the vicinity ranges from about 10 to
20 inches per year Rainfall at the site typically occurs as occasional light afternoon
showers except during periods of regional stormy weather when heavier rainfall may be
expected
The site is situated on the lower northwestern flank of Mount Hualalai The
topography of the site is relatively gentle sloping down toward the ocean northwestward
from the central portion of the island Existing elevations range from about 35 feet Mean
GEOLABS HAWAII

Sea Level MSL at the mauka portion of the site to about sea level along the shoreline
The site elevations have been raised by the placement of engineered fills during
construction of the resort development
Formerly portions of the site had been partially developed as a resort hotel until
the project was terminated and a new design concept was developed The remainder of
the site had existed as undeveloped generally barren rocky land covered with brush and
scattered small trees Several naturally occurring anchialine ponds were preserved and
incorporated into the resort development At the time of the drywell injection testing
construction of the resort was nearly complete The 11 drywells including the three
drywells which were injection tested are completed and are in a functional state
REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Hawaii is composed of five basalt shield volcanoes built by the
extrusion of lavas during the Pleistocene and Holocene Epochs The site is located on
the lower northwestern flank of Mount Hualalai a now dormant volcano which last
erupted between 1801 and 1803 This mountain is thought to be of an intermediate age
between Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa its larger neighboring mountains Hualalai appears
to have entered the late stage of the eruptive cycle Rocks of Hualalai Volcano cover a
large portion of the western side of the island of Hawaii Most of its lavas are alkalic
olivine basalt and are composed of both pahoehoe and a a flow types
Earthquake Conditions
The island of Hawaii is designated in the 1994 Uniform Building Code as Seismic
Zone 3 Seismic Zone 3 is considered to be an active zone of earthquake occurrence
Most of the earthquakes that occur on the island of Hawaii are related to subsurface
magma movement and are termed volcanic earthquakes

The drywells were constructed in both compacted fill and relatively competent
layered basalt rock formation The drywells generally consist of simple excavated pits
which are lined with perforated precast concrete rings to maintain the structural integrity
of the drywell excavations Due to the stable below grade construction of the drywells
and the placement of interlocking precast concrete rings seismic conditions pertaining to
the drywells are not considered significant
Flood and Tsunami
Based on the grading plans for the project earthwork grading to provide positive
drainage of the site has generally been provided A drainage system consisting of gravel
sumps were approved for construction and use at the site The purpose of the gravel
sump structures are to aid in the collection and percolation of stormwater runoff into the
subsurface By incorporating the gravel sumps into the drainage design for the site the
number of drywells were reduced
Because the resort development was constructed primarily in fractured rock
formation and fills the overall permeability of the site is anticipated to be high The highly
permeable ground condition combined with the generally low rainfall occurrence in the
area allow us to believe that flooding should not be a significant concern for this site
Portions of the site are located within the expected tsunami inundation zone for
this portion of the island of Hawaii Therefore it is anticipated that tsunami flooding could
affect the operation of the drywells which are located within the tsunami inundation zone
especially near the shoreline areas
Land Use
The resort site is situated on land which is below the established Underground
Injection Control Line for this portion of the island of Hawaii The site is in a rural area
with a zoning classification of V 2 25 The site is being developed as a planned resort

SYSTEM OPERATION
It is proposed to use the new drywells for disposal of stormwater runoff flowing
from roof downspouts paved surfaces and other graded areas of the resort site Use of
the drywells will be intermittent and variable and will be dependent upon weather
conditions such as storm duration and intensity
The influent will not be treated prior to disposal and the volume and rate of
injection will vary according to the intensity of rainfall Based on a 10 year storm event
Qi9 the calculated design maximum capacity is 2 cubic feet per second per drywell
Therefore for the drywell system consisting of 11 drywells the maximum design
capacity is 22 cubic feet per second Injection will be under gravity pressure i e the
head pressure that builds up within the depth of the drywell Since the new drywells are
intended for intermittent disposal of stormwater runoff only there is no additional standby
system other than the gravel drainage sumps located throughout the site
GEOHYDROLOGIC CONSIDERATIONS
The site is centrally located at Latitude North and Longitude 155 59
36 West The ground surface elevations at the site range from approximately sea level
at the anchialine ponds and near the shoreline to about 35 feet MSL at the mauka
portion of the resort site Native subsurface conditions encountered during the drywell
construction include layered basalt rock formation generally consisting of interbedded
clinker and massive to fractured pahoehoe basalt rock However the upper portions of
the drywells were constructed in compacted fill materials Some of the drywells were
installed entirely in fill materials especially at the low elevations of the site

Groundwater was observed in Drywell No 23 at the time of our injection testing The
observed groundwater is believed to represent the brackish groundwater table based on
the invert elevation of the drywell
The regional characteristic of groundwater occurrence in the area is fresh to
brackish water in a basal volcanic aquifer of relatively high permeability Published data
indicates that the groundwater table slopes towards the ocean at a gradient of about
1 foot per mile Regional discharge of groundwater flow is generally along the coastline
The lithologies of the three logged drywell excavations are presented in Appendix

surface to the bottom of the drywell after construction
WELL CONSTRUCTION
Based on our observation of the drywell excavations the drywellis generally
encountered well graded fill overlying interbedded layers of hard pahoehoe and medium
hard a a basalt of the Hualalai Volcanic Series The drywells were constructed by
using traditional excavation methods The drywell construction
proceeded intermittently beginning the week of and continued in
May 1996
The drywell excavation was dug a minimum of approximately 6 feet in diameter
Following excavation the drywells were lined with precast perforated concrete rings and
the annular space was backfilled with 6 inch minus well graded rock material
The completed drywells range in depth from about 8 to 20 feet below the finished
ground surface The as built constructed characteristics of the 11 total project drywells
are listed in Appendix C This information was provided by the site work contractor

INJECTION TESTING
The subsurface permeability of the selected drywells at the site was tested by the
short term injection of clear fresh water from a fire hydrant water source Injection rates
were measured by use of an in line paddie wheel flow sensor During testing clean water
was injected at rates of about 320 to 418 gallons per minute for a period of about
15 minutes for each test During the injection of water the variations of the water level in
the drywells were recorded with respect to time and rate of injection Following the
conclusion of the test the time lapse for the drywell to completely drain the water was
also recorded Groundwater was observed in Drywell No 23 prior to the beginning and
following the injection testing
These tests indicate that at an average injection rate of about 350 gallons per
minute the subsurface materials have an approximate coefficient of permeability k with
a range on the order of approximately 2 000 to 3 000 feet per day The variability of the
calculated subsurface permeabilities may be due in part to the method of drywell
construction used and the nature of the subsurface materials Results of the three
injection tests are presented in Appendix B
