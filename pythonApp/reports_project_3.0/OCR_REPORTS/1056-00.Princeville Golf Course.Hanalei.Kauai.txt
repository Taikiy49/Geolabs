In accordance with our proposal letter dated May 4 1981
this report presents the results of our preliminary geologic
reconnaissance study at the above referenced project site
The purpose of the reconnaissance study was to review the
general soil conditions in the area in order to evaluate the
proposed pond embankment from a soil engineering feasibility
point of view
Our reconnaissance study consisted of a review of the
general geologic and soils maps in the area field obser
vations shallow hand auger probings and test pits in the
accessible areas and laboratory soil tests
The test pit and probing logs and laboratory test data are
presented in the Appendix The approximate locations of the
test pits and probings are presented on the Site Plan Plate
1
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
A new 18 hole golf course is planned on the eastern side of
the existing Princeville Development Two large pond areas
are planned as part of the golf course These pond areas
will also serve as storage areas for the irrigation water
into the main Princeville Development

An embankment with vertical heights up to 40 feet on the
downstream and 20 feet on the upstream is planned near the
13th green Cuts up to 20 feet are planned in the upstream
ponding area
A second embankment up to 10 to 15 feet in vertical height
is planned between the 16th and 17th holes Cuts up to 15

Also included in our study was a proposed sump area to
intercept and store water from the existing stream located
across the highway from the proposed golf course Cuts
generally less than 5 feet are anticipated for the ponding
area
SITE DESCRIPTION
Embankment at 13th Green
The proposed embankment area at the 13th green is located in
a natural drainageway approximately 20 to 35 feet deep and
about 200 to 240 feet wide
Except along the northern side the general topography in
the area consists of rolling terrain which is currently used
as pasture and is vegetated by low grasses and shrubs To
the north the ground slope steepens drastically to form
high and steep cliffs facing the ocean The vegetation also
changes to a heavy forest of guavas Christmas berries plum
and haole koa
Near the proposed embankment area the side slopes of the
drainageway generally average a 3 1 H V ratio
Water was not noted in the drainageway at the time of our
exploration

GEOLOGY AND SOIL CONDITIONS
Embankment at 13th Green
The proposed dam site is located on the Princeville Plateau
east of Anini Stream This plateau was built up during the
late Pliocene early Pleistocene by extrusion of basaltic
lavas from the Pooku vent during the post erosional Koloa
volcanic series The on site soils are residual derived
from in situ weathering of these basic igneous rocks
The drainageway has been cut into these deep residual soils
by erosion from surface run off waters
On the eastern side of the drainageway north of the trans
verse center line of the proposed dam there is an exposure
of highly weathered decomposed basalt This indicates
that rock may be encountered at shallow depths

The local soils generally range in the order of stiff to

Embankment Between 16th 17th Holes
The proposed dam site is located in the central western
portion of the Princeville Plateau which was built up by
lavas originating from post erosional Koloa volcanic series
at Pooku vent Anini Stream cut a deep valley into these
flows at a lower stand of the sea Subsequently this
valley was backfilled with alluvium and graded to a higher
stand of the sea Following this alluviation the stream
was rejuvenated and has cut into this older alluviun
Near the base of the valley there is a small terrace of
recent alluvium 5 to 10 feet above the current stream
channel which is crossed by several abandoned stream chan
nels It is unclear as to whether this channel was graded
during another high stand of the sea or whether this terrace
reflects a temporary change in stream alignment
The current stream channel is lined with boulders up to 3
feet in diameter The boulders are sub angular to sub
rounded indicating little water working It is most likely
that they are lag deposited from the dissection of the older
alluvium rather than being transported by the stream in its
current form
The investigation at this site consisted of drilling six
hand auger probes along the transverse center line of the
proposed dam All of these probes encountered boulders at
shallow depths These boulders are in a matrix of medium
stiff wet silt with some sand and gravel Water was
encountered in each of the probes when a boulder was
encountered indicating seepage along the soil boulder
interfaces
Further visual reconnaissance was made both up and down
stream from the dam site Similar conditions were noted
throughout the area covered by this reconnaissance
Proposed Sump Area
The area is located on the Princeville Plateau which was
built up during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene by
lavas originating from the Pooku vent

Anini Stream cut into these flows developing a deep
valley This valley was subsequently filled with alluvium
which was graded to a higher stand of the sea The stream
has now been rejuvenated and is recutting a new channel into
this old alluvium
Our investigation in this area consisted of excavating 3
test pits with a Ford backhoe These pits extended to 8
feet below the ground surface which was the maximum reach of
the backhoe
The test pits revealed soils consisting generally of silt
with a trace of sand and gravel and occasional cobbles
These soils are derived from in place weathering of alluvium
deposited during the higher stand of the sea Traces of
relic structures such as boulders cobbles etc were
observed The soil presently exists in a stiff condition
with high moisture content
In one test pit saprolite derived from in situ weathering
of Koloa basalts was encountered This material is medium
stiff to stiff and wet
In another pit a layer approximately 3 feet thick of poorly
cemented gravel and sand conglomerate was encountered thus
presented relatively hard digging for the backhoe Below
this the pit revealed stiff wet silts derived from the
weathering of alluviun In this particular layer the boul
ders and cobbles were weathered to a saprolitic state O Be
retaining their original rock structures
The south bank of the stream was inaccessible for test pit
excavation but surface conditions led to the conjecture that
similar conditions exist on that side
The remainder of the reconnaissance involved visual examina
tion of the stream banks and surrounding area This exami
nation showed that the vertical bank along the north side
was cut by hand excavating equipment and was cut through old
alluvium The exposed soils are now quite hard but seem to
be due to some lateritic properties weathering and cemen
tation of the soils The soils on the interior of the bank
are probably only medium to stiff

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field reconnaissance of the area our general
observations and recommendations for each site are presented
below
Embankment at 13th Green
A relatively large embankment is planned at this location
It appears that this site is underlain by residual soils
over weathered rock formation Some loose recent alluvial
soils are anticipated in the bottom of the drainageway area
It appears that an earthfill dam embankment would be sui
table for this location Due to the lack of readily
available rock and gravel and abundance of on site fine
grained soil it appears that a homogeneous type of dam
embankment with provisions for a downstream toe drain would
be suitable for the site This assumes that provisions to
prevent overtopping of the dam can be incorporated The
loose alluvial soils in the bottom of the drainageway would
have to be removed down to the stiff residual soil or rock
prior to placement of the embankment fill
Additional studies for the embankment dam design should
include
l Borings to evaluate the competency of the founda
tion soils to determine its settlement and shear
strength parameters Of particular concern would
be the effect of the embankment dam on the stabili
ty of the very steep natural slope just downstream
of the dam site
2 laboratory tests to evaluate the strength charac
teristics of the embankment fill The project
site is in an area of relatively high rainfall
resulting in high natural moisture contents of the
in situ soil Therefore it may not be practical
to obtain the normally specified 90 compaction
of the fill and lower strength values may have to
be used in the design and
3 field and laboratory permeability tests of the
in situ soil and rock and compacted fill The
determination of the permeabilities are required
to determine if special procedures to reduce seep
age through or below the dam are necessary

Embankment Between 16th 17th Holes
The planned embankment dam is relatively small however it
would be situated in a narrow steep sided portion of Anini
Stream which is quite rugged with difficult access
The reconnaissance indicated that the stream area is under
lain by significant deposits of alluvial soils which are
generally porous and have low strengths Therefore these
alluvial soils would have to be removed prior to embankment
construction
If a suitable spillway structure can be located a homo
geneous soil type of embankment dam with provisions for
downstream toe drainage could be used However due to the
narrow and steep side stream topography in the area sui
table spillway location may not be available Therefore a
concrete dam structure may be required due to the possi
bility of overflow Due to the relatively low tolerance for
settlement a concrete dam structure would probably require
significant overexcavation of the soft stream bottom deposit
to allow its bearing on very stiff residual soil or rock
Additional studies similar to those presented in the pre
ceding section would also be recommended for this site
Proposed Sump Area
The field exploration encountered relatively competent allu
vial and residual soils
The area appears to have good promise for the proposed stor
age of water in an excavated empondment However this pond
may need to be lined with an impervious liner to minimize
seepage loss of stored water by percolation into the porous
soils encountered in the test pits
Additional field studies should be performed to further
evaluate the permeability of the in situ soil
