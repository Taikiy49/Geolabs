INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed new Clubhouse and Residence at the Waimea Country Club
located on the north side of Mamalahoa Highway approximately four miles east of
Waimea in the Hamakua District on the Island of Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project site is located approximately four miles east of Waimea in
the Hamakua District on the Island of Hawaii and is located in the vicinity of the current
Waimea Country Club clubhouse Based on the information provided we understand
that the proposed construction will consist of a new clubhouse a residential structure
walkways driveways a parking lot cart paths and utilities
Based on the preliminary site plan provided the proposed new clubhouse and
residence will have footprint areas of about 5 300 and 2 850 square feet respectively
We understand that the proposed buildings will be one story structures constructed of
concrete with on grade slabs
Details pertaining to the proposed construction were not available at the time this
report was prepared We understand that the site was previously mass graded for the
current clubhouse and gradually slopes down toward the golf course Based on the
information provided to us we anticipate that the site grading for the proposed project will
GEOLABS HAWAII

involve minor cuts for utilities and fills on the order of 1 to 4 feet relative to the existing
ground surface in order to attain the design finish grades for the proposed structures
Based on our experience in the Waimea area the project site is generally
underlain by volcanic ash Pahala Ash over basalt rock formation at shallow depths
Pahala Ash is generally a thixotropic soil i e strength is lost when remolded and or
disturbed such as during a seismic event

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Mauna Kea which has
formed the west central portion of the island The project site is generally underlain by
basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series which were deposited during the
Pleistocene Epoch This Hamakua Volcanic Series is typically composed of basaltic lava
flows in the form of a a characterized by rough spiny or rubbly surface of fragmental
clinker interbedded with massive basalt At the project site the lava flows are mantled by
a surficial layer of palagonitized ash locally referred to as Pahala Ash which is generally
highly plastic and thixotropic in nature

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered a near surface layer of stiff to very stiff
clayey silt known as Pahala Ash Ash soils generally have relatively low strength If the
in situ moisture contents are high enough they lose strength when remolded and or
disturbed Therefore we recommend that the ash soils be over excavated to expose firm
natural ground within the footprint of the proposed new structures and extending a
minimum lateral distance of 5 feet beyond the building footprint In addition areas within
the proposed parking lot and driveways extending a minimum lateral distance of at least
2 feet should be over excavated to provide the minimum 12 inch thick layer of select fill
material Therefore special attention should be given to the Site Grading section of this
report For the purpose of discussion engineered fill is defined as compacted fill which
may consist of either general fill material or select fill material
Based on our field exploration we believe that a shallow foundation system
consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on engineered fill may be used
for support of the proposed buildings at the site Detailed discussions and
recommendations for design of foundation site grading and other geotechnical aspects
of the project are presented in the following sections of this report
Site Grading
Based on the site plan provided to us we anticipate that grading for the proposed
construction will consist of minor cuts for utilities and fills on the order of up to about 4 feet
relative to the existing ground surface for the proposed structures To reduce the
potential for distress to the future structures resulting from the settlement of the ash soils
encountered during our field exploration we recommend that the ash soils be over
excavated within the limits of the new clubhouse and residential structure to expose firm
natural ground and replaced with compacted engineered fill as discussed below Site
grading operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii It is
important that a representative from our office observe the site grading operations to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2803 10 Page 7
evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and
scarification processes and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those
encountered in our field exploration
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing should be over excavated to expose firm
natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted
engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of
off site
After clearing and grubbing the areas within the footprint of the proposed new
clubhouse and residential structure should be over excavated to remove the ash soils
encountered during our field exploration to expose stiff natural ground The over
excavation should extend a minimum lateral distance of 5 feet beyond the limits of the
new clubhouse and residential structure In addition areas within the limits of the
proposed parking lot and driveways extending a minimum lateral distance of at least
2 feet should be over excavated to provide a minimum 12 inch thick layer of select fill
material
The subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture
conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction For subgrades subjected to vehicular
traffic the compaction requirement should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Where
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2803 10 Page 8
shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soil
be re scarified moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum and
recompacted to the specified requirements
General fill material needed to raise the ground surface from the existing grades or
over excavated subgrades to the finished subgrades should consist of well graded
materials less than 6 inches in largest dimension If imported material is required the
material should consist of non expansive granular material such as crushed coral
mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine
with no particles larger than 6 inches in largest dimension and should contain between
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a
laboratory CBR value of 15 or more and should have a maximum swell value of 1 percent
or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883
Select fill material for the pavement subbase and trench backfill should consist of
select granular material that is non expansive including crushed coral mudrock basalt or
cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles
larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain a maximum of 20 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of
20 or more and should have a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 Imported fill materials should be tested
by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with our recommendations prior to delivery to the
project site for its intended use
Based on our field exploration the excavated on site ash soils resulting from the
over excavation should not be reused as a source of general fill material however they
may be stockpiled for use as topsoil As an alternative the over excavated ash soils may
be thoroughly mixed with the general fill not to exceed a 1 4 ratio over excavated
material to general fill material and placed in the lower portion of the embankment fill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2803 10 Page 9
General fill should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Select fill and backfill
should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The compaction requirement of the fill and
backfill material within the upper 2 feet below the finish subgrade in the pavement areas
should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Fill placement
should commence at the lowest point and continue upward in level compacted lifts
Foundations
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the site we believe that shallow
spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed buildings at
the site An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot p s f
may be used for the design of foundations bearing on engineered fills This bearing value
is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as
those caused by wind or seismic forces In general footings should be embedded a
minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finish grades
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement
If the foundations are designed in accordance with our recommendations we
estimate total settlements to be less than 1 inch with differential settlements on the order
of inch
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2803 10 Page 10
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing material Resistance
due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This value assumes that the
concrete for footings is cast neat against well compacted materials and the soil around
footings is well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected A coefficient of friction of
0 4 may be used for footings bearing on the engineered fills
For footings bearing on the engineered fills the footing subgrades should be
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of
reinforcing steel or concrete If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the footing
subgrades they should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The
over excavation should be backfilled with select fill material compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down to the
underlying competent material
Footing subgrades should not be allowed to dry before placing concrete If
shrinkage cracks appear in the footing subgrades the soil should be thoroughly
moistened to close all cracks and recompacted to provide a firm base prior to concrete
placement
We anticipate that the proposed porte cochere of the clubhouse will be subjected
to uplift forces Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the following 1 dead
weight of the structure and 2 weight of backfill material above the footing Contribution
of dead weight from the backfill may be estimated using a unit weight of 110 p c f

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed
structures To minimize the potential for structural distress due to minor movements we
recommend that the slabs on grade be free floating Based on the subsurface soils
encountered at the proposed site we recommend that the subgrade be properly prepared
and kept moist prior to placement of cushion fill and slab concrete in accordance with the
Site Grading section of this report If the prepared subgrades become soft spongy or
yielding due to weather the soft material should be removed and replaced with select fill
material compacted to the specified requirements
For more uniform support of interior slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of select granular
fill such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below the slab
The cushion fill layer may also serve as a capillary moisture break To reduce future
moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture
vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the gravel cushion
layer Two inches of moist fine sand should be placed above the plastic membrane to
provide protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete The sand
should be moistened slightly prior to concrete placement
Where concrete slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a
6 inch layer of base course compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
be provided below the slabs in lieu of the 4 inch thick cushion layer The 2 inch moist fine
sand and vapor barrier may be omitted for these slabs The base course should consist
of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2803 10 Page 12
For design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of about 250 pounds per
cubic inch p c i may be used for the compacted base course A minimum slab
thickness of 6 inches may be used for preliminary design purposes Provisions should be
made for proper load transfer across the slab joints which are subjected to vehicular
traffic The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any unpaved area should be embedded
at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
Exterior concrete walkways may be proposed for the subject project We
recommend that a minimum 12 inch thick layer of select granular material or select
borrow subbase be provided below the concrete walkway slab Contraction joints should
be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right
angle intersections
The area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab with
non expansive impervious soil and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce
the potential of water ponding around the foundation

Moist Subgrade Soil
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a minimum depth of about
8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction CBR and density tests
should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to
confirm the adequacy of the above section Joints for rigid pavements are recommended
at about 8 to 10 foot intervals and at discontinuities to allow for some relative movements
of the concrete
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere on the
site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in
or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be extended a
minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soil below the subbase course aggregate to

reduce migration of landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain
system could be constructed to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation For
long term performance we recommend that a subdrain system be constructed adjacent

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines will be required for the project In general a
granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials
such as No 3B Fine gravel should also be used for the trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce
the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes
It is critical that the free draining material be used to reduce the potential for formation of
voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support around the sides of
the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of the select fill material The
backfill should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence
Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement subgrade should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the proposed new clubhouse and residence should be
sloped to shed water away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is
also advised that gutter systems be installed around the building and that the discharge
be diverted away from the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2803 10 Page 15
watering near foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to
foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water
infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and
footing foundations

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings to depths of about 10 8 to 15 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
the test are presented on Plate B 1
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio Test ASTM D 1883 was performed on
the near surface soil to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soil Results
of the test are presented on Plate B 2
