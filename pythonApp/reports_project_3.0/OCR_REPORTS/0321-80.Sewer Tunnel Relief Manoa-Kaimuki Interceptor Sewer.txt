I INTRODUCTION
A GENERAL
This report presents the results of our Soils Engineering
investigation for the proposed Sewer Tunnel Relief from Ward
Avenue to Makiki and Wilder Avenue Portion of the Manoa Kaimuki
Interceptor Sewer the miscellaneous sewer lines along Lunalilo
Street near Victoria Street Victoria Street near Kinau Street
and Wilder Avenue between Makiki Street and Piikoi Street and
the proposed deep manhole at Keanu and Elizabeth Streets which
is to connect to the existing tunnel
The investigation consisted of a series of subsurface borings
laboratory testing and engineering analyses of the acquired data
Available information and the study of the existing sewer in the
vicinity of the project were utilized to supplement the informa
tion obtained during our investigation From our engineering
studies recommendations concerning the design anticipated
stresses and construction of the tunnel in trench sewer and
manholes are presented

1 General
The City of Honolulu is located on the southwest
side of the elongated Koolau Volcanic dome which forms
the northeast portion of the island of Oahu Honolulu

deposits of coral sand and coral rock

60 inch R C P TRENCH INSTALLATION
1 General
The subsurface conditions in this area are favorable for
trench installation operations Excavation of the hard
coralline LIMESTONE located in some of the borings may prove
to be somewhat difficult The remaining soils in this area
should be well suited to trenching

2 Loads on Pipe
Loads on conduits due to gravity earth forces increase
with increasing trench width It is therefore important to
keep the trench width Bq to a minimum or employ sloping
14
sides or a subditch See Plate No 14
The unit weight of the backfill material and the coeffi
cient of friction between the backfill material and the trench
Sides also directly influence the earth loads imposed on the
conduit The applicable unit weights and friction parameters
for the various material in the preceding Section III B or
a maximum unit soil weight of 115 pcf and a K tan of 16
may be used for load calculations
Superimposed loads on the conduit due to concentrated
or distributed loads should also be considered in the design
3 Pipe Bedding
_ Distribution of the stresses against the bottom of a pipe
has a great effect on the load supporting capacity of the
pipe Pipe bedding the contact between the foundation and
the pipe is therefore an important design consideration
For this increment we recommend Class A Bedding which
can be one of the following
ny
a Concrete Cradle The pipe shall be bedded in a
monolithic cradle of plain or reinforced concrete _
having a minimum thickness of one fourth the inside
pipe diameter or a minimum of 4 inches under the
barrel and extending up the sides for height equal
to one fourth the outside diameter The cradle shall
15
have a width at least equal to the outside diameter of
the pipe barrel plus 8 inches A free draining well
graded granular backfill shall be used extending to
24 inches above the crown of the pipe and shall be
compacted carefully The allowable bearing capacity
for the respective soils and rock given in the preceding
Section III B should be used for design of the cradles
b Concrete Arch The pipe shall be embedded in care
fully compacted free draining well graded granular
material having a minimum thickness of 18 inches between
barrel and bottom of trench excavation and extending
halfway up the sides of the pipe The top half of the
pipe shall be covered with a monolithic plain or reinforced
concrete arch having a minimum thickness of one fourth
the inside diameter at the crown and having a minimum
width equal to the outside pipe diameter plus 8 inches
The load factor for Class A concrete cradle bedding
is 2 8 for plain concrete with carefully tamped backfill
and up to 3 4 for reinforced concrete with p 0 4 percent
in which p is the ratio of the area of steel to the area
of concrete at the invert
The load factor for Class A concrete arch type bed
ding is 2 8 for plain concrete up to 3 4 for reinforced
concrete with p 0 4 percent and up to 4 8 for reinforced
concrete with p 1 0 percent in which p is the ratio of

the area of steel to the area of concrete at the crown

5 Settlements
Settlements adjacent to open cuts cannot be predicted
because they depend on accidental feature of the subsoil
and construction methods The settlements may be caused by
erratic loss of ground associated with seepage or runs of
cohesionless material or by strains in the soil mass
associated with stress relief caused by removal of the
excavated material
Generally settlement due to stress relief of the
ground surface adjacent to cuts in medium dense to dense
granular material is inconsequential
Settlements of adjacent ground can be minimized by
preventing erratic loss of ground and by introducing points
20
of support for the side walls of the bracing system as soon
as practicable and at appropriately close vertical spacing
If these steps are taken and the workmanship is good the
settlements will be negligible

3 Stresses on Sewer
For the design circular tunnel section short term
stresses should be negligible and spalling would not be
21
expected if a circular cross section is maintained Long
term it may be expected that vertical stress will eventually
approach a magnitude equal to the overburden weight The
length of time for this to occur is indeterminant however
it is obvious that such a condition has slowly been reached
in the existing tunnel It is concluded that initial stress
on a tunnel liner plate tube would likely be negligible but
that design should consider the possibilities of external
pressures of 1100 1b t Live loads would add
at an overburden depth of 30 feet and at a depth
of 20 feet At overburden depths greater than 30 feet live
loading becomes negligible The final design stresses for
reinforced concrete Linings should be calculated at total
overburden weight since it must be assumed that frictional
resistance will gradually be relaxed over an extended period
of time and may be reached during the design life of the
sewer The final external vertical pressure should therefore
be taken at a maximum of 5600 1b f t2 Calculation of final
stress must consider deflection of the circular cross section
and the lateral restraining forces of the enclosing material
4 Construction
The structural properties of the cinder are somewhat
unusual The material is not sufficiently lithified to
be technically considered as a rock tuff but is much more
competent than a sand or gravel Its strength is due to
the interlocking of irregularity shaped grains rather than
22
to cementation By standard penetration test it ranges
from compact to very compact Excavations in this material
may be expected to stand well for extended periods of time
A variety of excavation methods will be available to the
contractor ranging from hand digging and loading through
excavation with pneumatic tools and machine loading
Although the material could be readily excavated by boring
machine mole it is doubtful that the scope of work would
justify such a method Light blasting may occasionally be
desireable and should be permitted Hydraulic excavation
is also possible but should be tightly controlled if permitted
For either recommended alternate the annular space out
side of the liner plate should be limited to a pay line
of not more than one inch greater radius than the liner
plate to minimize overexcavation All annular openings
should be grouted as tunneling progresses but with the
pay line limiting the amount of grout to be paid for
The grout should be placed from the bottom and progress
upward simultaneously along each side and with adequate
provision to preclude the creation of air pockets It is
greatly important that the annular space be filled and
particularly the side since it is only this lateral support
that will limit deflection and shortening of the vertical
axis Cast in place concrete linings should include mesh
reinforcing with proper consideration given for I D tensile
stresses at top and bottom and 0 D tensile stresses at the
23
sides
A possible complication during construction would be
that of encountering water in proximity to the existing
tunnel resulting from exfiltration A weakened zone was
encountered in hole N 14 at a depth of between 36 to
4 feet to 24 5 where moisture is retained
above a thin layer of sandy silt A similar slightly weak
zone occurs at a comparable elevation in hole N 17 although
moisture was not noted As a contingency contract specifi
cations should make provision for encountering water satu
rated cinder eventhough the likelihood is remote Water
could also originate from other leaking sewers or watermains
in proximity to the tunnel without having been encountered
during exploration drilling
It is further recommended that all possible flexibility
be allowed the contractor in the selection of tunnel construc
tion methods The specifications and plans should detail
the required final product and adequate safety provisions
Th other words specify what is to be done but not how to
do it The reason for this is that there are relatively
few tunnel contractors subcontractors and most utilize
a variety of specialized procedures dependent upon ground
conditions By allowing flexibility of method the bid
prices are likely to be lower than if great detail is
incorporated in the specification However it must be
24
emphasized that safety provisions will be strictly enforced

6 Settlements
The magnitude and character of the loss of ground and
settlement are strongly influenced by methods and detail
of construction The nature of the surrounding soil includ
ing groundwater conditions is also a decisive factor govern
ing loss of ground and settlement
The relatively dense cohesionless granular material
encountered in the project area should experience negligible
loss of ground and settlement provided that construction
procedures are expertly carried out so that no runs occur
7 Open Trench Alternate
The question has been posed concerning the practicality
of constructing the Makiki Street sewer trunk by trenching
25
rather than tunneling Although the unit costs for tunneling
are quite high we do not believe that trenching would be
an economic alternative Some of the reasons against trench
ing are as follows
1 Removal and replacement of street paving etc
2 Relocation of utilities and disruption of services
3 Access problems for public and contractor
4 Excavation and compacted replacement of at least
14 times greater material volume than with tunneling
5 Extensive shoring
6 Difficulty of excavating trench to depths exceeding
50 feet
7 Inconvenience to public and expense of repairing
damages to private property
8 Objectionable environmental effects
Even if trenching were feasible it is likely that a
liner plate tunnel would still be required beneath the
Lunalilo Freeway It is doubtful that an encased pipe of
the size required could be successfully jacked beneath the
Freeway with adequate control of the desired grade For
trenched portions the only material savings would be that
26
of the deletion of the liner plate and the peripheral
grouting

placed on a flat trench bottom The granular bedding shall
have a minimum thickness of one fourth the outside pipe
diameter and shall extend halfway up the pipe barrel at the
sides The remainder of the side fills and a minimum depth
of 12 inches over the top of the pipe shall be filled with
carefully compacted material
