conditions at the site to develop a soil/rock data set for the formulation of geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of the foundations for the new
awnings planned at the project site. To accomplish this, we completed the following tasks
and work efforts:
1.
Mobilization and demobilization of a truck-mounted drill rig and two
operators to and from the project site.
Drilling and sampling of two borings extending to depths of about 16.5 feet
below the existing ground surface.
Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by an
engineer from our firm.
Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the field exploration
as an aid in classifying the materials encountered and evaluating their
engineering properties.
Analyses of field and laboratory data for the formulation of geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of the foundations
planned at the project site.
Preparation of this formal letter report summarizing our work on the project
and presenting our findings and recommendations for design of foundations
planned at the project site.
Coordination of our work on the project by an engineer from our firm.
Quality assurance of our work, and client/design team consultation by a
principal engineer from our firm.
Miscellaneous work efforts, such as drafting, word processing, and clerical
support.

FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2. In general, the borings extended to
depths of about 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight solid-stem augers. The
approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer, who monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System, as shown on the Boring Log Legend, Plate 3. Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the attached Logs of
Borings, Piates 4 and 5.
Soil samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550, Ring-Lined Barre! Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition,
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by
driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered 3 inches of asphaltic
concrete underlain by 4 inches of basaltic gravel fill material. Below the basaltic gravel,
stiff to very stiff silty clay was encountered to depths of about 5 to 6 feet below the
existing ground surface. The silty clay graded to hard up to the maximum depths
explored of about 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Groundwater was not
encountered in the borings at the time of our field exploration. However, it should be
noted that groundwater levels may vary significantly depending on seasonal rainfall, time
of year, and other factors.
Detailed descriptions of the subsurface materials encountered from our field
exploration are presented on Plates 4 and 5. Laboratory tests were performed on selected
soil samples obtained during our field exploration, and the test results are presented on
Plates 6 through 8.
LABORATORY TESTING
The following laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples obtained
from the field exploration to aid in classifying the materiais encountered and to evaiuate
the engineering properties.
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties. The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths.
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D 4318) was performed on a selected soil sample
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. Results of this test
are summarized on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths and presented
graphically on Plate 6.
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D 3080) were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics. The results of the direct shear tests
are presented on Plates 7 and 8.

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Shallow Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, we recommend that a shallow
foundation system be used to support the new awnings planned at the project site. An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for
design of the new foundations. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be
increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces.
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades. Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements
should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement.
The bottom of the footing excavations should be re-compacted to provide a firm
and unyielding base prior to the placement of reinforcing steei or concrete. if soft and/or
loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations, the soft and/or
loose materials should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm materials. The
over-excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill materials compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction, or the footing may be extended deeper to
bear directly on the underlying competent material.
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with the
recommendations presented herein, total settlement of foundations is estimated to be on
the order of less than one (1) inch with differential settlements less than about one-half
(7%) inch.
Lateral loads acting on the new structures may be resisted by passive earth
pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil. A
coefficient of friction of 0.35 may be used between the footing and the in-situ soils.
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). This assumes that the
soils around the foundation are well-compacted. Unless covered by pavements or slabs,
the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected.
Resistance to uplift loads may be provided by the dead weight of the structure and
the footing foundation and adhesion between the sides of the foundation and the
surrounding soils. An adhesion value of 500 psf may be used for the in-situ soils. The
adhesion value provided assumes that the concrete for the foundation will be cast neat
against the in-situ soils. In addition, the weight of the backfill material placed above the

Site Preparation
In general, we recommend that the following guidelines be used for the minor site
grading anticipated. At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits
should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Pavements, slabs, debris, deleterious
material, and other unsuitable materials within the grading limits should be removed and
disposed of properly off-site.
Soft, loose, and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated
to receive fill or future improvements should be over-excavated to expose firm or dense
material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill. The
excavated soft, loose, and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site.
Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation
and compacted fill placement to account for variation in the over-excavation quantities.
In general, existing utilities that are to be abandoned within the building footprint
and/or limits of the proposed improvements should be removed. The resulting excavation
should be properly backfilled with on-site fill materials moisture-conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content, placed in 6 to 8-inch loose lifts, and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction. Utilities to be abandoned in-place under the proposed
structures should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure.
In areas to receive fills, the subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of about
8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The subgrades for areas subjected to

moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures. Optimum moisture is the
water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade, we
recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or scarified and
prepared again as recommended above. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over-excavation of the soft and/or loose areas and replacement with well-compacted fill.

If imported fill materials are required for the project construction, imported fill
materials should consist of non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coral or
basalt. The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension. In addition, the material should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory
CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less.
Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use.
In general, site preparation operations should be observed by a representative from
Geolabs. It is important that a representative from our office observe the site preparation
operations to evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the
excavation and scarification process and whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are
similar to those encountered in our field exploration.
LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this letter report are based, in
part, upon information obtained from fieid borings. Variations of subsurface conditions
between and beyond the borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations
may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident,
it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided in this letter report.
The locations of the field borings indicated in this report are approximate, having
been taped from features shown on the Loading-Dock Area Floor Plan prepared by
A. Kawabata, Inc. on October 6, 2003. The physical locations and elevations of the
borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used.
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict
the approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a gradual
transition. Water level was not encountered in the borings during the field exploration.
However, it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall,
temperature, and other factors. These data have been reviewed and interpretations made
in the formulation of this report.
