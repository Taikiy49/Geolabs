INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Caltech Submillimeter Observatory Base Facility project in
Hilo on the island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design
input only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Based on the information provided we understand that a base facility for the
Caltech Submillimeter Observatory is planned in the Hilo area on the island of Hawaii
The proposed project site is bounded by Achoku Street to the east Nowelo Street to
the south Komohana Street to the west and open land to the north
The proposed base facility will be a one story structure with concrete floor and
metal roof An access driveway and a parking lot serving the new facility are also
planned for the subject project Based on the existing topography and the proposed
finish floor elevation of the base facility building we anticipate that the new structure
will straddle across cut and fill areas We anticipate that fills up to about 5 feet in
thickness will be required to attain the finish grades in some areas
Based on the information provided us maximum column loads are estimated to
be on the order of 8 5 kips per column with wall loads of approximately 700 pounds per
lineal foot Our previous experience in the area suggest that the proposed base facility
site may be underlain by a thin layer of residual soils over basalt formation at shallow
depths Special attention would be given to the potential presence of near surface

or vehicular traffic

SITE DESCRIPTION
The proposed Caltech Submillimeter Observatory Base Facility site is located in
Hilo District of South Hilo on the island of Hawaii The site is bounded by Komohana
Street to the west Nowelo Street to the south and Aohoku Street to the east
At the time of our field exploration the site exists as undeveloped native land
Based on the topographic site plan ground surface elevations range from about 304
feet Mean Sea Level MSL to about 320 feet MSL The site was densely vegetated
with shrubs trees and fern growth The ground surface was observed to have a rolling
uneven topography which generally sloped gently down to the east from the western
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3416 00 Page 5
boundary of the site A 4 to 10 foot high natural slope or berm was observed bordering
Nowelo Street
The site was partially cleared with a bulldozer to allow access to the boring
locations within the proposed building footprint The exposed ground surface was
observed to consist of a thin soil horizon of sandy silt of generally less than about 6
inches in thickness Other areas of the site were observed to consist of layered basalt
rock formation at the ground surface with minimal soil cover Thicker soil deposits
appeared to be concentrated in the topographic depressions Some areas of the site
in particular the area surrounding Boring No 3 appeared to have a surficial layer of
light brown sandy silt with basalt gravel possibly representing an in situ surficial
ash clinker deposit
REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These include the following Kohala which has been long extinct Mauna
Kea which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted
in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the eastern flank of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano
which has been built up by basaltic lava flows These flows are of Pleistocene and
Recent Age and belong to the Ka u voicanic series The lava flows were subsequently
covered by the volcanic ash deposits
Basaltic lava flows generally occur in two forms pahoehoe and a a Pahoehoe
is characterized by a smooth ropy or billowy surface and frequently contains voids
and or lava tubes A a is characterized by a clinkery surface with a dense inner core
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3416 00 Page 6
The lava flows at the project site are a transitional phase between the two forms and
have characteristics of each
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 to depths of approximately 21 to 25 feet below
the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the drilled borings are shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered a surface deposit of ashy soil with
clinker material of approximately 1 to 4 feet thick consisting of brown sandy silt
intermixed with gravel and cobble sized basalt fragments The soil horizon was
underlain by hard basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depth drilled of
approximately 25 feet below the existing ground surface A 10 inch void was
encountered in Boring No 1 at a depth of approximately 6 feet below the existing
ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration However groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on factors such as
seasonal rainfall groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other factors
For detailed description of the materials encountered please refer to the Logs
of Borings presented on Plates A 1 through A 4 of Appendix A

Foundations
Based on the competent subsoil conditions encountered and the relatively light
structural loads anticipated for the subject project it is our opinion that shallow
foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be used for support
of the proposed facility Based on the existing topography and the proposed finish floor
elevation we anticipate that the base facility will straddle across cut and fill areas In
order to provide more uniform support for the foundation and to reduce the potential for
foundation distress resulting from differing subgrade conditions we recommend that the
footings be extended through the surficial soils or fill materials and bear on the
underlying basalt rock formation for support of the proposed base facility
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4 000 pounds per square foot p s f may
be used for the design of footing foundations bearing on the basalt rock formation
encountered at shallow depths This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may

Slabs On Grade
As previously indicated the proposed building will straddle cut and fill transitions
To reduce the potential for differential settlements at the cut and fill transition due to
differing subgrade materials we recommend that concrete slabs be supported on at
least 12 inches of granular fill material The granular fill should extend a minimum of
2 feet beyond the perimeter of the proposed structure The intent of the 12 inch
granular fill layer is to reduce the potential for slab cracking due to differential
settlements at the cut and fill transition
In addition to the 12 inches of granular fill we recommend that a minimum 4 inch
thick layer of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation cushion be placed
below the slab to provide more uniform support of the slab To reduce future moisture
infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor
barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the gravel cushion
layer Two inches of moist fine sand should be placed above the plastic membrane to
provide protection for the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete
Thickened edges of slabs if provided adjacent to any unpaved area should be
embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer of No 3B Fine gravel over the
12 inches of select fill be provided below the concrete walkway siab Construction
joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion
joints at right angle intersections

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials shculd be removed and disposed of properly off site The
surficial sandy silt material should be removed and may be stockpiled for use in
landscaped areas if appropriate
After clearing and grubbing the areas within the proposed building limits and the
area extending a minimum of 2 feet laterally from the edge of the building should
be over excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 12 inch thick
capping layer of granular fill material The over excavated materials may be
used as fill materials provided that the materials meet the gradation requirements
presented in the Fills and Backfills subsection of this report Any loose or soft
spots encountered at the over excavated subgrade levels should be removed
and replaced with compacted select fill
Due to the potential presence of cavities as commonly encountered in basaltic
lava flows a program of probing and grouting at the footing locations is generally
recommended However because of the light structural loads anticipated for the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3416 00 Page 11
subject project and the relatively low bearing pressure recommended it is our
opinion that a probing and grouting program may be omitted for the subject
project In order to reduce the potential for loss of support resulting from
collapse of the cavities below the concrete slabs on grade and footing
foundations we recommend that the over excavated subgrades be proof rolled
with a minimum 20 ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of 6 passes prior to
fill placement to assist in detecting and collapsing near surface cavities Any

The proof rolling operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs

level
Fills and Backfills
Fill and backfill materials to be used as general and structural fill material up to
slab subgrade level should consist of well graded granular materials less than
6 inches in largest dimension The on site cut materials may be used as fill or
backfill materials provided they are processed and reduced to a relatively well
graded select granular material with a maximum size of about 6 inches Any
imported material if needed should be well graded from coarse to fine with no
particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should have
a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell
potential of not more than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883 It should contain less than 20 percent passing the No 200

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rock boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the
number of passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of
compaction should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project
under the observation of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii On the basis
of this testing program the number of passes may then be used as the field
criterion for adequate compaction

near the existing ground surface However excavations into the deeper

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings to depths of approximately 21 to 25 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled
using truck mounted drilling equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field geologist who monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
