REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology and
subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea level
during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The
subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age coral algal
reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea which are intercalated
with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau
Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to present
sea level on Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred
During that rise the valleys in the project area were drowned In the last 10 000 years or so
the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have
continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick
deposits of loose and or soft sediments along the coast in the areas that were formerly
valleys and drainageways
In the early part of this century the Waikiki area consisted of low marshy areas As
the City of Honolulu grew and the Waikiki area was urbanized man made fills were placed
GEOLABS INC
W O 3792 10 Page 6
to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons The fill was placed upon lagoonal deposits
consisting of silty sands with coral fragments The lower clayey material was deposited
during periodic influxes of non marine alluvial debris which apparently smothered the coral
reef development Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years
have brought the Waikiki area to its present form Many of the resulting fills are of poor
quality in terms of supporting heavy structural loads

Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our borings and the moderate to
high structural loads anticipated for the proposed three story building structure we believe
that appreciable settlements would occur if the retail complex is supported on a shallow
foundation system bearing on the surface granular fill soils Therefore we evaluated several
foundation alternatives including driven pile foundations mat foundation with surcharge
program soil stabilized jet grouted columns soil stabilized stone columns and drilled shaft
foundations for support of the proposed retail complex Based on our evaluation of the
various foundation alternatives described these foundation alternatives may be considered
viable alternatives
Based on discussions with the project design team we understand that driven pile
foundations are not desirable because of the noise and vibrations generated during the pile
installation process Because of the restrictions imposed on the future development adjacent
to a mat foundation structure the mat foundation alternative was also deemed undesirable
for the subject development Based on further discussions and the information provided we
understand that the drilled shaft foundation alternative is the preferred foundation support
system for the subject development Therefore we recommend that a foundation system
consisting of cast in place concrete drilled shafts be used to support the proposed
commercial development

shaft to reduce the size of the footing at each column location Based on the structural loads

Soil Profile Type for Seismic Design
As previously indicated the subsurface conditions at the project site consist of surface
fills 6 feet thick over loose lagoonal deposits 30 to 35 feet thick overlying stiff alluvial soils
extending to over 100 feet below the ground surface Based on the average penetration
resistance and the undrained shear strength of the subsurface materials encountered in the
borings we believe that the project site may be classified as a stiff soil profile Therefore we
believe that the seismic design of the building structure at the project site may be designed
based on a Soil Profile Type Sp in accordance with Table 16 J of the Uniform Building Code
1997

Uplift Load Resistance
In general uplift loads imposed on the foundations may be resisted by a
combination of the dead weight of the drilled shaft and by shear along the shaft
surface and the adjacent soils Considering that the drilled shafts are designed
based on adhesion between the shaft and the surrounding soils we recommend
that an uplift load capacity on the order of up to 80 percent of the respective drilled
shaft compressive load capacities be used to resist uplift lads The recommended
uplift load capacity includes the weight of the drilled shaft The structural capacity
of the shaft member in tension should be checked by the project structural engineer
Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on the building structure may be resisted by a combination of
the lateral load capacity of the drilled shafts and the passive pressure acting against
the near vertical faces of the footings grade beams Passive earth pressure against
the near vertical faces of the footings grade beam may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 and 150 pounds per square foot per foot of depth
pcf for above and below groundwater conditions respectively Additional lateral load
resistance may be provided by frictional resistance developed between the floor slabs
and the underlying cushion fill materials A coefficient of friction of 0 45 may be used
between the concrete floor slabs and the cushion fill materials
The lateral load resistance of drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the drilled shaft allowable deflection at the top of the
drilled shaft and the induced moment in the drilled shaft The lateral load analyses
were performed using the program LPILE plus for Windows which is a
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference laterally loaded deep foundation
program originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin The program
solves for deflection and bending moment along the deep foundation under lateral
loads as a function of depth

Foundation Settlements
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundation will occur as a result of compression of the
drilled shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the subsurface
soil conditions described in this report Total drilled shaft settlements are estimated
to be on the order of less than 1 inch Differential settlements between columns
supported on drilled shafts are estimated to be less than 0 5 inches We believe that
a significant portion of the settlement is elastic and should occur as the loads are
applied Post construction foundation settlements should be approximately 0 5 inches
or less
Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
In general the performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractors method of installation and construction procedures The following
conditions would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled
shaft foundations
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the frictional
resistance between the shaft and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling
the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the drilled holes The drilled shafts are
designed to be embedded into the stiff clayey soils encountered in the borings The
drilled shaft contractor will need to demonstrate that the proposed drilling equipment
and coring tools where appropriate will be capable of installing the drilled shafts to
the recommended depths and dimensions by performance of a trial shaft program
Based on our field explorations the project site is underlain by loose and or soft
lagoonal deposits extending to depths of approximately 30 to 40 feet below the
existing ground surface Due to the loose and or soft consistency of these materials
caving in and or sloughing of these materials will likely occur during the drilling
operations To reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes temporary casing
of the drilled holes will be required during the drilled shaft installation Care should be
GEOLABS INC
W O 3792 10 Page 15
exercised during removal of the temporary casing to reduce the potential for necking
of the drilled shaft Therefore a minimum 5 foot head of concrete above the bottom
of the casing or adequate concrete head to counter hydrostatic pressures should be
maintained during removal of the casing The anticipated shallow groundwater level
at the project site may also pose construction difficulties since proper observation of
the sides and bottoms of the drilled shaft may not be possible
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is generally not recommended unless
specifically approved by Geolabs upon evaluation of the type of drilling fluids
proposed Because of the shallow groundwater table anticipated within the depths
of the drilled shaft excavations concrete placement by tremie methods will be
required during construction of the drilled shafts under such conditions The concrete
should be placed in a suitable manner by displacing the water in an upward fashion
from the bottom of the drilled hole A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 7 to
9 inch slump range should be used to provide close contact between the drilled
shafts and the surrounding soils The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner
to reduce the potential for segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix In
addition the concrete should be placed promptly after drilling within 24 hours to
reduce the potential for softening of the sides of the drilled holes
It is imperative that a representative from Geolabs be present at the site to observe
the drilling and installation of drilled shafts during construction Although the drilled
shafts are designed based primarily on skin friction the bottom of the drilled hole
should be relatively free of loose materials prior to placement of concrete Therefore
observation of the drilled shaft installation operations by Geolabs is necessary to
confirm the assumed subsurface conditions and should be designated a Special
Inspection item

commencement of foundation construction

Based on the granular nature of the compressible soil deposits encountered at the
project site we believe that a settlement waiting period of about 4 to 6 weeks should be
incorporated into the construction schedule for the subject development The settlement
waiting period should commence after completion of the fill placement It should be
recognized that it is difficult to accurately predict the exact time required for the filled ground
to settle since the settlement rates will be affected by variations of the subsoil structure and

Dewatering Method
Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered at the project site dewatering
with cut off walls such as interlocking sheet piles may be considered However
sumps will be needed to collect water that percolates up into the base of the
excavation or infiltrates through the sheet piles The sheet piles should be driven to
a sufficient depth to reduce the potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce
the amount of dewatering within the excavations in areas underlain by granular
subsurface soils with high permeability Use of an interlocking sheet pile shoring
support system should allow the groundwater levels outside the excavations to be
maintained close to the original pre construction levels Therefore some type of
groundwater control requirement should be specified in the contract documents

C The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were previously explored by drilling and
sampling three borings Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about 80 5 to
101 5 feet below the existing ground surface Three deep borings designated as Boring
Nos 4 through 6 were drilled to depths of about 101 5 feet below the existing ground surface
during our current field exploration In addition Boring No 7 was drilled to a depth of about
26 5 feet and was converted to a monitoring well for groundwater monitoring and sampling
purposes The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with hollow stem
augers and rotary wash methods
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 7
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
_ ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX D
Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the in situ soils was evaluated by performing eight falling head
permeability tests in Boring Nos 4 and 7 at various depths in the borings The drilled borings
had a diameter of about 4 inches and the tests were conducted at depths of about 14 to
25 4 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of testing
The falling head method was performed by filling the boreholes with water and
allowing the water to infiltrate into the ground The holes were cased to the full depth of the
boring at the time of the test The water levels and corresponding times were recorded for
the calculation of subsurface permeability The results of the permeability tests conducted
are presented on Plates D 1 through D 8
