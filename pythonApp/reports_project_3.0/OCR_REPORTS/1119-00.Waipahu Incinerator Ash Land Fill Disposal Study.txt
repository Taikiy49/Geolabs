As requested we have performed a soil investigation of the
proposed Waipahu incinerator ash land fill material to be
disposed of at Makalena Golf Course
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
It is our understanding that certain areas of the golf course
have experienced salt intrusion problems which restrict the
growth of grass in the area One solution to the problem being
considered is to use the incinerator ash from the neighboring
Waipahu Incinerator as a source of capillary break and site
fill material above the lowlying areas within the existing golf
course This would also dispose of the incinerator ash

The purpose of our investigation was to explore the existing
subsurface ash fill conditions at the Waipahu Incinerator ash
land fill to evaluate the Suitability of the ash as a capillary
break material
Our work was done in general accordance with our proposal of
Our study consisted of the following
1 Two to 26 5 feet deep in the existing
ash land fill next to the Waipahu Incinerator
2 Three percolation tests in the drilled borings
3 Laboratory soil testing including grain size
analysis and a simulated capillary test
ASH LAND FILL CONDITION
Field Exploration
Two borings were drilled in the existing ash land fill area in
order to determine the condition of the in situ material
GEOLABS HAWAII
The two borings indicated that the ash fill material consists
primarily of glass and metal fragments with some gravel and
sand The fill has been compacted with a dozer and it is
generally in a loose to medium density condition
Laboratory Testing
The laboratory soil tests indicate that the particles are
mostly sand size with little fines The sieve analyses on the
recovered ash samples indicated that according to the grain
size distribution the material contains about 30 fine sands
and silts which is the critical particle size for capillary
action The moisture contents of the ash material was found to
be relatively low reflecting high glass and metal content
A capillary test was simulated on the ash fill sample In the
boring a relatively undisturbed sample of the ash material was
obtained in a clear plastic cylindrical sampling tube In the
laboratory the bottom end of the plastic tube with the reco
vered ash sample was then immersed in a shallow pan with
colored water The capillary rise of the colored water could
then be monitored from the outside of the tube The sample was
left in the water for a period of 6 days with no visible
capillary rise noted

Percolation Testing
The percolation tests performed in the borings indicate moder
ate percolation rates quite similar to the results generally
obtained from a sandy silt material
The log of borings laboratory test and percolation test
results are presented in the appendices of this report
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The capillary potential of a soil generally increases with de
creasing particle size Therefore silts and fine sands are
particularly susceptible to capillary action due to the small
particle size and corresponding void spaces An effective
capillary break material would then be an open graded gravel
material with large void spaces which would prevent capillary
rise action
Based on the field exploration and testing the ash fill
material can generally be classified as a Silty sand or sandy
Silt These soils are generally marginal as an effective
capillary break material due to the Significant amount of fine
sands and silts It is our belief that capillary rise on the
order of 1 to 3 feet can be anticipated from these ash soils
The actual capillary rise experienced depends on many other
factors such as composition of particles and the degree of
compaction or density of the material
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1119 00 Page 5
Our simulated laboratory capillary test did not produce a
visible capillary rise in the recovered undisturbed ash
sample This may indicate that the relatively loose condition
of the ash produces void spaces which are large enough to
restrict the capillary rise
Based on the aforementioned it appears that although the ash
material contains sufficient amount of fine sands and silt size
particles to prevent it from being completely capillary free
we believe the total height of capillary rise may be limited
Therefore there is a possibility that if a minimum thickness
of 4 to 5 feet of ash is utilized and the material is placed
with low compaction then the rise of the salt water due to
capillary action may not reach the ground surface However it
should be pointed out that since it is very difficult to pre
dict the actual height of capillary rise in the field there
will be some risk when using the ash material to attempt to
eliminate the salt water intrusion particularly if the
underlying ocean water level is close by
A more effective but more costly method to prevent capillary
rise in these conditions would be to utilize a layer of clean
gravel such as 3B Fine a minimum of 8 inches thick over the
affected area Prior to filling over the clean gravel with
soil or ash a choker layer of 6 inches of base course rock

should be placed over the clean gravel to prevent the fines of
the upper fill from washing into the gravel Soil or ash can
then be placed over the base course rock layer

APPENDIX C
PERCOLATION TESTS
Falling head and constant head percolation tests were
performed in the bore holes
The holes were first drilled to about 5 foot depth The
falling head tests were performed by filling the holes with
water then allowing the water to percolate through the ash
fill The drop in water level with time was monitored and
recorded
The constant head test was performed by filling the
hole with water then continuously adding water to keep the
hole filled The rate of inflow necessary to keep the hole
filled was recorded
The test results are presented on Plates C 1 thru C 3
