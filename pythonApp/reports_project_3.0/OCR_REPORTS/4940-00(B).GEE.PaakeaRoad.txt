FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Detailed discussion of our geotechnical engineering recommendations for design of
bridge foundations pavements and site preparation are presented in the following
sections of this report
Bridge Foundations
Based on the anticipated structural loads and the subsoil conditions encountered
at the project site we recommend that the new bridge structure be supported by a
foundation system consisting of 36 inch diameter cast in place concrete drilled shafts
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally from
skin friction between the shafts and the upper stiff clayey soils and underlying soft to
medium hard coral formation The end bearing component of the drilled shafts has
been discounted due to difficulties associated with obtaining a clean bottom below the
groundwater level during construction

The vertical compressive load capacity used in the analyses is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as those

the allowable compressive load capacity Drilled shafts should have a minimum

Uplift Load Resistance
Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead weight of the drilled shaft
and shear along the shaft surface and the adjacent soils In general an uplift load
capacity of up to 250 kips per shaft may be used for the drilled shafts The uplift
load capacity of the drilled shaft provided is intended for transient load applications
Therefore the uplift load capacity should not be further increased for transient load
applications For sustained uplift loads we recommend that the uplift load capacity
provided above be reduced by 50 percent to resist sustained loads The structural
capacity of the drilled shaft in tension should be checked by the project structural
engineer

Foundation Settlements
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundations will result primarily from elastic
compression of the drilled shaft and the subgrade response We estimate that
the total settlement of the drilled shaft supported foundation to be less than
1 inch with differential settlements between drilled shafts not exceeding about
one half that amount We believe that these settlements are essentially elastic
and should occur as the loads are applied
Construction Considerations
The performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures As a result of these
potential variations we recommend that a representative from Geolabs be
present to observe the installation of drilled shafts during construction In our
opinion the following may have a significant impact on the effectiveness and cost
of the drilled shaft foundations
Although dense boulders were not encountered in the borings drilled at the
proposed bridge foundation locations during our field exploration there exists the
potential for presence of boulders embedded in the alluvium colluvium deposits
Therefore the drilled shaft contractor will need to have the appropriate equipment
and tools to drill through these obstructions where encountered Appropriate
measures will also be needed to avoid dislodging boulders into the drilled shaft hole
during the drilling and shaft installation process
In addition the drilled shafts are designed to be embedded into the stiff clayey soils
and hard coral formation encountered at depth Therefore coring into the hard
coral formation will be required The drilled shaft contractor will need to have the
appropriate drilling equipment and coring tools capable of installing the drilled
shafts to the recommended depths and dimensions
Workmanship
The load carrying capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes

Structure Approach Slabs
As previously indicated fills of up to about 10 feet thick will be required at the
abutment areas of the new concrete bridge To reduce the potential for abrupt differential
settlements between the drilled shaft supported bridge structure and the new compacted
fills we recommend that structure approach slabs be provided at the abutment locations
The structure approach slabs should be supported on a minimum of 8 inches of
aggregate subbase course placed on a prepared subgrade The subgrade should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction The
aggregate subbase course should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density

clayey soils with localized areas exposing coralline materials Laboratory testing
performed on the clayey soils generally indicated a relatively low CBR value of less than
5 and a swell of over 10 percent Based on the field exploration and laboratory test
results it is our opinion that the following new pavement sections may be used for
preliminary design and cost estimating purposes

Where expansive clayey soils are encountered at or near the bottom of the
base course layer the pavement subgrade should be over excavated to the coral layer or
to provide the 18 inch aggregate subbase layer as recommended above The
over excavated subgrade should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction If coralline deposits are exposed at or near
the bottom of the base course layer the subgrade should be proof rolled by a minimum

aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to not less than

above sections The recommended sections assume that good drainage will be provided

Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the anticipated design finish grades for the
proposed roadway improvements we anticipate that the majority of the grading work
will consist of new fills of up to about 10 feet thick along the areas leading up to the
abutment locations of the new bridge structure In general the area within the contract
grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed at the on set of earthwork
Vegetation debris deleterious material and other unsuitable materials should be
removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during
clearing below any areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be
over excavated to expose firm and or stiff material and the resulting excavation should
be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic
soils should be properly disposed of off site
Existing structures and pavements that are to be demolished should be
completely removed Over excavation resulting from the demolition should be backfilled
with compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should be
removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with granular fill
material moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch
level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Utilities that are to be abandoned in place under the proposed structures should be
backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure
After clearing grubbing and demolition future pavement areas should be
excavated where necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the base
course or subbase layer In areas where expansive clay soils are encountered at the
pavement subgrade level the pavement subgrade should be scarified to a depth of
about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base course subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the
pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water
ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction Where concrete
curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we
recommend that the curbs extend a minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soils below
the base and subbase course layers to reduce migration of landscape water into the
pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect
excessive water from landscaping irrigation For long term performance we
recommend that a subdrain system be considered along the sides of the roadway

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths ranging from
about 6 5 to 80 feet below the existing ground surface A bulk sample designated as
Bulk 1 was obtained of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics The approximate locations of the test borings and bulk sample are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling
equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches n
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock coral formations encountered at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense rock formation with a
high RQD would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous
methods of excavation

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct
shear tests are presented on Plates B 1 and B 2
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio Test ASTM D 1883 was performed on
a bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The test results are presented on Plate B 3
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationship The test results are presented on Plate B 4
