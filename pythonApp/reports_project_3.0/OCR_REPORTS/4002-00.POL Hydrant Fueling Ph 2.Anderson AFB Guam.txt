INTRODUCTION

for the Replace POL Hydrant Fueling System Phase 2 project located within Andersen Air
Force Base in Guam Mariana Islands The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Vicinity Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations and
site grading only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Guam is the largest and southernmost of the Mariana Island archipelago
which is an island arc parallel to the trend of the subduction zone forming the Mariana Trench
The Mariana Trench is part of a belt of trenches which extend from Japan to the Palau Islands
and include the Japan Trench Mariana Trench Yap Trench and Palau Trench
The Island of Guam can be broadly divided into three geologic provinces the southern
mountains the central mountains and the northern plateau The formation of the island
started during the Eocene Epoch by the eruption of basaltic rhyolite lavas The mountainous
central portion of the island is composed of these Eocene volcanic rocks and conglomerates
tuffs and limestones of Eocene and Oligocene in age Lava flows and limestone formations
of Miocene Epoch make up the majority of the southern one third of the island The plateaus
of the northern portion of the island were formed while this part of the island was still beneath
the sea level During the Oligocene and Miocene Epochs a complex series of coral and algal
reefs formed an elongated platform of limestones and related deposits Tectonic activity has
lifted these coral algal limestones above sea level to form the northern Guam Plateau The
project site is situated in a relatively level portion of the northern plateau which forms the main
portion of Andersen Air Force Base
Weathering and solution working of the limestone has partially re crystallized the
calcium carbonate of the limestone forming massive limestones in some areas and poorly to
well cemented detrital limestones in others Typically the detrital limestones are highly
variable in strength characteristics and are frequently karstic or cavernous in nature
In addition the limestone formations are generally porous Except during heavy storms the
majority of the precipitation naturally percolates through the formation producing little or no
surface water runoff
Typically the surface of the underlying limestone is irregular due to solution working
Frequently formations such as those encountered at the project site will have buried pinnacles
of massive limestone with spaces between the pinnacles filled with weathered material and

detrital material The surface soil horizon on the limestone plateau is very thin generally less
than 50 mm thick
GEOLOGIC AND SEISMIC HAZARDS EVALUATION
Guam is designated as Seismic Zone 3 by the Uniform Building Code UBC 1994
However we understand that Seismic Zone 4 criteria will be used for design of this project
Therefore we conducted an evaluation of possible geologic and seismic hazards that may
impact the development of the proposed project An evaluation of geologic and seismic
hazards typically addresses surface fault rupture ground shaking ground failure liquefaction
lateral spreading and ground lurching tsunamis and seiches and seismically induced
flooding
As previously discussed Guam is situated adjacent to the subduction zone that forms
the Mariana Trench The island is tectonically active and has experienced approximately
20 earthquakes exceeding magnitude 7 0 since 1902 Of these events fifteen occurred prior
to 1950 and five have occurred subsequent to that date Several large faults and fault zones
that traverse across the Island of Guam include the following Pago Bay Adelup Point Fault
River Valley Fault Zone Tamuning Barrigada Fault Pugua Fault and
Mount Santa Rosa horst block
The proximity of the project site to faults and fault zones is a major consideration when
evaluating geologic and seismic hazards Of the major faults and fault zones mentioned
above the trace of the Pugua Fault lies closest to the project site at approximately 8 kilometers
to the west of the project site The Pugua Fault stretches from off shore near Uruno Point and
extends south to about Taguac Several minor faults located about 3 kilometers south of the
project site are also present
Based on our evaluation of the project site we do not believe that the geologic and
seismic hazards described above are significant considerations for this project With the
exception of moderate to strong ground shaking we believe that the potential for surface fault

CLIMATE
The Island of Guam is located in the North Pacific Ocean between 144 degrees
36 minutes and 144 degrees 58 minutes longitude and 13 degrees 13 minutes and 13 degrees
40 minutes latitude as shown on the Project Location Vicinity Map Plate 1 In general the
climate of Guam is warm and humid with little variation in temperature from month to month
Based on information obtained from the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration
NOAA Guam s mean annual temperature is about 27 degrees Celsius throughout the year
Typically annual rainfall averages from about 2 000 to per year The rainy season
is during months of July through November when monthly rainfall can average over 300 mm
per month
Tropical storms and typhoons often pass through or near the Island of Guam Tropical
storms are defined as closed pressure systems with wind speeds ranging from 60 to
120 kilometers per hour Typhoons are similar phenomena with wind speeds exceeding
120 kilometers per hour During the rainy season tropical storms and typhoons are common
in the vicinity of Guam Tropical storms and typhoons bring high intensity rainfall when their
zone of influence is near Guam In extreme events in the past a typhoon has produced winds
of over 280 kilometers per hour over 3 5 meters of inundation and 650 mm of rainfall in one
day

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered limestone formations at or near the existing
ground surface Where surface soils were encountered the thickness of the soils fills was
limited to less than 1 meter The fills generally consisted of very stiff sandy silts and dense
coralline sands and gravel The limestone formations encountered in our borings were
generally severely fractured and ranged from soft to medium hard soft rock is harder than
very stiff to hard soil However the borings indicated that localized hard and crystallized
areas are present in the limestone formations
Considering the relatively dense near surface fills and the limestone formations
encountered at the site we recommend that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread
and or continuous footings be used to support the proposed structures The shallow
foundations should bear on the limestone formation and or compacted structural fill
However it should be noted that limestone formations are typically karstic or cavernous in
nature Therefore we recommend that a program of probing and grouting at the proposed
aboveground fuel tanks be conducted during construction considering the critical nature of the
tank structures The intent of the probing and grouting program is to reduce the potential for
possible loss in foundation support from collapse of underground cavities The probing and
grouting of the foundations for the supporting facilities such as the pump house will not be
required
We anticipate that a substantial volume of trench excavation will be required for
installation of the new fuel lines As mentioned earlier limestone formations underlying the
project site are typically severely fractured and soft to medium hard Based on our field
exploration we believe that the majority of the limestone formation at the project site may be

Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe that
the underlying limestone formation or structural fill needed to raise the site would provide
suitable foundation support for the proposed fuel tanks and pump house We believe that a
shallow foundation system consisting of spread footings and or continuous footings may be
used to support the fuel tanks and other structures
An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for design
of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the limestone formation and or compacted
structural fill This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces In general
footings should be embedded a minimum of 500 mm below the lowest adjacent finished grade
If foundations are located next to utility trenches they should be embedded below a 45 degree
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench As an alternative
to embedding the footings deeper the trench should be backfilled with concrete to reduce the
potential for appreciable foundation settlement
Due to the potential for presence of cavities as commonly encountered in limestone
formations we recommend that a program of probing and grouting at the proposed fuel tanks
be conducted during construction A qualified geotechnical engineer should observe the
probing and grouting operations to evaluate the extent of cavities if encountered Probing and
grouting requirements are further discussed in the Foundation Probing section of this report
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with the
recommendations presented herein total settlements of footings are estimated to be on the
order of 25 mm with differential settlements on the order of about 15 mm or less
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance between
the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure
developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings A coefficient
of friction of 0 45 may be used for footings bearing on the limestone formation or compacted

Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area voids and or cavities may be present in the
limestone formations encountered at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of
foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities below a footing we recommend that
a program of cavity probing and grouting be conducted during construction for the proposed
fuel tanks considering the critical nature of these tanks
For estimating purposes we recommend that probeholes be drilled along the
perimeter ring foundation spaced at approximately 3 meters on center In addition we believe
that a series of probeholes with a spacing of about 3 meters on center should be drilled
starting at the center of the tank and radiating out to the perimeter in about 3 meter increments
in radius The probeholes should be at least 75 mm in diameter and should extend to at least
3 meters below the planned bottom of footing elevation If cavities and or voids are
encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probeholes should be
drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or
void The probeholes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout
injected at low to moderate pressures A typical sand cement grout should have a slump of
about 150 to 200 mm
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during bidding
In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting operations
are completed The contractor should also be made aware that a longer lag time between

schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
qualified geotechnical engineer The probing and grouting program should be monitored for

take and or changed conditions are observed
Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed pump house
building In general the subgrades for slabs on grades should be scarified to a depth of at
least 200 mm moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted
to not less than 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
We consider the slabs on grade for the pump house building to be industrial type slabs
Therefore we recommend that the concrete slabs on grade be supported on a minimum
150 mm cushion fill to provide more uniform support under the slabs The cushion fill layer
should consist of aggregate subbase course compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction For design of structural slabs bearing on the aggregate subbase course a
modulus of subgrade reaction of per square meter per meter of deflection
kN m may be used in the design

compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum
dry density
Excavation
The proposed project will involve the installation of approximately 4 890 lineal meters
of stainless steel pipes We understand that the average fuel line invert will generally
be located approximately 2 5 meters below the existing ground surface Therefore we
anticipate that excavations of up to about 3 meters in depth may be necessary for
installation of the new fuel lines
Based on our field exploration the proposed site is underlain by a limestone formation
near the existing ground surface The limestone formation encountered in our borings
appeared to be severely fractured and ranged from soft to medium hard Our borings
indicated that the limestone formation generally breaks down into gravelly sands and
sandy gravel It is anticipated that the majority of the limestone formation may be
excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such as excavating with large
backhoe excavators However limestone formations typically contain localized hard
and crystallized zones Therefore we anticipate that difficult excavation conditions may
arise during construction at localized areas It may be necessary to excavate the
trenches in some areas by using hoe ram or by chipping

observation of the existing site and field data from the borings performed at the site

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings it appears that most
of the cut slopes would expose the existing fills consisting of sandy silts with coral
gravel or limestone formations In general we believe that cut slopes may be designed
with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Permanent fill slopes constructed of
controlled fill or structural fill should also be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V
or flatter
Fills to be placed on slopes with inclinations steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and
benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The
filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal
compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations Fill
slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio
to obtain a well compacted slope face Water should be diverted away from the tops
of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the
potential for erosion of the finished slopes
Utility Lines
A large portion of the proposed project involves the installation of about 4 890 lineal
meters of fuel lines We understand that the fuel lines will consist of stainless steel pipes
which require special sand bedding and special handling requirements Therefore the
discussions presented in this section of the report may not be fully applicable to the stainless
steel pipes

It is anticipated that most of the trenches for utilities will be excavated in the surface fills
and limestone formations We understand that installation of other utilities may also be
required for the project We envision that these utility lines will consist of concrete or ductile
iron pipe materials In general granular bedding consisting of 150 mm of crushed stone gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended below the pipes Free draining granular
materials such as crushed stone gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for
the initial trench backfill up to about 300 mm above the pipes This is to provide adequate
support around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill thus reducing the possibility
of damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill above the crushed stone gravel should consist
of controlled fill material generally less than 75 mm in maximum size compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction Where trenches will be located in paved areas the upper
1 meter of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction
Selected samples of the subsurface soils retrieved from our field exploration were

tests are provided on Plate B 1 of Appendix B The minimum resistivity values of the materials
tested ranged from 6 490 to 32 600 ohm cm Based on the results of the minimum resistivity
tests conducted the on site materials may be classified as low to non corrosive to steel and
metallic pipes in direct contact with the subsurface materials The generally low chloride and
sulfate contents of the soils tested also indicate that the soils are generally not corrosive to
concrete substructures However normal precautionary measures such as minimum
concrete cover over the reinforcing bars should be observed for all concrete substructures
Drainage
The finished grades outside the new structures should be sloped to shed water away
from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that
gutter systems be installed around the building structures and that the discharge be diverted
away from the foundation and slab areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations

and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration into the

The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab
edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration In
addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained
to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
21 borings designated as Boring Nos 101 through 121 extending to depths of approximately
1 8 to 14 8 meters below the existing ground surface In addition two core samples
designated as Boring Nos 122 and 123 were obtained from selected locations of the parking
aprons along the taxiway to measure the thickness of the existing asphaltic concrete
pavement The approximate locations of the borings and core samples are shown on the
Site Plans Plates 3 1 and 3 2 The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling
equipment and the core samples were obtained using a portable coring machine
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 23
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 75 mm
OD Modified California sampler with a 63 5 kilogram hammer falling 760 mm In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
50 mm OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the last 300 mm of a 450 mm drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the limestone formations encountered at the site were obtained using
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses
a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring
attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if of material is
recovered from a core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 80

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 100 mm in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 750 mm of sound material is recovered from a core run the RQD would
be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical Handbook
of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Eight sets of and minimum resistivity tests were
performed by E L Pacific on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration to
evaluate the corrosivity of the in situ soils to buried metallic substructures In addition four
sets of chloride contents and sulfate contents were performed by
E L Pacific to evaluate the corrosivity of the in situ soils to buried concrete in contact with the
soils The results of these tests are summarized on Plate B 1
Twenty two sieve analysis tests including hydrometer tests ASTM D 422 were
performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and
to aid in soil classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on
Plates B 2 through B 6
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample to evaluate
the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct shear test are
presented on Plate B 7
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils
The test results are presented on Plate B 8
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk sample
of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content relationship The
test results are presented on Plate B 9
