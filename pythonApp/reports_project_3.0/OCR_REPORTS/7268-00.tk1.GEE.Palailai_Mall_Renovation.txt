In accordance with our fee proposal dated July 27, 2015, we performed a 
geotechnical engineering exploration for the Palailai Mall Renovation project in the 
Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. This letter report summarizes the findings 
from our field exploration and laboratory testing and presents our recommendations for 
pavement design. It should be noted that the recommendations provided herein are 
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located at the existing Palailai Mall in the Kapolei area on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. The existing pedestrian mall is generally bounded by 
Kamokila Boulevard to the north, James Campbell Building to the east, Haumea Street 
to the south, and Bankoh Hale O Kapolei Building to the west. 
Based on the information provided, the existing pedestrian mall is surfaced with 
concrete pavers, which are showing signs of distresses, such as cracking and settling.  
Therefore, it is desired to renovate the pedestrian mall by removing the existing 
concrete pavers and repaving with new concrete pavements, including a fire lane.

of the surface and subsurface conditions to develop a soil/rock data set to formulate 
geotechnical engineering recommendations for the pavement design. To accomplish this, 
we conducted an exploration program consisting of the following tasks and work efforts:

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling three borings, designated 
as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, each to a depth of about 11.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface.  The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

The existing concrete pavers were observed to be about 2.25 inches thick and 
underlain by approximately 2 to 3 inches of sand leveling course.  Approximately 19 to 
21 inches of dense sandy gravel, believed to be base course, was encountered below the 
sand leveling course.  Below the base course layer, the borings generally encountered 
fills, older alluvium, and saprolites, consisting of stiff to hard clayey silts and silty clays, 
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 11.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow drilled borings at the time of our 
field exploration.  In general, the site is at an elevation several tens of feet above the 
highest basal groundwater table in the area.  However, groundwater levels can 
significantly vary depending on rainfall, temperature, surface runoff, groundwater 
withdrawal, and other factors.

presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3 of Appendix A.  Results of the 
laboratory tests performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration are

GEOTECHNCIAL ENGINEERING RECOMMENDATIONS 
The deterioration of the concrete pavers appears to be due to the lack of 
confinement and interlocking of the paving blocks.  Some of the individual concrete 
pavers could be lifted out by hand.  It appears that vehicle traffic could cause “rocking” 
and loosening of the individual paving blocks, resulting in cracking and settling of the 
concrete pavers. 
Our field exploration indicated that the subgrade soil conditions are suitable for 
pavement support.  It appears that there is about 20 inches of dense sandy gravel base 
course type material below the sand leveling course.  Based on our evaluation, it is our 
opinion that the concrete pavers may be replaced with a concrete pavement placed 
over the recompacted existing base course material.  The sand leveling course should 
be removed and disposed of off-site.  Detailed discussions of our geotechnical 
engineering recommendations are presented in the following sections. 
Pavement Design 
The concrete pavers and sand leveling course should be removed to expose the 
underlying base course layer.  Considering the new pavements will be subjected to light 
vehicular use and occasional fire truck traffic, it is our opinion that the following new 
pavement section may be used for the pedestrian mall renovation project: 
6.0 Inches Portland Cement Concrete over Existing Base Course Material 
Recompacted to a Minimum of 95 Percent Relative Compaction 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with

Site Preparation 
We anticipate minimal site grading work will be required for the proposed 
pavement reconstruction project.  At the on-set of earthwork, the areas within the 
contract grading limits should be cleared and grubbed thoroughly. The existing concrete 
pavers and sand leveling course should be removed to expose the underlying base 
course material.  Soft and yielding areas encountered below pavement areas should be 
over-excavated to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be 
backfilled with base course material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction.  Additional base course material required for the new pavements should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in maximum 
8-inch level loose lifts, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for 
over-excavation and engineered fill placement to account for variations in the 
over-excavation quantities.

Based on our experience in the Kapolei area, differential slab movements 
between adjacent concrete pours have been observed on several occasions. We 
believe this may be attributed to the lack of maintenance of the subgrade moisture 
content prior to the subsequent concrete pours. It should be noted that moisture 
conditioning of the clayey subgrades is an important requirement considering the 
expansive nature of the on-site clayey soils. Therefore, the subgrade soils (including the 
overlying base course material) underneath the concrete slabs should be properly 
moisture-conditioned and maintained moist until the placement of concrete. In order to 
reduce the potential for differential slab movement, we recommend abutting slabs (from 
different concrete pours) be structurally connected with dowels or other structural 
connections.  In addition, the subsoils within the tree wells should be adequately soaked 
to induce swelling of the expansive clayey soils prior to the concrete placement.

whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and scarification 
process and to confirm whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to those 
encountered in our field exploration.

Construction Monitoring 
We recommend retaining Geolabs to provide geotechnical engineering services 
during construction of the proposed project. The critical items of construction monitoring 
that require "Special Inspection" include observation of excavation and subgrade 
preparation and placement and compaction of the fill materials. A Geolabs representative 
should monitor other aspects of earthwork construction to observe compliance with the 
intent of the design concepts, specifications, and/or recommendations, and to expedite

conditions differ from those anticipated at the time this report was prepared. The 
recommendations provided herein are contingent upon such observations. 
If actual exposed subsurface conditions encountered during construction differ 
from those assumed or considered in this report, Geolabs should be contacted to review 
and/or revise the geotechnical engineering recommendations presented herein. 
LIMITATIONS 
The findings and discussions submitted herein are based, in part, upon information 
obtained from the drilled borings. Variations of subsurface conditions between and beyond 
the borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become 
evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be 
necessary to re-evaluate the findings and discussions provided herein. 
The locations of the field borings indicated herein are approximate, having been 
estimated by taping from visible features shown on the Site Utilities Plan transmitted by 
Engineering Concepts, Inc. on November 16, 2015.  The locations of the field borings 
should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method used. 
The stratification lines shown on graphic representations of the borings depict the 
approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual transition. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. It must be noted that groundwater level changes may occur due to seasonal 
precipitation, surface runoff, and other factors.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, each extending to a depth of about 11.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill 
rig equipped with continuous flight, solid-stem augers. The approximate boring locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in 
general accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch outside diameter (OD) Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 
30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general 
accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by 
driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The 
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three One-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples 
of the near-surface clayey soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the subsurface soils.  
The results of these tests are summarized on Plate B-1. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected samples 
collected to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. Test results are presented on 
Plate B-2. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the in-situ clayey soils to evaluate their pavement support 
characteristics.  The test results are presented on Plates B-3 and B-4.
