SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, our borings drilled for the roadway reconstruction project encountered 
approximately 0 to 4 inches of AC underlain by highly variable soils consisting of volcanic 
ash, clinker, fill, alluvium, saprolite (extremely weathered basalt formation) and basalt 
formation extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 6.9 feet below the 
existing pavement surface. The volcanic ash encountered generally consisted of medium 
stiff sandy silt. The clinker encountered consisted of medium dense to very dense silty 
gravel with some cobbles. The fill encountered generally consisted of medium dense 
sandy gravel and very stiff silty clay. The alluvium encountered generally consisted of very 
stiff to hard silty clay and medium dense to dense silty gravel. The saprolite encountered 
consisted of medium dense to dense silty gravel, very dense gravelly cobbles and very stiff 
clayey silt. A slightly weathered basalt formation was encountered in one of the drilled 
borings at a depth of about 4 feet below the existing ground surface.  
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. It should be noted that groundwater level may vary with surface runoffs, 
seasonal rainfall, time of the year, and other factors. 
Our field survey indicated that majority of the areas of Piilani Highway under 
consideration for this project appear to exist in moderate to poor conditions. Based on our 
engineering evaluation and construction considerations, we believe that Piilani Highway 
within the project limits may be reconstructed with a new pavement structural section 
consisting of 2.5 inches of AC on 4.0 inches of ACB over 6 inches of aggregate subbase 
course.   
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface 
should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off 
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
pavement design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering analyses performed for the Piilani Highway Pavement Reconstruction 
project in Kaupo on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the reconstruction of the existing pavements only. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
Based on the information provided, the scope of work for this project involves the 
reconstruction of Piilani Highway in Kaupo, from Milepost 34.8 to Milepost 35.8, where 
there are sections of extremely deteriorated asphalt pavement and only gravel. We 
understand that it is desired to conduct soil borings along the roadway and to determine 
if a more efficient pavement section can be utilized, which would be equivalent to the 
County’s typical pavement section for major collector rural road (Class “A”: 2½” 
AC/+5” ATB/+8” aggregate subbase).  
Special attention was given to the potential presence of near-surface soft and/or 
loose soils, which may be a contributing factor to the pavement failures observed along 
portions of the roadway under consideration. The findings obtained from our field 
exploration and laboratory testing programs were used as a basis to formulate 
geotechnical engineering recommendations for the pavement reconstruction of the 
project roadway.

obtain information on the surface and subsurface conditions along the roadway 
alignment through literature research, past project experience, site reconnaissance, and

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene 
Epoch). The project site is located in the Kipahulu area on the southeastern flank of the 
Haleakala Volcano. 
The Kipahulu District of Maui was created by lava flows from the Kula Volcanic 
Series and Hana Volcanic Series. A long period of volcanic dormancy followed in this 
region of Maui after cessation of the Kula Volcanic Series about 150,000 years ago. 
During this period, the Kipahulu Valley generally was carved by stream erosion. 
Subsequently, new volcanic activity (about 13,000 to 30,000 years ago) subjected the 
valley to two series of lava flows (the early and late Hana Volcanic Series). 
The basalt formation of the lava flows generally is underlain by a veneer of 
stream deposited alluvial soils. The alluvial soils may consist of a poorly sorted mixture 
of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines. The alluvial soils may also include cinder 
sands and volcanic ash materials from upslope areas.  
2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The existing Piilani Highway within the project limits is generally a very narrow 
two-lane undivided roadway. The approximate limits of the roadway extend from 
Milepost 34.8 to Milepost 35.8 and are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
There are limited shoulder areas beyond the edge of the roadway, which 
generally consist of cut grass, low profile dry stack and Concrete Rubble Masonry 
(CRM) rock walls, few utility posts and a waterline along the edge of the roadway. The 
surface soils along the shoulder areas below the vegetation generally consist of brown 
sandy gravel with silt and are generally in a damp to dry condition. The existing 
Piilani Highway alignment is generally sloping downwards in an easterly direction within 
the project limits.

Our office conducted a site reconnaissance in March 2023 to collect general 
information for the existing pavement conditions along the portion of the roadway under

The Piilani Highway within the project limits was observed to be in poor to 
extremely severe conditions. Majority of the roadway appeared to be highly distressed 
with structural defects. Where asphalt concrete was present, alligator and alligator block 
cracking were observed to be widespread and generally high in severity. The pavement 
thickness was measured at various locations and found to range from 0.25 to 
1.0 inches. The poor to extremely severe condition areas consist of large portions of the 
asphaltic concrete (AC) pavement missing from the surface. Numerous to continuous 
potholes were observed at various locations along the alignment within the project 
limits. The ride characteristics of the roadway within these subject areas were observed 
to be generally irregular and bumpy in the areas of poor pavements as well as the 
soil/gravel roadway.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement 
areas would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing 
pavements should be performed from an engineering point-of-view. Existing pavements 
with minor distresses and severity may be rehabilitated with an AC overlay with 
cold-planing. However, reconstruction of the existing pavements would be prudent for 
those areas where the observed distresses are likely caused by failures in the 
underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials. Detailed information for the 
following areas would be required in order to provide input design parameters for the 
pavement rehabilitation/reconstruction design and analyses: 
Traffic Index 
Condition of the Existing AC Layer 
Resistance (R) Value of the Subgrade Materials 
We observed pavement failures along majority of portions of Piilani Highway 
within the project limits. Therefore, the pavements within the project limits should be 
reconstructed with a new pavement structural section designed based on a 20-year 
design life in accordance with the guidelines presented in the pavement design manual 
prepared by the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, 
Material Testing and Research Branch. In general, Piilani Highway within the project 
limits may be reconstructed with a new pavement structural section consisting of 
2.5 inches of AC on 4.0 inches of asphalt concrete base (ACB) over 6.0 inches of 
aggregate base course.

3.4 
Design Pavement Section 
As discussed in the “Existing Site/Pavement Conditions” section herein, majority 
of areas within the project limits are in poor conditions with moderate and severe 
distresses likely caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade 
materials. Therefore, Piilani Highway within the project limits should be reconstructed 
with a new pavement section in lieu of an AC overlay section.  
Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicate that the roadway alignment 
under consideration for this project is generally underlain by variable subgrade soils 
ranging from sandy silt, sandy gravel, silty gravel to silty clay.  Based on the laboratory 
test results and the traffic loading data provided, we have determined the following 
flexible pavement design section for the proposed Piilani Highway Pavement 
Reconstruction project. Detailed calculations and analyses of the pavement design are 
presented on Plates C-3 in Appendix C.

The exposed pavement subgrade materials should be proof-rolled with a large 
vibratory drum roller (minimum 10 tons static weight) a minimum of eight passes. The

scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to 
those encountered in our field exploration.

Aggregate subbase course materials required for the project should conform to 
Subsection 703.17 of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge 
Construction (2005).  Aggregate subbase materials should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with AASHTO T-180 or ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Asphalt concrete base material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D2041. Polymer modified 
asphalt (PMA) pavement material should be used for the AC pavement surface course 
and constructed in accordance with Section 401 – Hot Mix Asphalt (HMA) Pavement of 
the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction (2005), 
and its amendments. 
3.5 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the test borings, bulk samples, and laboratory test data. 
Variations of conditions between and beyond the test borings and bulk samples may 
occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until 
construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to 
re-evaluate the recommendations presented herein. 
The test boring locations are approximate, having been obtained using a 
hand-held Global Position System (GPS) device and shown on the Google Earth Pro 
Imagery. Elevations of the field borings were estimated from Google Earth Pro Imagery 
dated January 13, 2013. The physical locations and elevations of the borings should be 
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on the graphic representations of the borings 
depict the approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition. Water level data, if encountered in the borings, were measured at the 
times shown on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. 
These data have been reviewed and interpretations made to formulate this report. 
However, it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall, 
temperature, and other factors.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignment by drilling and 
sampling 6 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending to depths of about 
4.5 to 6.9 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate boring locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket Penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits of the near-surface fine-grained soils. 
The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Four Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships.  The test results are presented on Plates B-3 and B-4.

Ninyo & Moore on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement
