INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed GTE Hawaiian Telephone Hilo Underground Ductline Project

located in downtown Hilo on the island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the

This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These findings and recommendations are intended
for design input only and may not be appropriate to form the basis for construction cost
estimates nor for construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to install a new underground ductline system for the GTE Hawaiian
Telephone Operations Division in downtown Hilo on the island of Hawaii The new
ductline will begin at the GTE Hawaiian Telephone Operations Building located on Kinoole
Street The proposed alignment is planned along Kinoole and Ponahawai Streets and
terminates at the intersection of Kilauea and Ponahawai Streets as shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2 We anticipate that approximately 2 200 lineal feet of new ductlines and nine
underground box structures will be installed for the subject project
We understand that the underground box structures will measure about 6 feet wide
by 12 feet long and will be about 7 feet deep The top of the underground box structure
will be subjected to heavy traffic loading The inverts of the new ductline system are
anticipated to be about 3 to 4 feet below the existing ground surface with portions of the
alignment having deeper inverts for grade adjustments It is anticipated that the typical
ductlines would be encased concrete with pipes embedded in the concrete Special
attention should be given to the excavation shoring and bedding requirements for the new
ductline system

SITE DESCRIPTION
The proposed project site is located in the downtown area of Hilo on the island of
Hawaii The proposed new underground ductline begins at the GTE Hawaiian Telephone
Operations Building near the intersection of Kalakaua and Kinoole Streets The new
ductline extends southeast along Kinoole Street passes Haili and Mamo Streets turns
northeast along Ponahawai Street and terminates at the junction of Ponahawai and
Kilauea Streets
Both Kinoole and Ponahawai Streets are approximately 50 feet wide Three and
four story buildings presently occupy the area along Kinoole and Ponahawai Streets
Elevations of the existing ground surface along the proposed ductline alignment varies
from about 17 to 40 feet Mean Sea Level MSL The proposed ductline alignment
is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These include the following Kohala which has been long extinct Mauna
Kea which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in
1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is underlain by Pahala Ash which is an air laid deposit of volcanic
ash from the Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Loa mountain during the Pleistocene
Epoch The Pahala Ash generally has high in situ moisture contents and low in situ
densities and is characterized as having thixotropic properties i e it is susceptible to
significant loss of strength when remolded under equipment vehicular or seismic loads

drilling and sampling nine borings to depths ranging from 8 to 10 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan

below the volcanic ash deposit in five of the borings at depths of about 8 to 10 feet below

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered approximately 2 feet of granular fill
overlying a deposit of volcanic ash consisting of reddish brown silt to approximately 8
to 10 feet below the existing ground surface Weathered basalt rock was encountered
below the volcanic ash deposit in five of the borings In general groundwater was not
encountered in the borings at the time of our exploration with the exception of one boring
where seepage water was noted at about 91 feet below the surface
The significant geotechnical engineering consideration for this project is the
presence of the soft volcanic ash deposits along the proposed ductline alignment The
volcanic ash_has high in situ moisture contents and has a high shrinkage potential of up
to 40 percent upon drying The high moisture content and soft consistency of the
volcanic ash would pose difficulties during construction of the trench backfill for the
proposed ductline
Excavation of the shallow trenches in the soft volcanic ash may be stable over a
short duration However because of the sensitive nature of the volcanic ash material and
the potential for vibration due to traffic along the roads there is a strong potential that the
trench excavation may become unstable Because of the sensitive nature of the volcanic
ash the relatively shallow ductline trenches may experience some sloughing of the sides
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3163 00 Page 7
and potential instability of the trenches during construction Therefore it may be prudent
to provide shoring of the sides of the excavation during construction of the proposed
ductlines
Trench Excavation
Based on the information available to us the anticipated inverts of the ductline will
vary from about 3 to 4 feet below the existing ground surface with deeper inverts up to
about 7 feet for the underground box structures Based on this information trench
excavations on the order of 5 to 9 feet may be required to place the ductlines and the
bedding below the ductlines In general the method and equipment to be used for
excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety
considerations Based on the subsurface conditions encountered the trenches may be
excavated by conventional equipment unless basalt rock is encountered If basalt rock
is encountered in the trenches heavy construction equipment would be required
Trench Support
Because basalt rock was encountered in five of the borings at depths of about 8
to 10 feet below the surface driving sheetpiles for shoring the trench excavation would
likely encounter refusal conditions when driven to the basalt rock In addition the
proposed ductline alignment is likely to traverse other utility lines therefore it would be
difficult to avoid truncating the existing utility lines if sheetpiles are to be used as shoring
Therefore the trenches should be shored by methods other than sheetpiles
Because of the sensitive nature of the volcanic ash material encountered in our
exploration we believe that the trench excavation would need to be supported during
construction to reduce the potential for excessive ground subsidence due to trench wall
instability In general the contractor should be responsible for the method and installation
of shoring Shoring methods such as trench timber shoring or box shoring may be
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3163 00 Page 8
considered As an alternative trench shields may be used for protection of personnel
during the ductline construction however sloughing of the sides of the trench may still
OCCUr
The shoring and bracing system used to support the trenches must comply with
all applicable safety requirements The adequacy and safety of the shoring and bracing
installation operations should be made the sole responsibility of the contractor His
representative who should be required to continually monitor the excavation work at the
site would therefore have the opportunity to observe changing conditions which might
affect the excavation stability during construction
Even if good construction procedures are followed some movement of the
excavation support bracing system may be anticipated because of changes in earth
stresses during excavation Movements depend greatly on workmanship e g how
quickly and tightly the support is installed It also depends on the types of subsoils the
depth and width of excavation and the type of support In view of the complexity of the
problem it is difficult to accurately estimate the amount of movement to be anticipated
of a bracing system and the adjacent ground Therefore it is important to realize that the
excavation support should be installed as early as practicable and that measurements of
vertical and horizontal movements are required especially in areas where ground
movements may result in damage to existing adjacent structures
The ground adjacent to excavations should be continuously monitored for cracks
or dips and other indications of movements and possible sloughing of the excavation
walls Such monitoring is particularly critical in areas adjacent to existing structures and
utilities

Pipe Bedding
We understand that the proposed ductlines will be encased in concrete To
provide more uniform support for the ductlines encased in concrete bearing on the soft
volcanic ash we recommend that the concrete bank be supported on a cushion fill
consisting of at least 12 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
wrapped in a filter fabric Mirafi 140N or equivalent The cushion fill would also serve as
a working platform A typical trench detail is shown on Plate 3
Backfill
In general trench backfill for the ductline trenches should be placed in accordance
with the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction September 1986 The
zone of backfill extending from the top of the concrete to within 2 feet of the existing
ground surface should consist of compacted select borrow wrapped in a filter fabric
Mirafi 140N The filter fabric is recommended around the compacted select borrow to
reduce the potential for migration of the select borrow into the soft volcanic ash which
is anticipated on the sides of the trench to within 24 inches of the ground surface Above
this level the backfill material should consist of base course material to somewhat match
the existing conditions A filter fabric is not required above this level since we anticipate
that the existing sandy gravel material would provide adequate confinement for the base
course to be placed below the asphalt concrete A typical section of the ductline trench 7
showing the compaction requirements and the requirement for filter fabric placement is
presented on Plate 3
The trench backfill above the concrete should be placed in maximum 10 inch level
loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 95 percent of its maximum dry
density as determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 to reduce
the potential for future ground subsidence The upper 2 feet of the trench backfill base
course also should be compacted to 95 percent of its maximum dry density as
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3163 00 Page 10
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Compaction by water
ponding or jetting should not be allowed
Earth Pressure Loads on Pipe
Loads on buried conduits are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the
pipes the unit weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance between the backfill
material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads on the buried ductline an
average unit weight of 125 pounds per cubic foot p c f for backfill material and a
coefficient of friction of 0 25 between the backfill and trench sidewall are recommended
Earth pressure forces on the pipe increase rapidly with increased width of the trench
Therefore the width of the trench should be kept to a minimum Traffic loads on the
buried ductline system should also be considered since the proposed ductline alignment
is located within travelled ways
Settlements
Primary settlement of the new ductline system construction is normally caused by
the difference in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier
compacted backfill material placed over the ductline The net increase in loading will
cause settlement of the underlying soft subsoils below the trench invert Based on our
calculations primary settlement on the order of 1 inch is anticipated for the proposed
ductline
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good
workmanship will be engaged during construction Additional settlement could occur if
improper trench support procedures are used and or the trenches are improperly
backfilled However since the ductlines are encased in concrete the estimated 1 inch
of settlement should not adversely affect the ductlines

Underground Box Structures
We understand that nine underground box structures willbe constructed along the
proposed ductline alignment Because these box structures would be subjected to heavy
vehicular loading we recommend that these box structures be supported on a 24 inch
layer of gravel such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gravel wrapped in a
filter fabric Mirafi 140N or equivalent An allowable bearing pressure of 1 500 pounds
per square foot p s f may be used for design of the underground box structure bearing
on the 24 inch gravel layer The increase in load on the subsoils due to the manhole
structure should be small Therefore large settlements are not anticipated
The lateral earth pressures acting on the proposed underground box structures will
depend on the type of backfill used the extent of backfill and the compactive effort on
the backfill material around the structures Therefore we recommend that granular
material such as select borrow be used as backfill around the structures A filter fabric
Mirafi 140N or equivalent should be provided between the wall backfill and the sides of
the trench to reduce the potential for migration of the backfill into the soft volcanic ash on
the sides of the trench
The recommended lateral earth pressure for design of the underground box
structure wall expressed as an equivalent fluid pressure is 50 pounds per cubic foot
p c f The above value assumes that the backfill around the underground box structure
is properly placed and compacted Surcharge loading due to traffic and other applicable
sources should be added to the above values for design For uniform surcharge stresses
imposed on the loaded side of the wall a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure
equal to 45 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of the
wall may be used in design

Existing Utility Lines
It is anticipated that existing utility lines would be encountered during construction
of the ductline system Therefore we recommend that all existing utility lines along the
proposed ductline alignment be located accurately prior to construction and that an
adequate program be devised to reduce potential damage to the existing utility lines
during construction
Construction Monitoring
As the project will involve approximately 2 200 feet of open trench excavation on
the order of 5 to 9 feet deep in a well developed urban area we believe that the
importance of construction monitoring including field observation and engineering
supervision should be emphasized It is our understanding that the extent of
construction monitoring required may vary with individual contractors However we
believe that the following items of work should be considered
Prior to Trench Excavation
1 A damage survey of all the existing structures and utilities adjacent to the
proposed ductline alignment should be conducted including photographs
and crack surveys prior to the commencement of construction
2 Settlement markers should also be installed as much as possible in a
straight line pattern adjacent to the existing structures on both sides of the
proposed ductline alignment We recommend that one marker be installed
at approximately 30 foot to 50 foot intervals along the proposed ductline
alignment

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
A one inch ring swell test was performed on a selected sample to evaluate the
swelling potential of the material The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test was performed on a selected sample of the soils to
evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of the test are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths and on Plate B 2
Two direct shear tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the
shear strength of the material Results of the direct shear tests are presented on Plates
B 3 and B 4
