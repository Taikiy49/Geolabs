Foundation Investigation
Settlement of Building A
Iroquois Point Elementary School
In accordance with your request and our proposal of December 6
1984 we have performed a foundation investigation for the above
referenced project The approximate location of the building is shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and recommendations
Purpose and Scope
The purpose of this investigation was to evaluate possible causes
of the observed foundation settlement and to provide recommendations for
remedial work
The scope of investigation included the excavation of two 2 test
pits adjacent to the affected footings performance of in situ density
tests laboratory testing of samples obtained engineering evaluation of
the field and laboratory data and report preparation
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration logs and
photographs of test pits and laboratory test results are presented in
the appendices of this report
Site Description
The Iroquois Point Elementary School is located on Cormorant Avenue
in Ewa on the island of Oahu Hawaii
The structure concerned in this project Building A is a
single story classroom building which is surrounded in all but the
western side by lawn areas West of the classroom building separated
by a concrete paved walkway is the single story administration building
The area is in general level with drainage flowing in a southerly
direction
Four of the toilet areas located at the southwestern portion of
Building A indicated some definite signs of distress The toilet room
and those walls perpendicular to the classroom wall have separated about
Z inches at the top while those parallel to the classroom are severly
cracked because of settlement of the wall footing Slight cracks are
also found on the sidewalk at both ends of the toilet rooms According
to contours on the site plan fill varying from a depth of 2 to 3 5 feet
was required to achieve the final grade

Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by excavating
two 2 test pits to depth of 4 6 to 5 1 feet below the existing ground
surface Test Pit No 2 was further probed to a depth of 7 3 feet by
using a hand auger in order to determine the depth to the underlying
hard coral layer The approximate test pit locations are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
The two test pits revealed similar subsoil conditions underneath
the sidewalk which consisted of about 4 inches of coral sand underlain
by 2 feet of sandy coral gravels with boulders up to 12 inches
Directly underneath the wall footing Test Pit No 1 encountered about 2
feet of medium loose silty sand overlying 4 inches of medium dense
clayey sand Very dense cemented coral sand was located at 5 1 feet in
this test pit In Test Pit No 2 up to 4 feet of medium loose to
medium dense coral fine sand was encountered underneath the wall footing
with the hard coral layer found at 7 3 feet below the surface
Groundwater was encountered 6 feet from the ground surface at Test
Pit No 2 during our exploration
Discussion and Recommendations
Causes of Settlement
The test pits revealed that the wall footing was underlain by a 2
to 4 feet thick layer of medium loose silty sand In situ density tests
performed on this layer of soil indicated that the in place density is
relatively low and varied from about 85 to less than 80 of the maximum
density established by the ASTM D 1557 78 method Additionally it
appears that during heavy rainstorm or high tidal situation the water
level could rise and saturate the sands under the footing thereby
reducing the bearing capacity and densifying the sand fill thus causing
the settlement of the wall footing

It is our understanding that the adjacent bearing wall footings
which do not show signs of movements were constructed on the coral
formation and therefore are not affected by settlement of the
underiying sand
Methods of Remedy
We believe that since the settlement has occurred over a period of
approximately 20 years it probably has reached a relatively stable
condition Further settlement may still occur but the magnitude is
expected to be small probably less than one fourth of an inch
Based on the above and since the damaged walls are not bearing
walls and do not appear to jeopardize the structural integrity of the
building one option would be to simply repair the damaged portions of
the wall with no effort to improve the soil conditions below the
footings The damaged portions of the wall should be replaced and the
cracks properly grouted in order to prevent the danger of any falling
debris The cracks should be monitored to see that the movements are
not accelerating
On the other hand if it is felt that soil improvement should be
carried out to minimize future settlement the following alternatives
may be considered
1 Densification of Foundation Soil by Compaction
The soil underlying the wall footing can be compacted by using
a pheumatic hoe ram which can densify the soil by vibration and
displacement similar to compaction piles The hoe ram can be
used to drive a closed end pipe into the soil causing
displacement and densification Additional sand can then be
placed into the hole and compacted Sufficient holes should be
driven until the desired density is obtained Effort should be
made so that the density of the compacted soil would reach at
least 90 of the maximum density as established by ASTM
D 1557 78 method

2 Displacement Or Compaction Grouting
This method involves the use of highly viscous sand cement and
water displacement grout which acts as a radial hydraulic jack
that compresses the soil underneath and surrounding the bottom
of the wall footing The hardened grout mixture is a bulb of
strong and relatively incompressible material The method is
relatively expensive but the degree of disturbance caused to
the surrounding area will be small
3 Extending the Footing down to the Stable Coral Layer
This method requires the underpinning of affected footings to
transfer the load to the underlying dense coral layer at 2 5 to
4 5 feet below the bottom of the existing footing For the
design of underpinning and new footing we believe that an
allowable bearing pressure of 4000 p s f can be used for the
underlying coral layer The underpinning should be carried out
in small sections to maintain support of the wall footing
Design Review
Plans and specification for any corrective measures should be
forwarded to the soil engineer for review and written comments prior to
construction to see that the intent of our recommendations is included
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that any footing excavation backfilling and
grouting be observed by the soils engineer during construction to see
that adequate effect has been made to improve the subsoil conditions and
that the latter are consistent with the findings of our investigation
If conditions encountered during construction are different from
those presented in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made

INVESTIGATION LIMITATIONS
Our services consist of professional opinions and recommendations
made in accordance with generally accepted soil and foundation
engineering principles and practices This warranty is in lieu of all

The analysis and recommendations submitted in this report are based
on our site reconnaissance and soil information from test pits and
laboratory test data obtained from this soil investigation
