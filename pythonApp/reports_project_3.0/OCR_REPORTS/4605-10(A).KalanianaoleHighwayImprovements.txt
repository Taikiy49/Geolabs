2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu encompasses approximately 604 square miles of land area
that may be divided into four major geographic provinces The geographic provinces
include the following 1 Koolau Range 2 Waianae Range 3 Schofield Plateau and
4 Coastal Plains The majority of the Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of
basaltic lava from the two primary shield volcanoes known as Waianae and Koolau The
older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in geochronologic age
and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger Koolau Volcano is
estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island
Waianae Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano remained active
therefore the westward flowing lava from the Koolau Volcano continued to bank against
the weathered products of the Waianae Volcano to create the elevated area known as
the Schofield Plateau located between the two volcanoes
Following the eruption of the bulk of the lavas the coastal areas of the Island of
Oahu were partially submerged and emerged cyclically due to large scale sea level
fluctuations in response to periods of worldwide glaciation In addition to the sea level
fluctuations the island mass has undergone a gradual subsidence due to the island s
mass and isostatic adjustment of the earth s crust beneath the island As a result of the
cyclic sea level fluctuations and the gradual subsidence of the island the erosional
baseline likewise fluctuated and major streams were drowned and buried and later
emerged to renew their incision
The lowering of the base level resulted in an increased stream gradient and
consequently an increased rate of stream down cutting The effect of changes in
erosional baseline impact the evolution of landforms by influencing the rate of stream
down cutting and sediment deposition in addition to changing the coastal exposure to
ocean wave erosion The combined effects are evidenced by the evolution of steep

The natural processes of erosion are closely related to the development and
activation of the geologic hazard called rockfall Ground surface erosion may be
defined as the process of land denudation by the wearing away of soil and rock by both
physical and chemical processes The processes may include the weathering of rock to
form soils the mass wasting of blocks of earth materials and the erosive activity of
streams waves wind and underground water on the soil and rock materials
In Hawaii as well as other tropical regions the processes of mass wasting
combined with stream and ocean wave erosion are the most important forces acting to
continually erode and stabilize landforms Mass wasting may be defined as the
downslope movement of a mass of rock and or soil debris under the influence of gravity
by means of events such as rockfall landslide mudflow soil avalanche and soil creep
The driving force behind mass wasting is the force of gravity

2 3 Climatic Considerations
Makapuu resides at the eastern end of the windward side of the Island of Oahu
The general climate at Makapuu is relatively drier than most windward Oahu locales
due to its coastal position The climate may be summarized as relatively dry and
exposed to windy conditions Rainfall typically is light except during periods of storm
activity when precipitation increases dramatically Average annual rainfall is estimated
to range on the order of about 15 to 30 inches per year and the rainfall generally
increases further inland and at the higher elevations of this portion of the Island of
Oahu The project site is openly exposed to the predominant easterly and northeasterly
trade winds that blow for much of the year
Because the project site is located in an exposed coastal environment with
frequent rough ocean and surf conditions the amount of sea spray entrained in the air
is believed to be relatively high especially when the strong trade winds are predominant
and large surf events are experienced The effects of corrosion on exposed metallic
structures resulting from the coastal environment should be considered

Rockfall Impact Barrier
In general the rockfall impact barrier is a specialized heavy duty containment
barrier designed to intercept and retain large boulder impacts The barrier is
designed to absorb the energy of a rock impact and dissipate the energy through
the use of flexible nets which act as a braking element The rockfall impact barrier
would thereby catch rocks as opposed to bouncing or striking with enough force to
topple the retention system
The impact barrier typically consists of support posts or steel beams with lateral
cable supports and flexible braking elements Special cable ring nets and wire
mesh materials span between the support posts or steel beams The posts or
beams may be fastened to the ground plate with a hinge pin The hinge pin acts as
predetermined breaking point and protects the anchorage from destruction in case
the post or beam is directly impacted from a rockfall The impact barrier may be
designed for varying load impacts that are estimated based on the existing site
conditions such as boulder size slope inclination and composition computed rolling
velocities and possible boulder bounce heights from rockfall impacts analyzed
using the Colorado Rockfall Simulation Program CRSP computer software
In general the CRSP computer program simulates various size boulders falling
from specified slope geometries to provide statistical information for the anticipated
rockfall velocity rock bounce height and rolling energy Our rockfall simulation
analysis was performed in the areas from Station through 18 00 In
general the following assumptions were used in our analyses
Size of boulder up to 4 feet in diameter with a weight of about
2 5 tons per boulder
Rockfall impact barrier designed based on 90 percent cumulative
probability to retain the rockfall from the upper natural mountainside

In general a micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter usually less
than 12 inches drilled and grouted pile with steel reinforcing The micropile
foundation typically is constructed by drilling a borehole placing reinforcing steel in
the hole and grouting the borehole Micropiles are desirable because they can be
installed readily in access restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and
ground conditions In addition installation of the micropiles generally causes
minimal disturbance to adjacent soils and the environment
Based on our engineering analyses we believe that a micropile system with a
minimum grout bulb diameter of 5 to 6 inches may be used to support the barrier
posts Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated we recommend the
micropiles extend a minimum depth of 15 feet below the bottom of the pile cap In
addition we recommend the top 5 feet of the micropile should be cased through
the loose and or soft upper talus deposits with a permanent steel casing to
provide for a stiffer foundation element Based on our analyses we recommend
provide for sufficient lateral load resistance for support of the barrier post as
shown on the Micropile Foundation Plan and Details Plate 13

ground Therefore the contractor should exercise care when drilling and placing

Inspection and Maintenance of Rockfall Impact Barrier
It should be noted that rockfall impact barriers require routine visual inspection to
ensure optimal performance of the system In addition periodic maintenance work
to repair or replace broken or worn barrier system components following large rock
impacts may be necessary Therefore a contingency fund may be set aside for the
routine visual inspection and maintenance work
The routine visual inspection should be performed about once per year minimum
or following large rockfall or landslide event s The visual inspection serves to
identify potential rock debris accumulations that require removal and to inspect for
possible changes in the system condition or the effects of corrosion that may
reduce the barrier performance

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling thirteen borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 and Boring Nos 101
through 109 extending to depths of about 30 to 100 5 feet below the existing ground
surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools The approximate locations of the
borings for the project are shown on the Site Plans Plates 3 1 and 3 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1
through A 13
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using a 140 pound hammer dropping 18 inches The
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
Four Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 and C 136 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on Plate
B 1
Five Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The
test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentations test results are provided on Plate B 2
Three Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics Direct shear test results are
presented on Plates B 3 through B 5
A One inch Ring Swell test was performed on a relatively undisturbed soil
sample to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils under a surcharge
pressure The test results are summarized on Plate B 6
Sixteen Unconfined Compressive Strength tests ASTM D 2938 were performed
on selected rock core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the
basalt formation encountered Unconfined compressive strength test results are
presented on Plate B 7
