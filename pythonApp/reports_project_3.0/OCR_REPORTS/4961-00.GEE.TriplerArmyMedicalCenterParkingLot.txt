PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located at Tripler Army Medical Center in the Moanalua area of
the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1 Based on the information provided we understand
that it is proposed to construct a new parking lot on the western side of Jarrett White Road
The project will consist of a total of 126 parking stalls We envision that flexible
pavements consisting of asphaltic concrete will be used for the new parking lot Based on
the grading plans provided we understand that fills on the order of about 7 to 8 feet will be
required on the south and east sides of the project site to achieve the design finished
grades Because of the limited amount of space along the east side of the project site we
understand that a retaining wall will be required along this area for grade separation

FIELD EXPLORATION
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about 10 3 to 21 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers In addition three bulk soil samples
were obtained of the near surface soils to determine the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The approximate locations of the borings drilled and the bulk soil samples
collected at the site are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Boring Log Legend Plate 3 Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings
Plates 4 through 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths

LABORATORY TESTING
Selected soil samples from the field exploration were tested in the laboratory with
an emphasis on evaluating the moisture density plasticity and strength characteristics
The following laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples obtained from the
field exploration
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics Results of the direct shear test are
presented on Plate 7
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soils The tests were performed in accordance with ASTM D 1883 and the test results are
presented on Plates 8 through 10

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Detailed discussion of our geotechnical engineering recommendations for design of
pavements retaining walls and site grading are presented in the following sections of this
letter report
Pavements
As previously mentioned we understand that it is planned to construct a parking lot
at the project site We envision that flexible pavements consisting of asphaltic concrete
will be used for the parking lot
We have made our preliminary pavement designs assuming the pavement
subgrade soil will consist of the clayey near surface soils encountered during our field
exploration with CBR values ranging between 21 5 and 31 5 and expansion values
ranging between 0 5 to 1 0 percent In addition we have assumed that the loading on the
pavements will consist of light vehicular traffic If site grading exposes soil other than
those assumed additional tests should be performed to confirm or revise the
recommended pavement sections for actual field conditions Based on the above
assumptions we recommend the following pavement section be used for preliminary
design purposes
Flexible Pavements
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
6 0 Inch Select Borrow Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
14 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness over Compacted Moist Subgrade
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density The select borrow subbase and aggregate base course should
be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
The recommended pavement sections assume that good drainage will be
provided for areas adjacent to the pavements To reduce the potential infiltration of water
into the pavement structural section the pavement surface and the subgrade should be
sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to

Retaining Walls
We understand that retaining walls are planned along the east side of the project
site for separation of grade The following general guidelines may be used for preliminary
design of retaining walls
Retaining Wall Foundations
In general we believe that retaining walls may be supported on shallow continuous
footings An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf
may be used for footings bearing on the in situ soils This value may be increased
by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind and or seismic
forces The bottom of footing elevations should be recompacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction
The foundations should have a minimum width of 18 inches and should be
embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade
For sloping ground conditions the footing should extend deeper to obtain a
minimum 6 foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of
the footing to the face of the slope Foundations located next to utility trenches or
easements should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as
deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the wall may be resisted by frictional resistance developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted
in situ soils or engineered fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot
of depth pcf This assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted The
passive pressure should be reduced for foundations located on slopes Unless

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system should consist of about 1 cubic foot of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
wrapped with non woven filter fabric at each of the weephole locations The
weepholes should be spaced not more than 6 feet apart Unless covered by
concrete or asphaltic concrete the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of
relatively impervious materials such as the on site soils to reduce the potential for
significant water infiltration behind the walls
Site Grading
Based on the information provided we understand that about 2 to 8 feet of fills will
be required to achieve the design finished grades for the project In general areas within
the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed at the on set of
earthwork Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed
and disposed of properly off site
Soft loose and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated
to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or dense
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The
excavated soft loose and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation
and compacted fill placement to account for variation in the over excavation quantities
After clearing and grubbing pavements concrete slabs and exterior walkways that
are to be demolished should be completely removed Over excavation resulting from the
demolition should be backfilled with well compacted fills Existing utilities to be abandoned
should be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with fill
material placed in 8 inch loose lifts moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities to be
abandoned in place under the proposed structures should be backfilled by pumping lean
concrete or Controlled Low Strength Material CLSM under low pressure
The areas to receive fill and finished subgrades should be scarified to about
8 inches moisture conditioned to 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The compaction requirement for the
finished subgrades at the pavement areas should be increased to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

In general the excavated on site soils may be re used as a source of general fill or

Imported general fill materials should consist of materials with particle sizes of 3 inches or

Select borrow subbase and aggregate base course materials should meet the

Specifications for Public Works Construction 1986 Imported fill materials should be
tested by Geolabs for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the
project site for the intended use
General fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The general
fills and backfills within the upper 3 feet of the pavement subgrade should be compacted to
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Aggregate base course and select borrow
subbase materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction In general compaction should be
accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other similar types of acceptable
compaction equipment

Underground Utility Lines
In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended below new utility pipes planned at the
project site Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above
the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation
of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the
pipes Improper backfill material around the pipes and placement could result in
settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils with a
maximum particle size of 6 inches The trench backfill should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement
areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement surface should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

LIMITATIONS
The findings and discussions submitted in this report are based in part upon
information obtained from the field borings Variations of subsoil conditions between and
beyond the borings may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not
become evident until construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will be
necessary to re evaluate the findings and discussions provided in this letter report
The locations of the field borings indicated in this report are approximate having
been estimated by taping and pacing from the features shown on the Topographic Survey
Map prepared by ControlPoint Surveying Inc on August 19 2002 The approximate
elevations of field borings were estimated from the contour lines shown on the same plan
The physical locations and elevations of the borings should be considered accurate only to
the degree implied by the methods used
The stratification lines shown on graphic representations of the borings depict the
approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote a gradual transition
As previously mentioned groundwater was encountered in our borings at the time of our
exploration However it must be noted that groundwater level changes may occur due to
tidal fluctuations seasonal precipitation surface runoff and other factors
