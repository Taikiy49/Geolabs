PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The main objectives of the project are to improve safety and efficiency by
separating the cargo and passenger operations and to create additional berth space to
accommodate the growing cruise industry and the needs of the general maritime
industries These objectives will be accomplished by the construction of a new pier
Pier 4 west of the existing Pier 3 Young Brothers will be allowed to conduct operation
solely on the new Pier 4 The existing Pier 2 which is currently being used by both the
cruise industry and Young Brothers will be used by the cruise industry only The
project has been separated into the following phases
Phase 1 Dredging
e Phase 2 Construction of Pier 4
e Phase 3 Cargo Yard Improvements
This report pertains to the Phase 1 portion of the project only The dredging area
for the approach fairways for the new pier will be about 497 000 square feet The
planned dredging depth is set at about 25 feet Mean Lower Low Water MLLW
Based on the available information the majority of the mudline is at about 5 to 10 feet
MLLW The mudline along the area near the transition from the proposed dredging
area to the existing dredged basin adjacent to Pier 3 ranges from 11 to 24 feet MLLW

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an
area of approximately 4 030 square miles The island was formed by the activity of the
following five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during recent
geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea
both still active
The project site is within the Hilo Harbor area Hilo Harbor lies at the intersecting
slopes of the Mauna Loa and Mauna Kea Volcanoes The lava flows of the Mauna Loa
Volcano are relatively young in terms of geologic time and may be on the order of only
750 to 1 500 years old Below the basaltic lava flows of the Mauna Loa Volcano the
underlying lava flows of the Mauna Kea Volcano are believed to be Pliocene to
Pleistocene in age and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series
The land surrounding the Hilo Harbor area was reclaimed from the bay by the
placement of fill over the lagoonal deposits and or coralline detritus The project site is
generally underlain by lagoonal deposits and deposits of calcareous sediments also
known as coralline detritus Alluvial deposits associated with the Wailuku River
underlie the lagoonal deposits and calcareous sediments Basalt formation from the
recent Mauna Loa lava flows may be encountered at greater depths below the alluvial
deposits
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is within the Hilo Harbor area on the Island of Hawaii as shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The proposed dredging area is west of Pier 3 as
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on the hydrographic survey performed by the Army Corps of Engineers
the mudline of the proposed dredging area is relatively level with mudline elevations
ranging from about 5 to 10 feet MLLW The area that transitions from the existing
dredged basin adjacent to Pier 3 to the proposed dredging area has mudline
elevations ranging from 11 to 24 feet MLLW

Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general

the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock or consolidated materials encountered at the site were
obtained by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation or consolidated
materials The rock or consolidated material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The mudline was encountered at about 10 to 13 5 feet below the water level In
general a thin layer of harbor deposits about 1 to 2 5 feet thick was encountered below
the mudline The harbor deposits consisted of very soft sandy silt and loose silty sand
Lagoonal deposits and coralline detritus consisting of loose to medium dense silty
gravel and sand were encountered below the harbor deposits extending to the
maximum depths explored
Severely fractured coral ledges were encountered in Boring and
106 located along the top of slope that transitions from the proposed dredge area to
the dredged basin adjacent to Pier 3 The coral ledges were about 1 to 5 5 feet thick
and were encountered between depths of about 12 5 and 16 5 feet and about 23 5 and
31 feet below the water level The relative hardness of the coral encountered ranged
from soft to medium hard
A basalt rock ledge was encountered in Boring No 106 between the depths of
about 23 to 25 5 feet below the water level The basalt rock encountered was generally
severely fractured and hard During the jet probing operations performed by Sea
Engineering Inc rock was observed in the area near Boring No 106 In addition
basalt rock was observed along the shoreline
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at Phase 1 of the project site
we believe that conventional dredging methods using clamshell buckets and cutterhead
dredge methods may be used for the dredging
The dredged material consisting of coralline detritus and coral formation and
basalt rock may have the potential for re use as fill material at Phases 2 and 3 if
properly segregated from the harbor deposits and dewatered
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report

Site Grading
Dredging of the area will consist of excavations of up to about 20 feet below the
existing mudline Based on the subsurface conditions it is anticipated that the majority
of the dredged material will generally consists of coralline gravels and sands with
varying amounts of silt A thin layer of harbor deposits consisting of sandy silts will also
be excavated from the proposed dredging area
It is anticipated that conventional dredging methods using clamshell or dragline
buckets may be utilized for the dredging work Hydraulic dredging methods utilizing
cutterhead dredge may also be utilized This type of dredging method consists of a
rotary cutter attached to a pipe and centrifugal pump
Some areas of harder coral formation may be present within the loose to
medium dense coralline detritus In addition a basalt rock ledge was observed and
was encountered in the borings near the shore It is anticipated that these severely
fractured formations could most likely be broken and removed with clamshell or dragline
buckets However if dense coral and basalt rock are present a cutterhead dredge
designed to cut through rock may be required
Cut slopes should be designed and constructed at a maximum of two horizontal
to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter
Due to the difficulties of maintaining grade control while working underwater and
the tendency for material to migrate while an area is being dredged consideration
should be given to over dredging slightly about 1 to 2 feet below the planned dredge
depth and allowing the material to settle at the contract dredge depth
All dredging work should be done in accordance with the applicable state and
federal requirements including permits water quality certifications and environmental
protection
Dredged Material
Based on the borings drilled it is anticipated that the majority of the dredged
material will generally consist of coralline detritus mainly consisting of sand and gravel
with some silt If this material is properly handled and dewatered it may have some
potential for re use as fill for Phases 2 and 3 of the project
Care should be exercised to segregate the dredged coralline detritus from the
harbor deposits sandy silt which was encountered below the mudline and ranged in
thickness from about 1 to 2 5 feet If the dredged harbor deposits are mixed with the
dredged coralline detritus it may render this composite dredged material unusable as
fill
