INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical investigation
performed for the proposed maintenance hangar modifications at the Marine
Corps Air Station at Kaneohe Bay Oahu Hawaii The general location of
the project site and vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map and
Site Plan Plates 1 and 2 respectively
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of the investigation was to evaluate the subsurface soil
conditions underlying the existing Hangars and 105 to
provide soil data for the design of the proposed maintenance hangar
modifications The modifications proposed are two story structures with

dimensions of 150 by 52 feet to be used as administrative spaces and
workshop areas The main objective of the investigation was to determine
the allowable bearing capacities and associated settlements for shallow
spread or continuous footing foundations
The scope of our investigation was performed in general accordance
with our proposal of The investigation included drilling
and sampling twelve 12 borings laboratory soil testing field soil
testing including electric cone and standard penetration resistance tests
engineering analysis utilizing the laboratory and field data and report
preparation
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration laboratory testing
electric cone penetration testing and boring logs are presented in the
appendices of this report The approximate locations of the borings are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
PROJECT DESCRIPTION
The existing maintenance Hangars and 105 are
structures approximately 400 by 250 feet in plan dimension which were
constructed in the 1940 s and are founded on piles The proposed hangar
modifications are two story masonry CMU structures with plan dimensions
of 180 by 52 feet to be located along the center width line of the hangars
as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The proposed two story structures are to be used for workshop areas
and administrative spaces The total design load on all interior bearing
walls is 5 kips per linear foot while the total loads on the exterior
walls are approximately 5 to 6 kips per linear foot Specifically the

GENERAL SITE CONDITION
The four existing hangars at the project site are located between
the waterfront and First Street within the Marine Corps Air Station at
Kaneohe Bay on the island of Oahu Hawaii The hangars are currently
used for aircraft maintenance and storage
The existing hangars are rigid frame steel structures with large
sliding steel doors on both longitudinal sides The offices and work shop
areas are presently located at the ends of the hangar with the interior
portions used for repair and storage of the aircrafts
The hangar floor consists of a 5 to 6 inch thick concrete
slab on grade Many cracks were noted in the concrete slab
The floor surface elevation of the hangar floor is at about 7 to 8
feet above MSL

Soil profiles have also been interpreted from the electric cone
penetration test results which are presented in Appendix Cc The cone
penetration tests were performed for a variety of reasons Static cone
penetration tests such as those used at this project site do not compact
the soil to the same extent as standard penetration tests Therefore the
cone penetration value is considered to be more representative of the
undisturbed in situ soil resistance Additionally more soil properties
can be estimated based on emphirical correlations from cone rather than
standard penetration data A major advantage to performing both cone and
standard penetration tests on different borings in the same inmediate
vicinity is the opportunity to verify the in situ soil properties which
are estimated from the test results These estimated soil properties are
then used to calculate the appropriate bearing capacities and
corresponding settlements

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the anticipated soil conditions and column loads we
believe that shallow spread or continuous wall footings bearing on the
in situ soils may be used This type of foundation is recommended for a
number of reasons These include the subsurface conditions the required
design loading of 5 and 6 kips per linear foot for interior and exterior
walls respectively and the permissible amount of settlement for the
proposed buildings based on the type of structures and anticipated uses
Foundation
We feel the soils underlying this site are capable of supporting the
required design loads with a shallow foundation and that the expense of a
deeper pile or pier foundation can be avoided It is also our inter
pretation that these loads are not large enough to require the use of a
GEOLABS HAWAIli

shallow raft foundation When comparing shallow foundations rafts are
often recommended over spread or continuous footings when the loading is
large enough to require a more even loading distribution The larger
quantity of excavated soil is also necessary to further reduce the loading
on the bearing layer
The recommended spread or continuous footings should be placed a
minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Uplift
wind or other lateral loading condition may further increase the embedment
of the footing foundation
Review of the structural as built foundation and floor plans
indicates that some new footings will be constructed adjacent to existing
pile caps It is desirable to minimize the additional loading on the
existing piles which will reduce the potential of possibly overloading
these piles For this reason we recommend that where new footings are
to be placed adjacent to the existing pile caps the new footings be
lowered to below the top of the pile cap This will allow for a small
quantity of the loading from the new footing to be distributed to the
existing pile caps If the new footings adjacent to the existing pile
caps were to be placed at the same embedment depth of 24 inches as
recommended for the remaining new footings a larger portion of the new
loading would be distributed to the top of the pile cap This may result
in the overloading of the existing piles and is therefore to be avoided
An allowable bearing capacity of 2500 p s f may be used for the
foundation design The bearing capacity value is for total dead plus live
load only and may be increased by one third for short duration wind
loading
The bottom of the footing excavation should be recompacted to a
minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density per ASTM D 1557 78 Prior to
concrete placement footing backfill should also be compacted to a minimm
of 95 of its maximm dry density

The allowable bearing capacities for the soils underlying all four
hangars have been generalized to a single recommendation of 2500 p s f
This is particularly for the convenience of the structural engineer in
design and to reduce the potential of construction mistakes which may
arise from complicated design plans which specify numerous footing widths
and embedment depths Our engineering analysis of each hangar indicated
that the soils underlying the southeast portion of the site are more
competent than those of the northwest By more competent we are
referring to the slightly higher bearing capacity and corresponding
slightly lower settlement of the soils in the southeast corner of the site
than those in the northwest corner
Settlements
Settlements under the design loading are anticipated to be on the
order of less than one half of an inch total and one fourth of an inch
differential for Hangars 102 103 and 105 Slightly higher settlements up
to three fourths of an inch total are anticipated for Hangar 101 where
softer soil conditions were encountered
It is our opinion that the majority of the predicted total
settlement will occur during the construction process This opinion is
based on the granular soils encountered in the exploratory borings and the
observation that settlement of granular soils occurs immediately when
compared to the rate of settlement of cohesive soils The structural
advantage to having the majority of total settlement occur during
construction is that a smaller quantity of the total settlement will
remain to occur after the buildings have been erected It is the later
settlement specifically the differential settlement which has the
potential to produce structural cracking Therefore it is recommended to
pour the slab as late as possible during the construction process An
additional suggestion for minimizing the potential of structural cracking
is to pour the slab in sections which will provide for articulated
movement rather than a single large less flexible slab

Lateral Resistance
Resistance to lateral loads for the hangar structure can be obtained
by passive pressure and frictional resistance A frictional coefficient
of 0 4 may be used between the bottom of the footing and the dense soil
In addition an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 p s f per foot of depth
may be used to determine the passive resistance
Slab On Grade
For the light floor loads anticipated a 4 inch concrete slab over 4
inches of well graded aggregate base course is recommended A plastic
vapor barrier over the base course could be used should a capillary break
be deemed necessary to protect floor covering
Utility Construction
For the same settlement magnitude and rate considerations described
earlier it is recommended that all utility construction either exposed
or embedded in backfill material be delayed as much as possible Short
spans and flexible joints for the utility lines may be used to reduce
damages due to differential settlement
For those utility lines embedded in trench backfill in order to
minimize the required compactive effort and possible damages to the pipe
the backfill around and up to 1 foot above the pipe should consist of
clean granular material such as sand The backfill should be lightl
tamped or jetted as appropriate
Generally for backfilling the utility trenches the compacted
density of the backfill from the pipe bed up to 12 inches above the pipe
should be a minimum of 85 of ASTM D 1557 78 The remaining portion
should be compacted to a minimum of 90 If trenches are located under
the roadways the top 12 inches of the backfill from the finished grade
should be compacted to a minimum of 95

Design Review
Plans and specifications for the foundations should be forwarded to
the soils engineer for review and written comments prior to construction
to see that the intent of our recommendations is included
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the footing excavations and subgrade
preparation be observed by one of our soils engineers during construction
to see that adequate compaction has been obtained and that the subsurface
conditions are consistent with the findings of our investigation If
conditions encountered during construction are different from those
presented in this report then appropriate modifications to the design
should be made
INVESTIGATION LIMITATIONS
Our services consist of professional opinions and recommendations
made in accordance with generally accepted soil and foundation engineering
principles and practices This warranty is in lieu of all other

The analysis and recommendations submitted in this report are based
on our site reconnaissance soil information from boring data and
laboratory tests obtained from this soil investigation

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on
selected samples to aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Boring Logs
at the appropirate sample depths
Three Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected samples of
the underlying fine grained material to further identify their physical
characteristics The test results are summarized on Plates B 1 through
B 6 and the Plasticity Chart Plate B 9
Thirteen sieve analysis were performed to determine the grain size
distribution of the soils The test results are summarized on Plates B 1
through B 6
One consolidation test was performed on a sample selected from a
soft layer to evaluate its settlement characteristics The consolidation
curve is presented on Plate B 7
One direct shear test and two triaxial shear tests were performed on
undisturbed samples to evaluate the strength characteristics of the soil
The direct shear test results are summarized on Plate B 8 and the
triaxial shear tests results are presented on Plates B 10 and B 11
