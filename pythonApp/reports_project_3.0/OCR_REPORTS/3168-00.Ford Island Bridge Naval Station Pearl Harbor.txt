INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed Ford Island Bridge at the Pearl Harbor Naval
Station in Pearl Harbor on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity
of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our preliminary field exploration and
laboratory testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived
from our analyses of the data obtained from the field exploration and laboratory testing
of selected samples The recommendations contained herein are intended for design
input for foundations of the proposed bridge project
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed Ford Island Bridge will be located in East Loch of Pearl Harbor on
the island of Oahu Hawaii as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 It is
proposed to construct a bridge from the northeast shore of Ford Island to the main
shore of the Pearl Harbor Naval Station near Halawa Landing as shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2 The proposed bridge alignment commences at Saratoga Boulevard at
about Station 10 00 on the Ford Island side and terminates at the intersection of

will consist of a reinforced concrete bridge structure fixed portion The proposed
bridge will have an opening of about 650 feet wide by 45 feet deep for navigational
crossing of aircraft carriers This bridge opening will consist of a floating bridge section
In addition to this bridge opening a 30 foot high by 100 foot wide opening is also
planned for a small boat navigational crossing under the fixed portion of the bridge

METHODOLOGY
As indicated above the proposed alignment of the Ford Island Bridge generally
follows the old alignment on the Ford Island side but it deviates by about 600 feet on
the Halawa side Based on the borings drilled for the previous preliminary geotechnical
investigation in 1984 and our knowledge of the conditions at Pearl Harbor the
subsurface conditions along the alignment on the Ford Island side are anticipated to be
relatively uniform with a relatively thin layer of harbor sediments The principal
controlling factor for design of foundations for the proposed bridge is the vertical and
lateral extent of the soft harbor sediments in Pearl Harbor As encountered in the
preliminary study conducted in 1984 the soft harbor sediments were significant in
vertical and lateral extent along the previous alignment especially toward the Halawa
side Therefore our current geotechnical study for this project was directed at
evaluating the vertical and lateral extent of the soft harbor sediments and the
engineering properties of the supporting soils

Based on the available information the thick soft harbor sediments were
believed to be present on the Halawa side of the proposed alignment Since the
presence of the thick soft harbor sediments will significantly affect the bridge
foundation design our geotechnical engineering efforts were directed at confirming the
presence or absence of thick soft harbor sediments along the alignment especially at
the locations of the anchor piers for the floating bridge section Based on the available

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older voicano Waianae forms the bulk
of the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3168 00 Page 8
of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in
Central Oahu
Pearl Harbor is located on the southwest flank of the Koolau shield and its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic
fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch The stratigraphic history of
Pearl Harbor is quite complex due to its large areal extent the large number of streams
and drainageways entering it and a complex series of sea level changes The
subsurface conditions at the project site are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age
coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea that are
intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in the
Pearl Harbor vicinity is Koolau basalt
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene by thinly
bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased approximately
2 3 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the island of Oahu has
subsided as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity
Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of
the reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3168 00 Page 9
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers melted global sea
levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to present sea level on Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal sediments
may have also resulted in deposits of coral algal limestone and marls within the
predominantly lagoonal sedimentary unit
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy
conditions During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments
The geologic history of the project area has been further complicated by the
deposition of recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of the vents of
members of the Honolulu Voicanic Series including Aliamanu Salt Lake Makalapa
Kamanaiki Punchbowl and other minor vents These
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3168 00 Page 10
post erosional events following the cessation of shield building were contemporaneous
with the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations
Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature due to the interaction of
groundwater with the rising magma resulting in steam explosions which expelled large
quantities of pyroclastic material predominantly ash and cinder Some of the
eruptions produced lava flows which filled the valleys and moved seaward inundating
coral reefs and alluvial deposits
About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that
rise the deep valleys in Pearl Harbor were submerged In the last 10 000 years the
sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have
continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments of Pear
Harbor resulting in thick deposits of soft harbor sediments in the lochs and channel
areas
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have
brought the Ewa Honolulu area to its present form A great many of those projects
were originally constructed for agricultural residential and or military development

The Koolau basalt is the oldest geologic unit within the project area It generally
consists of interbedded pahoehoe and a a flows of basaltic lavas and may have a
mantle of cobbles and boulders or residual soil overlying the top of the rock Based
on the idealized soil profile it appears that the top of basalt slopes gradually down
gradient from the east Halawa side toward the west Ford sland side
The Koolau basalt is the aquifer material of the Pearl Harbor Artesian Basin
which is one of the major developed groundwater bodies supplying fresh water for the
Island of Oahu The aquifer is the permeable lava rock while the upper confining
member caprock is the relatively impermeable alluvial and lagoonal soil deposits
There is no lower confining member as is the usual case in a basal fresh water lens
The alluvial and lagoonal soil deposits above the Koolau basalt may be grouped
into four major units 1 Old Alluvium 2 Old Lagoonal Deposit 3 Young Alluvium

Old Alluvium
The old alluvium unit generally overlies the Koolau basalt and consists of
terrigenous sediments which have been transported by stream action from
the upper reaches of the streams The materials encountered in the
borings drilled for the current and previous explorations consist mainly of
very stiff to hard brown clayey silts and silty clays In Boring
and 103 the bottom of the old alluvium unit contains some sands and
gravels above the underlying Koolau basalt unit and may also contain
artesian water of the basal fresh water system
Contained within the old alluvium unit are some layers of dark gray to
black and light gray lagoonal silts which are termed lagoonal facies
These lagoonal facies probably reflect localized lagoonal conditions along
the depositional sequence of the old alluvium and are generally very stiff
to hard where encountered
Based on laboratory shear strength tests of the old alluvium undrained
shear strengths between 1 and 12 kips per square foot k s f were
obtained for this material as shown on Plate B 4 of Appendix B Based
on our evaluation of the data and materials encountered we believe that
an average undrained shear strength of 4 5 k s f may be used to arrive
at pile capacities deriving support from skin friction We believe that
portions of the old alluvium unit may be very hard and would pose
significant difficulties during pile installation

2 Old Lagoonal Deposit
The old lagoonal deposit unit consists of marine or lagoonal sediments

3 Young Alluvium
The young alluvium is located above and separated from the old alluvium
unit by the desiccated and over consolidated old lagoonal deposit The
young alluvium consists mainly of stiff to hard brown to dark brown
clayey silts and silty clays with varying amounts of sand and pockets of
gravel and cobbles due to a higher energy depositional environment
Similar to the old alluvium unit localized layers of lagoonal marine
sediments consisting mostly of very stiff to hard gray clayey silts were

SEISMIC _ DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS
The peak ground acceleration to be used in design of the proposed Ford Island
Bridge is 0 18g where g is the acceleration due to gravity as required in the design
criteria Since a peak ground acceleration is given for design the earthquake ground
motions will not need to be modified to account for the subsurface soil conditions at the
site However the shape of the elastic response spectra would need to take into
consideration the type of soil profile at the site In general Soil Groups 1 and 2 would
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3168 00 Page 19
be considered deep stiff soil sites and Soil Groups 3 and 4 would be considered deep
soft soil sites
In order to provide design input motions for structural analysis of the proposed
bridge elastic response spectra will need to be generated We understand that only
limited earthquake ground motion data of significant amplitude is available for the island
of Oahu and the State of Hawaii Because of the small data set for earthquake ground
motions from Hawaii the elastic response spectra for this project was developed using
the Newmark Hail Method The Newmark Hall Method is an empirical method of
constructing elastic response spectra based on normalized values for free field ground
motion The normalized values for free field ground motion utilized in the Newmark Hall
Method are based on a large database of earthquake ground motions We believe that
the elastic response spectra constructed by the Newmark Hail Method would be
appropriate for use in the State of Hawaii
Based on the Newmark Hall Method and an input peak ground acceleration of
0 18g the peak ground velocity is estimated to be approximately 9 inches per second
0 75 feet per second and the peak ground displacement is estimated to be about 7
inches 0 55 feet The estimated values for free field ground motion are considered
appropriate for deep stiff soil sites since normalized values for the Newmark Hall
Method based on deep soil sites were used Therefore we believe that the estimated
free field ground motions obtained by the Newmark Hall Method are applicable to Soil
Groups 1 and 2 The elastic response spectra constructed by the Newmark Hall
Method for free field ground motions and for 2 and 5 percent damping factors are
plotted in tripartite form on Plate 4
Significant thickness of soft harbor sediments are present in areas designated
as Soil Groups 3 and 4 The significant thickness of soft harbor sediments will likely
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3168 00 Page 20
absorb the high frequency content of earthquake ground motions resulting in long

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the previous and current preliminary explorations it is evident that the
design of the proposed Ford Island Bridge would be significantly influenced by the
vertical and lateral extent of the very soft to soft harbor sediments in Pearl Harbor It
appears that the harbor sediments encountered along the old alignment were thicker
and the consistencies of the harbor sediments were very soft to soft from the mudline
extending to near the bottom of the harbor sediment layer
Based on the borings drilled for this current preliminary exploration the lateral
and vertical extent of the soft harbor sediments appear to be less extensive than those
encountered along the previous alignment The deep harbor sediments appear to be
closer to the shore on the Halawa side The thickest portion of the harbor sediments
encountered in the four borings was about 70 feet below the mudline Based on the
in situ vane shear tests performed on the harbor sediments the very soft to soft harbor
sediment layer appears to be confined to the upper portion of the layer within 25 to 35
feet of the mudline The lower portion of the harbor sediment layer appears to grade
from soft to medium stiff and is capable of providing some support to pile foundations
Because of the significant unsupported lengths of the pile foundations due to the
very soft harbor sediments and the depth of water in the harbor at the eastern portion
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3168 00 Page 21
of the bridge alignment Halawa side lateral support of the pile foundations is of major
consideration To resist the lateral loading imposed on the pile foundations a system
of vertical and battered piles are proposed to resist the design lateral loads Due to the
limited lateral resisting capacity of the upper soft harbor sediments the unsupported
free standing length of piles along this portion of the alignment is significant
Because of the significant unsupported length of the piles two high capacity pile
types are under consideration for supporting the proposed bridge We believe that 24
inch octagonal and or 36 inch cylindrical precast prestressed concrete piles may be
used efficiently to support the proposed bridge structure These high capacity piles
would derive support from a combination of frictional resistance between the pile shaft
and the surrounding very stiff to hard soils and end bearing on the hard alluvial and
lagoonal soil deposits
The previous geotechnical investigation recommended the use of 54 inch
diameter cylindrical piles vertical for support of the bridge structure However based
on our evaluation of the subsurface conditions along this new alignment we believe
that a system of smaller diameter vertical and battered piles may be used more
efficiently to support the bridge structure Based on our analyses a combination of
24 inch octagonal and 36 inch cylindrical prestressed concrete piles may be used to
support the bridge structure
Because of the significant unsupported length of the piles where thick soft
harbor sediments exist we believe that 36 inch cylindrical high capacity piles should
be used to support the bridge structure Because of the limited lateral supporting

Pile Driving Criteria
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy
of at least 100 000 foot pounds The hammer should be equipped with an energy
control level such that driving within the loose and soft soil deposits may be performed
at a lower energy level to reduce the development of tensile stresses within the piles
which could damage the piles
The piies should be driven to the depths recommended by the project
geotechnical engineer to develop the required structural capacity Because high
GEOLABS HAWAII

to hard clayey silts and silty clays will be necessary to develop the required structural
capacity Some dense sands and gravels and or hard lagoonal facies which may
result in hard driving conditions may be encountered during pile driving Because of
the long embedment depths needed and the large displacement piles planned the use
of a driving shoe may be necessary to obtain the required pile penetration
Because local variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the
bearing soils the supporting capacity of each pile must be confirmed in the field during
driving This should be accomplished by using the Wave Equation Analysis to evaluate
pile bearing capacity The driving criteria will be developed after the selection of the
pile driving equipment for test pile and production pile driving Additional pile driving
criteria are as follows
1 The driving process should be continuous without interruption for each
pile The design capacity of the pile may be modified when the driving
process is interrupted for more than 4 hours
2 During easy driving when the pile tip is still within the loose and or soft
soil deposit the hammer stroke should be carefully controlled with
reduced energy levels such that excessive tensile stresses are not
induced in the pile
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy to
the top of the pile and also reducing damage to the piles The project geotechnical

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The preliminary exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four off
shore borings to depths ranging between 90 and 235 feet below the water surface
The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig on a steel shelled barge
Locations of the four off shore borings were surveyed The approximate locations of
the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer and geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic presentation
and description of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Soil samples were obtained from the borings by driving a 3 inch O D Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Relatively undisturbed soft soil
samples were obtained by hydraulically pushing a 3 inch O D thin wall Shelby tube into
the soil formation A Denison type sampler which uses a 3 inch O D thin wall Shelby
tube was used to obtain relatively undisturbed samples of the stiffer soils The
hydraulic pressures needed to advance the sampler where available are shown on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer and torvane shear tests were performed on the samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample The torvane shear test provides a
quick estimate of the undrained shear strength of the soil tested Results of the pocket
penetrometer and torvane shear tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Because of the very soft to soft consistency of the harbor sediments it would be
difficult to obtain relatively undisturbed soil samples of the material To better
estimate the in situ undrained shear strength of the soft harbor sediments in situ field
vane shear tests were performed on the soft harbor sediments A total of eight in situ
field vane shear tests were conducted in two off shore borings at various depths The
field vane shear apparatus used consisted of a blade with dimensions of 5 inches in
height and 2 5 inches in width diameter
