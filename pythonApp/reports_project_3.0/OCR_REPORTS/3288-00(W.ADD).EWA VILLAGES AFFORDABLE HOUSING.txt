REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shielded
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae ended lava flows from Koolau
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau
Weathering and erosion of this plateau generated sediments which were
transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and further south these
alluvial sediments accumulated along with and interbedded with marine sediments and
coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from zero
feet in the area of H 1 Freeway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3288 00 Page 7
the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian
basal aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of these sediments has continued from
earlier geologic time through the present

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Laboratory tests performed on the near surface clayey soils indicate that the
materials generally have a moderate to high shrink swell potential when subjected to
fluctuations in the moisture content Therefore for future slab on grade construction
we recommend that the expansive clayey soils be capped with a minimum of 12 inches
of non expansive select granular material to reduce the potential of distress resulting
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3288 00 Page 8
from the swelling of the near surface expansive soils In anticipation that post and
beam construction is selected for the new residential structures we had recommended
that short drilled piers be used to support the lightly loaded post footings We envision
the near surface soil under the post and beam structure will exhibit soil shrinkage in the
future due to loss of soil moisture under a sheltered environment Therefore no
allowance for frictional load support should be provided in the foundation design

Foundations
We understand that post and beam construction will be used for the proposed
one story structures for the subject affordable housing foundation project For the
foundation design of these post and beam construction an allowable bearing pressure
end bearing on 6 inches of compacted base course material over the very stiff to hard
in situ clay soils This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased
by one third s for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
These piers are intended to provide support by end bearing only Tight frictional
contact between the surrounding soil and the piers or widened tops on the piers are
neither needed nor desired to provide load support Widened tops or mushroom
caps could provide a bearing surface for the underlying expansive clay soils to exert
uplift pressure which could result in foundation movement In order to avoid the
formation of mushroom caps at the surface and to minimize the potential uplift of the
piers resulting from skin friction between the pier and surrounding soil a waxed mold
plastic pipe or felt may be considered as a form for casting the concrete The bottom
of the drilled holes should be cleaned of loose soils and recompacted with a pneumatic
vibratory rod prior to placing the base course cushion layer The base course cushion
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3288 00 Page 9
should be placed as soon as possible after drilling in one level lift approximately 6
inches in loose thickness and compacted to 95 percent relative compaction The
surface of the base course cushion should be recompacted again to provide a firm
bearing surface prior to the placement of concrete In general the bottom of the drilled
piers should be supported on the above prepared compacted base course layer at a
minimum depth of 3 feet below the lowest adjacent finish grades A graphic illustration
of this foundation system is shown on Plate 3
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines
We estimate the footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for cast
in place drilled piers bearing on the above prepared compacted base course layer and
the very stiff to hard in situ clay soils to be less than 1 inch total and inch differential
The long term pier footing settlements due to near surface soil shrinkage particularly
at the middle portion of the structure will be very difficult to predict We believe that
the 3 foot embedment depth recommended for the drilled pier foundations should
extend below the zone of normal soil shrinkage to reduce the potential for pier footing
settlement due to shrinkage of the in situ soils However it should be made aware that
variations of subsoil conditions may occur and the zone of soil shrinkage could extend
below the 3 foot zone at localized areas Therefore provision for remedial measures
may be required to correct any potential foundation distress resulting from excessive
soil shrinkage in the future
Footing excavations and recompaction should be observed by a representative
of Geolabs Hawaii prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the
adequacy of the foundation bearing conditions and the embedment depths

Slab On Grade
Concrete slab on grade construction is anticipated for the proposed carport
structures at the subject housing development Our exploration indicates that the site
is generally underlain by moderately expansive clayey soils Unless concrete slab on
grade supported by these expansive soils are properly designed there is a potential
for future distress to the concrete slabs resulting from wetting and subsequently
swelling of these expansive clayey soils
To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from the swelling of the
on site expansive soils we recommend that the expansive clayey soils be capped with
a minimum of 12 inches of non expansive select granular fill material The select
granular fill should be placed as soon as possible after the scarification and
recompaction of the existing subgrade soils The prepared capping fill and the
underlying subgrade soil should be wetted and kept moist continuously prior to final
placement of concrete
The select granular fill would serve as a capping fill layer of moist soil above the
underlying potentially expansive subgrade soils and this capping fill would also reduce
the potential loss of moisture from the prepared moist subgrade soils In our opinion
the reduction in potential drying and wetting of the clayey subgrade soils should reduce
the impact on the slab due to potential heaving of the underlying expansive clayey soils
when wetted Compaction requirements for the subgrade soils and select granular fill
are discussed in the Site Preparation section of this report
To reduce the potential for drying of the subgrade soils and to reduce the costs
of form construction we recommend that the slab edges be cast neat against the
soils The slab thickened edges should incorporate a sufficient amount of top and
bottom longitudinal steel reinforcement The top and bottom steel reinforcement bars

Site Preparation
All areas to receive slabs and pavements should be over excavated to a depth
of 10 to 12 inches below the bottom of the base course layer If zones of soft loose
or saturated organic soils are encountered at the over excavated subgrade level
deeper over excavation may be required to expose firm material The resulting
excavation should be backfilled with compacted select granular fill material The
organic clayey material resulting from the over excavation should be removed and
properly disposed of off site
Areas to receive fills should be scarified to a depth of about 10 inches moisture
conditioned to between 2 and 5 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to about 85 to 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density as determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Select granular fill
material should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3288 00 Page 14
The select capping fill should be non expansive granular material such as
crushed coral mudrock or cinder sand The material should be well graded from
coarse to fine with no particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension The material
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a
swell potential of not more than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883 It should contain less than 20 percent passing the No 200 sieve
Any imported fill material should be tested and approved for its intended use by our firm
prior to delivery to the project site
Utility Trench
We envision that small diameter utility lines will be required for the affordable
housing project A granular bedding consisting of 4 inches of No 3B Fine gravel is
recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the trench backfill up
to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and
to reduce compaction of the backfill thus reducing the potential for damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular material or
the on site soils The backfill should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent of
the optimum moisture placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet
of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to 95 percent
relative compaction
