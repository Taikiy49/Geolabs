REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which
time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano and on the coastal
plain of Southern Oahu Therefore the geomorphology and subsurface conditions in the
area are directly related to the glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during the
Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The coastal
plain was built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano which forms the eastern
two thirds of the Island of Oahu The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of
alluvium derived from erosion of the volcano interbedded with coral reefs and associated
deposits
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation Most of the coastal plains were developed during the
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly As the glaciers grew and
advanced less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below
the present stands of the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water
became available such that the sea levels rose to above its present level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
The subsurface conditions in the Ala Moana area are dominated by fossil
Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits which are intercalated with
terrigenous sediments laid during the higher stands of the sea Therefore the project site
is generally underlain by deposits consisting of calcareous sediments and lagoonal
deposits A surface layer of man made fill was placed over these deposits for the
development of the Ala Moana area within the last century

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
As mentioned previously field exploration consisting of drilling and sampling
borings was not conducted for this geotechnical engineering study Instead research
and review of available in house soil and boring data from the project vicinity was
performed in order to evaluate the subsurface conditions at the site Based on available
information and our experience in the area the native soils at the project site generally
consist primarily of fills underlain by lagoonal deposits and coral formation Based on the
Geologic Map of Waikiki Moilili and Kakaako by Charles C Ferrall 1976 an alluvial
channel which runs in a north south direction is located between Keawe Street and Coral
Street The general geologic classifications are described as follows
Fills
The fill materials consist of variable soils containing some rock fragments which
were placed by man during the development of the Kakaako coastal areas The fill
materials generally consist of both calcareous and non calcareous soils containing
various mixtures of gravel sands silts clays and potentially some embedded rock
fragments such as hard coralline or basaltic cobbles and boulders The fills were
typically placed to achieve the desired finished grades and to provide reclaimed
land for development The fill materials may be encountered as both
unconsolidated and semi consolidated deposits having variable density resulting
from man induced compaction of the fill materials In general the fills may extend
to depths of about 3 to 8 feet below the existing ground surface
Lagoonal Deposits
Lagoonal materials generally consist of variable light gray calcareous sediments
clay silt and sand mixed with some consolidated coral reef formation and broken
granular fragments of coralline reef materials gravelly coralline detritus The
materials are generally deposited in low wave energy shallow marine
environments such as bays and lagoons which are commonly protected by an

outer fringing coralline reef and an inner shoreline environment The deposits are
typically soft and or loose and provide infill among localized in situ hard coral reef
structures The deposits are variable in type and consistency as a result of
changing depositional environments affected by short term storm wave action and
long term global fluctuations in sea level Lagoonal deposits may be encountered
interbedded within coralline detritus deposits or as layers or void infilling within
porous and cavernous coral reef formation materials The lagoonal deposits may
extend to depths of about 25 to 35 feet below the existing ground surface
Coral Algal Reef Formation
Tan or grayish white medium hard to very hard organic rock consisting of
moderately to strongly cemented coral and algal reef materials with a fine grain
calcareous crystalline rock groundmass The rock may contain varying degrees of
vugs and may be non porous to very porous by interconnected vug structures The
material densities logged by SPT sampling may vary widely over a short interval of
depth with very dense coral algal limestone immediately underlying overlying a
seam of medium dense material
The rock is essentially formed by live marine organisms that consist of coral and
calcareous algae plants that deposit calcium carbonate In general the corals
build the rock framework with lime secretions derived from dissolved calcium
carbonate from seawater The algae growth then in fills and coats the coral surface
to armor the coral against surf action The hardened algae growth is what provides
much of the cementing agent to form hard limestone
The rock may be cored with variable recovery due to the presence of
interconnected voids and vug space present in the rock mass The rock may be
sampled by SPT with density ranging from medium dense to SPT refusal however
SPT penetration is generally limited to the medium dense very porous rock type
containing a high volume of void space in the rock framework The open vugs and
voids commonly encountered within the porous rock framework may be partially or
wholly filled with fine sediments making it difficult to differentiate upon cursory
inspection of an SPT sample between mildly cemented coral detritus and strongly
vugular coral algal limestone rock It is best to retrieve cored samples of the
material to evaluate the true cemented hardness and volume of vugs within the
rock
Large voids with or without sediment in filling may be encountered in this material
The voids are believed to have formed either by dissolution of the buried limestone
by groundwater or due to the original depositional character of the reef building
organisms

Coralline Reef Formation
Tan or white or grayish white rock medium hard generally porous organic rock
consisting or coral and some algal reef material The rock typically consists of an
open framework of coral growth with abundant small voids and vugs The rock is
similar to the coral algal limestone except that lesser amounts of algal material and
limestone crystallization are present making the rock somewhat less dense or hard
The rock may be cored with variable recovery to be expected or sampled by SPT
The rock generally ranges from loose to dense depending on the amount of open
framework in the coral material Some coral material contains such a high volume
of internal open framework that blow count densities may appear low loose and
the material sampled by SPT may be mistaken for unconsolidated coralline detritus
Additionally the material densities logged by SPT sampling may vary widely over a
short interval of depth with dense coral immediately underlying and or overlying a
seam of loose material Finally some coral reef may consist of cemented white
calcareous silt and gives the appearance of chalk like material
GEOTECHNICAL CONSIDERATIONS
Based on our geotechnical engineering study of the project site several types of
geotechnical considerations may have potential impacts to the design and re construction
of the sewer lines The geotechnical considerations may include but are not limited to the
following
loose soft ground conditions
high groundwater levels
pipe support in the loose soft ground conditions
existing underground utilitiy lines
New sewer line installation methods consisting of open trench and trenchless
microtunneling and pilot tubing methods are discussed in detail in the following sections
Pipe Support
We anticipate that the proposed new sewer line inverts would generally be located
within the loose soft lagoonal deposits In order to provide support for the new manholes
and pipelines from potential future settlements jet grout columns should be considered for
support of the proposed new sewer line and manholes
Jet grouting is a technique utilizing a special drill bit and injection monitor with radial
horizontal nozzles to produce stabilized soil cement columns The jet grouting technique
is a process that produces soil cement columns by pumping neat cement grout slurry
through horizontal jets injected at high pressures The horizontal jets of cement grout

the drill bit is slowly rotated and withdrawn to form a soil cement column
The jet grout columns would derive vertical support primarily from bearing on the

Open Trench Method
As mentioned previously the project site is underlain by a thin surface fill crust
overlying loose soft lagoonal deposits and coral formation Shallow groundwater is
anticipated along the proposed sewer line alignments
Since groundwater and loose soft soils will likely be encountered at the anticipated
pipe invert elevation of the proposed new sewer lines shoring and dewatering of the
excavation are anticipated for the open trench installation method
Excavation Method
It is anticipated that conventional excavation techniques may be used for the
proposed trench excavations However it should be noted that existing abandoned
underground utilities and other below grade concrete structures may exist at the
site and would require heavy equipment excavation In general the contractor
should determine the method and equipment to be used for excavation subject to
practical limits and safety considerations The excavated soils from the trench
excavation should be stockpiled no closer than a horizontal distance equal to the
depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for
ground movement
Excavation Support
Since the proposed new sewer lines may extend to depths of about 11 to 19 feet
below the existing ground surface within the loose soft lagoonal deposits shoring of
the sides of the trenches will be necessary to facilitate the proposed trench
excavations and to protect personnel working in the trench Excavation support
such as interlocking sheet piles may be used to shore the trench excavations

Even with the use of steel sheet piles we believe that the installation of interlocking
steel sheet piles may be difficult due to the potential presence of coral formation at
the site which may preclude the sheet piling from attaining the proper embedment
Therefore we believe that the sheet pile locations will likely need to be predrilled in
order to achieve the required penetration depths below the ground surface The
pre drilling in the sheet pile locations prior to installation would also reduce
significantly the amount of vibration generated during sheet pile installation
Consideration should also be given to avoiding the use of vibratory hammers for the
installation and extraction of the sheet piles at the site Vibrations generated from
the vibratory hammers may damage adjacent structures and may induce
liquefaction of the underlying loose soft lagoonal deposits
In addition installation of the sheet piles may be difficult due to the significant
amount of existing utility lines at the site Based on the available information
several box culverts cross the proposed sewer line alignments A box culvert
crosses Auahi Street from north to south along Coral Street and box culverts cross
Ala Moana Boulevard from north to south along Ward Avenue Depending on the
depths of these box culverts underpinning of the culverts may be required when
installing the proposed new sewer lines using open trench methods
There is a potential to truncate the existing utility lines during installation of the steel
sheet piles Therefore jet grouted support walls may also be considered for
shoring of the trench excavations Jet grouting is discussed in more detail in the
Pipe Support section herein In addition a typical detail of jet grout pipe support
for open trench is shown on Plate 3
Dewatering
Based on our research and review of available in house soil and boring data in
the project vicinity shallow groundwater is anticipated within the trench
excavations Therefore dewatering of the proposed trenches may be necessary
to facilitate the installation of the proposed sewer lines Based on our experience
in the area the permeability of the subsurface soils at the site may be moderately
to highly permeable
In general the dewatering operation should be conducted in such a manner that
the dewatering will not cause areal ground subsidence which may cause
potential damage to the nearby existing structures Therefore consideration
should be given to a dewatering system that includes a deep cut off wall to
reduce the volume of water to be removed within the excavation and to reduce
the areal extent of groundwater drawdown outside of the trench excavation

Trenchless Methods
Two trenchless methods which provide high accuracy in line and grade may be
considered for installation of new sewer lines at the site The two trenchless methods
include microtunneling and pilot tube microtunneling In general use of these trenchless
techniques may offer the following major advantages or benefits as compared to the
conventional open trench methods of construction
Reduced disturbance and potential damage to adjacent utilities existing
roads and surrounding structures
Improved public and job site safety
Reduced surface disruption to vehicular and pedestrian traffic
Reduced disturbance to local residents and businesses
Reduced air and noise pollution and vibration
Greater flexibility in alignment selection and reduced need for utility
relocations
Reduced need for extensive shoring system
Reduced need for significant dewatering
Smaller volumes of spoils that require transportation and disposal
Reduced potential for treatment of contaminated soil and groundwater
Microtunneling
Microtunneling is an installation method where a pipe is jacked behind a remotely
controlled steerable laser guided tunnel boring machine Jacking and receiving
pits will be required for microtunneling The jacking pit is generally rectangular in
shape to accommodate the length and width of the tunnel boring machine the
jacking frame reaction block and pipe sections to be jacked into place Typically
the jacking pit is about 20 feet long by 16 feet wide The receiving pit is relatively
smaller about 16 feet long by 12 feet wide since it will only accommodate the
tunnel boring machine for retrieval at the end of the sewer pipe installation
Microtunneling is performed with specialized equipment that is very costly For the
microtunneling method to be cost effective the sewer pipe size should be greater
than of 30 inches in diameter Tunneling lengths on the order 1 000 feet are
achievable
In addition to the primary support jet grout columns secondary jet grout columns
will be required along the proposed new sewer line alignments The secondary jet
grout columns would be installed between the primary pipe support columns to
provide a relatively continuous jet grouted zone along the centerline of the sewer
line alignments to facilitate steering of the microtunneling boring machine within the
loose soft lagoonal deposits Typically the secondary jet grout columns would
extend from about 2 feet below the planned invert of the sewer pipe to about 1 foot

Pilot Tube Microtunneling
Pilot Tube Microtunneling PTMT should be considered for installation of pipes
having a diameter of 30 inches or less PTMT uses a guidance system consisting
of a camera mounted theodolite and a Light Emitting Diode LED target that is
located in the steering head
The first step of this method consists of drilling a pilot hole by pushing pilot tubes
smaller diameter than pipe size which is attached to the steering head from the
jacking to the receiving pit The pilot tubes are steered to achieve the required line
and grade of the sewer line After the steering head reaches the receiving pit the
pilot hole is enlarged slightly larger than the product pipe by attaching a reamer
followed by steel casing with augers inside to the end of the pilot tubes The casing
is pushed through while the augers rotate and remove the soil cuttings As the
reamer and steel casing are advanced the pilot tube sections are being retrieved at
the receiving pit After the reamer reaches the receiving pit the product pipe is
attached to the end of the casing and pushed through until the product pipe
reaches the receiving pit As the product pipe is advanced the casing sections are
being retrieved at the receiving pit
The jacking and receiving pits are either circular about 8 to 12 feet in diameter or
rectangular about 10 feet long by 8 feet wide in shape PTMT drive lengths are
on the order of about 250 feet The anticipated subsurface conditions at the site
should not pose any issues with PTMT installation however PTMT may not be a
viable option if the sewer line invert depth is about 10 feet below the groundwater
level
Additional Geotechnical Field Exploration
Based on preliminary geotechnical engineering study the project site may be
underlain by loose soft lagoonal deposits at shallow depths with high groundwater levels
Because of the potential problematic soil and groundwater conditions that may exist at the
project site it is highly recommended that a comprehensive geotechnical field exploration
be performed for the design of the project
