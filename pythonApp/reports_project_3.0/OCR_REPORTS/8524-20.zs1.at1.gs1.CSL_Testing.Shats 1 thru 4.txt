As requested, we performed Crosshole Sonic Logging (CSL) testing on Shaft
Nos. 1 through 4 installed for the above project. Based on our field observations and the
CSL test results, the following results and recommendations are presented for your review
and consideration.
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project specification, Section 02200 — Drilled Shafts, requires performing CSL
testing on the drilled shafts installed for the project. We performed CSL testing on June 22
and July 5, 2023 for Shaft Nos.1 through 4 installed for the above project.
Shaft No.1 was drilled from June 20 to 21, 2023, followed by concrete placement
on June 22, 2023. Shaft No. 2 was drilled on June 27, 2023, followed by concrete
placement on June 28, 2023. Shaft No. 3 was drilled on June 23 to 26, 2023, followed by
concrete placement on June 26, 2023. Shaft No. 4 was drilled on June 29, 2023, followed
by concrete placement on June 30, 2023. The approximate locations of the shafts are
presented on Site Plans, Plates 1.1 and 1.2. Five 2-inch diameter steel CSL access tubes
were installed along the fabricated reinforcing cage for each shaft. Details of the drilled
shaft as-built conditions are summarized in the following table:

CROSSHOLE SONIC LOGGING TESTING 
In general, the CSL testing was conducted in accordance with ASTM D6760 using 
the CHUM CSL Testing equipment manufactured by Piletest. Crosshole Sonic Logging 
tests were performed between all the possible access tube combinations in the drilled 
shaft (five tubes resulting in ten profiles). 
Shaft Nos. 1 and 3, the northernmost access tube, was designated as Tube 1 and 
subsequent tubes were numbered in a counterclockwise direction. This was due to the 
layout of the tubes in these shafts, where the north half of the shaft contained three tubes, 
and the south half contained two tubes. 
Shaft Nos. 2 and 4, the southernmost access tube, was designated as Tube 1 and 
subsequent tubes were numbered in a counterclockwise direction. This was because the 
tube layout in these shafts was reversed, with two tubes in the north half and three tubes 
in the south half. 
The steel access tubes were extended an average of approximately 3.3 to 4.1 feet 
above the top of the drilled shaft concrete for access considerations. We understand that 
potable water was used to fill the access tubes after concrete placement, and the water 
level was maintained in the access tubes until CSL testing. Based on our observation in 
the field, the water levels within the access tubes were above the top of the shaft concrete 
in the five access tubes at the time of testing. 
The CSL test results are based on the percentage decrease in velocity as 
correlated to the following Concrete Condition Rating Criteria (CCRC). Deviations from the 
following values are generally used for determining the Concrete Condition Rating. The 
CCRC is summarized in the table below.

As listed in the table above, increases in First Arrival Times (FATs) of 10 percent or 
less may be considered insignificant. Increases in FATs greater than 10 percent are 
considered a failure to meet the specified “Good” Concrete Condition Rating and coring 
may be required to intercept the suspected or defective zones. 
The average apparent sonic wave velocity can be determined from the CSL test 
results by dividing the average tube distances by the average first arrival times for each 
tube combination in a drilled shaft. It should be noted that the average apparent sonic 
wave velocity computed in this manner is not an absolute number and should be used with 
caution because the actual tube distances below the top of the drilled shaft are not known 
accurately. 
FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
A summary of the CSL test results, including the tested concrete length, computed 
apparent sonic wave velocity, and comments on the concrete condition, are presented on 
Plate 2. The CSL test results for the drilled shafts tested are summarized on Plates 3.1 
through 3.4. The individual CSL test plots for each tested tube combination (profile) and a 
plan showing the tube locations are presented on Plates 4.1 and 4.16.  
Based on the CSL test results, the increased first arrival times (FATs) were 
generally less than 10 percent, corresponding to a calculated wave velocity reduction of 
less than 10 percent, and the wave energy was relatively uniform in all the profiles for 
Shaft Nos. 1 through 4, except for the areas within the profile as described below. It should 
be noted that changes in the relative energy can sometimes indicate defects depending on 
the degree of the reduction and the associated increase in FAT.  
Shaft No. 2 
• Profile 1-3: FAT increases between 10.2 and 17.1 percent from 4.9 to 6.4 
feet below the top of the concrete. 
• Profile 2-4: FAT increases between 10.0 and 19.6 percent from 19.9 to 21.5 
feet below the top of the concrete. 
Shaft No.3 
• Profile 1-4: FAT increases between 10.4 and 14.8 percent from 10.8 to 12.7 
feet below the top of the concrete. 
• Profile 2-4: FAT increases between 10.0 and 13.6 percent from 1.0 to 2.6 
feet below the top of the concrete.

The average apparent sonic wave velocities for Shaft No. 1 ranged from 
approximately 9,380 to 15,536 feet per second. Based on the results of the CSL test and 
FAT increases of less than 10 percent, the concrete integrity of Shaft No. 1 within the 
testing depth would be classified as “Good” based on the above CCRC. 
The average apparent sonic wave velocities for Shaft No. 2 ranged from 
approximately 9,173 to 15,568 feet per second. Based on the results of the CSL tests and 
FAT increases greater than 10% but less than 20%, the concrete integrity of Shaft No. 2 
within the testing depth would be classified as “Questionable” based on the above CCRC. 
The average apparent sonic wave velocities for Shaft No. 3 ranged from 
approximately 8,835 to 13,461 feet per second. Based on the results of the CSL tests and 
FAT increases greater than 10% but less than 20%, the concrete integrity of Shaft No. 3 
within the testing depth would be classified as “Questionable” based on the above CCRC. 
The average apparent sonic wave velocities for Shaft No. 4 ranged from 
approximately 9,204 to 15,093 feet per second. Based on the results of the CSL tests and 
FAT increases greater than 10% but less than 20%, the concrete integrity of Shaft No. 4 
within the testing depth would be classified as “Questionable” based on the above CCRC. 
Based on the review of the CSL testing data, the areas having FAT increase 
greater than 10% and 20% in Shaft Nos. 2 through 4 are localized and relatively low with 
relative uniform energy level.  It should be noted that the FAT increases measured above 
are between CSL tubes that cross with the steel beam in the middle of the rebar cage. 
Since no significant anomalies were measured between the CSL tubes within the 
concrete portion of the shaft and localized and relatively low FAT increases between the 
CSL tubes crossing the middle steel beam, we recommend acceptance of Shaft Nos. 1 
through 4 for the project.
