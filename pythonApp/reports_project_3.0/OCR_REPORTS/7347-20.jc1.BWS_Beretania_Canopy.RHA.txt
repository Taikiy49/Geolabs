This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering consultation for 
the BWS Beretania Canopy Roof Cover project in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location 
Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field observation of test pits and 
presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the project. 
These recommendations are intended for the design of shallow foundation and 
slabs-on-grade only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject 
to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project involves the design and construction of a new canopy roof cover for 
electrical equipment at the BWS Beretania facility. It was desired to conduct a limited 
field exploration using test pits to confirm foundation bearing stratum at the site. 
Due to limited space available, the test pit method utilizing a Komatsu PC35 
excavator with an 18-inch wide bucket was selected. Based on the available structural 
drawings, we envision relatively light structural loads from the new canopy structure.  
We envision the subsurface conditions consisting of surface fills over weathered 
basalt formation. Special attention was given to characterizing the thickness of the 
surface fills and depth of the rock formation for the bearing support.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone eruptions. These later eruptions are 
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present sea level.

When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
The project site is on the southwestern flank of Punchbowl Hill (Puowaina), near 
the center of Honolulu, built against the end of a spur of the Koolau Range. The 
geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of in-situ chemical weathering of 
basaltic lava flows. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions by excavating three test pits, within the 
new canopy roof cover footprint, extending to depths of about 2 to 3 feet below the 
ground surface.  The approximate test pit locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the project site generally is underlain by surface fill materials 
consisting of gravel and/or base course materials extending to depths of about 2 to 
2.5 feet below the existing ground/pavement surface. The surface fill materials generally 
were underlain by weathered basalt formation varying in tannish to brownish colors 
extending to deeper depths. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the test pits at the time of our field 
exploration.  It should be noted that groundwater levels are expected to fluctuate with 
variation in rainfall, surface water runoff, groundwater withdrawal, and other factors. 
SEISMIC DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS 
We understand that the project site may be subjected to seismic activity, and 
seismic design considerations will need to be addressed in accordance with Section 
1613 of the International Building Code (2006). Our field exploration generally 
encountered thin surface fills overlying weathered basalt rock formation extending to the 
greater depth. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the test pits and geologic 
setting of the site, the project site may be classified as a “Very Dense Soil and Soft 
Rock”, Site Class “C”. 
SHALLOW FOUNDATIONS 
In general, we believe the new canopy roof cover structure may be supported on 
a shallow foundation system consisting of spread footings bearing on the in-situ 
weathered rock formation.  Our field exploration indicated that the weathered rock

formation may be expected at shallow depths ranging from 2 to 2.5 feet below the 
ground surface underlying thin surface fills.  
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may 
be used to design shallow foundations bearing on the in-situ weathered rock formation. 
This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for 
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the 
lowest adjacent exterior grade.  Where surface fill materials are exposed at the footing 
subgrades, the fill materials should be removed to expose the underlying rock formation 
or to the maximum depth of about 2 feet below the bottom of the footing. The 
over-excavation should be backfilled with granular structural fills up to the bottom of the 
footing and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The 
over-excavation should also extend laterally beyond the footing edge a minimum of 
2 feet. 
The structural fill material should consist of imported non-expansive, select 
granular materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
Foundations next to retaining walls, other foundations, utility trenches, or 
easements should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward 
from the bottom edge of the structure or utility trench. Alternatively, footings should be 
extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines or bottom of the retaining 
walls. This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade 
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable 
foundation settlement. 
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate that total footing settlements may be on the order of 
1 inch or less. We estimate the differential settlements between adjacent foundations to 
be on the order of about 0.5 inches. 
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed 
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth 
pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system.  A coefficient 
of friction of 0.40 may be used to evaluate the sliding resistance between the bottom of 
the foundation and the weathered rock formation.  Resistance due to passive earth 
pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square 
foot per foot of depth (pcf).  This assumes that the backfill around the foundation is well 
compacted.  Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 
12 inches of soil should be neglected.

Resistance to uplift loads may be mobilized by the dead weight of the structure 
and the footing.  In addition, the weight of the soil below the ground floor slab-on-grade

SLABS-ON-GRADE 
For the interior building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic), we recommend 
providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs.  The open-graded gravel cushion fill 
would serve as a capillary moisture break and would provide for uniform support of the 
slabs.  To reduce the potential for excessive future moisture infiltration through the slab 
and subsequent damage to floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is 
recommended on top of the open-graded gravel cushion layer. 
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic, we recommend providing a 
6-inch layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs in lieu of the 4-inch thick gravel 
cushion layer.  The moisture barrier may be omitted for these slabs.  The aggregate 
subbase should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction.  For the design of structural slabs supported on 
6 inches of aggregate subbase, a modulus of subgrade reaction of about 200 pounds 
per square inch per inch of deflection (pci) may be used for the slab supported on the 
recompacted in-situ soils.  Where slabs are intended to function as rigid pavements for 
trucks, a minimum slab thickness of 6 inches may be used for preliminary design 
purposes.  In addition, provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the 
slab joints that will be subjected to vehicular traffic. 
We envision that some exterior concrete walkways may be required for the 
proposed project. We recommend supporting the concrete sidewalks on at least 
6 inches of non-expansive select granular fill compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction.  The non-expansive select granular fill materials should be placed 
as soon as possible after the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the 
recommendations presented in the following section.  The non-expansive select 
granular fill materials and the underlying subgrade soils should be wetted and kept 
moist until the final placement of concrete. To reduce the potential for substantial 
shrinkage cracks in the slabs, crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal 
to the width of the walkways with expansion joints provided at right-angle intersections.

The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at

EARTHWORK 
At the on-set of earthwork, the areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious material and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site.  Soft and 
yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be over-excavated to 
expose firm or dense material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted engineered fill. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be 
properly disposed of off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, existing structures and pavements that are to be 
demolished should be removed completely. Over-excavations resulting from demolition 
should be backfilled with compacted on-site soils or select granular fill materials. 
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed, and the resulting excavation 
should be properly backfilled with excavated on-site soils or select granular fill materials 
placed in 8-inch loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. Utilities to be abandoned in-place under the proposed structures should be 
backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure. 
Imported structural fills should consist of non-expansive, select granular fill 
materials, such as crushed coral and basalt.  The materials should be well graded from 
coarse to fine with particles no greater than 3 inches in largest dimension.  The 
materials should have a California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a 
swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.  The 
materials also should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 
sieve.  The structural fill material should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in 
loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
General fill materials needed to fill the site may consist of materials with a low to 
moderate expansion potential, such as the on-site soils generated from the cut areas.  
Imported general fill materials should consist of materials with particle sizes of 6 inches 
or less in maximum dimension and should be free of deleterious materials.  The 
material should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 12 or higher 
and a swell potential of no more than 1 percent when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D1883.  General fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in 
loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.

DRAINAGE 
The finished grades outside the building structure should be sloped to shed water 
away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding.  
Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided.  
Planters next to foundations (within 3 feet) should be avoided or have concrete bottoms 
and drains to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface. 
To reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface, the 
foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges 
immediately after setting of the concrete.  In addition, drainage swales should be 
provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water runoff 
away from the foundations and slabs. 
LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted in this letter report are based in 
part upon the information obtained from our exploration and our experience at the 
project vicinity. Variations of subsoil conditions may occur, and the nature and extent of 
these variations may not become evident until construction is underway.  If variations 
then appear evident, Geolabs should be contacted to re-evaluate the recommendations 
presented herein. 
The field test pit locations indicated herein are approximate, having been 
estimated by taping from the features shown on the Foundation Plan provided by 
Ronald N.S. Ho & Associates Inc. on December 6, 2016. The physical locations should 
be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method used. 
Water level data from the test pits were measured at the times presented in the 
text of this report.  This data has been reviewed and interpretations made in the 
formulation of this report.  It must be noted that fluctuations may occur due to variation 
in rainfall, temperature, and other factors.
