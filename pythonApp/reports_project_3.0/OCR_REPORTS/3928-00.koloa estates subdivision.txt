INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Koloa Estates Subdivision project located in the Koloa area on the
island of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of site grading
pavements and sewage pump station slab foundation The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a 32 lot residential subdivision known as Koloa Estates
in the Koloa area on the island of Kauai Hawaii We understand that the Koloa Estates
Subdivision project is the first component of a major residential community called the
Koloa Project The long range master plan for the Koloa Project envisions an 18 hole
golf course a network of parks landscaped roadways a community shopping center and
a variety of single family and multi family residential units
The Koloa Estates Subdivision will have about 30 residential lots and two larger
lots for church congregations The residential lots will be about 0 5 acres each The
proposed subdivision construction will consist primarily of on site and off site
infrastructure improvements including roadways water lines storm drains and sanitary
sewer lines In addition a sewage pump station is planned near the southern portion of
the site Lot grading and house construction are not included in the scope of this project

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lava of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lava of
the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as thick flows of
dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various
locales Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low
cinder cones at the vent Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern
half of the island of Kauai
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill
the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Kauai remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and
lower relative to the present sea level on Kauai
Most of the soils in the project area are residual and saprolite soils derived from
the in situ weathering of igneous rocks In general residual and saprolite soils grade to
weathered basalt formations with depth Agricultural developments within the last century
have brought the project site to its present conditions

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The Koloa Estates Subdivision project is located in the Koloa area on the island of
Kauai Hawaii The project site is bounded by Poipu Road to the southeast and a
chain linked fence and rock walls to the northeast as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the majority of the project site is level to gently sloping from north to
south Most of the open and nearly level portions of the site are currently being used for
agricultural purposes Based on the topographic map provided the elevations at the site
range from about 116 feet Mean Sea Level MSL to 180 feet MSL Several access
roads traverse the agricultural lands Based on our field reconnaissance of the site the
vegetation is generally light consisting of some crops and native grasses However
large trees and bushes are present at localized areas In addition piles of rocks were
present at various locations throughout the site during our field exploration
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the subject site were explored by drilling and
sampling five borings to a depth of about 20 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Generally our field exploration encountered surficial fills alluvial soils or residual
soils of less than 3 feet below the existing ground surface consisting of very stiff to hard
clayey silts with gravels Below the near surface soils relatively unweathered to highly
weathered basalt formation was encountered to the maximum depth explored of about
20 feet below the existing ground surface The borings indicated that the basalt formation
was interbedded with layers of clinker materials of about 2 feet thick In addition several
cavities and or voids of about 1 foot in height were also encountered in the borings Lava
tubes and blisters are commonly encountered in basalt lava flows However the size and
extent of these lava tubes and blisters can be highly variable

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered a relatively thin mantle of very stiff to
hard clayey silts over highly weathered to relatively unweathered basalt rock formation
across the project site The basalt rock formation was encountered at depths ranging
from about 1 to 8 5 feet below the existing ground surface In general we believe that the
on site silty soils and basalt rock fragments generated from the planned excavations may
be reused as a source of fill material provided that they are properly processed and
placed as recommended in the Site Grading section of this report Some screening of
the over sized materials generated from the on site excavations may be necessary prior
to using them as fill materials
Based on our laboratory tests the near surface clayey silt soils generally have a
slight potential for shrink swell when subjected to changes in the moisture content
Therefore we recommend that the pavement subgrade be over excavated a minimum of
6 inches below the base course layer and be replaced with compacted select borrow
subbase in areas where the clayey silt soils are exposed at the subgrade level However

that the 6 inch select borrow subbase layer may be eliminated As a result we

recommended pavement sections where appropriate If possible we recommend that

Based on the gently sloping terrain at the subject site we anticipate that site
grading work for the proposed subdivision development will generally consist of cuts and
fills less than about 10 feet in height In addition trench excavations of up to about
10 feet in depth may be required for the installation of various utility lines Items of
grading that are addressed in the following subsections include the following 1 Site
Preparation 2 Fills and Backfills 3 Boulder Disposal 4 Fill Placement and Compaction

Site grading operations should be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office observe the site
grading operations to evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the
excavation process and to confirm that the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to

reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials Excavated rock
materials greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension may be disposed of in
deep fill areas as recommended in the Boulder Disposal subsection of this report
The near surface silty soils encountered in cut areas may be reused as a source of
fill material provided that the recommendations presented in the Fill Placement
and Compaction Requirements subsection are properly followed
After clearing and grubbing areas designated to receive fills should be scarified to
a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage
by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the subgrade
should be proof rolled and a filter fabric such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent should
be placed on the subgrade prior to placement of fill material Where shrinkage
cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soil be
thoroughly moistened to close cracks or scarified moisture conditioned and
recompacted Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft
areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill at no additional cost to
the Owner
In areas where the cut subgrades expose basalt rock formation the subgrades
should be proof rolled with a minimum 20 ton vibratory roller or a Caterpillar D 9
bulldozer or similar heavy equipment for a minimum of 6 passes to help detect and
collapse near surface cavities Loose areas or cavities disclosed during clearing
and proof rolling operations should be backfilled with well compacted engineered

Fills and Backfills
The fill and backfill materials to be used as general fill material within the upper
4 feet from the finish subgrades should consist of well graded materials less than
6 inches in largest dimension In general the near surface silty soils encountered
during our field exploration should be suitable for use as general fill materials
provided that they are moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction The on site cut
materials generated from excavations into the underlying basalt formation may be
used as general fill or backfill materials within the upper 4 feet from the finish
subgrades provided that they are screened of the over sized materials greater
than 6 inches in largest dimension and processed to meet the above gradation
requirements
This requirement is to facilitate future construction of building foundations and
utility trench excavations Over sized rock fragments may be used in deep fills or
broken down to smaller sized materials and incorporated into the fill material If
the excavated materials do not contain sufficient fines to produce a well graded
material off site borrow or on site rock crushing of large sized rock fragments or
boulders should be considered to provide the required gradation and particle sizes
to develop a well graded material
On site materials consisting of boulders up to 3 feet in diameter may be used as
rock fill provided that the top of the rock fill layer is placed at least 4 feet below the

finish subgrades In general placement of boulders in fills should be performed in
accordance with the procedures recommended in the Boulder Disposal

If imported material is required the material should be non expansive granular
material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in

Boulder Disposal
Over sized materials such as cobbles greater than 6 inches in dimension and
boulders i e rock fragments larger than 12 inches in size are anticipated from
cuts into the basalt formation and may be used in general and rock fills provided
that the following recommendations are followed
1 Boulders should not be placed in general fills within 4 feet of the finish
subgrades
2 Boulders up to 3 feet in largest dimension may be placed at the bottom of
deep fills greater than 7 feet in depth and must not be nested together
They should be placed such that the compaction equipment will be able to
compact between and around them
3 Boulders up to 3 feet in maximum dimension should not encroach within
4 feet of the finish subgrades or within 2 feet horizontally or within 2 feet
vertically of utility inverts
4 Care must be exercised to avoid placement of boulders in the proposed
utility alignment and within the depths of the proposed utility lines to reduce
the potential for encountering boulders during excavation of the utility
trenches

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to at
least 90 percent relative compaction Backfills behind retaining structures should
be compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction
Over compaction of the retaining structure backfill would exert additional lateral
earth pressures on the structure and should be avoided Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content corresponding to
the maximum dry density Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot
rollers vibratory rollers multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of
acceptable compaction equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
boulders Placement should be by haul trucks traversing previously placed lifts
and dumping at the edge of the currently placed lift Spreading of the rock fill
should be by a bulldozer to facilitate seating of the rock The rock fill should be
watered heavily during placement by water trucks traversing in front of the current
rock lift face and spraying water continuously during placement Compaction
equipment such as a 20 ton compactor or other similar compaction equipment
providing suitable energy to achieve the required compaction should be utilized
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rock boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number
of passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction
should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the
observation of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii On the basis of this testing
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3928 00 Page 12
program the number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for
adequate compaction
Excavation
Our field exploration disclosed that the site is underlain by fractured basalt rock
ranging from medium hard to very hard within 1 to 8 5 feet below the existing
ground surface It is anticipated that the basalt formation may be excavated with
normal heavy excavation equipment such as ripping with large bulldozers where
the rock is near the existing ground surface However excavations into the
deeper formations and localized hard areas may require the use of hoerams
chipping or blasting
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based
on field data obtained from the borings performed at the subject site We
recommend that contractors be encouraged to examine the site conditions and soil
data to make their own interpretation
Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings it appears that
most of the cut slopes would expose the stiff silty soils and relatively dense basalt
rock with frequent fractures In general we believe that the cut slopes planned at
the site should be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical
2H 1V or flatter Where the cut slopes expose dense basalt formation at the
slope face the cut slope may be steepened to a slope inclination of 1H 1V
Cavities that may be exposed on the cut slope face should be backfilled and or
grouted if necessary
Permanent fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or
flatter We also recommend that V ditches or earth berms be provided at the
tops and bottoms of slopes In addition we also recommend that erosion control
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3928 00 Page 13
measures be implemented as soon as the slopes are completed to reduce the
potential for erosion
Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling
operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal
compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope
ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face In the event that over cutting of a
slope occurs keying and benching requirements should be implemented instead
of backfilling the slope to the design grade with sliver fills Alternatively cut slopes
may be buttressed with fill slopes constructed of non expansive fills Water should
be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided
as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes

foundations bearing on the recompacted silty soils an allowable bearing pressure

foundations The allowable bearing pressure may be increased to for
foundations bearing on the underlying basalt rock formation These bearing
values are for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces

Utilities
New utility lines including water lines storm drains and sanitary sewer lines will be
required for development of this project It is anticipated that most of the trenches for
utilities will be excavated in the near surface soils and or hard basalt rock formation or
rock fill As previously recommended placement of boulders within the utility trench
corridors and within the anticipated depths of the utility lines should be avoided
In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be
used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate
support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3928 00 Page 19
reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical that free draining materials be
used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes which could result in settlement of
the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site soils or granular
materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum size The backfill material should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in maximum 8 inch level
loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to
reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement
areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling five
borings to a depth of approximately 20 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings
were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
