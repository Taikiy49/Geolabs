for the Smith Beretania Park project located in Downtown Honolulu on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location
Map Pilate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the design of foundations slabs on grade basement walls
site preparation and shoring only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age
After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep valleys into
the Koolau Shield voicanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and
tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is situated over the edge of a former stream which traversed through
this area The base member or bedrock consists of basaltic lava flow from the Honolulu
GEOLABS INC
W O 2434 10 Page 5
Volcanic Series The bedrock was deeply scoured by stream erosion at the time when the sea
level was lowered to as much as 300 feet below its present elevation As the great glaciers
melted and the sea level rose erosion of the bedrock ceased and extended periods of
deposition followed The deposits generally consist of alluvium and ash washed down from
the upper valleys
The alluvium was subsequently covered by a sandy beach deposit and eventually
covered by a coralline reef formation that developed from accretion of coralline debris when
the sea level rose to an elevation higher than its present level As glaciers formed causing
the sea level to drop the reef deposit was subjected to cementation As the sea level
continued downward the upper coralline and alluvial deposits were also dissected by stream
erosion As the sea level rose again to its present level the stream channels were filled with
fine grained and organic soils which were deposited in a quiet water lagoonal environment
Finally the area was filled within the last century for residential and commercial development

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
eight borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 8 extending to depths ranging from about
26 to 71 5 feet below the surface of the existing parking lot The approximate locations of the
borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration at the existing parking lot generally encountered an asphaltic
concrete pavement of about 1 to 2 inches thick Below the asphaltic concrete surface of the
parking lot our borings generally encountered a surface fill layer about 5 to 6 feet thick
consisting of materials ranging from loose silty sands to medium stiff clayey to sandy silts In
Boring No 5 thin layers of cinder sands and brown silts were encountered at the bottom of
the surface fill layer
Underlying ihe surface fill encountered coral formation which
generally extended to depths of about 20 to 30 feet below the existing ground surface Based
on the results of our exploration the upper 5 to 7 feet of the coral formation below the surface
fill layer is relatively hard and well cemented However with increasing depth the degree of
cementation decreases and the coral formation generally grades to medium dense to dense
and is in a fragmented condition
Below the coral formation the borings generally encountered stiff to very stiff alluvial
soil deposits consisting of sandy silts and silty sands with some weathered basalt gravel
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 71 5 feet below the existing ground
surface
Groundwater levels were encountered at depths of about 24 to 27 feet below the
existing pavement surface at the time of our field exploration It should be noted that our field
exploration was conducted in January 1990 The groundwater levels measured generally
corresponded to between about Elevations 1 0 and 3 8 feet MSL Normally groundwater
levels near shoreline areas are generally encountered between Elevation 0 and 2 feet MSL

encountered at the site we believe that shaliow spread and or continuous footings may be
used for support of the proposed underground parking structure An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 4 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of footing

The foundations should bear directly on the relatively hard well cemented coral
formation If fragmented coral formation is encountered at the foundation subgrade level the
material should be scarified to a depth of about 10 inches moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations they should
be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation should be
backfilled with select granular fill material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying competent
material Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades If foundations are located next to utility ttenches or easements they
should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate the total foundation settlements to be on the order of about
1 inch We estimate that differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on
the coral formation to be on the order of about 0 5 inches

Foundation Probing
Cavities and or voids are commonly encountered within the coral formations in Hawaii
To reduce the poieniial for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities and or
voids below foundations we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting at the
foundation locations be implemented during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probe holes be drilled at each spread
footing location one probe hole for every 50 square feet of footing area and at 10 feet on
center along wall foundations The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and
should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation elevation if cavities
and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probe
holes should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the vertical and lateral extent of
the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with
sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures We recommend that a low strength
sand cement grout with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the grouting operations
In lieu of the sand cement grout fluid lean concrete such as controlled low strength material
flowable fill may also be used for the grouting operation
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during bidding In

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be utilized for the basement floor of the
parking structure and the entrance driveway ramp These slabs will be subjected to vehicular
traffic and will function as rigid pavements Therefore we recommend that a 6 inch layer of
aggregate subbase be provided directly below the slabs to support the slabs subjected to
dynamic loads due to the vehicles The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction For design of structural floor
slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per cubic inch per inch of deflection pci
may be used for the compacted aggregate subbase We recommend that the concrete slabs
be designed by a structural engineer for the intended loads Provisions should be made for
proper load transfer across the slab joints that will be subjected to vehicular traffic
Slab subgrades below the aggregate subbase should be proof rolled with a minimum
10 ton vibratory drum roller or similar heavy equipment a minimum of six passes to detect
the presence of potential near surface cavities or loose zones If the proof rolling operations
detect a near surface cavity or loose zone the area should be ripped open cleaned of debris
and then backfilled with compacted fill material or lean concrete The proof rolling operations
should be conducted under the near continuous observation of a representative from Geolabs
during the site preparation

Excavation Support
The basement construction for the Smith Beretania Park will involve a large excavation
in a well developed urban area In addition the basement excavation will abut or be
within proximity to the existing improvements along the boundaries of the site Based
on the available plans and information the existing 22 story high rise building adjacent
to the site is supported by a 4 foot thick mat foundation bearing on the coral formation
A covered walkway runs along the northwest side of the tower adjacent to the existing
parking lot It appears that the limits of the proposed deep excavation will be located
about 8 feet from the edge of the existing mat foundation In addition the proposed
excavation will be setback a lateral distance of about feet from the one story
commercial buildings along Nuuanu Avenue Plans for the foundations of these
buildings were not available for use in the preparation of this report However we
believe that the foundations of these existing structures generally bear on the upper fill
materials at shallow depths
Therefore the planned basement excavation should be adequately shored to reduce
the potential for damage to the surrounding buildings utility lines and streets caused
by earth movements toward the basement excavation Excavated materials should not
be stockpiled adjacent to the planned excavation within a horizontal distance equal to
the depth of the excavation to reduce the potential for significant ground movements
The excavation support and shoring system used is the responsibility of the contractor
and must comply with all applicable Federal State and local safety requirements

Based on the competent coral formation encountered at the project site we believe that
use of steel sheet pile shoring will likely encounter difficulties due to the potential for
penetration refusal during the sheet pile installation operations In lieu of steel sheet
piling we believe that a soldier pile and lagging with or without tiebacks shoring
system a soil nail earth retention system and or chemical grouting may be used for
shoring support of the proposed basement excavation The use of horizontal bracing
or struts inside the excavation will not be practical since the anticipated width of the
excavation is over 120 feet
Lateral Earth Pressures for Shoring
We believe that a cantilever soldier pile and lagging system which mobilizes lateral
load resistance through the use of passive pressure on the portion of the soldier pile
that extends below the bottom of the basement excavation may be used for some
portions of the basement excavation The soldier piles should consist of wide flange
steel beams and heavy timbers should be used for the lagging The lateral earth
pressures presented in the Basement Walls section of this report may be considered
in the design of a cantilever shoring system
Tied Back Shoring System
For temporary shoring along the 22 story high rise building adjacent to the excavation
a restrained shoring system consisting of soldier pile and lagging with temporary
tiebacks may be considered for shoring purposes A tied back wall is a structural
system that uses an anchor embedded in the ground to secure a tendon which applies
a lateral force on the retaining structure Lateral load resistance can be mobilized
through the post tensioned tiebacks and through the use of passive pressure acting on
the foundation members extending below the bottom of the basement excavation
Tiebacks are advantageous since they are external to the excavation and permanent
construction may proceed without hindrance from internal bracing Tiebacks have
been employed primarily for temporary excavations generally utilizing soldier piles with
lagging for soil retention or mixed in place soil wall Since tiebacks will encroach into

W O 2434 10 Page 18
and below the adjacent properties permission for encroachment from the owners of
the adjacent properties should be obtained prior to the tieback design installation
Soil Nail Earth Retention System
As an alternative to the cantilever soldier pile shoring and or tied back wall system a
soil nail earth retention system may be considered for shoring of the basement
excavation A soil nail earth retention system consists of a series of individual
reinforcing bars grouted into drilled holes to stabilize an excavation or slope A
shotcrete facing is generally applied to the face of the excavation to provide the
appearance of a conventional concrete retaining wall The reinforcing bars provide
both additional tensile and shear strengths to the soil and reinforce the sidewall of the
excavation The reinforced soil mass behaves in a manner similar to a gravity retaining
wall except that it lacks a footing
Chemical Grouting
Consideration may also be given to the use of chemical grouting to stabilize the
excavation planned for the proposed development By pressure injecting low viscosity
chemical grouts into the granular soils it is possible to form a strong sandstone like
material The cohesion added by the chemical grout provides increased bearing
capacity or prevents loosening caused by the excavation The chemically injected soil
mass also behaves in a manner similar to a gravity retaining wall
Underpinning of Existing Structures
The proposed basement excavation is located within about 8 feet of the existing
22 story high rise building located at the eastern portion of the site Since the high rise
building is supported on a mat foundation bearing on the in situ coral formation we
believe that underpinning of the high rise building may not be necessary provided that
an adequately designed and properly installed shoring system is used along the face
of the excavation

Installation Considerations
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His her representative who should be required to be
continuously present on site during excavation and construction work will have the best
opportunity to promptly observe any changing conditions during construction such as
unforeseen subsoils unexpectedly high groundwater table inappropriate construction
sequence or techniques etc which may adversely affect shoring stability
However it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation
Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to accurately estimate the
magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship
such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing tieback supports are installed
on the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical In addition considering the depth of the excavation
we recommend that the adjacent ground be continuously monitored for cracks dips
and or other indications of movements with instruments which is further discussed in
the Instrumentation and Monitoring section of this report

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility connections such as water sewer and drain lines will be
required for the project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be
used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes or 12 inches above
the groundwater level It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides
of the pipes Improper backfill material and placement could result in settlement of the backfill
and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes or
groundwater level to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated
on site soils The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less
than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence
Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the
pavement surface should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling eight
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 8 extending to depths ranging from about 26 to
71 5 feet below the existing pavement surface The approximate locations of the borings
drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled with a truck mounted drill
rig equipped with hollow stem augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The biow counts needed to drive the sampier the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sampie depths
Core samples of the rock coral formations encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Four sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 ASTM D 1140 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B 1
