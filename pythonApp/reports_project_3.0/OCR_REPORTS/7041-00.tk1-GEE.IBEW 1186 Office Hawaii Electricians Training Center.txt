SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the available subsurface information, the project site is generally 
underlain by compacted fill materials extending to depths of up to about 14.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The surface fill is generally underlain by alluvial deposit, 
consisting of hard to medium stiff silty clays with varying amounts of sands and gravel, 
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 20.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at depths between 
about 12.6 and 13.8 feet in the 2005 field exploration conducted by others. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we believe that 
shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous footings may be used to 
support the proposed new construction. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf 
may be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted on-site 
soils. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) 
for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Based on the laboratory test results, the near-surface fill soils exhibit slight 
shrink/swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture content. 
Therefore, special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation requirements and 
the slab-on-grade design. To reduce the potential for distress to the future slabs-on-grade 
resulting from swelling of the near-surface fill soils, we recommend providing a minimum of 
6 inches of non-expansive, select granular fill material below the slab cushion. 
We understand that a Keystone Reinforced Soil Retaining Wall (KRSRW) system is 
planned at the rear end of the property. The system combines the Keystone modular 
concrete wall units with geogrid reinforcement and compacted soil to result in a reinforced 
soil mass that supports the earth pressure and surcharge loads. Design of the KRSRW will 
need to take into consideration both external and internal stabilities of the structure. In 
evaluating external stability, the KRSRW structure must satisfy four stability conditions:  
(1) bearing failure; (2) translational sliding; (3) overturning stability; and (4) overall slope 
stability. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene 
Epoch). The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located between these 
two volcanoes. The project site is situated on the isthmus connecting the two volcanic 
domes. 
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts 
of the two volcanoes. During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fell to stands lower than 
present sea level exposing large areas of coralline sand on what was the ocean bottom. 
Wind action transported these coralline sands inland forming extensive areas of sand 
dunes. In some areas, these dunes have been mantled by man-made fill which was 
apparently placed during the period that the area was used for the cultivation of sugar 
cane. 
2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is at Lot 7-A on the northern side of Ala Makani Street within the 
Maui Business Park Phase 1 industrial subdivision development in the town of Kahului 
on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The lot is a trapezoidal-shaped parcel encompassing 
approximately ½ acre. 
Based on our field observations, the project site is generally open vacant land 
with approximately 2 to 3 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel placed at the front side of the 
property. The overall site is generally covered with knee-high weeds. A drainage 
easement consisting of a 5-foot by 4-foot box culvert runs alongside the northern 
boundary of the lot.  
The topography across the majority of the project site is relatively level with 
existing ground surface elevations ranging between about +16 to +17 feet MSL, except 
at the northern side of the property where it slopes down to about +10 feet MSL towards 
Dairy Road.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
Based on the field exploration conducted in 2005 by others, it appears that the 
project site is underlain by a surface mantle of fill extending to depths of about 8.5 to 
14.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The surface fills generally consist of clayey 
gravel with sands and cobbles over a 2 to 4-foot thick layer of boulders at the bottom. 
Occasional surface pockets of silty clays with relatively small amounts of gravel and 
cobbles were observed at the existing ground surface. The fill is generally dense and 
appeared to have been adequately compacted. The surface fill is generally underlain by 
alluvial deposit, consisting of hard to medium stiff silty clays with varying amounts of 
sands and gravel, extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 20.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface.  
Groundwater was encountered at depths between about 12.6 and 13.8 feet 
below the ground surface in the drilled borings conducted in 2005. However, 
groundwater levels can vary significantly depending on rainfall, temperature, surface 
water runoff, groundwater withdrawal, and other factors. 
The subsurface conditions at the project site, especially along the proposed MSE 
retaining wall alignment, are further explored during our current field exploration. Our 
current field exploration consisted of excavating and sampling five test pits, designated 
as Test Pit Nos. 1 through 5, extending to depths of about 2.8 to 5.4 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The approximate test pit locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
Our test pits generally encountered surface fill materials consisting of sandy silts 
and silty gravel with varying amounts of cobbles and boulders, extending to the 
maximum depth explored of approximately 5.4 feet below the existing ground surface, 
when excavation refusal was encountered.

Seismic Design Considerations 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in 
the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to 
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or 
during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of 
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small that 
they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of the earthquakes 
are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on 
the Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the 
Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) are 
related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking 
diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has 
experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, 
earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii. 
It should be noted that some earthquakes in Hawaii are caused by the burden of 
the island masses overlying the lithosphere, which cause the lithosphere to sag beneath 
the immense weight. The isostatic adjustment of the Pacific lithosphere beneath the 
islands’ overburden causes some moderate to strong intensity earthquakes. The last 
earthquake to occur as a result of the lithosphere adjustment was the 1973 Honomu 
Earthquake, Magnitude 6.2 (M6.2), beneath the Hamakua Coast of the Island of Hawaii. 
The October 15, 2006 Kiholo Bay earthquake, Magnitude 6.7 (M6.7) is also a result of 
lithosphere adjustment. Both of these earthquakes were widely felt throughout the 
Hawaiian islands.  
It should be noted that the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes 
greater than M5+. In addition, other lithospheric origin earthquakes have occurred within 
the Island of Maui in the past, such as the Maui Earthquake of 1938 (~M6.5) centered

3.1 
Foundations 
In general, we believe the proposed new construction may be supported on a 
shallow foundation support system bearing on the recompacted on-site soils. Based on the 
generally stiff/dense subsurface conditions, we believe shallow isolated spread and/or 
continuous footings may be used to support the proposed new construction. An allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of shallow foundations 
bearing on the recompacted on-site soils. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads 
and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind 
or seismic forces. 
For footings bearing on the recompacted on-site soils, the bottom of the footing 
excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a 
relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and/or 
concrete. Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations 
should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm materials. The over-excavation 
may be backfilled with the on-site soils compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction, or the bottom of footing may be extended down to bear directly on the 
underlying competent materials. 
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest 
adjacent finished grades. Footings adjacent to planned (or existing) retaining walls should 
be embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations, or the 
planned retaining walls should be designed to resist the additional structural loads. 
If foundations are next to utility trenches or easements, the footings should be 
embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of

Slabs-On-Grade 
Based on the available field exploration and laboratory test results, the near-surface 
fill materials appear to be slightly expansive when subjected to moisture fluctuations. 
Unless slabs-on-grade constructed above these expansive soils are properly designed, 
there is a potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs-on-grade resulting from 
shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content. To

reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from swelling of the subgrade soils, we 
recommend properly preparing the subgrade soils prior to construction of improvements 
on top. In addition, we recommend providing a minimum 6 inches of non-expansive, select 
granular fill materials below the slab cushion. 
For building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery vibration), we 
recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of open-graded 
gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. The open-graded gravel cushion fill 
would provide uniform support of the slabs and would serve as a capillary moisture break. 
To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through the slab and subsequent 
damage to floor coverings, we recommend placing an impervious moisture barrier, such 
as a plastic membrane, on top (or on the bottom) of the open-graded gravel cushion fill 
layer. Flexible floor coverings, such as carpet or sheet vinyl, should be considered 
because they can better mask minor slab cracking. In addition, we recommend designing 
interior walls to incorporate some flexibility in accommodating a small amount of possible 
ground movements. 
Where the slabs will be subject to vehicular traffic or machinery vibration, we 
recommend providing a 6-inch layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs in lieu of the 
4-inch thick gravel cushion fill layer. The moisture barrier may also be omitted for these 
slabs. The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates compacted 
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
For the design of slabs supported on aggregate subbase, a modulus of subgrade 
reaction of about 200 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection (pci) may be used for 
the compacted aggregate subbase. Where slabs are intended to function as rigid 
pavements for trucks, a minimum slab thickness of 6 inches may be used for preliminary 
design purposes. Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the slab joints 
that will be subject to vehicular traffic. The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved 
areas should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade. 
In order to reduce the potential for distress due to differential movements between 
the heavier footings and the lighter building slabs, we recommend using free-floating

3.3 
Keystone Reinforced Soil Retaining Wall 
Based on the information provided, we understand that a Keystone Reinforced 
Soil Retaining Wall (KRSRW) system is planned at the rear end of the property. The 
system combines the Keystone modular concrete wall units with geogrid reinforcement 
and compacted soil to result in a reinforced soil mass that supports the earth pressure 
and surcharge loads. This composite essentially forms a gravity wall structure with an 
ability to tolerate significant total and differential settlements. In addition, KRSRW are 
also desirable due to the flexibility of the wall, ease of construction, high load carrying 
capacity, and economy. 
Design of the KRSRW will need to take into consideration both external and 
internal stabilities of the structure. In evaluating external stability, the KRSRW structure 
must satisfy four stability conditions:  (1) bearing failure; (2) translational sliding; 
(3) overturning stability; and (4) overall slope stability. Geotechnical design parameters 
to evaluate these stability conditions are presented in the following sections. Some of 
the geotechnical parameters necessary in evaluating the internal stability of the KRSRW 
are presented in the "Reinforced Fill and Backfill Materials" subsection below. The 
internal and external stabilities of the KRSRW will need to be evaluated by the design 
engineer. We have evaluated the overall slope stability for the KRSRW, and the results 
are summarized in the "Overall Slope Stability" subsection.

such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Lateral loads acting on the retaining structures may be resisted by frictional 
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil 
and by passive earth pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the 
foundation system. For the design of the KRSRW, a coefficient of friction of up to 
0.38 and 0.55 may be used between the reinforced fill and the recompacted 
on-site soils or existing drainage culvert, respectively. 
The retaining wall should be embedded a minimum of 2 feet below the lowest 
adjacent finished grade. In addition, the footing should be extended deeper to 
obtain a minimum 6-foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside 
edge of the footing to the face of the slope for sloping ground conditions. 
Where applicable, the wall subgrades should be compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction to provide a firm an unyielding base. Soft and/or 
loose soils encountered at the wall subgrades should be over-excavated to a 
minimum depth of 2 feet below the bottom of wall elevation. The over-excavation 
should also extend a minimum of 2 feet laterally beyond the front face of the 
walls. The resulting over-excavation should be backfilled with select granular 
materials.

We have evaluated the overall slope stability of the wall structures planned for

3.3.4 Reinforced Fill and Backfill Materials 
We believe that the reinforced fill material for the segmental retaining wall should 
consist of imported select granular fill materials. In general, the imported select 
granular fill materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no 
larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should also contain less 
than 15 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a 
California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 25 or higher and a swell potential of 
one percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. 
In addition, the reinforced fill material (imported select granular fill materials) 
should have an angle of internal friction of at least 35 degrees when tested by the 
standard direct shear test (ASTM D3080). The sample to be tested should be 
compacted to 95 percent relative compaction at moisture contents above the 
optimum. 
Reinforced fill materials should be placed in level loose lifts not exceeding 
8 inches in loose thickness and be compacted to at least 95 percent of the 
maximum dry density established in accordance with ASTM D1557 at moisture 
contents above the optimum.

3.4 
Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site. Soft and/or 
yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to 
receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm materials, and the resulting excavation 
should be backfilled with well-compacted fills. The excavated soft and/or organic soils 
should be properly disposed off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate. 
Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation 
and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the over-excavation quantities. 
After clearing and grubbing, the exposed subgrades should be scarified to a 
minimum depth of 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Where shrinkage cracks develop in the subgrade soil after compaction, we 
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above. Saturation and 
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage 
may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well-compacted fill. 
The need for over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil conditions should be evaluated in 
the field by a Geolabs representative.

Utility Trenches 
We anticipate the utilities for the new facility will consist primarily of water, 
electrical, sewer, and drain line connections. In general, good construction practices 
should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities. 
The contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used for trench 
excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations. In addition, the 
excavations should comply with the applicable federal, state, and local safety 
requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design and 
installation. 
In general, we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes for uniform support. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above 
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes. It is critical to use this 
free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches 
of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench 
backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage.

3.7 
Drainage 
Finished grades outside the new structures should be sloped to shed water away 
from the slabs and foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding around the 
structure. It is also advised to install gutter systems around the new building structure 
and to divert the discharge away from the slab and foundation areas. Excessive 
landscape watering near the slabs and foundations should also be avoided. Planters 
next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce 
the potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil 
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength. The foundation excavations 
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of 
the concrete to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface. 
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to 
drain surface water runoff away from the slabs and foundations.
