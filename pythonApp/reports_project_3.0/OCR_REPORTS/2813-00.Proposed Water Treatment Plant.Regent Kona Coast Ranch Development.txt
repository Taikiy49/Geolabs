INTRODUCTION
As requested we have performed a geotechnical exploration for the proposed
Water Treatment Plant for the Off site Water System for the Regent Kona Coast Ranch
Development in Kailua Kona on the island of Hawaii The geotechnical exploration was
performed in general accordance with our proposal dated
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our geotechnical exploration was to obtain an overview of the
subsurface conditions at the project site in order to obtain data for the formulation of
preliminary geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the design of the site
grading and structure foundations for the proposed water treatment plant facility

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five 5 shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has not been
active during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa
and Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is located on the northwestern flank of Hualalai which makes up
the west central portion of the island and consists partly of the prehistoric basaltic lava
flows of the Hualalai Voicanic Series which were most likely deposited during the late
Pleistocene Epoch Stearns and Macdonald 1946
Hawaiian volcanic activity is typified by an abundance of flank eruptions along rift
zones or zones of weakness in the earth s crust The project site is situated in the
northwest rift zone of Hualalai
The lava flows at the site are of the a a type with dense zones of hard rock and
loose zones of clinker and voids of varying degrees overlying and intermixed with older
pahoehoe flows The clinker phase is highly permeable Lava tubes are not uncommon
in pahoehoe flows Collapsed lava tubes cinder cones underlain and surrounded by
depositional scoriaceous cinder and spatter cones are located in the near vicinity of the
project area

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
In general it is our opinion that development of the site for a water treatment facility
is feasible from a geotechnical engineering point of view Field reconnaissance of the site
indicated that the site is underlain by volcanic rock formation
The data obtained from geophysical exploration by the use of ground penetrating
radar GPR indicates that the location is underlain by rock formation with few anomalies
which could be interpreted as voids On the basis of the findings from the geophysical
exploration it was decided to implement a second phase of exploration at the site which
consisted of the drilling and sampling of four 4 borings to further explore the subsurface
conditions at the site
In general our borings at the water treatment site encountered relatively competent
basaltic lava formation Based on the data from the drilled borings it appears that most
of the anomalies observed in the GPR survey were pockets of loose clinker The volcanic
rock formation extended to the maximum depths explored
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings during the time of our
exploration
Based on our findings construction of the proposed Water Treatment Plant is
feasible provided that our recommendations are followed The underlying volcanic rock
formation should provide adequate bearing for the proposed structures Due to the
possible existence of cavities within the volcanic formation we recommend that a program

Site Preparation
Prior to the commencement of any earthwork all areas within the grading limits
should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting stripped and
processed material should be stockpiled for use in landscaping and or general yard fill
Any voids or yielding areas disclosed during the clearing operations should be over
excavated to firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted engineered fill
Based on our exploration the majority of the cut and materials generated from cuts
will be volcanic rock materials including clinker material The on site excavated rock
materials should be suitable for use as engineered fill if properly processed placed and
compacted If additional imported material is required the material should conform to the
requirements stipulated in Section 30 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works
Construction Select Borrow for Subbase Course dated September 1986
If the volcanic rock materials encountered at the site are processed and reduced
to a relatively well graded select granular material with an average maximum size of about
six 6 inches and an absolute maximum size of twelve 12 inches the material may be
utilized as structural fill
In areas to receive fills the existing surface should be proofrolled with a vibratory
drum roller with a static weight of a minimum of 20 tons to expose and collapse any
near surface voids Any exposed voids should be backfilled as described above

Generally fills required should be placed in level lifts not exceeding twelve 12
inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 of the dry density as
established by ASTM D 1557 78 Trench and structural backfills should be placed in level
lifts not exceeding eight 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
90 maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 The upper two 2 feet of
fills and backfills below road and footing or slab subgrade level should be compacted to
a minimum of 95 maximum dry density
Any fills to be placed on slopes having ratios steeper than five horizontal to one
vertical 6H 1V should be keyed and benched into the underlying soil or basalt formation
to lessen potential instability of the new fill
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
excavated volcanic material instead a testing program to determine the number of
passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project On the basis of this testing
program the number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate
compaction
Design slope ratios of up to one and a half horizontal to one vertical may
be used for cut slopes This ratio may also be used for fill slopes provided that on site
granular material is used

Probing and Grouting
To reduce the possibility of foundation settlement due to collapse of loose pockets
or cavities in clinker or lava tubes in the underlying lava rock the footing
excavations should be probed at a spacing of approximately ten 10 feet on
centers with a minimum of one probe drilled at each spread footing location If a
cavity is located below a footing additional probe holes should be drilled to
delineate the horizontal extent of the cavity The probe holes should have a
minimum diameter of three 3 inches and extend below the bottom of the footing
to a depth of at least ten 10 feet The probe holes should be grouted from the
bottom of the hole using low pressures The probing and grouting operations
should be monitored by the project geotechnical engineer to see that these
recommendations are carried out properly and to locate potential voids near the
footing excavations as well as to recommend modifications to the grouting
procedures which may expedite the construction program

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration Techniques
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by performing an initial
geophysical survey utilizing ground penetrating radar followed by the drilling of four 4
borings to obtain rock core samples A brief description of the two exploration methods
is provided below
Ground Penetrating Radar
Ground penetrating radar utilizes impulse radar technology to obtain real time
profiles of the subsurface materials A radar frequency pulse is transmitted into the
subsurface and when it encounters an interface between two materials with differing
electrical properties it is reflected back to a receiver on the surface In a relatively
homogeneous medium this reflection is noted as an anomaly
The reflected pulse is intercepted by the receiver and then electronically processed
and printed on a graphic recorder producing a record similar to a marine depth recorder
chart As the transmitter and receiver antenna array is moved across the ground surface
a real time graphic record of the subsurface is generated Markers can be inserted into
the data print to reference distances as the array is moved providing a means to
physically locate anomalies
Ground penetrating radar is generally considered most applicable to the location
of subsurface voids and buried structures Depth of penetration and data resolution are
dependent upon soil type moisture content electrical properties antenna frequency and
power
Drilling and Sampling
The materials encountered in the borings were examined and classified in general
accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2488 84 Description and Identification of
Soils Visual Manual Procedure by a field engineer from our firm
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the boring logs at the appropriate sample
depths

Core samples of soil and rock materials encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material
left in the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and
testing
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses This designation is only applicable to cores retrieved from in situ
rock formations RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
that is in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks if 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0
foot core run the RQD would be 50 and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The rippability or foundation support characteristics of a rock mass is a function
of the relative hardness of the rock its relative quality brittleness and _ fissile
characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD would be very difficult to rip and would
probably require blasting or splitting for excavation
