SEISMIC DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS 
Based on the International Building Code, 2006 Edition (IBC 2006), the project sites 
may be subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be 
addressed. The following sections provide discussions on the seismicity of the Island of 
Maui and the soil profile (site class) for seismic design. 
Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during volcanic 
eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground movement of magma 
that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences 
thousands of earthquakes each year, but most of the earthquakes are so small that they 
only can be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of the earthquakes are 
strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes directly associated with the movement of magma are 
concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island of 
Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) are related to 
volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with 
increased distance from the Island of Hawaii.  
The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui also has experienced earthquakes greater than 
M5+. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have occurred in the County of Maui.

Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the International Building Code, 2006 Edition, the project sites may be 
subject to seismic activity and the potential for soil liquefaction at the project sites will need 
to be evaluated. 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the 
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess pore 
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes. In this 
process, when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration (such as during 
an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in 
pore water pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up 
of pore water pressure, the effective stress (internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under 
sustained vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could equal the overburden 
pressure, essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a 
viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both 
horizontal and vertical movements, and if not confined, will result in significant 
deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained 
sands and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the liquefaction 
characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows: 
1. 
Grain Size Distribution - Fine and uniform sands and silts are more 
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well-graded sands.

In general, the subsurface information obtained from the test pits indicate that the

with no evidence of loose granular soils) and the absence of groundwater within the 
depths of the test pits. Therefore, the potential for liquefaction is not a design consideration 
at the project sites. 
FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project sites, we believe 
that shallow foundations consisting of a perimeter ring footing poured monolithically with 
the tank floor slab may be used for support of the proposed new pressure break tank 
structures. Detailed discussions and recommendations for foundation, earthwork, and 
pavement design are presented in the following sections. 
Tank Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project sites, we believe 
that shallow foundations consisting of a perimeter ring footing poured monolithically with 
the tank floor slab may be used for support of the proposed new pressure break tank 
structures.  Our field exploration indicated that soft to stiff volcanic ash soils may be 
expected at or near the foundation subgrade level of the proposed pressure break tanks.  
To improve the foundation bearing performance and to provide more uniform support for 
the tank structures, we recommend over-excavating the subsurface materials below the 
bottom of the tank floor and foundations a minimum of 2 feet below the perimeter ring 
footing bottom elevation level.  The over-excavation should be replaced with compacted 
structural fill materials.  The over-excavation for the compacted structural fill should extend 
beyond the outside edges of the perimeter ring footing a minimum of 2 feet.  The tank floor 
slab may be placed directly on the compacted structural fill materials.

granular materials compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density.  A layer of structural geogrid, such as Tensar SG-2 or equivalent,

In general, the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finish grades.  Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on 
sloping ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal

Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded 
below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility 
trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines.  This requirement is necessary to avoid 
surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce 
the potential for foundation settlement.

Based on the proposed finish tank floor elevations and the existing grades at the 
project sites, we anticipate that site grading work for the proposed new pressure break 
tank project will consist of cuts ranging from about 1.5 to 3.5 feet deep at the tank 
structures and up to about 6 feet deep for the site development.  Items of grading that are 
addressed in the following subsections include the following:  (1) Site Preparation, (2) Fill

A Geolabs representative should monitor site grading operations to observe

the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our exploration. 
Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site.  
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm natural material 
and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted engineered 
fill.  The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site 
or used in landscaping areas, if appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, the areas within the proposed tank structures should 
be over-excavated to provide the minimum 2-foot thick compacted structural fill 
layer for more uniform support.  Prior to placement of the structural fill material, the 
bottom of the over-excavation should be proof-rolled with a sheepsfoot roller a 
minimum of eight passes to detect the presence of near-surface soft and/or loose 
zones.  The proof-rolling operations should be conducted under the observation of 
a Geolabs representative. 
Because soft soils may be expected at the over-excavated subgrade level, 
pumping of the subgrades may occur during the proof-rolling operations. 
Therefore, we recommend lining the bottom of the over-excavation with a layer of 
woven geotextile fabric, such as Mirafi 500X, Amoco 2006, or equivalent, prior to 
placement of the structural fill.  To improve the foundation bearing support 
performance, we recommend providing a layer of structural geogrid, such as 
Tensar SG-2 or equivalent, at about the mid-depth of the 2-foot thick structural fill 
layer.  The geotextile fabric and structural geogrid should be overlapped a 
minimum of 18 inches between adjacent rolls.

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Based on our field exploration and laboratory tests results, it appears that the in-situ 
soils exist in a moist state with high moisture contents.  Therefore, drying or 
aeration of the excavated materials may be necessary prior to their use as general 
fill materials.  Aeration to lower the soil moisture and more compaction effort along 
with thinner fill lifts to achieve the specified compaction may be necessary and

Cut and Fill Slopes 
We anticipate that the proposed cut slopes at the south and east sides of the 
pressure break tanks would generally expose the medium stiff to stiff volcanic ash 
soils as encountered in the test pits.  In general, we believe that the planned cut 
slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical 
(2H:1V) or flatter. 
Excessive surface run-off over the slope face may cause erosion of the exposed 
soils, thus potentially jeopardizing the long-term stability and performance of the cut 
slope.  Therefore, cut slopes should be protected by appropriate slope planting or 
other means as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished 
slopes.  In addition, V-ditches may be considered at the top and bottom of the cut 
slopes to provide proper drainage of surface run-off water. 
Permanent fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. 
Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the 
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding.  The filling operations 
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers 
in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations. Fill slopes should be 
constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a 
well-compacted slope face.  In addition, slope planting should be provided as soon 
as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes.

Utility Trench 
We anticipate the utilities for the new pressure break tanks will consist primarily of 
influent, effluent, and washout lines. In general, good construction practices should be 
utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities. The 
contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used for trench excavation, 
subject to practical limits and safety considerations. In addition, the excavations should 
comply with the applicable federal, state, and local safety requirements. The contractor 
should be responsible for trench shoring design and installation. 
In general, we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes for uniform support. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above 
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes. It is critical to use this 
free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of 
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill 
could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of compacted on-site soils (with a 
maximum particle size of 6 inches) or select granular fill material. The backfill should be 
placed in maximum 8-inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than 
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground 
subsidence. Where trenches are below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench 
backfill below the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. 
Because weathered basaltic materials were encountered at relatively shallow 
depths during our field exploration, it appears that some of the trench work for utility 
installation likely will involve excavation in the basaltic rock formation and will require hard 
ripping or the use of hoerams. The contractor must exercise care to avoid over-ripping, 
which would disrupt the structure of the rock formation and result in a potential loss of 
bearing strength for footings in its vicinity.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of excavating, sampling, and backfilling of 
two test pits, designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 8 and 
9 feet below the existing ground surface. The test pits were excavated with a CASE 580 
Extendahoe excavator. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the test pits by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the excavation 
operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further 
reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general 
accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for 
Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log 
Legend, Plate A. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on 
the Logs of Test Pits, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample.  Pocket penetrometer test results are 
presented on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths.
