SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered a relatively thin layer of volcanic ash and fill 
materials on the order of about 1 to 4 feet thick. The near-surface soils are generally 
underlain by interbedded layers of a’a clinker and dense basalt formation extending 
down to the maximum depth explored of about 20 feet below the ground surface. The 
basalt formation encountered was relatively unweathered and very hard. The clinker 
materials generally consisted of medium dense sandy gravel and gravelly cobbles. 
In general, we recommend over-excavating the cut subgrades by a depth of 
about 3 feet below the design finished subgrades and backfilling with compacted 
structural fill materials.  We also recommend capping the fill areas of the project site 
with a minimum 3-foot thick layer of structural fill materials.  After clearing and grubbing, 
the over-excavated cut subgrades and areas to be filled should be scarified to a depth 
of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  Where scarification of the 
subgrades is not practical due to the presence of basalt formation, the exposed 
subgrades should be proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller for a 
minimum of eight passes. 
Structural fill materials should consist of well-graded granular materials generally 
less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence 
of voids in the compacted mass. Fill and backfill materials below the minimum 3-foot 
thick structural fill layer may consist of general fill materials. General fill and backfill 
materials should consist of well-graded granular materials, the majority of which are less 
than 12 inches with an absolute maximum dimension of 18 inches. Materials between 
12 and 18 inches in particle size should be limited to about 15 percent or less of the 
total volume of general fill materials. 
In general, we envision that most of the cut slopes would expose volcanic ash, fill 
materials, clinker materials and dense basalt formations. We believe the cut slopes 
planned at the site may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. In areas 
where dense basalt formations are exposed at the cut slope faces, the cut slopes may 
be steepened to inclinations of 1H:1V or flatter. Permanent fill slopes constructed of the 
general fill and structural fill materials recommended herein may be designed with a 
slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Makena H-2 Subdivision project in Makena on the Island of Maui, 
Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location 
Map, Plate 1. This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and 
laboratory testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations for the design of the 
proposed project. 
The recommendations provided herein are intended for the design of site 
grading, slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, pavements, underground utility lines, and 
storm water runoff disposal system only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The existing H-2 parcel is located east of and adjacent to the Maluaka Makena 
Subdivision and is bounded by the Makena Alanui Road to the east and south, in the 
Wailea District on the Island of Maui, Hawaii.  
The project will consist of developing a 28-acre site into 53 units with mass 
grading work consisting of cuts and fills of approximately 79,000 and 71,000 cubic 
yards, respectfully. New roadways, including sidewalks, and a public parking lot will be 
constructed to serve the new subdivision. In addition, subsurface infrastructure will be 
installed including water, sewer, drainage, TV and telephone lines.  We have assumed 
that recommendations for new house structures and improvements on the individual lots 
will be the responsibility of the lot owners and are specifically excluded from our scope 
of work under this report. 
As part of the field exploration program, infiltration tests were performed at the 
drainage retention areas to evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the subsurface 
materials for design of the storm water runoff disposal system in order to meet the 
drainage requirements for Low Impact Development (LID).

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes, the older Tertiary Epoch 
(West Maui) and the more recent Pleistocene Epoch (East Maui), also known as 
Haleakala (Pleistocene Epoch). The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain 
located between these two volcanoes. The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows 
from Haleakala Volcano ponding against West Maui Volcano. The gently sloping plains 
subsequently were filled with alluvium washed down from the slopes of West Maui and 
East Maui (Haleakala) Volcanoes. The erosional processes are dominated by the 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the mountain walls, and the soil materials are 
transported downslope toward the isthmus primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once these 
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become 
dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. 
The project site is on the western flank of the East Maui Volcano, which forms 
the east side of the Island of Maui. The East Maui Mountain is primarily composed of 
lavas from the Kula and Hana Volcanic Series. The basalt formations at the project site 
may be part of the Kula Volcanic Series, which consist of a thick series of alkalic a`a 
lava flows. 
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is a 28-acre parcel, located east of and adjacent to the Maluaka 
Makena Subdivision in Makena on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. It is approximately 
bounded to the north by Makena Golf Course, to the east and south by Makena Alanui 
Road, and to the west by Makena Keoneoio Road. 
Geologically, this area lies on the lower flanks of Haleakala Volcano, considered 
to be dormant, which last erupted nearby in 1790 at La Perouse Bay.  USGS maps 
show that the project site lies on lavas from the Hana Volcanic Series deposited 
between 30,000 and 50,000 years ago.  The geomorphology of the site shows many 
basaltic rock outcrops throughout the area.  A blanket of orangish brown volcanic ash 
dominates the surface soils throughout the majority of the site.  Aeolian deposit, 
consisting of wind-blown coralline sands, blankets the eastern portion of the project site.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling 14 borings, designated as 
Boring Nos. 1 through 14, extending to depths of about 11.5 to 17.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. Five borings identified as I-1 through I-5 were drilled at the 
proposed retention basin locations to depths of about 10 to 20 feet below the existing 
ground surface. In addition, four bulk samples of the near-surface soils, designated as 
Bulk-1 through Bulk-4, were obtained to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on the borings drilled, the subsurface conditions at the site generally 
consist of a relatively thin layer of volcanic ash or fill materials, on the order of about 
1 to 2 feet thick with localized areas extending down to about 4 feet below the existing 
ground surface.  The volcanic ash generally consists of soft sandy silts and the fill 
materials generally consist of dense sands and gravel with some cobbles. 
The upper volcanic ash and fill materials are generally underlain by interbedded 
layers of a’a clinker and dense basalt formation extending down to the maximum depth 
explored of about 20 feet below the ground surface. The basalt formation encountered

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, 
existing structures, and other unsuitable materials, should be removed and 
disposed of properly off-site. 
Loose and/or soft soils, where encountered, should be over-excavated to expose 
firm and/or dense materials, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled 
with well-compacted fills. The excavated loose and/or soft soils should be 
disposed of properly off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate.

After clearing and grubbing, the cut subgrades and areas to be filled should be 
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Where scarification of the subgrades is not practical due to the presence of 
basalt formation, the exposed subgrades should be proof-rolled with a minimum 
10-ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes. 
We envision that one to two-story single-family residential structures will be 
constructed for the subdivision development.  It should be noted that excavation 
for building footings and utility installation may encounter difficulty in the cut 
areas where subgrade materials consist of hard basalt formation.  Therefore, in 
order to avoid significant rock excavation conditions during building construction, 
we believe that consideration should be given to over-excavating the cut 
subgrades by a depth of about 3 feet below the design finished subgrades and 
backfilling with compacted structural fill materials.  We believe that it would be 
more cost effective if excavation of the hard basalt formation were conducted 
during the mass grading phase of the project in lieu of the building construction 
phase of the project. In general, we also recommend capping the fill areas of the 
project site with a minimum 3-foot thick layer of structural fill materials. 
3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
Structural fill materials should consist of well-graded granular materials generally 
less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the 
occurrence of voids in the compacted mass. In addition, structural fill materials 
should contain less than 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve by 
weight. In general, the excavated on-site materials may be reused as a source of 
structural fill materials, provided that they are processed to meet the maximum 
particle size and gradation requirements presented above.

Fill and backfill materials below the minimum 3-foot thick structural fill layer may 
consist of general fill materials. General fill and backfill materials should consist 
of well-graded granular materials, the majority of which are less than 12 inches

Where over-cutting of the finished cut subgrades occur during the grading 
operations, backfilling of the over-cut area should be treated as fill areas. 
Therefore, the above fill material recommendations should apply to the over-cut 
areas, and the fill materials required for backfilling will depend on the depth of 
over-cut below the finished subgrade. 
The maximum particle size of the fills and backfills should be limited to 3 inches 
or less for fills and backfills to be placed in confined locations. Confined locations 
are defined as areas where the compaction equipment is limited in size to less 
than 2 tons in static weight. In addition, if the contractor elects to use 
smaller-sized compaction equipment (less than 2 tons in static weight), the 
maximum particle size of the fill and backfill materials also should be limited to 
3 inches in dimension.  
Aggregate base and subbase course materials required for the project should 
consist of crushed basaltic aggregates and should conform to Sections 31 and 
30 of the County of Maui Standard Specifications (September, 1986). 
Imported fill materials (where required) should be free of organics and 
deleterious materials and should be suitable for the intended use. In addition,

3.1.3 Boulder Disposal 
Boulders (i.e., rock fragments larger than 12 inches in maximum size but less 
than 3 feet in largest dimension) encountered during site grading, may be used in 
rock fills, provided that the following recommendations are followed: 
1. 
Boulders less than 3 feet in largest dimension may be utilized as 
rock fills, provided that the top of the rock fill layer is placed at least 
5 feet below the finished subgrades. Boulders larger than 3 feet in 
maximum dimension should not be used as rock fill unless reduced 
in size. 
2. 
Boulders greater than 18 inches in largest dimension should not be 
exposed at the fill slope face. Boulder placement should be set 
back a lateral distance of at least 6 feet measured from the edge of 
the fill slope face. 
3. 
Consideration should be given to avoid placement of boulders in 
the proposed utility line alignment (roadways) and within the depths 
of the proposed utility lines to reduce the potential for encountering 
the boulders during excavation for the utility trenches. 
Compaction of boulders in the rock fills should be performed in accordance with 
the recommendations presented in the following “Fill Placement and Compaction 
Requirements” subsection. It should be noted that disposal of boulders in the 
general fill material will require that the entire fill mass be compacted and 
properly “choked-off” at the surface (and each layer of rock fill) to reduce the 
potential for migration of fines through the voids between boulders.

3.1.5 Excavations 
The project site generally is underlain by volcanic ash and fill materials near the 
ground surface overlying interbedded layers of basalt formation and clinker 
materials. It is anticipated that the volcanic ash, fill materials, clinker, and highly 
fractured basalt formation near the ground surface may be readily excavated with 
normal heavy excavation equipment, such as excavators, and also ripped with 
large bulldozers. However, some of the deeper excavations in the dense 
pahoehoe and/or a`a lava likely will require the use of hoerams or chipping. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are 
based on our visual observation of the existing basalt formation and field data 
from the borings. Contractors should be encouraged to examine the site 
conditions and the subsurface data to make their own reasonable and prudent 
interpretation.

3.2 
Slabs-on-Grade 
Exterior concrete slabs, such as walkways, may be required for the project. In 
general, we recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick cushion fill layer consisting of 
select borrow subbase below exterior concrete slabs that will not be subjected to 
vehicular traffic. The select borrow subbase should be moisture-conditioned to above 
the optimum moisture, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. To 
reduce the potential for significant slab cracking due to concrete shrinkage, crack 
control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with 
expansion joints at right-angle intersections.

Retaining Structures 
Some retaining structures, such as site retaining walls, may be required for grade 
separation for the project development. Therefore, the following general guidelines are 
provided and may be used for design of retaining structures at the project site. 
3.3.1 Retaining Structure Foundations 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions at the project site, we envision 
the new retaining structures would be supported on shallow foundations, such as 
continuous footings, bearing on the recompacted in-situ soils and/or new 
compacted fills.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square 
foot (psf) may be used for the design of footings bearing on the recompacted 
in-situ soils and/or  new compacted fills. The bearing value is for supporting 
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by up to one-third (⅓) for transient 
loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.

In addition, retaining wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches and 
the bottom should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest 
adjacent grade.  For sloping ground conditions, the bottom of footing should 
extend deeper to obtain a minimum 6-foot setback distance measured 
horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope. Wall 
footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in 
stepped footings. 
In general, the retaining structure should be constructed on similar foundation 
subgrade materials in order to provide for uniform support across the structure. 
Where the new structure will straddle varying subgrade conditions, special 
provisions should be made in these locations. Based on our evaluation, we 
anticipate some of the structures may straddle across existing/new fills and 
basalt formation. In order to provide uniform support across the entire structure, 
footing subgrades should be over-excavated by at least 12 inches and backfilled 
with select borrow subbase where basalt formation is exposed at the bottom of 
footing elevation. 
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements, the footing should 
be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the 
bottom edge of the utility trench, or the footing should be extended to a depth as 
deep as the inverts of the utility lines.  This requirement is necessary to avoid 
surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional structural loads and 
to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement.

coefficient of friction of 0.35 may be used for footings bearing on the 
recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills. Resistance due to passive 
earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of

3.3.4 Drainage 
In general, retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the potential for 
build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system should consist of 
permeable material, such as ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation material, placed near 
the bottom and along the length of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or 
weep holes. 
As an alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or 
EnkaDrain, may be used instead of the permeable drainage material. The 
prefabricated drainage product should also be connected hydraulically to a 
perforated pipe at the base of the retaining wall. Unless covered by concrete or 
asphaltic concrete, the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively 
impervious materials, such as well-graded soils, to reduce the potential for 
significant water infiltration behind the walls.

encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-19. 
Soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. 
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 
18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings 
indicates the number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow 
counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow 
counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the 
publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in 
Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of

The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) determinations were performed on selected 
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties. The test results 
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Six Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Twenty-three Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D7012) were 
performed on selected rock core samples to evaluate the uniaxial compressive strength 
of the rock formation encountered. The test results are presented on Plate B-3. 
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D1883) were 
performed on the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-4 through B-6. 
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557A & C) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-7 through B-10.

Infiltration Tests 
Five infiltration tests were conducted at the proposed retention basin sites to 
evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered at 
selected boring locations. The infiltration tests were performed in Boring Nos. I-1 
through I-5 at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. These tests 
were performed in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of the State 
of Maryland Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I 
and II” (rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with other states’ procedures and 
may generally be considered as an industry standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by augering a boring to the selected 
test depths of about 10 to 20 feet below the existing ground surface. Upon reaching the 
test depth, a 4-inch diameter PVC solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled hole 
to allow infiltration only through the soil exposed on the bottom of the boring. Falling 
head infiltration tests were performed to determine the average infiltration rates of the 
underlying subsurface materials. Due to the highly permeable nature of the subsurface 
materials, the infiltration tests were modified from four to eight trials with each trial 
consisting of measuring the time required for the 2-foot of water to drain away. The 
infiltration rates are then calculated based on the result of the last trial for each test 
location. The test results are presented on Plates C-1 through C-5.
