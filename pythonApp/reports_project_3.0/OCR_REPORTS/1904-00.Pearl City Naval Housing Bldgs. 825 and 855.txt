In accordance with your request we have performed a study to
evaluate possible causes of the observed movements distress at the above
referenced buildings and possible remedial measures which may be taken
Our scope of work included field observations review of available
building and grading plans three 3 drilled borings at each building
laboratory soil testing level survey readings inside the units
engineering analysis and report preparation This report summarizes our
findings and recommendations
SITE DESCRIPTION
The Pearl City Naval Housing is situated at the southern portion of
the Pearl City Peninsula Access to the area is through Lehua Avenue
The general site topography is gently sloping to flat with ground
elevations ranging from about 10 feet to 15 feet above sea level Both
buildings are located to the east of Lehua Avenue with access through Palm
Avenue Building 825 or Kirkbride Avenue Building 855

BUTEDING DESCRIPTION AND OBSERVED DISTRESS
Building 825
This building is rectangular in shape with plan dimensions of 28 feet
by 131 feet There are four units in this building The end units A and
D consist of one story structures while the two middle units are
two stories All of the ground floors have slab on grade while the second
level has a wood floor The exterior ground floor walls and the interior
bearing walls which serve as unit separation walls are constructed of
CMU while the remainder of the structure is constructed of wood
The major signs of distress at this building consist of the
following
1 A large crack in the exterior cMu bearing wall between the A and
B units
2 Cracks in the interior bearing walls between the A and B units
and the end wall of the D unit
3 Noticeable slopes in the ground floor slabs and second floor
level in the B and C units The floors slope down toward the
direction of the D unit
4 The front and rear sliding doors of the units particularly the B
and C units are not square and difficult to close

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Building 825
Three borings were drilled in front of the building at the locations
indicated on the attached Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled
with a truck mounted drill rig to depths of 16 5 to 26 feet below the
existing ground surface Detailed information of the soil conditions
encountered are shown on the Logs of Borings in Appendix A
Borings 1 and 1A which were drilled at Units D and between Units B
and C respectively encountered a surface layer of stiff clayey silt
fi11 about 5 feet thick Soft clayey silt with organic matter lagoonal
deposit was encountered below the surface fill layer The thickness of
the soft layer varied from about 20 feet near Unit D to about 3 feet

soils to the depth of the borings Boring 2 which was drilled in front
of Unit A did not encounter the soft lagoonal soils The groundwater
level was measured at about 7 5 to 13 feet below the existing ground
surface
Two borings were drilled outside of the A and D units with a truck
mounted drill rig to depths of 16 5 to 26 5 feet below the existing ground

a depth of about 5 5 feet below the floor level The boring locations are
indicated on the attached Site Plan 2 Plate 3 Detailed information of
the soil conditions encountered are shown on the Logs of Borings in
Appendix A
All three borings encountered stiff clay to silty clay to the full
depth of the borings It was noted that the upper 3 to 4 feet of soil

Building 825
A Level survey was conducted on the ground floors of Units A
through D and second floors of Units B and C for Building 825 The

at various spots on the vinyl tile floors Which were accessible The

CONCLUSIONS
It appears that the distress to the building has been caused by
ground settlement primarily due to compression of the soft soil under the
weight of the approximately four feet of fill which was placed for the
site development Due to the relatively light structural loads the
settlement due to the structure itself appears to be small
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1904 00 Page 7
Dk No 40
The boring data survey readings and observed damages are consistent
with this settlement hypothesis The general trends of the slab
settlement along with the generalized subsurface cross section are
presented on Plate 4 It can be seen that the magnitude of the
settlements corresponds directly to the thickness of the soft layer
beneath the site
A critical element in the determination of the recommended repair to
the building is the potential future long term settlements Since the
fill and building have been in place for at least 20 years it appears
very likely that the primary settlements have occured However due to
the organic nature of the soft soils some long term secondary settlements
may occur We estimate that the secondary settlements may be on the order
of about 1 inch
Building 855
The survey readings confirmed the visual observations which indicated
that the interior portion of the concrete Slabs were higher than the
portions of the slab along the perimeter of the units Based on these
findings possible causes of the movements were as follows
1 Settlement of the load bearing walls
2 Heaving of the lightly loaded central portion of the slabs due to
expansive clay soils
3 Settlement of the exterior walls due to loss of moisture and
shrinkage of the subsoils
These three possible causes were evaluated with the boring and
laboratory data Based on this information the following
conclusion were reached
a It does not seem likely that the building footings had
settled since the structure loads are light and the borings
disclosed that the near surface soils are stiff
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1904 00 Page 8
Dk No 40
b Although the near surface soils were found to have high
expansion potential the soils under the interior of the slab
were not saturated and the slab cracking pattern did not
indicate heaving of the soils
c The two borings drilled outside the building disclosed that
the upper 3 feet of the soils were very dry This was
contrary to the boring drilled inside the unit which found
the soils to be uniformly moist Based on the field
exploration it appears that the observed building distress
has been caused by shrinkage of the near surface soils along
the perimeter of the building
The near surface soils are presently very dry and therefore have the
potential for swelling if they are saturated in the future In order to
reduce future movement of the building the subsoils should be prevented
from future moisture changes i e wetting or drying
RECOMMENDATIONS FOR REMEDIAL MEASURES
Building 825
The key element in the design of remedial measures for this building
is the anticipated future settlement If the future settlement is
minimal the remedial work can be concentrated on the building
superstructure with the foundations left as is However if significant
future settlement is anticipated costly foundation work will be required
Based on the available data it is our opinion that the primary
settlement at this site has essentially occured however long term
secondary settlement on the order of 1 inch may still occur at Units c and
D Long term settlement estiamtes are very approximate since they are
affected by minute details of the soil structure layering and organic
content of the soil Due to the importance of the anticipated
settlements we recommend that a Survey program be conducted at the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1904 00 Page 9
Dk No 40
building for a period of about one year in order to obtain a better
estimate of the future settlement If it is not feasible to conduct this
survey then our above settlement estimate may be used as a guide If the
anticipated future settlements are not tolerable the following
alternatives may be considered to reduce future damage to the building
Alternate 1 Micro pile Support for the bearing walls The bearing
walls for Units B C and D may be underpinned to transfer the structural
loading through the soft layer and into the stiff underlying alluvium to
reduce the effects of the long term settlement The limited working
space particularly inside the units will limit the type and size of
underpinning equipemnt therefore it appears that small diameter
micro piles would be appropriate for the underpinning system The
micro piles may consist of 4 inch diameter pipes driven or drilled into
the stiff alluvium The micro piles should extend a minimm depth of 20
feet into the stiff alluvium for a design vertical capacity of 10 kips
Specialized portable drilling equipment will be needed for the
installation of the micro piles
Alternate 2 Separation of Units Much of the damage to the units
appears to be caused by differential settlements between the units This
can be reduced by separating the one and two story units thereby allowing
the units to settle more uniformly
Building 855
The near surface soils at this building site have high shrink swell
potential It appears that the observed damage has been caused by
shrinkage of the soils below the perimeter of the building The surface
soils were found to be much drier than the deeper soils indicating
shrinkage of the near surface soils with corresponding settlements at the
ground surface The laboratory tests indicated that the near surface
soils are expansive and therefore could swell if saturated in the
future Ideally the dry near surface soils should be moistened to above
optimum moisture content to reduce the future swelling potential of the
soil prior to remedial work However it may take many months to years
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1904 00 Page 10
Dk No 40
for the subsoils to reach equilibrium if they are saturated now
Therefore if it is desired to repair and occupy these units in the near
future an alternate scheme may be to attempt to maintain the present soil
moisture constent under the building Two possible alternatives to
attempt to maintain the present moisture under the buiding are presented
below
Alternate 1 Cutoff Wall A concrete cutoff wall approximately 3
feet deep may be installed around the building to create a barrier to
reduce moisture migration under the slabs In order to reduce the
possibility of underminning the existing footings the cutoff wall may be
installed about 3 feet away from the exterior walls with a concrete cap
poured between the cutoff wall and the building exterior All joints
should be sealed to prevent moisture migration The cutoff wall should be
a minimum of 4 inches thick and poured neat against the excavation
Alternate 2 Perimeter Slab In order to reduce costs the cutoff
wall may be replaced with a perimeter concrete Slab to cover and protect
the near surface soils around the building The slab should extend a
minimum of about 6 feet beyond the building It should be noted that the
concrete slab particularly along the edges may experience shrinking and
swelling due to changes in the soil moisture under the slab therefore
the slab should be sloped toward the outside grade to avoid ponding of
water next to the building foundations
In addition to the above the spread footings supporting the rear
portion of the second floor level should be extended to a minimum depth of
3 feet to reduce the effects of future moisture changes

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling six
6 borings to depths of 5 5 to 26 5 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans Plates 2
and 3 A truck mounted drill rig was used to drill the borings outside
of the buildings A hand auger was used to drill one boring inside the
building
The soils encountered in the borings were classified by visual and
textural examination in the field by our engineer who continuously
monitored the drilling operations the classifications were further
reviewed by visual inspection and testing in the laboratory The soils
were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Most soil samples were obtained by driving a 2 inch standard split
hammer free falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the
last 12 inches are shown on the Togs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
In the soft layers a 3 0 inch diameter stationary piston sampler
was used The average hydraulic pressures in p s i required to
advance the tube into the soft material are shown at the appropriate
depths on the Logs of Borings
