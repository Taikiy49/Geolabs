PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located in the rear of the residential lot at 3043 Numana Road in 
Kalihi Valley in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. An existing 30-inch diameter 
concrete drain line runs along the side of the property and discharges into Kalihi Stream. 
We understand that the drain line and headwall area on the Kalihi Stream slope are 
damaged and the development of remedial measures is desired. The project site is shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on as-built drawings and our field observations, the existing 30-inch drain line 
at the front of the lot is connected to a catch basin with an invert elevation of +352 feet 
Mean Sea Level (MSL). The drain line slopes down towards the rear of the lot and exit at a 
Concrete Rock Masonry (CRM) headwall structure with a pipe invert elevation of +317 feet 
MSL. 
Access to the project site is limited to walking up Kalihi Stream or by a narrow area 
along the north side of the lot at 3043 Numana Road.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae and 
Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger Koolau 
shield is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and forms the 
majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae Volcano became extinct while 
Koolau Volcano was still active, and it was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking 
against its eastern flank.  
Kalihi Valley was formed by stream erosion of the Koolau Range basalt flows. This 
initial period of erosion and valley carving was followed by several periods of relative rise 
and fall of sea level, which permitted valley filling by alluvium followed by subsequent periods 
of erosion. During the Quaternary Age, Honolulu Volcanic Series lava flowed down the 
valley and was subsequently partially covered by Quaternary Age alluvium. 
The project site is on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano and on the western 
bank of Kalihi Stream. The project site is primarily underlain by geologic units consisting of 
Honolulu Volcanic Series basalt formation and older alluvial deposits.

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
A site reconnaissance of the project site was conducted by our engineer on 
July 16, 2020 to evaluate the existing site conditions. In general, the slope area around 
and below the existing drainpipe headwall was recently cleared of vegetation. The 
headwall consisted of a CRM wall bearing on a basalt rock formation. It appeared that the 
steep slope directly below the CRM headwall consisted of basalt rock formation. The 
slope then flattens out at the existing damaged splash pad. The existing concrete splash 
pad appeared to be underlain by alluvium. Below this area, a plastic sewage pipe was 
observed exposed at the existing ground surface underlain by concrete debris, cobbles, 
and boulders. 
The height of the stream bank slope ranged from about 25 to 30 feet with inclination 
from about 0.4 Horizontal to 1 Vertical (0.4H:1V) to 0.7H:1V.   
Vegetation and man-made debris were observed on the small slope between the 
CRM headwall and the existing CRM retaining wall at the top of the slope belonging to 
the adjacent residence. A large tree was also observed growing close to the CRM 
headwall. 
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the information provided, we understand that a slope protection system is 
desired in the area around the existing drain pipe outlet to mitigate erosion. We also 
understand that the City and County of Honolulu wants the slope protection system to 
extend beyond either side of the CRM headwall by at least 8 feet. 
Rock Scaling 
Prior to applying the slope protection system, rock scaling should be performed 
within the slope remediation area. Rock slope scaling activity typically consists of 
experienced scaling crews employing manual labor and hand tools to remove loose rocks 
and precariously positioned rock fragments from the slope face. The scaling crews 
performing the rock slope scaling should be experienced in the performance of rock slope 
scaling by rappelling and/or working from crane baskets.  Rock slope scaling work 
typically proceeds by starting from the top of the slope and working downward in a 
controlled and orderly manner. 
In general, rock slope scaling involves the careful removal of existing loose rocks 
and potential hazardous debris from an exposed and vegetation-free slope face. 
If loose boulders are discovered during the vegetation removal and rock scaling 
process, or if additional unforeseen slope instability is caused by the rock scaling process, 
Geolabs should be contacted immediately to evaluate the slope conditions and course of 
action on a case-by-case basis.

In general, each rock slope scaling crew typically consists of a scaling supervisor, 
who should be a specialist in rock slope scaling, and one to three experienced rock slope 
scalers. Due to the limited width of the scaling area, we anticipate up to two rock scalers 
may be used. The scaling supervisor should have a minimum of five years of high slope 
scaling experience with a minimum of 2,000 hours of demonstrated experience 
supervising slope scaling. The scaling supervisor should have completed the American 
Red Cross “basic first aid course,” or equivalent, and should have experience or training 
in the use of emergency remote rescue techniques. 
Each slope scaler is an individual who is engaged in accessing the slope face and 
removing loose rock and materials from the slope face using a variety of hand tools such 
as pry bars at locations that require modified rock climbing techniques for the safe 
execution of the work. Each slope scaler should have a minimum of two years experience 
with a minimum of 1,000 hours of demonstrated experience performing similar slope 
scaling work. 
The scaling crews are typically supported by an ancillary ground crew that is 
responsible for the cleanup of the materials and debris deposited at the base of the slope. 
It should be noted that rock slope scaling work may be considered “hazardous” 
work and should be classified as a “high risk” activity. Visibility is the most essential 
attribute to the successful performance of rock slope scaling by the rock slope scaling 
crews. Therefore, night work for rock slope scaling at the site should be discouraged for 
crew safety reasons. In addition, the difficulty in maintaining proper and complete 
illumination of the work site makes night work for rock slope scaling undesirable. 
Furthermore, it should be noted that night work for rock slope scaling should be avoided 
because of potentially poor visibility and shadow conditions, which may reduce the ability 
of the rock slope scalers to properly assess the rock slope conditions and detect the 
presence of loose materials and precariously located rocks. 
Shotcrete Slope Protection  
Based on the basalt rock formation observed on the exposed slope face, we 
recommend the installation of a fiber-reinforced shotcrete facing to protect and reduce 
future erosion on the existing slope. We understand that the existing headwall structure 
for the 30-inch drain line will be removed.  Therefore, the shotcrete facing should be 
installed around the opening of the existing 30-inch drain line. 
The existing vegetation and man-made debris within the limits of the slope 
protection system should be removed. All existing woody stem shrubbery and trees on 
the slope face should be cut flush with the finish ground surface, sprayed, and killed to 
reduce the potential for undesired regrowth on the slope face. In addition, existing ground 
cover vegetation should be removed to completely expose the slope surface in 
preparation for the installation of the slope improvements.

As previously noted, we observed at least one large tree growing near the CRM 
headwall. The existing large tree(s) located within about 5 feet of the limits of the slope 
protection system should be cut close to the existing ground surface, sprayed, and killed. 
Subsurface tree trunk remnants should not be excavated and instead be left in-place. 
After clearing the existing vegetation and debris, we recommend installing a 
minimum 6-inch thick layer of fiber-reinforced shotcrete on the exposed slope. The 
shotcrete should contain 7.5 pounds of Strux 85/50 synthetic structural fiber per cubic 
yard or 13 pounds of Cem-FIL Anti-Crak 67/36 alkali resistant glass fiber per cubic yard. 
The shotcrete should be placed above, below, and at least 8 feet laterally on either side 
of the existing CRM headwall for a total width of about 18 linear feet of treated slope area. 
At the base of the slope, the shotcrete should be keyed into the existing ground to prevent 
undermining due to scour. The shotcrete key should extend down at least 4 feet or until 
solid basalt rock formation is encountered. In addition, the upstream and downstream 
edges of the shotcrete slope protection should be keyed into the existing slope a minimum 
depth of 2 feet and 4 feet for the slope areas above and below the design flood level, 
respectively, or until solid basalt rock formation is encountered.  
The shotcrete slope protection system should be well-drained to reduce the 
potential for build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of 
about 1 cubic foot of permeable backfill material, such as open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), wrapped with non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or 
equal) placed up against the base of the slope. The wrapped permeable backfill should 
be hydraulically connected to a weephole that daylights at the front of the shotcrete slope. 
The weepholes should be spaced no more than 5 feet apart.  If prefabricated drains are 
used along the slope, the weepholes should be hydraulically connected to the 
prefabricated drains.
