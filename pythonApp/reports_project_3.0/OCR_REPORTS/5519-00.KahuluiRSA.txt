This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Runway Safety Area RSA Improvements project at the Kahului
Airport in the District of Kahului on the Island of Maui Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for
the airport improvement project These recommendations are intended for design of
pavements foundations slabs on grade retaining structures and site grading only
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted
at the end of this report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes the older West Maui
Tertiary Epoch and the more recent East Maui also known as Haleakala Pleistocene
Epoch The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain located between these
two volcanoes The project site is located at the northern area of this gently sloping
plain
The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows from Haleakala ponding on
West Maui It is comprised of alluvium washed from the slopes of West Maui and
East Maui Haleakala The erosional processes are dominated by the detachment of
soil and rock masses from the mountain walls and the soil materials are transported
downslope toward the Isthmus primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials
reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant
and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the
stream flows transmit large volumes of water for very short duration Because of this
the transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load
consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When
the erosional base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes
begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the
materials _ Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the
coarse grained portion of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or
conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors
and upstream in headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is
transported downslope covering the older alluvial soil deposits Depending on the local
base level and rate of transport these newer sediments are generally transient in terms

Underlying the alluvial soil deposits are overlapping lava from the West Maui and
East Maui volcanoes The bulk of the Haleakala Shield was built during the late
Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows of the
Honomanu Volcanic Series During the Pleistocene Epoch the characteristics of the
lava changed to very hard thickly bedded flows of andesitic composition These lavas
have been grouped as the Kula Volcanic Series Typically the basalt rock formation
consists of thinly to thickly bedded a a and pahoehoe type lava flows Development of
the Kahului Airport in the past decades has brought the project site to its present

2 2 Site Description
Kahului Airport is located on the northern edge of the land bridge between
Haleakala and the West Maui Mountain Range on the Island of Maui Hawaii The
airport occupies 1 391 acres of land and is located three miles east of the town of
Kahului The airport has two intersecting runways with air carrier facilities for domestic
overseas and interisland commercial service Runway 2 20 is the primary runway with
asphaltic concrete paved surface measuring about 6 995 feet long and 150 feet wide
Runway 5 23 is a secondary runway with asphaltic concrete paved surface measuring
about 4 990 feet long and 150 feet wide Kahului Airport provides commuter air taxi and
general aviation operations including helicopter operations in separate locations
Vehicular access to the passenger terminal commuter air taxi cargo scenic tour
operations general aviation facilities and airport support facilities is via a roadway
network that connects to the Haleakala and or Hana Highways
The limits of the airport are generally bounded by chain link fence with an
existing perimeter road that is paved only in some sections The majority of the existing
ground surface outside the runways taxiways is relatively flat and consists of silty sands
and clayey silts with basalt rock formation at shallow depths in localized areas The
runway safety area is generally covered with short grasses while the existing mounds

Cavities and Lava Tubes
Some portions of the project site is generally underlain by Pahoehoe lavas which
typically contain larger and more extensive lava tubes Lava tubes are usually
difficult to detect solely by visual observation of the ground surface unless the tube
structure has experienced a prior collapse event that breaches the roof cover
Often lava tube structures are encountered during site excavation and compaction
when significant disturbance to the near surface rock materials is experienced
Therefore special attention should be given to the site preparation procedures to
systematically proof roll the subgrade prior to construction of embankments and
improvements over the basalt rock formation at the project site

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions across the airport site were explored by drilling and
sampling 22 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 22 extending to depths of
about 7 5 to 22 4 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using
a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools In
addition three test pits designated as TP 1 through TP 3 were excavated at select
locations to depths of about 6 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface using a
CASE 580 backhoe equipment The approximate locations of the borings drilled and
test pits excavated are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings and test pits were classified by visual
and textural examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings and
Test Pits Plates A 1 through A 25
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths In addition grab samples were obtained from selected depths in the
excavated test pits during our field exploration program
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and classification

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense rock formation with a
high RQD would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous
methods of excavation

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs
of Borings and Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths
Three 1 inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples of the surface clayey soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the
near surface soils The test results are presented on Plate B 1
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B 2
Ten Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 ASTM C 136 were performed on
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in
soil classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on
Plates B 3 and B 4
Two sets of corrosion tests including minimum resistivity
chloride content and sulfate content were
performed by Oceanic Analytical Laboratory Inc and Del Mar Analytical on selected
soil samples obtained from our field exploration The results of these tests are
summarized on Plate B 5
To evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on site clayey soils
two unconfined compression tests were performed on selected in situ samples in
accordance with ASTM D 2166 The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths Individual stress strain curves of the unconfined
compression tests are presented on Plates B 6 and B 7
Six unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected rock
cores to evaluate their unconfined compressive strength of the rock formation
encountered Results of the unconfined compression tests are presented on Plate B 8
Two Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the
direct shear tests are presented on Plates B 9 and B 10

Appendix B
Laboratory Testing
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils and potential borrow material to evaluate the
pavement support characteristics of the near surface soils and quality of the potential
borrow material Results of the CBR tests are presented on Plates B 11 through B 14
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils and potential borrow material to evaluate the dry
density and moisture content relationships The test results are presented on
Plates B 15 through B 18
