REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kaho olawe is located about 11 kilometers to the south of the island of
Maui and about 27 kilometers to the southeast of the island of Lana i Kaho olawe
encompasses approximately 117 square kilometers in land area with a maximum elevation
of about 450 meters Mean Sea Level MSL at the summit of Lua Makika on the eastern
portion of the island
Kaho olawe is believed to be composed of a single shield volcano formed by the
extrusion of successive layers of thinly bedded pahoehoe and a a basalt lava flows that
emanated from the primary eruptive rift zones and caldera at Lua Makika It is believed that
at one time the islands of Molokai Lana i Maui and Kaho olawe were joined as a single
landmass during the geologic evolution of the Hawaiian Islands
In situ chemical weathering of the basaltic lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to
2 million years The weathering process has formed a thin mantle of residual soils which
grade to saprolite with depth In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and
is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite generally grades to
basaltic rock formation with depth
Intermittent stream flow has carved gullies and some deep gulches into the residual
soil and weathered rock surface of the island The climate is generally arid and windy due
to the leeward location with respect to the island of Maui Most rainfall occurs during large
Kona storm events or as light misting trade wind showers The geomorphology of the island
is most significantly affected by wind erosion and surface scouring which has created a
generally barren hard pan soil surface
The lower slopes and coastal areas of the island including the base camp show
exposure of basalt rock formation with some generally thin alluvial and wind blown soil cover
The soils generally consist of brown to dark brown clayey silts derived from land erosion and
wind blown sediments The coastal areas of Kaho olawe are expressed as a series of
protruding rocky headlands separated by small bays and sandy beach shorelines Today
the smail bays appear to represent drowned valleys formed when Pleistocene age sea levels
stood significantly lower than the present day
SITE DESCRIPTION
The existing leaching field is located within the limits of the Kaho olawe Base Camp
at Honokanaia on the southwestern shore of the island of Kaho olawe as shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1 The leaching field is located southwesterly of the existing
carpentry building and northwesterly of the existing Range Control and Operations Center
building The leaching field resides on generally flat to very gently sloping ground adjacent
to a rocky bank bordering the shoreline beach as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The

LEACHING FIELD
Based on the available as built documents and photographs of the leaching field
construction the site was prepared by excavating from the ground surface to depths of about
0 9 to 1 2 meters below the surface The bottom of the leaching field may correspond with
the upper surface of the in situ rock formation observed at the site The discharge pipes
consist of five parallel 100 millimeter mm diameter perforated pipes approximately
15 meters in length and set about 0 9 meters apart The as built data indicates that granular
backfill consisting of rock and some soil was placed surrounding the
perforated PVC pipes Based on our test boring data and site observations the near surface
backfill generally consists of clayey silt soils and cobbles excavated on site materials
The leaching field discharge pipes are connected to a distribution box which is fed
from a small holding tank with an estimated capacity on the order of about 190 liters The
holding tank is covered and set below grade The holding tank is the only visible and
accessible component of the leaching field system therefore the effluent introduction and
head level measurements were performed from this point
PERCOLATION TESTING

the capacity of the existing leaching field system The tests were performed by pumping
seawater into the holding tank and measuring the resultant head build up in the holding
tank The water source for testing was pumped from the approximately 3 785 liter capacity

The percolation test flow rate was stepped from a minimum of about 0 38 liters per

Based on the results of the relatively short duration tests it appears that the existing
leaching field system is capable of handling flows in excess of the maximum tested flow
rate of about 3 8 liters per second By extrapolation of the current test data flow rates up
to about 6 3 liters per second may be disposed of without the head level in the holding tank
reaching the top of the distribution line leading to the leaching field
However due to the limited water supply extended duration tests could not be performed
to evaluate the potential for development of seepage conditions along the exposed
shoreline bank
During the two days of percolation testing the bank located on the makai side of the
leaching field above the beach was observed for signs of water seepage Seepage of
water was not observed within the duration of the testing However some dampness and
salt crystal precipitate was observed along the exposed basalt rock fractures of the bank
bordering the leaching field and beach area below The dampness and salt crystal
precipitate is believed to be the result of wicking and evaporation of brine water as it
percolates through the basalt rock fractures Based on conversations with the base camp
personnel the damp conditions and salt precipitate features were observed by others as
existing before our site visit
Based on our observations the brine effluent appears to be satisfactorily
percolating downward into the basalt rock formation underlying the leaching field It does
not appear that the effluent is flowing laterally along the surface of the rock formation and
seeping out of the exposed rock bank However it is not certain if seepage conditions may
develop as a result of high application rates on the order of greater than 3 2 liters per
second for prolonged periods of time It is possible that some brine seepage could
develop on the rock bank
In addition to the stepped rate percolation testing of the leaching field falling head

by hand auger and a portable gasoline powered auger unitil refusal conditions were

0 3 to 0 6 meters below the ground surface The materials encountered include dark
brown clayey silts with some sands and basalt cobbles The soils were observed as very

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the short duration and stepped rate testing performed it appears that the
existing leaching field should be capable of accommodating a long term constant brine
application rate of about 2 0 liters per second without developing seepage conditions along
the exposed shoreline bank Extrapolation of the test data suggests that application rates
on the order of about 4 0 to 6 0 liters per second may be accommodated by the existing
leaching field although the long term effects pertaining to the development of potential
seepage along the exposed bank are not known based on the currently available data
If the leaching field is to be designed to accommodate a brine disposal capacity in
excess of 3 0 liters per second we believe that it would be prudent to conduct some longer
term percolation tests using seawater at high flow rates to evaluate the potential for
seepage conditions at the shoreline bank to develop In order to obtain uninterrupted flow
of 3 0 to 6 0 liters per second a larger pump or a combination of smaller trash pumps
would be required to provide the flow from the shoreline area A test duration of perhaps
5 days of pumping at about 8 to 10 hours per day should be sufficient to evaluate for
seepage and leakage conditions at flow rates ranging between 3 2 and 6 3 liters per
second During the pumping the exposed shoreline bank and surrounding area located
down gradient of the leaching field should be observed for brine seepage
