PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS

existing three story building was built in 1913 Based on review of the as built plans we
understand that the existing building has a footprint of about 400 feet in length and about
62 feet in width About one third of the building on the western side has a basement level
The building is supported by a shallow foundation system
We understand that seismic retrofit of the existing building is required Therefore it
is desired to estimate the site class with respect to seismic design consideration One of
the evaluation approaches for the site class evaluation is to drill borings extending 100 feet
below bottom of the footing to examine the soil rock consistency and shear strength
We envision that bottom of the foundations for the existing basement level may be
situated at or near natural groundwater level Considering the project site is in the vicinity
of the Pacific Ocean the groundwater level may fluctuate with the tide Therefore we
understand that it is necessary to monitor groundwater level as part of the exploration

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau shield reached the
end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
The geologic conditions underlying the project site are the result of a complex
series of deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio eustatic sea level changes
and post erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch Following the
cessation of volcanic activity of the Koolau Volcano alluvial soil materials were deposited
in a deltaic or estuarine environment in the area of Pearl Harbor
The sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 100 meters below and
rising about 30 meters above the present sea level Concurrent with a rise in sea level
during the late Pleistocene era eruption of the Salt Lake Makalapa member of the
Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash
which consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally known as mudrock layers Subsequent
erosion dissected and thinned those mantles of volcanic tuff
During the recent epoch as sea level rose to its current stand the area was
covered with soft and loose lagoonal marine deposits These deposits were
subsequently covered with man made fills placed during the middle portion of this century
for land reclamation projects during construction of the existing Pearl Harbor Naval
Shipyard The project site is located at the edge of the former shoreline of Pearl Harbor
The subsurface conditions of the project site generally consist of fill materials overlying
weathered volcanic tuff materials coralline formation and alluvium deposit at greater
depth

FIELD EXPLORATION
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 101 and 102 The borings extended to about 104
and 113 feet below the existing ground surface using a truck mounted drill rig equipped
with continuous flight solid stem augers and HQ casing The boring locations are shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D 2487
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1 of Appendix A
Graphic representations of the materials encountered and other observations such as
Appendix A
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock and or hard materials encountered at the project site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock and or hard
material left in the hollow core of the bit are mechanically recovered for examination and
description Rock cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description
System as shown on the Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2 of Appendix A
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of soil rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material
recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if
3 7 feet of material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be
74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

LABORATORY TESTS
Samples recovered from our field exploration were visually examined and
classified in our laboratory in Honolulu Hawaii Selected samples were tested to evaluate
the physical characteristics and engineering properties of the soils encountered The
laboratory testing program was formulated with the emphasis on evaluating the moisture
density plasticity gradation and shear strength of the soils encountered
Moisture Content and Unit Weight tests were performed on selected soil samples to
aid in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and correlating
the layers between the borings The moisture content tests were performed in general
accordance with ASTM D 2216 The unit weight tests were performed in general
accordance with ASTM D 2937 The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths in Appendix A
To provide information on the particle size distribution of the soils encountered
three Sieve Analysis tests were performed to evaluate the gradation characteristics and to
aid in soil classification The tests were performed in accordance with ASTM C 117 and
C 136 Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B 1 of Appendix B
To evaluate the unconfined compressive strength characteristics of the volcanic tuff
formation encountered at the project site three Unconfined Compression tests were
performed on the selected rock core samples in accordance with ASTM D 7012 The test
results are presented on Plate B 2 in Appendix B

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
two borings extending to depths of approximately 104 and 113 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the borings encountered about 10 feet of fill and a relatively thin layer of
volcanic tuff rock overlying coral reef formation and stiff alluvium extending to the
maximum depth drilled of about 113 feet below the existing ground surface
Six borings were drilled by others and boring logs were included in the RFP
documents Approximate locations of the six borings are also shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 Combining the two borings drilled and the six borings presented in the RFP
documents it appears that thickness of the fill placed on the volcanic tuff rock varies
significantly from little or none up to 10 feet within the building footprint Details of the
subsurface profile near building grid lines A and D are presented on the Generalized
It should be noted that contaminated groundwater was encountered at the time of
our field exploration The drilling operation was immediately shutdown Environmental
protocol was implemented by containing drill cutting and fluids using steel drums and
heavy duty absorbent pads with associated drill mudpad and personal protection
equipment The contaminated drill cutting and fluids were disposed by the general
contractor Nan Inc
We encountered groundwater at depths of 10 to 11 feet below the existing ground
surface equivalent to Elevation of about 1 feet Mean Sea Level in the borings drilled
during our field exploration It should be noted that groundwater levels are expected to
fluctuate with tide variation in rainfall surface water runoff groundwater withdrawal and
other factors
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered from our field exploration are
reference the boring logs provided by the RFP documents are included in Appendix C
GROUNDWATER LEVEL MONITORING
A monitoring well was installed in Boring No 102 at a depth of about 25 feet below
ground surface The monitoring well consisted of 10 feet of screen and 15 feet of solid
2 inch diameter PVC pipe with a 6 inch metal cover
A vibrating wire piezometer was installed at the bottom of the well for continuous
monitoring of groundwater level The piezometer data was recorded in a data logger in
one hour interval for 7 days between December 13 and 20 2011 The piezometer data
was further verified by periodic hand measurements as quality control The results of the
groundwater level monitoring are presented on the Plate 4

classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as being a Stiff Soil site corresponding to

where columns at Gridline 16 serve as grade transition from basement level up to ground

Based on the above as built information and subsurface conditions encountered at
the project site the existing footings may be supported on the medium dense fill material
or weathered volcanic tuff rock We recommend an allowable bearing pressure of up to
3 000 pounds per square foot psf for design of shallow foundations bearing on the fill
material The bearing pressure may be increased to for footings bearing directly
on the weathered volcanic tuff rock
This allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased
by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces In
general footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent
grades
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below an
imaginary 45 degree plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or
the foundations should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
The bottom of footing excavations in fill materials should be recompacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface
prior to placing reinforcing steel and or concrete Soft and or loose materials encountered
at the bottom of footing excavations should be over excavated until dense materials are
exposed in the footing excavation The over excavation should be backfilled with select
granular fill materials moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Alternatively the bottom of
the footing may extend down to bear directly on the underlying competent material
Where the footing subgrade is within transition of fill and tuff rock we recommend
over excavating footing subgrade in tuff rock at least 6 inches for cushion fill to provide
uniform support
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of
0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the compacted fill materials Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds
per square foot per foot of depth pcf assuming that the soils around the footings are well
compacted The passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of the soil should be neglected
unless covered by pavements or slabs

EXCAVATION
Based on the information provided we understand that bottom of the new footings
may be located at about 3 5 feet below the existing slab equivalent to elevation of about
3 5 feet MSL in the basement area and 14 5 feet MSL in the non basement area
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings and interpreted
from the RFP borings we envision rock excavation for the new footing construction Due
to limited headroom space and proximity of adjacent existing footing structure use of
conventional heavy excavation equipment such as a large bulldozer excavator or similar
heavy construction equipment may not be feasible to achieve the excavations into the
rock Therefore special rock excavation technique may be implemented to reduce
excessive vibration for potential damage to the existing aging structure
