INSTRUMENTATION MONITORING
As part of the landslide study we drilled four borings each to a depth of
approximately 100 feet in November 2001 and September 2003 Four borings were
converted to inclinometer monitoring stations designated as Inclinometer Nos 1
through 4 to measure the lateral deflection of the soil at various depths Inclinometer
locations I 1 through I 4 are shown on the Site Plan Plate 1
As part of the landslide remediation design we drilled an additional nine borings
each to a depth of approximately 100 feet in 2008 Five of the borings were converted to
inclinometer monitoring stations designated as Inclinometer Nos 5 through 9
Inclinometer locations I 5 through I 9 are also shown on the Site Plan Plate 1
In general the inclinometers were read at approximately 2 to 3 month intervals to
measure the rate of ground movement and to monitor the effects of heavy rain in
relation to movement The cumulative displacements measured by the inclinometers
one set per year are presented on Plates 2 through 10 Additional data sets of the
cumulative displacements collected in approximately 2 to 3 month intervals are

Based on inclinometer Nos 1 through 3 and 6 it appears that the sliding zone
may be relatively shallow approximately 11 to 15 feet below the ground surface
Inclinometer Nos 4 and 5 are located between Inclinometer Nos 2 and 3 It appears
that the slide zone may be located slightly deeper than Inclinometer Nos 1 through 3 at
a depth of approximately 20 feet below the ground surface
We believe that the ground movement slip circle may not extend to the existing
slope toe as evident by no significant ground movement measured by Inclinometer
Nos 7 and 8 This may be partially due to the buttress installed at the slope toe in the
previous landslide remediation project in the 1970 s
Inclinometer No 9 indicated lateral creep movement at shallow depths We
believe that this creep movement may be related to traffic surcharge laterally acting on
the surface fill materials and medium stiff clayey soils encountered in the boring The
existing leaning electrical utility pole with leaning angle of up to about 7 degrees from
vertical axial adjacent to Inclinometer No 9 may be caused by the creeping ground
movement
Status of Earth Movements
The project road and adjacent slope continue to experience slow downhill creep
movements Based on the history of the earth movement monitoring sudden
catastrophic earth movements are not likely however cannot be completely ruled out
Extreme rainfall events and the increased subsurface groundwater seepage associated
with such rain events have the potential to accelerate the earth movement in the short
term and cause significant disruption to the existing pavements and fixed structures in
the area
The continued earth movements will continue to adversely affect road surfaces
and buried utilities Continued maintenance of the infrastructure is important to reduce
sources of water infiltrating into the subsurface which can accelerate movements
Long Term Repair Options
A long term repair option utilizing a drilled shaft wall to support the road fill has
been developed and is described in our report entitled Geotechnical Engineering
Services Mona Street Landslide Remediation Hawaii dated
Status of Earth Movements
We will continue monitoring the lateral ground movement through the installed
instrumentations and mapping the pavement conditions
