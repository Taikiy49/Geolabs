SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered about 1 to 1.5 feet of surface fills 
underlain by coralline deposits consisting of dense coral reef formation and medium dense 
coralline detritus extending to the maximum depth drilled of about 20.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. It should be noted that localized loose pockets were encountered 
within the coralline detritus. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the 
time of our field exploration. It should be noted that water levels may vary with seasonal 
precipitation, storm surge conditions, groundwater withdrawal, and other factors. 
The water infiltration percolation tests were conducted at the site at a depth of 
about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. The soil permeability at the site varied from 
8.5 x 10-5 to 3.1 x 10-4 cm per second.  We anticipate that the overall coral formation would 
have good infiltration capacity; however, zones of the hard, near-surface coral formation 
encountered at the site may provide a semi-impervious barrier that would inhibit the rapid 
percolation of stormwater into the substrata during a storm event in localized areas. 
We believe the proposed swimming pool structure may be supported on a shallow 
mat foundation bearing on the recompacted in-situ soils.  We recommend using an 
allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,000 pounds per square foot (psf) to design the 
shallow mat foundation bearing on the recompacted in-situ soils. 
To provide a uniform surface below the mat foundation, we recommend supporting 
the mat foundation on at least 6 inches of select borrow compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. We also recommend proof rolling at the bottom of the 
foundation subgrade, to detect loose/soft spot. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Kalaeloa Rental Homes at 484 Independence Road project in the 
Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are 
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the proposed development and the new swimming pool. These recommendations are 
intended for the design of foundations, retaining structures, and pavements only. The 
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at 
the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located near the southwestern corner of the existing Kalaeloa 
Rental Homes at 484 Independence Road adjacent to the former Barbers Point Airfield.  
Based on the information provided, we understand that the project includes construction 
of a new swimming pool, expansion of existing parking lots, and re-configuration of a 
recreation park. 
Due to the new development, we anticipate that design of the stormwater runoff 
disposal system is required to meet the new drainage requirements for Low Impact 
Development (LID) guidelines.  
Detailed structural design of the improvements and swimming pool structure was 
not available at the time this report was prepared.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be 
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. 
The project site is situated on the Ewa Coastal Plain, which extends seaward off 
the southeastern flank of Waianae and the southwestern flank of Koolau. This plain was 
formed by the accumulation of a thick wedge of sediments and coral reef formations on 
the flanks of the two volcanoes.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to 
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. 
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous 
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered stiff clayey surface fills and dense to 
medium dense coralline deposits extending to the maximum depth drilled of about 
20.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The coralline deposits consisted of dense 
coral reef formation and medium dense coralline detritus.  It should be noted that localized 
loose pockets were encountered within the coralline detritus. 
We recommend using shallow mat foundation bearing on the recompacted in-situ 
soils for support of the proposed swimming pool structure, with an allowable bearing 
pressure of up to 4,000 pounds per square foot (psf).  We also recommend supporting the 
mat foundation on at least 6 inches of select borrow compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction.  
To detect loose and/or soft spot, we recommend proof rolling at the bottom of the 
foundation subgrade. 
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical recommendations for 
design are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Swimming Pools 
In general, we believe the proposed new swimming pool structure may be 
supported on shallow mat foundation system bearing on the recompacted in-situ 
coralline materials.  Our field exploration indicated that the dense coralline formation 
may be expected at shallow depths. However, localized loose pockets were 
encountered within the coralline detritus. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may 
be used to design the shallow mat foundation bearing on the recompacted in-situ 
coralline materials.  This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased 
by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Due to the sloping bottom of the swimming pool, we envision that the swimming 
pool foundation will bear on the coralline materials.  To provide a uniform surface below

Where loose/soft spots are detected (yielding and/or pumping), over-excavation

The structural fill material should consist of imported non-expansive, select granular 
materials compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture 
is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry 
density. 
Foundations next to retaining walls, other foundations, utility trenches, or 
easements should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward 
from the bottom edge of the structure or utility trench. Alternatively, footings should be 
extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines or bottom of the retaining 
walls. This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade 
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable 
foundation settlement. 
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate that total footing settlements may be on the order of 
1 inch or less.  We estimate that the differential settlements between adjacent 
foundations to be on the order of about 0.5 inches.

3.2 
Site Grading 
At the on-set of earthwork site grading, the areas within the contract grading limits 
should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious material and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site.  Soft and 
yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be over-excavated to 
expose firm or dense material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted engineered fill. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly 
disposed of off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, existing structures and pavements that are to be 
demolished should be removed completely. Over-excavations resulting from demolition 
should be backfilled with compacted on-site soils or select granular fill materials.  Existing 
utilities to be abandoned should be removed, and the resulting excavation should be 
properly backfilled with excavated on-site soils or select granular fill materials placed in 
8-inch loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Utilities 
to be abandoned in-place under the proposed structures should be backfilled by pumping 
lean concrete under low pressure. 
Imported structural fills should consist of non-expansive, select granular fill 
materials, such as crushed coral and basalt.  The materials should be well graded from 
coarse to fine with particles no greater than 3 inches in largest dimension.  The materials 
should have a California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of

3.3 
Underground Detention/Infiltration System 
In accordance with the stormwater requirements, infiltration tests and test 
pits/borings are required to support the on-site stormwater disposal design.  To 
characterize the water infiltration rates at the project site, we conducted a cased 
borehole percolation test in one boring at a depth of about 5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. 
The water infiltration rates into the subsurface soils were tested by performing 
cased borehole percolation tests at the bottom of the test boring in general accordance 
with the procedures described in the Maryland Stormwater Management manual. The 
boring was pre-soaked with water for at least 4 hours prior to testing.  Water was 
introduced into the boring to a depth of about 2 feet above the bottom of the boring; 
and, the drop of the water level in the boring was measured with respect to time for a 
falling head test until the water level approached the bottom of the borehole or for one 
hour. 
Water infiltration was not observed in the testing boring at a depth of about 5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. We believe that bottom of the testing borehole was 
located within the dense coralline formation as a semi-impervious barrier. To verify in-situ 
representative infiltration characteristic, the casing was removed for falling-head 
permeability testing. The test was carried out in three trials until achieving a steady-state 
with relatively consistent permeability coefficient.  Detailed testing data and results are 
presented on Plates C-1 through C-5 and also summarized in the following table.

3.4 
Pavement Design 
We envision new pavements may be required for the project. In general, we 
anticipate the vehicle loading for the new pavements would consist of primarily 
passenger vehicles and light trucks with limited heavy trucks. Therefore, we have 
assumed generally light to medium traffic loading conditions for pavement design 
purposes. 
We have made our pavement design assuming the pavement subgrade soil will 
consist of primarily the on-site stiff clayey fills. Based on the site conditions encountered 
and the traffic loading conditions presented above, the following flexible pavement 
section may be used for design purposes. 
Flexible Pavements 
  2.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete 
  6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction)  
  8.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on a Moist Compacted Subgrade 
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. The aggregate base and subbase course materials 
should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates compacted to no less than 95 percent 
relative compaction. CBR and field density tests should be performed on the actual 
subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above 
section.

3.5 
Underground Utilities 
We envision that new underground utilities and utility connections will be required 
for the project.  In general, good construction practices should be utilized in the 
installation and backfilling of the trenches.  The contractor should determine the method 
and equipment to be used for trench excavation, subject to practical limits and safety 
considerations.  In addition, the excavations should comply with the applicable local 
safety requirements.  Trench shoring design and installation should be the responsibility 
of the contractor. 
In general, we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the pipes for support.  
Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), also should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above 
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes.  It is critical to use this 
free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches 
of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes.  Improper trench 
backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 12 inches above the pipes 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on-site soils.  The backfill 
should be placed in maximum 8-inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no 
less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future 
ground subsidence.  Where trenches are below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the 
trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to depths of about 5 to 
20.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped 
with continuous flight augers and coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis.  These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in 
general accordance with ASTM D3550/D3550M, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by 
driving a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 
30 inches.  In addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general 
accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by 
driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The 
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths.  The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used.  The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  The pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils.  The test results are presented on Plate B-5.

In-Situ Permeability Tests 
One In-Situ Permeability test using falling-head method was performed at a 
selected location (B-3). The in-situ permeability test provides the coefficient of 
permeability within the testing interval. For the falling head testing, the water level was 
measured versus time until it reached equilibrium steady state. The permeability test 
was conducted in three trials until achieving a steady state of permeability. The results 
of the permeability test are presented on Plates C-1 through C-3.
