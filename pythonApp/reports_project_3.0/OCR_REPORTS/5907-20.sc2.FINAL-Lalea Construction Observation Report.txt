Project Considerations
The project site is at the northern end of the existing Lalea townhouse development
adjacent to Hawaii Kai Drive on the Island of Oahu Hawaii as shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1 A portion of the Lalea development including two townhouse
buildings and a rear driveway is exposed to potential rockfall hazards from the adjacent
mountain slopes In an effort to improve safety and reduce the risk for dangerous rockfall
encroachment on the housing area rockfall protection improvements were constructed
The constructed improvements consist of about 330 linear feet of impact capacity
rockfall barrier fencing on the lower slope above the rear driveway area
This report addresses the construction of the rockfall impact barrier fencing and
associated ground anchor system at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 This report provides a generalized description of our field observations conducted
during the rockfall impact barrier construction and provides a summary of the wire cable
ground anchor installations The records for the wire cable anchor proof testing performed
by the contractor are presented in Appendix A

Barrier Post Foundations
The field layout of the ten concrete block foundations for the barrier posts was
performed in accordance with Shop Drawing No 1 dated by Geobrugg
North America In general the layout consisted of 40 foot post spacing for the 10 foot high
portion of the barrier and 35 foot post spacing for the 12 foot high portion of the barrier
The ten post foundations identified as P 1 through P 10 on the Site Plan were
excavated using a small Bobcat track mounted backhoe and manual labor In general the
excavations encountered near surface materials consisting of stiff dark brown clay with
embedded basaltic cobbles and boulders representing colluvium The colluvial material
extended to an average depth of about 2 feet below the existing ground surface Below the
surficial colluvial material extremely weathered volcanic tuff consisting of very stiff reddish
brown clayey silt with fine sand was encountered extending toward and beyond the floor of
the foundation excavations The floor of the foundation excavations were generaily dug to
about 3 5 to 4 feet below the existing ground surface
At the floor of the excavated foundations 4 inch diameter micropile boreholes were
drilled with the required batter to a minimum depth of about 15 feet using a pneumatic rock
drill mounted on a Caterpillar 315C track backhoe The micropile boreholes generally
encountered medium hard to hard gray volcanic tuff extending to depths of about 15 to
18 feet below the floor of the foundations Loose black cinder sand was typically
encountered at depths between about 9 and 12 feet below the floor of the excavations A
water mist was added to the compressed drilling air to help stabilize the borehole through
the zone of loose cinder sand material
After drilling galvanized steel bars for the micropiles were inserted in the boreholes
The galvanized bar for the micropile installations consisted of solid threaded Grade 150
No inch diameter bar manufactured in 10 foot lengths A Grade 75 galvanized
coupling was used to provide the minimum required bar length of 17 feet The coupled
joint was deployed in the lower portion of the borehole The micropiles were grouted in the
drilled boreholes using a design grout mix consisting of one pound of Sika Intraplast N
a fluidizing expanding admixture to one sack 94 pounds of Type II Portland Cement
and 4 5 to 5 gallons of water The grout was mixed in a dual high speed colloidal mixer
and pumped under low pressure via tremie method Four micropiles were installed from
the floor of each of the ten excavations prior to pouring the concrete block foundations
Following the installation of the micropiles the block foundations were poured
Hawaiian Cement supplied the approved 4 000 psi concrete mix min 28 day strength for
the block foundations The concrete was line pumped and vibrated neat against the
sidewalls of the excavated holes Some temporary wood frame form work was installed to
contain the concrete along the exposed sides and down slope portions of the excavations

Barrier Maintenance Considerations
Rockfall impact barriers require routine non technical visual inspection to ensure
the desired performance of the system In addition periodic maintenance work to repair or
replace broken or worn barrier system components following large rock impacts may be
necessary The routine visual inspection should be performed about once per year
minimum or following known rockfall impact s The visual inspection serves to identify
potential rock debris accumulations that may require removal and to inspect for possible
changes in the system condition or effects of environmental corrosion that may reduce the
barrier performance
Barrier maintenance such as the repair or replacement of worn broken
components i e the braking elements may be required immediately following a larger
rockfall impact A large rockfall impact may be defined as an impact involving the
deployment of the braking ring elements and deflection and or sagging of the nets under
rockfall impact load The frequency of barrier maintenance typically depends on the size
and frequency of rockfall events Rockfall impact barriers are designed to require minimal
routine maintenance Where a low incidence of rockfall activity is experienced a thorough
inspection of the system components by qualified persons should be conducted at
intervals of about 5 to 10 years In corrosive environments i e coastal and forest settings
and where rockfall activity is relatively persistent qualified persons should perform the
thorough inspections at intervals of about 5 years or less

Due to the difficult access condition of some barrier installations and layperson
unfamiliarity with the system components owners typically establish a contract with a local
rockfall specialty contractor to perform the routine inspections large volume rock debris
removal and other system maintenance when needed In some cases where the barrier
is clearly visible from readily accessible locations such as a roadway or the base of a
low height slope the owner may elect to perform the routine visual inspections
themselves
A rockfall specialty contractor who maintains the proper equipment and trained
personnel to conduct work on difficult access slopes should be contracted for the safe
removal of large rockfall debris including repairs to the barrier In some cases the anchor
system and net support ropes may be under a significant load following a large rock
impact Removal of debris or replacement of system components may require the careful
release of the load by experienced personnel A rockfall specialty contractor typically
utilizes portable equipment which is transported onto the slope to perform the potentially
hazardous work Furthermore the rockfall specialty contractor would typically employ
temporary safety controls to protect down slope persons and property during the
performance of the work where needed
It is important to note that the need for routine maintenance on rockfall impact
barriers should be minimal because the barriers are designed by the manufacturer to
withstand multiple rock impacts within the design impact energy without the need for
replacing components In addition it is usually not necessary to remove rock debris if there
is only a light accumulation of rocks in the bottom of the net that does not inhibit the
available net capture height
However if a significant large and damaging rock impact that causes visible
deployment of the brake ring elements and or excessive flexure sagging of the net system
is experienced the barrier should be inspected immediately by a qualified person and
provisions for emergency repair should be initiated as soon as practical This inspection
and repair procedure is suggested to assist in maintaining the desired level of protection
should another rock impact be experienced following the initial deforming impact We
recommend that the available barrier spare parts are kept on site for ready use should the
need to perform an emergency repair arise
Manual provided by the barrier manufacturer Geobrugg North America LLC should be
referred to for more specific and detailed information regarding the system operation and
maintenance needs

Rockfall activity may be classified as a natural event which cannot be calculated or
predicted precisely Therefore it is impossible for Geolabs Inc to guarantee absolute

good engineering practice The degree of rockfall protection also may be diminished by
failure to use manufacturer approved replacement parts or by environmental corrosion of
the various components
