PRELIMINARY RECOMMENDATIONS  
REPLACE 16-INCH TRANSITE WATER LINE AT BOUGAINVILLE 
ALIAMANU MILITARY RESERVATION 
HONOLULU, OAHU, HAWAII 
Dear Mr. Luke: 
This letter report presents our preliminary geotechnical recommendations for the 
design of the proposed Replace 16-Inch Transite Water Line at Bougainville project 
located at Aliamanu Military Reservation in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The 
general project location is presented on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  It should be 
noted that the preliminary geotechnical recommendations presented herein are based on 
the information provided and our experience in the project vicinity. 
Therefore, the preliminary geotechnical recommendations provided herein may 
need to be modified upon completion of our pending field exploration and laboratory 
testing program. A detailed summary of our findings and recommendations will be 
contained in our geotechnical engineering report, which should be consulted when it 
becomes available. 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located at Aliamanu Military Reservation (AMR) in Honolulu on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  This project will consist of replacing approximately 5,750 linear feet 
of 16-inch transite pipe with a 16-inch ductile iron pipe.  
The replacement water line will begin at S1/S2 Tanks on the west side of AMR.  The 
water line will continue north along Bougainville Drive.  Then, the water line will turn south 
on Aliamanu Drive.  The water line will turn on Bougainville Loop and head towards AMR 
Child Development Center near Kauhini Road. The new water line will terminate near 
Valve 2540.  The existing transite water line will need to remain in operation during the 
installation of the new water line.

Anticipated Subsurface Conditions 
Based on available geologic and soil information and the topography of the area, 
we anticipate the subsurface conditions for the water line alignment along Bougainville 
Drive would consist of a thin layer of fill over soft to medium hard volcanic tuff formation.   
We anticipate the subsurface conditions for the water line alignment along 
Aliamanu Drive and Bougainville Loop would consist of a thin layer of fill over alluvial 
deposits.  We anticipate that the alluvial deposits would consist of high-plasticity clayey 
soils. 
We do not anticipate groundwater at shallow depths below the existing ground 
surface at the site. However, it should be noted that groundwater may occur or fluctuate 
depending on seasonal precipitation and other factors. In addition, subterranean seepage 
may be encountered during construction due to high rainfall in the area and sloping 
terrain. 
Trench Excavation 
We anticipate that the pipe inverts of the new 16-inch ductile iron water line would be 
on the order of about 5 feet below the existing ground/pavement surface. Installation of the 
proposed water line will involve excavation into the underlying volcanic tuff formation and 
clayey alluvial deposits. In general, we anticipate that the alluvial deposits along the 
proposed water line alignment may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. 
Where soft to medium hard volcanic tuff is anticipated, trench excavations into the 
volcanic tuff formation may require chipping and/or the use of hoerams to remove the 
materials and to achieve the design invert elevations. The contractor must exercise care to 
avoid over-ripping, which would disrupt the structure of the rock formation and result in a 
potential loss of bearing strength for foundations in its vicinity. 
Trench Excavation Support 
In general, we anticipate excavation depths of up to about 5 feet below the existing 
ground/pavement surface would be required for the installation of the new water line. Deeper 
excavations may be required where the water line traverses existing underground utilities. 
Where deep excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary shoring 
should be implemented for the trench excavations. Based on our site observations, we 
believe that temporary cut slopes and benching for open-cut excavations are not feasible 
since the water line alignment will be adjacent to pavements and utility pole structures. 
Therefore, trench walls would have to be cut near-vertical necessitating the use of temporary 
shoring during construction. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable safety 
requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and safety of 
the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative should be on-site at all times during

excavation and construction work for the opportunity to promptly observe changing or 
unforeseen conditions, such as high groundwater, inappropriate construction sequence or 
techniques, etc., which may affect the shoring stability. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal to 
the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for 
excessive ground movement. 
It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the 
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction. The properly braced shoring is 
essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent ground 
into the excavation, which may result in potential settlement or distress to adjacent 
structures or other improvements. 
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the adjacent 
ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. Due to the 
complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the magnitude of 
movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship, such as how quickly 
and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed, the subsurface conditions, the 
size of the excavation, and the rate of excavation. 
Therefore, it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed 
properly and as early as practical. The adjacent ground should be continuously monitored 
for cracks, dips and/or other indications of movements with instruments until the subsurface 
water line is finally backfilled. 
Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load-supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the underlying fills, alluvial deposits, and volcanic tuff 
formation, a minimum of 6 inches of granular cushion material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) should be provided below the pipe. Where soft and/or loose 
soils are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, a stabilization layer consisting of an 
additional 24 inches of open-graded gravel, such as No. 3B Fine gravel, wrapped in a 
non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below the cushion 
layer for uniform support. A typical trench detail is provided on Plate 3. 
The granular cushion layer should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of 
one-fourth (¼) of the outside pipe diameter, or trench width, whichever is greater and should 
extend 12 inches above the pipe. Before the placement of cushion material, a Geolabs 
representative should observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm materials 
are exposed at the bottom of the trench or whether a stabilization layer should be provided 
below the cushion layer.

Trench Backfill 
In general, the first zone of backfill extending from the cushion material to at least 
12 inches above the top of the pipes should consist of select free-draining granular material 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) to reduce the compaction effort required and resulting 
stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the 
potential for the formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate 
support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement 
and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to the 
pavement subgrade should consist of aggregate subbase material meeting the 
requirements stipulated in Subsection 703.17 of the State of Hawaii, Standard 
Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction, 2005 (HSS). 
As an alternative, to facilitate the backfilling and reduce the time required for applying 
compactive effort, Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) may be used to backfill the 
trenches.  The use of CLSM would also reduce the potential for future settlement of the 
trench backfill under the repeated wheel loading from traffic on the roadway. 
Aggregate base course materials required for the flexible pavement restoration 
section should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates and should conform to 
Subsection 703.06 of the HSS.   
Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance with these recommendations 
prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. Geolabs (or an accredited laboratory) 
should test and evaluate the imported fill materials for conformance with these 
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfill materials consisting of aggregate subbase materials should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 
8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
The upper 3 feet below the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. 
Aggregate base course should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to 
the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of 
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

Pavement Restoration 
It is anticipated that asphaltic concrete pavement will be used to repave the top of 
the trenches along the water line alignment within the roadway. In general, we anticipate the 
vehicle loading for these pavements will primarily consist of passenger vehicles, light pick-up 
trucks, school buses, and refuse vehicles. 
We have assumed that the pavement subgrade will consist of aggregate subbase 
course material or CLSM with a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 30 or 
higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM 
D1883. In general, the new pavements along the water line trenches should match the 
existing pavement sections or the following flexible section may be used, whichever is 
greater.

Valve Vault Foundations 
We envision that concrete valve structures may be required for the proposed water 
line. Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions at the site, we recommend an allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) for the foundation design of 
the concrete valve structures. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be 
increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by seismic forces. A 
bedding layer consisting of a minimum of 6 inches of granular material, such as No. 3B 
Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) should be provided below the bottom slab of the 
valve structure.  The bedding layer should also be extended a minimum of 12 inches 
beyond the perimeter of the bottom slab.

should be neglected.

Settlements 
Primary settlement of the water line is normally caused by the difference in the unit 
weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill material 
placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the underlying 
subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our engineering analyses, settlement on the 
order of less than 1/2 inch is anticipated for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could occur if 
improper trench support is used. 
Corrosion Potential 
The design of water line should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on 
the substructure.  Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the 
lower the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects. 
A total of nine field resistivity surveys, designated as R-101 through R-109, using the 
in-situ Wenner 4-Pin Method and an AEMC Model 4630 resistance meter manufactured by 
AEMC Instruments. Locations of the field resistivity surveys are presented on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. The method utilizes four surface probes or pins (electrodes) to evaluate the average 
resistivity (an indication of corrosivity) of the underlying soil mass.  The field resistivity survey 
was used to evaluate the average resistivity values of the soil mass at the approximate 
survey locations. Results of the field resistivity surveys are presented on Plates 4.1 through 
4.4. It should be noted that the field resistivity survey may be influenced by temperature, 
weather conditions, soil moisture, existing underground utilities and other factors. 
The soil resistivity surveys were performed at the existing ground surface along the 
shoulder and grass areas of the existing roads.  The depth of penetration of a resistivity 
sounding is a function of the electrode spacing.  Since the depth of the water line invert was 
not available at the time of our testing, the average soil resistance along the proposed 
alignment was evaluated at each survey location with the electrodes aligned at an equal 
spacing of generally 2, 3, 4, 5, and 10 feet at the ground surface.

Based on the Board of Water Supply, City and County of Honolulu, Water System
External Corrosion Control Standards, Volume 3 (2021), soil corrosivity can be categorized

Based on the results of our field resistivity survey, the subsurface conditions between

Corrosion Category B. We believe that it may be appropriate to consult with a professional
corrosion engineer to review the test results and provide detailed recommendations for
corrosion protection.
