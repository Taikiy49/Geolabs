INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Kahilu Road Improvements project near the town of Waimea in the District
of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the design of site grading and pavements for the proposed project The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed Kahilu Road Improvements project is located parallel to and south of
Mamalahoa Highway north of the Puukapu Homesteads east of the town of Waimea in the
District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii It is proposed to improve the existing Kahilu
Road estimated to be approximately 5 000 lineal feet with the construction of road and
shoulder pavement sections earth and or asphalt paved roadway swales new domestic
water lines and surface water drainage systems We anticipate that only minor cuts and fills
will be required to achieve the design finished grades for the proposed improvements
Based on previous experience in the vicinity of the project site we anticipate that the
project site is underlain by volcanic ash over basalt formation with clinker materials at
relatively shallow depths Special attention was given to the thickness of the surficial
volcanic ash and its pavement support characteristics In addition due to the generally poor
percolation characteristics of the materials in the area preliminary data on the subsurface
percolation characteristics at the site are also desired for design of the surface water run off
disposal systems

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which had activity during
recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea
which are both still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of the Mauna Kea shield volcano
The project site is underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna
Kea which were deposited during the Pleistocene Epoch Stearns and Macdonald 1946
The Hamakua Volcanic Series is typically composed of basaltic lava flows which are mantled
by a surface layer of palagonitized ash locally referred to as Pahala Ash The Pahala Ash
is generally highly sensitive and thixotropic in nature The ash soil at the project includes
some Pahala Ash and also ash where the in situ moisture content is generally lower and the
soil exists in a friable state
Based on previous experience in the Waimea area the lava flows underlying the site
are of the a a form which is characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly surface of fragmental
clinker A dense core of massive basalt which frequently has a thinner less continuous
layer of clinker below the massive core underlies the clinker The clinker surface of the lavas
at the site have been mantled by the Pahala Ash as discussed above

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three borings along
the existing dirt road extending to depths ranging from about 9 to 10 5 feet below the existing
ground surface Two additional borings were drilled along the roadway extending to a depth
of about 50 5 feet below the existing ground surface for percolation testing Three bulk
samples of the near surface soils were obtained to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the materials The approximate locations of the borings and the
near surface bulk samples are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration generally encountered a surficial deposit of brown to dark brown
volcanic ash consisting of medium stiff to very stiff clayey silt soils The thickness of the ash
soils ranges from approximately 1 5 feet near the eastern portion of the project limits to
approximately 9 feet toward the western portion of the project limits In general the ash
soils are underlain by saprolite soils and basalt formation with increasing depth The
saprolitic materials consist mainly of very stiff clayey silts with gravel Clinker layers

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered a surficial deposit of volcanic ash
consisting of medium stiff to very stiff clayey silt soils The thickness of the ash soils ranges
from approximately 1 5 feet near the eastern portion of the project limits to approximately
9 feet toward the western portion of the project limits The ash material generally has
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 4043 00 Page 7
relatively low strength characteristics and is highly susceptible to erosion in a dry to friable
state Therefore special consideration should be given to the site grading and subgrade
preparation requirements for the subject project
Our laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests indicated that the near surface
ash soils generally have variable pavement support characteristics ranging from very poor
to good Itis difficult to visually distinguish the volcanic ash soils with the better pavement
support characteristics in the field Therefore we recommend that the pavement subgrade
be over excavated 18 inches below the aggregate base course layer and be replaced with
compacted select borrow subbase course especially in the areas where the Pahala Ash soils
will be encountered Grading in some areas may encounter in situ soils with higher
CBR values Under such conditions the 18 inch select borrow subbase course may be
reduced to 6 or 12 inches depending on the exposed soil conditions Therefore we
recommend that the proposed roadway construction be observed and tested by a
representative from our office to evaluate the actual exposed subgrade conditions and to
modify the above recommended pavement sections where appropriate

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site or in a designated
area to reduce potential contamination of the excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during the clearing and grubbing operations
below areas designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted
general fill The excavated soft soils should be properly disposed of off site
After clearing and grubbing areas beneath the future pavements should be
over excavated to a minimum depth of 6 inches below the bottom of the base course
layer base bid Where necessary over excavation of up to 18 inches below the
bottom of the aggregate base course layer may be required depending on the soil
conditions encountered during the site preparation operations This over excavation
is to provide for the select borrow subbase course recommended below the pavement
section
lf zones of soft loose or saturated soils are encountered at the over excavated
subgrade level additional over excavation may be required to expose the underlying
firm material The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with compacted
general fill up to the bottom of the select borrow subbase course Due to the potential
for presence of soft and or wet soils contract documents should include unit prices
for over excavation and general fill placement to account for variations in the
over excavation and backfill quantities

The over excavated subgrade should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and proof rolled to obtain a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Due to the potentially thixotropic
nature of the near surface ash soils use of the vibratory action of the compactor
should be carefully controlled to avoid disturbing the ash soils In addition
moisture conditioning of the ash soils should be carefully controlled to avoid
over wetting of the soils It should be noted that the near surface ash soils may be
sensitive and difficult to achieve the specified compaction Therefore additional
compaction effort with proper moisture control and thinner fill lifts may be necessary
to meet the specified compaction criteria
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the subgrade soils
may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for pumping subgrade
conditions As a preliminary guide one sack of cement may be used for 50 square
feet of subgrade area This cement mix should be adjusted in the field based on the
exposed subgrade soil conditions As an alternative to cement treatment the
pumping subgrade may be proof rolled and a filter fabric such as Mirafi 500X or
equivalent may be placed on the subgrade prior to the placement of fill material
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks or the subgrade should
be scarified moisture conditioned and recompacted as recommended above
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement
weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted general fill at no additional cost to the owner
Fills and Backfills
The fill and backfill materials to be used as general fill material up to the bottom of the
select borrow subbase course may consist of the excavated on site materials with the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4043 00 Page 10
exception of the very wet ash soils greater than 75 percent moisture content
provided that the over sized materials greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension
are removed In addition the near surface cinder sands encountered at the site may
be used as a source of general fill material
If imported materials to be used for general fill are required the imported materials
should be non expansive granular material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt
or cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no
particles larger than 6 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell value of
one percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883 Imported fill
materials should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use
Fill slopes constructed of the approved on site soils may be designed with a slope
inclination of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter It should be noted that
the on site volcanic ash soils are highly susceptible to erosion especially in a dry to
friable state Therefore fill slopes should be protected by appropriate slope planting
or by other means such as placement of geotextile fabrics on the slope face
immediately after the slope is completed to reduce the potential for erosion of the
exposed slopes
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fills and backfills up to 2 feet below the finished grades of the pavement areas
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in
level lifts not exceeding about 6 to 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to at
least 90 percent relative compaction Fill select borrow subbase and aggregate
base course materials within 2 feet of the finish grade of pavement areas should be
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density

of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Optimum moisture is the water content corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment
Due to the sometimes dry and windy site conditions moisture conditioning of fill
materials will be a critical aspect during fill placement In some areas the in situ soils
appear to be dry relative to the optimum moisture content Therefore water trucks
should be available in sufficient number to provide adequate water for
moisture conditioning during compaction State and Federal governments have
enacted stringent regulations pertaining to the release of dust from construction sites
Therefore it is also advisable to institute a dust control program during grading
operations to reduce the amount of airborne dust due to the nature of the on site soils

Surface Water Run Off Disposal Systems
We understand that drywells are planned for surface water run off disposal at the
proposed road improvements project Two deep borings designated as Boring Nos 2
and 3 were drilled to a depth of about 50 5 feet below the existing ground surface Boring
No 2 was drilled at a location near the existing culvert which was proposed by the project
civil engineer Boring No 3 was located approximately 600 feet east of Boring No 2
Based on our two deeper borings the site appears to be underlain by basalt rock formation
interbedded with clinker layers starting at depths of about 10 to 15 feet below the ground
surface In Boring No 2 relatively non porous basalt formation was encountered at depths
from about 16 to 25 feet and from 35 5 to 43 feet below the existing ground surface In
Boring No 3 relatively non porous basalt formation was encountered at a depth between
15 and 41 feet below the existing ground surface
Percolation tests were conducted at various depths to evaluate the infiltration
characteristics of the subsoils to provide data for design of drywells used for disposal of
storm water runoff A total of four field percolation tests were performed at depths of
approximately 20 and 50 feet below the existing ground surface in each of the two deeper
borings Based on the four percolation tests conducted the permeability of the subsoils at
about 20 feet is about 0 005 feet per minute feet min The permeability of the subsoils at
about 50 feet was better ranging from about 0 030 to 0 075 feet min The results of our
percolation tests are presented on Plates C 1 and C 2 of Appendix C

information obtained from field borings Variations of subsoil conditions between the borings
may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until
construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to
re evaluate the recommendations provided in this report
The location of the field borings and bulk samples indicated in this report are
approximate having been estimated by taping from the features and property lines shown
on the Topographic Map by Engineers Surveyors Hawaii Inc transmitted on
Elevations of the borings were estimated by interpolation from the
elevation points shown on the same map The physical locations and elevations of the
borings and bulk samples should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the
methods used
The stratification lines shown on graphic representations of the borings depict the
approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote a gradual transition
Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the graphic
representations and or in the text of this report These data have been reviewed and
interpretations made in the formulation of this report However it must be noted that
fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall tides temperature and other factors

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings to depths ranging from about 9 to 10 5 feet below the existing ground surface
using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers Two additional borings
were drilled to a depth of about 50 5 feet below the existing ground surface for percolation
testing The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

Laboratory Testing

APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
The permeability of the in situ soils were estimated by performing four constant head
percolation tests in Boring Nos 2 and 3 at depths of approximately 20 and 50 feet below the
existing ground surface The test results are presented on Plates C 1 and C 2
The constant head test was performed by pumping water into the borehole until the
water surface has stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time The
waiter height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which to
base the calculation of the subsoil permeability
