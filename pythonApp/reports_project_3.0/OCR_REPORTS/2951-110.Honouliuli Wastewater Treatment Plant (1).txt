REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
in age As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau banked against its
eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau known as the Schofield Plateau
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating sediments
which were transported to the coast These alluvial sediments accumulated and interbedded
with marine sediments and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging
in thickness from zero feet in the area of H 1 Freeway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This
wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock over the
artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of these sediments has continued from
earlier geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the growth
and retreat of the great continental glaciers As the glaciers advanced less water was
available to fill the ocean basins and sea levels fell below the current sea level As the glaciers
melted and retreated more water became available and sea levels rose to and often above
the current level When the stand of the sea was high and prolonged marine deposits
accumulated at elevations above the current sea level
The coral algal deposits are composed of medium hard limestone with an open
lattice like structure It is believed that these coral deposits were laid during the Kaena
95 feet Mean Sea Level MSL stand of the sea and subsequently eroded to near their
GEOLABS INC
W O 2951 110 Page 6
current elevation during the Waimanalo 25 feet MSL stand The coral formation has also
been subjected to subaerial weathering
Coral algal materials are chemically composed of calcium carbonate During subaerial
weathering slightly acidic rain and groundwater can slowly attack the carbonate dissolving
the weaker portions of the formation This creates solution cavities ranging from small pores
to large caverns

One hundred sixty eight 168 soil resistivity surveys were conducted at 36 locations
designated as R 1 through R 36 along the proposed utility alignment using the in situ Wenner
4 Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured by Nilsson Electrical
Laboratory Inc The method utilizes four surface probes or pins electrodes to evaluate the
average resisitivity an indication of corrosivity of the underlying soil mass The average
resistivity values of the soil mass extending from the existing ground surface to the test depth
was assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations shown on the
Site Plans Plates 1 through 3
Our work was performed in general conformance with ASTM Test Designation G 57 95
and the requirements provided in the Water System External Corrosion Control Standards
GEOLABS INC
W O 2951 110 Page 9
Volume 3 1991 The average soil resistance along the proposed utility alignment was
evaluated at each survey location with the electrodes aligned at equal spacing of generally 3 to
19 feet at the existing ground surface The depth of penetration of a resistivity sounding is a
function of the electrode spacing Therefore the spacing of the electrodes depends on the
proposed invert of the utility line
The resistance meter Nilsson Model 400 impresses a voltage between the outer
electrodes causing an electrical current to flow between them The voltage drop between the
inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive built in voltmeter As a result the soils
resistance in ohms can be found directly on the dial The soil resistivity in ohm cm which
is directly related to both resistance and spacing can then be computed The evaluated soil
resistances the computed resistivity values and the corresponding corrosion ratings are
presented on Plates C 1 through C 10 Field Resistivity Testing Summaries are presented on
Plates C 11 through C 14 It should be noted that the soil resistivity may be influenced by
temperature weather conditions existing underground facilities soil moisture and other
factors

Utilities
The methods and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the
contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavation should comply
with all applicable local state and federal safety requirements Trench shoring design and
installation should be the responsibility of the contractor Trench shoring and bracing should
conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the
pipes The initial backfill up to about 6 to 12 inches above the pipes should consist of
free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the
pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material be used
to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use of on site soils as backfill immediately
around utility pipes is not recommended
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the finish
subgrade should consist of the approved on site soils or import material The fill material
should consist of materials less than 3 inches in largest dimension The fill material should
also be free of vegetation and deleterious materials Imported fill materials should be tested
by Geolabs Inc for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project
GEOLABS INC
W O 2951 110 Page 11
site for its intended use In general the on site silty clay and coralline material should be
suitable for fill material The grayish brown clay located at the Honouliuli Stream should
not be utilized as fill material
The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future
ground subsidence The upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade
should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical compaction
equipment should be used to compact the materials at the project site Water tamping jetting
or ponding should not be used to compact the backfill material
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91
test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density

APPENDIX C
Field Resistivity Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Resistivity Testing
One hundred sixty eight 168 soil resistivity surveys were conducted at 36 locations
designated as R 1 through R 36 along the proposed utility alignment using the in situ Wenner
4 Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured by Nilsson Electrical
Laboratory Inc The method utilizes four surface probes or pins electrodes to evaluate the
average resisitivity an indication of corrosivity of the underlying soil mass The average
resistivity values of the soil mass extending from the existing ground surface to the test depth
was assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations shown on the
Site Plans Plates 1 through 3
Our work was performed in general conformance with ASTM Test Designation G 57 95
and the requirements provided in the Water System External Corrosion Control Standards
Volume 3 1991 The average soil resistance along the proposed utility alignment was
evaluated at each survey location with the electrodes aligned at equal spacing of generally 3 to
19 feet at the existing ground surface The depth of penetration of a resistivity sounding is a
function of the electrode spacing Therefore the spacing of the electrodes depends on the
proposed invert of the utility line
The resistance meter Nilsson Model 400 impresses a voltage between the outer
electrodes causing an electrical current to flow between them The voltage drop between the
inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive built in voltmeter As a result the soils
resistance in ohms can be found directly on the dial The soil resistivity in ohm cm which
is directly related to both resistance and spacing can then be computed The evaluated soil
resistances the computed resistivity values and the corresponding corrosion ratings are
presented on Plates C 1 through C 10 Field Resistivity Testing Summaries are presented on
Plates C 11 through C 14 It should be noted that the soil resistivity may be influenced by
temperature weather conditions existing underground facilities soil moisture and other
factors
