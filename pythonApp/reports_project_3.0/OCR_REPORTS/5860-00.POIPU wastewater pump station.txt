PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Koloa-Poipu region is experiencing a strong economic growth trend, which
requires new wastewater conveyance and treatment infrastructure. This project involves
the expansion of the wastewater collection system of the existing Poipu Wastewater
Reclamation Facility (WRF) in Poipu, on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The expansion
requires a new gravity collection system, Wastewater Pump Stations (WWPS) and force
mains. The expansion is designed to convey the wastewater flows to both the existing
Poipu WRF and the proposed Koloa-Poipu Regional WRF. Our task mainly focuses on the
design of the WWPS, which includes a control building for electrical and odor-control
equipment, generator, fuel tank and other appurtenances. The overall project location is
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
Based on the information provided, a wet pit submersible design is proposed for the
future pump station. We anticipate that site grading, footing excavation, utilities, and site
drainage will be required for the construction. The WWPS location is shown on the
attached Site Plan, Plate 2.

SITE DESCRIPTION
The proposed WWPS is generally at the southern end of the Poipu WRF. It is
bounded by a circular tank at the north, fence and shower trees at the south, and
two paved roads at both the east and west. Currently, the project site is relatively flat and
open. Based on the available plans, the existing ground surface elevations are about
+44 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).
REGIONAL GEQLOGY
The Island of Kauai was built up by a single large shield volcano, which has
subsequently been eroded deeply and covered partially with later volcanic materials. The
Island of Kauai is the oldest of the main Hawaiian Islands and today encompasses
approximately 555 square miles in area with a maximum elevation of about 5,170 feet
above Mean Sea Level. It is believed that during the Tertiary Period (Pliocene Age),
basaltic lavas from the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series built a roughly circular island
volcano from the depths of the ocean which is estimated to have been about 20,000 feet
deep at the time. Near the end of the Pliocene Age, this main shield building volcanic
phase came to a halt.
Following a long period of erosion and subsidence, volcanism was renewed during
the early Pleistocene Age with the Koloa Volcanic Series. This post-erosional volcanic
activity laid a veneer of fresh lava and interbedded sediments over the older
Waimea Canyon Series.
After the cessation of this period of renewed volcanism, the geologic forces of
weathering, erosion and deposition during the late Pleistocene and Recent Epochs have
shaped the island to its present form. The rocks in the vicinity of the project site have been
mapped as Koloa Basalts. In general, Koloa Basalts are poorly to moderately permeable.
The Koloa Basalt located in the vicinity of the project site is believed to have erupted from
nearby vents that appear today as circular shaped cinder and spatter cone vents. These
vents are responsible for much of the Koloa Basalt that covers the southeastern portion of
Kauai, including the project site.

Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and
textural examination in the field. He monitored the drilling operations on a near-continuous

System as shown on the Log Legend, Plate 3.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. Meanwhile,
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. In
addition, core samples of the rock formation encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113, Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and classification.
In general, our field exploration encountered stiff to very stiff fill material underlain
by basalt formation. The fill material contains clayey silt with some sand and gravel. The
thickness of the fill layer varies from 3 feet at Boring No. 1 to 7.5 feet at Boring No. 2. The
basalt formation below the fill layer is generally hard to very hard. Some layers of basaltic
clinker were encountered interbedded within the basalt formation. The clinker layers
ranges from medium dense to dense. The basalt formation extends to the maximum depth
explored. No basalt formation was encountered at Boring No.3 due to the relatively
shallow depth of the boring. We did not encounter groundwater at the time of our field
exploration. It should be noted that groundwater levels might vary due to tides, seasonal
precipitation and other factors.
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in our field exploration are

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the proposed WWPS site generally encountered stiff to very
stiff fill material with hard to very hard basalt formation at deeper depth. We did not
encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration.
We understand that a new WWPS is proposed for this project and that a wet pit
submersible WWPS is envisioned. We also understand that the new pump station will
include a control building, generator and fuel tank, and other appurtenances. Based on the
relatively competent subsoil conditions encountered at the site, it is our opinion that
shallow footing foundations may be used to support the proposed structures planned. An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for
design of footing foundations bearing on the properly compacted fills. Detailed discussions
and recommendations for the project design are presented in the following sections.
Site Grading
We anticipate that the majority of the site grading work will involve excavations for
the wet pit and footings. It is important that a representative from our office or a qualified
geotechnical engineer be present to monitor the grading operations to observe whether
undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation process, and whether the
exposed soil/rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our exploration.
Site Preparation
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site or stockpiled
in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated
materials.

Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over-excavated to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well-compacted engineered fill. The excavated soft soils may be
used as fill, provided that it meets the requirements for fill material.
After clearing and grubbing, the existing ground should be scarified to a depth of
10 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture,
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight)
corresponding to the maximum dry density.
In the event shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction, the soil should be
thoroughly moistened to close cracks or prepared again as recommended above.
The excavated soft soils may be used as fill, provided that it meets fill material
requirements.
Fills and Backfills
General fill material should consist of materials less than 3 inches in largest
dimension, with a CBR swell of less than 1 percent. The fill material should have a
low expansion potential and should be free of vegetation and deleterious materials.
We believe that most of the on-site clayey material generally may be suitable for fill
material within 1 foot of finish subgrade below slabs-on-grade.
Structural fill should consist of non-expansive granular material, such as crushed
coral, basalt or cinder sand. The material should be well-graded from coarse to fine
with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum
swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883. It
should conform to Sections 703.06 and 703.17 of the State of Hawaii, Standard
Specifications for Road, Bridge, and Public Works Construction, 1994 (HSS).
Geolabs, Inc. should test imported fill materials for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use.
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above
the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Compaction
should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, multiple-wheel
pneumatic-tired rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment.

Foundations
We understand the “design-build” concept of the proposed WWPS is to construct a
wet pit submersible WWPS. Based on the competent soil condition on site, we envision
shallow foundations will be used for the propose structure. Due to the soil changes at
shallow depth at the project site, we believe that different design parameters should be
used for above-ground structures and below-ground structures.
Above-Ground Structure
Generally, stiff to very stiff clayey silt was encountered near the surface at the
proposed project site during our field exploration. Based on the current design
concept and the generally competent subsoil condition encountered, we believe
that spread and/or continuous footings may be used for support of the various
above-ground improvements planned for the expansion project. An allowable
bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for design of footing
foundations bearing on the stiff in-situ soils or new compacted fills. This bearing
value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for
transient loads, such as those caused by seismic forces.
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades. Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements
should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from
the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement.
For footings bearing on new compacted fills, the bottom of the footing excavations
should also be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior
to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete. Relative compaction refers to the
in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. We estimate that
footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings bearing on the
recompacted in-situ soils or new compacted fills as recommended herein to be less
than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the order of one-half (72) inch.

Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed between

Below-Ground Structure
The pump station is envisioned to be a wet pit submersible WWPS in order to
mitigate the odor released to the neighborhood. Based on the information provided,
the bottom of the wet well will be at approximately 24 feet below the existing grade
(+20 feet Mean Sea Level). Generally, hard to very hard basalt formation with
interbedded clinker layers was encountered at this depth during our field
exploration. For the structures bearing on the in-situ hard basalt rock formation, we
believe that concrete mat footings should be used to provide the necessary bearing
support for the submersible structure.
We recommend placing the foundation mat on a minimum 4-inch thick cushion fill.
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be
used for the design of the concrete mat bearing on a minimum 4-inch thick cushion
fill. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by
one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by seismic forces.
As mentioned, a deep excavation is anticipated for the wet well. Due to the limited
space of the project site, we envision temporary shoring will be necessary in order
to stabilize the excavation. The contractor should be responsible for proper shoring
and should conform to the applicable health and safety requirements. We estimate
that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings bearing on
the compacted structural fills as recommended herein to be less than 1 inch total
with differential settlements on the order of one-half (72) inch.
A Geolabs representative should monitor the footing excavations prior to placement
of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions and the
required embedment depths.

Slabs-On-Grade
Based on our field exploration, stiff to very stiff clayey soils with low expansion
potential underlies the proposed above-ground structures. However, some soil
expansion/shrinkage might occur due to moisture fluctuations. To reduce the potential for
future distress to the lightly loaded slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of
the clayey soils, we recommend properly preparing and capping the subgrade soils below
the building structure with at least 12 inches of structural fill material (capping fill) below the
slab cushion. The capping fill should also extend beyond the new slab-on-grade footprint a
minimum of 12 inches.
We strongly recommend placing the non-expansive select granular capping fill as
soon as possible after the subgrade soils are prepared, in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the “Site Preparation” section herein. The completed
capping fill and the underlying subgrade soils should be maintained above optimum
moisture content prior to placement of concrete. We believe it is very critical to maintain
the moisture content in order to minimize future shrink/swell movements. Where shrinkage
cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade, we recommend thoroughly moistening
the soil to close the cracks. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade
due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas
and replacement with well-compacted engineered fill.
In general, we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill
consisting of No. 3 Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) between the concrete slab
and capping fill to serve as a capillary break and to provide more uniform support of the
slab. To reduce the potential for moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor
coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the cushion fill.
To reduce the potential for water infiltration into the slabs and foundations, we
recommend embedding the thickened edges of slab adjacent to unpaved areas at least
12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade. Areas adjacent to the slab should be
backfilled with on-site clayey soil tightly against the slab and graded to divert water away
from the slabs.
Excavation
Our field exploration disclosed that the proposed pump station site is underlain at
shallow depths by basalt formation ranging from hard to very hard. It is anticipated that
some of the basalt formation may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment,

However, based on the location and scope of the proposed project, we believe that a
hoeram might be most appropriate in order to excavate the project site to the design
grade. Based on limited space of the proposed project, we envision that temporary shoring
will be required for the project construction.

Utilities
We envision that new utility lines and connections may be required. In general,
good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the
trenches for the new utilities at the site. The contractor should determine the method and
equipment to be used for trench excavation, subject to practical limits and safety
considerations. In addition, the excavations should comply with the applicable federal,
state, and local safety requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench
shoring design and installation.
Based on the results of our field exploration, it appears that conventional
excavation methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation. However, some of
the utility trench excavations will likely encounter the hard to very hard basalt formation as
described on the Logs of Borings, which may pose some excavation difficulties during the
trench excavation as describe at the “Excavation” section above.
In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) is recommended under the pipes for support.
Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel, should also be used for the
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support
around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill, thus reducing the
possibility of damaging the pipes. We believe it is critical to use a free-draining granular
material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. The use of on-site soils as backfill
directly around utility pipes is not recommended and could result in pipe damage.
In general, the excavated on-site soil or select granular material may be used from
a level 1 foot above the pipes to the finish subgrade. The backfill material should be
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, placed in
level lifts about 8 inches in loose thickness, and mechanically compacted to no less than
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence.
Where trenches are below pavement areas, the upper 2 feet of the trench backfill should
be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. Mechanical compaction
equipment should be used to compact the materials at the project site. Water tamping,
jetting, or ponding should not be used to compact the backfill material.

Site Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding. It is highly recommended to
install gutter systems around the structures and to divert discharge away from the
foundation and pavement areas. Blockage or clogging of drainage system could cause
ponding of water at the structure and foundations, resulting in foundation and structure
distress.
Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be
avoided. Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils. Gravel strips adjacent to slabs and
foundations should be avoided.
Because ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent loss
of strength of the near-surface soils, we believe these drainage requirements are very
important for the proper performance of proposed structures. Immediate backfill of the
foundation excavations after setting of concrete is strongly recommended to reduce water
infiltration. Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain surface run-off away from foundations.
