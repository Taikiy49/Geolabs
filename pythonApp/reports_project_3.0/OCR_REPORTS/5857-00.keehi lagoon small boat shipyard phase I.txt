REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae volcano forms the bulk of the western
third of the island while the younger Koolau shield forms the majority of the eastern
two-thirds of the island. It is believed that Waianae Volcano became extinct while Koolau
Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in
Central Oahu.
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by
thinly-bedded basaltic lava flows. The main  shield-building activities ceased
approximately 2.5 million years ago. Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the Island
of Oahu has subsided by as much as 1,200 feet since the cessation of this early
volcanic activity (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970).
During that period of subsidence, coral-algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of the Island of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth. The
growth of the reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence. A series of
lagoons formed behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments
accumulated in the lagoons (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970).
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great
continental glaciers advanced, the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global
sea levels rose because more water was available. The land mass of Oahu remained
essentially stable during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature.
These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher
and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu.
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials. In addition, renewed subaerial
erosion of the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils
under relatively high-energy conditions.

During periods of no significant sea level changes, continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments. During these stable periods, the growth of fringing coral/algal reefs formed
distinct ledges of reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea
during that period. About 15,000 years ago, a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred.
During that rise, the valleys in the project area were submerged. In the last
10,000 years or so, the sea level has adjusted to its present stand. Terrigenous and
marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low-energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft/loose sediments along the coast in areas
that were formerly valleys and drainageways.
The area north of the project site is geologically situated on the southwestern flank
of the Koolau Volcanics. These volcanics are composed of basaltic lava flows of early
Pleistocene Age. The basalt is overlain by a complex inter-layered system of terrestrial
and marine sedimentary deposits. During the later Pleistocene Age, the sea level rose and
fell several times as a result of glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulting in local swamp
conditions, which were later superimposed by more recent deposits of terrestrial detritus of
sand, gravel, silt and clay eroded from the older rock of the area. In recent time, the efforts
of man have shaped the low-lying land to its present form by the placement of fill in the
low-lying area.
The Honolulu Coastal Plain is composed of interbedded alluvial, marine, and
post-erosional volcanic deposits. There is evidence that a basaltic flow of the Honolulu
Volcanic Series, possibly originating from the Makuku vent in Nuuanu or from
Punchbowl, reached the eastern portion of what is now Sand Island.
During the Waipio stand of the sea at Elevation —-60 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL),
this layer of basalt would have provided a resistant surface, which could have diverted
the flows of Nuuanu and Kapalama Streams to the west. The diversion of these
two streams would have resulted in deposition of extensive subaerial sediments
consisting of alluvial clays and silts. These exposed alluvial deposits would have
become consolidated due to seasonal moisture changes.
Subsequently, as the sea level rose to the Waimanalo stand of the sea (at
Elevation +25 feet MSL) and then fell to its present level, the stream-laid alluvium was
buried beneath backreef deposits of sands as a fringing coral reef grew and emerged
on the seaward margin of the island. Land reclamation projects in the past century have
built Sand Island and the surrounding areas to its current topography.
In this century, the project vicinity used to be formerly occupied by the Mokauea
Fishery. There were five large fishponds that spanned the project site as well as
adjacent areas. These fishponds ran along the eastern shores of what used to be called

Liquefaction Potential
Liquefaction occurs when a build-up of excess pore water pressure is induced in
saturated cohesionless soils resulting in a substantial loss of strength. The most
common cause of this phenomenon is the cyclic loading due to earthquakes. This
process may permit the soil to move both vertically and horizontally, and if not confined,
can result in significant deformations. Loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained sands and
loose silts with little cohesion are most susceptible to liquefaction. Generally,
liquefaction is less likely to occur if the loose deposit is deeper than 50 feet below the
ground surface or the deposit contains an appreciable percentage of clay materials.
We believe that the loss of soil strength and associated ground movements could
potentially have a severe impact on the proposed development. The subsurface
information obtained from the drilled borings in the vicinity suggest that the project site
could be underlain by loose silty sand lagoonal deposits at depths ranging from about
10 to over 50 feet below the existing ground surface. Based on our engineering
analysis, the loose lagoonal deposits, if present at the project site, could have the
potential to liquefy and lose significant strength under seismic conditions, assuming a
peak ground acceleration of 0.15g (Zone 2A based on the Uniform Building Code,
1997). Therefore, it is our opinion that the project site could be subjected to appreciable

Ground Settlement & Settlement Monitoring
We understand that the near shore area of the project site will be filled for the
construction of the small boat shipyard. As mentioned in the “Anticipated Subsurface
Conditions” section of this report, the project site could be underlain by a thick lagoonal
deposit of very loose and soft soils. The placement of additional fill at the project site would
induce filled ground settlements due to consolidation of the underlying very loose and soft
soils resulting from the new fill loads.
To allow the underlying soils to reach equilibrium under the new fill loads and to
reduce the potential for adverse effects of post-construction settlements, the actual
settlement rate resulting from the new fill placement should be monitored. Therefore, we
recommend that a settlement monitoring program be implemented to evaluate the
magnitude and rates of settlements. For a deposit of highly compressible soils, such as the
lagoonal deposits anticipated at the site, the rates of the estimated settlements could vary
by as much as 50 to 100 percent from the actual settlement period required. Therefore,
provisions should be made for potential delays in the project construction schedule if a
longer period is required.
We recommend that a settlement waiting period be observed in the construction
schedule after placement of the new ffills. Construction of the pavements and
improvements above and in the fills should not commence until the survey readings from
the settlement waiting period indicate that the fills have achieved equilibrium or are near
equilibrium.
To monitor the actual settlement rate, we recommend installing a series of
settlement gauges in the fill areas at the site. Geolabs should establish the settlement
gauge locations during the design phase. In general, a professional surveyor should read
the gauges optically, and the readings should be transmitted to Geolabs for review in a
timely manner. The settlement gauges should be established on the existing ground
surface prior to any filling operations. Readings should be taken on a weekly to bi-weekly
basis until the settlement rate has stabilized.

Building Foundation
As discussed in the “Anticipated Subsurface Conditions” section of this report,
the surface soils generally consist of man-made fills from land reclamation projects in the
past century. Based on the anticipated near-surface soil conditions, we believe that
lightly-loaded structures may be supported on a shallow foundation system consisting of
inter-connected continuous grid footings bearing on the surface fill layer. Lightly-loaded
structures are defined herein as structures with column loads of not more than 70 kips
per column. In general, an allowable bearing pressure on the order of about 2,000 to
3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design of shallow foundations
bearing on the recompacted surface fill soils. The subgrade soils at the bottom of
footing excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction.
In general, we recommend embedding the footings a minimum of 18 inches
below the lowest adjacent finished grades. However, it should be noted that the depths
of the footings should be kept as shallow as possible within the surface fill layer.
Therefore, the embedment depths of footings should be limited to about 18 to 24 inches
below the existing ground surface.
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations, we estimate that total settlements of footings supported on the
recompacted surface fills may be on the order of about 1 to 2 inches. We estimate that
the differential settlements between adjacent foundations supported on the
recompacted fills to be on the order of about 0.5 to 1 inch.
Due to the relatively shallow groundwater conditions anticipated at the site,
foundations may be located below the groundwater table. If foundations are located
below the groundwater table, then provisions for dewatering the footing excavations
should be considered. The dewatering method selected by the contractor should have
minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the proposed excavation and
maintain stability of, and provide safe and dry working conditions within the excavation.
As previously mentioned, the project site could be subjected to appreciable
ground settlement in the event of liquefaction during a moderate earthquake (M6+).
Therefore, the Client should be aware that the shallow foundation support system
recommended may experience appreciable settlement, and the building may sustain
some damage that would disrupt the operation of the facility. If the estimated foundation
settlement during a moderate seismic event is not acceptable, consideration should be
given to the use of a deep foundation system for support of the building structures.

Driven Pile Foundation
For heavier building structures with column loads greater than 70 kips or to
reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement resulting from liquefaction
during a seismic event, we believe that driven pile foundations should be considered for
support of the planned structures. In addition, we envision that driven pile foundations
will be required for the support of the finger pier planned at the dry dock area.
In general, the driven piles would develop their load carrying capacity from side
resistance between the pile shaft and the surrounding soils and/or end-bearing on the
underlying hard coral formation, if present at the project site. An allowable pile design
capacity of 50 tons may be used for preliminary design purposes based on a 12-inch
square prestressed concrete pile. The allowable compressive load capacity of the piles
is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads,
such as wind or seismic forces. In general, piles should be spaced a minimum of 3 feet
center-to-center to avoid reduction in vertical capacity due to group action. Depending
on the actual subsurface conditions encountered, pile splicing of the precast piles may
be required for the project. Due to possible variation in the length of the piles, unit prices
should be obtained during bidding for add-ons, etc. Detailed recommendations for the
driven pile foundation would be provided when additional structural loads becomes
available and upon completion of the field exploration program.
It should be noted that obstructions, such as debris and boulders, are commonly
encountered in man-made fills. In addition, a shallow limestone shelf could be present
at the project site based on the diving survey and water jet probing conducted by
Sea Engineering, Inc. To reduce the potential for damaging adjacent structures and to
facilitate pile installation, we recommend predrilling the proposed pile locations prior to the
pile driving operation. The predrilling depth should be determined after completion of the
field exploration program and modified at the conclusion of the test pile program, if
implemented for the project.
Sheet Pile Bulkhead Installation
Based on the information provided, we understand that the near shore area will be
raised to create a relatively level area for the small boat yard. Therefore, we envision that
sheet pile bulkheads with tie-rods and deadman anchors will be required for the project
construction. The sheet pile bulkhead along the perimeter of the shipyard will generally
serve two purposes: (1) to provide lateral support for the existing soils and new fills; and
(2) to maintain stability of the soil mass resulting from the dredging operation in front of the
bulkhead. Design of the sheet pile bulkhead will need to consider both overturning stability
and overall slope stability. The design engineer will need to evaluate the overturning
stability. A minimum factor of safety of 1.5 should be obtained for the overall slope stability
of the sheet pile bulkhead.

For waterfront structures, such as sheet pile bulkheads, the maximum lateral
pressures may be expected when the tide is at its lowest level. A receding tide may cause
higher water levels behind the sheet pile than in front of it, depending on the type of soils
behind the sheet piles. The high water level behind the sheet pile bulkhead will introduce
additional seepage pressures due to hydrostatic load. For the type of soils anticipated at
the project site, an estimated hydrostatic pressure based on the maximum tide difference
may be considered for the sheet pile design.
Tie-rods and deadman anchors will be required to provide the necessary lateral
restraint for the sheet pile bulkhead, especially if soft lagoonal deposits are encountered at
the project site. To avoid the need for deep excavations and significant dewatering, we
generally recommend placing the tie-rods and anchors above the water level or at about
Elevation 0 feet MSL. In order for the anchorage system to develop the full passive
pressure resistance, the anchors must be located a sufficient distance behind the
bulkhead wall such that the resisting anchor passive wedge does not intersect the active
soil wedge behind the bulkhead. The passive earth pressure will be fully engaged when
the deflection is at about 2 percent of the height, measuring from the finished grade to the
bottom of the deadman anchor. In addition, a minimum cover of 3 feet should be provided
between the finished grade and the top of the deadrnan anchor.
We recommend properly designing all substructures including steel sheet piles,
tie-rods, and other metallic substructures in contact with the soils to protect against
potential corrosion. A corrosion engineer should be consulted for detailed
recommendations on corrosion protection. As a minimum, the exposed portions of the
steel sheet piles should be surfaced with an epoxy coating to reduce the potential for
corrosion of the exposed portions of the steel sheet piles.
Boulders may be present along the sheet pile bulkhead alignments, especially near
the shoreline area. The potential for the presence of boulders may pose some difficulties
to the contractor during sheet pile installation. Therefore, the contractor should be made
aware of this site condition. To facilitate installation of the sheet pile, it may be necessary
to perform a boulder sweep and/or predrilling along the sheet pile bulkhead alignment.
After removal of the boulders, the sheet pile may then be installed to the desired depth
without significant resistance due to obstructions. Based on the diving survey and water jet
probing conducted by Sea Engineering, Inc., we understand that a shallow limestone shelf
may be present below the harbor sediments. Therefore, predrilling through the shallow
limestone shelf may be required in order to provide sufficient embedment for the sheet pile
bulkhead.
Some dewatering of the excavations will likely be necessary for installation of the

Dredging
We envision that the existing harbor floor in front of the sheet pile bulkhead will be
dredged to accommodate the dry dock construction. In addition, we recommend that the
area behind the new sheet pile bulkhead, which will be backfilled, be dredged of the soft
harbor sediments to expose the underlying limestone shelf, if present, prior to the
backfilling operations. We anticipate that the proposed dredging operation may involve
excavation into the surface harbor sediments, soft/loose lagoonal deposits, and/or shallow
limestone shelf. Depending on the actual subsurface conditions encountered, we believe
that a dredged slope inclination of up to three horizontal to one vertical (3H:1V) or flatter
with riprap protection may be used for the design of the dredged slope in front of the sheet
pile bulkhead, if appropriate.
The dredged materials should not be stockpiled closer than 15 feet away from the
planned bulkhead location to reduce the potential for additional lateral loads acting on the
bulkhead structure. In general, the excavated on-site granular soils may be re-used as a
source of structural fill materials above +2 feet MSL provided that they meet the
requirements stated below. The wet and soft soils generated from excavations into the soft
harbor sediments from the dredging operations should not be used as fills or backfills and
should be disposed properly off-site. The contractor should exercise care in separating the
unusable upper soft harbor sediments from the underlying usable coralline materials
during the dredging operations.
Because of the relatively shallow water table and proximity to the shoreline, drying
or aeration of the excavated materials may be required prior to their use as compacted
fills. Therefore, a dewatering area should be designated for the stockpiling of the usable
excavated granular materials. Due to the porous nature of the granular deposits, we
believe that the time required for the aeration of the excavated materials should be
relatively short, on the order of less than about one week, depending on the weather and
the thickness of the stockpile. Some aeration of the stockpile material will be necessary in
order to re-use the excavated materials as compacted fills.
Excavated on-site or imported materials to be used as structural fill and backfill
should consist of crushed coral and basalt. The material should be well graded from
coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. In addition, the fill
material should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve.
The material should have a CBR value of 20 or more and a swell of 1 percent or less when
tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883.

Based on the current design concept, we anticipate that excavations will be needed
for the installation of utility lines and deadman anchors at the project site. Based on the
anticipated subsurface conditions, excavations for the project will likely encounter
groundwater and, possibly, soft soil conditions.
In general, the contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used
for excavation subject to practical limits and safety considerations. The surface sediments
and soft/loose lagoonal deposits may be excavated with normal excavators and/or
backhoes. However, hard ripping or special excavating methods may be required to
remove boulders and limestone shelf that are present in the subsurface. Therefore, the
contractor should make allowances for the additional time and expense that may be
required by the hard excavation.
Excavation for installation of utility lines may need to be adequately shored to
reduce the potential for adjacent ground movement. The excavated materials should not
be stockpiled adjacent to the planned excavation within a horizontal distance equal to the
depth of the excavation to reduce the potential for ground movement. The contractor is
responsible for the excavation support and shoring system used and must comply with all
applicable safety requirements.
One possible method of shoring would be to use interlocking sheet pile shoring.
Use of a sheet pile shoring system may also serve as a cut-off wall to aid in the dewatering
operations, which is further discussed in the "Dewatering" section of this report. It is
recommended that the sheet piles used for shoring the excavation be left in-place to aid in
dewatering the excavation. Based on our field observations, boulders may be present in
the surface soils and also the new fill areas. Therefore, difficulties may arise during
installation of the interlocking sheet pile walls when the boulders are encountered.
The contractor should be made solely responsible for the adequacy and safety of
the shoring installation. Therefore, we recommend that the contractor retain a qualified
geotechnical engineer to design and evaluate the shoring system used. His/her
representative, who should be required to be continuously present on site during
excavation and construction work, will have the best opportunity to promptly observe
changing conditions during construction, such as unforeseen subsoils, unexpectedly high
groundwater table, inappropriate construction sequence or techniques, etc., which may
affect shoring stability. However, it must be noted that some minor movements of the
shoring system and the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses
during excavation. Due to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately
estimate the magnitude of movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon
workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are
installed, the subsoil conditions, the size of the excavation, and the rate of excavation.

It is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed properly and

Dewatering
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater level
is above the bottom of the proposed excavation, such as for the utility line installation.
Therefore, we recommend including dewatering provisions in the contract documents for
construction. Since the excavation will involve discharge of groundwater, a National
Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permit may be necessary. The
contractor should consult their independent consultant for the latest regulations and
information pertaining to site-specific dewatering during construction.
The contractor is responsible for dewatering for construction. The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor, and he/she
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on the conditions encountered. The dewatering method selected
should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the proposed
excavation. It should be noted that the granular backfills recommended below the water
level and the underlying coralline deposits anticipated at this site are highly permeable and
capable of transmitting large quantities of water.
It is our opinion that the definition of "Dewatering" in the contract documents should
be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater table
and/or to exclude the water from the excavations to allow construction of the proposed
structures under safe and dry conditions. These works or systems may include, but are not
limited to, grouting, cut-off walls, tremie concrete plugs or combination of the above and/or
other possible methods. Based on previous experience, an interlocking sheet pile shoring
system may work successfully in reducing the amount of seepage water. Therefore, a
sheet pile shoring system should be considered for the construction cost estimate of this
project.
The contactor must carefully evaluate the potential impact of the dewatering system
selected on depressing the natural groundwater table prior to dewatering. It is
recommended the contractor retain a qualified geotechnical engineer to design and
evaluate the dewatering system used.
