Our exploratory borings drilled at the bridge site crossing Kapaa Stream generally
encountered a surface fill layer to depths of 14 to 15 5 feet The surface fill layer consisted
of soft to hard silty clay and clayey silt with gravel and cobbles The fill layer was underlain
by loose to dense and soft to medium stiff beach and lagoonal deposits to depths ranging
from about 16 5 to 107 feet below the existing ground surface Below the beach and
lagoonal deposits a medium stiff to stiff alluvial deposit followed by a hard basalt rock
formation was encountered to the maximum depth explored of 148 feet below the existing
ground surface In the boring drilled at the southern abutment basalt rock formation was
encountered at about 16 5 feet in depth A hard coral formation about 4 feet thick was
encountered within the lagoonal deposit at about 48 feet in depth in the boring drilled at
the northern abutment Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at depths
from about 12 4 to 15 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field
exploration
Based on the soft and loose fill and lagoonal deposits encountered at relatively
shallow depths and the moderate to high structural loads anticipated for the northern
abutment structure we believe that appreciable settlements will occur if a shallow
foundation system is used to support the proposed northern bridge abutment structure
Therefore we recommend that the northern abutment structure be supported on a deep
foundation system consisting of 16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles
and 36 inch diameter drilled shafts The concrete piles will be utilized in the front row of
the abutment footing and the drilled shafts in the back row The driven piles will extend
through the highly compressible lagoonal deposits and bear on the underlying soft to very
hard basalt rock formation The driven piles will derive support primarily from end bearing
resistance A preliminary pile length of 105 feet may be used for the northern abutment
structure To facilitate pile driving through the underlying coral formation we recommend
that the pile locations be predrilled to about elevation 38 feet MSL prior to pile driving If
predrilling is not feasible the use of steel H pile tips embedded in the concrete piles may
be considered to assist in driving through the coral layer Because of the large lateral
loading 36 inch diameter drilled shafts are recommended for the back row foundation
elements The drilled shafts will derive support primarily from side resistance A
preliminary shaft length of 40 feet may be used

1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Kapaa Stream Bridge for the Kapaa Kealia Bike
Pedestrian Path project in Kapaa on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and
engineering analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of bridge
foundations retaining structures and site grading only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene
Epoch more than 2 4 million years ago Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas
of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation
The majority of the Island of Kauai is covered by lavas of the Waimea Canyon
Volcanic Series These lavas consist of four distinct formations Napali Olokele
Haupu and Makaweli These formations are comprised of thin bedded a a and
pahoehoe flows to massive basalt flows that ponded in calderas and graben
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of
Kauai These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded
from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various locales Associated with
the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at the vent
During the Pliestocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted
global sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Kauai
remained essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in
nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both
higher and lower relative to present sea level of Kauai
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenious sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs

The basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lavas of the Koloa Volcanic Series are
generally characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin
clinker layers The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils which
grade to saprolite with depth In general saprolite is composed of mainly silty material
and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to
basaltic rock formation with depth
2 2 Existing Site Conditions

Kapaa Kealia Bike and Pedestrian Path at the Kapaa Stream crossing in Kapaa on the

covered with an asphalt concrete pavement The existing bridge abutment structures
are of concrete construction and the middle pier structure appears to be of concrete and

Abandoned abutment structures are located adjacent to the existing bridge

Based on available plans the existing ground surface elevations range from

Predrilling
Based on our field exploration a coral formation about 4 feet thick was encountered
within the lagoonal deposits at the Northern Abutment Therefore we believe that
predrilling will likely be necessary to facilitate the pile driving operations through the
coral formation layer A predrilling depth to about elevation 38 feet MSL is
anticipated for this project
The required predrilling depth should be verified and or modified by Geolabs during
the test pile program depending on the subsurface conditions encountered and pile
driving equipment used We wish to emphasize that the diameter of the predrilled
holes should be limited to approximately 22 inches or less to provide the driven
piles with sufficient soil contact for lateral load resistance If an over sized predrilled
hole is encountered during construction the annular space between the piles and
predrilled holes should be backfilled with pea gravel or grouted with tremie concrete
to provide the recommended lateral load capacity
If predrilling is not feasible steel H pile tips cast into the concrete piles may be used
to assist in driving through the coral layer
Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering energies in the
range of about 25 000 to 50 000 foot pounds in the field The hammer should be
equipped with an energy level control such that driving within the loose soft soils
may be controlled at a lower energy to reduce tensile stress development which
could damage or cause cracking of the piles
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy
to the top of the pile and also reducing damage to the piles Geolabs should review
the pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate if the equipment is capable of
installing the piles to the recommended capacity The contractor should forward
pile and driving equipment data forms to Geolabs prior to construction for

Construction Considerations
The performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures As a result of these potential
variations we recommend that a representative from Geolabs be present to
observe the installation of drilled shafts during construction In our opinion the
following may have a significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled
shaft foundations
Based on our field exploration the northern abutment location for the proposed
Kapaa Stream Bridge is underlain by layers of sand and silty sands and gravel
extending to a depth of approximately 99 feet below the existing ground surface
Due to the granular nature of these materials there is a potential for caving in of the
materials during the drilling operations To reduce the potential for caving in of the
drilled holes temporary casing of the drilled holes may be required during drilled
shaft installation Care should be exercised during removal of the temporary casing
to reduce the potential for necking of the drilled shaft
Some dense cobbles and gravels were encountered in the borings drilled at the
proposed bridge foundation locations during our field exploration Therefore the
drilled shaft contractor will need to have the appropriate equipment and tools to drill
through these obstructions where encountered
Workmanship
The load carrying capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the

3 3 Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area voids and or cavities may exist in the basalt
rock formation at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support
resulting from the collapse of cavities below a footing we recommend that a program of
cavity probing and grouting at the foundation location southern abutment be
implemented during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that a probe hole be drilled for every
100 square feet of foundation area or at 10 feet on center along the abutment and pier
foundations The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should
extend to a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of footing elevation If
cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation
additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the
vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities
discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate
pressures We recommend that a low strength sand cement grout mixture with a slump
range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the grouting operations As an alternative to
sand cement grout Controlled Low Strength Material CLSM may also be used for the
grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site
we recommend that the probe drill be made available on site until the probing and
grouting operations are complete In addition it should be noted that a longer lag time
between the probing grouting operations and the foundation construction might be
required in the construction schedule

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the proposed replacement bridge site were
explored by drilling and sampling 2 borings designated as Boring Nos K 1 and K 2
extending to depths of about 55 to 148 feet below the existing ground surface The
borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight
augers and coring tools The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 3
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer or geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B 1
Three Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on
Plates B 2 through B 4
