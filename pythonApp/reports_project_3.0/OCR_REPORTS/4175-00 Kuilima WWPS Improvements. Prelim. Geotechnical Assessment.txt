Subsurface Conditions
A new sewage pump station was planned adjacent to the existing facility The
excavation was made but the project was not completed due to difficulties with the
dewatering operations The boring data and site observations of the excavation and
dewatering operations at the adjacent site indicate that the area is underlain by
relatively hard coral formation The coral formation is very porous with many moderate
size voids and cavities The coral formation probably extends well below the depths of
the structures
Discussion and Recommendations
All of the alternatives being considered will require dewatering of the excavation
in order to construct the improvements The coral formation under the site is very
porous and will require some form of cutoff wall to reduce the permeability of the
formation to limit the amount of pumping necessary
The most common form of cut off wall used is a steel sheet pile wall The steel
sheets are driven into the ground by a vibratory hammer and each section is interlocked
with the adjacent section to form a continuous wall The steel sheets must be driven to
a sufficient depth below the bottom of the excavation to reduce water flowing up
through the base of the excavation If the sheet piles are driven to a sufficient depth
and the interlocks between sections are tight water flow is greatly reduced Sheet piles
are ideal in sandy soils but are limited in hard formations such as coral since they
cannot be driven to a sufficient depth There is a possibility that the sheet piles could
be driven within the limits of the previous excavation made during construction of the
existing structures However at this time the limits and depths of the previous
excavation are unknown and believed to be insufficient Also the sheet piles could not
be installed where the existing pipes connecting the structures are present
Another method to reduce the permeability of the coral would be pressure
grouting Pressure grouting is performed by drilling a series of holes around the
excavation and then pumping cement grout into the holes under pressure to fill the void
spaces in the coral formation The holes are generally 2 to 4 inches in diameter and
should extend to about two times the depth of the excavation The first series of holes
are drilled at a spacing of about 3 feet The second series of holes are drilled in
between the first series Depending on the grout penetration achieved a third or fourth
series of holes may be required The holes may be drilled at a small angle to reach the
areas below the existing pipes and the bottom of the excavation
