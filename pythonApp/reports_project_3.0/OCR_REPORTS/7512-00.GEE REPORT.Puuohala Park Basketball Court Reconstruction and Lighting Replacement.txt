In accordance with the scope of services outlined in our fee proposal dated 
November 28, 2016, we performed a geotechnical engineering exploration for the 
Basketball Court Reconstruction and Lighting Replacement project at Puuohala Park in 
the Wailuku area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii.  This report summarizes the findings 
from our field exploration and laboratory testing, and presents our findings and 
recommendations for the project design. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are intended for design input only and are subject to the limitations noted at the 
end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is within the existing Puuohala Park in the Wailuku area on the 
Island of Maui, Hawaii.  Based on the information provided, it is proposed to demolish the 
existing basketball court and construct a new play court with a sport court modular athletic 
surfacing at the same location.  In addition, the existing basketball court lighting system will 
be demolished and new sports lighting with electrical systems will be installed as part of 
the project. 
Our experience in the area suggests that the site may be underlain by surface fills 
overlying alluvial deposits at shallow depths.  Special attention was given to the swelling 
potential and lateral load support characteristics of the subsoils at the project site 
pertaining to the design of the new play court and lighting system.

PURPOSE AND SCOPE 
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration was to obtain an overview 
of the surface and subsurface conditions at the site to formulate geotechnical engineering 
recommendations for the design of the play court pavement and lighting system 
foundation. To accomplish this, we conducted an exploration program consisting of the 
following tasks and work efforts: 
1. 
Research and review of available in-house soils and geologic information 
pertaining to the project site and its vicinity. 
2. 
Coordination of the stakeout, utility toning, and clearance of the boring 
locations by our geologist. 
3. 
Mobilization and demobilization of a truck-mounted drill rig and operators to 
and from the project site. 
4. 
Drilling and sampling of three borings at selected locations extending to a 
depth of about 16.5 feet below the existing pavement surface for a total of 
approximately 49.5 lineal feet of field exploration.  
5. 
Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by our 
geologist. 
6. 
Laboratory testing of selected soil samples obtained during the field 
exploration as an aid in classifying the materials and evaluating their 
engineering properties. 
7. 
Engineering analyses of the field and laboratory data to develop 
geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of the 
play court pavement and lighting system foundation. 
8. 
Preparation of this report summarizing our work on the project and 
presenting our findings and recommendations. 
9.

10. 
Quality assurance of our work and client/design team consultation by our

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling three borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to a depth of about 16.5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface.  The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
Our field exploration disclosed that the existing basketball court is generally 
underlain by 4 inches of Asphalt Concrete (AC) over base course materials consisting of 
medium dense silty gravel. Below the pavement section, our borings generally 
encountered fill materials and alluvial deposits consisting of stiff to very stiff silty clays 
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 16.5 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration.  
However, it should be noted that the water level at the site may be influenced by seasonal 
precipitation, surface run-off, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration methodology and the Logs of Borings 
are presented in Appendix A. Results of the laboratory tests performed on selected soil 
samples obtained from our field exploration are presented in Appendix B. 
FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration results, the project site is generally underlain by stiff 
to very stiff clayey soils extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 
16.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  Laboratory test results indicated that the 
clayey soils exhibit a slight to moderate expansion potential when subjected to moisture 
fluctuations. Detailed discussions and recommendations for pavement and foundation 
design are presented in the following sections herein. 
Light Pole Foundations 
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances, the proposed 
new light pole structures may be supported by a foundation system consisting of

cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts.  The cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts would derive 
vertical support principally from skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils. 
We understand that the project structural engineer will be designing the drilled shaft 
foundations based on the lateral bearing pressure recommended for the in-situ soil 
conditions.  As mentioned above, the project site is generally underlain by stiff to very stiff 
clayey soils extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 16.5 feet below 
the existing pavement surface.  Based on Table 1804.2 of the International Building Code, 
2006 Edition (IBC 2006), the in-situ soils encountered at the project site may be 
considered as Class of Materials No. 5, which includes clay, sandy clay, silty clay, clayey 
silt, silt and sandy silt (CL, ML, MH and CH).  Based on this material classification, a lateral 
bearing pressure of 100 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf), up to a maximum 
value of 1,500 psf, may be used for the design of the drilled shaft foundations. Unless 
covered by pavements or slabs, the lateral bearing resistance in the upper 12 inches 
below the finished grade should be neglected. 
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundation will result from elastic compression of the 
shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the soils encountered at the 
site. We anticipate that the total settlements of the drilled shafts are estimated to be less 
than 0.5 inches. We believe that a significant portion of the settlement will be elastic and 
should occur as the loads are applied. 
Construction Considerations 
Drilled shafts are desirable for the light pole foundations because of the significant 
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations.  
However, the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the 
contractor's method of construction and construction procedures.  In our opinion, the 
following conditions would have an impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft 
foundations. 
Dense Boulders 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by surface fills 
and alluvial deposits consisting of stiff to very stiff clayey soils.  Although boulders 
were not encountered within the drilled depth of our borings, it should be noted that 
boulders could be present in the alluvial deposits underlying the project site.  
Therefore, coring into potentially dense boulders should be anticipated during the 
drilled shaft construction.  Appropriate drilling equipment (and coring tools) should 
be utilized by the drilled shaft contractor to install the drilled shafts to the depths and 
dimensions recommended by the project structural engineer.

The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend, to a large extent, on the 
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils.  Therefore, proper

construction techniques are important.  The contractor should exercise care in 
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes. 
A low-shrink concrete mix with high slump (6 to 9-inch range) should be used to

concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for

Pavement Design 
Based on the information provided, it is proposed to demolish the existing 
basketball court and construct a new play court with a sport court modular athletic 
surfacing at the same location.  Laboratory test results indicated that the near-surface 
clayey soils exhibit a slight to moderate expansion potential when subjected to moisture 
fluctuations.  Based on our evaluation, we recommend using the following rigid pavement 
section for preliminary design purposes.  The manufacturer of the sport court modular 
athletic surfacing product should be consulted for additional requirements. 
Rigid Pavements 
  Sport Court Modular Athletic Surfacing 
  4.0-Inch Portland Cement Concrete 
12.0-Inch Aggregate Subbase 
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, and compacted to 
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The aggregate subbase materials should 
consist of crushed basaltic aggregates compacted to no less than 90 percent relative 
compaction. The California Bearing Ratio (CBR) and field density tests should be 
performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the 
adequacy of the above section. 
In addition, paved areas should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off-site. Surface water ponding should not be allowed 
on-site during or after construction. Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping 
in or adjacent to the pavement areas, we recommend extending the curbs a minimum of 
2 inches into the soils below the aggregate subbase to reduce the potential for migration of 
appreciable landscape water into the pavement section. Alternatively, a subdrain system

Site Preparation 
We believe that only minor site grading work will be required for the project 
construction.  In general, areas within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly 
cleared at the on-set of earthwork.  Debris, deleterious materials, and other unsuitable 
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site.  Soft, loose, and yielding 
areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or future 
improvements should be over-excavated to expose firm or dense material, and the 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with compacted granular fill.  The excavated soft, 
loose, and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site.  In addition, contract 
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and 
compacted fill placement to account for variation in the over-excavation quantities. 
Existing structures and pavements to be demolished should be completely 
removed. Over-excavations resulting from the demolition operations should be backfilled 
with compacted granular fill material. Existing underground utilities to be abandoned 
should be removed, and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with the 
excavated on-site materials. The on-site materials should be moisture-conditioned to at 
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content, placed in 6 to 8-inch level loose lifts, 
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Utilities to be abandoned 
in-place under the elevated generator platform structure should be backfilled by pumping 
lean concrete or Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) under low pressure. 
After clearing and demolition, the areas to receive fill and finished subgrades in cut 
areas should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of 
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.   
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft and/or loose areas and 
replacement with compacted granular fill.  A Geolabs representative should evaluate the 
need for over-excavation in the field. 
In general, the excavated on-site soils may be re-used as a source of general fill or 
backfill materials, provided that they are free of deleterious materials (vegetation) and are 
screened of particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension.  Imported fill materials 
should consist of non-expansive, select granular materials, such as crushed coral or 
basalt.  The materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger 
than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent

Utility Trench 
We envision that new electrical duct lines will be required to service the proposed 
new lighting system. In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) is recommended under the duct lines. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as clean sand or No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, 
No. 67 gradation) should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 6 inches 
above the duct lines to provide adequate support around the pipes, and to reduce the 
compaction effort of the backfill, thus reducing the possibility of pipe damage. It is critical to 
use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the 
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper 
backfill material and placement procedures could result in backfill settlement and pipe 
damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the duct lines 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on-site soils or imported select 
granular fill material. The backfill should be placed in maximum 6-inch level loose lifts, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and mechanically 
compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future 
ground subsidence. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content corresponding to 
the maximum dry density.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three boreholes, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to a depth of about 
16.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate boring locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550/D3550M-17, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Testing 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples 
of the near-surface clayey soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils. The test 
results are summarized on Plate B-1. 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentations of the test results are 
provided on Plate B-2. 
One Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression test (ASTM D2850) was 
performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the 
alluvial clay soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the 
applied confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test results and 
the stress-strain curve are presented on Plate B-3. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the in-situ clayey soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5.
