SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the subsurface conditions, the project site is generally underlain by 
reclamation fills and lagoonal deposits extending to about 48 to 153 feet below the 
existing ground surface. It should be noted that the lagoonal deposits encountered at 
about 46 feet below the ground surface contained up to 9 percent of organic content. 
Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at depths of about 1.5 to 5 feet 
below the existing ground surface, equivalent to elevations of approximately +0.1 to 
+3 feet MSL, at the time of our field exploration. Due to the proximity of the project site to 
the Pacific Ocean, water levels at the project site will likely be influenced by tidal 
fluctuations, seasonal precipitation, and storm surge conditions. 
We envision placement of new fills to raise the site to the finished grade elevation 
will induce some ground settlements as a result of consolidation of the underlying 
compressible lagoonal deposits. Based on our engineering analyses, we anticipate 
ground settlements generally on the order of about 6 to 10 inches resulting from the new 
fill surcharge planned for the project. We also recommend placing the new fills as soon 
as practical to allow the anticipated ground settlements to occur and to minimize impact 
to the new structure construction. In addition, we recommend placing the new fill up to 
the design finished grade plus 30 inches higher to account for the estimated settlement 
and to allow the estimated ground settlements to occur. 
We envision the new warehouse structure will be relatively light for the project. We 
recommend supporting the new structure on 16.5-inch octagonal precast, prestressed 
concrete piles. We also recommend extending the piles to a depth of about 105 feet below 
the existing ground surface to achieve an allowable compressive load capacity of about 
100  kips. 
As an alternative, we recommend implementing a deep soil stabilization consisting 
of installing 3-foot diameter jet-grouted columns. The jet-grouted columns should be 
spaced at about 6 feet on-center in a triangle grid pattern. Based on this configuration, 
each of the jet-grouted columns may be extended to a depth of about 70 feet below the 
existing ground surface. 
To renovate the existing maintenance building, we recommend installing 
micropiles to strengthen the existing spread footings supporting the building structural 
loads. The existing steel truss building may be re-leveled to restore the level floor 
condition prior to structurally connecting the micropile foundation to the spread footings. 
We recommend installing 5.5-inch diameter micropiles to a depth of about 120 feet below 
the ground surface to achieve an allowable compressive load capacity of about 40 kips.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct 
shield volcanoes - Waianae and Koolau. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of 
the western one-third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority 
of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Waianae Volcano became extinct while the 
Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below the Koolau 
Volcano lavas in Central Oahu. 
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by 
thinly-bedded basaltic lava flows. The main shield-building activities ceased 
approximately 2.5 million years ago. Evidence from drilled wells indicates that the Island 
of Oahu has subsided by as much as 1,200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic 
activity (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During that period of subsidence, coral-algal reefs began to grow on the southern 
coast of the Island of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth. The 
growth of the reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence. A series of lagoons 
formed behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated 
in the lagoons (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred 
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers advanced, the level of the ocean fell since there was less water 
available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global 
sea levels rose because more water was available. The land mass of Oahu remained 
essentially stable during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. 
These glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and 
lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in the

resulting in thick deposits of soft and/or loose sediments along the coast in areas that 
were formerly valleys and drainage ways. 
The project site is located on the seaward fringe of the Honolulu Coastal Plain. The 
coastal plain is composed of interbedded alluvial, marine, and post-erosional volcanic 
deposits. Extensive development of the plain and reclamation of the low-lying areas have 
given the land its present shape. There is evidence that basaltic lava flow of the Honolulu 
Volcanic Series, possibly originating from the Makuku vent in Nuuanu or from Punchbowl, 
reached the eastern portion of what is now Sand Island. During the Waipio stand of the 
sea at Elevation -60 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL), this layer of basalt has provided a 
resistant surface, which has diverted the flows of Nuuanu and Kapalama Streams to the 
west. The diversion of these two streams has resulted in the deposition of extensive 
subaerial sediments consisting of alluvial clays and silts. These exposed alluvial deposits 
have become consolidated due to seasonal moisture changes. 
Subsequently, as the sea level rose to the Waimanalo stand of the sea at about 
Elevation +25 feet MSL and then fell to its present level, the stream-laid alluvium was 
buried beneath backreef deposits of sands as a fringing coral reef grew and emerged on

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code (2012 Edition), the project site may be 
subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed. 
The following sections provide discussions on the soil profile type for seismic design and 
liquefaction design consideration at the project site. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to 
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before 
or during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the 
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not 
erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but 
most are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. 
However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause 
minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes

on the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii (over 
90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree

The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of

To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes greater

the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of 1938 (M6.8). Some houses 
on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a result of the Lanai Earthquake 
of 1871 (M7+). 
In the last 150 years of recorded history, we are not aware of earthquakes greater 
than Magnitude 6 that have occurred on the Island of Oahu. An earthquake of 
Magnitude 4.8 to 5.0 occurred along the Diamond Head Fault in 1948 on the Island 
of Oahu. The moderate tremor resulted in broken store windows, ruptured building 
walls, and broken underground water mains. 
2.4.2 Soil Profile Type for Seismic Design 
Our field exploration generally encountered surface fills over very soft to medium stiff 
and very loose to loose lagoonal deposit extending to depths of about 48 to 153 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The loose lagoonal deposit was about 12.5 to 
116.5 feet thick. 
We envision that the loose lagoonal deposit encountered at the site may have a factor 
of safety less than 1.0 with regard to potential soil liquefaction. Therefore, we 
anticipate that the project site could be subjected to ground settlements in the event 
of liquefaction during a moderate earthquake (M6+). However, we believe that lateral 
spreading associated with liquefaction would not be a significant design 
consideration based on the relatively flat topography of the project.

3.1 
Ground Settlements 
The subsurface conditions encountered at the site generally consisted of 5 to 
8.5 feet of reclamation fills overlying soft/loose lagoonal deposits extending to depths of 
about 48 to 153 feet below the ground surface. Based on the results of the laboratory 
consolidation testing, we believe the ground settlement would likely result primarily from 
the consolidation of highly compressible clayey/silty lagoonal deposits under the 4-foot of 
new fills planned for this project. 
Based on the laboratory test results, the highly compressible lagoonal deposits 
appear to be normally consolidated. Based on our engineering analyses, we anticipate 
ground settlements generally on the order of about 6 to 10 inches due to the new 4-foot 
of fill surcharge. To reduce the impact of the appreciable ground settlements caused by 
consolidation of the compressible deposits, we recommend placing the new fills as soon 
as practical to allow the anticipated ground settlements to occur, prior to the building 
construction. 
It should be recognized that it is very difficult to accurately predict the exact time 
for the ground to settle because the settlement rate is affected by variations of the soil 
structure and the history of the soil deposition. Therefore, a settlement-monitoring 
program should be implemented.

placement to the finished grade elevation plus 30 inches high for surcharge. The

3.3 
Deep Soil Stabilization 
As an alternative of the surcharging waiting-period and driven pile foundation, we 
recommend that deep soil stabilization be implemented consisting of installing 3-foot 
diameter jet-grouted columns, to transfer the new fill loads onto the underlying competent 
bearing layer and minimize the impact to adjacent structure/facility from damage due to the 
fill surcharge. 
In general, jet-grouting is a technique utilizing a special drill bit and injection monitor 
with radial horizontal nozzles to produce stabilized soil-cement columns.  The jet-grouting 
technique is a process that produces soil-cement columns by pumping neat cement grout 
slurry through horizontal jets injected at high pressures.  The horizontal jets of cement grout 
slurry cuts and mixes the surrounding in-situ materials with the neat cement slurry grout as 
the drill bit is slowly rotated and withdrawn to form a soil-cement column. 
In order to provide support for the new fill surcharge, we recommend that 
jet grouted columns be installed. The jet-grouted columns would derive vertical support 
primarily from skin friction along the sides of the jet-grouted columns and/or from bearing 
on the medium dense soils. Based on our evaluation of the subsurface conditions and the

3.3.1 Jet-Grouted Column Foundations 
Based on our experience, the jet-grouted columns should have a minimum 
average diameter of 3 feet.  Due to the nature of jet-grouting, deviations from the 
specified minimum average diameter of the jet-grout column is anticipated 
depending on the subsurface conditions.  However, the jet-grout column should 
not have a diameter of less than 2.5 feet.  In addition, the grout mix should have a 
specific gravity of at least 1.6 and should be able to produce jet-grouted columns 
with a 7-day unconfined compressive strength of at least 200 pounds per square 
inch (psi) and a 28-day unconfined compressive strength of at least 400 psi. 
In general, the jet-grouted column foundations should extend from about +3 feet 
Mean Sea Level (MSL) to about -67 feet MSL. It should be noted that local 
variations in the subsurface profile should be anticipated. Because the depths of 
each column foundation will vary depending on the subsurface conditions at each 
column foundation location, a Geolabs representative should be present to

3.3.2 Jet-Grouting Equipment 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the site, we recommend using 
the single fluid method of jet-grouting for support of the reclamation fill surcharge 
for this project. The drilling equipment should be capable of advancing the jetting 
rods to the depth required. The drilling equipment also should be equipped with

Grout mixers, holding tanks, and associated equipment should be capable of

should be equipped with the necessary gauges to measure and record grout 
pumping pressures, flow rate, and total grout used for each column foundation.

Micropile Foundations 
Based on the as-built information provided, we understand that the existing steel 
truss maintenance building is supported partially on 12-inch square concrete precast piles 
along the waterfront side and 3.5-foot square spread footings along the land side. Based on 
the information provided, we understand a differential settlement of up to 12 inches was 
estimated between the columns on the waterfront and land sides. Considering the relatively 
light structural loads, we envision that the existing maintenance building may be renovated 
by strengthening the spread footing and re-leveling the steel truss structure to restore the 
level condition. 
To strengthen the existing spread footings, we recommend installing micropiles to 
provide sufficient axial compressive load resistance. To engage the vertical support, the 
micropiles will require to be pre-loaded prior to structurally connecting to the existing 
spread footings. The project structural engineer should be consulted for the detailed 
structural connections.  
We recommend that the capacity of the micropile foundation be derived primarily 
from skin friction between the micropile shaft and the medium dense/stiff  lagoonal 
deposits encountered in the borings. 
A micropile consists of a small diameter (usually less than 12 inches), drilled and 
grouted, pile with steel reinforcing. The micropile foundation typically is constructed by 
drilling a borehole, placing reinforcing steel in the hole, and grouting the borehole.

Based on the availability of local material inventory, we envision that a micropile

To achieve the allowable compressive load capacity with a factor of safety of 2, we 
estimate that the 5.5-inch micropile would need to extend to a depth of about 120 feet

grout bulb (bonded zone) developed within the medium dense/stiff lagoonal deposits with 
a 50-foot grout zone (neglected) within the very soft/loose lagoonal deposits. The center 
reinforcing steel bar should consist of a Grade 75 all threaded No. 10 reinforcing bar with 
a cross sectional area of 1.27 square inches. 
To facilitate the micropile drilling and ensure the quality of the grouting, we 
recommend advancing the steel casing to the bottom of the micropile during the drilling 
operation. The steel casing may be withdrawn during grouting operation while a minimum 
of 5 feet grout is maintained above the bottom of the casing at all times.  To reduce risk 
of micropile “necking” within the loose/soft lagoonal materials, we recommend leaving 
about 50 feet of permanent steel casing in-place at the top of each micropile (unbounded 
zone).  The permanent casing should have an outside diameter (OD) of about 5.5 inches

3.4.1 Uplift Load Resistance 
Uplift loads imposed on the micropiles may be resisted by shear along the grout 
bulb (bonded zone) and the adjacent soils.  In general, an uplift load capacity of 
up to 35 kips per pile may be achieved with a bonded zone extending a minimum 
of 70 feet below the bottom of casing for the 5.5-inch diameter micropile. The uplift 
load capacity provided is intended for transient load applications only. Therefore, 
the uplift load capacities should not be further increased for transient load 
applications. For sustained uplift loads, we recommend reducing the uplift load 
capacity provided above by 50 percent to resist sustained uplift loads. The capacity 
of the pile member in tension should be checked by the project structural engineer. 
3.4.2 Lateral Load Resistance 
It should be noted that the micropile foundation system provides low lateral load 
resistance due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile.  Therefore, the 
lateral load resistance contributed by the vertical micropiles should be neglected.  
Lateral loads imposed on the new structure foundations may be resisted primarily 
by passive earth pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and 
grade beams and by shear acting along the sides of the pile caps and grade 
beams. 
Passive pressure resistance may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure 
of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf) above the groundwater level 
and 150 pcf below the groundwater level.  The normal high groundwater level may 
be assumed at an elevation of about +3 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  Additional 
resistance to lateral loads may be provided by shear resistance between the sides 
of the pile caps and grade beams and the adjacent soils.  A side shear resistance 
of up to 150 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for transient loads such as 
wind or seismic forces only.

3.6 
Corrosion Potential 
Two sets of laboratory corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum 
Resistivity (ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), 
were performed by our office and Test America Laboratories, Inc. on selected soil 
samples obtained from our field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site soils. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower 
the resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of 
metallic objects. The on-site soils encountered near the existing ground surface at the 
project site have a resistivity value on the order of magnitude of less than 500 ohm-cm, 
corresponding to a corrosion rating of 1 (Extremely Corrosive) based on the guidelines 
provided by the City & County of Honolulu – Board of Water Supply. 
Based on the results of the laboratory corrosivity tests, we believe that a corrosion 
rating of 1 (Extremely Corrosive) may be used for the proposed development. Design of 
metallic substructures, such as water line pipes or other metallic piping, should consider 
the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH value, chloride content, and sulfate content found in 
the soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate contents of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. Levels of the chloride 
and sulfate contents of the on-site soils encountered near the existing ground surface are 
moderately high on the order of up to about 1,500 parts per million (ppm) and 500 ppm 
or less, respectively. We recommend consulting with a professional corrosion engineer 
to review the test results and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion protection.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
seven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 7, extending to depths of about 48 to 
153 feet below the existing ground surface. Two bulk samples of the near-surface soils, 
designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, were obtained to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock cores were described in general accordance with 
the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1.1 through A-7.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods for 
the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative 
Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock 
Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material is

The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality 
of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting drilling induced 
fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, 
the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. 
It should be noted that RQD does not apply to coral material. However, the RQD values of 
the coral material are included in the boring logs for rough characterization of the coral 
material. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock, based 
on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals."

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Thirteen Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Twenty-four Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136), including hydrometer tests 
(ASTM D422), were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation 
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plates B-3 through B-7. 
Four Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-8 through B-11. 
Five Consolidation tests (ASTM D2435) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the materials encountered. Consolidation test 
results are presented on Plates B-12 through B-16. 
Five Triaxial Unconsolidated Undrained (UU) Compression tests (ASTM D 2850) 
were performed on selected in-situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strengths 
of the saprolite soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressures were used as 
the applied confining pressures for the relatively “undisturbed” soil samples. The test results 
and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-17 through B-21. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the 
soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-22 and B-23. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate their relationships between the moisture 
content and the dry density. The test results are presented on Plates B-24 and B-25.
