GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING EXPLORATION
VILLAGES OF KAPOLEI 16 INCH RECYCLED WATER MAIN
Dear Mr Urasaki
In accordance with our fee proposal dated we have recently
conducted a geotechnical engineering exploration for the Villages of Kapolei 16 inch
Recycled Water Main project This letter report summarizes our work on the project and
presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of the
recycled water main valve manhole foundation and thrust blocks for the above project In
addition possible trenchless methods for the railroad crossing are described
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located in the Ewa area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
project consists of the installation of approximately 1 800 linear feet of 16 inch recycled
water main and appurtenances along an existing HECO power line easement from
Hawaiian Railway right of way to the projection of Kapolei Parkway The new water main
will connect to existing 16 inch recycled water mains at the north and south ends of the
alignment
We understand that valve manhole structures and thrust blocks for the new
recycled water main will be constructed for the project In addition the proposed recycled
water main alignment crosses two existing railroad tracks We understand that one of the
railroad track is currently being used by the Hawaiian Railway Society
REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger

The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating sediments
that were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and further south
these alluvial sediments were interbedded with marine sediments and coral algal reef
formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from zero feet in the area of
H 1 freeway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and
serves as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of
southern Oahu Deposition of these sediments has continued from earlier geologic time
through the present
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located in the Ewa District on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
project site is located north of North Hanson Road and west of Varona Village as shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The new recycled water main alignment is located along an existing overhead
electric utility corridor that includes tall metal power poles with high voltage lines and
shorter wooden poles with lower voltage lines The two power pole lines are situated
about 45 feet apart
An existing 20 inch water line alignment is situated within the electric utility corridor
and runs parallel with the overhead power pole alignments In addition an existing
underground 8 inch sewer line and 24 inch drain line crosses the new recycled water main
alignment near the northern portion of the site Near the southern portion of the site the
new water main crosses two underground petroleum pipelines and two existing railroad
tracks located at the ground surface
The project site is relatively flat and is covered with grass and brush Based on the
topographic survey map received from Parsons Brinckerhoff on the
existing ground surface generally slopes up to the north with elevations range from 51 to
58 feet Mean Sea Level MSL

two borings to depths of approximately 20 feet below the existing ground surface The

The boring drilled at the northern end of the project generally encountered a

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration generally encountered stiff to hard alluvial soils and soft to
hard coral formation at the project site Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled
borings at the time of the field exploration
A granular bedding layer should be provided below the new water main pipes and
the bottom of the valve manhole structures to provide more uniform support for the pipes
and the manhole structures The granular bedding should consist of 6 inches of open
graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
Thrust blocks are anticipated at the vertical and horizontal bends along the pipe
alignment Lateral bearing pressures of 1 500 and 3 000 pounds per square foot may be
used for the design of thrust blocks bearing on the stiff to hard silty clay soils and the soft
to hard coral formation respectively
Trenchless methods of pipe installation were evaluated for the railroad crossing
These trenchless methods included horizontal directionai drilling HDD auger boring and
microtunneling Based on our evaluation it appears that HDD and auger boring
installation methods are feasible for the railroad crossing Pipe installation for the short
trenchless crossing using microtunneling was considered very costly and not a viable
option

Detailed geotechnical engineering recommendations regarding open trench

Open Trench Installation
Based on the information provided we understand that excavations on the order of
about 5 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface will be required for the water line
installation The pipe invert at the northern and southern ends of the water line is at about
Elevation 48 9 and 42 6 feet MSL respectively Based on the field exploration the
trench excavation may generally encounter stiff to hard silty clays and soft to hard coral
formation
In general the method and equipment to be used for trench excavation should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The
excavated soils from the trench excavation should be stockpiled no closer than a
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to
reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement In addition the trench excavation
should comply with all applicable local state and federal safety requirements
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important
design consideration In general we recommend that granular bedding consisting
of 6 inches of open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be provided
below the pipes The granular bedding should extend beyond the sides of the pipe
by a minimum width of one fourth 1 4 the outside diameter of the pipe The
contractor should confirm that the excavated trench bottom exposes firm and or
dense maiterials prior to the placement of the bedding material
Trench Backfill
The first zone of backfill initial trench backfill extending from the bedding material
to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes should consist of open graded
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation Itis critical that a free draining granular
material be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches
of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper
trench backfill around the pipes could result in settlement of the backfill and
damage to the pipes The use of on site soils as backfill immediately around the
pipes is not recommended

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the finished subgrade may consist of the excavated on site soils provided that the
excavated on site soils are generally less than 6 inches in maximum particle size or
select granular material Select granular fill and imported fill should consist of
non expansive granular material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder
sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles
larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of less than
1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883
Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs Inc for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use
The backfill material should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in relatively thin lifts normally 8 inches in level loose lifts
and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to
reduce the potential for significant future ground subsidence Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum
dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Excavation
Based on the topographic plan and our field exploration excavation may involve
cuts into the underlying coral formation It is anticipated that the coral rock may be
excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such as ripping with large
bulldozers where the material is near the existing ground surface However deep
excavations into coral formations may require the use of hoerams
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based
on field data obtained from the borings performed at the subject site We
recommend that contractors bidding on this project be encouraged to examine the
site conditions and soil data to make their own interpretation

Trenchless Methods for Railroad Track Crossing
Trenchless installation methods were evaluated for the railroad track crossing at the
southern portion of the site The trenchless installation methods considered were
horizontal directional drilling auger boring and microtunneling A brief description and
discussions of the different trenchless methods are presented below
Horizontal Directional Drilling
Horizontal directional drilling HDD is a trenchless steerable installation method for
the installation of pipes conduits and cables in a shallow arc using a surface
launched drilling rig Typically a small diameter pilot hole is first bored using either
controlled fluid jetting or fluid assisted mechanical cutting or combinations of both
at the drill bit Then the pilot hole is enlarged by reaming to accommodate the pipe
to be installed After the hole has been enlarged to the proper size the pipe is
pulled into the reamed hole by the drilling machine Most HDD systems use slurry
during drilling to stabilize the walls of the borehole and to reduce the frictional drag

Hawaii California

on the pipeline being installed The slurry or drilling mud typically consists of a clay
material called bentonite and water HDD is a steerable method of installation
Steering corrections are made by rotating the slanted cutter shoe to the desired
orientation prior to advancing the shoe and drill rods without rotation of the rods
In general the HDD pilot hole is drilled nearly horizontal with entry and exit angles
between 8 and 15 degrees measured from horizontal Shallow working pits may be
required at each end of the bore to allow placement of the drill pipe and to allow
transition to the conventional pipe trench installation Typically high density
polyethylene HDPE steel and polyvinyl chloride PVC pipes may be installed by
HDD methods Recently ductile iron pipes with special joint connections have
been installed using HDD
The primary advantage of utilizing HDD for the railroad crossing is the relatively low
cost of installation In addition the excavation of deep entry and exit pits are not
required for this method of installation One of the disadvantages of this method of
pipe installation includes the large 2 000 feet bending radius required for the
large 30 to 36 inches outside diameter casing size needed for the crossing The
required bending radius may be reduced by the installation of special ductile iron
pipes without an outer casing if allowed Should polyethylene encasement of the
ductile iron pipes be required for corrosion protection special precautions needs to
be taken to prevent damage to the polyethylene wrap during installation
Because of the large area available on the northern side of the railroad tracks we
anticipate that the pipe string will be pulled back from the northern side of the
tracks Therefore the HDD drill rig will be situated on the southern side of the
tracks Based on the topographic plans the distance from the track to the southern
fence line is about 80 feet This distance appears sufficient to provide enough
cover over the estimated pipe alignment below the tracks Based on the above we
believe that it is feasible to use HDD methods for the railroad crossing
Auger Boring
Auger boring is commonly used for relatively short distances of less than 100 feet
to install sewer water electrical oil or gas lines beneath streets highways
railroads and other structures where open trench is not practicable or desirable
The auger boring method consists of utilizing a continuous flight auger to excavate
through the soil as the pipe is being pushed forward The excavated materials at
the cutting head are transported through the flights of the auger back to the entry
pit The cutting spoils are then removed from the pit Entry and exit pits are usually
constructed for this method of pipe installation The auger boring machine is set up
in the entry pit The auger boring equipment generally consists of a drilling
machine mounted horizontally on a track system Therefore the drilling head can

travel horizontally along the tracks while installing the auger and pipe sections The
track system is anchored at the base and at the back of the pit The auger and pipe
sections are advanced forward by the auger boring machine The auger boring has
a tendency to drift off a desired alignment as the boring progresses Therefore this
method of installation should not be used for installations where holding line and
grade is critical
Typically a steel casing pipe is installed using auger boring methods Subsequent
to the installation of the casing pipe the product pipe is installed within the casing
pipe and the annulus between the casing and product pipes are filled with cement
grout
Auger boring works best in pipeline installation above the ground water level and
within subsoil consisting of clays and silts Augering through running sands
gravels cobbles and boulders will be difficult Since this method does not require
the use of sophisticated computer equipment the cost of auger boring installation is
more economical compared to microtunneling
One of the advantages of auger boring is that it is more economical compared to
microtunneling Also the installation length using auger boring will be much shorter
than the HDD method Based on the location of the tracks and underground
petroleum lines it is anticipated that the tunneling drive will be about 100 feet in
length The disadvantages include construction of entry and exit pits are required
and difficulties may be encountered augering through the underlying soft to hard
coral formation Based on the above we believe that the installation of the new
water main below the railroad tracks by auger boring is feasible
Microtunneling
Microtunneling is an installation method where a pipe is jacked behind a remotely
controlled steerable laser guided tunnel boring machine TBM capable of installing
underground pipes at relatively accurate lines and grades The tunnel boring
machine can either be auger slurry or earth pressure balance type machines In
general the TBM is located below ground at the level of the pipe being installed
Therefore jacking and receiving pits are required to accommodate the TBM and
other microtunneling equipment As the TBM excavates the soils the pipe behind
the TBM is being pushed simultaneously by hydraulic jacks During the
microtunneling process the excavated material is removed by using a mixture of
water and slurry pumped through a pipe for disposal balanced screw auger or a
screw conveyor system
Pipes that can be installed by microtunneling include polymer concrete pipe
fiberglass lined polymer concrete pipe fiber reinforced plastic pipe steel and
Permalock casing pipe reinforced concrete pipe and vitrified clay pipe

Different types of cutter heads are used for various types of soil rock The length
and depth of the drive and groundwater conditions are also taken into consideration
when determining the type of cutter head used Ground conditions where cobbles
and boulders are encountered might halt the progress of the TBM If the pipe
alignment is located in an area consisting of different soil types the cutter head
may encounter a mixed face condition which could lead to steering difficulties
surface settlement or deflection of the TBM causing an offset in the line and grade
As previously mentioned the TBM is located below the ground surface therefore
jacking and receiving pits are required for microtunneling methods The depth of
the pits is usually about 3 to 4 feet below the pipeline invert depth The length and
width of the shafts are sized based on the sizes of the TBM and jacking frame
Because the receiving pit does not have to accommodate the jacking frame the
receiving pit is usually smaller in size relative to the jacking pit Due to the relatively
deep depths required for microtunneling shoring of the jacking and receiving pits
may be required
The primary advantages of microtunneling methods are that relatively accurate line
and grade can be achieved and it is suitable for almost all ground conditions
provided that the right equipment is used Since the new water line is under
pressure obtaining precise line and grade during pipe installation is not critical
The primary disadvantage of microtunneling methods for the project site is the
relatively high cost associated with the short run needed for the pipe installation at
the railroad track
Based on the above discussions we believe that installation of the proposed
recycled water line by microtunneling will be very costly Therefore we believe
that recycled water line installation by microtunneling for the railroad track
crossing is not a viable option
