2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes which include the following Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea
which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801
and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the northeastern flank of the Mauna Loa Shield
Volcano which had been built up by the successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows
and pyroclastic materials The lava formation encountered at the site appears to consist
of both a a and pahoehoe type flows which spread and ponded as they approached the
ocean A a lava is typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface
resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders
Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal
structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe type
lava These cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and they
represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow
cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving
a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along
the flow Blisters and pockets are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is
transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and_ inter flow
contacts cavities and along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities
is high therefore they serve as the primary water transmission features

2 4 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by
shifts in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily
to volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most of the earthquakes
are so small that only instruments can detect the minor earthquakes However some of
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate
damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the
Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent of
earthquakes are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and degree of
ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the southern portion of the
Island of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater
than Magnitude 6 M6 Based on information obtained from the United States

3 2 Foundation Probing
Based on our exploration and experience in the area voids and or cavities
appear to be present in the basaltic lava flows at the project site To reduce the
potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities below
foundations we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting of the
reservoir tank structure foundation be implemented during construction Foundation
probing and grouting may be omitted for other minor structures planned at the site
For estimating purposes we recommend that probe holes be drilled at 10 foot on
centers for the perimeter ring foundation In addition probe holes should be drilled at
each isolated spread footing location Probe holes also should be drilled within the tank
floor area at about 10 foot on center starting at the center of the tank and radiating out
at 10 foot radial increments The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter
and should extend to a depth of at least 15 feet below the planned bottom of foundation
lf cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing
operation additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacings to help delineate
the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities
discovered should be backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material CLSM with a
slump of about 6 to 9inches The CLSM should be injected at low to moderate
pressures As an alternate to CLSM sand cement grout with a slump of about
6 to 9 inches also may be used for the grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during

choking layer of smaller sized boulder materials to fill in the voids between the
boulders of the preceding rock fill layer
Conventional compaction testing generally is not practical in fills composed of rocks
and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of passes by a
compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be conducted
at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a

representative from Geolabs Based on this testing program the number of passes
may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction
For preliminary estimating purposes compaction may be accomplished by using a

Excavation
Based on our field exploration the project site generally is underlain by volcanic
ash materials over fractured pahoehoe lavas ranging from medium hard to very
hard It is anticipated that the near surface materials may be excavated with
normal heavy excavation equipment such as excavators and ripping with large
bulldozers However some of the excavations in the dense pahoehoe formations
may require the use of hoe rams or chipping
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based
on our visual observation of the existing basalt formation and experience with
similar type subsurface conditions at the project site We recommend that
contractors be encouraged to examine the site conditions and boring data to make
their own interpretation
Cut and Fill Slopes
In general we envision that most of the cut slopes would expose dense basalt
formation In general we believe that the cut slopes planned at the site may be

at the cut slope faces the cut slopes should be flattened to 2H 1V or flatter

should be considered in the design of the project A representative from Geolabs
should observe and map the cut slopes during construction to evaluate whether the
exposed cut slope materials are consistent with our assumptions

3 5 Concrete Pads
We envision that concrete pads and exterior flatwork would be constructed at the
project site In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion fill layer
consisting of open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be provided below the
concrete pad for uniform support As an alternative the concrete pad may be
constructed directly on the compacted structural fill subgrade in fill areas provided that
the subgrade is properly prepared and compacted To reduce the potential for
substantial shrinkage cracks in the concrete slabs crack control joints should be
provided at intervals equal to the width of the concrete pads with expansion joints at
right angle intersections
3 6 Pipelines
We envision that a water pipeline will be installed at the project site It is
anticipated that most of the trenches for the pipelines will be excavated in the medium
hard to very hard basalt rock encountered in the borings drilled for the project In
general we recommend that a granular bedding layer consisting of 6 inches of
open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be provided below the pipes for
uniform support of the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as open graded gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the
sides of the pipes Improper backfill material around the pipes and improper placement
of the backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes

3 7 Thrust Blocks
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at the bends of the new pipeline
The following recommendations may be used for the preliminary design and sizing of
the thrust blocks Lateral loads acting on the thrust blocks may be resisted by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the thrust blocks and the bearing materials
and by passive earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the thrust block
For frictional resistance a coefficient of friction of 0 5 may be used for the concrete
thrust blocks bearing on the compacted structural fill or basalt formation
In general we envision that the bottom of the thrust blocks may be embedded at
least 4 feet below the existing ground surface Based on our field exploration dense
basalt formation generally were encountered at relatively shallow depths Therefore we
believe that a lateral bearing pressure of 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be
used for the design of thrust blocks This assumes that the soils around the thrust
blocks are well compacted minimum of 90 percent relative compaction

The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 The borings were
advanced to a maximum depth of about 50 feet below the existing ground surface using
a truck mounted drill rig equipped with coring tools The approximate locations of the
borings drilled for the project are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
