PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is at the Pakini Nui Wind Farm facility in the South Point area on the 
Island of Hawaii.  There is an existing substation at the beginning of the facility that contains 
the existing transformer.  The transformer sits on an elevated concrete pad.   
We understand that a new transformer that is slightly heavier than the existing 
transformer is planned to replace the existing transformer.  A structural assessment was 
conducted by Phree Creations, LLC to determine if the existing transformer pad foundation is 
in compliance with the 2006 International Building Code.  Based on the structural 
assessment, the existing transformer foundation does not meet current seismic code 
requirements.  Therefore, retrofitting the transformer foundation with a helical pile or other 
deep foundation system is being considered.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Hawaii, the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago, covers an area of 
approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of five shield 
volcanoes including the following:  Kohala - long extinct; Mauna Kea - some activity during 
recent geologic times; Hualalai - last erupted in 1801 and is considered dormant; and 
Mauna Loa and Kilauea - both still active.  The project site is situated on the southern flank 
of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano.

Mauna Loa is  composed of two separate shield volcanoes:  Mauna Loa and 
Ninole. The latter having been covered by Mauna Loa lavas, left only a few localized 
surface expressions visible at present. The Mauna Loa shield has been built by volcanic 
eruptions along two principal rift zones that extend southwestward and east-northeastward 
from the summit caldera. 
The majority of the surface rock exposures at Mauna Loa consist of lavas and 
volcanic deposits belonging to the Ka’u Volcanic Series (Pleistocene and Holocene age) 
and the older Kahuku Volcanic Series (Pleistocene age). In addition, a regional ash 
deposit, identified as Pahala Ash (Pleistocene age), forms a soil mantle overlying and 
interbedded within the rocks of the Ka’u Volcanic Series. The ash deposits typically occur 
as kipukas (isolated deposits) with some significant accumulations ranging up to about 
30 feet in thickness. 
Pahala Ash is a low plasticity silt derived from the weathering of ash, cinder, and 
Pele’s Hair. It generally has low shear strength, and when the moisture content is high 
enough, it becomes thixotrophic, i.e. it loses strength when remolded. Therefore, the 
typical Pahala Ash may be potentially liquefiable during seismic events. However, it should 
be noted that the volcanic ash soils encountered at the project site appear to be different 
from the typical “Pahala Ash” in that the moisture content of the silts encountered at the 
site possess lower moisture contents (less than about 80 percent). 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration at the project site consisted of drilling and sampling 
one boring, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 26 feet below the 
existing ground surface.  The boring was drilled outside of the substation fence line at the 
northwest side of the substation.  The boring is located on the order of about 70 feet away 
from the existing transformer pedestal.  The approximate boring location is shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The boring generally encountered volcanic ash consisting of very stiff sandy silt and 
dense silty sand extending to a depth of about 11 feet below the existing ground surface. 
The volcanic ash is underlain by weathered volcanic tuff consisting of hard clayey silt 
extending to a depth of about 16 feet below the existing ground surface. The weathered 
volcanic tuff is underlain by basalt rock formation that is generally hard and moderately 
fractured. The basalt rock formation extended to the maximum depth explored of about 
26 feet below the existing ground surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the boring at the time of our field exploration. 
However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, 
time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
We understand that the transformer foundation does not meet the current building 
code seismic loading requirements; therefore, the transformer foundation will need to be 
retrofitted to meet the building code requirements.  
Based on the information provided, helical piers or micropiles are being considered 
to provide resistance to overturning of the transformer foundation. Based on the 
information provided by the project structural engineer, the helical piers or micropiles 
would need to provide an uplift load capacity of 10 kips for seismic loading. Detailed 
discussions and recommendations for the helical piers and micropiles are presented in 
the following sections.  
Helical Piers 
Helical piers generally consist of a solid high strength steel shaft with one or more 
circular steel plates welded to the shaft. The piles are screwed into the underlying soils to 
obtain the required pile capacity. Chance® SS175 helical piles with a 1.75-inch shaft 
width and 6 and 8-inch diameter helical plates may be considered for providing the uplift 
capacity necessary to retrofit the transformer foundation. The uplift load capacity would 
be derived from skin friction between the helical pier shaft and helical plate edges and the 
surrounding very stiff and dense volcanic ash soils. 
Based on the soil conditions encountered in the boring drilled, a helical pile with 
6 and 8-inch diameter helical plates would need to be installed within the very stiff and 
dense volcanic ash to a depth of about 12 feet below the top of the concrete slab in order 
to achieve an uplift load capacity of 5 kips for seismic loading.  The uplift load capacity 
provided is for transient loading and should not be further increased.  Based on the uplift 
load capacity, two helical piers each extending to a depth of about 12 feet below the top 
of the concrete slab are required to provide resistance to the anticipated seismic load.  
In general, the helical piers installed in groups should have a minimum 
center-to-center spacing of 5 times the diameter of the largest helix to avoid reduction in 
vertical load capacity due to group action and to facilitate drilling of the helical piers.  
Micropile Foundations 
Micropiles may be used as an alternative to helical piers. The advantage of using 
micropiles is that a higher uplift capacity can be achieved by embedding the micropile into 
the basalt rock formation encountered at a depth of about 18 feet below the top of the 
concrete slab. In general, a micropile consists of a small diameter (usually less than 
12 inches), drilled and grouted pile with steel reinforcing.   
Based on the soil conditions encountered in the boring drilled, a micropile with a 
5.5-inch diameter permanent steel casing and 5.5-inch diameter grout bulb should be 
used for the foundation retrofit. The permanent casing (unbonded zone) should extend to

Construction Considerations 
The performance of the helical piles/micropile depends upon the contractor’s 
method of construction and construction procedures. Therefore, we recommend that a 
Geolabs representative is present to observe the installation of the helical piles/micropile 
during construction. Construction of the piles should be performed by personnel 
experienced in the installation of helical piles/micropile. 
Helical Pier/Micropile Proof Testing 
It should be noted that the uplift load capacities of the helical piers or micropile are 
highly dependent on the installation methods employed by the contractor. Therefore, the 
uplift load capacities of the helical piers or micropile may vary considerably between 
different contractors.  In order to determine whether the contractor’s methods of 
installation are adequate and to determine the uplift load capacities, we recommend 
performing a pullout test (proof test) on one helical pier or micropile during construction to 
confirm the uplift load carrying capacity of the installed helical piers or micropile. The 
pullout test should consist of subjecting the helical pier or micropile to a maximum test 
load of 5 or 10 kips, respectively. The maximum test load should be held for at least 10 or 
60 minutes. The pullout test on the helical pier or micropile also are integral parts of the 
foundation system design.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 
one boring, designated as Boring No. 1. The boring extended to a depth of about 26 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring location is shown on the Site 
Plan, Plate 2. The boring was drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.   Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Log of Boring, Plate A-1. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the boring drilled in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material 
is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be 
shown on the Log of Boring as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Log of 
Boring as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of 
the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals."

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The tests results are presented on the Log of Boring at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits for soil classification. The test results are 
summarized on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depth.  Graphic presentation 
of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 and C136) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification.  
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-2. 
One unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression test (ASTM D2850) was 
performed on a selected in-situ soil sample to evaluate the undrained shear strength of 
the cohesive soils.  The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressures were used 
as the applied confining pressures for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample.  The test 
results and the stress-strain curve are presented on Plate B-3. 
One-Dimensional Swell or Collapse of Soils (ASTM D4546 Method A) were 
performed on a selected cohesive soil sample to evaluate the swelling and/or collapse 
potential of the cohesive soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-4. 
Two Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were performed on 
selected core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the basalt rock 
formation encountered. The test results are presented on Plate B-5.
