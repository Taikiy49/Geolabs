SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling one additional boring
to a depth of about 9 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate location
shown on the Site Plan Plate 1
Our field exploration indicated that the currently proposed relocation site is
underlain by a relatively thin surficial fill layer consisting of very stiff clayey silts and
dense coral sands and gravels of approximately 4 feet thick This was underlain by

Pile Foundations
Based on the data obtained from our field exploration and laboratory testing
piles driven through the upper fill crust and compressible deposit to a depth of
approximately 70 to 75 feet below the surface is needed to develop the required
capacity to support the proposed gas tank structure
However due to the limited number of piles a test pile program to delineate the
actual production pile length and driving criteria would not be cost effective and will not
be required for the subject project To account for potential variation in subsoil
conditions at the proposed gas storage tank location we recommend that production
piles with a minimum length of 85 feet be used for the subject project
We recommend that 16 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles with
an allowable compressive load capacity of 40 tons per pile be used to support the
proposed gas tank structure The piles will derive load supporting capacity from
frictional resistance and or end bearing in the stiff alluvial deposits To develop the
required pile capacity of 40 tons a pile embedment of approximately 85 feet below the

Pile settlements are estimated to be on the order of less than one inch with

Settlements should be elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
Predrilling
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered during our field exploration it is our
opinion that the piles should be able to penetrate through the surficial fill crust
without the need for predrilling However predrilling through the upper fill crust
may be considered if the vibration generated by the pile driving operation
through the surficial fill crust is detrimental to the existing surrounding petroleum
facilities Therefore unit prices for predrilling should be obtained during the
bidding process
Pile Driving Criteria
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a minimum rated
energy of 30 000 foot pounds such as a Delmag Model D19 32 The hammer
should be equipped with an energy control level such that driving within the
loose and soft deposits may be performed at a lower energy level to reduce the
development of tensile stresses within the piles which could cause damage
cracking to the piles

Because local variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the
bearing soils the supporting capacity of each pile must be confirmed in the field
during driving This should be accomplished by using a driving resistance
procedure such as the Engineering News Record ENR formula or the more
sophisticated Wave Equation Analysis to evaluate pile bearing capacity The
driving criteria will be developed after the selection of the pile driving equipment
for production pile driving Additional pile driving criteria are as follows
1 The driving process should be continuous without interruption for each
pile A pile may be rejected or its design capacity modified when the
driving process is interrupted for more than 4 hours
2 During easy driving when the pile tip is still within the loose sandy soil
the hammer stroke should be carefully controlled with reduced energy
levels so that excessive tensile stresses are not induced in the pile
3 During the pile driving operations the geotechnical engineer may elect to
redrive retap certain piles to evaluate the effect of soil set up freeze on
pile capacity
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to
be driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required
energy to the top of the pile and also reducing damage to the piles Geolabs

Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on foundations may be resisted by a combination of the
resistance of the pile soil system to deflect and passive resistance on the front side of
pile caps Due to the possible future settlement of the ground surface sliding friction
between the bottom of the slab and the supporting subgrade should be neglected due
to uncertainty regarding long term soil contact
The lateral load resisting capacity of the 16 inch octagonal pile is estimated to
be on the order of 10 kips per pile This assumes a lateral deflection of inch at the
top of the pile for a free head condition For passive soil resistance an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth may be used
