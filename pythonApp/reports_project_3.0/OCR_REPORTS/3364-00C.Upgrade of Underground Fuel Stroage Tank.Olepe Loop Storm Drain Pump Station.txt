INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Upgrade of Underground Fuel Storage Tank project at the Olepe
Loop Storm Drain Pump Station located in Pearl City on the island of Oahu Hawaii
The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended for the design of foundation site grading and
excavation only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
We understand that it is proposed to install a new underground fuel storage tank
at the Olepe Loop Storm Drain Pump Station in Pearl City on the island of Oahu
Hawaii Based on the information provided to us we understand that the proposed
new fuel storage tank will be a fiberglass tank The new tank will have plan dimensions
of approximately 11 feet long by 4 feet wide and 3 to 4 feet high At this time there are
three proposed tank locations as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Detailed information for the project was not available at the time this report was
prepared Based on available information we understand that the top of the new fuel
tank will be embedded about 3 to 4 feet below the existing ground surface Therefore
to install the new underground fuel storage tank an excavation of up to 7 to 9 feet
below the existing ground surface will be required

In addition based on our previous experience in the area the project site is
underlain by soft loose deposits at relatively shallow depths with high groundwater
levels Therefore we anticipate that shoring and dewatering of the excavation for the
new underground fuel storage tank will be needed for the proposed construction

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be of the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of the late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age When the building of the Waianae and Koolau
volcanoes came to an end only a slight embayment existed on the southern coast of
Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3364 00 C Page 5
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition caused the top of the basaltic lava
flows to be highly weathered and mantled by residual soils During the Pleistocene
Epoch the Kaena stand of the sea level at approximately 90 feet Mean Sea Level
a delta of silt sand and gravel built out into the embayment near the project site
Agricultural land development and reclamation projects within the last century have
brought the project site to its present form

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsoil conditions at the subject site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings to depths of about 22 and 26 feet below the existing ground surface
The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 At the
time of our exploration there was only one proposed underground fuel storage tank
location which was to be located to the north of the old pump building Therefore all
three borings drilled were located around this proposed tank location
In general our field exploration encountered near surface fills consisting of stiff
clayey silts to depths of about 4 to 7 feet below the existing ground surface
Underlying the near surface fills alluvial soils consisting of loose sands over soft
lagoonal deposits were generally encountered in Boring Nos 1 and 2 to the maximum
depth explored of about 22 and 26 feet below the existing ground surface
respectively In Boring No 3 lagoonal deposits consisting of soft to very soft silty soils
with sands and organics were encountered below the near surface fills to the maximum
depth drilled of approximately 26 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was encountered in all three borings at a depth of about 4 feet
below the ground surface at the time of our field exploration This groundwater level
corresponds to an elevation of about 1 foot MSL Due to the proximity of Pear
Harbor it should be noted that groundwater levels measured in the borings during our
field exploration will likely be affected by tidal fluctuations In addition the groundwater
levels at the project site may also be influenced by seasonal precipitation and storm
surge conditions

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
In general our field exploration indicates that the proposed project site is
underlain by near surface fills over very soft loose sandy and silty soils extending to a
depth of about 26 feet below the existing ground surface Groundwater was
encountered at about 4 feet below the ground surface at the time of our field
exploration
We understand that the top of the proposed tank structure will be embedded
about 3 to 4 feet below the existing ground surface Therefore construction of the
underground fuel storage tank will involve an excavation on the order of 7 to 9 feet
below the surface Because construction of the underground fuel storage tank will
replace the soil with lighter material fuel there is a net decrease in stresses on the
underlying soils On this basis we recommend that a concrete mat be used to provide
the necessary bearing support and uplift resistance for the tank structure Because of
the soft soils anticipated at the foundation subgrade level we recommend that the
concrete mat bear on a minimum 2 foot gravel layer wrapped in a filter fabric for more
uniform bearing support The gravel layer would also serve as a working platform
We anticipate that an excavation of up to about 7 to 9 feet and subsequent
backfilling will be required for construction of the new tank structure Due to the
presence of loose soft subsoils and high groundwater levels we believe that shoring
and bracing will be needed to support the excavation and to aid in dewatering during
construction
Because of the loose soft subsoils encountered some movement of soils around
the new tank excavation should be anticipated due to changes in the earth stresses
during and after construction In addition it should be noted that dewatering for
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3364 00 C Page 8
construction of adjacent structures in the future may result in settlement of this
underground tank structure Therefore we recommend that some flexibility in the
connection of the piping network to the underground fuel storage tank be incorporated
in the design to accommodate possible earth movements

Foundations
We envision that a concrete mat will be needed below the proposed tank
structure to provide the necessary uplift resistance and foundation support An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 1 000 p s f may be used for the design of the
concrete mat bearing on a minimum 2 foot thick gravel layer wrapped in a filter fabric
This allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third for transient loads such as temporary wind and or seismic forces
Due to the presence of very loose soft subsoils near the foundation level we
recommend that a minimum 2 foot gravel layer wrapped with a layer of filter fabric
such as Mirafi 140N or equivalent be provided below the concrete mat for more
uniform bearing support and as a working platform during construction The gravel
layer should consist of clean granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM
C 33 No 67 gradation The filter fabric should be lapped a minimum of 24 inches at
the ends of each roll and along the sides Guide specifications for the filter fabric are
presented in Appendix C of this report
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the footing and the bearing materials and by passive earth
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3364 00 C Page 9
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the foundation A coefficient of
friction of 0 35 may be used between the base of the mat footings and the supporting
materials Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated using an equivalent
fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f above the
groundwater table and 150 p c f below the groundwater for footings embedded in the
in situ soils These values assume that the concrete for footings is placed directly
against the footing excavations Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be neglected
Due to the high groundwater levels encountered in our borings at the proposed
tank site it is anticipated that the proposed underground fuel storage tank will be
subjected to buoyant uplift forces Resistance to uplift loads may be mobilized by the
dead weight of the tank structure the concrete mat and the backfill material above the
concrete mat Contribution of dead weight from the backfill may be estimated using a
groundwater For evaluating uplift forces due to hydrostatic pressures a high
groundwater level elevation of 3 feet MSL may be used for design to take into
considerations of tidal fluctuation
Settlements of the new tank structure will result from the compression of the
soft loose subgrade soil layer due to the placement of the heavier backfill material We
estimate the total settlement of the tank structure to be on the order of 1 inches We
believe that most of the settlements should occur during the construction period
Site Grading
Final grading plans were not available to us at the time this report was prepared
However we anticipate excavations and backfills of up to 7 to 9 feet relative to the
existing ground surface will be required for the project construction Site grading
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3364 00 C Page 10
operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii It is important
that a representative from our office observe whether the exposed soil conditions during
excavation are similar to those encountered in our exploration
We understand that additional fill placement is not planned for this project
Because of the presence of compressible soils at the site placement of additional fills
will induce consolidation of the compressible soils This consolidation will significantly
affect the foundations of the proposed tank structure and could induce areal settlements
which may also affect the surrounding existing structures and infrastructure The
following recommendations are intended to provide guidelines for the design of site
grading work
1 At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits
should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris
rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed
of off site
2 The on site granular soils generated from the excavation may be reused
as a source of fill and backfill material However the on site soft and wet
clayey silty soils should not be reused as fill or backfill material It should
be noted that the on site soils are likely saturated and may require drying
of the soils prior to use as compacted backfill material
3 Imported fill and or backfill material should consist of well graded granular
materials of less than 3 inches in maximum dimension The material
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 15 or
more and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when
tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 In addition
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3364 00 C Page 11
it should contain between 10 and 30 percent passing the No 200 sieve
imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance
with our recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use In addition open graded material such as No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should not be used as backfill
material from the top of the concrete mat up to the ground surface to
reduce the potential for migration of the surrounding fine soils into the
backfill material
4 All fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches
in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as
a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density Fill placement should commence at the lowest
point and continue upward in level compacted lifts

er 8 a a
The selected excavation shoring system must comply with all applicable safety
requirements It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be
retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the shoring system
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be the sole
responsibility of the contractor His representative who should be required to
be continuously present on site during excavation and construction work will
have the best opportunity to promptly observe any changed conditions during
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3364 00 C Page 13
construction such as unforeseen subsoils unexpectedly high groundwater table
inappropriate construction sequences and or techniques all of which may affect
shoring stability
However it should be noted that some movement of the shoring system and the
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during
excavation The magnitude of movement is difficult to accurately estimate due
to the complexity of the stress changes The magnitude depends greatly upon
workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports
are installed and also on the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and
the rate of excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be
installed properly and as early as practical and that the adjacent ground be
continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of movements
with instruments such as settlement markers and inclinometer Such monitoring
is particularly critical in areas adjacent to existing structures and utilities
We strongly recommend that the contractor retain a competent engineering
consultant to design and to coordinate the implementation of the excavation
support system
Dewatering
Dewatering will likely be necessary since the groundwater levels encountered in
our field exploration were above the bottom of the excavation Dewatering should be
the sole responsibility of the contractor The dewatering operations should be
coordinated with the shoring support so that the stability of the excavations is not
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3364 00 C Page 14
jeopardized and the operations should be carried out without softening the bottom of
the excavations
Because granular soils were encountered in our borings we anticipate that
dewatering of the excavation may involve relatively large volumes of water due to the
relatively permeable granular soils encountered Therefore we recommend that a cut
off wall system such as interlocking sheetpiles should be considered in design to aid
in dewatering the excavation
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents
should be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural
groundwater table and to exclude the water from the excavations to permit construction
of the proposed underground fuel storage tank structure under safe and dry conditions
Therefore these works or systems should include but not be limited to pumping
Dewatering Method
The method and equipment to be used for the excavation dewatering should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety consider
ations We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in
selecting a suitable method of dewatering
1 The method should result in the least disturbance or damage to
adjacent existing structures
2 The method should maintain stability of and also provide safe and
dry working conditions in the excavations
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3364 00 C Page 15
3 The method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modifications to
accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of
the excavations is generally not recommended The resultant areal
depression of the natural groundwater table could induce
consolidation of the compressible subsoils resulting in potential
ground settlements which would affect the existing structures
Construction Precautions
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the
natural groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to
dewatering It is also recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be
retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His qualified representative who should be
required to be continuously present at the site will have the best opportunity to
promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and to implement
as soon as possible necessary precautionary or remedial measures including
but not limited to stopping or slowing down the dewatering operations
Where encountered at the excavation bottoms permeable granular subsoils may
be susceptible to piping and quick conditions Therefore the project
dewatering operations should be done without pumping out soil fines pumping
clear water only and should be coordinated with the shoring installation so that
the excavation stability is not jeopardized The dewatering operations should be
GEOLABS HAWAII

carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the excavation
bottoms
Utility Trench
We envision that a new piping network will be required for the project It should
be noted that groundwater was encountered at about 4 feet below the ground surface
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used
under the pipes Where soft and or loose soil deposits are encountered near the pipe
invert elevations we recommend that the utility pipe be supported on an additional
18 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped in a filter
fabric Mirafi 140N or equivalent The backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes
should consist of free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the
potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill A typical section of
the trench detail is presented on Plate 4
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular material or
the approved on site soils The soft and or wet excavated soils should not be used as
trench backfill The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet
of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction

Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3

ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
