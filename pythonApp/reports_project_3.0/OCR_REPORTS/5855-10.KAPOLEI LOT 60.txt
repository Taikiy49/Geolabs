In accordance with our fee proposal dated May 7, 2007, we have performed shear
wave velocity testing in support of the detailed design of the buildings.
This letter report summarizes the additional field exploration performed and our
findings and recommendations in support of the seismic design of the planned building.
These findings and recommendations are intended for design input only and are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this letter report.
Project Considerations
The project site is located on the eastern side of Manawai Street and south of the
Kapolei Library in Kapolei on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The site encompasses about
3.1 acres and will involve the development of three commercial buildings ranging from
three to seven stories high. On-grade parking and related infrastructure are also planned.
The existing grade is relatively level, therefore, minimal grading is anticipated.
Borings for the geotechnical exploration indicate that the subsurface conditions
consist of stiff alluvial soil over coral detritus to the maximum depth drilled of about 25 feet.
Based on the boring data, the site may be classified as a Soil Profile “D” for seismic
design. In order to refine the soil profile classification, we conducted shear wave velocity
profiling in an. effort to more closely analyze the seismic design considerations. We
conducted Cone Penetrometer Testing (CPT) with seismic shear wave velocity tests for
the additional field exploration. The CPT method involves inserting a sensitive probe into
the soil with constant speed. Various sensors embedded in the probe continuously
measure the tip resistance, side friction, and induced pore pressures as the probe is
systematically pushed through the soil. In general, subsurface explorations consisting of
CPT yields more accurate results for highly sensitive materials such as the loose granular
soils because the data is obtained directly from the in-situ soils. Therefore, CPT with
seismic shear wave velocity profiling is considered to be the industry standard for higher
order analysis, such as the detailed seismic analyses, especially in these subsurface
conditions.

Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our work on this project was to perform seismic shear wave velocity
testing of the subsurface materials. The seismic shear wave velocity profiling, extending to
a depth of about 100.6 feet below the ground level, was conducted using seismic cone
penetration testing equipment. Our scope of work included the following tasks and work
efforts:
1. Mobilization and demobilization of a truck-mounted drill rig and
two operators to and from the project site.
2. Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the cone penetration test
probe by our engineer.
3. Setting-up of the seismic cone penetration testing equipment.
4. Performance of seismic cone penetration test to a depth of approximately
100.6 feet below the existing ground surface. Backfilling of the probe hole
after drilling and seismic SCPT work.
5. Analyses of the field data to formulate an average shear wave velocity and a
“Soil Profile” for seismic analysis for the project.
6. Preparation of this letter report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and recommendations of a “Soil Profile” for seismic
analysis of the project.
7. Coordination of our overall work on the project by our engineer.
8. Quality assurance of our work and client/design team consultation by our
principal engineer.
Field Exploration
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling using (CPT) equipment was performed in an
effort to more closely analyze the seismic design considerations for the project site. Shear
wave velocity testing using the cone penetration test method was performed at a testing
location, designated as CPT-1, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The test was
performed to depth of about 100.6 feet below the existing ground surface using seismic
cone testing equipment (SCPT). After coring at the location, the bored hole was backfilled
with 0.25-inch coated bentonite pellets using the casing as a tremie pipe. When the
bentonite pellets encountered water, they swelled and softened as a coupling medium
from the cone penetrometer to the surrounding soil.

The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10-ton
steel electronic subtraction cone with an apex angle of 60 degrees and an area of
1.55 square inches (10 square centimeters) into the ground. The depth of penetration was
recorded at about 2-inch (5-centimeter) intervals using a depth sensor attached to the
cone rod.
The cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the arrival of a shear
wave propagated from the ground surface. The seismic measurements are made when
the SCPT has stopped and a shear wave is sent into the subsurface. A shear wave is
generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a switched hammer. The
propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone was measured at each
depth interval. The vector difference of these depths divided by the time difference
provided the average shear wave velocity over the depth interval. Detailed descriptions of
the materials encountered at the probe location are presented on the Log of Boring,
Plates 4.1 through 4.3.
Soil Profile Type
The shear wave velocities measured and the weighted average shear wave
velocity of the soil profile at the location is presented on Plate 5. The weighted average
shear wave velocity was calculated based on the average shear wave velocity method of
the Uniform Building Code (Section 1636.2.1, 1997 Edition).
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered during our field exploration, the
weighted average shear wave velocity was measured at about 1,280 feet per second.
Based on the shear wave velocity test results, we believe the project site may be classified
from a seismic analysis standpoint as being a “Very Dense Soil” site corresponding to an
S, soil profile type based on the Uniform Building Code Table No. 16-J, 1997 Edition.
Limitations
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon
information obtained from the seismic cone penetration test. Variations of subsurface soil
conditions and shear wave velocities beyond the test probe may occur, and the nature and
extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If
variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations
provided in this letter report.
The location of the field cone penetration test indicated in this report is
approximate, having been taped from features shown on the Site Plan transmitted by
Design Partners on June 7, 2007. The physical location of the cone penetration test
should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method used.
