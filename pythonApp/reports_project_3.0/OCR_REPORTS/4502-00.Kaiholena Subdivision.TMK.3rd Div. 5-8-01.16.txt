INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Kaiholena Subdivision project in the Kaiholena area in the District of North
Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end
of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS

District of North Kohaia on the Island of Hawaii Based on the information provided it is
proposed to develop the project site into an agricultural farm lot development The project will
involve a subdivision with about 12 lots ranging in size from about 15 to 32 acres each The
lots will be turned over on an as is basis with the individual owners being responsible for
individual lot grading and installation of individual wastewater systems
The project development will involve the installation of approximately 2 400 lineal feet

tank will measure approximately 20 feet in diameter and 24 feet in height and will be of steel
construction with a 6 inch thick concrete floor slab The finish floor elevation for the new water
tank has not been set at the time this report was prepared

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The project site is underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of
Mauna Kea which were deposited during the Pleistocene Epoch Stearns and Macdonald
1946 The Hamakua Volcanic Series is typically composed of basaltic lava flows which are
mantled by a surface layer of palagonitized ash locally referred to as Pahala Ash The ash
soil at the project site differs from the Pahala Ash found in other locations on the Island of
Hawaii in that the in situ moisture content is generally lower and the soil exists in a friable
state
Based on our experience in the area the lava flows underlying the site are of the a a
form which is characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly surface of fragmental clinker A dense
core of massive basalt which frequently has a thinner less continuous layer of clinker below
the massive core underlies the clinker The clinker surface of the lavas at the site have been
mantled by the Pahala Ash as discussed above
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located off Kawaihae Mahukona Road in the Kaiholena area in
the District of North Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The project site is presently vacant land
vegetated with some trees and dry grasses Access roads serving the area consist of a thin
layer of gravel over cobbles and boulders in a very dry and bony state
The terrain at the site is gently rolling with existing ground surface elevations ranging
from about 220 to 245 feet Mean Sea Level MSL along Kawaihae Mahukona Road to over
350 feet MSL in higher grounds The general trend of the surface drainage is in a westerly
direction

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly
cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials
should be removed and disposed of properly off site or in a designated area to reduce
potential contamination of the excavated materials The volcanic ash soils encountered
in cut areas may be stockpiled and reused as a source of general fill material provided
that the recommendations presented in the Fill Materials subsection are followed
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm natural material and
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill materials The
excavated soft soils should be properly disposed of off site
After clearing and grubbing areas beneath future pavements should be
over excavated where necessary to the bottom of the aggregate base course layer
If zones of soft loose or saturated soils are encountered at the over excavated
subgrade level deeper over excavation may be required to expose the underlying firm
GEOLABS INC

material The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with compacted fill
materials
The over excavated subgrade should be moisture conditioned to about the optimum
moisture content and proof rolled to about 90 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Due to the generally thixotropic
nature of the surface ash soils use of the vibratory action of the compactor should be
carefully controlled to avoid disturbing the ash soils In addition moisture conditioning
of the ash soils should be carefully controlled to avoid over wetting of the soils
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the subgrade should
be proof rolled and a filter fabric such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent should be placed
on the subgrade prior to placement of fill material Where shrinkage cracks are noted
after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened
to close the cracks or the subgrade should be scarified moisture conditioned and
recompacted Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas
and replacement with well compacted engineered fill

be ripped open cleaned of debris and then backfilled with compacted fill material
Loose areas or cavities disclosed during clearing and proof rolling operations should
be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill materials

Fill Materials
We recommend that the upper 3 feet of fill below the pavement finish grades be capped
with structural fill materials to serve as a choking layer for the larger sized general fill
below and also to facilitate construction of the pavement section above Structural fill
materials should consist of well graded granular materials less than 6 inches in
maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the
compacted mass If the excavated materials do not contain sufficient fines to produce
fragments or boulders should be considered to provide the required gradation and
particle sizes to develop a well graded material Imported material if necessary
should be observed and or tested by Geolabs for its suitability prior to being transported
to the site for the intended use A typical section showing the recommended types of
fill materials is provided on Plate 3 of this report
Where appropriate such as within utility corridors the thickness of the structural fill
layer may be increased to a minimum of 4 feet or 1 foot below the invert elevation of
utilities to facilitate construction of the underground utilities It should be noted that the
requirements for trench backfill materials call for utilizing 6 inch minus materials such
as structural fill material to backfill the trenches
General fill and backfill materials below the structural fill capping layer may consist of
well graded granular materials of which the majority portion are less than 12 inches in
size with an absolute maximum dimension of 18 inches The on site cut and or
stockpiled material if less than 18 inches in maximum dimension may be used as
general fill or backfill materials other than for trench backfill provided that they are
processed to meet the above gradation requirements
Volcanic ash soils generated from cut areas should be suitable for use as general fill

moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to at
least 95 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills should be placed in
level lifts not exceeding 18 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction
should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of
acceptable compaction equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
majority of the rocks or 5 feet whichever is less Placement should be by
haul trucks traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the
currently placed lift Spreading of the rock fill should be by bulldozer to facilitate
seating of the rock The rock fill should be watered heavily during placement by
water trucks traversing in front of the current rock lift face and spraying water
continuously during placement Compaction equipment such as a Caterpillar D 9
bulldozer or similar heavy compaction equipment providing suitable energy to
achieve the required compaction should be utilized The number of passes usually
six to eight passes to be made to achieve proper compaction should be determined
at the on set of site grading by the performance of a trial compaction test or by plate
bearing tests Subsequent layers of rock fill above the previously placed rock fill
should provide a choking layer of smaller sized boulder materials to fill in the voids
between the boulders of the preceding rock fill layer
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rocks boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number
of passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should
GEOLABS INC
W O 4502 00 Page 14
be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation
of a representative from Geolabs Based on this testing program the number of
passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction For
preliminary estimating purposes compaction may be accomplished by using a large
vibratory drum roller having a static weight of at least 15 tons traveling at a speed
of about 200 to 300 feet per minute The roller should pass over the fill surface at

Excavation
Based on our field exploration at the site the project site is generally underlain by
medium dense to dense basalt cobbles and gravel with volcanic ash at the existing
ground surface The basalt cobbles and gravel with volcanic ash is underlain by
medium hard to hard basalt formations with occasional clinker layers It is anticipated
that the volcanic rock may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment
such as ripping with large bulldozers where the rock is near the existing ground
surface However excavations into the deeper formations will likely require the use of
hoerams chipping or blasting
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials is based on

performed at the site We recommend that contractors be encouraged to examine
the site conditions and the boring data to make their own interpretation

Slab On Grade
Based on our field exploration the proposed water tank site is generally underlain
by a surface layer of volcanic ash over dense clinkers and hard basalt rock formation at
shallow depths The surface volcanic ash soil when wetted has relatively low strength
characteristics and when in a dry state is susceptible to erosion In addition if the in situ
moisture contents are high enough they become thixotropic i e they lose strength
temporarily when remolded and or disturbed Therefore we recommend that the surface
volcanic ash within the water tank footprint and 2 feet beyond be removed to expose the
GEOLABS INC
W O 4502 00 Page 18
underlying dense clinkers or hard basalt rock formation As previously indicated cavities
are commonly encountered in the basaltic lava flows Therefore the excavated subgrades
should be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of eight passes
to assist in detecting and possibly collapsing near surface cavities
In addition a minimum of 6 inches of compacted aggregate subbase should be
provided below the bottom of the tank floor for more uniform bearing support The 6 inches
of compacted aggregate subbase course should extend a minimum of 2 feet laterally beyond
the limits of the tank structure The aggregate subbase course should consist of crushed
basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction For the design
of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of about 250 pounds per square inch per
inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted aggregate subbase course The area
adjacent to the water tank slab should be backfilled tightly against the slab with compacted
on site soils and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water
ponding around the slab and foundation

Based on the information provided asphaltic concrete pavements will be used for the
proposed roadways planned for the subdivision development In general we anticipate that
the vehicle loading for the proposed roadways for this project would consist primarily of
passenger vehicles and light trucks Therefore we have assumed generally light to medium
traffic loading conditions for pavement design purposes We understand that the following
pavement section is generally required by the County of Hawaii Department of Public Works
County of Hawaii Standard Detail R 34
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
4 0 Inch Base Course
6 0 inch Select Borrow Subbase
12 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness over Subgrade
In general the purpose of the subbase course required in the County of Hawaii
standard section is to serve as a foundation or stabilizing course such that the subsequent
GEOLABS INC

base and surfacing layers may be constructed on top of an inferior subgrade soil The
standard pavement section does not take into consideration good quality subgrade conditions
Since the subgrade conditions along the proposed roadway alignment at the proposed
subdivision project will consist primarily of basalt cobbles and gravel with some volcanic ash
compacted granular fill material or basalt rock formation it is our opinion that a subbase
course for stabilization foundation purposes is not necessary due to the high quality of the
subgrade materials anticipated at the subject site If the roadway subgrade exposes poorer
subgrade soils volcanic ash at the site we recommend that the poorer subgrade soils
volcanic ash be removed and be replaced with structural fill material 6 inch minus material
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

aggregate base course layer can be considered as a subbase material since its pavement

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
seven borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 7 extending to depths ranging from
about 12 to 20 feet below the existing ground surface A bulk sample designated as
Bulk 1 was obtained from the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics The approximate locations of the test borings and bulk sample are shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment
equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 7
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
W O INC SEPTEMBER2000 Page A 1
Appendix A Continued
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be
50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical Handbook
of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Two one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples to
evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils under different surcharge loads
The results of these tests are summarized on Plate B 1
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio Test ASTM D 1883 was performed on the
near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils The test
results are presented on Plate B 2
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on the typical
near surface soils encountered at the site to evaluate the moisture content and dry density
relationship The test results are presented on Plate B 3

APPENDIX C
Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the in situ soils were evaluated by performing a total of two falling
head and three constant head tests at Boring 4 and 6 The tests were performed
at depths of about 20 feet below the existing ground surface
During the falling head test the borehole was carefully filled with water to the top of
the borehole The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon
which to base the calculation of the permeability values of the subsoils
The constant head test was performed by pumping water into the borehole until the
water surface stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time The water
height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which to base the
calculation of the subsoil permeability The test results of the field permeability tests are
presented on Plates C 1 through C 5
