Dear Mr. Leong: 
This letter provides our geotechnical engineering recommendations for the Hilea and 
Ninole Stream Bypass Bridges project located in the Kau area on the Island of Hawaii.  It 
should be noted that borings were not performed for this project.  Our site observations and 
experience in the general area were used for the development of our recommendations. 
Anticipated Subsurface Conditions 
Based on our site observations, we anticipate that the abutments at the Hilea Stream 
Bypass Bridge will be underlain by basalt rock formation mixed with cobbles, boulders, and 
volcanic ash.  For the north abutment at the Ninole Stream Bypass Bridge, we anticipate 
that the underlying soil will consist of basalt cobbles, boulders and volcanic ash.  We 
anticipate that the south abutment will be underlain by a basalt rock formation.  We do not 
anticipate encountering groundwater in the abutment excavations.  
Bypass Bridge Abutment Structures 
Due to the potential of encountering voids/cavities and soft/loose soils at the 
abutment subgrade level, we recommend that proof-rolling be performed on the abutment 
finished subgrade.  The proof-rolling should consist of at least 6 passes of a 10-ton roller or 
equal to detect cavities and soft/loose soils near the finished subgrade surface.  
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site, we recommend 
utilizing shallow spread and/or continuous footings for foundation support of the new 
abutment walls planned at the site. Based on our site observations, we believe that the 
following values may be used to evaluate the bearing support, sliding resistance, and 
passive pressure resistance of the planned retaining walls based on LRFD design methods.

Geosynthetic Reinforced Soil (GRS) Wall 
We understand that it is desired to construct GRS wall structures behind the 
abutments for the bypass bridges. The GRS wall structures will be about 7 to 8 feet in 
height. 
The GRS wall structures are constructed utilizing high-density polyethylene 
reinforcement, or other geosynthetic reinforcing elements with layers of compacted 
granular fill. The reinforcing elements are installed to reinforce the backfill zone and 
improve the shear strength of the reinforced soil zone. This composite system essentially 
forms a gravity structure with an ability to tolerate significant total and differential 
settlements. The GRS wall structures are desirable due to the ease and quickness of 
construction, high load carrying capacity, and cost savings.

We believe that the recommended values presented in the “Bypass Bridge Abutment 
Structures” section may be used to evaluate the bearing support, sliding resistance, and 
passive pressure resistance of the GRS wall foundation. 
In general, the exposed subgrades should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and recompacted to at least 
95 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior 
to the placement of the reinforced soil foundation.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
established in accordance with ASTM D1557 (AASHTO T-180). Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the structure excavations 
should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm materials.  The over-excavation 
may be backfilled with the on-site soils compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction, or the bottom of footing may be extended down to the underlying competent 
materials. 
Based on our analyses, a minimum reinforcement length equal to 1.2 times the wall 
height is recommended. The reinforcing geotextiles should have a minimum ultimate tensile 
strength of 4,800 lb/ft.  The reinforcing geotextiles should be spaced at 12-inch vertical 
spacing.  The cut slope behind the GRS system should be sloped back at two horizontal to 
one vertical (2H:1V) inclination.  
We believe that the reinforced fill material for the GRS structure should consist of 
imported aggregate base course material.  The reinforced fill material should have an angle 
of internal friction of at least 40 degrees when tested by the standard direct shear test in 
accordance with AASHTO T-236 Test Methods (ASTM D3080) and using rock correction 
procedures. The tested sample should be compacted to 95 percent relative compaction at 
moisture contents above the optimum. 
Reinforced fill materials should be placed in level loose lifts not exceeding 8 inches 
in loose thickness and compacted to at least 95 percent of the maximum dry density 
established in accordance with AASHTO T-180 (ASTM D1557) at moisture contents above 
the optimum. 
The abutment walls should be well-drained to reduce the potential for build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures.  Geocomposite drains along the excavation slope connected to drain 
pipes extending to the wall face may be considered.
