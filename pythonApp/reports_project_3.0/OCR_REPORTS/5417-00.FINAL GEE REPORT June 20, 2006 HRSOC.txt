2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during
which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded
against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late
Pleistocene Epoch The dips of the lava beds generally range from about 3 to
5 degrees The lava flows on the plateau have weathered to depths of 5 to 100 feet and
are characterized by the red colors of the soil The deep narrow canyons such as
Waikakalaua Kipapa and Kaukonahua Gulches are a result of this weathering
2 2 Climate
The Hawaiian Islands is located in the tropics The islands climate includes mild
temperatures throughout the year moderate humidity persistence of northeasterly
trade winds significant differences in rainfall within short distances and infrequent
severe storms Most of the State of Hawaii has only two seasons The two seasons
are summer between May and October and winter between October and April
The average temperature on the Island of Oahu ranges from 74 to 88 degrees
Fahrenheit with humidity of about 53 percent during the day The project site is located
in an interior lowland The interior lowland is an area of sometimes intense local
afternoon rain from well developed clouds that form as a result of local heating of the
land during the day The annual rainfall is about 25 to 50 inches in most areas and as
much as 60 inches in some Most of the rain falls in the winter season between
October and April

Residual Soils
Residual soils are completely weathered volcanic rocks that have lost all visible
rock texture and characteristics The residual soils at the project site are typically
brown to reddish brown in color The residual soils may be mottled with various
colors Residual soils are comprised mainly of clayey materials with a stiff to very
stiff consistency Typically residual soils occur overlying saprolitic soils or
weathered basalt formation It should be noted that relict boulders of hard rock may
be encountered in residual soils In addition there is potential for encountering
hard basalt rock ledges in residual soils because the volcanic rock may not have
weathered uniformly throughout the rock formation
Extremely Weathered Basalt Saprolitic Soils
Extremely weathered volcanic rock is referred to as saprolitic soils or saprolites
Saprolitic soils represent rock that has been reduced by weathering to soil like
components with weathered rock fragments The soil material retains the remnant
rock texture such as layering vesicularity and some fracture patterns Saprolitic
soils are typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks and are composed
mainly of silty materials The coloring scheme of saprolitic soils depends on the
type of parent volcanic rock from which the soils are derived and the degree of
chemical weathering that the material has experienced Because the parent
material does not weather uniformly throughout the formation relict boulders of
hard rock and or hard rock ledges may be encountered within saprolitic soils

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most of the earthquakes are so small that only sensitive equipment can detect the
small tremors However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt
and a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii
over 90 percent of earthquakes are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic
activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the
Island of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the
Island of Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have
occurred in the County of Maui The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands
of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu For example several small
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of
In addition some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly
damaged as a result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 M7
Over the last 150 years of recorded history we are not aware of reported
earthquakes greater than Magnitude 6 occurring on the Island of Oahu We

Liquefaction
Liquefaction occurs when a build up of excess pore water pressure is induced on
saturated cohesionless soils resulting in a substantial loss of strength The most
common cause of this phenomenon is the cyclic loading due to earthquakes This
process may cause both vertical and horizontal movements in the soil and if not
confined can result in significant deformations Loose uniformly graded
fine grained sands and loose silts with little cohesion are most susceptible to
liquefaction Generally liquefaction is not likely to occur if the loose deposit is
deeper than 50 feet below the ground surface The greater overburden pressure
can be sufficient to prevent liquefaction in deeper deposits

backfill material within the trench for the pipe and a coefficient of friction of 0 30
may be used For reinforced concrete pipes earth forces upon the pipe increase
rapidly with the width of the trench Therefore the width of the trench should be
kept to a minimum Traffic loads on the buried pipes also should be considered for
the portion of the pipes located in roadways
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great effect upon the load
supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore pipe bedding is an important design
consideration In general we recommend providing a bedding layer consisting of
6 inches of free draining granular material ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below
the pipes The granular bedding should extend beyond the sides of the pipe a
minimum width of one fourth the outside diameter of the pipe Prior to placing the
bedding material a qualified engineer should observe the excavated trench bottom
to confirm that firm materials are exposed at the bottom of the trench or if the
bedding material should be extended deeper
Backfill
The first zone of backfill initial trench backfill extending from the bedding material
to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes should consist of free draining
granular material ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation The purpose of using the
free draining granular material is to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes
from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material be
used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and
to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils having
particles less than 6 inches in largest dimension or select granular material The
backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in

Settlement
Primary settlement of underground pipes is normally caused by the difference in the
unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted
backfill material placed over the pipes The net increase in loading will cause some
minor settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert Based on our
estimates primary settlement of less than 0 5 inches is anticipated for the project
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good
workmanship will be engaged during construction
3 9 Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive
landscape watering near the foundations and slabs also should be avoided
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations because ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or
slab edges immediately after the concrete has set to reduce the potential for
appreciable water infiltration into the subsurface In addition drainage swales should be
provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface runoff away
from foundations and slabs

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling 68 borings extending to depths ranging from about 11 5 to 101 5 feet below
the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the General Site
Plan Plate 2 The approximate boring and bulk sample locations also are shown on the
enlarged Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 7 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers
Materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by an engineer or geologist who observed the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with
the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of
the materials encountered in the borings drilled are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 68
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Nine one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
of the near surface soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The
test results are presented on Plate B 1
Four Expansion Index of Soil tests ASTM D 4829 were performed on selected
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface
soils The test is a standardized test used to determine the expansive characteristics of
the soil The test results are presented on Plate B 2
Fifteen Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to determine the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil
classification Results of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depth Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on
Plates B 3 and B 4
Nine Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics The test results are presented
on Plates B 5 through B 13
Four Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the soils The test
results and the stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 14 through B 17
Five unconsolidated undrained Triaxial Compression tests ASTM D 2850 were
performed on selected samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the soils
The unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression test results are presented on
Plates B 18 through B 22
Eight laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were
performed on the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 23 through B 30
Three Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on the
near surface soils to determine the dry density and moisture content relationships The
test results are presented on Plates B 31 through B 33

Nine Soil Resistivity Tests using the Soil Box Method ASTM G 57 were
performed on selected soil samples retrieved from our field exploration program to
determine the resistivity values at or near the in situ moisture contents and the minimum
resistivity values In addition four Soil Resistivity Tests and four pH tests
EPA SW9045B were performed on selected soil samples The test results are
summarized on Plates B 34 and B 35
