In accordance with the scope of services outlined in our Fee Proposal dated 
June 12, 2014, we performed percolation testing services in support of the improvements 
to the HECO Iwilei Baseyard in the Iwilei area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
This letter report summarizes work performed and presents our findings and results for the 
percolation testing performed at the project site. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located at 855 North Nimitz highway within the existing HECO 
Iwilei baseyard in the Iwilei area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The site is on the makai 
side of North Nimitz highway adjacent to the access road to Pier 31. The project location 
and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. We understand a 
drainage basin is contemplated as part of improvements to the baseyard.  
The purpose of our exploration was to perform percolation testing for the design of 
the drainage basin planned at the site. To determine the infiltration characteristics of the 
subsurface soils, two field infiltration tests designated as P-1 and P-2, were conducted in 
general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of the State of Maryland 
Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” 
(rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with other state’s procedures and may 
generally be considered an industry standard.

Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling two borings, designated as 
Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 6.5 and 7 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on the borings, the project site generally is underlain by surface fill materials 
placed over lagoonal deposits. Surface fills consisting of medium dense silty/clayey sands 
generally extended down to about 3 to 4 feet below the ground surface. The surface fill 
layer is underlain by lagoonal deposits consisting of loose clayey sands and gravel 
extending down to the maximum depth explored of about 7 feet below the ground surface.  
We encountered groundwater at depths ranging from about 6.5 to 7.0 feet below 
the ground surface. The groundwater levels encountered generally correspond to 
elevations of about +1.0 to +1.5 feet Mean Sea Level at the time of our field exploration. 
Due to the proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean, water levels are expected to 
vary with tidal fluctuations and storm events. It should be noted that water levels also may 
vary with seasonal rainfall, time of the year, and other factors.  
Field Infiltration Testing 
The field infiltration tests were performed by augering two borings, designated as 
P-1 and P-2 adjacent to Borings B-1 and B-2, respectively. The approximate infiltration 
test locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The infiltration test holes were augered 
to the selected test depth, generally about 3 feet below the existing ground surface. Upon 
reaching the test depth, a 4-inch inside diameter PVC solid casing was set to the bottom of 
the drilled hole to allow infiltration only through the soil exposed at the bottom of the 
boring.  
Falling head infiltration tests were performed to determine the average infiltration 
rates of the underlying subsurface materials. Each test consisted of four trials of filling the 
casing with 24 inches of water and taking periodic readings over a 1-hour trial period. The 
infiltration rates are then calculated based on the results of the fourth and last trial for each 
test location. The calculated infiltration rates at each test location are summarized in the 
following table. The field infiltration test results are presented on Plates 5 and 6.

It should be noted that the infiltration values presented above are the rates of 
infiltration through the soil exposed at the bottom of the tested borehole and reduces the 
lateral spreading of the water in the subsurface materials. Based on the results of the field 
infiltration testing, the average infiltration rates at these locations indicate that an 
infiltration/detention system and similar methods of storm water management would not be 
feasible for the project. 
Limitations 
The recommendations submitted in this letter report are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field borings and infiltration tests. Variations of the 
subsurface conditions may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not 
become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be 
necessary to re-evaluate the findings and discussions provided in this letter report. 
The field boring and infiltration test locations indicated herein are approximate, 
having been estimated using a hand-held Garmin™ eTrex Vista HCx. Elevations noted on 
the borings were estimated from Google Earth™. The physical locations and elevations of 
the borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods 
used. 
This geotechnical engineering exploration conducted at the project site was not 
intended to investigate the potential presence of hazardous materials existing at the 
project site. It should be noted that the equipment, techniques, and personnel used to 
conduct a geo-environmental exploration differ substantially from those applied in 
geotechnical engineering.
