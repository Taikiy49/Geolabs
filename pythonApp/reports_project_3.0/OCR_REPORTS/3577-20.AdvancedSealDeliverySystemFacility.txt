REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern tip of Pearl City Peninsula in Pearl
Harbor on the south central coastal plain of Oahu The geologic conditions underlying
the Pearl Harbor lochs were the result of a complex series of depositional and erosional
events due to glacio eustatic sea level changes and post erosional volcanic activities
during the Pleistocene Epoch
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly
bedded basaltic lava flows Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano
alluvial materials were deposited in an estuarine and deltaic environment in the area of
Pearl Harbor The sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 100 meters below
and rising about 30 meters above the present sea level As Oahu sank coral reef grew
around the shorelines keeping pace with the subsidence of the island
During the Recent Epoch as sea level rose to its current stand the Middle Loch
area was covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of cohesive soils with
localized granular soils over the underlying alluvium Land development and reclamation
projects in the past 40 to 60 years have added man made fills along the shoreline
bringing the site to its present configuration

Liquefaction
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In this
process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such as during
an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in
pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build
up of pore water pressure the effective stress internal strength of the soil is reduced
Under sustained vibration the pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden
pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as
a viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both
horizontal and vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant
deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that liquefaction
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3577 20 Page 14
will not occur if the deposit is greater than 15 meters below the ground surface In deeper
deposits the greater overburden pressure is generally sufficient to prevent liquefaction
from occurring The major factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of a soil
deposit are as follows
1 Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
3 Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake
In general the subsurface information obtained from the on land borings appears
to indicate that the site is underlain by medium dense to dense coral sands and gravel
_ overlying stiff alluvial soils Based on our laboratory test results the underlying granular
soils are generally moderately to well graded Therefore on the basis of our exploration
data and laboratory test results we believe that the subsoils at this site are not highly
susceptible to liquefaction

Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy
between 67 800 and 94 900 newton meters The hammer should be equipped
with an energy control level such that driving within the loose soft soil may be
controlled at a lower energy to reduce tensile stress development within the piles

Subsoil Permeability
The in situ permeability of the underlying subsoils near the bottom elevation of the
proposed water tank was evaluated by performing a constant head permeability
test in Boring No 8 The calculated permeability of the tested subsoils is on the
order of about 1 4x107 meters per second Details of the field permeability test
and the test data are summarized in Appendix E
It is our opinion that the above calculated permeability value should be used with
caution due to the normally heterogeneous nature of the coralline subsoils The
actual subsoil permeability may range broadly and also vary locally in terms of
orders of magnitude It should be noted that the permeability of the subsoils at
the site appears to be highly permeable based on the field test conducted

Therefore special attention should be given to the site specific dewatering plan
for the proposed water tank structure
Dewatering Method
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and
he she should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be
required during construction depending on the conditions encountered The
dewatering method selected should have minimal impact on the groundwater level
surrounding the proposed excavation As previously indicated the underlying
coralline and granular materials at this site are highly permeable and capable of
transmitting large quantities of water
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents
should be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural
groundwater table and or to exclude the water from the excavations to allow
construction of the proposed structures under safe and dry conditions These
works or systems may include but are not limited to grouting cut off walls tremie
concrete plugs or any combination of the above and or other possible methods
Based on previous experience with similar site conditions an interlocking sheet
pile cofferdam with a tremie concrete plug at the bottom of the proposed
excavation has worked successfully in reducing the amount of seepage water It
should be noted that heavy well pumping without cut off walls under similar site
conditions has been attempted but it was found to be unsuccessful in dewatering
the excavation in the very permeable coralline deposits Therefore a sheet pile
cofferdam with a tremie concrete plug should be considered for the construction
cost estimate of this project Where used the tremie concrete plug should be
designed to resist the anticipated hydrostatic uplift force

Dewatering Considerations
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of a
suitable method of dewatering
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or damage to

b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide safe
and dry working conditions in the excavation
Cc The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modifications

Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils

Corrosion Protection
Two samples of the subsurface soils retrieved from our field exploration were
tested for pH and minimum resistivity The results of these tests are presented on

Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Two sets of pH ASTM D 2976 and minimum resistivity ASTM G 57 tests were
performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration The results of
these tests are summarized on Plate C 1
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are presented on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentation of the test
results is provided on Plate C 2
Six sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on Plates C 3 and C 4
Three direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plates C 5
through C 7
Four unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on cohesive
soil samples to evaluate the compressive strength of the soils The results are presented
on Plate C 8
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The test results are presented on Plates C 9 and C 10
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates C 11 and C 12

As part of our scope of work on this project the near surface soils underlying the
project site were sampled during our field exploration and tested for indication of potential
contamination by leakage from existing underground fuel lines or other external paths
A total of 21 soil samples were retrieved at selected depths from Boring 3 11
12 and 24 through 32 for environmental laboratory testing The sampling tools were
washed with Liquinox detergent solution prior to each soil sampling event
The soil samples retrieved were sealed placed in an ice chest after retrieval and
were transported to a laboratory for analyses The environmental laboratory testing was
performed by Environmental Laboratory of the Pacific and included Total Purgeable
Petroleum Hydrocarbons TPPH with BTEX Distinction Total Extractable Petroleum
Hydrocarbons TEPH Polynuclear Aromatic Hydrocarbons Cadmium and Lead
Results of the environmental laboratory tests are summarized on Plates D 1 and D 2 with
