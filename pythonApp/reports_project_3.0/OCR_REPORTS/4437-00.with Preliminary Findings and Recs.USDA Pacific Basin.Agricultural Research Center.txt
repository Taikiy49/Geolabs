Project Considerations
The project site is located west of Komohana Street in the vicinity of the University
of Hawaii Hilo campus on the Island of Hawaii It is proposed to construct a new
agricultural research center We understand that the research building will include
development of an approximately 40 acre site to include construction of buildings roads
drainage waste disposal facilities and parking areas Grading plans and layouts were not
available at the time this letter was prepared However based on the elevation range of
approximately 70 feet across the site it appears that significant cuts may be required for
the proposed development
Existing Site Conditions
The subject lot is located west of Komohana Street and south of the intersection
with Nowelo Street The lot is rectangular in shape bounded by Komohana Street to the
east and undeveloped land to the south west and north A utility easement traverses the
southern end of the lot in an east west direction and contains utility poles with overhead
power lines and exposed abandoned underground waterlines

In general the project site slopes down from the west side of the lot towards

Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration at the time of this preliminary recommendation consisted of
drilling and sampling seven borings ranging in depths from approximately 25 to 50 feet
below the existing ground surface Approximate boring locations are shown on the
attached Site Plan Plate 1 Based on our subsurface exploration the project site is
generally underlain by medium hard to very hard dense to vesicular basalt rock formation
In Boring No 2 which is located in the southern portion of the lot a large cavity
approximately 5 5 feet in height was encountered at approximately 34 feet below the
ground surface Voids generally averaged 0 5 to 2 feet in height and were encountered
approximately 25 to 45 feet below ground surface in all of the other borings Clinker
layers were encountered in varying thicknesses and at varying depths in all of the
borings The clinker materials generally consisted of highly weathered basalt fragments
in a silt matrix An ash layer was encountered in Boring No 4 approximately 36 5 feet
below ground surface and about 2 feet thick Detailed descriptions of the materials
encountered are shown on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 7
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 5 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 70 percent and would be shown
as REC 70 Core runs during our field exploration were typically 5 feet in length

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 3 0 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the

Discussion and Preliminary Recommendations
Based on the information provided we understand that excavations and fills will be
required for the proposed new agricultural research center However we also understand
that the building layout and extent of the excavations and fills have not been finalized at
this time
Site Grading
Excavations are anticipated in the proposed construction of the new agricultural
research center Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by
medium hard to very hard basalt rock formation The rippability of a rock mass is a
function of the relative hardness of the rock its relative quality brittleness and fissile
characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD would be very difficult to rip and would
probably require more arduous methods of excavation Our field exploration disclosed
that the project site is generally underlain by basalt rock ranging from medium hard to hard
and very hard in localized areas Therefore it is anticipated that the basalt rock may be
excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such as ripping with large bulldozers
Excavations in hard basalt in localized areas and into the deeper formations may require
the use of hoerams chipping or blasting
If fills are required for the subject project the on site excavated materials may be
used as general backfill materials provided they are screened of deleterious material
processed and reduced to a relatively well graded select granular material with a
maximum size of about 6 inches

Foundation Support and Preparation
Based on the competent subsoil conditions encountered during our field
exploration it is our opinion that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or
continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed new buildings An
allowable bearing value of 3 000 pounds per square foot psf on fill and on
basalt rock may be used
As previously indicated our field exploration encountered a cavity in some of the
borings drilled at the site Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lava flows
The cavities encountered were about 0 5 to 5 5 feet in height Therefore in order to
reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities
below proposed buildings we recommend that the building subgrades be proof rolled
and a program of cavity probing and grouting be conducted at footing locations for the
proposed new buildings
After clearing and grubbing building pad subgrades should be proof rolled with a
minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller or other similar heavy equipment prior to concrete or
fill placement to aid in detecting and collapsing near surface cavities Loose areas and or
cavities disclosed during clearing and proof rolling operations should be excavated to
expose firm materials and the over excavation should be backfilled with compacted
engineered fill
After proof rolling operations we recommend a program of cavity probing and
grouting be performed at footing locations For preliminary estimating purposes we
recommend that probe holes be drilled at the footing locations and or spaced at a
maximum of 10 feet for continuous footings The probe holes should be at least 3 inches
in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of footing
elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing
operation additional probes should be drilled at closer spacings to help delineate the
vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void Following the drilling of each probe
hole a 2 inch PVC pipe should be inserted into the probe holes to preserve the open hole
integrity for grouting A field engineer or geologist from Geolabs should be present to log
the probe holes and to determine the locations of additional probe holes needed
The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement
grout We recommend that a low strength sand cement grout mixture with an
approximately 6 to 9 inch slump range pumped at low to moderate pressures be utilized
for the grouting operation
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of cavities
and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or changed
conditions are observed

1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed USDA Pacific Basin Agricultural Research Center in the Hilo
area on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses
for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the
end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is located west of Komohana Street in the vicinity of the University
of Hawaii at Hilo campus on the Island of Hawaii It is proposed to construct a new
agricultural research center We understand that the research building will include
development of an approximately 30 acre site to include construction of buildings roads
drainage waste disposal facilities and parking areas Grading plans and layouts were not
available at the time this report was prepared However based on the elevation range of
approximately 70 feet across the site it appears that significant cuts may be required for
the proposed development

would be used to formulate a summary of the soil rock conditions for the design of

objectives we propose an exploration program generally consisting of the following tasks

2 3 Geology
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes which include the following Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna
Kea which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in
1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield
Volcano which has been built up by successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials The near surface lava flows are from the Mauna Loa Volcano
overlying the Mauna Kea Shield The lava flows of the Mauna Kea Shield are believed
to be Pliocene to Pleistocene in age and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua
Volcanic Series The near surface lava flows of the Mauna Loa Shield may be on the
order of only 750 to 1 500 years old
The lava formation at the site appears to be pahoehoe flow which is
characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava These
cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava

3 2 Building Foundation
Based on the competent subsoil conditions encountered during our field
exploration it is our opinion that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or
continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed new buildings An
allowable bearing value of on fill and on basalt rock may be used
for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Where footings are located adjacent to slopes or on slopes the footings should be
embedded to a sufficient depth to provide a minimum horizontal set back distance of at
least 8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope If
foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be embedded

trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines

footings bearing on engineered fill materials or in situ basalt rock formation as
recommended herein to be less than one inch total with differential settlements on the
order of one half inch

Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads within a
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be considered in the
design For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall
a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 27 percent of the vertical
surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of the wall which is free to deflect
cantilever may be used in design For walls that are restrained a rectangular
distribution equal to 44 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the
entire height of the wall may be used for design Additional analyses during
design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of point loads and line
loads

Since the project site is located on the seismically active Island of Hawaii
Seismic Zone 4 in accordance with the Uniform Building Code dynamic lateral

forces are accounted for in the design of the retaining structures
Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system should consist of about 1 cubic foot of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
wrapped with non woven filter fabric at each of the weephole locations The
weepholes should be spaced not more than 6 feet apart Unless covered by
concrete or asphaltic concrete the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of
relatively impervious materials such as the on site soils to reduce the potential
for significant water infiltration behind the walls

3 6 Percolation Tests
Percolation tests were performed in Boring Nos 11 and 12 to evaluate the
subsurface permeability conditions at the site The constant head method was used for
the percolation tests After the boring was drilled water was injected into the boring The
flow rate was recorded by timing and calculating the volume of water injected as a
relatively constant flow rate was achieved The head levels within the test borings were
monitored by an electric water level sounder during the injection of water Results of the
constant head percolation tests are presented in Appendix C The test indicated that the
basalt formation have coefficient of permeability k in the range of about 0 149 to
0 087 feet per minute
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the basalt rock
formation at the site is highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant
voids in the lava or the presence of embedded clinker seams which greatly affect the
disposal capacity of a dry well or seepage pit In general the greater the number of
clinker seams and or voids encountered by the dry well or seepage pit excavation the
higher the overall permeability and disposal capacity

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling sixteen
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 16 to depths ranging from about 25 to
50 feet below the existing ground surface using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers and rock coring tools The approximate locations of the borings
are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 16
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals

APPENDIX B
Field Percolation Testing
The permeability of the in situ soils was estimated by performing a total of three
constant head percolation tests in selected boring locations at depths ranging from
approximately 35 5 to 50 feet below the existing ground surface The test results are
presented on Plates B 1 through B 4
The constant head tests were performed by pumping water into the borehole until
the water surface had stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time
The water height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which
to base calculation of the subsoil permeability
