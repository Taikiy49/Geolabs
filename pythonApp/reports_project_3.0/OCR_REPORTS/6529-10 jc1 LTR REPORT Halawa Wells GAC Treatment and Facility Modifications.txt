PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is desired to improve the existing Halawa Wells Facility with new GAC treatment
units and facility modifications A design alternative study was previously conducted as
part of the feasibility study We understand that the detailed design of the modification
project requires refining design parameters assumed in the design alternative study
Based on the information provided we understand that the modification consists of
installing GAC units and constructing backwash tank booster pumps microfiltration and
filter storage The modifications will require construction of new retaining walls supported
by the soil nail system The new soil nail retaining wall will require soil rock excavation up
to 20 feet
The rock excavation may induce ground vibration which may potentially cause
damage to the aging existing water tank Additional field exploration consisting of seismic
refraction testing was used to estimate the rock hardness associated to assess the
potential vibration during rock excavation

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the majority of the western one third of the
island The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Ice Age in age and forms the bulk of the eastern two thirds of the island After a long
period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the
Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and
tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano The project area is
generally composed of basaltic rock built by extrusion of the lavas of the Koolau Volcanic
Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular
basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical weathering of the Koolau lava
flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The weathering process has formed a
mantle of residual and saprolitic soils In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty
material while residual soils are more clayey Both residual and saprolitic soils are typical
of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The residual soils and saprolite grade to
basaltic rock formation with increased depth
FIELD EXPLORATION
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 101 through 103 The borings extended to about
20 to 30 feet below the existing ground surface using a track mounted drill rig equipped
with continuous flight solid stem augers and HQ casing The boring locations are shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D 2487
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1 of Appendix A
Graphic representations of the materials encountered and other observations such as
groundwater levels are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3 of
Appendix A
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Standard Test Method for Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Core samples of rock and or hard materials encountered at the project site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with

drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock and or hard
material left in the hollow core of the bit are mechanically recovered for examination and
description Rock cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of soil rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material
recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if
3 7 feet of material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be
74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD also is a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of

The seismic refraction surveys were conducted by Global Geophysics using a

survey was conducted along the top of the slope in two segments between the boring
locations as identified as Line 1 and Line 2 Details of the survey methodology and

Based on the results of the seismic refraction surveys performed and correlation
with the boring information the extremely to highly weathered soft rocks have
compressional P wave velocities generally ranging between 1 250 feet per second to
about 5 000 feet per second High P wave velocities were recorded in moderately
weathered hard basalt rock with an average of approximately 12 000 feet per second
The high velocities were recorded at the seismic refraction survey locations at
depths ranging from about 8 to 12 feet in segment Line 1 and from about 9 to 18 feet in
segment Line 2 The depths of the high velocities are equivalent to elevations from about
254 to 266 feet Mean Sea Level MSL in segment Line 1 and from about 262 to
271 feet MSL in segment Line 2
LABORATORY TESTS
Samples recovered from our field exploration were visually examined and
classified in our laboratory in Honolulu Hawaii Selected samples were tested to evaluate
the physical characteristics and engineering properties of the soils encountered The
laboratory testing program was formulated with the emphasis on evaluating the moisture
density plasticity and gradation of the soils encountered
Moisture Content and Unit Weight tests were performed on selected soil samples to
aid in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and correlating
the layers between the borings The moisture content tests were performed in general
accordance with ASTM D 2216 The unit weight tests were performed in general
accordance with ASTM D 2937 The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths in Appendix A
One Atterberg Limits test was performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate the
liquid limit LL plastic limit PL and plasticity index PI as an aid in classifying the soils
The Atterberg Limits test was performed in general accordance with ASTM D 4318
Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B 1 of Appendix B
To provide information on the particle size distribution of the soils encountered
two Sieve Analysis tests were performed to evaluate the gradation characteristics and to
aid in soil classification The tests were performed in accordance with ASTM C 117 and
C 136 Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B 2 of Appendix B

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
three borings extending to depths of approximately 20 to 30 feet below the existing ground
surface Borings were drilled on the northern side of the facility in the terraced sloping
ground behind the proposed soil nail retaining walls The approximate boring locations are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the project site is underlain by a thin layer of surface fill over extremely
to highly weathered soft rock about 10 to 17 feet thick and moderately weathered hard
basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depth drilled of about 30 feet below the
existing ground surface
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration It should be noted that groundwater levels are expected to fluctuate with
variation in rainfall surface water runoff groundwater withdrawal and other factors
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered from our field exploration are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3 of Appendix A
SEISMIC DESIGN CONDIERATIONS
We understand that the project site may be subjected to seismic activity and
seismic design considerations will need to be addressed in accordance with Section 1613
of the International Building Code 2006 Our field exploration generally encountered
very stiff and or dense extremely to highly weathered rock overlying moderately
weathered hard basalt formation extending to the maximum depth of about 30 feet below
the existing ground surface
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings and geology
setting of the site the project site may be classified as a Very Dense Soil and Soft Rock
Site Class C

This allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased

Foundations next to existing footing utility trenches or easements should be
embedded below an imaginary 45 degree plane extending upward from the bottom edge
of the utility trench or should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
The bottom of footing excavations in fill materials should be recompacted to at
least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing
surface prior to placing reinforcing steel and or concrete Soft and or loose materials
encountered at the bottom of footing excavations should be over excavated until dense
materials are exposed in the footing excavation The over excavation should be backfilled
with select granular fill materials moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Alternatively the
bottom of the footing may extend down to bear directly on the underlying competent
material
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of
0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted fill materials Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf assuming that the soils around the
footings are well compacted The passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of the soil
should be neglected unless covered by pavements or slabs
A Geolabs representative should observe the footing excavations prior to placing
the reinforcing steel and or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions and the
required embedment depths Observation of the foundation excavations should be
designated a Special Inspection item in accordance with Section 1704 of the

Based on the information provided we understand that retaining walls up to about
20 feet high will be required The soil nail retaining wall system would serve both as
temporary shoring of the excavation and the permanent retaining wall
A soil nail retaining wall system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize an excavation or slope A shotcrete facing is
generally applied to the face of the reinforced excavation providing the appearance of a
conventional concrete retaining wall The reinforcing bars provide both additional tensile
and shear strengths to the soil and reinforce the sidewall of the excavation The reinforced
soil mass behaves in a manner similar to a gravity retaining wall but lacks a footing In
general the face of the soil nail retaining wall system is slightly battered at a slope
inclination of about one horizontal to twelve vertical 1H 12V
Soil nail retaining walls are normally installed in a cut condition only If a fill
condition exists at the proposed soil nail retaining wall we recommend placing the fills first
to allow construction of the soil nail retaining wall entirely in a cut condition
Design of the soil nail retaining wall system will need to consider both the internal
and external stability of the reinforced soil mass The design of the internal stability
includes establishing the size spacing orientation and length of the grouted reinforcing
bars The external stability includes overall slope stability of the reinforced excavation
The geotechnical design parameters needed for evaluating the internal and external
stability of the soil nail retaining wall system are provided in the following table

Based on our slope stability analyses the length of the soil nails may be about

retaining wall system of up to about 20 feet high should have a factor of safety of 1 3 or
greater for the combined internal and external stability for the service loading condition
For the seismic loading condition a factor of safety of 1 1 or greater for the combined
internal and external stability should be achieved
Based on our analysis we recommend the length of the soil nails to be 10 or
15 feet for the retaining wall up to 12 feet or 20 feet in vertical height respectively The soil
nails should be installed in both horizontal and vertical spacing of 5 feet and in 15 degree
angle from horizontal We also recommend corrosion protection for the soil nails such as
a fully encapsulated double corrosion protection system To achieve a pull out design
capacity of 15 or 33 kips in soft or hard rock respectively we recommend installing soil
nails in 8 inch diameter drilled hole with 11 reinforcing bar 13 8 inches diameter The soil
nail pull out capacity should be verified during construction by proof testing 10 percent of
the production nails to 150 percent of the design capacity
As part of the special inspection in accordance with IBC 2006 Section 1704 Level
1 Special Inspection we recommend that Geolabs be retained as design geotechnical
engineer of record for observation of the drilling grouting and proof testing during soil nail
retaining wall construction The proof testing nails should be selected by Geolabs after
installation based on the drilling and grouting records
The design of the soil nail retaining wall system does not include hydrostatic
pressures that might be caused by groundwater or water trapped behind the walls
Therefore the soil nail retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of
hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system would consist of 2 foot wide strips of a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain placed on the face of the
excavation between the soil nails with typical spacing of 5 feet center to center The
prefabricated drainage product should extend from the top to the base of the wall and
should be hydraulically connected to a weephole at the base of the wall The weepholes
should discharge to appropriate outlets
In addition an interceptor drainage ditch should be installed near the top of soil
nail retaining walls to collect surface runoff and reduce water infiltration behind the
walls

on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings and structural cross sections
provided we anticipate that excavation will involve cuts into the underlying extremely to
highly weathered soft rock and slightly weathered hard basalt rock As mentioned
previously our laboratory testing indicates that the unconfined compressive strength of the
hard basalt rock may range from 1 900 to 25 000 psi

In general it is our opinion that conventional heavy excavation equipment such as
a large bulldozer excavator or similar heavy construction equipment may achieve the
excavations into the anticipated extremely to highly weathered soft rock However
excavations into the moderately weathered hard basalt rock may require the use of special
rock excavation technique to reduce excessive vibration for potential damage to the
existing aging water tank structure and adjacent residential houses
The contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used for
excavation subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavations should
comply with all applicable local safety requirements The above discussions regarding the
rippability of the surface materials are based on field data obtained from the borings and
seismic refraction geophysical surveys performed at the subject site Contractors
proposing to work on this project should be encouraged to examine the site conditions to
make their own interpretation
We also recommend implementing a vibration monitoring program during the hard
rock excavation The vibration monitoring should provide guidance and indication that the
excavation may generate excessive vibration and may potentially damage the aging
existing water tank and or private resident houses If the vibration monitoring indicates the
contractor s excavation technique is generating excessive vibration the contractor s
excavation operation should be immediately terminated and should not resume until an
alternative method is developed which would not damage the adjacent existing structures

DPP grading permit for the construction generating cut slope As mentioned previously
the proposed soil nail retaining walls require cutting existing slope to widen existing service
driveway

the cut slopes as the temporary shoring and the permanent retaining walls A shotcrete
facing will be placed to the face of the reinforced excavation providing the appearance of a
conventional concrete retaining wall

GEOPHYSICAL METHODS AND FIELD PROCEDURES
Seismic refraction was used to determine the depth to bedrock and rock quality The following
paragraphs describe the method and field procedures
Seismic refraction
Seismic refraction is the traditional method for determining the rock velocity for rock rippability
using a controlled energy source hammer blank shotgun shells or chemical explosives to
generate a seismic signal The seismic signals are received by a series of geophones 24 for
example that are connected to a seismic cable laid on the ground surface in a linear manner The
geophones evenly spaced along the geophone cable are placed on the ground surface The
seismic energy source is discharged at several places along the array and off both ends
The seismic wavelets travel through the earth to the geophones that convert the acoustic energy in
the ground to an electric signal in the geophone cable The seismograph detects the arriving
electric signals with respect to time and stores the records for future data processing The seismic
data is processed to determine the seismic velocity of the earth material through which the energy
has traveled and to model the subsurface geology This geophysical model depicts the earth in
cross section showing the velocity and thickness of the subsurface layers below the seismic line
The seismic refraction survey was conducted using a Geometrics Geode 24 channel digital
seismograph The sensors were Mark Products vertical geophones and the seismic energy
sources were a 20 lbs sledgehammer The typical field procedure consisted of laying out the
cables and planting the geophones at intervals The hammer was pounded at seven locations
along the geophone array Data were collected and saved in digital format and a field record was
produced on the computer screen to QA QC the data in real time
