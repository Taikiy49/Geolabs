Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes which include the following Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which
has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the
site appears to consist of both a a and pahoehoe type lava flows which spread and
ponded as they approached the ocean Ava lava is typically characterized by a porous

Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling ten borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10 extending to depths of
about 10 to 11 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of
the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by dense
basalt formation interbedded with clinker layers extending from the ground surface to the
maximum depth explored of about 11 5 feet below the existing ground surface The basalt
formation was generally medium hard to very hard and ranged from relatively
unweathered to moderately weathered
It should be noted that voids of up to about 2 feet in height were encountered in the
borings drilled for this project As indicated previously lava tubes and cavities are
commonly encountered in basaltic lava flows Therefore the presence of lava tubes
and or cavities should be anticipated at the project site
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration However groundwater levels may change due to seasonal precipitation
surface water runoff and other factors Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered
during our field exploration are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 10
of Appendix A
Percolation Tests
Ten percolation tests eight constant head and two falling head tests were
performed to evaluate the subsurface permeability characteristics at the project site
Based on our field exploration at the project site the near surface materials generally

Hualalai Development Company Page 4
consist of slightly to closely fractured pahoehoe basalt formation The results of the
percolation tests performed at the site are presented in Appendix B of this report
Based on the constant head percolation tests the near surface materials generally
have coefficient of permeability k in the range of about 4 6 x 107 to 8 1 x 10 feet per
minute which corresponds to 4 x 10 to 4 1 x 10 centimeters per second The
falling head percolation tests indicated that the near surface materials generally have
coefficient of permeability k in the range of about 2 3 x 10 to 6 4 x 10 feet per minute
which corresponds to about 1 2 x 10 to 3 2 x 10 centimeters per second
Based on the percolation test results we believe that an average hydraulic
conductivity of about 0 3 feet per minute may be used for the near surface materials at
the project site The average hydraulic conductivity value generally corresponds to an
infiltration rate of about 3 200 gallons per day per square foot of surface area or about
0 005 cubic feet per second per square foot
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the basalt rock formation
at the site is highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids in the lava
or the presence of embedded clinker seams which greatly affect the disposal capacity of
a seepage pit or detention basin In general the greater the number of clinker seams
and or voids encountered in the excavations for seepage pits and or detention basins the
higher the overall permeability and disposal capacity

Six percolation tests four constant head and two falling head tests were
performed to evaluate the subsurface permeability characteristics at the project site
Based on our field exploration at the project site the near surface materials generally
consist of slightly to closely fractured pahoehoe basalt formation The results of the
percolation tests performed at the site are presented in Appendix B of this report
Based on the constant head percolation tests the near surface materials generally
have coefficient of permeability k in the range of about 4 6 x 10 to 1 2 x 10 feet per
minute which corresponds to about 2 4 x 10 to 6 3 x 10 centimeters per second The
falling head percolation tests indicated that the near surface materials generally have
coefficient of permeability k in the range of about 2 3 x 10 to 6 4 x 10 feet per minute
which corresponds to about 1 2 x 10 to 3 2 x 10 centimeters per second

Based on the percolation test results we believe that the hydraulic conductivity of
near surface materials may range from about 0 05 to 0 12 feet per minute These
hydraulic conductivity values generally correspond to infiltration rates of about 550 to
1 300 gallons per day per square foot of surface area or 0 0008 to 0 002 cubic feet per
second per square foot
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the basalt rock formation
at the site is highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids in the lava
or the presence of embedded clinker seams which greatly affect the disposal capacity of
a seepage pit or detention basin In general the greater the number of clinker seams
and or voids encountered in the excavations for seepage pits and or detention basins the
higher the overall permeability and disposal capacity
Limitations
The analyses submitted in this report are based in part upon information
obtained from the field borings and percolation tests Variations of subsurface
conditions between and beyond the field borings may occur and the nature and extent
of these variations may not become evident until construction is underway If variations
then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the recommendations provided
in this report

Elevations of the borings were obtained based on interpolation between

field borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods
used
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict
the approximate boundaries between soil rock types and as such may denote a gradual
transition Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the
graphic representations and or presented in the text of this report These data have been

encountered in the borings at the time of our field exploration However it must be noted
