INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Kamamalu Play Area Phase 1 project located on the Kamehameha Schools
campus on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The site is located within the Bishop Circle area
between Kamamalu and Kuihelani Buildings The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended to assist in the design of the retaining wall foundations
basketball backboard foundations covered play area foundations road pavements utilities
and earthwork only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

We understand that there are two separate projects involved in constructing the new
play areas known as the Kamamalu Play Area These two separate projects have been
designated as Phase 1 and Phase 2 of the Kamamalu Play Area project This report
specifically addresses the design aspects of the improvements for the Phase 1 project only
A separate report will be issued to address the design aspects of the improvements for the
Phase 2 project
It is proposed to develop the Phase 1 project site into a new playground with asphaltic
concrete pavements for a level play area We understand that the play area will be surfaced
with new asphaltic concrete pavements In addition the new play area will include the
construction of six basketball backboards and possibly a roof structure for a covered play
area We also understand that some benches will be constructed on the slopes above the
existing retaining wall to the north of the project area The slopes above the existing retaining
wall will be terraced with low CRM retaining walls Improvements such as concrete sidewalks
and underground utilities are also planned for the proposed project The proposed
improvements are shown approximately on the Site Plan Plate 2

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger shield
Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene ice Age in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its
eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the
Schofield Plateau
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lavas
of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed
dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical weathering of the
Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The weathering process has
formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic soils In general saprolite is composed mainly of
silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades
to basaltic rock formation with increased depth Fills placed during the initial development of
the campus underlie the ground surface at the project site

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As previously indicated the subsurface conditions at the site generally consist of fill
materials overlying highly to completely weathered saprolite basalt formation at shallow
depths One of the borings in the area encountered cobbles and boulders from depths of
about 3 to 6 feet The fill materials encountered consisted of a mixture of sands and gravel
with silty clays Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field
exploration

Based on the results of our exploration we believe that the proposed CRM retaining
walls basketball backboards and covered play area structures may be supported on shallow
foundations The shallow foundations should bear on properly compacted fills or the in situ
materials Due to the presence of cobbles and boulders and basalt formation near the
ground surface footing embedment for the proposed structures in the area should be kept to
a minimum In general we recommend that the structure foundations be limited to a
maximum embedment depth of about 3 to 4 feet below the existing ground surface to reduce
the potential for encountering the dense rock and boulders Deeper footing embedment will
likely encounter the dense rock and boulders requiring more extensive excavation methods
in order to achieve the design depths
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering recommendations
for design are presented in the following sections of this report
Retaining Wall Foundations
We understand that CRM retaining walls are planned adjacent to the existing
wooden stairway area in the northern portion of the site Some of the CRM retaining walls
will be terraced with wall heights ranging from about 2 to 5 feet high
Wall Foundations
In general we envision that the retaining wall footings will likely bear on the existing
medium dense to stiff fill materials Based on these foundation conditions an
allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be
utilized for the design of the wall footings bearing on the recompacted fill materials
This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by up to
50 percent for transient loads such as those caused by wind and seismic forces In
general wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches and should be
embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade In addition wall
footings should be embedded an additional amount to provide a minimum horizontal
setback distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the
GEOLABS INC
W O 4495 00 Page 7
slope face Wall footings parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed
in stepped footings
The bottom of the footing excavations should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and concrete Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test
procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density Foundation subgrades should not be
allowed to dry before placing concrete If shrinkage cracks appear in the foundation
excavations the soil should be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks and
recompacted to provide a firm base prior to concrete placement
Lateral loads acting on the wall structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundations and the bearing soil and by
passive earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation
system A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the
recompacted fill materials Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot
of depth pcf for footings embedded in the fill materials This assumes that the soils
around the footings are well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs
the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative from
Geolabs prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation
bearing conditions and the required embedment depths

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
A typical drainage system consists of 1 cubic foot of permeable material such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped with filter fabric at each
of the weephole locations The weepholes should be spaced not more than 6 feet
apart Unless covered by concrete or asphaltic concrete the upper 12 inches of the
wall backfill should consist of relatively impervious materials such as the on site silty
clay to reduce water infiltration behind the walls

New footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent

Site Preparation
In general we anticipate that the grading work for the project will involve only minor
cuts and fills of less than 3 feet At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading
limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site or stockpiled in a
designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials Soft and
yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be over excavated to expose
firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted
fill
Prior to filling and or construction of improvements the existing ground after
clearing and grubbing should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture
conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction For pavement subgrades the compaction requirement
should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
In general the excavated on site materials may be re used as a source of general
fill material provided that they are free of deleterious materials and that the maximum
particle size is limited to 3 inches Select granular fill and imported fill if required should
consist of non expansive granular material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or
cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger
than 3 inches in largest dimension In addition the material should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR
value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 Imported fill materials should be
tested by Geolabs for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the
project site for the intended use
GEOLABS INC
W O 4495 00 Page 12
Fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Aggregate base course and select
borrow subbase materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction
It is anticipated that cut and fill slopes will be required for the construction of the new
CRM retaining walls Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings we
anticipate that the cut slopes would expose soils consisting of medium dense gravel and
sand stiff silty clay with gravel or highly weathered basalt formation In general we
believe that cut slopes exposing these soils may be excavated at an inclination of 2H 1V
or flatter
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills
placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope
to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the
lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the
above fill placement recommendations Water should be diverted away from the tops of
slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for
significant erosion of the finished slopes

Underground Utilities
It is anticipated that some underground utilities and utility connections will be
constructed for the proposed project The methods and equipment to be used for excavation

In general we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B

the potential for future ground subsidence The upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the
pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction

The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 101 through 104 extending to depths of about 6 5 to
15 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger and
coring equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses
a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit
is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring
attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs
of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be
50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical Handbook
of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD would
be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of excavation
