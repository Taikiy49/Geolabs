INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Public Baths Force Main Replacement in the Waikiki area
on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design
input only and may not be appropriate to form the basis for construction cost estimates
nor for construction bidding purposes

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk
of the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority
of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in
central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of the Koolau shield Its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the
Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated
by fossil Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits which are
intercalated with terrigenous sediments laid during high stands of the sea The base
of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3139 00 Page 8
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene by thinly
bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased approximately
2 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the island of Oahu has
subsided as much as 1200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity
Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of
the reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers melted global sea
levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to present sea level on Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in
the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3139 00 Page 9
upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively
high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments During these stable periods the growth of fringing coral algal reefs formed
distinct ledges of reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea
during that period About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred
During that rise the valleys in the project area were submerged In the last 10 000
years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine
sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft loose harbor sediments along the coast
in the areas which were formerly valleys and drainageways
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have
brought the Waikiki area to its present form Many of the resulting fills are of poor
quality in terms of supporting heavy structural loads

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions at the project site
consist generally of medium dense to very dense sands underlain by very loose to very
dense sandy gravel Shallow groundwater conditions were generally encountered
during our field exploration Based on our evaluation of the subsurface conditions with
respect to the proposed force main construction the principal geotechnical engineering
considerations for the proposed Public Baths Force Main Replacement project include
the following
1 excavation conditions and the potential need for support of the
excavations along the proposed force main alignment
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3139 00 Page 11
2 general dewatering conditions for the force main trench excavation and
the potential effects on the surrounding structures and
3 bedding requirements for support of the force main pipe
We understand that the force main pipe inverts generally will be about 5 to 6 feet
below the existing ground surface and the trench will be about 6 to 8 feet deep
Deeper inverts may be required at localized areas when traversing other existing
utilities in the area Trenches extending to depths greater than 5 feet below the surface
will likely encounter the groundwater table and will likely require dewatering during
construction to install the proposed force main In addition shoring of the trenches will
also be necessary to support the sides of the trenches if deeper than about 5 to 6 feet
Where dewatering of the trenches will be necessary we believe that an adequately
designed cut off wall such as sheet pile shoring should be considered to reduce the
influence of the dewatering on adjacent existing structures due to the high permeability
of the subsoils
Recommendations addressing the above geotechnical engineering consider
ations and other geotechnical aspects of the proposed force main replacement are

Excavation Method
It is anticipated that conventional earthmoving equipment may be used to
excavate the trenches based on the subsurface conditions encountered
However it should be noted that existing abandoned underground utilities and
other below grade concrete structures may exist at the site and would require
heavy equipment for excavation The method and equipment to be used for
excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and
safety considerations The excavated soils should be stockpiled no closer than
a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the
excavation to reduce the potential for ground movement
Excavation Support
Based on the information provided to our office trenches will extend to depths
about 6 to 8 feet Based on the near surface soil conditions encountered in our
boring we believe that shoring of the sides of the trenches will be necessary to
protect personnel working in the trench Because the proposed force main
replacement will be along the roadway sloping of the sides of the trenches will
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3139 00 Page 13
not be possible All trench excavation dewatering and construction works
should conform to Federal State and other safety requirements
Temporary shoring of the force main trenches will likely require the use of sheet
pile shoring especially where dewatering of the trenches will be necessary
However it should be noted that the proposed force main will likely traverse
other utility lines therefore care should be exercised to avoid truncating the
existing utility lines if sheet piles are to be used as shoring If sheet pile shoring
is used it would also serve as a cut off wall for the trench dewatering
operations Therefore to use the sheet piles as a cut off wall the sheet piles
should be driven to a sufficient depth to reduce the potential for areal ground
subsidence and to reduce the amount of water to be dewatered within the trench
due to the high permeability of the subsoils For preliminary design of cantilever
sheet pile shoring lateral earth pressures due to an equivalent fluid weighing 35
pounds per cubic foot p c f may be used above the groundwater level and 80
below the groundwater level to resist lateral loads
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with all applicable
safety requirements It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer
be retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the shoring system used
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His representative who should be required to
be continuously present on site during excavation and construction work will
have the best opportunity to promptly observe any changing conditions during
construction such as unforeseen subsoils unexpectedly high groundwater table
inappropriate construction sequence or techniques etc which may affect
shoring stability

Even if good construction procedures are followed some movement of the
shoring system and the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth
stresses during excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is
difficult to estimate accurately the magnitude of ground movement The
magnitude of ground movements also depends greatly upon workmanship such
as how quickly and tightly the shoring supports are installed the subsoil
conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be
installed properly and as early as practical and if necessary the adjacent
ground should be continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other
indications of movements with instruments
Dewatering
The trenches will be about 6 to 8 feet deep and groundwater was encountered
at 4 5 to 8 5 feet below the existing ground surface It is anticipated that localized
dewatering may be possible for the shallow force main trenches For trenches that
extend below the high ground water levels dewatering with a cut off wall is
recommended Therefore we recommend that dewatering provisions be included in
the contract documents for the proposed construction
Since the excavation dewatering will involve discharge of groundwater from the
dewatering operation into the adjacent drainage system a National Pollutant Discharge
Elimination System NPDES permit will be necessary Three of the drilled borings
were converted into monitoring wells during our field exploration for sampling
groundwater as required by the NPDES permit Groundwater sampling for NPDES
permit will be conducted at a later date and the results will be presented in a separate
report The contractor should consult their independent consultant or the State of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3139 00 Page 15
Hawaii Department of Health for the latest regulations and information pertaining to the
NPDES permit application
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection
of equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and he she
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on the conditions encountered The dewatering method
selected should have minimal impact on the existing groundwater levels surrounding
the proposed excavation
Dewatering operations should be performed such that only clear water is
pumped Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in a blowing or
heaving of the excavation bottom or sides
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents
should be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural
groundwater table and or to exclude the water from the excavation to allow construction
of the proposed force main replacement structures under safe and dry conditions
These works or systems may include but are not limited to well pumping grouting
cut off walls tremie plugs or any combination of the above and or other possible
methods
It should be noted that the subsoil conditions at the site consist of medium dense
to dense sands and gravel which are generally highly permeable soils Because the
proposed force main replacement is located in a developed area the dewatering
operation should be conducted in such a manner that the dewatering will not cause
areal ground subsidence which may cause potential damage to the existing structures

W O 3139 00 Page 16
Therefore consideration should be given to a dewatering system that includes a cut off
wall
Dewatering Considerations
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of
a suitable method of dewatering
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or damage
to existing buildings roads and the environment
b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide safe
and dry working conditions in the excavation
Cc The dewatering method should be _ sufficiently flexible to allow
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the
natural groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to
dewatering It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be
retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His qualified representative who should be
required to be continuously present on site during dewatering activities will have
the best opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3139 00 Page 17
construction and to implement as soon as possible necessary precautionary or
remedial measures including but not limited to stopping or slowing down the
dewatering operations
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils
may be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations
should be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the
excavation bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be
done without pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and should be
coordinated with shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not
affected
Any groundwater drawdown outside the excavation will cause settlement
resulting from consolidation of the loose compressible soils Therefore the use
of a deep well system outside the excavation to draw down the groundwater
level should not be allowed
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches
connected to excavations If this occurs the granular bedding and or backfill in
the utility trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal
groundwater drawdown resulting in settlements and potential damage to utility
lines and or other existing adjacent structures
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important
design consideration For pipes bearing on the medium dense to very dense sands
a minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular bedding material should be provided
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3139 00 Page 18
below the pipe For pipes with inverts near the soft loose soils an additional 18 inch
of No 3B Fine gravel wrapped in a filter fabric Mirafi 140N or equivalent should be
provided below the bedding layer for more uniform support A typical section of the
trench is presented on Plate 6
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of
one fourth the outside pipe diameter and should extend half way up the sides of the
pipe The bedding material should consist of clean granular material such as No 3B
Fine gravel Before the placement of bedding material the excavated trench bottom
should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii to confirm that firm materials
are exposed at the bottom of the trench or if the bedding should be extended deeper
as recommended above
Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Standard
Specifications for Public Works Construction September 1986 The first zone of
backfill extending from the bedding material to at least 12 inches above the top of the
pipes should consist of select free draining well graded granular material to reduce the
compaction effort required and resulting stresses on the pipe The granular material
should be well graded less than 1 inch in maximum size and should have less than
10 percent passing the No 200 sieve Compaction by water ponding or jetting may be
allowed provided the granular material is self draining such as No 3B Fine gravel
The trench backfill from 12 inches above the top of the pipes to within 2 feet
below the finished subgrade may consist of the excavated on site sandy soils The
backfill should be placed in thin lifts normally 8 inches in loose lift thickness with each
lift mechanically compacted to a minimum 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3139 00 Page 19
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 The trench backfill from 2 feet below the finished subgrade
elevation up to the subgrade elevation should consist of select borrow compacted to
not less than 95 percent relative compaction A typical section of the trench is
presented on Plate 6
Precautions should be taken against the occurrence of pipeline floatation before
and during trench backfilling due to possible influx of large quantities of groundwater
into the trenches It should be noted that the uplift buoyant forces due to groundwater
influx may lift empty or even partially filled pipes
Earth Pressure Loads on Pipe
Loads on buried conduits are influenced by the width of the trench the size of
the pipes the unit weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance between the
backfill material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads on the buried force
main pipes an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot p c f for backfill
material and a coefficient of friction of 0 4 between the backfill and trench sidewall are
recommended Earth pressure forces upon the pipe increase rapidly with increased
width of the trench Therefore the width of the sewer line trench should be kept to a
minimum Traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be considered since the
proposed sewer line alignment is located within travelled ways
Settlements
Primary settlement of the new force main construction is normally caused by the
difference in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier
compacted backfill material placed over the sewer pipes The net increase in loading
will cause settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert Based on our
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3139 00 Page 20
calculations primary settlement on the order of less than 1 inch is anticipated for the
project
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good
workmanship will be engaged during construction Additional settlement could occur
if improper trench support or dewatering procedures are used Therefore we believe
that the proposed force main should be designed with sufficient flexibility to
accommodate possible settlements
Manhole Structures
In general we believe that the bottom of the manhole structures will be bearing
on the medium dense to very dense sands encountered within 15 feet of the ground
surface in our borings To provide more uniform support for the manhole structures
we recommend that the manhole structures be supported on a 6 inch layer of cushion
fill consisting of select borrow or No 3B Fine gravel For these foundation conditions
an allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot p s f may be
used for design Settlements on the order of less than 1 inch are anticipated for these
structures
The lateral earth pressures acting on the proposed underground manhole
structures will depend on the type of backfill used the extent of backfill and the
compactive effort on the backfill material around the structures Therefore granular
material with less than 10 percent passing the No 200 sieve is recommended for use
as backfill around the structures to reduce the compactive effort required which may
cause additional lateral earth pressures We recommend that the new manhole
structure be designed for an equivalent fluid pressure of 50 pounds per cubic foot
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3139 00 Page 21
above values assume that the backfill around the manhole structures is properly placed
and compacted
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges traffic loads line loads and point
loads within a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the structure should be
considered in the design For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side
of the wall a rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 45 percent of the
vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire depth of the structure may be used

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
eight borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 8 at the approximate locations
shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 3 and 4 The depths of the borings range from
about 16 to 36 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled
using a truck mounted drill rig
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A of Appendix A Graphic presentation
of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 1
through A 8 2
After drilling and sampling Boring and 7 these borings were converted
into monitoring wells during the field exploration These monitoring wells will be used
for groundwater sampling purposes to provide baseline groundwater quality data as
required by the NPDES permit The following describes the procedure in which these
three monitoring wells were installed
After the three borings were drilled to the maximum depths explored of about
26 feet deep monitoring well casings were lowered into the drilled borings The
casing consisted of 2 inch O D Schedule 40 flush threaded PVC pipes Ten feet of
made up the lower portion of the well Fifteen feet of blank 2 inch O D Schedule 40
flush threaded PVC pipes composed the upper portion of the well The blank PVC
casing extended upward to the ground surface The space between the casings and
the walls of the borings were backfilled with a No 3 filter sand pack to above the
slotted section of the monitoring well The remaining portion of the monitoring well was
sealed with layers of bentonite and cement grout The well housings were then set in
place with concrete and the monitoring wells were enclosed in traffic rated hole covers
