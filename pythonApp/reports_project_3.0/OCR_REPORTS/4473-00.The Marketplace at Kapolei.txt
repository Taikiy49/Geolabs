REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As the volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the
Schofield Plateau The project site is located on the southwestern flank of the Waianae
Volcano
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau generated
sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and to the
south these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments and
coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the sedimentary
wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to over 1 000 feet
at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or
caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu Deposition of sediments has
continued from earlier geologic time through the present
GEOLABS INC
W O 4473 00 Page 6
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the
oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of accumulated
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys
in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous reef
materials Agricultural development within the last 100 years and recent mass grading work
have brought the Kapolei area to its present form

Based on our field exploration the project site can generally be divided into four
distinct areas designated as Areas 1 through 4 These areas are generally underlain by a
surface layer of fill soils consisting of very stiff to hard silty clays and dense to very dense
silty sands and gravel of variable thickness The surface fill soils are underlain by highly

Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration
Based on the relatively stiff dense subsoil conditions encountered in our borings we
believe that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be
used to support the proposed buildings planned at Areas 1 3 and 4 Foundations for the
proposed buildings may bear on the recompacted on site soils or the new compacted fills
needed to achieve the design finished grades in these areas
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at Area 2 and the proposed grading

about 10 feet thick To reduce the potential for significant differential settlements across the
building we recommend that the building in this area be supported on a drilled shaft
foundation system The drilled shaft foundation should consist of a minimum 16 inch
diameter cast in place concrete drilled shaft and should be about 15 feet deep The drilled
shafts would derive vertical support primarily from skin friction and end bearing in the existing
and new fills placed at the site A design compressive load capacity of up to 50 kips may be
used for the design of the drilled shaft foundations
Based on our laboratory tests the surface clayey soils exhibit a slight to moderate
shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture content Therefore
special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation requirements and the
slab on grade design for this project To reduce the potential for distress to the future
slabs on grade resulting from swelling of the clayey soils we recommend that a minimum

soils or new compacted fills This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces

Drilled Shaft Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at Area 2 and the proposed grading
we envision that the building in this area will straddle cuts and fills of up to about 10 feet thick
at opposite ends of the building structure To reduce the potential for significant differential
settlements associated with differential fill thickness across the building we recommend that
the building in this area be supported on a drilled shaft foundation system
In general we recommend that each column location be supported by one drilled
shaft designed for the total compression load of the column The drilled shaft should consist
of a minimum 16 inch diameter cast in place concrete drilled shaft and should be about
15 feet in length The drilled shafts would derive vertical support primarily from skin friction
and end bearing A design compressive load capacity of up to 50 kips for dead plus live
loads may be used for the design of the drilled shaft foundation The design compressive
load capacity may be increased by up to 50 percent for transient loads such as those
caused by wind or seismic forces Drilled shafts should be spaced a minimum of 3 5 feet
center to center to avoid further reduction in vertical load capacity due to group action and
to facilitate drilling of the shaft holes

Uplift Load Resistance
In general uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead weight of the
drilled shaft and by shear along the shaft surface and the adjacent soils Considering
that the drilled shafts are designed based on adhesion between the shaft and the
surrounding soils we recommend that an uplift load capacity of up to 30 kips be used
to resist uplift loads The uplift load capacity provided includes the weight of the
drilled shaft The structural capacity of the shaft member in tension should be
checked by the project structural engineer
Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on the building structure may be resisted by a combination of
the lateral load capacity of the drilled shafts and the passive pressure acting against
the near vertical faces of the footings grade beams Passive earth pressure against
the near vertical faces of the footings grade beam may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf We
believe that the lateral load capacity of the drilled shafts and the passive pressure
acting against the footings grade beams may be used in combination because both
components are strain compatible and would be mobilized
Lateral load resistance for drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of the surrounding
soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of the shaft and the
induced moment in the shaft The lateral load analyses were performed using the

difference laterally loaded deep foundation program originally developed at the
University of Texas at Austin The program solves for deflection and bending moment
along a deep foundation under lateral loads as a function of depth The analysis was
carried out with the use of non linear p y curves to represent soil moduli

Construction Considerations
The performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures The load bearing capacities of
drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the contact between the drilled shafts and
the surrounding soils Therefore proper construction techniques are important The
contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into
the holes
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch range should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soil The
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce segregation of the
aggregates from the concrete mix A representative from Geolabs should be present
to observe the installation of drilled shafts during construction to confirm the subsoil
conditions and the embedment depths Therefore observation of the drilled shaft
foundation construction should be designated a Special Inspection item in
accordance with Section 1701 of the Uniform Building Code 1997

areas Our exploration indicates that the site is generally underlain by slightly to moderately
expansive clayey soils at or near the existing ground surface Unless slabs on grade
supported by these expansive soils are properly designed there is a potential for future
distress to the structures resulting from wetting and subsequent swelling of the expansive
clayey soils
To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from the swelling of the on site
expansive soils we recommend that non expansive select granular fill material capping fill

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Four one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples to
evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils under different surcharge pressures
The results of these tests are summarized on Plate B 1
One sieve analysis test ASTM C 136 ASTM D 1140 was performed on a selected
soil sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B 2
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio Test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soils The test results are presented on Plate B 3
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plate B 4
