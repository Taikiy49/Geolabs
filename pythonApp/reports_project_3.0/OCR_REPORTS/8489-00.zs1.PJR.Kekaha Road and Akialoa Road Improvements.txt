SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the information provided and the results of our pavement distress survey, 
we anticipate that portions of Kekaha Road will be reconstructed. Based on our field 
exploration, we anticipate that the majority of the pavement subgrade soils along 
Akialoa Road between Kaumualii Highway and Kekaha Road and Kekaha Road from 
Akialoa Road to Pueo Road will likely consist of near-surface fill and beach deposits 
with a design R-value of 55. We anticipate that the pavement subgrade soils along 
Kekaha Road from Pueo Road to Kaumualii Highway will likely consist of near-surface 
fill consisting of clayey silt with a design R-value of 7. 
Typically, new Hawaii Department of Transportation (HDOT) pavements (or 
County pavements that receive federal funding) include a drainage layer to facilitate the 
removal of stormwater from the pavement section and increase the pavement life. 
However, the project site is located in a relatively low rainfall environment (leeward side 
of Kauai), and the roadway pavements are generally underlain by relatively permeable 
subgrade soils (coralline beach sand). In addition, since the existing pavement sections 
along Akialoa Road and Kekaha Road do not include a drainage layer, only pavement 
structural sections without a permeable drainage layer were considered in our 
pavement design. 
Based on the traffic volume assumptions described herein, the R-values obtained 
for the subgrade soils, and our economic analyses, we recommend using flexible 
pavement structural sections for Akialoa Road and Kekaha Road within the project limits.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai is a dissected basaltic dome that was initially formed by a 
single large shield volcano, which has subsequently been deeply eroded and partially 
covered with later (younger) volcanic materials. The Island of Kauai is the oldest of the 
main Hawaiian Islands and today encompasses approximately 1,456 square kilometers 
(562 square miles) in area with a maximum elevation of about 1,569 meters (5,148 feet) 
above Mean Sea Level (MSL) at Mt. Wai’ale’ale near the center of the island. It is 
believed that during the Tertiary Period (about 5 to 6 million years ago in the Pliocene 
Epoch), basaltic lavas belonging to the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series built a roughly 
circular island volcano from the ocean floor that was estimated to have been about 
6,100 meters (20,000 feet) deep at the time. Near the end of the Pliocene Epoch, this 
main shield-building volcanic phase came to a halt. 
Following a long period of erosion and island mass subsidence, volcanism was 
renewed during the early Pleistocene Epoch (about 1.5 million years ago) with the 
eruption of the Koloa Volcanic Series from multiple vents throughout the island. This 
post-erosional volcanic activity laid a veneer of fresh lava and interbedded volcanic 
sediments over the older deposits of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series. Because of 
the long period of erosion between the eruption of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series 
and the Koloa Volcanic Series, deposits of the Koloa Volcanic Series typically filled the 
erosional valleys and depressions that existed at that time. Consequently, the basaltic 
rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are commonly found overlying thick residual soil and 
alluvial deposits. 
The dominant geologic processes of weathering, landform erosion, and sediment 
re-deposition followed during the late Pleistocene and Recent Epochs. Combined with 
the erosional and depositional effects of the Pleistocene sea-level fluctuations, which 
occurred in response to the worldwide advance and retreat of the continental ice sheets, 
the Island of Kauai has evolved to its present form.

The project site is on a low-lying coastal area of Mana Plain on the western side

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located along Akialoa Road from Kekaha Road to 
Kaumualii Highway, Kekaha Road from Akialoa Road to Kaumualii Highway, and 
Alae Road from Kekaha Road to Iwipolena Road in Kekaha on the Island of Kauai, 
Hawaii. Kekaha Elementary School is located on Kekaha Road between Amakihi Road 
and Alae Road. The remainder of the alignment generally traverses through residential 
neighborhoods. 
Akialoa Road, Kekaha Road, and Alae Road generally consist of two-lane 
asphaltic concrete (AC) paved roads with one lane of traffic in each direction. The roads 
do not have dedicated shoulder areas, bike lanes, and pedestrian sidewalks except for 
the central portion of Kekaha Road that was observed to have a sidewalk on the 
southern side. There are three bus stops within the project limits: one on the east side 
of Akialoa Road (northbound direction), one on the west side (southbound direction) 
and one on the south side of Kekaha Road (eastbound direction).  
Based on the topographic survey plan provided, the existing ground surface 
elevations are highest on the western end of the alignment and slope downward toward 
the east. The highest elevation of about +18.5 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) is located 
along Akialoa Road between Ulili Road and Puaiohi Place, and the lowest elevation of 
about +5.5 feet MSL is located on the eastern end of Kekaha Road near the intersection 
with Kaumualii Highway.

Our borings on Akialoa Road (Boring Nos. 1 through 3) generally encountered a

encountered a pavement structure consisting of about 4 to 6 inches of AC underlain by

the pavement structure, fill material consisting of relatively stiff/dense clayey silt and/or 
coralline sand were encountered to depths of 1.2 to 3.5 feet followed by medium dense

beach and shallow marine deposits generally consisted of coralline sand with varying

soft lagoonal deposits extending to depths of about 24 to 24.5 feet. On the west side of 
Cox Drainage Channel, medium hard to hard coral formation was encountered 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 25.4 feet below the existing ground

was encountered to the maximum depth explored of about 27 feet.

(Fatigue) 
Alligator cracks are a series of interconnecting cracks caused by

Block Cracking 
Block cracks are inter-connected cracks that divide the pavement

Depression 
Depressions are localized pavement surface areas with elevations 
slightly lower than those of the surrounding pavement. 
Edge Cracking 
Edge cracks are cracks parallel to and usually within 1 to 1.5 feet of 
the outer edge of the pavement. 
Longitudinal 
Cracking 
Longitudinal cracks are cracks parallel to the pavement’s centerline 
or laydown direction. 
Transverse 
Cracking 
Transverse cracks are cracks extending across the pavement at 
approximately right angles to the pavement centerline or direction of 
laydown.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the information provided and the results of our pavement distress 
survey, we anticipate that portions of Kekaha Road will be reconstructed. Typically, new 
Hawaii Department of Transportation (HDOT) pavements (or County pavements that 
receive federal funding) include a drainage layer to facilitate the removal of stormwater 
from the pavement section and increase the pavement life. However, the project site is 
located in a relatively low rainfall environment (leeward side of Kauai), and the roadway

beach sand). In addition, since the existing pavement sections along Akialoa Road, 
Kekaha Road, and Alae Road do not include a drainage layer, only pavement structural 
sections without a permeable drainage layer were considered in our pavement design. 
Based on the traffic volume assumptions described herein, the R-values obtained

pavement structural sections for the project.  
3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
The flexible pavement sections presented in this report were generally 
determined based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the revised Pavement 
Design Manual dated March 2002. The Pavement Design Manual was prepared by the 
State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing and 
Research Branch. The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem 
Stabilometer method developed and used by the California Department of 
Transportation (Caltrans). 
The design procedures for the rigid pavement sections are generally based on 
the design procedures described in the Portland Cement Association “Thickness Design 
for Concrete Highway and Street Pavements.”

3.4 
Design Pavement Section 
The revised Pavement Design Manual requires a highly permeable drainage 
layer below the pavements unless the site has a very low annual rainfall, or the site 
consists of pavement subgrades that are free-draining (permeability greater than 
100 feet per day). The project site is located in a relatively low rainfall environment 
(leeward side of Kauai), and the roadway pavements are generally underlain by 
relatively permeable subgrade soils (coralline beach sand). In addition, since the 
existing pavement sections along Akialoa Road and Kekaha Road do not include a 
drainage layer, only pavement structural sections without a permeable drainage layer 
were considered in our pavement design. 
Based on the traffic volumes anticipated for the new pavements, asphalt 
concrete base (ACB) should be used as the base material in flexible pavement sections

3.5 
Subgrade Preparation Below Pavement Section 
We anticipate that the new pavement will be constructed at or near the existing 
roadway elevation. In general, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed at the on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, 
deleterious material, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and properly 
disposed of off-site or disposed of in a designated area to reduce the potential for 
contamination of the excavated materials. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over-excavated to expose 
stiff and/or dense materials. The resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
aggregate subbase (ASB) materials. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be 
properly disposed of off-site. 
After clearing and grubbing, the future pavement areas should be excavated, 
where necessary, to the pavement subgrade level (i.e., bottom of the ACB, AB, or ASB 
layer). The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches,

moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to no less 
than 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry 
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T180 (ASTM D1557). Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we 
recommend scarifying and re-preparing the soils as recommended above. Saturation 
and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor 
drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
well-compacted fill. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit 
prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the 
over-excavation quantities. 
3.6 
Fill Materials 
In general, the excavated on-site near-surface soils may be used as a source of 
general fill materials, provided that deleterious materials such as vegetation are 
removed and over-sized materials greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension are 
screened. Imported fill materials, if required, should consist of non-expansive select 
granular material, such as crushed coralline or basaltic materials. The materials should 
be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest 
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 
sieve. The materials should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should 
have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less. Imported fill materials should be tested for 
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the 
intended use.

optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate 
base course materials required for the new pavements should conform to the 
requirements stipulated in Subsection 703.06 (Aggregate for Untreated Base) of the

3.8 
Pavement Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, special attention 
should be given to the surface drainage of the pavements.  
As previously mentioned, a permeable drainage layer was not incorporated into 
our pavement design. Therefore, it is essential to slope the pavement surface 
adequately and to maintain positive drainage gradients at the pavement surface such 
that surface water may be carried off the pavement quickly for proper discharge. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed on-site during or after construction.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
15 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 15, extending to depths of about 11.2 to 
27 feet below the existing ground surface. Seven bulk samples of the near-surface soils, 
designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-7, were obtained to evaluate the moisture-density 
relationship and pavement support characteristics of the near-surface soils. The 
approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Overall Site Plan, Plate 2, 
and the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.12. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-15. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits to aid in soil classifications. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Thirteen Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on 
Plates B-2 through B-4. 
One one-inch Ring Swell test was performed on a relatively undisturbed sample 
to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils. The test results are 
summarized on Plate B-5. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-6 and B-7. 
One Consolidation test (ASTM D2435) was performed on a selected soil sample of 
the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the 
materials encountered. The consolidation test results are presented on Plate B-8. 
Seven laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D2844) were performed by 
Ninyo & Moore on selected bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the 
pavement support characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on 
Plates B-9 through B-15.

1. Executive Summary 
1.1 Project Description 
The West Kauaʻi Community Plan is a land use and transportation plan for the communities of Eleʻele, 
Hanapepe, Kaumakani, Waimea, and Kekaha on the island of Kauaʻi. Key elements of the plan include: 
Identifying future development opportunity areas within the Community Plan area 
Estimating growth to accommodate future development 
Preparing a Focused Growth Scenario which outlines future land use changes 
Recommendations to improve roadway, transit, and bicycle/pedestrian networks to support the 
anticipated level of new development 
Preparing a Multi-Modal Transportation Plan reflecting these recommended improvements 
The land use program and roadway network were developed through an extensive consultation process 
involving Agency Staff, Community Members, business owners, and other stakeholders.   
The purpose of this transportation study is threefold: 
Present background information regarding the transportation system within the Community Plan 
area 
Discuss the operations of the transportation system prior to the implementation of the West 
Kauaʻi Community Plan 
Address the operations of the transportation system with the implementation of the West Kauaʻi 
Community Plan 
1.2 Transportation Analysis 
This study addresses several specific questions related to the future performance of the transportation 
system: 
How does the roadway network perform prior to the buildout of the Planned Growth Scenario? 
How does the roadway network perform after the buildout of the Planned Growth Scenario?

Does the implementation of the Planned Growth Scenario cause any element of the roadway 
network to fail that would otherwise operate at an acceptable level? 
How would the non-vehicular transportation system operate after the implementation of the 
Planned Growth Scenario and the Multi-Modal Transportation Plan? 
If there are roadway segments which operate in a deficient fashion after the implementation of 
the Planned Growth Scenario, what policy options or physical improvements might be available to 
reduce vehicular demand? 
Can several site-specific improvements be implemented within the constraints of the overall 
transportation system? 
The Island of Kauaʻi Travel Demand Forecasting Model (TDFM), which was prepared by the Hawaiʻi 
Department of Transportation with the assistance of CH2MHill, was used for reference in conjunction with 
the Trip Generation Manual (10th Edition, 2017) by the Institute of Transportation Engineers (ITE) and existing 
traffic counts to forecast future roadway volumes for operational analysis. Traffic volumes associated with 
new development were distributed across the network and any resulting changes in roadway performance 
were noted.   
1.3 Findings 
The analysis concluded that there are several segments of Kaumualiʻi Highway, which would operate at 
LOS F prior to and after the implementation of the Planned Growth Scenario associated with the West Kauaʻi 
Community Plan. While the typical approach would be to recommend widening the roadway to 
accommodate additional projected demand, we instead concluded that the presence of the following 
elements in the Multi-Modal Transportation Plan could limit the need to widen the roadway in the future: 
Signalized intersections, signal improvements or roundabouts at several intersections along 
Kaumualiʻi Highway, including Laulea Street, Halewili Road, Waialo Road, Moi Road, Makeke Road, 
and Waimea Canyon Drive 
Proposed shared use paths at the following locations: 
o 
Kaumualiʻi Highway from Hanapepe to west of Kekaha 
o 
Between Waimea Canyon Middle School and Kekaha Road along an existing dirt road 
o 
Along the Waimea River levee 
o 
From Poke Road across the Waimea River and along the Pioneer Hi-Bred International site 
o 
Along the Hanapepe River levee 
o 
Along Lower Saki Mana Road 
o 
Around Port Allen Airport and along Puolo Road and Lokokai Road 
o 
Safe routes in each town that enable safe walking and/or biking for short trips to schools, 
parks and other community services

The provision of a robust multi-modal transportation network as outlined in the WKCP has the potential to 
minimize increases in vehicular traffic on Kaumualiʻi Highway, which may limit future congestion in the Study 
Area. However; this approach would also require modifications and enhancements to existing transit 
operations to ensure enough capacity exists on bus routes to accommodate future growth. Providing 
convenient and attractive travel options will allow residents, employees and visitors the opportunity to forgo 
or reduce the use of personal vehicles to meet the overall mobility goals within the Community Plan area. 
Additionally, an increase in services and employment in West Kaua’i associated with development under 
the Planned Growth Scenario could reduce vehicle miles traveled on the roadways in Kaua’i by shortening 
commutes and trips associated with other routine activities, potentially improving congestion within West 
Kaua’i, as well as on other parts of the island. Shorter trip lengths improve access by creating opportunities 
for using other modes of transportation, such as walking and biking to nearby destinations, including nearby 
transit stops to access destinations further away.  This opportunity to shift to non-automotive modes also 
creates opportunities to reduce congestion on roadways, reduce emissions, and improve health by 
providing active transportation alternatives for residents, employees and visitors, alike.

2. Analysis Parameters 
This chapter outlines the geographic scope of the traffic impact analysis, including the study intersections 
and roadways, and the analysis methodologies and significance criteria employed in this study. 
2.1 Project Study Area 
The study area for the WKCP is along the Kaumualiʻi Highway corridor on the southwest coast of Kauaʻi and 
is generally bound by Imiloa Road to the north and Halewili Road to the south. The study segments are 
shown on Figure 2-1. 
2.2 Analysis Scenarios 
This report presents data for two scenarios including: 
Existing - This scenario reports existing traffic counts and transit data provided by The Kauai Bus 
for November 2018 to November 2019. 
Planned Growth Scenario - This scenario analyzes transportation conditions with implementation 
of the Planned Growth Scenario. This scenario includes programmed roadway improvements and 
shifts in existing traffic volumes due to changes in school zoning. This scenario also relies on land 
use and transportation data within the Kauaʻi TDFM as well as existing counts and ITE Trip 
Generation Manual (10th Edition, 2017) volumes for development proposed in the Planned Growth 
Scenario.

One method commonly used to assess the operations of a roadway is level of service (LOS). LOS is a 
quantitative approach that defines how a facility operates by comparing observed or forecasted conditions 
against various types of performance metrics. Potential performance metrics include seconds of delay or 
capacity. 
For purposes of this analysis, the observed and projected traffic volumes on a segment basis are compared 
against the capacity to determine the volume to capacity ratio (V/C ratio). Typically, LOS A indicates free-
flowing conditions, LOS C is indicative of a moderate level of congestion, and LOS F indicates significant 
levels of congestion.  For purposes of this analysis, a V/C ratio in excess of 1.0 is classified as LOS F as shown 
in Table 2-2 below. A level of service of E or worse is generally considered undesirable, as it represents

3.1 Existing Roadway Facilities 
This section describes the existing roadways within the Study Area, primarily focusing on regional roadways.  
Major roadways within the study area include: 
Kaumualiʻi Highway, also known as Route 50, is one of the primary roadways on the island and general 
travels from east to west through the central portion of the island This roadway is one of the backbone 
infrastructure elements on Kauaʻi and serves a critical role in the movement of traffic on a regional basis.  
This roadway currently has one travel lane in each direction.  
Alae Road is a north/south roadway that connects Kaumualiʻi Highway to Kekaha Road and Kokee Road. 
This roadway also has one travel lane in each direction. Its speed limit is 15 miles per hour. 
Kekaha Road is a two-way, two-lane road that extends from Alae Road to Kaumualiʻi Highway. The Kekaha 
Road speed limit is 25 miles per hour.  
Waimea Canyon Drive is a north-south roadway that connects to Route 50 to the south and intersects 
with Kokee Road to the north. Like the other roadways in the study area, this facility also has one travel lane 
in each direction with left turn pockets provided at Haina Road and Maule Road. Its speed limit is 25 miles 
per hour. 
Waimea Road is a two-way east-west road that extends from Menehune Road to Poke Road in the central 
area of Waimea.  
Menehune Road is a north-south roadway that connects to Kaumualiʻi Highway in the south. It has one 
lane on each direction. When it reaches the Kiki a Ola bridge to the north, it narrows to 12 feet or less. 
Hanapepe Road is a two-way local road that connects to Kaumualiʻi Highway at both ends. It provides 
access to the central areas of Hanapepe and Eleele. As with all other roadways, this roadway has one travel 
lane in each direction. 
Moi Road is a north-south roadway that extends from Kaumualiʻi Highway to Walea Street. It has one lane 
in each direction and its speed limit is 25 miles per hour.

3.2 Existing Transit Facilities 
The County of Kauaʻi operates a fixed route bus fleet throughout the island. Service is provided on several 
routes throughout the study area including: 
Kekaha-Lihue Mainline (Route 200/100) has stops in all towns along Kaumualiʻi Highway within the study 
area. Service along this route is provided from approximately 5:15 AM to 11:00 PM Monday through Friday.  
Weekend and holiday service is offered from 7:15 AM to 8:45 PM. The bus travels through the communities 
approximately every 30 to 60 minutes. 
According to the data provided by The Kauai Bus for November 2018 to November 2019, the Kekaha 
Mainline route has a daily ridership of approximately 750. The ridership increased by nearly 90% when 
compared to previous data collected in 2007, but is similar to data collected in 2011 from the Kauai 2012 
Transportation Data Book.   
One metric commonly applied to transit routes is number of persons board per hour, which is a function of 
total persons boarding a bus divided by total number of hours the buses are in service.  According to the 
Transportation Data Book, the boardings per hour on the Kekaha Mainline were 11.1, some of the highest 
in the system. This value is consistent with the overall averages for the system.  
Koloa Shuttle (Route 30) serves Kalaheo, Lawai, Koloa, and Poipu. It provides connections to the Kekaha-
Lihue Mainline Route in both Kalaheo and Lawai. Service along this route is provided hourly from 6:15 AM 
to 10:51 PM Monday through Friday. Weekend and holiday services are offered from 7:15 AM to 6:15 PM. 
The Koloa Shuttle route carried 116 riders per day in 2019, which is over triple the 33 riders per day reported 
in 2011. The shuttles can carry 42 passengers per trip. The Kauai Bus is planning to make this a bi-directional 
service, which will make service more efficient. The extra capacity and service enhancements could shift 
commuters between West Kauai and South Kauai from private vehicles to public transit.

3.3 Bicycle and Pedestrian Facilities 
Pedestrian access often occurs through de facto sidewalks, such as where parking lots provide some limited 
level of connectivity between parcels. In many instances, pedestrians are forced to walk in shoulders or in 
the grass along the front of each parcel. Where sidewalks are present, sidewalks are discontinuous and 
limited in terms of length.  This creates particular challenges for persons with disabilities, mothers pushing 
strollers, and for everyone when the heavy rains flood the shoulders and force pedestrians into the street.  
The lack of sidewalks near schools is of particular concern since many children walk to school. 
There are currently no separated bicycle facilities within the study area. Similar to pedestrians, cyclists are 
forced to use shoulders and areas of the roadway, often having no recourse but to mix with automobile 
traffic.  
According to the American Community Survey (ACS) 2018 data profiles for communities in West Kauai, 
commute trips made by active transportation modes ranged from between one percent (in Eleele) to ten 
percent (in Waimea). While the margins of error were high, the data shows that few trips in West Kauai are 
currently being made using active transportation modes.

5. Kauaʻi Travel Demand Forecasting 
Model (TDFM) 
This section documents the use of the Kaua’i TDFM for the purposes of the West Kaua’i Community Plan 
Transportation Study. 
5.1 Model Background 
The Kauaʻi TDFM was developed by the State of Hawaiʻi to facilitate the development of Long-Range Land 
Use and Transportation Plans for the Islands of Maui, Hawaiʻi, and Kauaʻi.  The model was a collaborative 
effort between HDOT and CH2MHill, a private consultant.   
Summary information germane to this study related to the structure of the model and the model 
assumptions are discussed below.  
One key element of the model is that it is intended to be a tool to support long-range planning efforts. The 
following should be noted: 
The travel demand model is developed as a tool to assist in understanding the relationship between 
the socio-economic patterns on each island along with the roadway network.  The model assists in 
understanding where capacity issues may occur given the network and socio-economic 
assumptions.  The travel demand model is not able to perform detailed operational analysis, such 
as adding turn lanes at an intersection.  
For purposes of this study, the model was primarily used to test the effects of alternative land use 
development patterns on the transportation network, and to validate traffic assignment and growth 
associated with the West Kaua’i Community Plan. 
5.2 Model Structure 
Like many other similar tools, the Kauaʻi TDFM is a three-step travel demand model which has the following 
phases: 
Trip generation – This process involves the calculation of trips produced by land uses within the 
model.  Land use data, broken down into sub-areas known as traffic analysis zones (TAZ’s) is one 
of the two major inputs into any travel model.  The development of TAZ’s typically follows the 
boundaries of Census Blocks and Census Block Groups.  In the case of the Kauaʻi TDFM, the TAZ 
West Kauaʻi Community Plan Traffic Impact Study 
January 2020 
 19 
land use data reflects households, employment, and visitor trips which are associated with hotels 
and major attractions. The input land use data reflects updated information as of 2007, when the 
model development process began. The documentation supplied for each model indicates that 
sources such as the County Assessor were used to develop and verify land use data.  
Trip distribution – Once trips are generated by each land use and aggregated to the TAZ level, 
these trips are distributed.  For example, trips generated at the household level (productions) are 
matched with employment locations (attractions).  This distribution process considers the relative 
location of each trip and the time required to travel for this trip in match trips up to their origin 
and destination. 
Trip assignment – The final step of the process is the assignment process in which trips are 
assigned to roadways within the model. These roadway networks are the second major input to 
the travel model. 
Following the development of the model data, a validation process is implemented which compares the 
model results for the Existing Year against traffic count data. Once a travel model meets standardized criteria 
related to its performance during the validation process, the model can be used for future traffic forecasts.  
As noted in the Kauaʻi TDFM documentation, the model met accepted guidelines for validation at the overall 
island-wide level. The documentation notes several locations where the model was either under predicting 
or over predicting as compared to existing counts; however, none of the identified locations were within 
the study area. 
2020 and 2035 Future Year models were also developed. Fehr & Peers employed the 2035 Kauaʻi TDFM in 
forecasting volumes for the Planned Growth scenario since it represents a reasonable build out scenario 
time frame.

TDFM documentation indicates the following items: 
Only a few fully funded regional transportation improvements are assumed in the model.  
According to the documentation provided, several improvements to sections of Kaumualiʻi 
Highway are assumed. However; no significant changes in the roadway network are assumed for 
the future 2020 and 2035 scenarios. As shown in Figure 5-1, all the study area roadways are 
projected to maintain one (1) lane in each direction for the 2035 analysis scenario.

6.1.3 Potential Mobility Improvement 
Strategies 
While the traditional approach for many traffic engineers and transportation planners would be to 
recommend the expansion of a roadway which is projected to operate deficiently, our recommendations 
recognize many factors which are unique to transportation on Kauaʻi and reflect the context environment.  
These factors include: 
Widening significant sections of Kaumualiʻi Highway would likely be extremely costly and have 
significant negative impacts on the adjacent properties and the environment 
The study area currently lacks significant facilities for alternative travel modes; thereby forcing 
people to drive where they might otherwise walk, bike, or use transit 
The widening of the roadway may not be fully compatible with recent long-range transportation 
planning efforts  
The assessment of daily capacity often does not fully address the benefits of targeted 
improvements such as the addition of turn lanes, roundabouts, traffic signal upgrades, and other 
related strategies to increase capacity at intersections, which are the constraint points of the 
roadway network 
Therefore, we recommend that the analysis consider the likely effects of targeted improvements and policies 
which would moderate travel demand and minimize congestion. These policies and targeted improvements 
are described below: 
Signalize intersections, improve signals, or install roundabouts at several intersections along 
Kaumualiʻi Highway, including Laulea Street, Halewili Road, Waialo Road, Moi Road, Makeke Road, 
and Waimea Canyon Drive to improve vehicle and pedestrian access to communities 
Bicycle facilities on Kaumualiʻi Highway between Hanapepe and Kekaha 
Bicycle facilities along Waimea Canyon Drive 
Shared use paths through towns and connecting towns as shown on the transportation maps 
Improved pedestrian access in towns and to bus stops 
Increased transit service and connections along the Lihue-Kekaha bus route 
6.1.4 Travel Mode Shift Analysis 
Using the data above, we can estimate the required level of vehicle trip reduction using the following 
approach: 
Focus on reducing incremental trips associated with the Planned Growth Scenario on the segments 
of Kaumualiʻi Highway that are already projected to operate at LOS F above the 1.0 capacity 
threshold  
Focus on diverting approximately 2,200 daily vehicle trips to alternative travel modes. When typical 
auto occupancy factors are applied to this, it results in approximately 2,500 daily person trips that 
would need to change modes.

While transit is one likely mode of diversion, there will need to be significant increases in transit 
service to shift an additional 2,500 daily person trips from single occupancy cars to transit to reduce 
the impacts.  For example, between 2007 and 2011, the ridership on the Lihue-Kekaha bus route 
nearly doubled due to significant transit service improvements, increasing to almost 750 riders per 
day. Between 2011 and 2019, no additional transit service was added and ridership has remained 
largely unchanged.  A comparable increase in ridership either in terms of percentage or absolute 
magnitude might be possible, especially if peak hour capacity constraints are addressed.  Currently, 
the Kekaha-Lihue Mainline route operates 6 buses during the morning and evening peak hours 
which has an estimated latent capacity for another 300 to 500 persons per day. Transit investments 
that reduce headways, increase operating hours, and add more transit connections would provide 
greater capacity and incentive to use the service.  Improvements such as hourly weekend service, 
later weekend service, later weekday service, more weekday peak service, and more weekday 
midday service are all priorities in the Kaua’i Short Range Transit Plan (SRTP) which would increase 
system capacity and could increase ridership. The Kaua’i SRTP also proposes serving the Waimea 
Athletic Field before KVMH and only serving Pacific Missile Range Facility (PMRF) and Syngenta for 
certain trips. Ridership increase would be related to a more reliable and intuitive service. 
Another transit-related set of improvements would be local shuttles which could supplement the 
fixed-route transit service. The proposed West Side Shuttle in the Kaua’i SRTP would serve Koloa, 
Kalaheo, Hanapepe, Hanapepe Heights, and Ele’ele. Benefits from shuttles are likely to relate to 
persons traveling to specific destinations such as large hotels or employment centers rather than 
residential locations.  
In addition to transit, significant reductions in traffic volumes along Kaumualiʻi Highway would also 
require some diversion of vehicular traffic to bicycle and pedestrian routes.  According to data 
provided by the American Community Survey (ACS) for 2012, nearly 20 percent of all commute 
trips on the island of Kauaʻi travel by car but have a work trip of 10 minutes or less. Trips of this 
distance are often able to be easily made by walking or bicycling if the facilities are present. By 
providing bicycle and pedestrian facilities where they currently do not exist, some level of diversion 
may be possible.  An analysis of travel patterns on Kaumualiʻi Highway indicates that as much of 
10 percent of the vehicular traffic on these deficient segments of the highway are local as opposed 
to longer-distance through trips. The Planned Growth Scenario creates better opportunities for 
converting a significant portion of these local trips from autos to walking and biking.  
Therefore, reducing vehicular demand along Kaumualiʻi Highway will require implementing the 
multi-modal transportation measures shown on Figure 4-1 along with a meaningful diversion of 
trips to transit. This diversion of transit trips can only occur if there is sufficient capacity in the 
transit system, which will require expansion of service in the future as outlined in the Short Range 
Transit Plan (SRTP).

7. Evaluation of Multi-Modal 
Transportation Network 
This chapter provides an overview of the proposed multi-modal transportation network, as provided on 
Figure 4-1.  
As discussed in Chapter 3, there are limited non-automotive facilities within the study area.  The most robust 
facilities are related to transit. One of the primary transit routes on the island travels through West Kauai on 
Kaumualiʻi Highway, traveling east-west through the island from Kekaha to Lihue. The Kekaha to Lihue bus 
route has some of the highest boardings of all of the transit routes within the service area and also provides 
a high level of efficiency as measured by boardings per service hour.  
Where the study area is most deficient is in terms of bicycle and pedestrian conditions. Sidewalks are 
generally absent throughout the study area. Pedestrian access often occurs through de facto sidewalks, 
such as where parking lots provide some limited level of connectivity between parcels. In many instances, 
pedestrians are forced to walk in shoulders or in the grass along the front of each parcel, and sidewalks are 
often discontinuous and limited in terms of length. This creates significant challenges for persons with 
disabilities, mothers pushing strollers, and for everyone when heavy rains flood the shoulders and force 
pedestrians into the street. Lack of sidewalks near schools is a concern since many children walk to school. 
Designated bicycle facilities are even sparser than the pedestrian facilities. Currently, there are no 
designated facilities within the study area. Similar to pedestrians, cyclists are forced to use shoulders and 
areas of the roadway, often having no recourse but to mix with automobile traffic. The Focused Growth 
Scenario will create mixed-land use opportunities that will result in shorter trip lengths and more active 
transportation options. 
7.1 Multi- Modal Transportation Plan 
Improvements 
The proposed Multi- Modal Transportation Plan responds to existing deficiencies by providing an extensive 
network of bicycle and pedestrian facilities on many of the major roadways in the area.  Key elements of 
this plan include: 
Providing continuous bicycle facilities along Kaumualiʻi Highway where none currently existing.  A 
shared use path would be provided along Kaumualiʻi Highway between Hanapepe and Kekaha 
Providing shared use paths in and around Kekaha, Waimea, Hanapepe, and Eleele

Bicycle facilities generally consist of four types of facilities, which are outlined below:   
Shared Use Paths provide a separate right-of-way and are designated for the exclusive use of 
bicycles and pedestrians with vehicle cross-flow minimized. Generally, the recommended pavement 
width for a two-directional bike or multi-use path is ten (10) feet.  
Bike Lanes provide a restricted right-of-way and are designated for the use of bicycles with a striped 
lane on a street or highway. Bicycle lanes are generally five (5) feet wide. Adjacent vehicle parking 
and vehicle/pedestrian cross-flow are permitted.  
Bike Route or Signed Shared Roadways provide for a right-of-way designated by signs or shared 
lane pavement markings, or “sharrows,” for shared use with pedestrians or motor vehicles. 
West Kauaʻi Community Plan Traffic Impact Study 
January 2020 
 30 
Separated/Buffered Bikeways or Cycle Tracks provide a restricted right-of-way with physical 
separation and are designated for the use of bicycles with a raised barrier such as curbs or bollards. 
Separated bikeways are generally five (5) feet wide with a three (3) foot minimum horizontal and 
vertical separation area. Adjacent vehicle parking is permitted, and vehicle/pedestrian cross-flow is 
restricted to selected locations (e.g., driveways) indicated by breaks in the barrier and buffer. 
7.2 Multi- Modal Transportation Plan Non-
Automotive Evaluation 
Our evaluation of the proposed Multi-Modal Transportation Plan indicates that this plan provides a 
significant level of improvement over the existing condition.  Coupled with the Focus Growth Scenario, this 
plan will result in the following benefits for residents, workers, and visitors in the area. 
The primary benefit is the segregation of bicycles and pedestrians from automobiles.  By providing 
dedicated facilities, bicyclists and pedestrians will be physically separated from vehicles which 
should reduce the incidents of collisions between the travel modes. One aspect of collisions 
between automobiles and bicyclists or pedestrians is that any collisions often result in serious 
injuries or fatalities.  Therefore, any improvement which segregates automobiles from other modes 
often provides a significant benefit to their safety.  
Fostered by the Focused Growth Scenario that will create shorter distances between land uses, a secondary 
benefit is the potential diversion of vehicular trips to non-vehicular modes, as discussed in Chapter 7.  
Providing opportunities for people to walk and bike will incentivize this mode shift, which is needed to meet 
the traffic operational needs for Kamualiʻi Highway, as well as sections of other roadways.

8.1.3 Option 3 Evaluation 
The third option would reconstruct two signalized intersections as roundabouts at Kaumualiʻi Hwy/Waialo 
Rd and Kaumualiʻi Hwy/Halewili Rd, as significant right-of-way is available based on the existing intersection 
geometries. A roundabout option was also considered for Waimea Canyon Dr/Kaumuali’I Hwy. Table 8-3 
shows the operational results for this option. We concluded the following potential issues related to the 
implementation of a roundabout:  
1. Roundabouts work best when traffic flows are balanced. However, over 75% of the vehicles using 
these intersections are on Kaumualiʻi Hwy, meaning that vehicles entering the roundabout from 
Halewili Rd or Waialo Rd would likely face delays. These side street vehicles may have to wait 
significant times for gaps in oncoming traffic.  
1)  
2. If there is queuing through the roundabout, then emergency vehicles may have problems 
navigating through the roundabouts.  In congested locations, emergency vehicles are often able to 
navigate through traffic signals and side street stop-controlled intersections more easily since the 
cars physically pull to the side of the intersection if needed. 
2)  
3. The final issue is one of overall capacity. Significant right-of-way is often needed for roundabouts 
to be implemented. A two-lane roundabout is needed to achieve LOS D at Waialo Rd. One lane 
roundabouts are needed at Halewili Rd and Waimea Canyon Dr.

9. Conclusion/Findings 
This chapter summarizes our conclusions and findings. 
9.1 Key Questions 
Key items addressed by this study include: 
How does the roadway network perform after the buildout of the Planned Growth Scenario? 
Does the implementation of the Planned Growth Scenario cause any element of the roadway 
network to fail that would otherwise operate at an acceptable level? 
How would the non-vehicular transportation system operate after the implementation of the 
Planned Growth Scenario and the Multi-Modal Transportation Plan? 
If there are roadway segments which operate in a deficient fashion after the implementation of the 
Planned Growth Scenario, what policy options or physical improvements might be available to 
reduce vehicular demand? 
Can several site-specific improvements be implemented within the constraints of the overall 
transportation system? 
9.2 Findings 
The analysis concluded that there are several segments of Kaumualiʻi Highway which currently operate at 
LOS F and would operate at LOS F under the Planned Growth Scenario associated with the West Kauaʻi 
Community Plan. The Focused Growth Scenario generates some additional trips, but these trips are shorter 
and provide opportunities to shift from auto to walk and/or bike trips.  Additionally, an increase in services 
and employment in West Kaua’i associated with development under the Planned Growth Scenario could 
reduce vehicle miles traveled on the roadways in Kaua’i by shortening commutes and trips associated with 
other routine activities, potentially improving congestion in West Kaua’i, as well as on other parts of the 
island.  
While the typical approach would be to recommend widening the congested study roadways to 
accommodate additional projected demand, we recommend that alternative approaches be considered: 
Add bicycle facilities on roadways such as Kaumualiʻi Highway  
Expand existing pedestrian facilities 
Expand transit service within the West Kauaʻi Community Plan area 
Instead of roadway widening, implement signal improvements, install signals, or install roundabouts 
at six intersections along Kaumualiʻi Highway 
Reconstruct intersections to improve local access and circulation
