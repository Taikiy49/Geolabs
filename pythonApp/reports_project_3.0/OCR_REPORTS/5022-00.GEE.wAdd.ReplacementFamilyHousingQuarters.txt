2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae Volcano is
estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity
during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano Shield volcanic
activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations
These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The housing development site of the Marine Corps Base Hawaii at Kaneohe Bay
is located on Mokapu Peninsula which was built by three volcanic vents of the Honolulu
Volcanic Series These vents are believed to be among the oldest of the series The
pyroclastic deposits and lava flows from vents formed salients permitting the growth of a
coral reef that merged with the main body of the Island of Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level of Oahu During the times of low stands of the sea
emerged coral reefs were blown inland by the prevailing winds to form sand dunes

In general fills and backfills composed of the on site silty and clayey soils should
be moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture content placed
in level lifts not exceeding 200 mm in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction The non expansive select granular fill materials and
other granular fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts on exceeding 200 mm in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction In general compaction
should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other similar types of
acceptable compaction equipment

3 6 Underground Utility Lines
In general we recommend that a granular bedding layer consisting of 150 mm of
open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be provided below new utility pipes
planned at the project site Free draining granular materials such as open graded
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to
about 300 mm above the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper backfill material around the pipes
and placement could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 300 mm above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils with
a maximum particle size of 150 mm The trench backfill should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum 200 mm level loose lifts
and mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce
the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are below
pavement areas the upper 1 meter of the trench backfill below the pavement surface
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
3 7 Drainage

be diverted away from the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape
watering near the foundations and slabs also should be avoided Planters next to
foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the
potential for appreciable water infiltration into the subsurface

The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling twelve test borings designated as Boring Nos 101 through 112 extending to
depths of about 6 1 to 6 6 meters below the existing ground surface The test borings
were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and
rotary coring tools The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologists who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 12
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 75 mm OD Modified California sampler with a 63 5 kilogram
hammer falling 760 mm In addition some samples were obtained from the borings
drilled in general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test
and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 50 mm OD standard penetration
sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the second and third 150 mm of an 450 mm drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket

Shear tests were performed in the Direct Shear Machine which is of the strain

sample was sheared under varying confining loads in order to determine the

percent of the maximum value was taken to determine the shear strength
parameters Test results are presented on Plates D1 through DS
