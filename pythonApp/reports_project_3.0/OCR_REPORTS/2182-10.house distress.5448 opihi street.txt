House Distress 5448 Opihi Street

In accordance with your request we have explored the near surface
soil conditions at the above referenced residence The purpose of our
exploration was to evaluate the possible causes of the observed distress
to the house as related to the foundation and or soil conditions The
study consisted of drilling two 2 shallow borings along the mauka
exterior wall of the house with portable drilling equipment excavating
three 3 shallow hand dug test pits to expose the actual house
foundation performing laboratory soil tests and engineering analysis
This report summarizes our findings and recommendations
SITE CONDITIONS
The existing lot is generally level with a one story wood and cw
house on the site A swinming pool is located in the rear of the lot
Cracks were visible in the CMU walls in the front portion of the house
The crack through the mauka bedroom wall is especially noticeable Cracks
were also noted in the low CMU wall in the rear portion of the house
where a previous extension was constructed Signs of movement were also
evident along the rear wall of the extension

FIELD EXPLORATION AND SOT CONDITIONS
Two 2 borings were drilled along the mauka wall of the house with
portable hand carried drilling equipment One 1 boring was drilled
Close to the large crack in the bedroom wall while the second boring was
drilled near the rear of the house The borings were drilled to depths of
about five 5 feet below the existing ground level Both borings
encountered gray to brown expansive clay soil to the full depth drilled
The clay soils were generally quite moist and stiff to medium stiff
Three 3 hand dug test pits were excavated along the foundation
walls to expose the house foundation conditions One 1 test pit was
excavated next to the large crack in the mauka bedroom wall another was
excavated along the mauka wall where no cracks were visible and the third
was excavated next to the crack in the wall between the original house and
the previously constructed extension The test pits along the mauka wall
TP 1 and TP 2 indicated poor foundation construction in fact no
foundation was encountered below the CMU wall The test pits encountered
a layer of boulders below the CMU wall underlain by moist and soft clay
soils
The locations of the borings and test pits are indicated on the Site
Plan Plate 2 Detailed information regarding the conditions encountered
in the borings and test pits are presented on Plates A 1 through A 5
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration indicates that the primary cause of the
observed house distress is poor foundation construction The two 2 test
pits that were excavated along the mauka wall of the house did not
encounter a footing below the CMU wall instead it appears that the wall
was constructed on a layer of boulders This lack of a footing is
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2182 10 Page 3
detrimental in two ways first the boulders cannot provide uniform
support for the wall resulting in differential movement of the wall
secondly the boulders allow water to accumulate under the house which
causes saturation and softening of the clay soils below the house
resulting in increased differential movement The result is erratic
behavior of the walls and differential movement between various portions
of the house
Remedial Work
Several alternatives for the remedial work at the subject house are
described below
Leave As Is Minor Patching
The least expensive alternative would be to leave the house
foundations in the present condition and only patch the larger
cracks With this alternative future movement of the house should
be expected and periodic repair would be required A structural
engineer should be consulted to evaluate the safety of the structural
members of the house on a periodic basis
Underpin Foundations
A new foundation could be installed below the existing house to
support the bearing walls This would involve hand excavating below
the walls to allow placement of concrete for a new footing The new
foundations would have to be constructed in small increments to avoid
destabilizing the existing house Due to the limited access around
and in the house this method would be very expensive Some
additional movements could still occur even after installation of the
new foundations since the soils under the house may have been already
saturated and weakened
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2182 10 Page 4
A cement grout could be pumped under the bearing walls to attempt to
cement the boulders and form a footing This method would require
drilling holes along the bearing walls both outside as well as
inside the house and pumping a cement grout into the holes under
pressure This method would probably be less expensive than the
underpinning alternative however there is some uncertainty whether
the cement grout could achieve sufficient penetration into the
subsoil to form a competent footing An experienced grouting
contractor is a must for this method
Demolition of Existing House
The extreme alternative would be to demolish the existing house and
construct a new house with proper foundations This method would be
the most expensive but would allow installation of proper foundations
and would have the least chance of future foundation movements The
new house should be properly designed by registered professional
architects and engineers and should include a soil exploration for
the house foundation design
New Addition
A new addition to the existing house is contemplated Due to the
inadequate foundations at the existing house we recommend that if a new
addition is constructed it should be structurally separated from the
existing house to avoid differential movements The existing soils are
expansive and therefore special foundations should be utilized to resist
the expansion forces of the clay soils General guidelines for the
foundation design of the addition are presented below
1 The adobe clay soils below the foundations have high volume
change potential and can shrink when dried or heave considerably
if saturated in the future Therefore we recommend that the

Design Review
Plans and specifications for site grading foundation and pavement
design should be forwarded to the geotechnical engineer for review and
written comments prior to construction to see that the intent of our
recommendation is included
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the site grading remedial work and footing
excavation be observed by the geotechnical engineer during construction to
see that adequate compaction has been obtained and that the subsurface
conditions are consistent with the findings of our study
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and
approved by the geotechnical engineer prior to hauling to the site for
use
If conditions are different from those presented in this report then
appropriate modifications to the design should be made
