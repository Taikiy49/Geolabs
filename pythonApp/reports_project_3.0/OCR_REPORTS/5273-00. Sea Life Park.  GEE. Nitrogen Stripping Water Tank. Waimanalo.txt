In accordance with our fee proposal dated May 27 2004 we have conducted a
geotechnical engineering exploration for the proposed Nitrogen Stripping Water Tank
project at the existing Sea Life Park facility This report summarizes our work on the
project and presents our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations derived

intended for the foundation design input for the nitrogen stripping water tank only and are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the existing Sea Life Park facility in the
_ Waimanalo area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 Based on the information
provided it is desired to construct a nitrogen stripping water tank measuring
approximately 18 feet high and 16 feet in diameter Detailed information pertaining to the
structural loads and water tank construction were not available at the time this report was
prepared Based on the information provided we understand that it is desired to evaluate
the soil bearing pressure and subsoil profile for seismic design consideration at the new
nitrogen stripping water tank site

Our boring generally encountered a surface fill layer consisting of stiff clayey silts

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
In general our field exploration encountered clayey silts and silty sands overlying
basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 53 feet
below the existing ground surface at the proposed nitrogen stripping water tank site
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled boring at the time of our field exploration
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration we believe
that concrete slab on grade with a perimeter ring footing may be used to support the new
water tank An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for the design
of the new water tank foundations bearing on 6 inches of compacted base course over the
recompacted in situ soils or new compacted fills placed to achieve the design finish
grades
Based on the laboratory test results we recommend that the near surface clayey
soils be capped with a minimum of 12 inches of compacted base course below the tank
floor slab areas The base course serves as a capping fill layer to reduce the potential for
distress to the future slab on grade resulting from swelling of the clayey soils and to
provide more uniform support for the tank slab
In general the subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The slab subgrade should
be kept moist and free of shrinkage cracks prior to placement of the base course and slab
concrete Detailed discussions of these items and our geotechnical engineering
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report

Soil Profile Type for Seismic Design
Our field exploration at the site generally encountered approximately 10 feet of stiff
clayey silts overlying nard basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depth explored
of approximately 53 feet below the existing ground surface Based on the average
penetration resistance N values of the subsurface materials encountered below the
foundation subgrade level and the geology of the area we believe that the project site may
be classified as a Very Dense Soil and Soft Rock Therefore we believe that the seismic
design of the building structure at the project site may be designed based on a Soil Profile
Type Sc in accordance with Table 16 J of the Uniform Building Code 1997
Slab On Grade
Based on our laboratory swell test results shown on Plate 5 the near surface
clayey soils exhibit a moderate shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the
soil moisture contents To reduce the potential for future distress to the slab on grade
resulting from shrinking and swelling of these clayey soils due to changes in the moisture
content we recommend that the subgrade soils below the slab concrete be properly
prepared and capped with at least 12 inches of base course compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction The 12 inch compacted base course would also serve to
provide more uniform support for the tank slab
The compacted base course should extend a minimum of 12 inches laterally
beyond the limits of the tank structure The base course should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction For the design of
structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of about 250 pounds per square inch per
inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted base course The area adjacent to
the tank slab should be backfilled tightly against the slab with compacted on site soils and
graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around
the slab and foundation
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the current design concept we anticipate
that minimal site grading work will be required for construction of the new water tank At
the on set of earthwork the areas within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly
cleared Vegetation debris deleterious materials and other unsuitable materials should
be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during
clearing below areas designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm
natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted
general fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
or used in landscaping areas if appropriate

After the clearing operation areas beneath future slab should be over excavated
where necessary to provide the minimum 12 inch thick compacted base course layer
below the slab concrete Prior to placement of the base course layer the subgrade soils
should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optinum moisture and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Where shrinkage cracks are noted after preparation of the subgrade we
recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks and recompacted
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted general fill
The on site clayey soils generated from excavations may be re used as a source of
general fill material if needed General fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding
8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
or other types of acceptable compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding
should not be allowed to compact the on site clayey and silty soils
Base course material should meet the material requirements of Section 30 of the
City and County Standard Specifications tmported fill material if required should consist
of non expansive imported granular material such as crushed coral basalt gravel or
cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles
larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of
20 or more and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in
accordance with ASTM D 1883 Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use
Base course and imported granular fill materials should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test
procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density

Waterlines
We understand that new waterlines may be required for this project In general for
support of the waterlines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
1 inch minus granular fill material be used under the pipes The 1 inch minus granular fill
materials should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the
pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes The 1 inch minus granular fill
material should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction It is critical
that the 1 inch minus granular fill materials be well compacted to reduce the potential for
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the
sides of the pipes Improper backfill material and placement procedures could result in
settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils or select granular
material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction Where trenches are located in paved areas the upper 3 feet of the
trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent
relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the water tank structure should be sloped to shed
water away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding
Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete
to reduce the potential for water infiltration
