INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Farrington Highway Improvements project located in the
Villages of Kapolei development on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommen
dations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended for the design of pavement structures site grading
and utility trenches only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in
age As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau banked against its
eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the ocean basins and sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available and the sea
levels rose above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was correspond
ingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level When the
sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated
at higher elevations
Physical and chemical weathering and agricultural development within the last
century have brought the project site to its present form

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results the project site is
underlain by fills and residual soils consisting of stiff to hard clayey and silty soils over
saprolite soils or basalt formation to the maximum depths explored The soils encoun
tered at the site are generally competent and should provide adequate support for the
new pavements planned for this project
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3284 00 Page 8
Our laboratory test results indicate that the near surface clayey soils have a
moderate to high swelling potential when subjected to changes in the moisture content
Therefore special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation and pavement
design to reduce the potential shrink swell effects on new pavement structures
To provide better subsurface drainage conditions we recommend that a
longitudinal edge drain system be used below the new pavement structures In addition
we anticipate that site grading consisting of cuts and fills on the order of about 2 to 7 feet
will be needed for the proposed roadway improvements
Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of pavement site grading
and other geotechnical aspects of the project are presented in the following sections of
this report

Based on the results of our laboratory test the near surface soils exhibit a
moderate to high expansion potential when subjected to changes in the moisture content
To reduce the swelling potential of the road subgrade the upper 8 inches of the subgrade
should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction The subbase and base
course should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO T 180
ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
If shrinkage cracks appear on the subgrade prior to placement of subbase course
the subgrade soils will need to be scarified to a minimum depth of 6 inches moisture
conditioned to a minimum of 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
recompacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
We understand that sidewalks are planned along the alignment of the proposed
roadway improvements To reduce the potential for cracking of the concrete due to
expansion of the moderately to highly expansive subgrade soils and to provide more
uniform support we recommend that a minimum of 6 inches of select borrow be
incorporated below the sidewalks The subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum
depth of 6 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction The select borrow base
should also be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Construction
joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion
joints at right angle intersections

Longitudinal Edge Drain System
Generally saturation of the structural section or underlaying foundation pumping
due to saturation of the base course and or subgrade soils is one of the primary distress
mechanisms observed in pavement structures In addition free water voids in the
pavement section repeated heavy wheel loads and an erodible base can also cause
pumping and pavement deterioration
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3284 00 Page 12
To reduce moisture infiltration into the pavement structure and to collect and divert
subsurface free water from the pavement section we recommend that an edge drain
system with outlet pipes on the sides of the roadway be provided The outlets must
discharge into appropriate drainage outlet structures such as drainage ditches culverts
or catch basins Daylighting the base course layer is not effective in draining since it
would be subjected to clogging from roadway debris and vegetation In addition
daylighted layers may allow silty material or storm water from ditches to enter the
pavement structure
The typical edge drain would consist of a 4 inch PVC perforated pipe with the
perforations facing down surrounded by a permeable backfill material such as No 3B
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation A typical edge drain section is presented on
Plate 3 The bottom of the edge drain trench should be at least 6 inches below the
bottom of the subbase layer A filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be
placed around the permeable backfill material and below the base course layer to reduce
the potential for migration of fines into the drainage material Care should be exercised
when placing the permeable base and or construction of the surface course to avoid
damaging the edge drain collector pipe As an alternative a geocomposite fin drain may
be used in lieu of a PVC collector pipe as shown on Plate 3

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce
potential contamination of the excavated materials
After clearing and grubbing the exposed subgrade as previously indicated should
be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches properly moisture conditioned to
about 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and recompacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction All subgrades should be kept moist
until covered with subbase course Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3284 00 Page 14
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as deter
mined by AASHTO T 180 ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during the clearing operation should be
over excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavations should be
backfilled with engineered fill compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Prior to placement of the backfill the over excavated subgrade
should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches properly moisture conditioned
to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Contract documents should include
additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill
placement to account for variations in the over excavation quantities
Fills and Backfills
Generally the on site materials generated from the excavation may be reused as
a source of fill and backfill materials provided that they contains no lumps or
fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension Aggregate subbase
course base course and asphalt treated base course material should meet the
requirements as specified in Section 700 of the Standard Specifications for Road
Bridge and Public Works Construction Department of Transportation State of
Hawaii 1994
All permanent fill slopes should have slope inclinations of 2H 1V or flatter All fills
placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched Benching
should be of sufficient width to permit operations of placing and compacting
equipment Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling past the design fill

the top of slopes and slope planting should be utilized to reduce the potential for

If imported fill materials are required as general fill and backfill materials the

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
The fill and backfill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches
in loose thickness moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
The fill and backfill material within the upper 3 feet below the pavement finish
grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The
pavement finish subgrade should be kept moist until covered by the aggregate
subbase course
Observations and soil density tests should be performed during grading to assist
the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper
moisture content on each fill lift Where compaction is less than required
additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture content
as necessary to obtain the specified compaction

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling ten
borings to depths of about 9 5 to 11 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate boring locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled
using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the test borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis The classifications were further reviewed by visual observations and
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic presentation of the materials

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was formulated to aid in evaluating pertinent
engineering properties of the soil and materials sampled
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 is used as an aid to soil classification and to aid
in evaluating certain properties such as relative saturation The moisture content
expressed as a percentage of the dry weight of the tested sample and unit weight are
shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Three one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results are summarized on
Plate B 1
Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is shown on Plate B 2
Three California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on the
near surface materials to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement subgrade
support The test results are presented on Plates B 3 through B 4
