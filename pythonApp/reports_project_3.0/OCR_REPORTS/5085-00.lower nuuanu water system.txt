2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island
Waianae Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and the
eastern flank of Waianae Volcano was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking
against its eastern flank After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported down
slope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials
reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant
and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
The project site is located in Nuuanu Valley an amphitheater headed valley
carved by erosion into Koolau Basalt Filling the base of this valley is a thick sequence
of alluvium interbedded with deposits of lava and pyroclastics of the Honolulu Volcanic
Series

Recent and Older Alluvium
Recent alluvium generally consists of red brown gray or black unconsolidated
soils consisting of silts sands gravel and clays with some weathered cobbles
and or boulders The soils may have a mottled appearance and range from very
soft and or very loose to very stiff and or dense Some shells or organic material
may be present in the alluvium near the coastline
The materials are typically deposited along streams and marshes and mantling
hills and pre existing topographic low features The materials are derived from
the erosion of terrestrial rock and soils transported by stream or storm water
runoff activity from a source region and deposited elsewhere Older more
deeply buried alluvium may be transformed to form an indurated rock identified
as siltstone sandstone or conglomerate depending upon the predominant
material grain size The indurated deposits may be identified locally as older
alluvium
Basalt Formation

3 1 Horizontal Directional Drilling Methods
Horizontal directional drilling HDD is a trenchless steerable installation method
using a multi axis drilling machine to bore a small diameter pilot hole in the subsurface
The pilot hole is bored using either controlled fluid jetting or fluid assisted mechanical
cutting or a combination of the two methods Depending on the subsurface conditions
water only or water mixed with bentonite and or polymer can be used as a drilling fluid ina
directional drilling application The pilot hole is reamed as necessary to accommodate

the pipe to be installed The pipe is pulled back into the reamed hole by the drilling
machine Most HDD systems use slurry to stabilize the walls of the borehole and to
reduce the frictional drag on the pipeline being installed Steering corrections are made in
the soils by rotating the slanted cutter shoe to the desired orientation and then advancing
the shoe and string without rotation
In general the HDD pilot hole is drilled nearly horizontal with entry and exit angles
between 8 and 15 degrees measured from horizontal Shallow working pits may be
required at each end of the bore to allow placement of the drill pipe and to allow transition
to the conventional pipe trench installation The product pipe should be installed in one
continuous operation preferably without pause and should protrude a sufficient distance
beyond the entry and exit points to allow proper termination or connection at a later time
Typically high density polyethylene HDPE steel and polyvinyl chloride PVC pipes may
be installed by HDD methods
The vertical and horizontal alignments of the drill hole are typically monitored from
the surface by a locator which tracks signals from the transmitter contained inside the drill
head housing Other methods of tracking the cutting tool such as in hole steering are
generally recommended to provide more precise line and grade information The
contractor should carefully monitor the drilling operation when approaching property
boundaries and existing underground utilities or structures to assure that proper
clearances are maintained
The feasibility of the HDD technique should be established based on ground
conditions diameter and length of the pipe or duct line and the required achievable
degree of accuracy in both alignment and grade HDD is less attractive where precise
line and grade tolerances are required in soils containing cobbles and boulders in a soil
matrix especially with larger diameter pipelines The HDD method is best suited for
soils with some cohesion i e clays However the method has been successfully used
in granular soils by adding bentonite to the drilling fluid to aid in the borehole stability
The HDD method is not particularly well suited for gravelly soils greater than 50 percent
gravel size particles due to difficulties in drilling the pilot hole and maintaining the

Pipe Size and Material
For the HDD method of water line installation the most important design
consideration for the pipe is the tensile stresses imposed on the pipe when it is
pulled into place through the drilled hole The magnitude of these tensile
stresses is a function of the length of the installation as well as the subsurface
conditions Other important pipe design considerations are the internal and
external pressures acting on the pipe during and after installation In addition
the selected pipe material must have adequate corrosion resistance or be
protected to resist internal and external corrosion
We envision that HDPE pipes will be used if the water line is installed by HDD
methods HDPE pipes generally have low tensile strength and may be
susceptible to failure during installation unless they are properly designed This
limits the length of installation that can be completed with an HDPE pipe
Therefore it is critical that the pipe be selected with an adequate allowable
pulling tensile capacity Pipe stress analyses should be performed to evaluate
if an HDPE pipe can withstand the pulling stresses during pipe installation and
the external pressures hydrostatic and soil on the installed pipeline
The HDPE pipe should be selected to provide the maximum pipe thickness and
strength for the proposed installation based on the results of the pipe stress
analyses considerations regarding HDD installation procedures probable drill
hole sizes and materials in filling the annulus between the HDD installed HDPE
pipeline and the drilled hole The contractor should adequately and properly
ballast the pipelines during installation into the HDD drill hole to reduce the pipe

Methods
Microtunneling is an installation method where a pipe is jacked behind a remotely
controlled steerable laser guided tunnel boring machine TBM capable of installing
underground pipes at highly accurate lines and grades The tunnel boring machine can be
auger slurry or earth pressure balance type machines In general the TBM is located
below ground at the level of the pipe being installed Jacking and receiving pits are
required to accommodate the TBM and other microtunneling equipment As the TBM
excavates the soils the pipe behind the TBM is being pushed simultaneously by hydraulic
jacks The excavated material is removed during the microtunneling process by a mixture
of water and slurry which is disposed either through a pipe or placed on a balanced
screw auger or placed on a screw conveyor system The microtunneled pipe can function
as the carrier water pipe or it can function as the sleeve for an internally mounted water
pipe
Pipes that can be installed by microtunneling include the following polymer
concrete pipe fiberglass lined polymer concrete pipe fiber reinforced plastic pipe
Permalock steel casing pipe reinforced concrete pipe and vitrified clay pipe
An auger type tunneling machine is typically used in ground conditions consisting of
over consolidated cohesive soils or in ground conditions consisting of cohesionless soils
where ground water is not encountered To reduce the potential for surface settlement
both slurry and earth pressure balance EPB type tunneling machines create internal
pressures to counter the external pressures from the in situ soils and groundwater Slurry
and EPB tunneling machines are suitable in soft and wet ground conditions In a slurry
W O INC Page 16
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
tunneling machine the pressure of the slurry counter balances the in situ soil and
groundwater pressures In an EPB tunneling machine the excavated soil is used to
counter balance the in situ soil and groundwater pressures by controlling the amount of
excavated soil being removed from the tunnel shield A slurry tunneling machine can
balance groundwater pressures up to about 100 feet below the groundwater table while an
EPB tunneling machine can balance groundwater pressures to no more than 10 feet
below the groundwater table
Selecting the right machine cutter head for the ground condition is critical Different
types of cutter heads are used for various types of soil and rock conditions The length
and depth of the drive and groundwater conditions are also taken into consideration when
determining the type of cutter head used Ground conditions where cobbles and boulders
are encountered might halt the progress of the TBM If the pipe alignment is located in an
area consisting of different soil types the cutter head may encounter a mixed face
condition which could lead to steering difficulties surface settlement and or deflection of
the TBM causing an offset in the line and grade
Surface settlement is usually considered to be the primary source of damage to
adjacent streets utilities and other surface facilities during the microtunneling operations
For the microtunneling construction method the loss of ground is generally associated
with soil squeezing raveling or running in front of or into the shield over cutting by the
shield shield steering adjustments and soil movement to fill the void between the pipe
and tunnel wall created by the differential diameters between the TBM and the pipe In
addition to surface settlement due to actual tunneling procedures surface settlement may
be caused by the dewatering operations within the jacking and receiving pits
Minor ground losses during the advance of the heading are unavoidable with
normal construction techniques The actual magnitudes of these losses are largely
dependent on the type of ground groundwater conditions overload factor in cohesive
soils size and depth of tunnel and the skill of the operator in steering the shield or
machine Empirical methods have been developed for estimating surface settlement due
to soft ground tunneling by study of observed settlements on past projects The typical
W O INC Page 17
Hawaii California

settlement pattern that develops above a soft ground tunnel is a trough shaped depression
resembling an inverted normal probability curve with the maximum settlement occurring
above the tunnel centerline The behavior of the ground is also influenced by the cover
above the tunnel When the cover is greater than about two tunnel diameters arching
develops in the ground above the tunnel reducing the potential for significant ground
movement and surface settlement

As mentioned previously the subsurface conditions at the stream crossings

Because groundwater was encountered at depths of about 6 8 to 13 6 feet below
the existing roadway we envision that a slurry tunneling machine will be required

counter balance the groundwater and in situ soil pressures
Soils that contain cobbles and boulders such as the older alluvium encountered in
our borings can be problematic for microtunneling equipment A boulder or cluster
of cobbles encountered in the heading of the tunnel boring machine may severely
hinder or even halt progress of the microtunneling operations When the tunnel
boring machine encounters cobbles in a soil matrix such as the older alluvium at
the project site the tunnel boring machine will tend to deflect off the boulders
causing a change in the direction of the planned pipe alignment Therefore we
recommend that the vertical alignment of the proposed water line to be installed by
microtunneling be kept above the older alluvium layer and within the basalt rock

Jacking and Receiving Pits
Since the TBM is located below the ground surface jacking and receiving pits will
be required for the microtunneling construction method The depths of the pits
are usually about 3 to 4 feet below the pipeline invert The length and width of
the shafts are sized based upon the sizes of the TBM and the jacking frame and
the lengths of the pipe segments The receiving pit does not have to
accommodate the jacking frame therefore the receiving pit is usually smaller
than the jacking pit Due to the relatively deep depths required for
microtunneling shoring of the jacking and receiving pits is usually required
Based on the available information the distance from the top of the bridge decks

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling four test borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 The borings were
extended to depths of about 45 5 to 52 feet below the existing ground surface The test
borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight
augers and rotary coring tools The approximate locations of the borings drilled are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer or geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket Penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of the rock formation and or boulder materials encountered at the
site were obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and classification

from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Eight Unconfined Compressive Strength tests ASTM D 2938 were performed
on selected core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the basalt
rock formation encountered Results of the unconfined compression tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths and are
presented on Plate B 1
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples of the upper clayey soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Four Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 including four Hydrometer
tests ASTM D 422 were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification Graphic presentations of the
grain size distribution are provided on Plates B 3
Two Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics The results of the direct shear
tests are presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
