PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The existing Kahala Wastewater Pump Station (WWPS) is at 4929 Kahala Avenue 
adjacent to the parking lot of the Waialae Beach Park in the Kahala area on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location 
Map, Plate 1. 
The existing Kahala WWPS building is located near Kahala Avenue and the 
northern lot boundary, adjacent to the Waialae Country Club facility. The existing concrete 
building consists of a one story above grade and a deep basement level for the pumps 
and motors. 
Based on the information provided, it is desired to improve and extend the life of 
the existing Kahala WWPS. The proposed improvements will consist of rehabilitation of 
the existing wet wells and replacement of the existing generator, pump motors, shafts 
and appurtenances. Outside of the WWPS building, the existing underground fuel tank 
will be removed, and a new aboveground 6,000-gallon diesel fuel tank will be installed at 
the same location near the northern boundary of the site. In addition, the existing electrical

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu is comprised largely of the weathered remnants of the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of the western 
third of the island, while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of the eastern 
two-thirds of the island. It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still 
active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu. The 
project site is located on the lower southern flank of the Koolau Volcano. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters 
protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the 
sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous 
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and 
induration of the calcareous reef materials.  
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site at the existing Kahala WWPS is located at 4929 Kahala Avenue 
adjacent to the parking lot of the Waialae Beach Park in the Kahala area on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. The existing WWPS is a one story concrete building with a deep basement 
level for the pumps and motors. 
The new fuel tank will be located on the northern side of the pump station, near 
the end of the driveway pavement. The two new HECO transformers will be installed on 
the southern side of the property. The project site is approximately rectangular and is 
secured with either a chain link fence or CRM perimeter wall along the property limits. 
The entrance facing Kahala Avenue is secured with a double swing gate. Based on our 
field observations and the project site plan, the project site is relatively flat with existing 
ground elevations at about +5 to +6 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the information provided, the existing WWPS will be renovated in 
anticipation of the proposed improvements for the Kahala Wastewater Pump Station 
Improvements project. Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered during our field 
exploration, we recommend supporting the fuel tank and electrical transformers with a 
shallow foundation system (reinforced concrete pad). Detailed discussions and 
recommendations for foundation design and earthworks are presented in the following 
sections. 
Shallow Foundation System 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend using 
shallow spread footing/concrete slabs-on-grade with thickened-edge (reinforced concrete 
pad) foundations to support the new fuel tank and electrical transformers. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used for the design of the concrete pad bearing on recompacted on-site soils and/or new 
compacted fills needed to achieve the design finished grades. The bearing value 
recommended is for dead-plus-live-loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for 
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Due to the shallow groundwater and loose and soft to very soft deposits encountered 
below the groundwater level, we recommend that the footing embedment be minimized.  In 
general, the bottom of the pad should be embedded a maximum of 12 inches below the

lowest adjacent exterior grade. Foundations located next to retaining walls, other 
foundations, utility trenches, or easements should be embedded below a 45-degree 
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench (or retaining 
wall). Alternatively, the footings should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the 
utility lines or bottom of the retaining wall. 
Our field exploration and experience in the area indicate that the near-surface sandy 
soils may be difficult to recompact to dense conditions once disturbed. To reduce the 
potential for appreciable difficulty in subgrade recompaction, we recommend properly 
preparing the subgrade elevation prior to fill placement and/or construction of improvements 
on top. In addition, we recommend providing a minimum 6 inches of compacted aggregate 
base course materials below the footings and concrete slabs-on-grade. The aggregate base 
course should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum water content and compacted 
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
The footing/slab-on-grade finished subgrades should be compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction prior to placement of aggregate base course. Due to relative 
high groundwater level encountered, soft or yielding ground conditions, if encountered, at 
the exposed subgrade level should be over-excavated until firm materials are encountered 
and backfilled with free-draining granular materials consisting of open-graded No. 3 Fine 
gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or 
equivalent) below the aggregate base layer for uniform support.  
If the foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the foundations to be less than 
1 inch.  
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by a combination of frictional 
resistance between the base of the foundation and the bearing material and by passive 
earth pressure developed against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of the 
footings. A coefficient of friction of 0.35 may be used for footings bearing on compacted 
aggregate base course materials. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be 
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth 
(pcf). The passive earth pressure value assumes the concrete footings will be cast neatly 
against the medium dense on-site soils or new compacted fills. Otherwise, the soils around 
the footings should be well-compacted (minimum of 90 percent relative compaction). Unless 
covered by pavements or slabs, the passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches 
below the finished grade should be neglected. 
It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the footings should be backfilled 
tightly against the edges of the slabs with relatively impervious soils as soon as concrete is 
cured or practical. These areas should also be graded to divert water away from the slabs 
to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs.

Underground Fuel Tank Removal 
Based on the as-built information provided, the existing 2,000-gallon underground 
fuel tank is a fiberglass tank of approximately 11 feet long and 6 feet in diameter and is tied 
down to a reinforced concrete base slab at a depth of about 11 feet below the existing 
ground surface. We understand that the existing underground fuel tank will be removed 
and replaced with a new aboveground 6,000-gallon diesel fuel tank. Based on the subsoil 
conditions encountered in our field exploration, it appears that temporary shoring will be 
necessary for the proposed fuel tank removal. In general, good construction practices 
should be utilized for the excavation and backfilling for the removal of the existing 
underground fuel tank. The contractor should determine the method and equipment to be 
used for excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations. In addition, the 
excavations should comply with the applicable federal, state, and local safety 
requirements. The contractor should be responsible for the excavation shoring design 
and installation. 
Due to the groundwater level encountered at 3.7 feet below the existing ground 
surface at the time of our field exploration, the bottom of the excavation will be below the 
groundwater level. In general, free-draining granular materials, such as No. 2 Rock 
(ASTM C33, No. 4 gradation), should be used for the initial excavation backfill up to about 
12 inches above the groundwater level to provide adequate support for subsequent 
backfill materials.  The No. 2 Rock backfill should be wrapped with a layer of non-woven 
filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) to prevent the potential migration of fines. The upper 
portion of the excavation backfill from the level 12 inches above the groundwater level to 
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of select granular fill or aggregate 
base course materials moisture-conditioned to above the optimum water content and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Select granular fill should consist of non-expansive granular material such as 
crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The material should be well-graded from 
coarse to fine, with particles no larger than 3 inches in their largest dimension. The 
material should also contain between 10 and 30 percent fines (particles passing the No. 
200 sieve). Select granular fill should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and 
should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D1883. Geolabs should test imported fill materials for conformance with these 
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry 
density. Compaction should be accomplished by rollers without vibratory actions or other

Site Grading 
We anticipate that the site grading work for the project will generally consist of 
minimal cuts and fills except for the backfill of the removed underground fuel tank. At the 
on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared. 
Vegetation, construction debris, deleterious materials, and other unsuitable materials should 
be removed and disposed of properly off-site or in a designated area to reduce the potential 
for contamination of the excavated materials with the spoils.  
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing should be over-excavated to 
expose firm natural ground, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with imported 
fill materials. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site. 
Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation 
and engineered fill placement to account for variations in the over-excavation quantities. 
Imported fill and backfill materials, if required, should consist of non-expansive select 
granular fill material. Fill and backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, 
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers 
to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of 
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor site grading operations to observe whether 
undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation process and to confirm 
whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our field 
exploration. 
Utility Trench 
We envision that new utility may be required for the proposed project. In general, 
granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) 
is recommended under the utility lines. If soft/loose soils are encountered at or near the 
invert of the pipes, a stabilization layer consisting of an additional 18 inches of No. 3B Fine 
gravel wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided 
below the bedding layer for uniform support. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation) should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the 
duct lines to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort 
of the backfill, thus reducing the possibility of pipe damage. It is critical to use this 
free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling one boring, 
designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 27 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The boring was drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight 
augers and coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and textural 
examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for 
Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Log of Boring, Plate A-1. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the boring drilled in general 
accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the boring drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Boring indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample 
depths. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Graphic 
presentation of the grain size distributions is provided on Plate B-1. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the sandy materials tested. The test results 
are presented on Plate B-2. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a 
bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-3.
