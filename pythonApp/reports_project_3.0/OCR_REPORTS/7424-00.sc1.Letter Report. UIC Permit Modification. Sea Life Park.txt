PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is at Sea Life Park Hawaii in the Waimanalo area on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii.  Based on the available information, three injection wells (Nos. 1, 3 and 5) 
were extended below their permitted depths. To rectify the unpermitted action, the State of 
Hawaii Department of Health (DOH) has granted permission to Sea Life Park Hawaii to 
submit a UIC Permit Modification Application along with a Geological Subsurface 
Condition report to address the injection well depth extensions. 
Our scope of work was to complete a geological report documenting our drilling, 
logging, and cement grout abandonment of a single test borehole drilled to a minimum 
depth of about 260 feet below the ground surface adjacent to the existing Injection Well 
No. 3. This report is prepared in support of the UIC Permit Modification Application for 
changes in injection well dimensions at existing Injection Well Nos. 1, 3 and 5.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age.  After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield, volcanic activity returned with a series 
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations.  These series are referred to as 
the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is near the coastline at the base of the Koolau Mountains at 
Makapuu in the Waimanalo area of the Island of Oahu.  Based on a review of available 
geological information, the surface of the project site is generally underlain by near-surface 
alluvial and colluvial deposits containing basaltic boulders and mixed soils with some 
dunes of calcareous beach sand. The surface deposits are underlain in the shallow 
subsurface by basaltic rock (Kaupo Flow) belonging to the Honolulu Volcanic Series, 
Koko Member. Based on our literature review, the lava rock of the younger Honolulu 
Volcanic Series overlies older lava of the Koolau Volcanic Series, which forms the 
basement rock formation comprising this portion of the Island of Oahu. 
SUBSURFACE EXPLORATION
Our field exploration was conducted in general conformance with our Proposed 
Plan for Test Boring Exploration document dated October 21, 2016. The subsurface 
exploration was conducted between November 21 and 29, 2016. The 4-inch diameter test 
boring identified as Boring No. 1 was drilled in the adjacent paved parking lot 
approximately 15 feet northwesterly of the existing Injection Well No. 3. The approximate 
location of Boring No. 1 and Injection Well No. 3 is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Boring No. 1 was drilled to a depth of about 261.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface using a CME-75 truck-mounted drill rig equipped with rotary wire line coring tools.  
Clean potable water was used as the drilling fluid. The drilling return fluid (where 
encountered) was collected and contained in a surface mud pan placed at the borehole 
collar.
The borehole was advanced through the near-surface overburden materials with 
4-inch diameter solid-stem augers from the ground surface to a depth of about 10.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The drilling method was then converted to HQ wire line 
coring with a diamond bit. Four-inch diameter temporary surface casing was advanced to 
a depth of about 27 feet below the ground surface in an effort to maintain the drilling fluid 
return circulation. Continuous cores of the subsurface materials were retrieved extending 
between about 10.5 feet and the terminal depth of about 261.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The borehole was logged by our geologist who observed the cores and 
monitored the drilling conditions during the boring advancement.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
one boring, designated as Boring No. 1, to a depth of about 261.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface.  The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.   
Our field exploration generally encountered asphaltic concrete pavement overlying 
surface fill and unconsolidated alluvium/colluvium consisting of brown and gray clayey and 
gravelly soils with boulders extending to a depth of about 8 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The unconsolidated overburden layer is underlain by hard basaltic rock formation 
(pahoehoe) with some limited volcanic tuff and cinder extending to about 61 feet below the 
existing ground surface. This layer sequence is believed to represent the late-stage 
rejuvenated volcanism of the Honolulu Volcanic Series (HVS) that occurred locally in the 
Makapuu area.
Below the HVS materials, semi-consolidated, medium dense to consolidated hard 
marine and shoreline deposits, generally consisting of tan to white interbedded coralline 
detritus, calcareous sandstone, and coral reef materials (including some beach rock 
containing inclusions of basaltic cobbles) were encountered between about 61 feet and 
about 106 feet below the ground surface. Beneath the marine deposits, we encountered 
hard to very hard a’a basalt rock formation containing interbedded medium hard to 
medium dense basaltic clinker extending to the maximum depth explored of about 
261.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The volcanic materials are believed to 
represent lavas of the older Koolau Volcanic Series that form the base of the stratigraphic 
section comprising the Island of Oahu. Substantial voids (detected by the free fall of the 
drill string) including open lava tubes in the basalt and cavities in the calcareous materials 
were not encountered. 
Surface return of the drilling fluid was lost intermittently to the formation at depths of 
about 13 and 45 feet below the ground surface. The return drilling fluid circulation was 
completely lost below about 45 feet extending to the total depth of the borehole. The loss 
of drilling fluid return circulation indicates that basaltic and coralline formations with 
generally good permeability were encountered. 
We encountered and measured groundwater in Boring No. 1 at depths ranging 
between about 33 and 36 feet below the existing ground surface as indicated on the logs 
of boring.  Groundwater levels were measured each day before the start of drilling to 
obtain stable measurements. Natural artesian (flowing) groundwater conditions were not 
encountered during our exploration. It should be noted that the groundwater level is 
assumed to fluctuate in response to tides due to the close proximity of the shoreline and 
the ocean. It should also be noted that groundwater levels at the project site may fluctuate

BOREHOLE BACKFILL 
Boring No. 1 was abandoned by completely backfilling the borehole with cement 
grout. The cement grout was mixed using approximately 6 gallons of clean water for each 
sack of Type I/II cement mixed in a 100-gallon capacity reservoir. The cement grout was 
thoroughly mixed and pumped via low pressure tremie method to fill the entire borehole 
from the bottom to the top. Borehole casings were removed in increments during the 
tremie grout placement. Excessive grout take and grout settlement was not experienced. 
The total cement grout volume applied to the borehole was about 460 gallons. 
The remaining 4-inch diameter borehole casings were removed when the stabilized 
grout level reached about 15 feet from the ground surface.  During the extraction of the 
remaining borehole surface casing, we encountered an unknown source of steady flowing 
water entering from the borehole sidewall at a depth of about 6 feet below the ground 
surface. Based on visual observation of the infiltration entering the borehole, we estimate 
the flow may be on the order of about 10 gallons per minute. The water was noted to be 
clear and odorless with a saline characteristic.  
Based on our observations and reconnaissance of the immediate area, no obvious 
utility line break or other possible leak source was identified in the vicinity. In an effort to 
evaluate whether the adjacent operating Injection Well No. 3 was contributing to the 
infiltration flow, we had the manager reduce the effluent flow to Injection Well No. 3 for 
about 30 minutes while we observed the condition of the borehole infiltration. Based on our 
observations, there was no change to the visible infiltration flow during the short-term test. 
Therefore, the source of the observed saline water infiltration in the shallow subsurface 
was undetermined. The borehole was isolated from the infiltration water and backfilled with 
cement grout.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling one boring, 
designated as Boring No. 1, to a depth of about 261.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface at the approximate location shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The boring was drilled 
using 4-inch solid stem augers and HQ wire line coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and 
textural examination in the field and by monitoring the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing 
in the laboratory.  Soils, where encountered, were classified in general conformance with 
the Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Symbols and abbreviations used in the boring logs are also described on the Soil Log 
Legend.  Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1through A-8. 
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch O.D. 
standard penetration sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 to 24-inch drive 
are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by 
International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.
