Dear Mr. Arana: 
In accordance with the project design requirements, a static compressive pile load 
test was conducted on an augered cast-in-place (ACIP) test pile (Test Pile No. 1) on 
June 1 and 2, 2016.  This letter report presents the results of our field observations, test 
data, engineering analysis, conclusions, and our recommendations. 
In general, the Kahului Consolidated Car Rental (ConRAC) Facility involves 
construction of a new multi-story consolidated rental car facility at Kahului Airport on the 
Island of Maui, Hawaii.  The project site generally is bounded by Aalele Street to the 
west, an air cargo facility to the southeast, and Keolani Place to the northwest. The site 
is also traversed by Lanui Circle, Hemaloa Street, and a large box culvert oriented in a 
northwest-southeast direction.  The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
We provided geotechnical engineering exploration services previously and 
recommended using ACIP pile foundation to support the new building structure. Our 
recommendations were presented in the report entitled “Geotechnical Engineering 
Exploration, Consolidated Car Rental (ConRAC) Facility, Kahului Airport, Kahului, Maui, 
Hawaii” dated April 29, 2016.  
The pile load test was conducted on a 24-inch diameter ACIP pile with a length of 
approximately 34.2 feet below the existing ground surface.  The ACIP pile was installed by 
Hawaiian Dredging Construction Company (HDCC) using a Bauer BG-28 drill rig equipped 
with automatic monitoring equipment providing the drilling information, such as drilling 
depth, drilling rate, auger torque, stroke count, etc., on a near-continuous basis. HDCC 
used continuous flight augers to drill the pile.  As the augers were removed, the hole was 
grouted from the bottom upwards.  After sound quality grout (based on visual observation)

To provide reaction support for the load test, ten 24-inch diameter ACIP reaction 
piles, with lengths ranging between approximately 28.9 to 31.8 feet long, were installed in 
similar fashion as the load test pile with the exception of using 3-inch diameter center bars 
in lieu of rebar cages.  The load test pile was completed on May 6, 2016, while the 
reaction piles were completed on May 20 and 23, 2016. Locations of the reaction piles are 
also shown on the ACIP Test Pile Location Plan, Plate 2. 
To better understand and predict the load transfer mechanism at the pile-soil 
interface, the load test pile was instrumented with six vibrating wire embedment strain 
gages (Model No. 4200A-2) manufactured by Geokon, Inc.  The strain gages were 
connected using a multiplexer to a Geokon Micro-10 data acquisition system to record the 
strains during the compressive load test.  The embedment strain gages were installed in 
the load test pile at the following depths below the existing ground surface.

The pile load test was conducted in general accordance with the Quick Load Test 
Method of ASTM D1143 and modified by our office to suit the design intents.  In general, 
the test pile was loaded to about 710 kips in approximately 70.0-kip load increments. Each 
load increment was held for at least 15 minutes or until the rate of the pile head movement 
has generally stabilized.  In addition, the static load test was held for approximately 4 
hours at the 560-kip load increment to evaluate the effects of creep. After reaching the 
maximum test load of 710 kips, the load test pile was unloaded in approximately six 
decrements and held for at least 4 minutes at each decrement. The results of the 
compressive pile load test are summarized below.

The load test readings and the load-displacement curve are presented on Plates 4 
and 5, respectively. Based on the shape of the load-displacement curve and our 
interpretation of the load transfer mechanism of the compressive load test, it is our opinion 
that the 24-inch diameter ACIP load test pile has failed at the maximum compressive test 
load of about 710 kips. It should be noted that the load test pile was terminated at the top 
of the boulder layer to determine the load carrying capacity of the ACIP piles bearing on 
the boulder layer due to early refusal conditions encountered during drilling. In addition, 
the actual ultimate pile load capacity may have been compromised due to ground 
disturbance caused by the failed installation of several micropiles that were originally 
intended for use as reaction piles in lieu of ACIP reaction piles. 
Based on the load test results, we recommend designing the 24-inch diameter 
ACIP piles that encounter early refusal conditions during drilling (bearing on basaltic 
boulders) with an allowable pile load capacity of 350 kips (175 tons). The drilling contractor 
should install the ACIP piles with a maximum effort to advance through the boulder layer 
when possible. In addition, we recommend conducting an additional pile load test on a 
24-inch diameter ACIP pile bearing on the hard basalt formation to verify the allowable 
load capacity of the ACIP piles terminated on the basalt formation.
