EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
This basis of design report was developed in support of the design of the rock slope
scaling and installation of draped wire mesh over the slopes at the Makapuu area The
basalt rock hillside extending from the upper Makapuu Scenic Lookout westward toward
the Lower Makapuu Scenic Lookout consists of a lower slope cut face and the upper slope
native mountainside The hillside consists of layered basalt rock containing alternating
seams of weathered clinker and hard rock materials The basalt rock hillside is eroding
and producing falling rock ranging from boulders to small stones Due to a lack of open
shoulder area along the roadway the falling rock has been reported to periodically
encroach upon the travel lanes of the highway There have been reports of personal injury
and traffic accidents involving the falling rock hazard
Based on our observation of the existing site conditions and a review of available
records we believe that a high danger exists for falling rock and that high priority should
be assigned to the construction of a rockfall protection system for the portion of
Kalanianaole Highway at the Makapuu area We understand that time constraints
preclude the construction of a permanent rockfall protection system for this site at this
time Therefore we understand that it is desired to implement a temporary rockfall
protection system to reduce the rockfall hazard on the highway at Makapuu
Based on surface reconnaissance of the rock slopes at the site it appears that
there are three main mechanisms generating rockfall events at the site The three
mechanisms related to rockfall are raveling failure boulder fall and differential erosion of
the alternating volcanic rock layers Based on the three mechanisms of rockfall observed
at the project site we believe that one of the most viabie alternatives for a temporary
rockfall protection scheme for the site would consist of scaling the rock slope and
installation of a draped wire mesh to control falling rock from the slope face
There are two potentially viable options for development of a temporary rockfall
protection system for the Makapuu site The first option consists of major scaling of the
loose rocks from the slope face only The second option consists of major scaling of the
loose rocks from the slope face and installation of draped wire mesh on the prepared
slope surface Because the weathering of rock is an ongoing process of erosion the
anticipated useful life of slope scaling alone is estimated to be generally less than about
5 to 6 years after which time the slope will likely need to be scaled again to remove the
build up of loose rock With the implementation of scaling and installation of draped wire
mesh the useful life of the improvements may be increased to about 10 to 12 years The
second option consisting of major scaling of the loose rocks and installation of draped wire
mesh on the prepared siope surface is the preferred alternative

In general the subject project involves rock slope scaling and the installation of
rockfall protection netting over a portion of Kalanianaole Highway Hawaii State Route 72
located in the vicinity of Makapuu in the District of Koolaupoko on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii Kalanianaole Highway is the only roadway transportation route that links the
eastern and southeastern portions of the Island of Oahu As a result the traffic volume is
relatively high with a large amount of tourist traffic traveling along this scenic coastal route
The Average Daily Traffic ADT is approximately 10 000 vehicles per day The general
location of the project site is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The portion of the highway located within the limits of the subject project site was
constructed in the 1920s The existing highway traverses the steep mountain slopes
located above Makapuu Beach and overlooking the Pacific Ocean Based on a review of
the available highway construction plans the highway was constructed mainly in cut
conditions with some substantial near vertical cut slopes In addition some portions of the
highway were constructed on areas of fill retained by a seaward side gravity retaining wall
The steep near vertically inclined hillside cuts of about 10 feet to over 50 feet in
height were made in the layered basalt rock that bounds the southern shoulder of the
roadway Several large cement rubble masonry CRM gravity retaining walls ranging in
heights of about 20 to 50 feet were constructed along the northern seaward shoulder of a
portion of the highway

In general the makai side of Kalanianaole Highway is bounded by steep north to
northeast facing slopes that fall in elevation down toward the shoreline located at the base
of the slopes Based on visual observation these slopes appear to consist of colluvial
talus materials overlying thin to medium bedded clinker and basalt rock formation
Very steep cliffs comprising the north and northeast facing slopes of the Koolau
Mountains bound the mauka side of Kalanianaole Highway The majority of these slopes
are composed of exposed basalt rock with minimal surface soil cover The basalt rock is
prominently layered with alternating beds of basalt clinker sandwiched between hard
basalt rock beds that represent individual lava flows of both a a and pahoehoe type flows
As a result the ground surface relief is somewhat irregular and stepped in character
The irregular hillslope surface is created by differential erosion between the weaker clinker
layers and more indurated basalt rock layers representing the core of the lava flows
Therefore periodic ledges of hard rock form greater relief in contrast to the more
decomposed and low relief character of the bounding clinker layers The geomorphic
conditions described are conducive to accelerated rock slope erosion and rockfall
The mountain slopes located westerly of the project site are composed of thick
accumulations of rocky colluvial talus mixed with some soils The deposits form a surface
wedge shaped apron that mantles the lower portions of the mountain slopes The talus
deposits are believed to have been formed by the accumulation of fallen rock and soil
debris which may have originated in part by landslide processes during the evolution of
the steep cliff line characteristic of the Koolau Mountain Range

SECTION 3 0 REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Hawaiian Archipelago consists of an elongate island chain comprised of the
eight major islands of the State of Hawaii and hundreds of small islets and atolls extending
for more than 1 000 miles to the northwest of the Hawaiian Island of Niihau The age of
the islands in the Hawaiian Archipelago increases progressing from the southeast toward
the northwest direction
The Hawaiian Archipelago is believed to have formed as a result of plate tectonic
movement working in combination with a relatively deep stationary focal point of volcanic
heat and magma lava generation The Pacific Plate containing the upper earth s crust
underlying the Central Pacific Ocean continues to siowly move in a northwesterly
direction while the nearly stationary hot spot of magma generation continues to produce
eruptive emissions beneath the plate As a result a continuous chain of volcanic islands
has formed and have slowly moved off in the northwesterly direction carried away from
the active center of volcanism by plate movement The Island of Hawaii is presently
located adjacent to the deep seated hot spot region The most distant volcanic island
remnants located far to the northwest of Hawaii have been reduced to submerged
seamounts as a result of a long history of land erosion and subsidence
3 1 Island of Oahu
The Island of Oahu encompasses approximately 604 square miles of land area
that may be divided into four major geographic provinces The geographic provinces
include the following 1 Koolau Range 2 Waianae Range 3 Schofield Plateau and
4 Coastal Plains The majority of the Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic
lava from two primary shield volcanoes known as Waianae and Koolau The older
volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in geochronologic age and
forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger volcano Koolau is
estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the majority
of the eastern two thirds of the island

Waianae Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano remained active therefore
the westward flowing lava from the Koolau Volcano continued to bank against the
weathered products of the Waianae Volcano to create the elevated area known as the
Schofield Plateau located between the two volcanoes
Following the eruption of the bulk of the lavas the coastal areas of Oahu were
partially submerged and emerged cyclically due to large scale sea level fluctuations in
response to periods of worldwide glaciation In addition to the sea level fluctuations the
island mass was undergoing a gradual subsidence due to the island s mass and isostatic
adjustment of the earth s crust beneath the island As a result of the cyclic sea level
fluctuations and the gradual subsidence of the island the erosional baseline likewise
fluctuated and major streams were drowned and buried and later emerged to renew their
incision
The lowering of the base level results in an increased stream gradient and
consequently an increased rate of stream down cutting The effect of changes in erosional
baseline impact the evolution of landforms by influencing the rate of stream down cutting
and sediment deposition in addition to changing the coastal exposure to ocean wave
erosion The combined effects are evidenced by the evolution of steep wailed
amphitheater shape valleys the filling of valleys to form broad gently sloping alluvial
floors and the initiation of renewed stream dissection into the older alluvial deposits to
form terraces at the bottom of the valley floors This cyclic activity during the Pleistocene
Epoch partially accounts for the present topography of today including the Ewa Honolulu
and Kahuku sedimentary coastal plains and the coastal perimeter of the Island of Oahu
Today both the Waianae and Koolau Mountains have lost most of their original
shield volcano outline and exist as elongated mountain ranges that are evolving largely by
erosional processes The Koolau Range with its highest peak of about 3 105 feet above
sea level is approximately 37 miles in length and is deeply incised by active stream cutting
and mass wasting processes In contrast the Waianae Range with its highest peak of
about 4 025 feet above sea level at Mount Kaala is about 22 miles in length The Waianae
Mountain Range has been carved into large valleys separated by steep ridgelines by a

The Koolau Shield Volcano was built by Pliocene Epoch eruptions along the
principal northwest trending rift zone and two secondary rift zones A dike complex series

the northwest rift zone and surround the vicinity of the large Koolau Caldera structure now

colluvial materials are often found deposited high on the valley walls having been exposed
by the rejuvenation of erosional forces
The Koolau Pali steep fluted cliff line located on the windward side of the Koolau
Mountain Range is believed to have been formed by a complex history of stream and
ocean wave erosion along with the deposition of thick alluvial deposits on the
amphitheater shaped valley floors The Koolau Pali is essentially a series of closely
spaced amphitheater valley heads separated by lower inter stream divides which are
partially buried in thick alluvial deposits The Koolau Pali may have initially formed as a
result of the large scale faulting and collapse of a large portion of the actively building
Koolau Summit The collapse of the summit formed a vast caldera stretching from about
Waimanalo toward Kaneohe along the central portion of the Koolau Pali Subsequently
the caldera was filled with lava which was rapidly altered and decomposed by rising
volcanic gas
Erosion of the caldera filling lavas by stream and ocean wave attack resulted in the
disintegration of the Koolau caldera structure and a southwestward directional retreat of
the evolving amphitheater valleys Erosion was slowed when the amphitheater valleys

The present day geographic form of the Island of Oahu is largely a result of slow
on going erosional processes that work to denude the existing landscape New volcanic
materials are currently not being erupted on the Island of Oahu However in terms of
relative geologic time the Island of Oahu could possibly experience another episode of
volcanism similar to the recent eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series which occurred
about 70 000 years ago Hence the forces of erosion are now the predominant processes
working to alter the existing landscape
The natural processes of erosion are closely related to the development and
activation of the geologic hazard called rockfall Ground surface erosion may be defined
as the process of land denudation by the wearing away of soil and rock by both physical
and chemical processes The processes may include the weathering of rock to form soils
the mass wasting of blocks of earth materials and the erosive activity of streams waves
wind and underground water on the soil and rock materials In Hawaii as well as other
tropical regions the processes of mass wasting combined with stream and ocean wave
erosion are the most important forces acting to continually erode and stabilize landforms
Mass wasting may be defined as the downslope movement of a mass of rock and or soil
debris under the influence of gravity by means of events such as rockfall landslide

3 2 Project Site and Vicinity Geology
The project site is located near the coastline at the eastern end of Waimanalo Bay
on the Island of Oahu The project site is situated on the windward side of the Koolau
Mountain lower flank at Makapuu The topography of the Makapuu area generally
consists of a narrow strip of low relief coastal plain bordered by the steep cliffs of the
Koolau Mountain Range The Koolau Mountain Range is located toward the south and
east of the project site and the Pacific Ocean is located toward the north
In general the geologic materials composing the surface deposits of the coastal
plain include unconsolidated and semi consolidated recent alluvial and colluvial
deposits with calcareous beach and dune sands The majority of the project site is
underlain by volcanic rock comprising the Tertiary Age Koolau Basalt Volcanic Series
Other geologic materials derived from the erosion and weathering of the Koolau Basalt
include colluvial talus and recent alluvium As mentioned previously the Koolau Basalt
rock erupted from the Koolau Volcano during the main shield building stage of the
Island of Oahu
Traveling in an eastward direction Kalanianaole Highway traverses the coastal
plain located in the vicinity of Sea Life Park and begins to climb the lower flank of the
Koolau Mountain Range located in the vicinity of Makapuu Beach Park As the highway
begins to climb the mountain flank the colluvial talus and basalt rock geologic materials
are encountered The highway gains elevation before passing over a topographic saddle
located at the upper Makapuu Scenic Lookout The highway traverses the steep cut
slopes consisting of Koolau Basalt rock The highway then falls in elevation as it traverses
outside of the project limits toward the southeasterly facing coastline
There are no known active geologic faults residing in the project area Rockfall
appears to be the primary geologic hazard that occurs at the project site Other
naturally induced potential hazards may include flash flood runoff from the steep rocky
slopes and drainage gullies during storm events

SECTION 4 0 SITE CONDITIONS AND ROCKFALL
Based on the prevalence of the steep rock slopes extensive rock talus deposits
and historical accounts of accidents and near collisions involving falling rock at the site
rockfall is a significant hazard that should be addressed at Makapuu There are some
environmental and site specific conditions that might have an impact on the type of rockfall
experienced and the frequency of rockfall occurrences Some of the affecting conditions
are the climatic influence existing vegetation type and density and the geologic conditions
of the rock material
4 1 Climatic Considerations
Makapuu resides at the eastern end of the windward side of the Island of Oahu
The general climate at Makapuu is relatively drier than most windward Oahu locales due
to its coastal position The climate may be summarized as relatively dry and windy
Rainfall is typically light except during periods of storm activity when precipitation
increases dramatically Rainfall is estimated to range on the order of about 15 to
30 inches per year and the rainfall generally increases further inland and at the higher
elevations of this portion of the Island of Oahu The project site is openly exposed to the
predominant easterly and northeasterly trade winds that blow for much of the year
Because the project site is located in an exposed coastal environment with frequent
rough ocean and surf conditions the amount of sea spray entrained in the air is believed
to be relatively high especially when the strong trade winds are predominant The effects
of corrosion on metallic structures resulting from the coastal environment should be
considered
The generally dry and windy climatic conditions encountered at Makapuu support
only a very thin surface soil cover and light vegetation growth on the steep volcanic rock
slopes Weathered rock is generally encountered at the surface of the very steep slopes
In general the surface of the weathered rock is more friable and weaker than underlying
layers therefore the raveling of rock fragments and decomposed rock is a common
occurrence

4 2 Vegetation
Vegetation at the project site is light and consists of scattered low shrubs and
grasses that have adapted to the drier and more exposed coastal environmenial
conditions The flatter slopes tops of the bluffs and sheltered areas generally support
thicker vegetation growth The steeper rock slopes typically support shrubbery growth
only on the localized ledge features The basalt rock cut slopes typically are devoid of
vegetation
Based on our reconnaissance of the project site there appears to be no large trees
located on the slopes where the interim rockfall protection systems are proposed Most of
the sparse vegetation covering the slopes is less than 1 to 2 feet in height The vegetation
is anticipated to be seasonal in growth with more robust growth and greater ground cover
occurring during the wetter winter months and following periods of prolonged rainfall
Mechanisms
Based on surface reconnaissance of the rock slopes along Kalanianaole Highway
at Makapuu it appears that there are three main mechanisms generating rockfall events at
the site The three mechanisms related to rockfall are raveling failure boulder fall and
differential erosion of alternating volcanic rock layers
Raveling of one or several rocks from the slope may occur due to a multitude of
causes The danger develops when the raveling rock has sufficient velocity and volume to
reach the highway surface Raveling may be caused by water pressure behind the rock
ice jacking not possible at this location differential weathering or erosion along faults or
shear zones weakening of rock due to temperature and moisture cyclic fluctuations and
loosening by animals or trees and wind Raveling may also result from ground vibration
Due to the weathered condition of the basalt rock slope surfaces at Makapuu raveling is
an important process that continually works to shed weathered and broken rock materials
from the slopes
One of the most frequent causes of raveling is by invasive tree roots prying on the
existing cracks located in the rock slope surface Tree roots frequently develop and grow
larger in the discontinuities of rock materials The added force of wind action on tree limbs
W O INC Page 14

and branches may create leveraged forces that are transferred to the roots lodged within
the discontinuity Over time the repetitive leveraging of the discontinuity can loosen rock
and cause it to fall However we believe that tree root pry is not a significant mechanism
for creating rock slope instability at this site because hardy growth of trees and vegetation
on the slope is minimal at Makapuu
On many highway cut slopes excavations encounter granular colluvial and talus
materials ranging from gravel to boulders in size The boulders may often be rounded and
solidly intact and as a result have the potential to develop momentum from rolling without
breaking apart Rounded boulders may readily roll through a ditch or other barrier
therefore rolling boulders are a significant rockfall hazard at the project site
In addition to raveling and boulder fall processes inter layered rock with variable
strength and rates of weathering commonly exhibit accelerated erosion and rockfall The
weaker layers tend to erode and undercut the more weathering resistant rock located
above This may lead to raveling of the harder layers or failure of undercut blocks of rock
as support from the underlying weaker layers is removed This type of rockfall mechanism
is especially common at Makapuu due to the alternating thin to medium thick layers of
basalt rock composed of clinker and hard rock seams
Based on the three mechanisms of rockfall observed at the project site we believe
that one of the most viable alternatives for a rockfall protection scheme for this site would
consist of scaling the rock slope and the installation of a draped wire mesh to control
falling rock from the slope face

build up of loose rock With the implementation of scaling and installation of draped mesh

the draped wire mesh based on the corrosive environment at the site The second option
consisting of major scaling of the loose rocks and installation of draped wire mesh on the
prepared slope surface is the preferred alternative

5 1 Rock Slope Scaling
Rock slope scaling consists of experienced crews employing manual labor and
hand tools to remove loose rocks and precariously positioned rock fragments from the
slope face The scaling crews performing the rock slope scaling should be experienced in
the performance of rock slope scaling by rappelling and or working from crane baskets
Based on the estimated area of the slope face designated for scaling about 18 000 to
21 000 square yards we envision that the slope face area can readily accommodate
three 4 man crews working simultaneously on the slopes Rock slope scaling work
typically proceeds by starting from the top of the slope and working downward in a
controlled and orderly manner in conjunction with the combined progress of all the scaling
crews working on the slope face Rock slope scaling should be typically performed on
slopes with a slope inclination steeper than about two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V
In general each rock slope scaling crew would consist of a scaling supervisor who
should be a specialist in rock slope scaling and three experienced rock slope scalers For
the additional slope scaling crews we anticipate that a scaler foreman in lieu of the
scaling supervisor and three 3 slope scalers would make up each additional scaling
crew The scaling supervisor should have a minimum of 5 years of high slope scaling
experience with a minimum of 2 000 hours of demonstrated experience supervising slope
scaling The scaling supervisor should have completed the American Red Cross basic
first aid course or equivalent and should have experience or training in the use of

emergency remote rescue techniques The scaling supervisor should also have a
minimum of two 2 years of experience in blasting operations controlled blasting
The scaler foreman is an individual who is directly in charge of and responsible for
the safety and work performed by a crew of three slope scalers The scaler foreman
should be actively engaged at the site location and should be engaged in actual slope
scaling activities for at least 50 percent of the slope scaling crew time The scaler foreman
should have a minimum of three years of high slope scaling experience with a minimum of
2 000 hours of demonstrated experience performing slope scaling Each scaler foreman
should have completed the American Red Cross basic first aid course or equivalent and
should have experience or training in the use of emergency remote rescue techniques
Each slope scaler is an individual who is engaged in accessing the slope face and
removing loose rock and materials from the slope face using a variety of hand tools at
locations that require modified rock climbing techniques for the safe execution of the work
Each slope scaler should have a minimum of two years experience with a minimum of
1 000 hours of demonstrated experience performing similar slope scaling work
The scaling crews would be supported by an ancillary ground crew such as a
general contractor that is responsible for cleanup of the removed loose materials
deposited at the base of the slopes on the highway
Prior to the start of the rock slope scaling a series of eyebolt anchors will need to
be installed along the top of the slope The eyebolt anchors will be used as rappelling
anchors The eyebolt anchors may also be used to anchor other slope scaling apparatus
such as a grouser or other spider type platform These platforms may be used to
supplement the manual slope scaling to loosen remove topographic overhangs and
ledges The scaling platforms are essentially dragged up and down the slope face to
abrade and remove weakened ledges and overhangs that may be too difficult to be
removed by hand and or manual methods
It should be noted that rock slope scaling work may be considered hazardous
work and should be classified as high risk work Visibility is the single most essential

5 2 Draped Wire Mesh Installation
As mentioned previously the draped wire mesh cover should assist in the
controlled release of loose rock that is shed from the slope face during weathering and
erosion Therefore it is not the primary intent to closely pin the wire mesh to the slope
face as a retention system The draped wire mesh should provide positive rockfall control
for the useful life of the wire mesh material We anticipate the majority of the large blocks
of rock and boulders would have been removed following the slope scaling effort
Therefore we envision that some loose rocks that may be retained by the draped wire

mesh would be generally less than 3 feet in diameter The following paragraphs provide a
general description of the draped wire mesh installation procedure
Once the rock slope scaling work has been completed installation of the draped
wire mesh may proceed Grouted rock anchors of about 10 feet in depth are grouted into
the solid rock located about 20 to 25 feet beyond the crest of the rock slope The grouted
rock anchors are generally installed at about 20 feet on centers along the slope crest
Galvanized steel wire rope 0 75 inch diameter is strung continuously along the top of the
slope through the additional grouted rock anchors known as lateral anchors at the corners
and at the bends in the alignment of the steel wire rope The wire mesh which typically
comes in rolls of about 6 to 13 feet wide by about 200 feet long up to 300 feet long is
possible is attached and draped from the 0 75 inch cabie ropes to cover the entire rock
slope In general a minimum of about 1 foot of overlap between adjacent rolls of wire
mesh is provided It should be noted that the wire mesh may be colored to approximate
the general coloration of the natural slope
The rolls of wire mesh are usually draped starting from the top of the slope If
access to the top of the slope is limited the rolls of wire mesh may be draped with the use
of a heavy lift helicopter The wire mesh is hung to within about 3 feet above the base
level to allow for removal of loose rock that may fall from behind the draped wire mesh
during installation The draped lengths of wire mesh are then tied together by the rock
slope scalers to complete the installation It should be noted that the existing highway
does not afford much room for the shoulders Therefore maintenance of loose rocks that
fall behind the draped wire mesh and onto the highway road grade will still be required
However it is envisioned that the frequency of the maintenance will be significantly less
than the current maintenance requirements with the implementation of the manual rock
slope scaling
Because of the exposed ocean environment which is very conducive to corrosion
the draped wire mesh and hardware should be corrosion resistant Therefore we propose
that the draped wire mesh and hardware be galvanized as a minimum or the hardware
be made of stainless steel The useful life of the draped wire mesh is estimated to be

As mentioned previously some existing overhead power lines traverse the existing
