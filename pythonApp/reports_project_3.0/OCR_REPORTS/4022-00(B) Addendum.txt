Mechanically Stabilized Earth Slopes
Mechanically stabilized earth slopes generally utilize polymer grid structures
geogrids with high tensile strength These systems generally provide a
cost effective solution to slope stability problems which may include the following
insufficient right of way high surcharge loads poor quality fills high seismic forces
steep slopes or difficult landslide repairs When reinforced slope geogrids are
placed in soil the grid geometry interlocks with adjacent soil creating a soil geogrid
composite with greatly enhanced engineering properties Different grid
configurations are available to provide optimum soil grid interaction for a range of
soil types and slope reinforcement applications Reinforced slope geotextiles work
in a similar manner to that of reinforced slope geogrids The construction
methodology is briefly described as follows
As the slope is constructed horizontal layers of reinforced slope geogrids or geotextiles
are placed in the compacted fill at predetermined levels The lengths of the reinforced
slope geogrid or geotextile layers are designed to anchor potential failure zones into
stable interior sections of the embankment or hillside As forces develop within a soil
mass the high modulus reinforced slope geogrids or geotextiles are immediately pulled
into tension The reinforced slope geogrids or geotextiles transfer this tensile force
from the unstable soil back into less stressed portions of the slope and stability is thus
maintained

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was
still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava
GEOLABS INC
W O 4022 00 B Page 6
During the late Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the continental glaciers advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below its present stand When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above its present level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans
of terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the
upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high
energy conditions
In some of the mountainous regions of Hawaii chemical weathering has altered the
basaltic flows pyroclastic ejecta to a soil like condition Erosional processes consisting of
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and transport downslope toward
the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium have also altered the natural condition
of the volcanic flows Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of the
valley alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are transported and
deposited as alluvium
When alluvial colluvial deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical
processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or
weathering of the material Erosion continually deposits newer material covering the
older alluvial colluvial deposits Depending on the rate of transport the newer
sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time The project area is
generally underlain by these older colluvial and alluvial deposits

Mechanically Stabilized Earth Slopes
Mechanically stabilized earth slopes generally utilize polymer grid structures
geogrids with high tensile strength These systems generally provide a
cost effective solution to slope stability problems which may include the
following insufficient right of way high surcharge loads poor quality fills high
seismic forces steep slopes or difficult landslide repairs When reinforced slope
geogrids are placed in soil the grid geometry interlocks with adjacent soil
creating a soil geogrid composite with greatly enhanced engineering properties
Different grid configurations are available to provide optimum soil grid interaction
for a range of soil types and slope reinforcement applications Reinforced slope
geotextiles work in a similar manner to reinforced slope geogrids The
construction methodology is briefly described as follows
As the slope is constructed horizontal layers of reinforced slope geogrids or
geotextiles are placed in the compacted fill at predetermined levels The lengths of
the reinforced slope geogrid or geotextile layers are designed to anchor potential
failure zones into stable interior sections of the embankment or hillside As forces
develop within a soil mass the high modulus reinforced slope geogrids or
geotextiles are immediately pulled into tension The reinforced slope geogrids or
geotextiles transfer this tensile force from the unstable soil back into less stressed
portions of the slope and stability is thus maintained

Gabion Wall Structure
Gabion walls are cellular structures formed of rectangular cages made of
zinc coated steel wire mesh filled with rocks of the proper size and mechanical
characteristics The individual units are firmly tied to each other with zinc coated
GEOLABS INC
W O 4022 00 B Page 23
wires to form a monolithic structure The wire mesh is hexagonal and double
twisted and the openings are about 3 to 4 inches in size
The gabions are normally dispatched from the factory folded and packed in order
to make transportation to the site easier and more economical The gabions are
opened and assembled on site Generally several empty gabions are joined
together set in place and firmly laced to the adjacent gabions along the edges in
contact both horizontal and vertical Once the filling is completed the gabion is
closed by lacing the lid along all edges and the internal cell diaphragms
The gabion wall should be supported on a 6 inch thick concrete pad bearing on
the existing stiff soils Prior to construction of the concrete pad the subgrade
soils should be compacted to a stiff condition An allowable bearing value of

in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of
an 18 to 24 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
