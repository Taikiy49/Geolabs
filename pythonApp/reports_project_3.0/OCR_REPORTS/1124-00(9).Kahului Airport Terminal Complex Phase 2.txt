PROJECT CONSIDERATION
According to a preliminary grading plan prepared by R T Tanaka
Engineers Inc for the subject project dated the
proposed subdivision site will be an area about 600 feet wide and
1 500 feet long with the longer side fronting Keolani Place just
west of Kalialinui Stream The preliminary grading plan is shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2
Page 2
Ground Transportation Subdivision
It is our understanding that the proposed subdivsion development
will require fill of up to 14 feet over the majority of the site to
revise the site grade Cut of up to 4 feet will also be required on
the southwestern corner of the subdivision Several new utility Lines
will be included with an access road in the subdivision
SITE CONDITIONS
The project site was formerly a part of the old U S Naval air
installation which occupied the existing Kahului Airport area
Most of the site is currently abandoned and overgrown with a
thick cover of grasses wild cane haole koa and kiawe trees The
site is traversed by several asphalt paved roads which are generally
in poor condition In addition there are several structures both
occupied and abandoned and equipment yards on the site
The occupied structures include a T shirt factory and sales
outlet a candy cookie factory and sales outlet and buildings in uses
related to the equipment yards The abandoned structures include old
concrete slabs ruins quonset huts in various states of disrepair and
a swimming pool
It was noted that the swimming pool and its decking showed signs
of distresses due to relative ground movements of up to 3 inches

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The general geologic setting of the Kahului Airport area has been
discussed in detail in our previous reports prepared for various
terminal complex expansion projects
In general the project site is located on the seaward margin of
the Kula lava flows that were encountered throughout the rest of the
airport area
The glacio eustatic sea level changes during the Pleistocene
epoch had an effect upon the depositional environmments of these
coastal areas As a result of the geologically recent rise in sea
level the old shoreline areas which had been dry land became lagoons
or marshes and were subsequently covered by wind deposited sand dune
sands and stream carried deposits alluvium
The higher southwestern portion of the site is Situated over the
Kula basalt flows while the more extensive lower areas are over the
dune sand alluvium and marsh lagoon deposits

Subsurf ace Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by excavating
and sampling 8 test pits at the approximate locations shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 The test pits were excavated by a rubber tired
backhoe and exploration depths ranged from 9 feet to 12 feet These
excavations were logged by one of our geologists and the logs are
presented on Plates A 1 to A 8
Some selected soil samples were tested in our laboratory to
examine their subgrade support characteristics These tests included
3 compaction tests 2 CBR tests 5 Atterberg Limits and 5 mechanical
sieve analyses together with moisture determinations The results of
these tests are presented on Plates B 1 through B 4
Based on our field exploration and laboratory test data two
major areas are identified the majority of the site is a lowlying
area underlain by marsh lagoon deposits and the remaining portion is
a smaller higher area in the southwestern corner of the site
underlain by Kula basalt flows These areas are indicated on the Site
Plan Plate 2

DISCUSSION RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The test pits disclosed that the lower area is underlain by soft
compressible organic silt and loose sand of marsh lagoon deposits near
and below the groundwater level In view of the thick fills up to 14
feet to be placed over the lower areas for site grading significant
ground settlements are anticipated The thickness of the compressible
loose sand could not be determined since it extended beyond the reach
of a backhoe Therefore the magnitude of potential settlements could
not be estimated with confidence We recommend that a close monitoring
of the time settlement rates be implemented to evaluate when and which
area can be released for new construction Alternatively better
estimates of the rates and magnitudes of settlements could be obtained
by the implementation of additional investigation work using borings
and undisturbed sampling
Fill Settlement
The proposed fill thickness will vary from 2 to 14 feet
averaging perhaps 10 feet It should be noted that the fill will
cause the ground to settle due to the consolidation of the soft
organic silt and loose sand deposits underlying the lower areas
The magnitude of the settlements is directly related to the
thickness of the soft layer However because the thicknesses of the
compressible deposits can not be determined by means of test pit
excavations the magnitudes of the settlement are unattainable
GEOLABS HAWAIi
Page 7
Ground Transportation Subdivision
Extrapolating the soils information from the Keolani Place Bridge
project it is probable that the compressible deposits may consist
mainly of loose lagoonal sand that may bottom out at about Elevation
15 to 25 feet MSL Such sandy materials will tend to consolidate
rapidly Based on these assumptions the settlements are estimated to
vary from zero to about 1 5 feet
If based on the above assumed conditions these settlements
should occur relatively fast probably within 3 to 6 months after the
application of the total fill weight Remaining long term settlements
after construction should be small
It is probable that the distributions and thicknesses of soft
organic silt and loose sand deposits would vary along the project
site As a result differential settlements may be significant
Therefore it is recommended that any existing utility lines such as
sewer water fuel or drain lines under the new fill area should
either be removed plugged or rerouted as deemed necessary to avoid
rupture due to the ground settlement from the new fill
Because of the abovementioned potential settlements new water
main or sewer main and other improvements should be delayed as late as
possible and at least several months after the mass grading is
completed

Flexible joints should be provided and shorter pipe sections
should be used for new sewer or water main along the filled street and
between the lower area and the higher area underlain by basalt rock
Settlement Monitoring
The actual ground settlement should be verified by monitoring
settlement observation markers which should be placed at various
locations Some should be placed before any fill is placed as primary
markers to develop better information on the time settlement history
Additional markers may be placed as appropriate after the mass
grading to complement the primary marker data for further gauging
differential settlements The recommended locations of the primary
settlement markers are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The settlement marker elevations should be read at least twice
weekly during the first months of the mass filling and once every week
thereafter The elevation reading should be continued for a period of
time even after the settlements have stabilized especially for the
primary markers
The results of the settlement marker reading should be forwarded
immediately to the soils engineer in order to have the pattern of
settlements evaluated for scheduling subsequent construction work

Site Preparation
Prior to the commencement of earthwork the site should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Any tree roots that are 1 5
inches or greater in diameter should be grubbed Where
encountered trash or rubbish fills or other unsuitable
material should be removed to firm soil
Unsuitable materials to be removed from the site during
clearing and grubbing work should include soft compressible
soils organic matter decayed vegetation trash and debris
deleterious materials large loose boulders and any other
material deemed unsuitable
Where encountered the top 12 inches of organic topsoil should
be stripped The resulting stripped and grubbed material
should not be used as fill
Existing utility lines should be properly removed or sealed and
abandoned Any cesspools if encountered should be thoroughly
cleaned of all organic materials and backfilled with compacted
granular material

Engineered Fill
ey
The term engineered fill used in this text covers any
fill of acceptable quality which is placed and compacted in
accordance with our recommendations for general filling
Because of a relatively large amount of settlement that may
occur some allowance should be made for the additional
fill quantities required to compensate for the ground fill
settlements
GEOLABS HAWAII
Page 12
Ground Transportation Subdivision
Engineered fill material should be non expansive soil free
of organic matter and form a dense stable soil mass when
compacted to the required density Generally the material
to be placed within the top 3 feet of the fill should have
a soaked CBR of at least 15 per ASTM D 1883 73 when
compacted to 95 of the maximum densities determined
according to ASTM D 1557 78 The total expansion of sample
during the soaking for CBR test should not exceed 13 The
largest particle size should be limited to 6 inches maximun
Below the top 3 foot zone the fill material should have a
soaked CBR of at least 8 when compacted to 95 of the ASTM
D 1557 78 density The total expansion should not exceed
3 The particle size should be limited to 8 inches
maximum
We recommend that the qualities of the fill materials be
evaluated by a soils engineer as soon as practical at the
borrow site or source to assess their suitability
The engineered fill material within the top 3 feet of the
finished grade should be placed in horizontal lifts not
exceeding 6 inches in compacted thickness and compacted to
dry densities not less than that stipulated under
COMPACTION For the engineered fill below 3 feet of
finished grade the thickness of the lift may be increased
to 8 inches

Subdrainage
If on site residual soil of reddish brown silt is used as
engineered fill proper subsurface drainage should be provided
especially in the vehicle pavement area It is recommended that
pavement subgrade be shaped to drain water away from paved areas
GEOLABS HAWAII

For example the pavement thickness for a heavy traffic road area may
be significantly thicker than the shoulders Thus the subgrade
elevation may be lower under the road than under sidewalk or shoulder
areas Such a situation is conducive to trapping water under the
pavement
Suitable subdrain or blankets should be provided to bleed off
accumulation of water in such a case or a thicker shoulder section
may be used One to 2 inch diameter weepholes should be provided
through the walls of catch basins or manhole to drain seepage water at
the subgrade level provided that water in the catch basin or manhole
will not rise above the weephole level

Spread Footings
It is our understanding that most of the structures planned
will be warehouse type and one to two story structures The
one story structures may be supported on thickened on grade slabs
An allowable maximum soil bearing pressure of 3000 p s f may
be used when the bottom of the footing is at least 12 inches below
the lowest adjacent grade The minimum footing width at the bottom
should be 18 inches
For individual footings the bottom of the footing should be
at least 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade The allowable
soil bearing pressure should also be 3000 p s f

The bottom of the footing adjacent to any utility trench
should be at or below the level of the trench bottom
A one third increase on the allowable soil bearing pressure
tay be allowed for wind or seismic loads
Any soft or weak spots encountered at the bottom of the
footing should be excavated to firm soil and backfilled with
compacted engineered fill The bottom of excavation should be
recompacted to 95 of the maximum density to minimize the
disturbance caused by excavating equipment
Horizontal forces may be resisted by lateral passive earth
pressure on the vertical face of the footings Generally for fill
using the on site residual soil an allowable soil resistance of
250 p s f per foot of depth may be used A base friction factor
of 0 40 may be used

allowable vertical bearing pressure of 2500 p s f is recommended

On Grade Floor Slabs
Slabs supported on grade may be used assuming that the fill
will be constructed according to the recommendations presented in
this report However the expansion potential of the fill material
should be evaluated prior to being used as fill Usually the
potential swelling of typical borrow soil of reddish brown silt or
clayey silt can be reduced by placing a 6 inch thick cushion layer
of S4C type crushed rock under the slab premoistening the subgrade
soils and providing thickened edge slab to reduce soil moisture
fluctuation under the slabs We recommend that the depth of the
thickened edge be 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
The design thickness of the slab will depend on the
anticipated use and traffic for the individual structures
Utility Trenches Backfill
To minimize the required compactive effort and possible damage
to the pipe the backfill around and up to 1 foot above the pipe
should consist of clean granular material such as sand and
lightly tamped or jetted as appropriate The remaining backfill
should be performed in accordance with the recommendations
presented under SITE GRADING However the backfill material
should not contain particle size in excess of 6 inches

It should be noted that dense hard basalt may be encountered
at higher area If rock is encountered at the bottom of the main
sewer and water line trenches an 8 inch thick cushion layer
consisting of sand or S4C is recommended below the pipe to provide
a more uniform pipe bearing surface This requirement may be
reduced to 3 inch cushion when flexible pipes are used
Design Review
Plans and specifications prepared for foundation and pavement

Construction Monitoring
In addition to a close monitoring of settlements as
recommended earlier it is also recommended that the clearing and
grubbing engineered fill placement and compaction foundation
excavation backfilling and subgrade preparation be observed by the
soils engineer during project construction This is to see that an
adequate compaction has been obtained and that the subsurface
conditions are consistent with the findings of our subsurface
investigation If conditions encountered are different from those
presented in this report appropriate modifications to the design
should be made
