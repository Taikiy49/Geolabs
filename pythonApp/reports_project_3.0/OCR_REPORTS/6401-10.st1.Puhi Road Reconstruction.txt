SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
New pavements will be constructed as part of the Puhi Road Rehabilitation project
Due to the silty nature of the pavement subgrade soils anticipated at the project site we
generally recommend incorporating a permeable drainage layer into the pavement design
However we understand the County of Kauai Department of Public Works would like to
evaluate pavement sections without a permeable drainage layer due to budgetary
considerations Therefore a permeable drainage layer was not considered in the design
of pavement sections
Based on the design guidelines Puhi Road would be classified as Medium
Volume roadways We understand Puhi Road is considered Urban for purposes of
pavement design Based on the Pavement Design Manual the roadway will need to be
designed for a pavement life span of at least 30 years
Based on the traffic data provided the R value of the subgrade materials and the
economic analyses performed a rigid pavement structural section would result in the most
economical pavement design over the design life of the pavement The recommended
pavement structural section for Puhi Road consists of 8 inches of Portland cement
concrete PCC over 6 inches of aggregate subbase AB course
The text of this pavement justification report should be referred to for detailed
discussion and specific recommendations for the pavement designs

Introduction
This pavement justification report presents the results of our analyses performed
for the new pavement designs for the Puhi Road Rehabilitation project in Lihue on the
Island of Kauai The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for pavement design only
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted
at the end of this report
1 2
Project Considerations
The project consists of reconstructing a portion of Puhi Road in Lihue on the
Island of Kauai The limits of the project are from the vicinity of Kaumualii Highway to
Kaneka Street for a total of about 1 820 lineal feet The existing Puhi Road is a
two lane roadway consisting of asphaltic concrete pavements The roadway has no
sidewalks curbs or gutters The new reconstructed roadway will have bike lanes on
both sides and curb and gutter on the eastern side of the roadway In addition concrete
sidewalk will be provided on the eastern side of the new roadway This pavement
justification report has been prepared in support of the new pavement designs
Subsurface information such as boring logs laboratory test results and dynamic
cone penetrometer data obtained from the preliminary study for the project was be used
to evaluate the new pavement sections for the project

Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series Following the cessation of
this main shield building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion
of basaltic lavas of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent
deposition of alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation
The Island of Kauai is the oldest of the main Hawaiian Islands and today
encompasses approximately 555 square miles with a maximum elevation of about
5 170 feet above Mean Sea Level at Mount Wai ale ale near the center of the island It
is believed that during the Tertiary Period about 5 to 6 million years ago in the Pliocene
Epoch basaltic lavas belonging to the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series built a roughly
circular island volcano from the ocean floor which is estimated to have been about
20 000 feet deep at the time Near the end of the Pliocene Epoch this main shield
building volcanic phase came to a halt
Following a long period of erosion and island mass subsidence volcanism was
renewed during the early Pleistocene Epoch about 1 5 million years ago with the
eruption of the Koloa Volcanic Series from multiple vents throughout the island This
post erosional volcanic activity laid a veneer of fresh lava and interbedded volcanic
sediments over the older deposits of the Waimea Canyon Series Because of the long
period of erosion that occurred between the eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series
and the Koloa Volcanic Series the deposits of the Koloa Volcanic Series typically filled
the erosional valleys and depressions that existed at that time Consequently the
basaltic rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are commonly found to overlie thick residual
soil and alluvial deposits
The dominant geologic processes of weathering landform erosion and sediment
re deposition followed during the late Pleistocene and Recent Epochs Combined with
the erosional and depositional effects of the Pleistocene sea level fluctuations which

Methodology of Pavement Design
In general we considered two types of pavement structural sections flexible and
rigid pavements in the pavement analyses for this project The flexible pavement
sections presented herein generally were determined based on the methodology
described in Chapter 3 of the revised Pavement Design Manual dated March 2002 The
State of Hawaii Department of Transportation Highways Division Material Testing and
Research Branch prepared the Pavement Design Manual The pavement design
methodology is based on the Hveem Stabilometer method developed and used by the
California Department of Transportation Caltrans
The design procedures for the rigid pavement sections were generally based on
the design procedures described in the Portland Cement Association PCA Thickness
Design for Concrete Highway and Street Pavements

Design Subgrade Conditions
The finish grades of the new roadway will generally follow the existing roadway
elevations Therefore the majority of the new pavements for the project will generally
be constructed on the existing subgrade materials consisting of stiff to very stiff clayey
silts
Two laboratory Resistance R Value tests were performed on the existing
subgrade materials Based on the laboratory tests the on site subgrade materials have
R values of about 52 and 55 Therefore an R value of 52 has been adopted for the
subgrade materials in our pavement design analyses for this project If site grading
exposes soils other than those assumed in the pavement design additional analysis
should be performed to confirm and or revise the recommended pavement structural
section for actual field conditions

Design Pavement Section
Due to the silty nature of the pavement subgrade soils anticipated at the project
site we generally recommend incorporating a permeable drainage layer into the
pavement design However we understand the County of Kauai Department of Public
Works would like to evaluate pavement sections without a permeable drainage layer
due to budgetary considerations Therefore a permeable drainage layer was not
considered in the design of pavement sections
The revised Pavement Design Manual dated March 2002 generally requires the
use of a Load Safety Factor LSF of 1 3 for concrete pavement design Based on
PCA s Thickness Design for Concrete Highway and Street Pavements the use of LSF
serviceability throughout the design period Therefore we request a variance from the
revised Pavement Design Manual to use LSF of 1 1 for the design of concrete
pavements for this project In addition we have assumed that dowel baskets will be
required at the concrete pavement joints for the project
We understand that the County of Kauai would like to utilize asphalt concrete
base ACB for the flexible pavement section Therefore we did not consider pavement
sections utilizing untreated aggregate base courses in the flexible pavements in our
pavement design analyses

Subgrade Preparation Below Pavement Section
We anticipate the majority of the new roadway pavements will be constructed on
existing subgrade materials In general the area within the contract grading limits
should be cleared and grubbed thoroughly at the on set of earthwork Vegetation
debris existing pavements deleterious materials and other unsuitable materials should
be removed and disposed properly off site or disposed in a designated area to reduce
the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose
stiff and or dense materials The resulting excavation should be backfilled with
aggregate subbase materials The excavated soft and or organic soils should be
properly disposed of off site
After clearing and grubbing the future pavement areas should be excavated
where necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the aggregate
subbase course layer The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth
of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction

3 6
Fill Materials
In general the excavated on site materials may be re used as a source of
general fill material provided that they are free of lumps and rock fragments greater
than 3 inches and free of deleterious materials Imported fill materials if required
should consist of non expansive select granular material such as crushed coralline or
basaltic materials The imported materials should be well graded from coarse to fine
with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The import materials should
have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of
1 percent or less Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use

Compaction Requirements
In general fill and backfill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction However it
should be noted that the on site soils have relatively high moisture contents which
would make compaction of the materials more difficult In addition the project is located
in an area of relatively high rainfall which would make earthwork operations more
difficult especially with the relatively high in situ moisture content clayey soils
Aggregate subbase materials required for the new pavements should conform to
the requirements stipulated in Subsection 703 16 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications
for Road and Bridge Construction 2005 HSS The aggregate subbase should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch level
loose lifts and compacted to not less than 95 percent of the maximum density
according to Section 106 09 Special Test Methods of the HSS
In addition field density tests should be performed on the compacted fills and
backfills in general accordance with ASTM D1556 In general field density tests should

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore special attention
should be given to the surface and subsurface drainage of the pavements As a
minimum the pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures
Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths ranging from
about 16 to 16 5 feet below the existing pavement surface The approximate boring
locations for this project are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled
using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
