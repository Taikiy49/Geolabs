1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and
engineering analyses performed in support of the Construction of Confined Disposal
Facility project as part of the Dredge Channel for T AKE Phase 2 at Naval Magazine
Pearl Harbor design build project in the West Loch area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our engineering
analyses performed for the proposed disposal facility construction project The
recommendations presented herein are intended for the design and construction of the
confined disposal facility based on the evaluation and characterization of the in situ
soils The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island As the
volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau banked against its eroded
eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau
As the main shield building on the Island of Oahu drew to a close during the
Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of continental
glaciation As the sea levels fluctuated fringing coral algal reefs began to grow on the
seaward margins of the island After long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations The series are referred
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked the Schofield Plateau generating
sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and
further south these alluvial sediments were interbedded with marine sediments
coral algal reef formations and volcanic deposits of the Honolulu Volcanic Series to
form a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from zero meters in the area of H 1
freeway to over 305 meters at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves
as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of southern
Oahu Deposition of these sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through
the present

3 2 Earth Berm Construction
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Debris deleterious materials and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed properly off site Soft and or yielding areas
encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill should be
over excavated to expose firm materials and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with well compacted fills The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly
disposed off site and or used in landscape areas where appropriate Contract documents
should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and compacted fill
placement to account for variations in the over excavation quantities
After clearing and grubbing the excavated subgrades underneath areas
designated to receive fill should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage
may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill
The need for over excavation due to soft subgrade soil conditions should be evaluated by
our field representative

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration conducted for the project the on site soils generally
consist of silty clay soils classified as CH by ASTM D 2487 extending to depths of about
0 6 to 4 3 meters below the existing ground surface Based on our evaluation it is our
opinion that these on site soils are suitable for use as fill materials in the construction of
the earth berms for the confined disposal facility provided that the materials are free of
organic matter boulders or topsoil Additional imported fill material required for the earth
berm construction should consist of relatively impervious materials such as silty clays or
clayey silts The materials should have a Plasticity Index greater than 30 and should also
contain more than 50 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve
Fill materials for the earth berm construction should be placed in near level lifts not
exceeding 300 millimeters in loose thickness moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
Permanent fill slopes for the earth berms constructed using the excavated on site
soils or imported fill materials may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter
Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing
slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding Use of geotextile fabrics over the fill
slope face should be considered to reduce the potential for significant erosion thus
enhancing the long term stability of the fill slopes Appropriate slope planting or other
permanent erosion control measures to reduce the potential for significant erosion of the
exposed slopes should be implemented as soon as possible after the finished slope faces
are completed

APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
The percolation characteristics of the on site soils and existing dredged materials
were evaluated by performing twenty two field percolation tests at the test locations
existing ground surface The percolation tests were performed in general accordance
with the State of Hawaii Department of Health s Administrative Rules Chapter
evaluate the permeability of the subsurface soils for wastewater disposal The field
percolation test locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The tests were performed in a 12 mm diameter borehole Approximately
25 millimeters of gravel was placed at the bottom of the borehole to protect the bottom
of the hole from scouring and sediments During testing the hole was carefully filled
with water to a depth of about 150 millimeters above the gravel and refilled as
necessary The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data
upon which the calculation of the percolation test was based The test results are
presented on Plates C 1 through C 22
