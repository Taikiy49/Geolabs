In accordance with your request, we have performed a geotechnical engineering 
exploration in support of the Kapolei Harborside Land Area 5, Phase 2 project in the 
Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. This report summarizes the findings from 
our field exploration and presents our recommendations for the proposed project. The 
findings and recommendations presented herein are intended for design input only and 
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is generally situated near the Barbers Point Harbor in the Campbell 
Industrial Park area of Kapolei on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii, as shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. Based on the information provided, we understand that it is 
proposed to construct a detention basin encompassing approximately 5.8 acres in area to 
support the Kalaeloa commercial development construction. The proposed detention basin 
site is located along Malakole Road adjacent to the Kapolei Harborside Drainage Channel 
to the north and the Island Energy Services facility to the south. 
In general, the project site terrain seems relatively flat with existing ground surface 
elevations ranging from about +10 to +22 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). Based on the 
information provided, we understand that the proposed mass grading construction will 
require site grading of about 5 to 15 feet of fills and cuts to achieve the design finished 
grades.

Site Preparation 
At the onset of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site or 
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the 
excavated materials. 
Soft and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing operations 
should be over-excavated to expose stiff and/or dense materials. The resulting 
excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill materials. The excavated 
soft soils may be used as a source of general fill material, provided that it meets the 
fill material and placement requirements. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and 
replacement with well-compacted fill. A Geolabs representative should evaluate 
the need for over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil conditions. 
Fills and Backfills 
In general, the excavated on-site soils should be suitable for use as general fill 
materials, provided that it is free of vegetation debris and the maximum particle 
size is less than 6 inches in the largest dimension. The on-site materials 
generated from the excavation may be used as a source of general fill or backfill 
materials, provided that they are screened of the oversized materials and/or 
processed to meet the above gradation requirements (less than 6 inches in 
largest dimension). 
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM 
D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
We wish to emphasize that moisture-conditioning of the fill materials is an integral 
and critical part of the earthwork recommendations provided herein. Compaction 
should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other types of 
acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding should not 
be allowed to compact the fills.  
Because moisture-conditioning and compaction of the subgrade soils are critical 
elements of earthwork, Geolabs should provide observations and perform soil 
density tests during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the appropriate

Excavations 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by soft to hard 
silty soils with interbedded layers of medium dense to very dense coralline detritus. 
It is anticipated that the on-site alluvial soils and coralline materials may be readily 
excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as excavators, and 
ripped with large bulldozers. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials are 
based on our field exploration data from the borings drilled and experience in the 
project vicinity. We recommend encouraging contractors to examine the site 
conditions and the subsurface data to make their own interpretations. 
Cut and Fill Slopes 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings, it appears most of 
the cut slopes would expose soils consisting of soft to hard silty soils or medium 
dense to very dense coralline detritus. In general, we believe cut slopes into the 
alluvial soils or coralline material may be constructed at an inclination of two 
horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. Fills placed on slopes steeper than 
5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability for 
the new fill embankment against sliding. The filling operations should start at the 
lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance 
with the above general fill placement recommendations.  
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Track-rolling of fill slopes should not be 
accepted. In the event that over-cutting of a slope occurs, keying and benching 
requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design 
grade with sliver fills. Surface water should be diverted away from the tops of 
slopes, and slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the 
potential for erosion of the finished slopes. 
Erosion Control Measures (Landscape) 
In general, shrinkage cracks may develop in the ground due to the lack of 
maintenance of the soil moisture content as the soils start to dry out under the sun 
and dry weather conditions. The development of shrinkage cracks in the ground 
was apparently intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions in the 
Kapolei/Ewa area resulting in large fluctuations in the soil moisture content. 
We recommend grassing the graded surfaces (including cut and fill slopes) to 
reduce the potential for significant soil erosion soon after the completion of the site 
grading. The relatively dry climatic conditions of the Kapolei/Ewa area likely will

Field Infiltration Testing 
We conducted falling head infiltration tests at two selected locations to evaluate the 
infiltration characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered. The tests were 
performed in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of the State of 
Maryland, Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” 
(rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with other states’ procedures and may 
generally be considered an industry standard. The results of the infiltration tests performed 
at the selected locations are presented in Appendix B. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by drilling a borehole, utilizing a 4-inch 
solid-stem auger to a selected test depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Upon reaching the test depth, a 4-inch diameter PVC solid casing was set to the bottom of 
the drilled borehole to allow infiltration only through the soil exposed at the bottom of the 
borehole. A falling head infiltration test was performed to determine the average infiltration 
rate of the underlying subsurface materials. The test consisted of four trials of filling the 
casing with 24 inches of water and taking periodic readings until the water drained out or 
up to one hour. The infiltration rate was then calculated based on the result of the fourth 
and/or last trial for the test location. The calculated infiltration rate at the test location is 
summarized in the table below.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
eight borings, designated as Boring No. 101 through 108, extending to about 16.5 to 
26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, two boreholes, designated as 
I-101 and 102, were drilled to a depth of about 5 feet below the ground surface for the 
infiltration testing access. The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. The boring was drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous 
flight augers. 
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM 
D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-10. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in 
general accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.
