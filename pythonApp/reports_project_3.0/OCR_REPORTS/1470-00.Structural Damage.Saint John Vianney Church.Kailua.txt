Purpose and Scope
The purpose of this investigation is two fold namely
1 to explore the subsoil condition below the footings of the
existing damaged office building to evaluate possible causes of
the structural damage and provide recommendations for remedial

Project Consideration
The northeastern corner of the existing 2 story office building
suffers distinctive structural damage due to some degree of ground
ovement One major and a few minor cracks appear on the north side of
the CMU wall at the ground floor level Also some very minor cracks
were found at the southwestern side of the building during the time of
our exploration It is our understanding that the cracking of the CMU
wall began soon after a small earthquake which occurred in May of this
year
It is also our understanding that three new single story buildings
are proposed within the existing St John Vianney Church and school
area The proposed layout of these buildings is shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2

However it is anticipated that such loading will be relatively light
Also very little to no site grading is apparently anticipated

Site Description
The project site is a result of the reclamation of the former
Enchanted Lake It is believed that site filling was done about 25 years
ago At present it is an open and level area encompassed on the north
and west by a drainage ditch Keolu Drive on the east and the neighboring
lots on the south Buildings located within the site include the St
John Vianney Church a residence building the church school and the
school adininistration offices
The damaged building is located next to the rectory on the
northwestern part of the site Slight heaving of the ground surface was
noted both along the northern CMU wall outside the building and at the
corner area inside the building where the major crack is located
Small subsidence of the ground surface was indicated by one of the
unjointed concrete slabs located just on the outside of the damaged
corner of the building This piece of slab was noticed to be at a
heaved position before the earthquake and had subsequently settled
since the earthquake
The sites for the three proposed buildings are currently occupied by
lawn areas which serve as part of the playground for the school Between
the proposed building sites was a concrete driveway and a large A C
paved parking area and playground
Some abandoned utilities such as old cesspool and water pipe are
believed to be buried under some of the building sites Their exact
locations are yet unknown

Discussion and Recommendations
General
The subsoil condition at the site as indicated by our borings is
very variable Hence the main concern on the proposed new buildings
will be on differential settlements The foundation system utilized
would depend on the magnitude of the column loads and the ability of the
structure to tolerate the resulting settlements
Damage to the office building is believed to be caused by settlement
of the underlying soil For repair work we suggest construction of new
footings extending to more suitable bearing layer
Detailed discussion and recommendations are presented as follows
Causes and Recommended Methods of Remedy for the Damaged Office Building
Causes of Damage to the Office Building
We believe that the cracks on the CMU wall were caused by settlement
of a layer of loose granular material located underneath and around
the footing and the wall This settlement was probably a result of
densification of the loose material which was initiated by the
vibration of the earthquake which struck the area in May of this
year
Based on our laboratory tests and visual observation the layer of
loose material can be classified as silty fine sand which under dry
condition is very susceptible to densification by vibration
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1470 00 Page 7
Heaving of the lightly loaded concrete slab and sidewalk was caused
by the entirely different clay material which was also underlying
the building Laboratory tests indicated that the swelling
potential of these soils might be classified as moderate 6 4
percent It is our opinion that these soils could heave the
lightly loaded slabs but not the heavier wall footing Therefore
we believe that the observed heaving of the ground surface is not
related to the cracks on the wall
Method of Remedy
To prevent further deterioration of the existing damaged condition
immediate correction should be carried out We believe that a
practical remedy is to construct new footings which will extend at
least 4 feet below the present footing elevation onto soil with
stiffer consistency In order to carry out the construction
underpinning of the existing wall footing is necessary
For the design of underpinning and new footing we believe that an
allowable bearing pressure of 2000 p s f can be used Compaction
of the bearing soil to achieve 90 of maximum dry density as
established by ASTM D 1557 78 method prior to the construction of
footing is recommended To minimize swelling of the bearing soil
compaction should be carried out at a water content wet of optimum
Conditions at the bottom of all footings should be inspected by the
soils engineer prior to concrete placement
Provided loadings on footing are kept within the allowable bearing
pressure of 2000 p s f minimal settlement is anticipated

Foundation Recommendations for the New Buildings
Information from various sources indicate that the existing fill has
been inplace for about 25 years and therefore would indicate that the
ground settlements due to the weight of the fill has essentially
stopped The laboratory consolidation tests appear to confirm this
Our borings not only revealed that the subsoil condition was
variable but also encountered a thin layer of soft compressible
materials underlying some of the proposed building locations
Since the proposed buildings have not been designed yet and details
of the structure are unavailable Several alternative foundation systems
were studied and are described below
The main consideration for the foundation design would be the
settlements caused by the new building loads For very light wood
structures shallow footing foundation may be utilized As the loading
increases the settlements would also increase and therefore for
heavier structures a surcharge program mat foundation or even pile
foundations may be required to reduce the foundation settlements
Shallow Spread or Continuous Footing Foundations
Based on economy and provided that the loadings are light shallow
spread or continuous footing appears suitable for foundation
support Nevertheless because of the non uniform subsoil
condition differential settlement may occur
Our preliminary analysis indicated that for 5 foot square footings
20 feet apart with loading varying between 800 to 1000 pounds per
square foot differential settlement of about three fourths of an
inch may occur within a span of 80 feet at the office building
while at the other two buildings anticipated differential
settlements are on the order of one half of an inch within similar
span
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1470 00 Page 9
Total settlement on the other hand varies between 1 to 2 5 inches
about 70 of which will occur at a slow rate Time required for 90
completion of the slow settlement varies between 1 5 to 2 years
To reduce the amount of settlement light wood framed structures
should be considered to reduce the structural loading Also the
use of individual spread footing with provisions for periodic
relevelling of the structural columns is recommended
If it is required to accelerate the slow occurring building footing
settlements we recommend the use of a surcharge fill program For
a surcharge fill height of about 10 feet the anticipated surcharge
period would be about 6 to 8 months Settlement gauges should be
installed in appropriate locations to monitor the actual ground
settlement and to determine the end of the settlement period
All footings should be embedded at least 2 feet below the lowest
adjacent grade and any soft or expansive soils encountered at the
bottom of the footing excavations should be removed to firm soils
and backfilled with compacted fill All footings should be tied
together with grade beams to improve the rigidity of the foundation
hence reducing the differential settlement
Generally slab on grade should be avoided unless a surcharge
program is first utilized to minimize settlements The on site
soils are moderately expansive and therefore if slab on grade is
used it should be placed over a 4 inch layer of compacted base
course over a minimum of 12 inches of non expansive import soil
The thickened edge around the slab on grade should extend a minimum
12 inches below the adjacent ground surface The slab subgrade
soils should also be kept moist and not be allowed to dry out prior
to concrete placement

Compensated Mat Foundation
Compensated mat foundation requires the construction of a large
concrete slab following the excavation of an amount of soil the
weight of which equals the combined weight of the building structure
and the mat Theoretically assuming that the mat is rigid enough
the system viz the structure and the foundation will float on
the soil mass and no settlement occurs
To overcome the non uniform subsoil condition at the site we
believe a compensated mat foundation despite its high cost may
provide a solution We recommend that excavation should not extend
beyond 8 feet below the ground where the water table was generally
encountered during our soil exploration
The use of compensated mat foundation may become economical if the
space generated by the excavation can be utilized for example
construction of a basement area Furthermore if the excavated
material can be utilized in other ways such as surcharging another
building area then the high cost of the mat foundation system may
be justified
Pile Foundation
Friction piles can provide another alternative as foundation
Support Because of the non uniform subsoil condition the depths
of penetration of pile to generate a certain capacity would vary at
different locations of the site The following will provide a
general guideline regarding the depth of penetration

Site Grading
Site filling should be avoided or at least minimized to less than
one foot since the weight of the new fill would initiate long term ground
settlements
The ground surface below the structures should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 6 inches and recompacted to a minimum of 90 of its
maximum dry density as established by ASTM D 1557 78 Soft pockets
encountered in the initial site preparation should be removed and
backfilled with select backfill material
Backfills should be placed in horizontal lifts not exceeding 6
inches in compacted thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 of its
maximum dry density as established by ASTM D 1557 78
The imported fill material if required should be granular
materials such as coral or tuff free of organic material non expansive
and generally less than 6 inches in size

Site grading should provide drainage of surface runoff away from all
foundations
Design Review
Plans and specifications for site grading and foundations should be
forwarded to the soils engineer for review and written comments prior to
construction usage to see that the intent of our recommendations is
included
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the filling footing excavation backfilling
and pile driving operations be observed by the soils engineer during
construction to see that adequate compaction has been obtained and that
the subsurface conditions are consistent with the findings of our
investigation
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and
approved by the soils engineer prior to hauling to the site
If conditions encountered during construction are different from
those presented in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made

Field Exploration
ek ee
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling
eight 8 borings to depths of 15 to 28 5 feet and one 1 cone
penetration test to depth of 30 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 A
truck mounted drill rig was used to drill the borings In addition two
2 shallow probings using hand auger were excavated below the footings
to depths of 4 feet
The soils encountered in the borings were classified by visual and
textural examination in the field by our engineer who continuously
monitored the drilling operations The classifications were further
reviewed by visual inspection and testing in the laboratory The soils
were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Log of Borings and Probings Plates A 1
through A 10
Soil samples were obtained from the boring by driving a 2 inch
standard split spoon sampler or a 2 4 inch I D split barrel sampler
with a 140 pound hammer free falling 30 inches The blow counts to
drive the sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the Log of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
The cone penetration test was performed by mechanically pushing an
electric cone into the ground The tip resistance and the shaft
friction were measured at regular depth of penetration Interpretation
of soil profile was carried out based on the magnitude of the recorded
forces and the friction ratio A graphic presentation of the result is
shown on Plate A 11

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
de ee
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on
selected samples to aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the log of
borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two Atterberg Limits tests were performed on the underlying
fine grained material to further identify their physical characteristics
towards change of water content The test results are summarized on
Plate B 1
Five sieve analyses were performed on the upper fill soil to
determine the grain size distribution of the soil The test results are
summarized on Plates B 1 and B 2
One expansion test was performed on a remolded air dried sample
obtained directly underneath the footing of the damaged building to
determine its swelling potential The test result is summarized on
Plate B 2
Two direct shear tests were performed on undisturbed samples to
evaluate the strength characteristics of the fill soil The test
results are summarized on Plates B 3 and B 4
Two consolidation tests were performed on samples of the soft layer
to evaluate its settlement characteristics The consolidation curves
are presented on Plates B 5 and B 6
