INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of a geotechnical engineering
investigation performed for the proposed Automated Message Exchange U S
Naval Communication Station at San Miguel the Republic of the
Philippines
The general location of the project site is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
PURPOSE AND SCOPE OF WORK
The purpose of our investigation was to evaluate the subsurface
conditions at the site to provide pertinent soil data such as shear
strength compressibility swelling potential and soil classification for
the site grading and foundation design of the proposed communication
station
Particular attention was drawn to determine a suitable bearing layer
for the building foundation and to study the effect of site filling on
different foundation systems Also the liquefaction potential of the
underlying loose granular soil was evaluated
The scope of the investigation was performed in general accordance
with our proposal dated and included a review of
available aerial photographs geologic literature and soil reports of the
general area field reconnaissance drilling of 6 borings laboratory
soils testing engineering analysis of the field and laboratory data and
report preparation

field vane shear tests were performed in order to obtain additional
in situ shear strength data

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a one story 16 638 square foot metal
framed building with a raised metal flooring on concrete subfloor metal

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The project site is located within the Western Volcanic Intrusive
Province of Iuzon and comprises Recent to Pliocene Quaternary Volcanic
deposits The E W Trending San Antonio Fracture Zone is probably the
closest identified major fault zone about 8 to 10 miles northerly of the
site
At the project site the volcanic deposits are primarily of
pyroclastic orgin and consist of lithic tuffs volcanic breccia
agglomerate and volcanic mudflows lahar believed to have been
generated by explosive eruptions from Mount Natib Mount Santa Rita and
Mount Balakibok three of the major volcanic centers which comprise the
zambales Bataan Volcanic Highlands
Examination of the geomorphology of the area leads to the conjecture
that the site is located on a marine deltaic flat located at the toe of a
rugged mountain terrain The area was cut during low stands of the sea by
streams originating in the mountainous areas to the east of the site
Numerous glacial eustatic sea level variations caused the emergence
and submergence of the land mass that produced soil deposits at various
levels both above and below the present sea level These low energy
deposits are usually soft and highly compressible From the geology map
and older 1955 air photos of the site it appears that the area was
ponded at one time possibly for the growing of rice then filled later
with undivided surficial deposits for the development of the naval base

Subsoil Condition
The subsurface condition at the project site was explored by
drilling and sampling six 6 borings to depth of 71 5 to 105 feet below
the ground surface at the approximate locations shown on Site Plan Plate
2 The borings were located based on the preliminary layout of the
building Subsequent to the field exploration the building location was
moved about 90 feet west and 40 feet north of the original location
However we believe the borings are still representative of the subsurface
conditions below the present building site
In general the top 20 to 25 feet of soils underlying the entire
site are relatively uniforn consisting of about five 5 feet of fill

Underlying the organic silty clay layer our borings encountered about 35

at depths of about 70 to 75 feet below the ground surface very stiff to
hard highly weathered tuff was encountered

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Our borings encountered a layer of soft organic clay and a thick
deposit of loose silty to clayey sand underlying the project site
Because of their high compressibility the existence of these soft and
loose layer have a major effect on the grading and foundation design of
the project
Fill of about 10 feet high is required to raise the site for flood
control and to compensate for the anticipated ground settlement The
weight of the new fills will cause considerable settlements due to
compression of the underlying soft and loose deposits Also some amount
of long term secondary settlement is anticipated from the organic soil
layer In order to accelerate the ground settlement vertical drains
and or surcharge fill are recommended in the building area
Both shallow spread footing and pile foundations were analyzed For
the use of shallow footings sufficient waiting time is required for the
majority of the settlement to occur and some amount of long term
settlement should be anticipated Also significant amount of damage
Should be anticipated if the underlying loose sandy layer liquefies during
a strong earthquake For pile foundations relatively long piles are
required to penetrate onto the hard bearing soil where support for the
load imposed by both the building and the anticipated downdrag may be
provided

Vertical Drains The time required for the settlement to occur
is governed primarily by the distance that the water in the soil
pores must travel to drain out Normally the water travels up
or down in the profile until a permeable layer is encountered
The settlement time varies with the square of the distance the
water must travel drainage path The presence of the vertical
drain reduces the drainage path the water must travel as it is
squeezed out of the soft soil Hence it greatly accelerates
the rate of settlement To be effective the drain has to
extend from the ground surface to the bottom of the soft layer
about 60 to 70 feet below the ground surface A schematic
diagram of a typical vertical drain installation is show on
Plate E 3
Different types of drain e g prefabricated and sand drains
are available However because of its comparatively lower cost
and relative ease of installation we believe that prefabricated
drains are preferrable for this project These drains usually
consist of a core of plastic and a filter sleeve of paper
fibrous material or porous plastic The size of the drains is
usually on the order of 100 mm wide by 1 to 7 mm thick

Liquefaction Evaluation
Soil Liquefaction
When a loose saturated sand deposit is subject to vibration such as
during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in
volume causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is
unable to occur rapidly enough due to the large extent of the sand
deposit to dissipate the build up in pore pressure the effective
stress internal strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained
vibration the pore water pressure build up could equal the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1681 00 Page 17
Dk No 14
overburden pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to
zero and causing it to act as a viscous fluid This phenomenon is
Called liquefaction The major factors affecting the liquefaction
characteristics of a soil are
1 Grain size distribution Fine and uniform sand are more prone
to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial relative density Loose sands are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential decreases with increased
relative density
3 Magnitude and duration of vibration Liquefaction potential
increases with increases in the magnitude and duration of the
earthquake
General Site Seismicity
Subic Bay is located in one of the most active seismic zones of the
world It has been categorized in the Navy Design Manual NAVFAC
P 355 as a Seismic Zone 4 area on a 0 to 4 zone scale indicating
the potential for great damage due to earthquake A number of
active faults such as the San Antonio Fracture zone Manila Trench
and the Iba Fracture Zone are located within a 20 to 25 mile limit
of the site
A detail seismicity study was not performed for the current
project However based on the results for the nearby Hospital
Replacement Project study located about 0 5 mile from the current
site all the above mentioned faults have the potential to generate
earthquakes with a magnitude in the 6 and 7 range on the Richter
scale

Consequences of Liquefaction
We believe that under a strong earthquake with magnitude in excess
of 6 on the Richter scale occuring at a distance of less than 20
miles from the site there is a strong possibility that the loose
sand underlying the site would liquefy
Liquefaction of the underlying sands could cause extensive ground
settlement and slope instability near the edge of the fill area
Ground settlement on the order of 1 to 3 feet may occur and
thereby disrupt utility service For a building supported by
shallow spread footing soil liquefaction may result in structure
tilting and failure
To minimize the liquefaction potential densification of the loose
sand below the fill would be required Densification methods such
as the use of vibrating probe and compaction piles or dynamic
consolidation may be used However these procedures must be done
by very experienced contractores and are very costly
In lieu of the densification procedure we believe that if the
communication station is supported by piles with a structural slab
system it may remain intact even with the large amount of ground
settlement from liquefaction

Relatively light structural loads are anticipated for the new
building Normally spread footing foundations bearing on the compacted
fill could be utilized However for this particular project pile
foundations may be preferable due to the following reasons

1 Even after the soil improvement program long term secondary

Pile Foundation
All the piles should extend through the fill and the soft loose
deposits and bear on the very stiff to hard weathered tuff
formation The anticipated length of pile is estimated to be on the
order of 90 to 100 feet
The piles will be driven after the fill has been placed to its
finish grade and allowed to settle Therefore the piles must
penetrate through the compacted new fill layers and we anticipate
that pre drilling through the fill may be required
Where fill is placed over a compressible soil the latter
consolidates and moves downwards relative to the pile The downward
movement would result in negative skin friction forces generally
known as downdrag force on the pile shaft The magnitude of the
downdrag force depends on the thickness of compressible layer
adhesion between the soil and pile and the pile perimeter area
Although a surcharge program will be utilized at the site to
initiate most of the ground settlement prior to the structure
construction some amount of long term settlement will still occur
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1681 00 Page 20
Dk No 14
Therefore downdrag forces are anticipated on the piles At the
project site the average downdrag force is estimated to be on the
order of 40 tons per pile
Calculations indicate that a 16 inch square prestressed concrete
pile bearing on the stiff to hard weathered tuff formation may be
designed for 100 ton total load carrying capacity However after
taking the downdrag force into account the net allowable pile
supporting capacity is reduced to 60 tons
Pile Driving
The piles should be driven with a hammer with a maximum rated energy
of at least lbs The hammer should be equipped with an
energy level control so that driving within the soft deposits can be
achieved at a reduced energy level to minimize the tensile stress
within the pile which could cause damage to the pile
The recommended pile driving criteria are as follows
1 The driving process should be continuous without interruption
for each pile A pile may be rejected or its design capacity
modified when the driving process is interrupted for more than 4
hours
2 During easy driving when the pile tip is still within soft
soil the hammer stroke should be carefully controlled so that
excessive tensile stresses are not transmitted to the pile
3 The piles must be driven to the weathered tuff formation layer
which is located about 80 to 100 feet below the existing ground
surface

4 The pile driving may be terminated when the blow counts are
steadily increasing with depth and reach 10 blows per inch for
each of the last 3 inches or aggregated blow counts of 120 blows
for the last foot are achieved
This criteria is based on the use of a diesel driving hammer
with maximum rated energy of lbs to drive a 16 inch
square concrete pile with a net design load of 100 tons This
driving criteria should be modified according to the actual

provisions are made for releveling of the building and or equipment
due to long term settlements Otherwise we would recommend that
the structure be supported by pile foundation
The proposed one story building will be situated on new fill over
the soft ground area
An allowable bearing value of 3000 p s f on compacted fill may be
used This bearing value may be increased by one third for short
duration wind or seismic loads The footings should be embedded a
minimum of 24 inches below the final finished grade For those
footing located close to the edge of fill slope sufficient

APPENDIX
Settlement Analysis
A ee
The project site is underlain by layers of compressible soft clayey
and loose sandy materials A settlement analysis was performed to
evaluate the effect of filling over this area
The settlement analysis was carried out by the use of the computer
program CONSOL developed by the Department of Civil Engineering at the
University of California at Berkeley The computer program calculates the
total and time rate of settlement for one dimensional layered soil masses
using an implicit finite difference method
Two of the most important soil properties that the program requires
as input are the compression index and the coefficient of consolidation
For the current analysis these latter data were obtained from six 6
one dimensional consolidation tests and twelve 12 time settlement plots
The results of the analysis can be divided into the following
catagories
1 Effect of the fill thickness on total primary settlement of soft
deposit the results are summarized on Plate C 1
2 Effect of surcharge thickness on the percentage of total primary
settlement after one year the results are summarized on Plate
C 2
3 Effect of site filling on the total primary settlement of
adjacent area the results are summarized on Plate 3
In addition to the primary settlement which occurs as a result of
consolidation long term secondary settlement due to soil creep will occur
over the design life of the structure The amount of secondary settlement
depends mainly on the thickness of the soft compressible layer and the
length of time after the completion of the primary settlement
Assuming that a soil improvement program will be carried out to
reduce completion time of the primary settlement to one year then the
estimate of the total secondary settlement over the design life of say 50
years will be on the order of 2 to 4 inches

APPENDIX D
Slope Stability analysis
Slope stability analysis for the perimeter of the fill area were
performed
The analysis were carried out by using the computer program STABR
developed by the Department of Civil Engineering at the University of
California at Berkeley The program calculates the factor of safety by
using the Ordinary Method of Slices and the Bishop s Modified Method both
of which involve circular failure surface STABR can also perform
pseudostatic analysis of the slope under seismic conditions
For the analysis of the perimeter fill slope the most critical
condition lowest factor of safety is at the end of the fill operation
when the underlying weak soil is under the load of the new fill As time
passes the underlying soil consolidates and gains strength Therefore
the factor of safety increases
For the current project stability analysis were performed for both
analysis an earthquake force represented by a seismic coefficient of
0 15g was applied to the sliding mass The results of the analysis are
presented on Plate D 1
Plate D 1 shows the variation of the factor of safety due to the
change in slope ratio It indicates that the critical slope ratio which
corresponds to a factor of safety of 1 5 during the end of filling
condition is about 5 1 With the use of this slope ratio the perimeter
Slope is found to have adequate long term stability even under seismic
condition

APPENDIX E
Vertical Drain Analysis
SA a A El Se ne
The most important design parameter for a drain system is the drain
spacing In general the closer the spacing the shorter will be the time
required to achieve a certain percentage of settlement In addition to
the drain spacing the required time of consolidation depends on the drain
size and the coefficient of consolidation
With the assumption that a band shaped drain 100 mm x 7 mm is
used the relationship between drain spacings and required time for 90
consolidation is calculated This relationship is shown on Plate E 1
Plate E 1 indicates that if 90 consolidation were to be achieved in 12
months then the required drain spacing is about 6 feet
Both the drain resistance and disturbance during installation may
cause the actual time for consolidation to be greater than the estimated
time shown on Plate E 1 Therefore Plate E 1 can only be used to provide
a rough estimate Modification to the estimates should be made after the
results from the test section are obtained
The drainage blanket used for the drain system should consist of
clean sand with no more than 3 fine passing the No 200 sieve A typical
gradation requirement per the Navy Design Manual DM 7 1 is shown on Plate
E 2
A schematic diagram of the vertical drain installation recommended
for this project is shown on Plate E 3
