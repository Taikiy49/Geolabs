This report presents the results of our soil and foundation
investigation performed for the above subject project The approximate
location of the project site and its vicinity is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
Our work was performed in general accordance with our proposal of
The purpose of the investigation was to explore the subsurface soil
conditions at the mauka distressed portion of the structure to provide
recommendations for the design of the underpinning of the continuous wall
footings at these locations Our investigation consisted of the
following
W O 924 30 Page 2
1 Four borings drilled with soil and rock cored samples taken to
depths of 8 to 57 feet below the existing ground surface and
floor slab
2 laboratory testing including soil and rock classifications
and moisture content on representative soil samples obtained
and
5 pressure grouting at selected depths in two boring locations to
monitor and observe the feasibility of strengthening the clayey
soils below the footings
The results of our field exploration and laboratory tests which we
used in the engineering analysis to formulate our conclusions and
recommendations are presented in the appendices of this report
The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
Geologically the site is quite complex It is located on the
lower reaches of the junction of Nuuanu and Pauoa Valleys which were
formed by the deep erosion of the Koolau Ridge The period of erosion
was followed by valley filling with alluvial deposits then covered by
the Honolulu Volcanic Series whose episodes were spread out over a
considerable period of time
The top of the lower basalt which was encountered at 40 5 to
47 foot depths may be associated with the Luakaha Vent an earlier Nuuanu
Flow of the Honolulu Volcanic Series This lower unit is probably many
tens of feet thick The alluvial soil layer above this was deposited by
streams during a cessation of volcanic activity This alluvial soil
layer was later covered by the upper thin Nuuanu Basalt Flow and
underlying cemented cinder and conglomerate material which appears to
have been from the Makaku cone Continued erosion and deposition by the
intermittent streams has resulted in the burial of these Pleistocene
Volcanics by more recent alluvial materials

Groundwater
Groundwater was encountered in some of the borings The measured
water levels below the drilled borings are shown on the logs of borings
In general the groundwater levels appear to be about 20 to 24 feet below
the existing grades and may fluctuate due to seasonal rainfall
influences High groundwater levels were encountered in some of the
previous borings and appeared to represent perched water level
Grouted mini piles were installed in two of the borings to obtain
information on the feasibility and effectiveness of utilizing the
mini pile system and grouting to support the affected footings

The borings indicate that the building site is underlain by a
medium stiff layer of clay which generally increases in thickness toward
the mauka end of the building This trend follows the observed
settlement of the building which also increases toward the mauka end
The upper clay layer is probably underlain by a thin layer of basalt
followed by stiff alluvium and a thick layer of basalt It appears that
the upper thin basalt layer could prevent percolation of rain water and
create a perched water level which could saturate the clay soils under
the footings
The recent borings generally confirm our previous investigation
results which indicate that the settlement of the footings are most
likely due to compression of the upper clay layer caused by saturation
and softening as a result of perched water condition
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 924 30 Page
The field investigation indicates that there are suitable
underlying layers capable to provide Support for underpinning of the
affected footings
Discussion with the structural engineer indicates that due to
structural requirements the underpinning supports can only be spaced a
maximum distance of 4 feet apart It is also our understanding that due
to budget constraints only the mauka end bearing wall footing will be
underpinned at this time The inner wall footing may be underpinned at a
later date
Guidelines for several underpinning options are presented below
Drilled Caisson Supported by Upper Rock Layer
1 This method would consist of drilling a hole next to the outside
edge of the footing down to suitable support The reinforcement
would be placed into the hole then filled with concrete A
reinforced concrete bracket would then be constructed under the
footing and attached to the top of the caisson to transfer the
building loads to the caisson
2 The upper rock layer is about 10 to 16 feet below the bearing wall
at the end of the building The caisson should extend about 1 foot
into this rock layer
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 9
3 An allowable bearing value of 20 000 p s f may be used in the
caisson design Skin friction on the caisson shaft should be
ignored in the design
4 Additional probes 1 to 2 inches in diameter should be made
during the underpinning work in 3 selected caisson holes with a
rock drill to verify that sufficient rock thickness approximately
3 feet is present below the bottom of the caisson
5 There are boulders in the soil layer above the upper rock layer
and therefore field inspection by a qualified engineer or
geologist is critical to see that proper penetration through the
boulders and bearing on the rock layer are achieved
6 We recommend that a contingency item be allowed in case the upper
rock is very thin and possibly 2 of the caissons may have to be
extended to the lower rock layer at about the 40 foot level
7 The caisson underpinning construction should be Staggered and
installed one at a time in order to minimize exposing too large an
excavation area under the existing footing which could cause
additional settlement
8 The excavation should be backfilled with granular material to
minimize potential settlement of the slab due to settlement of
loose backfill
GEOLABS HAWAII
WO 924 30 Page 10
Drilled Caisson Supported by Lower Rock Layer
1 The caissons should extend a minimum of 1 foot into the lower rock
layer which is about 38 to 42 feet below the footing level
2 An allowable bearing value of 30 000 p s f may be used in the
caisson design
3 The drilling would have to extend through the upper rock layer and
encounter ground water level prior to reaching the lower rock layer
Items 7 and 8 mentioned in the earlier section also apply to this
alternative
Mini Pile System Supported by Upper Rock Layer
1
The general procedure would be to drill a hole through the existing
footing down to suitable support Place a steel pipe into the
hole then pump grout under pressure into the hole to fill any
possible voids in the surrounding ground and to increase the
stiffness of the pile A concrete cap could be constructed between
the footing and the mini pile to transfer the wall loads to the
pile
The mini pile should have a minimum diameter of 6 inches and be
installed as described in the section above
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 924 30 Page ll
3 The piles should be staggered on both sides of the bearing wall to
minimize eccentricity
The underlying soils are impermeable and therefore cement or
chemical grout would have minimal penetration Compaction grouting in
which the grout is used to compress the soils into a denser mass
thereby reducing its compressibility could be used However it is
presently believed that compaction grouting under the affected footings
which are undergoing settlement due to softening of clayey soil does not
provide the adequate assurance of long term Stability
Conclusions
For underpinning the end bearing wall it appears that the caissons
to the upper rock layer may have an economic advantage over the other
systems since adequate bearing could be achieved at a shallow depth and
the area is accessible for caisson drilling rigs
For underpinning interior walls it appears that there would not be
sufficient space for accessibility of caisson rigs and therefore the
mini pile system may have a Significant advantage
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 924 30 Page 12
Based on our evaluation of the soil conditions and trial grouting
attempts in the two holes it is our opinion that compaction grouting
could be attempted at the site However for long term condition we
believe the underpinning method direct footing load transfer to an
underlying competent rock layer appears to have a better chance of
SsuCCESS
It is recommended that the soils engineer be present during
remedial construction to monitor the contractor s underpinning operations
The recommendations given in this report are contingent upon the
above inspections
