PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The proposed project consists of a new bike and pedestrian path primarily along 
the shoreline from Coconut Marketplace to the Uhelekawawa Stream. This section is 
about one mile long and will connect with the previously constructed Phases A and B. 
We anticipate a 10 to 12-foot wide concrete pavement is needed for the new path.  
The new bike/pedestrian path starts on Papaloa Road, near the Coconut 
Marketplace, and travels toward the ocean between the Kauai Shores Hotel and the 
Aston Islander on the Beach.  The path turns east and then follows along the shoreline 
towards Mokihana of Kauai. The path then turns north and runs along the eastern 
property boundary of the Coconut Plantation towards Kuhio Highway. About less than 
halfway towards Kuhio Highway, the path splits with one path turning east towards 
Uhelekawawa Stream and then following along the stream north towards the 
Uhelekawawa Stream Bridge with the other path continuing north towards the planned

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano built 
by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series beginning about 5 to 6 
million years ago. The eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series ended about 2½ million 
years ago and was followed by a long period of erosion. Following the cessation of this 
main volcano shield building phase, about 1½ million years ago, there was renewed

volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the post-erosional Koloa Volcanic 
Series and the concurrent deposition of the thick alluvial sediments of the Palikea 
Formation. 
Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as thick 
lava flows composed of dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north-south 
trends at various locales. Associated with the Koloa Volcanic Series lava flows are some 
deposits of pyroclastic materials (volcanic ash and cinders), which usually form cinder 
cones surrounding the vent. Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of 
the eastern half of the Island of Kauai, including the project site. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, many sea level changes occurred as a result of 
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great continental 
glaciers accumulated, ocean levels decreased. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or 
melted, global sea levels increased. The landmass of Kauai remained essentially stable 
during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These glacio eustatic 
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea, which were both higher and lower relative to the 
present sea level of the Island of Kauai. 
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters 
protected by fringing reefs. 
The basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lavas of the Koloa Volcanic Series are 
generally characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin 
clinker layers.  The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils which grade 
to saprolite with depth.  In general, saprolite is composed of mainly silty material and is 
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks.  The saprolite grades to basaltic rock 
formation with depth. 
SITE DESCRIPTION 
A site reconnaissance was performed to observe the surface conditions along the 
proposed path alignment. Nine grab samples of the near-surface soils were collected to 
evaluate the engineering properties of the near surface soils.  
Based on our field observations, the path alignment is generally covered with top 
soil, beach sand deposits and near-surface fills. Along the path alignment from Papaloa 
Road to about midway between Papaloa Road and the shoreline, the ground surface is 
generally covered by grass and top soil consisting of loose to medium dense sand with a 
little silt and traces of gravel. Then along the path alignment, the shoreline is generally 
covered by beach deposits consisting of loose sands. Between the planned path 
intersection and Uhelekawawa Stream Bridge, the ground surface was generally covered 
by asphaltic concrete pavements and near-surface fills consisting of loose to medium

SURFACE SOIL CONDITIONS 
The surface soil conditions along the new path were explored by obtaining 
surface grab samples, designated as Grabs No   s. 1 through 9, to depths of 6 to 9 
inches below the existing ground surface. The approximate locations of the grab 
samples are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2. 
The surface soil material generally consisted of top soil, fill, and beach sand. The 
fill and beach sand deposit consisted of loose to medium dense brown sand with traces 
of silt.  Groundwater was not encountered in the grab sample excavation at the time of 
our site reconnaissance. 
LABORATORY TESTS 
Moisture content, sieve analysis and remolded ring swell tests were performed on 
the grab samples collected. Moisture content (ASTM D 2216) determinations were 
performed on the grab samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties.  The results of these tests are presented on Plate 3. Five sieve analysis tests 
(ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected grab samples to evaluate the gradation 
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Graphic presentation of the grain 
size distribution is provided on Plate 4. Three one-inch ring swell tests were performed on 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils.  The results 
of ring swell tests are summarized on Plate 5. 
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our site reconnaissance, we observed that the project site is generally 
covered with top soil, fill, and beach sand deposits. We believe that the path pavement 
structure may be supported by the medium dense sandy soils. Detailed discussions and 
recommendations for the design of the path pavement are presented in the following 
sections. 
Path Pavement 
We anticipate a rigid pavement will be used for the new bike and pedestrian path 
for this project.  We understand the rigid pavement for the new bike and pedestrian path 
will be subjected to vehicular traffic in some areas. To reduce the potential for cracking 
at the edge of the pavement, we recommend that a thickened-edge pavement slab be 
utilized in areas subjected to vehicular traffic. 
Based on our field reconnaissance, the path pavement will be underlain by loose 
to medium dense fills and beach sand.  Based on the above, the following pavement 
section may be used.

Rigid Pavement 
6.0-Inch Portland Cement Concrete 
6.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade 
The subgrade soil under the pavement area should be moisture-conditioned to 
near the optimum moisture and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage 
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM 
D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density.  California Bearing Ratio (CBR) and density 
tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during construction 
to confirm the adequacy of the above section. 
Paved areas should be sloped, and drainage gradients maintained to carry 
surface water off-site. Surface water ponding should not be allowed on-site during or 
after construction.  
Subgrade Preparation Below Pavement Section 
We anticipate that the majority of the new pavements will be constructed in cut 
conditions or near the existing grades. In general, the area within the contract grading 
limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed at the onset of earthwork. Vegetation, 
debris, deleterious material, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and 
properly disposed of off-site or disposed in a designated area to reduce the potential for 
contamination of the excavated materials. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over-excavated to expose 
stiff and/or dense materials. The resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
aggregate subbase materials. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be 
properly disposed of off-site. 
Aggregate subbase materials should meet the material requirements for 
Subbase Course as specified in Section 30 of the Standard Specifications for Public 
Works Construction, County of Kauai, September 1986.  Aggregate subbase materials 
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in 
8-inch level loose lifts, and compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. 
After clearing and grubbing, the future pavement areas should be excavated, 
where necessary, to the pavement subgrade level.  The pavement subgrade soils 
should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to near the 
optimum moisture content, and compacted to no less than 95 percent relative 
compaction.  Compaction should be accomplished using sheepsfoot rollers, smooth 
drum rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment.

LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the limited site reconnaissance and surface soil samples 
collected (grab samples). Variations of subsoil conditions beyond and between the field 
data points may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become 
evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be 
necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The grab sample locations indicated in this report are approximate, having been 
taped from existing visible features indicated on the topographic survey plan received from 
WSP on June 21, 2018. The physical locations and elevations of the grab samples should 
be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
This report has been prepared for the exclusive use of WSP for specific 
application to the new bike and pedestrian path for the Lydgate Park-Kapaa 
Bike/Pedestrian Path Phases C & D project, located in Kapaa on the Island of Kauai, 
Hawaii in accordance with generally accepted geotechnical engineering principles and 
practices. No warranty is expressed or implied.  
This report has been prepared solely for the purpose of assisting the design 
engineers in the preparation of the bike and pedestrian path design, and site grading 
recommendations for the proposed project. 
The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated soil and/or rock conditions are 
encountered commonly during construction. Unforeseen subsurface conditions, such as 
soft deposits, hard layers, hard obstructions, or perched groundwater, may occur in 
localized areas and may require additional probing or corrections in the field (which may 
result in construction delays) to attain a properly constructed project. Therefore, a sufficient 
contingency fund is recommended to accommodate these possible extra costs. 
This geotechnical engineering exploration conducted at the project site was not 
intended to investigate the potential presence of hazardous materials existing at the site. It 
should be noted that the equipment, techniques, and personnel used to conduct a 
geo-environmental exploration differ substantially from those applied in geotechnical 
engineering.
