INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed improvements to the AT T International Makaha Cable Head Station in the
Makaha area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Piate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses The
recommendations presented herein are intended for design of foundations retaining walls
rockfall mitigation measures site preparation and pavements only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age Ice Age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking
against its eastern flank
The project site is located near the base of the lower southeast facing flank of Puu
Keaau which forms the northwestern ridge of Makaha Puu Keaau is an eroded ridge
expression of a portion of the Waianae Shield Volcano The area is composed of layered
basaltic lava flows and pyroclastic materials which are grouped and classified as the
Waianae Volcanic Series Typically surface deposits of consolidated alluvium and colluvium
are found overlying the basalt formation In general a layer of soil mantles the ground
surface of the slopes The soils generally increase in thickness when progressing down the
slopes to the middle of the valley

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As mentioned above our field exploration at the subject site generally encountered
very stiff clays with varying amounts of sand gravel cobbles and boulders underlain by
colluvial soil deposits consisting of dense boulders and clay The dense colluvial soil deposit
encountered in our borings may be considered competent materials for support of structure
foundations Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field
exploration It should be noted that the near surface clay soils encountered at the eastern
portion of the site where the new parking area is planned exhibit a moderate to high
expansion potential
Based on our field exploration we believe that the existing underground building mat
foundation bearing on the dense colluvial soil materials may be used to support the
above ground building addition Based on our analysis we believe that the bearing
pressures used in the original design of the mat foundation may be increased appreciably
and may be used to evaluate support of the new building addition on the existing
foundations
Since the occurrence of debris flows and falling boulders may exist at the project site
we believe that rockfall mitigation measures should be implemented at the site to reduce the
potential for damage to the building structure due to rockfall Detailed discussion of these
items and our geotechnical engineering recommendations are presented in the following
sections of this report

Wall Foundations
An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be utilized for wall foundations
bearing on properly compacted fill consisting of select granular materials or the very
stiff on site clayey materials This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may
be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces Wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches and should
be embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent grade For
footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground the footings should be
embedded an additional amount to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance
of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of the slope
Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in
stepped footings
If wall foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom
edge of the utility trench or extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable
foundation settlement
For footings bearing on the in situ soils the bottom of the footing excavations should
be recompacted to a firm and unyielding condition prior to the placement of reinforcing
steel and concrete If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of
the footing excavations they should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials The over excavation should either be backfilled with select granular
materials compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the bottom
of footing may be extended down to bear on the underlying competent material
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with procedures described in ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91

Landslide Potential
The project site is located along the base of the same ridge as the Makaha Valley
Towers Condominiums where a debris flow occurred in 1996 The ridge above the
condominiums consisted of a series of deep valleys and peaks formed by surface water
runoff and erosion This type of terrain is susceptible to debris flows where high
concentrations of runoff water transport debris down the valleys
Unlike the terrain above the Makaha Valley Towers Condominiums the ridge above
the project site contains fewer valleys and peaks and is therefore less susceptible to large
debris flows Although the potential for large debris flows to occur is less likely at the project
site smaller debris flows and falling boulders may still occur Therefore we recommend that
a catchment area be constructed along the upslope boundary of the site

to reduce the potential for migration of excess landscape water into the pavement section
Alternatively a subdrain system is recommended to collect the excess water from landscape
irrigation For long term performance we believe that a subdrain system should be
considered adjacent to the paved landscaped areas
Underground Utility Lines
We envision that new utility lines and utility connections may be required for the
proposed project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes for support Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also
be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils The backfill
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in about
8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where
trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the
pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from
the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems
be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation

and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should
be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and

foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings extending to depths ranging from about 15 to 42 1 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using truck mounted and track mounted drill rigs equipped
with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as
the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses
a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit
is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
