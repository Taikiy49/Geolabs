INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed improvements to Kalihi Uka Fire Station in the Honolulu area on the island
of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses The
recommendations presented herein are intended for design of foundations slabs on grade
site preparation and pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shieid voicanoes Waianae and Koolau The oider voicano Waianae forms the buik of the
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by
thinly bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased approximately
2 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the island of Oahu has
subsided as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity Macdonald
and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Epoch ice Age there were many sea level changes as
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available
to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially stable
during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to the
present sea level of Oahu
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action extended
the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal sediments
During these stable periods the growth of fringing coral algal reefs formed distinct ledges of
reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea during that period
About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that rise the
valleys in the project area were submerged in the last 10 000 years or so the sea level has
adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to
accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits of
soft loose sediments along the coast in areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways

The subsurface conditions at the proposed building addition site were explored by
drilling and sampling two borings extending to a depth of about 16 5 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As mentioned above our field exploration at the subject site generally encountered
soft to medium stiff silty clays underlain by alluvial deposit consisting of stiff to very stiff clay
Based on our field exploration and laboratory tests we believe that a shallow foundation
system consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the recompacted in situ
material may be used to support the building addition

potential for shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations In order to

supported on at least 24 inches of non expansive select granular fill materials In addition

Foundations
Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the site we
believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the
proposed building addition An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 000 pounds per square
foot psf may be used for design of foundations bearing on the recompacted in situ soils
This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade If foundations are located next to utility ttenches or easements they should
be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
For footings bearing on the in situ soils the bottom of the footing excavations should
be recompacted to a firm and unyielding condition prior to placement of reinforcing steel and
concrete
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4231 00 Page 8
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations
they should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation
should either be backfilled with granular material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction or the footing bottom be extended down to the underlying competent
material Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
procedures described in ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 In the event that firm material
is not encountered after excavating to a depth of 2 feet below the bottom of footing the over
excavation may be terminated and backfilled with select granular fill underlain by a geotextile
such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent The footing may then be constructed directly on the 2 feet
of well graded select granular fill material wrapped in a geotextile
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings
bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new compacted fills to be less than 1 inch total
with differential settlements on the order of less than 0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the building may be resisted by frictional resistance developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of
0 30 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted in situ soils Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds
per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils around the footings are
well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive pressure resistance
in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the
competency of the foundation bearing materials and the required embedment depths

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the building addition As
previously indicated our laboratory tests indicate that the near surface soils encountered at
the site consisting of soft to medium stiff silty clay exhibit a moderate to high expansion
potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations Therefore we recommend that the
slabs on grade be placed over a 24 inch layer of selected granular fill capping fill and the
subgrade be properly prepared and kept moist prior to placement of cushion fill and slab
concrete
The non expansive select granular fill should be placed as soon as possible after the
subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the
Site Preparation section of this report The capping fill and the underlying subgrade soils
should be wetted and kept moist prior to final placement of concrete Where shrinkage
cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils be
thoroughly moistened to close all cracks or prepared again Saturation and subsequent
yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer consisting of free draining
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below the
slab above the capping fill to provide more uniform support of the slab To reduce the
potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture vapor barrier is recommended on top of the cushion layer Two inches
of moist fine sand may be considered above the plastic membrane to provide protection of
the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at
least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab should
be backfilled tightly against the slab with relatively impervious soils on site soils and graded
to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the
foundations

Site Preparation
We envision that minimal site grading work will be required for the proposed project
construction In general we recommend that the following guidelines be used for the minor
site grading anticipated At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits
should be thoroughly cleared Vegetation debris rubbish asphalt concrete pavements
landscape lawn grass and other unsuitable materials within the grading limits should be
removed and disposed of properly off site
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to
receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or dense
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The
excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
Over excavation resulting from the clearing and demolition should be backfilled with
compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and
the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with select granular fill material placed
in 6 to 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum 90 percent relative compaction
Utilities to be abandoned in place under the proposed structure should be backfilled by
pumping lean concrete under low pressure
Laboratory tests indicate that the near surface clayey soils exhibit high shrink swell
potential when subjected to fluctuations in moisture content To reduce the potential for
distress to the future structures resulting from swelling of the clayey soils it is recommended
that the expansive clayey soils be capped with a minimum of 24 inch of non expansive select
capping fill material below the building slab areas The non expansive select granular fill
should extend a minimum of 2 feet beyond the perimeter of the proposed building structure
Prior to placement of the capping fill the subgrade soils should be
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted by proof rolling to a firm and unyielding condition Recompaction of the subgrade
soils to over 90 percent relative compaction should be avoided to reduce the potential for
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 4231 00 Page 11
future slab distress resulting from swelling shrinkage of the expansive clayey soils Where
shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soil
be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or scarified and prepared again as
recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and
replacement with well compacted fill Contract documents should include additive and
deductive unit prices for over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for
variation in the over excavation quantities
The capping fill recommended below slabs and imported fill materials should consist
of non expansive granular material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand
The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches
in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and shouid
have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less Imported fill material should be tested by
Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project
site for the intended use
Site preparation should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii It is

evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and
scarification process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those
encountered in our field exploration

As mentioned previously multiple parallel cracks with open fractures were observed
in the driveway and existing parking areas These may be caused by insufficient thickness
of pavements and soft soils encountered in our field exploration Based on the information
provided we understand that the driveway and parking areas will be re paved with asphalt
concrete In general we anticipate that the vehicle loading will consist primarily of fire trucks
pick up trucks and passenger vehicles We have made our preliminary pavement designs

assuming the pavement subgrade soil will generally consist of the near surface clayey soils
as encountered in the borings If site grading exposes soils other than those assumed
additional tests should be performed to confirm or revise the recommended pavement
sections for actual field conditions Based on the above assumptions we recommend that
the following pavement section be used for preliminary design and budgeting purposes
Asphaltic Concrete Pavements
3 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
18 0 Inch Select Borrow 95 Percent Relative Compaction
27 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Moist Subgrade
Based on the results of our laboratory tests the near surface clayey soils exhibit a
moderate to high expansion potential when subjected to changes in the moisture content
Therefore we recommend that the subgrade be properly prepared and kept moist prior to
placement of subbase and base course material The pavement subgrade should be
scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optinum moisture and compacted by proof rolling to firm and unyielding condition
Compaction to over 90 percent should be avoided to reduce the potential for future pavement
distress resulting from swelling shrinkage of the expansive clayey soils
Subgrades should be thoroughly moistened and kept moist until covered by the
subbase course If shrinkage cracks appear on the subgrade soils prior to placement of the
subbase course we recommend that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks
or scarified and prepared again as recommended above The subbase and base course
should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted not less than 95 percent relative
compaction
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during
or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent
to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be extended a minimum of 2 inches
into the subgrade soils below the aggregate base course to reduce the potential for migration

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines and utility connections may be required for the
proposed project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes for support Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also
be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes The granular material will reduce the compaction effort
of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this
free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill
could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils The backfill
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in about
8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below
pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from
the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems
be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation
and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 4231 00 Page 14
also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms
and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce
the potential for water infiltration
In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain surface water run off away from the foundations and slabs

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings extending to a depth of about 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 2
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed on
selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The
results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Four 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on natural samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are presented on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of this
test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
representation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
