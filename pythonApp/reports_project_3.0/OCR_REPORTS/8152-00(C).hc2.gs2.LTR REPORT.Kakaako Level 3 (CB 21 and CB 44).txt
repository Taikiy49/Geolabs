REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred 
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is on the coastal plain of Southern Oahu. The coastal plain was 
built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano, which forms the eastern two-thirds of the 
Island of Oahu. The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of alluvium derived 
from erosion of the volcano, interbedded with coral reefs and associated deposits. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. Most of the coastal plains were developed during the 
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly. As the glaciers grew and 
advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below

the present stands of the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water 
became available such that the sea levels rose to above its present level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea 
level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
The project sites generally are underlain by lagoonal deposits and coralline detritus 
consisting of calcareous sediments. A surface layer of man-made fill was placed over 
these deposits for the development of the Kakaako area within the last century. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the proposed new catch basin locations 
by drilling and sampling two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 and 102, extending to 
depths of about 17 and 21.5 feet below the existing pavement surface, respectively.  
Continuous penetration with a blunt tip was performed at Boring No. 102 from a depth of 
about 21.5 feet to about 41.4 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2, and Site Plans, Plates 3.1 
and 3.2. 
The boring drilled near CB#44 (Boring No. 101) generally encountered a surface fill 
layer about 1-foot thick underlain by a 1-foot layer of volcanic ash and cinder to 8.5 feet 
deep. Below the cinder, medium hard coral formation was encountered extending to the 
maximum boring depth of 17 feet below the existing pavement surface. The surface fill 
consisted of 7 inches of asphaltic concrete and medium dense silty gravel. The volcanic 
ash was composed of stiff sandy silt.  The cinder consisted of medium dense to loose 
sand. 
The boring drilled near CB#21 (Boring No. 102) generally encountered a surface fill 
layer about 3.5 feet thick underlain by lagoonal deposits to 38.5 feet deep. The lagoonal 
deposits were underlain by a coral formation extending to the maximum boring depth of 
41.4 feet below the existing pavement surface.  The surface fill consisted of 4 inches of 
asphaltic concrete, very stiff gravelly silt, and medium dense sandy gravel. The lagoonal 
deposits were composed of soft to very soft clayey silt.  The coral formation was soft.  
We encountered groundwater at depths ranging from about 5.6 to 8.5 feet below 
the existing pavement surface. The groundwater levels encountered generally correspond 
to elevations of about -2 to +0.8 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) at the time of our field 
exploration. It should be noted that water levels may also vary with seasonal rainfall, time 
of the year, variation in tidal fluctuations, runoff, seepage, temperature, and other factors.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered asphaltic concrete and fill materials placed over 
cinder sands, lagoonal deposits and/or coral formation extending to the maximum depth 
explored of about 41.4 feet below the existing pavement surface. The groundwater levels 
encountered generally correspond to elevations of about -2 to +0.8 feet MSL at the time of 
our field exploration. 
Based on the results of our field exploration, we anticipate the proposed new catch 
basins will likely bear on the soft to very soft lagoonal deposits and loose cinder sand 
materials underlying the project sites. Therefore, we recommend providing a subgrade 
stabilization layer below the bedding layer for uniform support of the new catch basins. 
The subgrade stabilization layer may be omitted if medium dense cinder sand or coral 
formation is encountered at the bottom of the bedding layer. Detailed discussions and 
recommendations for the design of the new catch basins are presented in the following 
section. 
New Catch Basins 
Based on the information provided, we understand retaining structures, such as the 
new catch basins, are planned for the project. Therefore, the following general guidelines 
are provided and may be used for the design of the retaining structures at the project sites. 
Retaining Structure Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, we 
envision the new catch basins will likely be underlain by the soft to very soft 
lagoonal deposits and loose cinder sand underlying the project sites. An allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 2,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the 
design of the catch basins, provided that the bedding layer is underlain by a 
subgrade stabilization layer as described below. This bearing value is for 
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, 
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
As mentioned above, soft to very soft and/or loose materials are anticipated at the 
bottom of the new catch basins. Therefore, we recommend providing a subgrade 
stabilization layer consisting of 24 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, 
No. 67 gradation) wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) 
below the 6-inch bedding layer for uniform support of the new catch basins. The 
stabilization layer should also extend beyond the sides of the catch basins a 
minimum of 18 inches. The subgrade stabilization layer may be omitted if medium 
dense cinder sand or coral formation is encountered at the bottom of the bedding 
layer.

Lateral loads acting on the catch basins may be resisted by friction between the 
bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil, and by passive earth pressure acting 
against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of 
0.35 may be used for footings bearing on the stabilization layer over the soft and/or 
loose soils. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an 
equivalent fluid pressure of 150 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). 
Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 
12 inches of soil should be neglected. For the design of retaining structures, the 
normal high groundwater level may be assumed at an elevation of +3 feet MSL. 
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate total settlements of the catch basins to be less than 
1 inch. We envision a majority of the settlements would occur during the catch 
basin installation. 
A Geolabs representative should observe the catch basin excavations to confirm 
the foundation bearing conditions and the required over-excavation depths. 
Static Lateral Earth Pressures 
Retaining structures, such as the new catch basins, should be designed to resist 
the lateral earth pressures due to the adjacent soils and surcharge effects. An 
at-rest lateral earth pressure of 60 pcf may be used for above groundwater 
condition. For below groundwater condition, an at-rest lateral earth pressure, 
including hydrostatic pressure, of 90 pcf may be used. 
The value provided above assumes that select granular materials will be used to 
backfill around the catch basin above the groundwater level. It is assumed that 
the backfill around the catch basin will be compacted to between 90 and 
95 percent relative compaction. Over-compaction of the backfill should be 
avoided.  Backfill below the groundwater level should consist of No. 3B Fine 
gravel. 
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, line loads, and point loads within a 
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be considered in the 
design. For walls that are restrained, such as the catch basins, a rectangular 
distribution equal to 50 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the 
entire height of the catch basin wall may be used for the design. Additional 
analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of point 
loads and line loads.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the proposed new catch basin locations were 
explored by drilling and sampling two exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 
and 102, extending to depths of about 17 and 21.5 feet below the existing pavement 
surface, respectively. Continuous penetration with a blunt tip was performed at Boring 
No. 102 from a depth of about 21.5 feet to about 41.4 feet below the existing pavement 
surface.  The approximate boring locations are shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2, 
and Site Plans, Plates 3.1 and 3.2. The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with 8-inch OD hollow-stem augers. 
Our field engineer/geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by 
visual and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, 
Standard Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling 
operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further 
reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general 
accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for 
Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with 
ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock 
formations were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as 
shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-2.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.
