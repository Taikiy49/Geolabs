SUMMARY OF RECOMMENDATIONS
The proposed structure should be supported upon
piles extending to the upper or lower coral layers
A 12 inch square concrete pile bearing in the upper
coral will provide an estimated allowable capacity
of 50 tons A 16 inch octagonal concrete pile
bearing within the lower coral will provide an
estimated allowable pile capacity of 100 tons
INTRODUCTION
This report summarizes the findings of our subsurface explorations
at the subject site and presents recommendations for the design and
construction of the foundations to support the proposed structure
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed development will consist of a 20 story reinforced con
crete hotel tower upon a multi story parking structure The

highrise structure will be approximately 48 feet by 142 feet in
plan dimensions The parking structure of four decks will essen
tially occupy the remaining property No basement below the
existing ground surface is anticipated
SITE CONDITIONS
Surface
The L shaped property consist of four lots abutting Olohana Street
Each lot is 50 feet in width by 100 feet in depth At the makai
end of the property a second lot abutting Namahana Street completes
the L shape with a width of 50 feet and a depth of 90 feet The
subject property is located approximately 50 feet makai of the
Ala Wai Boulevard in the Waikiki section of Honolulu
According to the topographic survey prepared by VIN Pacific the
ground surface at the site is essentially level at approximately
Elevation 5 feet The ground surface elevations at the boring
locations were estimated from the above topographic survey The
site is presently occupied by a series of one to three story
apartments
Geology
The proposed building site is blanketed by a layer of fill placed
as part of the overall development of the area between Ala Wai
Page 3

August 29 1974
Canal and Waikiki Beach The fill was placed upon alluvium water
deposited soils consisting of silty sand with coral fragments
The alluvium was deposited in a lagoonal or shallow offshore plat
form environment which existed behind the outer barrier reefs
The lagoonal environment was subjected to periodic influxes of
elastic non marine alluvial debris clays and silts which appa
rently smothered the reef development
The lagoonal deposits are underlain by an interbedded sequence of
coral layers and coral sands with some silt and clay seams These
materials were deposited during periods of changing sea levels
Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored utilizing five
test borings drilled at the locations shown on the Boring Location
Plan Plate 1 A detailed description of the field exploration
methods and laboratory testing procedures are including in the
appendix along with the descriptions of the soils encountered and
the laboratory test results
The subsurface explorations disclosed that the site is blanketed
with approximately five feet of fill varying from loose to medium
dense and containing some areas of soft organic clay
Beneath the fill lagoonal deposits consisting of loose silty sand
with coral fragments were encountered This loose material extends

to depths varying from 34 feet at Boring 5 to 42 feet at Boring 1
Beneath the lagoonal deposits a medium dense upper coral layer
was encountered with thicknesses varying from 10 feet at Boring 1
to 19 feet at Boring 5 Generally the bottom of the upper coral
layer was at approximately Elevation 51 except in the vicinity of
Boring 4 where the coral layer terminated at approximately Eleva
tion 45
A 7 to 9 foot thick layer of very stiff silt with some clay was
encountered beneath the upper coral This layer extends to depths
of 55 to 66 feet where the lower coral layer was encountered
The lower coral consist of interbedded medium dense to dense layers
of coral with some layers of cemented coral sand and ledge coral
The loose sand condition encountered in Boring 2 at a depth of
approximately 80 feet is attibutable to a quick condition
resulting from withdrawal of the sampling tools within submerged
soils
The groundwater level is influenced by tidal fluctuations and
was generally encountered 4 feet below the existing ground surface
at approximately Elevation 1

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The upper fill and loose lagoonal deposits cannot provide adequate
support for major foundations It is necessary to bypass this
upper layer and support the structure upon piles penetrating into
the upper or lower corals encountered from 35 to 60 feet below the
existing ground surface
High Capacity Piles
It is estimated that a 16 diameter octagonal concrete pile extend
ing to a depth of 60 to 70 feet below the existing ground surface
will develop an allowable bearing capacity of 100 tons per pile
This pile capacity would be developed from end bearing within the
lower coral formation
It is anticipated that the piles will require pre drilling in order
to penetrate the upper coral deposits The actual driving charac
teristics should be evaluated by a series of pile driving resistance
tests It is recommended that a minimum of six tests be performed
One pile from this group would be selected for a load test to
verify the 100 ton pile capacity
For estimating purposes it is anticipated that the piles will be
driven a minimum depth of 60 feet and will have an average length
Page 6
W O 541 00
August 29 1974
of 65 feet The piles should be driven to a final driving resis
tance of 18 blows per inch with a hammer delivering a rated energy
of 15 000 foot pounds Driving resistance criteria will be
established upon request for alternative pile hammers
Since the pile capacity will be developed almost entirely from end
bearing within the lower coral no reduction is necessary for group
action
Low Capacity Piles
The upper coral layer does not have sufficient thickness to provide
adequate support for high capacity piles It can provide adequate
support for 50 ton piles It is estimated that a 12 inch square
concrete pile extending five feet into the upper coral layer will
develop an allowable bearing capacity of 50 tons per pile This
pile should be driven to a final driving resistance of approximately
4 blows per inch with a hammer delivering a rated energy of 15 000
foot pounds
Due to the presence of the very stiff silt layer at depths of 45 to
60 feet below the existing ground surface the pile driving opera
tions for the low capacity piles should be closely monitored to
assure that the piles do not completely penetrate the upper coral
layer The penetration into this layer for a 50 ton capacity pile
should not exceed five feet

The allowable vertical pile capacity of 50 tons and 100 tons can
be increased by 1 3 for wind and seismic loadings
Uplift Resistance
Based upon the above driving criteria and a five foot penetration
into the upper coral layer the low capacity piles can be designed
for an allowable uplift resistance of five kips per pile for both
single and group piles This value is relatively low due to the
limited penetration into competent materials and the low strength
of the upper lagoonal deposits
The high capacity piles penetrating ten feet into the lower coral
layer can provide an allowable uplift resistance of 10 kips per
pile for both single and group piles
Lateral Capacity
The loose lagoonal materials also limits the lateral pile capacity
It is recommended that a value of one kip per pile be utilized for
long term lateral loadings This loading will produce a maximum
moment in the 16 pile estimated at 14 inch kips at a depth of
approximately 12 feet
It is estimated that a short term lateral capacity of 10 kips per
pile can be developed with less than inch deflection at the top
of the piles Short term loadings are considered as loads less
Page 8

than two hours in duration The maximum moment within the pile
would increase to 140 inch kips with a 10 kip lateral loading
Groundwater
Due to the high groundwater levels basements or elevator shafts
extending below approximately Elevation 2 should be designed to
resist hydrostatic uplift pressures
Grading
Fill placed as part of the project should be compacted to at
least 90 of the maximum dry density established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 72T Full time inspection should be performed to
verify the degree of compaction when the fill will be utilized for
structural support The on site soils are acceptable as backfill
providing the organic and other deleterious materials are removed
It is anticipated that the excavations into the on site materials
can be made essentially vertical horizontal to 1 vertical for
heights not exceeding 4 feet These soils will be stable on a
temporary basis but should not be utilized for long term conditions
Construction Inspection
It is recommended that the pile driving operations be monitored on
a full time basis by a qualified technician under the direction of

a soils engineer to verify that the piles have been driven in
accordance with the recommendations The pile capacities given
in this report are contingent upon such inspections
The pile load test to verify the capacity of the 100 ton pile should
be performed in accordance with the City and County s requirements
or ASTM Test Designation D 1143 69 The test setup and loading of
the pile should be made the responsibility of the contractor The
readings and test reports should be prepared by a registered engineer

APPENDIX
FIELD EXPLORATION
Subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling five
test borings utilizing truck meunted equipment The depth of the
borings varied from 50 to 100 feet below the existing ground sur
face The location of the borings are shown on the appendix
Boring Location Plan Plate l
The soils were classified by visual and textural examinations in
the field by our geologist who continuously supervised the drilling
operations The classification were verified by visual inspection
and testing in the laboratory All soils were classified in accord
ance with the Unified Soils Classification Systems A graphic
representation of the soils encountered is presented on the boring
logs Plates A 1 through A 5
Soil samples were obtained by driving either a 2 4 inch I D
diameter or a standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer
free falling a distance of 30 inches The blow counts to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the Log of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
