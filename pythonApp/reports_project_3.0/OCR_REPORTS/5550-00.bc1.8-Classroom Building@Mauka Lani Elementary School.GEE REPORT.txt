Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age
and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct
while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau
lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad
plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
Puu Makakilo is a cinder and spatter cone that was built by one or more eruptive
events which produced large quantities of pyroclastic material such as cinder spatter
and ash In some areas welding and or hypothermal action has fused or cemented the
pyroclastics into weak and strong rock e g tuff
In addition to the pyroclastics the eruptions produced lava flows of very dense
basalt which are interbedded with the pyroclastic materials Structurally Puu Makakilo
is a heterogeneous mixture of interbedded cinder tuff and dense basalt The units are
discontinuous and difficult to differentiate on an area basis in the field
In situ chemical weathering of the pyroclastics and basalts has produced a
surface layer of residual soil which mantles most of the project site This residual soil
grades rapidly to the underlying volcanic materials

Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
five borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 extending to depths of about 25 to
26 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the drilled
borings and the bulk sample collected are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

silty and clayey soils with some sand to a depth of about 3 to 7 5 feet below existing
ground surface Below the fill soft to medium hard basalt formation was encountered
extending to the maximum depths explored

exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to
fluctuation due to rainfall events seasonal precipitation and other factors occurring
off site

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered we believe that shallow
foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on compacted
structural fill may be used to support the proposed building
Based on our laboratory tests the in situ clayey soils exhibit low to moderate
shrink swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents To
reduce the potential for distress to the future building resulting from the swelling of the
near surface soils we recommend that the on site clayey soils be capped with a
minimum of 12 inches of non expansive structural fill capping fill material below slab
and foundation areas In general the subgrade soils should be compacted to provide a
relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement of fill material The
slab subgrade should be kept moist and free of shrinkage cracks prior to placement of
capping fill cushion fill and or slab concrete Our geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertaining to the design of foundations retaining walls site
preparation and pavements are further discussed in the following sections of this
report
3 1
Building Foundations
Based on the generally stiff subsoil conditions encountered we believe that
shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the proposed
buildings An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf
may be used to design shallow foundations bearing over the recompacted in situ soils
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing
over excavations these should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials The resulting over excavations should be backfilled with on site material to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction

Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soils and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the structural fill
material Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This
assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted The passive pressure should
be reduced for foundations located on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs
the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades Footings located adjacent to retaining walls should be embedded
deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations or the retaining walls
should be designed to resist the additional structural loads
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded
below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid
surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce
the potential for foundation settlement
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings
bearing on the compacted structural fill material over the in situ stiff clayey soils as
recommended herein to be less than 1 inch total with differential settlements on the order
of 0 5 inches
We recommend that a Geolabs representative observe footing excavations prior to
placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions
and the required embedment depths

Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by clayey
soils with low to moderate expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations

To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting
from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content
we recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with at least
12 inches of non expansive structural fill capping fill material below the slab cushion
The capping fill should extend laterally beyond the building footprint a minimum of 2 feet
or to the outside edge of the sidewalk walkway slab around the building if applicable
The non expansive structural capping fill should be placed as soon as possible
after the subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented
in the Site Preparation section of this report The prepared capping fill and the underlying
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to the final placement of concrete
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
thoroughly moistening the soils to close the cracks Saturation and subsequent yielding of
the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend placing
a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation below the slabs to serve as a capillary break and to provide more
uniform support of the slabs To reduce moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to
floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the No 3B Fine
gravel Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since
they can better mask minor slab cracking
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project Since the
walkways may tolerate some movement we recommend supporting concrete walkways
on 12 inches of non expansive structural fill The structural fill should be compacted to at
least 90 percent relative compaction Control joints should be provided at intervals equal to
the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
The thickened edges of slab adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at
least 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab

Retaining Walls
Retaining walls will be constructed as part of the site improvements The
following general guidelines may be used for preliminary design of retaining structures
Wall Foundations
In general we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the Building Foundations
section of this report It is our opinion that foundations for the building retaining
walls should also bear on recompacted in situ soils as recommended above Wall
footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches Wall foundations on relatively
flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade For sloping ground conditions the footing should extend
deeper to obtain a minimum 8 foot setback distance measured horizontally from the
outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope Wall footings oriented parallel to
the direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings

significant water infiltration behind the walls

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris deleterious material and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed off site Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill or
future development should be over excavated to expose firm material The resulting
excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft
and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site Contract documents should
include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill
placement to account for variations in the over excavation quantities
After clearing and grubbing the areas within the future building and pavement
limits should be over excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 24 inch thick
layer of non expansive structural fill material The over excavated subgrades should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above
the optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as
a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after the subgrade compaction we
recommend that the subgrade soil be prepared again as recommended above
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over excavating the soft areas and replacing these
areas with well compacted fill The need for over excavating due to soft subgrade soil
conditions should be evaluated by a Geolabs representative in the field
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the pumping
subgrade may be stabilized by cement treatment As a guide one sack of cement may be
used for approximately 25 square feet of subgrade area Alternatively the subgrade may

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the proposed project In
general for utility line support we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of
6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the
compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is
critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the
pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of select fill material The
backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in
maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than
90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence
Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement finish grades should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction

The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
five borings to depths of about 25 and 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the drilled borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths In addition relatively undisturbed soil samples were
obtained in general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Nine Ring Swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the near surface soils Two relatively undisturbed samples were
air dried and saturated with water Seven soil samples were remolded close to optimum
moisture condition air dried and saturated with water The results of the tests are
summarized on Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One Direct Shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample to
evaluate peak and residual shear strength characteristics The test results and shear
stress versus normal stress plots are presented on Plate B 3
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was
performed on a bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils Results of the test are presented on Plate B 4
