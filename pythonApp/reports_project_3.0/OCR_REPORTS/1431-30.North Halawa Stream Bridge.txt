INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of a geotechnical engineering
investigation performed for the proposed North Halawa Stream Bridge at
Halawa Valley Light Industrial Park Halawa Valley Oahu Hawaii
The general location of the project and its vicinity are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
PROPOSED CONSTRUCT ION
The proposed bridge is a concrete structure consisting of
prestressed girders supported on abutments located on both banks
of the North Halawa Stream Because of the large span of the bridge
intermediate supports for these girders are required as they turn across
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1431 30 Page 2
the stream These additional supports are also in the form of
prestressed concrete girders which are constructed across the stream and
are supported by columns on both sides of the stream see Site Plan
Plate 2 Loading along the abutments is anticipated to be about 16 4
kips per lineal foot while concentrated loads on the columns are
estimated to vary from 140 to 650 kips
At a short distance 12 to 15 feet behind the abutment located on
each side of the stream a retaining wall is proposed One of the
purposes of the walls is to retain additional fill material which is
required to raise the site to the designed elevation for the approach to
the bridge Up to about 12 feet of fill may be required Vertical
loading on the wall is anticipated to be about 3 8 kips per lineal foot
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our investigation was to evaluate the subsurface
soil conditions under the proposed locations for the bridge abutments and
the columns to provide pertinent soil data for the foundation design of
the proposed bridge structure
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1431 30 Page 3
The scope of investigation which was performed in general
accordance with our up dated proposal dated included
drilling and sampling of eight 8 borings laboratory testing of soil
samples obtained engineering evaluation of field and laboratory data
and report preparation In addition two piezometers were installed at
the site to provide a continuous monitoring of groundwater conditions at
the project site
Detailed description of our field exploration laboratory testing
and log of borings are presented in the appendices The approximate
locations of borings and piezometers are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
GENERAL SITE CONDITIONS
Site Location
The project site is located near the intersection between the
Halawa Dairy Road and the North Halawa Stream in Halawa Valley on the
island of Oahu Hawaii
The North Halawa Stream is presently crossed by an existing bridge
constructed in the 1930 s The stream is contained in an open
rectangular concrete channel which runs in a northeast southwest
direction The average height of the channel wall within the project
area is about 16 feet when measured from the existing ground surface
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1431 30 Page 4
The area along both banks of the stream is heavily vegetated with
trees and tall grasses Two power poles and a few electric poles with
anchors installed on both sides of the stream were noted
Much of the project area is underlain by utilities which include
sewer lines underground HECO cables telephone cables and a Navy fuel
line An approximate layout based on information provided by various
utility toning agencies is also shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 It can
be seen fron Plate 2 that a high concentration of underground utility is
located under the proposed west abutment of the bridge
Topography
The project site generally slopes toward the stream with slopes
varying from 6 on the western side to 2 on the eastern side of the
stream with side slopes varying anywhere from 4 to 40
The existing ground elevations vary from about 63 feet to 80 feet
The bottom of the channel is at an elevation of about 53 feet to 55 feet
SUBSURFACE CONDITION
Regional Geology
The site is located in the lower portion of Halawa Valley which has
been brought to its present form by a complex series of geologic events
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1431 30 Page 5
The original Halawa Valley was a youthful valley cut into the Koolau Dome
during a lower stand of the sea Several subsequent stages of
alluviation have filled the valley floor with fluvial deposits
At an intermediate stage between two series of alluvial deposition
pyroclastic material such as cinder was laid across the mouth of the
valley by members of the Honolulu Volcanic Series These deposits of
volcanic material most likely originating from Aliamanu and Salt Lake
Craters caused a diversion of north Halawa Stream to the west
Stream erosion and diversion of the courses of north and south
Halawa Streams have shaped the area to its present topography

The subsoil conditions at the project site were explored by
drilling and sampling eight 8 borings to depths varying between 45 and
81 5 feet below the existing ground surface Because of the many
existing underground utilities and anchors plus the overhead power lines
and anchor cables some of the originally planned borings had to be
relocated The approximate final locations of these borings are shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2

Subsoil Conditions
The subsoil conditions at the site consist primarily of alluvial
deposits composed of sandy to silty soil with variable amount of gravels
and boulders Based on the information obtained from an old airphoto
contour map prepared in 1969 we believe that the existing ground
profiles behind the east and west bank are the results of previous
grading operations during the construction of the existing channel
The subsoil conditions along the two proposed abutments as
disclosed from our field exploration are summarized below
East Abutment Borings 1 to 3 Boring 7

The subsoil conditions at the proposed east abutment consist of
about 15 to 20 feet of stiff to very stiff gravelly to boulderly
fill in a sandy to clayey silt matrix Below the fill layer to the
bottom of all borings were layers of silty to gravelly alluvial
soil with varying gradation and consistencies
In general between the depths of 30 to 45 feet the soil appears
to consist mostly of clayey silt which exhibits a less stiff
consistency and a relatively higher compressibility compared to the
very stiff gravelly soils below this layer
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1431 30 Page 7
However in Boring No 1 boulders were encountered down to depth
of 45 feet from the ground surface This is probably due to the
deposition effect caused by the intersection of two streams near
that particular location
West Abutment Borings 4 to 6
SS SM A
For the top 15 to 20 feet of soil at the proposed west abutment
considerably less amount of gravels and boulders were encountered
compared to that of the east abutment This top layer consists
mainly of stiff to very stiff sandy silt with some gravels
Soil conditions beneath the upper soil layer above the stream
bottom are similar to those below the east abutment with the
exception that the clay content is generally higher and hence the
soils are more compressible

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
In general our soil exploration disclosed that the subsoil
conditions at the site are suitable for the construction of the Halawa
Stream Bridge
Both spread footings and pile foundation were considered for this
project However the result of the spread footing analysis indicated
that in order to avoid causing distress to the existing channel wall the
spread footing foundations which were located immediately behind the
wall had to be placed at an elevation below the bottom of the channel
This would bring the footing closer to the compressible layer and hence
would result in settlements of the footings under the relatively heavy
loading Installation of a deep spread footing would also require large
areal excavation and a dewatering operation as well as removal and
reconstruction of the existing channel walls
The accumulated disadvantages of using spread footing foundation
as mentioned above leads to our belief that pile foundation appears to
oe more appropriate for the project Details about the proposed pile
foundations are presented in the following sections
For the proposed retaining walls behind both abutments we believe
that shallow spread footing foundations bearing on the recompacted
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1431 30 Page 9
on site material can be used For backfill behind the wall we recommend
the use of selected granular materials
Several underground utilities were noted during our field
exploration The locations of underground utility lines should be
verified If a utility line interferes with the structure it may be
necessary to move the line or adjust structural details to accommodate
the utility
Bridge Abutment and Supporting Columns
According to the present proposed abutment alignment very Limited
spacing on the order of 5 feet will be available between the
foundation structure and the existing channel wall In order to prevent
distress to the existing wall and to prevent the need for reconstructing
the channel wall with a higher design lateral load any additional
stresses due to the construction of the foundation system for the bridge
should be avoided This implies that a spread footing foundation should
be placed beneath the bottom of the existing channel
For a pile foundation on the other hand predrilling down to the bottom
of the channel wall would be required to minimize lateral stresses during
the pile driving
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1431 30 Page 10
Spread Footing Foundation
i me an em Se ss es
Because of the above mentioned requirement the bottom of the
spread footing foundation for the bridge would probably be located
at about 18 to 20 feet below the existing ground surface i e at
an elevation of 1 to 3 feet below the water table This would
bring the footing considerably closer to the underlying
compressible layer
Our laboratory consolidation tests indicated that this 10 to
15 foot thick layer of relatively compressible soil located at a
depth varying from 30 to 60 feet below the ground surface
generally had high moisture contents and was in most cases
normally consolidated Based on the laboratory test results and
with the use of an allowable bearing pressure of 2 500 p s f the
estimated total settlement for the abutment is on the order of 2
inches For the concentrated load area the estimated settlement
varies from a high value of inches under the 650 kips load to
a low value of inches under the 140 kips load Additional
settlement due to site filling is estimated to be on the order of 1
inch along the west abutment and 1 2 inch along the east abutment
Construction of the relatively deep footing foundation would
require a large area behind the footing to be excavated The
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1431 30 Page 11
excavation would probably be hampered by the various existing
utilities and proximity to the western property line In addition
dewatering may be required since most of the bottoms of the
footings would be below the water table The dewatering process
may also cause additional site settlement due to the increase in
vertical pressure caused by the change from bouyant soil weight to
wet soil weight
In view of the anticipated settlements and construction
difficulties we believe that pile foundation would be more
appropriate for the project
Friction Pile Foundation
A A a i a se
The use of pile foundation is recommended for the present project
The piles would develop their load capacity primarily from
frictional resistance or adhesion between the piles and the stiff
alluvial soil and some end bearing from the stiff alluvial soil
Because of gravelly and boulderly fill and short distance to the
existing channel wall predrilling of 18 to 20 feet for pile
foundation is recommended
Our analysis indicate that with the use of a 16 5 inch octagonal
prestressed concrete pile driven 75 feet into the existing ground
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1431 30 Page 12
an allowable design pile capacity of 40 tons may be used The
recommended capacity assume that predrilling of 18 to 20 feet
through the upper soil layer is required Further predrilling
requirement and its effect on the allowable pile capacity should be
carefully studied during the test pile program
Pile settlements and lateral movements on the order of less than
one half of an inch are anticipated
Lateral Pile Capacity
A A NR Ne he CY AN eae em mm Sm
The lateral load capacity of the pile was analyzed using the p y
curve method with our in house computer facilities This program
utilized the finite difference method of analysis where non linear
stress strain relationship of soil can be used Based on the soil
parameters developed and the laboratory stress strain curves
representative p y curves were developed for each major soil
category for input into the program
Since the lateral soil modulus varies with the deflection
experienced the computerized analysis first assumes a modulus
value then calculates the pile deflection based on the assumed
modulus The calculated deflection is then used to determine the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1431 30 Page 13
modulus from the p y curves The calculated modulus and the
assumed modulus are then compared The procedure is repeated until
the new assumed and calculated moduli are essentially the same
The piles driven on the east side of the stream will be fully
embedded while on the western side the piles will be partially
embedded
Lateral load acting at the top of each pile is anticipated to be on
the order of about 10 kips or less Based on this loading
condition we believe that the lateral deflection would be on the
order of about inches and 3 4 inch or less for the piles
located on the western side and eastern side of the stream
respectively
For further reference curves depicting the relationship between
lateral load and lateral deflection of the pile top are included in
Plate 3
Pile Driving
me mc a a le 8m
The piles should be driven with a hammer delivering a minimum of
about 24 000 foot pounds of energy The driving process should be
continuous without interruption for each pile A pile may be
rejected or its design capacity modified when the driving process
is interrupted for more than 4 hours
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1431 30 Page 14
Pile driving may be terminated when the blow counts are steadily
increasing with depth and reach 4 blows per inch for each of the
last 6 inches or aggregated blow counts of 48 blows for the last
foot
Test Pile Program
om me 88 et i es ste
A test pile program should be undertaken to fulfill the following
main objectives
1 To examine the drivability of piles with the use of one or a
few selected hammers
2 to determine if a driving shoe is required when the pile
encounters cobbles and boulders of various sizes during
driving
3 to determine if further predrilling underneath the channel
bottom is required
4 to help evaluate the driving criteria and
5 to more accurately determine the pile order lengths
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1431 30 Page 15
To achieve these objectives we recommend that eight to ten piles
be test driven at the site These test piles can be incorporated
into actual foundation later
As a part of the test pile program one pile load test is
recommended The test pile should be load tested to 80 tons in
accordance with the ASTM D 1143 test procedure
The test pile should be driven with the same hammer which is to be
used for production pile driving The test pile program should be
conducted under the close and continuous observation of a soils
engineer who will determine when driving may be stopped and to
provide guidance should additional measures be required due to
unforeseen driving conditions He should evaluate the final load
carrying pile capacity and production pile embedment lengths based
on the results of the test pile driving and pile load test
Retaining Wall
Retaining walls with heights on the order of 10 feet are planned
behind the bridge abutments Vertical loading on the wall foundation is
anticipated to be about 3 8 kips per lineal foot
In light of the relatively light loading we believe that shallow
spread footing bearing on the stiff on site soil can be used as
foundation for the proposed retaining wall

Lateral loads acting on the wall footing may be resisted by passive

around the foundation is well compacted The frictional resistance

The moisture content unit weight and Atterberg Limit
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the
classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of the
tests are presented on the Log of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths The Atterberg Limits are also summarized on Plates B 1 through
B 5
Eleven 11 sieve analysis tests were performed on samples of the
underlying soil to evaluate its grain size distribution The test
results are summarized on Plates B 1 through B 7
One 1 direct shear test was performed on a saturated sample of
the typical soil type in the area to evaluate the shear strength
parameters of the soil The test result is presented on Plate B 8
Nine 9 unconsolidated undrained triaxial tests and one 1
consolidated undrained triaxial test with pore water pressure measurement
were performed on undisturbed ring and shelby tube samples to evaluate
its undrained strength The test results are presented on Plates B 9
through B 18
Nineteen 19 consolidation tests were performed on the natural
soils to evaluate its settlement characteristics The curves are
presented on Plates B 19 through B 37
