INTRODUCTION
In accordance with our fee proposal of and the Underground
Injection Control UIC permit application No UH 1837 we have completed our visual
observations made during construction of 18 of the 71 new drywells planned for the
Villages of La opua development In addition we have also performed four injection
tests for the new drywells This engineering report has been prepared in support of
Underground Injection Control permit application No UH 1837 The approximate
location of the project site and vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes findings resulting from our field observations and testing of a
portion of the new drywells at the project site In addition the as built depths of the 71
project drywells are summarized and provided with this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Villages of La i opua project site is located in Kealakehe North Kona
Hawaii The proposed development will consist of residential units schools a
community center golf course and archaeological and botanical preservation areas
The Phase 2A of the Villages of La opua development includes the initial increment
of the second phase backbone infrastructure which generally includes the construction
of approximately 3 miles of paved roadways two water supply reservoirs and
associated underground utilities and drainage system to service the Villages of
La opua Planned Community
As stated in the Keahole to Kailua Development Plan runoff generated within
each area will be disposed of within each area by means of infiltration into the

Therefore the County of Hawaii requires that new developments create no net increase
in discharge of surface or storm water runoff The general practice on the island of
Hawaii is to utilize drywells as a method of disposal of surface runoff
As part of the Phase 2A construction a total of 71 new drywells have been
constructed for disposal of storm water runoff under UIC permit application
No UH 1837 Under the conditions set forth in the approval to construct the new

PHYSICAL CHARACTERISTICS
The site is located in Kealakehe District of North Kona on the island of Hawaii
The climate of the area is mild with an average annual temperature of about 74 F and
annual rainfall ranging from about 20 to 40 inches per year Rainfall at the site typically
occurs as daily light afternoon showers except during periods of stormy weather when
heavier rainfall may be expected
The site is situated on the lower western flank of Mount Hualalai The
topography is relatively gentle to moderate sloping down toward the ocean westward
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2934 20 Page 6
from the central portion of the island Elevations across the site range from about 280
to 580 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
Formerly the site had been undeveloped rocky land covered with brush and
scattered small trees At the time of the drywell injection testing the roadways had
been graded and compacted though pavements had not been placed The four
drywells which were injection tested were completed and are in a functional state
Regional Geology
The island of Hawaii is composed of five basalt shield volcanoes built by the
extrusion of lavas during the Pleistocene and the Holocene Epochs The site is located
on the western flank of Mount Hualalai a now dormant volcano which last erupted
between 1801 to 1803 This mountain is thought to be of an intermediate age between
Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa its larger neighboring mountains Hualalai appears to
have entered the late stage of the eruptive cycle Rocks of Hualalai Volcano cover a
portion of the western side of the island of Hawaii Most of its lavas are alkalic olivine
basalt and are physically composed of both pahoehoe and a a flow types
Earthquake Conditions
The island of Hawaii is designated in the 1994 Uniform Building Code as
Seismic Zone 3 Seismic Zone 3 is considered to be an active zone of earthquake
occurrence Most of the earthquakes that occur on the island of Hawaii are related to
subsurface magma movement and are termed volcanic earthquakes
The drywells were constructed in a relatively competent layered rock formation
with some rock fill The drywells generally consist of simple drilled pits which are
unlined except for portions of the drywell constructed in loose clinker material or fill and
where voids are present These potentially less stable zones were lined with perforated
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2934 20 Page 7
precast concrete rings to maintain the integrity of the localized zone Due to the below
grade construction of the drywells and placement of precast concrete rings in the
potentially less stable zones of the excavation seismic conditions are not considered
significant
Flood and Tsunami
Based on the grading plans grading to provide positive drainage of the site has
generally been provided Roadway culverts have been constructed to discharge
emergency overflow runoff to undeveloped land downslope of the roadways These
drainage culverts were designed to discharge excess runoff below the roadway in the
event that the drywells cannot dispose of all the storm water runoff being received In
addition the site is located inland of and at a higher elevation than the expected
tsunami inundation zone for this portion of the island of Hawaii Therefore it is
anticipated that tsunami flooding should not affect the operation of the drywells
Land Use
The site is situated on land which is both above and below the established
Underground Injection Control Line for this portion of the island of Hawaii The site is
in a suburban area zoned for residential development The site is being developed as
a planned residential community and is in conformance with local land use planning
and zoning regulations Construction and use of drywells at the site is considered to
be consistent with this use
SYSTEM OPERATION
It is proposed to use the new drywells for disposal of surface and stormwater
runoff flowing from paved roadways and graded areas of the drainage basin Use of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2934 20 Page 8
the drywells will be intermittent and variable and will be dependent upon weather
conditions and stormwater runoff quantities
The influent will not be treated prior to disposal and the volume and rate of
injection will vary according to the intensity of rainfall Based on a 10 year storm event
Q the design maximum capacity is 6 cubic feet per second per drywell For the
drywell system consisting of 71 drywells the maximum design capacity is 426 cubic
feet per second Injection will be under gravity pressure i e the head pressure that
builds up within the depth of the drywell Since the new drywells are intended for
intermittent disposal of storm water runoff only there is no additional standby system
nor are monitoring wells planned
The complete drywell system consists of 71 drywells along the shoulders of the
paved roadways and at the two reservoir sites Of the total 71 drywells for this phase
of the project 69 drywells are located down gradient of the Underground Injection
Control Line The remaining two 70 71 are located above the
Underground Injection Control Line

The site is centrally located at Latitude North and Longitude 156 00
08 West The ground surface elevations at the site range from approximately 280
to 580 feet MSL Subsurface conditions encountered were basalt rock formation
generally consisting of interbedded clinker and massive to fractured pahoehoe basalt
rock Groundwater was not observed in the drywells selected for observation or testing
during our field work

The regional characteristic of groundwater occurrence in the area is fresh to
brackish water in a basal volcanic aquifer of relatively high permeability Published
data indicates that the groundwater table slopes towards the ocean at a gradient of
about 1 foot per mile Regional discharge is generally along the coastline
The lithologies of the 18 logged drywell excavations are presented in Appendix
A of this report All depths reported in the logs were measured from the existing
ground surface to the bottom of the drywell after construction
WELL CONSTRUCTION
Generally the drywells encountered interbedded layers of hard pahoehoe and
a a basalt of the Hualalai Volcanic Series In general the drywells were constructed
by drilling an approximately 5 foot diameter core and excavating the rock core or
fractured rock materials
Based on visual observation of the 18 selected drywells the entire depth of the
drywells were unlined except for the placement of a precast perforated concrete ring
at the top of the drywell immediately below the concrete drywell cover Crushed rock
and cobbles were used as backfill behind the concrete rings and fill areas of the
sidewall The completed drywells range in depth from about 18 0 feet to 25 8 feet
below the finished ground surface
The as built constructed characteristics of the total 71 project drywells are listed
in Appendix C This information was provided by Belt Collins Hawaii
INJECTION TESTING
The subsurface permeability at the site was tested by the injection of clear fresh
water from a water tank truck with a capacity of approximately 4 000 gallons Injection
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2934 20 Page 10
rates were measured by use of an in line paddle wheel flow sensor During testing
clean water was injected at rates of about 150 to 230 gallons per minute for a period
of about 20 minutes depending on the flow rate for each test During the injection of
water the variations of the water level in the drywells were recorded with respect to
time and rate of injection Following the conclusion of the test the time lapse for the
drywell to completely drain the water was also recorded Groundwater was not
observed in any of the drywells prior to the beginning of the injection testing
These tests indicate that at an average injection rate of about 190 gallons per
minute the basalt formation has an approximate coefficient of permeability k with a
range on the order of approximately 400 to 1 000 feet per day The high variability of
the calculated subsurface permeabilities may be due in part by the method of drywell
construction used and the nature of the subsurface materials The core drilling of the
drywell bores provided a smooth equi dimensional excavation however the coring
process appeared to have generated a siurry of fine silty sand cutting material which
was observed to be accumulated in the natural rock fractures of the formation Over
time these accumulations may be washed away by repeated use and the overall
drywell permeability may increase Results of the injection tests are presented in
Appendix B
