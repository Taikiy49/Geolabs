In accordance with the project design requirements a static pile load test was
conducted on a dedicated augered cast in place ACIP pile on May 15 and 16 2006 for
the Nordstrom Ala Moana project In general the Nordstrom Building site is bounded by
Kapiolani Boulevard to the north Keeaumoku Street to the west Kona Street to the south
and the former Kapiolani Bakery building demolished to the east This letter report
presents the results of our field observations and the test data obtained from the static pile
load test The pile installation subcontractor responsible for installing the augered
cast in place pile for the load test was Morris Shea Bridge Company Inc
The pile load test was conducted on an 18 inch diameter augered cast in place pile
with a length of approximately 65 feet relative to the existing ground surface To better
understand the load transfer mechanism at the pile soil interface the load test pile was
instrumented with six vibrating wire embedment strain gages Model No VCE 4200
manufactured by Geokon The embedment strain gages were installed in the load test pile
at the following depths below the existing ground surface

The load test pile was located at the northern portion of the Nordstrom Building site
The test pile was installed on May 8 2006 using a drill rig equipped with automatic
monitoring equipment to provide the grout flow rate batch number pressure etc on a
near continuous basis
We understand that the ground elevation during pile installation was at about
4 8 feet Mean Sea Level MSL The load test pile was drilled to a tip elevation of about
60 2 feet MSL using a continuous flight auger and subsequently grouted under low
pressure from the bottom and up After sound quality grout based on visual observation
appeared at the top of the pile a reinforcing cage was inserted into the grouted hole In
addition the six embedment strain gages were installed onto a single threaded bar and
inserted into the grouted hole at about the center of the pile The depths at which the
embedment strain gages were installed are presented in the previous table
The piling subcontractor Morris Shea Bridge Company Inc provided the load
testing equipment including a reaction beam reference beams a hydraulic jack a
hydraulic pump and other accessories Details of the calibration for the strain gages and
hydraulic jack are included in the appendix of this report Based on the calibration data
provided by the subcontractor an equation relating the applied load kips and hydraulic
pump pressure psi was generated and used during the load testing The Strain Gage
Information and Calibration Sheets are presented on Plates A 1 through A 6 of the
Appendix
As requested Geolabs provided the data acquisition system including a
multiplexer a data logger and data acquisition program to collect the strain gage readings
during the load testing The strain gage readings were further used in the analyses and
evaluation of the static pile load test In addition displacements at the top of the load test
pile also were measured and recorded by Geolabs
The test pile was loaded in general accordance with the Quick Load Test Method
ASTM D 1143 and modified by our office to suit the subsurface conditions at the project
site The design load of the pile was 300 kips and the maximum test load on the pile was
planned at 750 kips In general the test pile was loaded to the maximum test load of
750 kips in 25 kip load increments Each load increment was held for at least 8 minutes or
until the rate of the pile head movement generally was stable
In addition the static load test was held for approximately 4 hours at the 300 kip
450 kip and 600 kip load increments to evaluate the effects of creep After loading the
test pile to the maximum test load of 750 kips the test load was unloaded in approximately
four equal decrements and held for at least 8 minutes at each decrement The results of
the compressive pile load test are summarized as follows

The readings and the load displacement curve of the static load test are presented
on Plates 1 and 2 respectively Based on the shape of the curve of the compressive load
test it appears that the load test pile is capable of sustaining the maximum test load of
750 kips and the rate of the pile head movement generally was stable without signs of
appreciable creep
The six embedment strain gages in the load test pile were connected using a
multiplexer to a Geokon Micro 10 data acquisition system to record the strains in the
six embedment strain gages during the compressive pile load test Analyses were
conducted on reducing the data from the embedment strain gages for evaluating the load
transfer between the ACIP pile and the surrounding soils
In order to interpret the load transfer in the load test pile the pile stiffness AE was
required However it is very difficult to estimate a uniform elastic modulus for the ACIP
pile using conventional empirical relationships due to the probable irregularities in the pile
cross section Therefore the tangent stiffness method Fellenius 1989 was used to
compute a linear function between the change in applied load and change in strain for
each successive load increment Based on our calculations a weighted average pile
stiffness AE of kips was used in our analysis
Based on the readings obtained from the embedment strain gages it appeared that
one of the six embedment strain gages SG 2 experienced some damage during the load
test pile installation The interpreted load transfer curves in the load test pile are presented
on Plate 3 of this letter report for reference
Based on the load transfer curves presented on Plate 3 the load test pile generally
behaved like a friction pile in the static load test with nearly 100 percent of the maximum
test load approximately 750 kips being resisted by adhesion between the ACIP pile and
the surrounding soils It should be noted that a large portion of the frictional resistance of
the pile about 600 kips out of 750 kips was being provided by the top 36 feet of the load
test pile In addition some of the frictional contribution to the load carrying capacity of the
pile needs to be discounted because of the depth of the pile cap
