Dear Mr Abalos
This letter report presents the findings from our preliminary short term slope stability
assessment update conducted at the Poipu Bay Golf Course in Poipu on the Island of
Kauai Hawaii This report summarizes current findings based on our review of the ground
surface monitoring survey data and our recent reconnaissance of the project site
The intent of this Short Term Slope Stability Assessment No 8 is to provide the
owner with an update of the slope stability condition of a portion of sea cliff adjacent to the
15th and 16th Holes of the golf course The preliminary findings presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted within and at the end of this letter report
Project Considerations
The project site is at the existing Poipu Bay Golf Course which is in the coastal
Poipu area on the Island of Kauai Hawaii A large multiple block type slope failure
occurred in March 2006 along a section of steep sea cliffs bordering the 15th and
16th Holes of the golf course The cliff failure occurred following an extended period of very
heavy rainfall in March 2006 The collapsed portion of the cliff is within the existing
shoreline conservation zone The collapse area involved the full height of the sea cliff and
extended approximately 300 feet long by about 25 feet wide

Site Reconnaissance
We conducted our site reconnaissance on to observe the existing site
conditions for visual signs of potential large scale shoreline slope instability The purpose
of our annual reconnaissance update was to review the existing ground surface conditions
along the top of the slope for visible signs of slope instability
The top of slope reconnaissance spanned between the 16th Hole Tees and the
15th Hole Fairway along a distance of about 1 400 linear feet The reconnaissance was
conducted by our geologist under generally dry fair weather conditions
Existing Condition Observations
Based on our reconnaissance the 10 existing ground surface monitoring points
appear to remain in satisfactory condition with no overt visible sign of significant
disturbance or physical deterioration Based on our observations it appears the monitoring
points are suitable for providing useful ground survey data The approximate location of
the 10 monitoring points is shown on the Site Plans 1 2 Plates 2 1 and 2 2
Based on our reconnaissance and review of the overall existing site conditions no
new area of potential slope instability was observed In addition overt visible signs of
progressive slope instability such as existing ground surface crack propagation and
widening were not observed at the project site
Based on our observations the condition of the existing ground crack previously
identified in the vicinity of Monitoring Point No 3 remains unchanged We observed no
discernible lengthening or widening of this existing crack We observed no overt visual
indication of progressive block instability or slippage which is also confirmed by the survey
data obtained from Monitoring Point No 3 The approximate location of the crack and
associated top of cliff potential block instability is shown on the Site Plan 1 Plate 2 1

Existing Ground Crack Near Monitoring Point No 3
A ground surface crack within the coralline sandstone formation represents an area
of potential cliff instability partial block detachment The crack was identified
during our Short Term Assessment No 4 The area of potential cliff instability was
reviewed for visible signs of crack propagation The open ground surface crack is in
the vicinity of Monitoring Point No 3 as indicated on the Site Plan 1 Plate 2 1
Monitoring Point No 3 is affixed to the potentially unstable block of coralline rock
seaward of the crack Survey data collected from Monitoring Point No 3 with the
passage of time should be useful in evaluating whether subtle and slow movement
such as block rotation or sliding may be occurring
The open surface crack is about 3 5 feet in length and trends roughly parallel to the
cliff face at a distance of about 10 to 12 feet inland from the cliff face Based on our
current observations the surface crack appears to extend vertically through the

Ground Surface Monitoring
As mentioned previously overt visible signs of large scale ground movement
along the top of slope between the 16th Hole Tees and the 15th Hole Fairway were not
observed at the time of our reconnaissance Although we observed no visible indication
of imminent large scale slope instability the timing of possible large block type slope
failures cannot be predicted and the adequacy of relying on periodic visual observations
to assess the stability of the slope is limited
Based on our review of the available monitoring point survey data no significant
deflection of the ground surface markers were detected by the survey data collected
from Monitoring Point Nos 1 through 10
Due to the limitations associated with visual observations we recommend
continuing the annual site reconnaissance and ground survey monitoring program The
assessments should be conducted at least annually and at interim times following
significant natural hazards such as large earthquake tsunami impact or an extreme
storm rainfall event

Public Access at Top of Slope
We observed some existing temporary safety fencing and a naupaka hedge
spanning between the March 2006 slope avulsion zone westerly toward the existing
active 16th Hole Gold Tee The fence was installed after the 2006 collapse event The
original fencing was intended to deter public access seaward of the fence where the
collapse occurred
The recommended fence extension adjacent to the active Gold Tee and the
recommended addition of fencing wing segments to create more of an enclosure
around the unstable rock near Monitoring Point No 3 should assist with reducing public
access to these hazardous zones where unstable rock and deep cliff undercuts reside
We continue to observe evidence of public access cemented fishing pole holders at
these potentially dangerous locations
Public Access at Toe of Slope
Based on our observations the shoreline along the toe of the cliff alignments are
highly susceptible to the real danger of falling rock hazards Natural weathering and
erosion contribute to periodic toppling failure of existing loose rock blocks and boulders
comprising the top edge of the cliffs The public should be discouraged from accessing
the shoreline directly below the coastal cliffs due to the dangerous rockfall conditions
Long Term Setback Recommendations
We recommend maintaining the existing temporary safety fencing and warning
signage such that public access makai of the fencing is discouraged until a permanent
mitigation scheme is implemented It is our opinion the permanent mitigation could
consist of a setback and realignment of the coastal foot trail and the affected portion of
the golf course further inland and away from the zone of potential coastal sea cliff
instability
For preliminary planning purposes a safe use setback may be approximated by
extrapolating a hypothetical failure surface at about a one fourth horizontal to
one vertical inclination extending from the maximum undercut demarcation
to the top of slope elevation This assumes that a future large block type slope failure
would initiate at the assumed undercut depth and the new cliff face produced would
boundary assuming a large block type failure encompassing the existing undercut
depths is presented on the Site Plans 1 and 2 Plates 2 1 and 2 2 The estimated new
top of slope boundary may be used for preliminary planning purposes provided some
additional buffer space beyond the extrapolated boundary is included to estimate the
preliminary setback boundary
A formal geotechnical engineering exploration is recommended to formulate
appropriate long term setbacks from the cliff face for portions of the golf course located
in close proximity to the steep potentially unstable cliffs

Limitations
This report is based in part on a visual assessment of the exposed and accessible
areas of the project site It is not possible to observe all of the existing site conditions
during a brief reconnaissance and literature review This report is also based on our review
of available survey data and mapping information provided by others The preliminary
findings and discussions submitted herein are based in part upon information obtained
from the available topographic survey prepared by others our site reconnaissance and
literature review Variations of surface and subsurface conditions from those described in
this report may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become
evident until a geotechnical engineering exploration is conducted at the site Our services
were performed in accordance with generally accepted standards of geotechnical
engineering practice no warranty is expressed or implied
The findings presented herein are considered preliminary and should be used only
for the short term less than about 1 year evaluation of the slope stability at the project
site Geological processes such as erosion and weathering are natural ongoing
evolutionary processes that may be dependent upon other significant external natural
factors such as seismic disturbance storm events and tsunami activity As a result the
site conditions may change with the passage of time and the preliminary findings
discussed in this report may require re evaluation
