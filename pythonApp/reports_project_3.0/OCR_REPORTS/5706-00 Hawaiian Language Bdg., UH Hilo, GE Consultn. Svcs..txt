Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an area
of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of the following
five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during recent geologic
time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both still
active
The project site is on the eastern flank of Mauna Loa which has been built up by
successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and pyroclastic materials The site is near
the contact between the southeast flank of Mauna Kea and the northeast flank of
Mauna Loa The near surface lava flows are from Mauna Loa and overlie the older
Mauna Kea Shield The lava flows of the Mauna Kea Shield are believed to be Pliocene to
Pleistocene in age and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series The
near surface lava flows of the Mauna Loa Shield may be on the order of only 750 to
1 500 years old

The lava formation encountered appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe type flows
originating from the northeast rift of Mauna Loa A a lava is typically characterized by a
porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock
fragments including cobbles and boulders Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth
rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe lava
Cavities form when lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow
cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes form when molten lava
drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a
large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and pockets are generally smaller in
horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and
along clinker layers Clinker and cavities serve as the major water transmission features
because of their high permeability
Previous experience in the project vicinity indicates that a thin surface layer of
volcanic ash locally referred to as Pahala Ash generally mantles the basalt rock
formation This surface layer of ash generally ranges from about 1 to 3 feet thick The ash
is characterized as having very high in situ moisture contents and is usually highly
compressible The ash also frequently exhibits thixotropic properties i e the material
loses strength when disturbed by remolding or when subjected to earthquake vibrations
Existing Site Conditions
The project site is adjacent to Nowelo Street between Komohana and
Lanikaula Streets on the northwestern side of the city of Hilo in the District of South Hilo on
the Island of Hawaii At present the site is vacant unimproved land and is covered with
dense vegetation and mature trees Two drainage courses traverse the site
Based on the available topographic map the existing ground surface elevation is
on the order of about 250 feet above Mean Sea Level MSL In general the topography is
undulating with higher elevations in the west and sloping down towards Hilo Bay to the
northeast
In general the surface soils consisted of a thin mantle of dark brown clayey silts
volcanic ash with some organics and cobble sized basalt fragments

Site Preparation
As previously mentioned the project site is generally underlain by basalt lava flows
covered with volcanic ash soils at the ground surface The ash soils generally have poor
structural characteristics with respect to foundation and pavement support Lava tubes and
cavities may be present in the volcanic rock formation underlying the site Therefore we
believe that special attention should be given to the site preparation aspects of the
proposed construction
The near surface ash materials are relatively soft and will compress when
subjected to structural and vehicular loads Ash soils have thixotropic properties i e they
temporarily lose strength when remolded or are subjected to vibrations such as seismic
events Therefore to the extent practicable site grading should be designed to remove
the ash soils from structural or pavement areas

The rock formation exposed by the removal of the ash soils and fill areas of the
project site should be ripped to a minimum depth of 3 feet below the existing ground
surface to assist in detecting and collapsing cavities and or voids contained in the porous
near surface materials In addition site preparation consisting of ripping the existing
ground surface should extend at least 10 feet laterally beyond the cut fill transition line
Proof rolling of the subgrades will also be necessary to assist in detecting and possibly
collapsing lava tubes and cavities The proof rolling process may be conducted using a
combination of Caterpillar D 9 or D 10 bulldozers and large vibratory drum rollers
minimum 20 tons static weight In addition a Geolabs representative should observe the
proof rolling operations during earthwork construction to evaluate the potential for
presence of lava tubes and or cavities near the existing ground surface
In general soft loose weak or yielding areas or cavities disclosed during the site
preparation operations should be over excavated to expose firm and or dense ground The
resulting excavation should be backfilled with compacted fill materials It should be noted
that backfilling of lava tubes may be extensive due to the nature of these formations
Therefore contract documents for the mass grading should include additive and deductive
unit prices for over excavating the lava tube features and backfilling with compacted fill to
account for variations in the over excavation and backfill quantities
Proposed grading plans for the development were not available at the time this
letter report was prepared In general the excavated on site rock materials may be used
as a source of fill materials provided that they are processed to meet the gradation
requirements of the various fill types In general structural fills should consist of
well graded granular materials less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient
fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass General fills should
consist of well graded granular materials the majority of which are less than 12 inches in
size with an absolute maximum dimension of 18 inches
Boulders i e rock fragments larger than 12 inches in maximum size but less than
3 feet in largest dimension encountered during site grading may be used in rock fills
provided that several provisions are followed In general boulders should be placed at
least 4 feet below the finished subgrades and at least 6 feet laterally away from the fill
slope faces In addition rock fills should be properly choked off at the surface to reduce
the potential for migration of fines through the voids between boulders

Lava Tubes and Cavities
Based on our experience in the area lava tubes and cavities are commonly
encountered in pahoehoe basalt formations Lava tubes and cavities may vary significantly
in size from small blisters to large lava tubes of 20 feet in diameter or more In general the
majority of small cavities are limited in lateral extent However it should be noted that
there is a potential for the occurrence of long lava tubes which may traverse the project
site The general orientation of the long lava tubes would generally be in a mauka makai
direction
Therefore special attention should be given to the site preparation requirements
prior to site filling and construction of improvements on the cuts and fills Based on our
experience on the adjacent sites the subgrades in fill areas should be ripped and
proof rolled prior to fill placement during the mass grading operations In addition the
finished cut surfaces should be proof rolled with a large vibratory drum roller in an effort to
detect potential near surface lava tubes and cavities In general lava tubes cavities and
blisters discovered during the site preparation operations and or encountered during
construction should be collapsed and backfilled with appropriate fill materials
Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated we believe that the proposed
structures may be supported on a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or
continuous footings As discussed above the existing volcanic ash soils have poor support
characteristics and should to the extent practicable be removed from structural areas
This recommendation will need to be further developed when the project development
scheme is better defined and additional site specific exploration is conducted
As previously mentioned lava tubes and cavities may be present in the basaltic
lava flows To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of
the cavities directly below building foundations a program of cavity probing and grouting of
the foundation locations for buildings over cut and fill areas with less than 5 feet of fills
may be necessary during construction

The US Geological Survey has divided the Island of Hawaii into nine lava flow
hazard zones with the highest risk assigned the designation of Zone 1 and the lowest risk
assigned Zone 9 The project site is situated in an area designated as Zone 3 In this zone
lava flows younger than the year 1800 cover between about 1 to 5 percent of the zone s
surface About 15 to 75 percent of the land surface in this zone is less than 750 years old
Lava inundation risk in Zone 3 is less than Zones 1 and 2 due to the distance from active
rift and vent areas Since the entire city of Hilo is within Zone 3 on a regional scale there
is no greater risk for this project site than for any other location in Hilo However it appears
that the site is situated on or very close to the northeast rift zone of Mauna Loa and there
are cinder cones located near the sites Therefore there would be a higher potential for
hazard from volcanic activity in this area than the southern portions of Hilo which are
further away from the rift zone
For pyroclastic fallout it should be noted that Hawaiian volcanic eruptions are
generally quiescent in nature with very little explosive activity that would eject pyroclastic
material over widespread areas
Most volcanic eruptions produce fragments of lava that are airborne for at least a
short time before being deposited on the ground These fragments are called tephra and
include ash cinders and Pele s hair In Hawaii tephra is usually ejected by lava fountains
and poses a serious hazard only in the immediate vicinity of an erupting vent The ash
soils generally identified as Pahala Ash appear to be Pleistocene or earlier in age
Therefore it is most likely that pyroclastic fall is a very minor hazard for the site

The Federal Emergency Management Agency FEMA has mapped most of the
project site on the Flood Insurance Rate Maps FIRM as Zone X an area outside the
flood hazard area However portions of the site along the stream have been mapped as
being subject to a 1 percent chance of inundation Site layout and grading design will have
to account for these areas
With respect to seismic activity it should be recognized that since the Island of
Hawaii is the site of three active volcanoes there is an appreciable level of seismic activity
associated with the volcanism Since the earliest records in 1868 there have been at least
14 seismic events on and around the Island of Hawaii that exceeded Magnitude 6 on the
Richter scale The Uniform Building Code UBC designates the entire island as Seismic
Zone 4 Therefore the level of seismic activity associated with development of the site
should be adequately addressed in the design of structural elements for the project
The project site appears to be situated near the northeastern rift of Mauna Loa
Localized faulting and ground movements are common along active rift zones during
volcanic activity Therefore it should be recognized that the project site has a somewhat
higher susceptibility to this hazard than portions of Hilo that are not along the rift zone
With the exception of the localized flooding hazard identified and the potential of
occasional groundwater seepage none of the geological hazards that have been identified
for this site present any substantively greater risk than would be posed for any other site in
the city of Hilo Although present the hazards associated with volcanic activity are
currently impossible to estimate due to limited amount of historic activity on Mauna Loa All
of the identified hazards except for those directly associated with volcanic activity i e
ground movements lava flows and pyroclastic fallout can be addressed through proper
project design However the magnitude of these hazards may present economic
constraints on development of the site
Summary
Based on our feasibility study our opinion is that the project site is developable
from a geotechnical engineering point of view However there are constraints placed on
the development associated with geological and other physical conditions that affect the
economic feasibility of the proposed project under the current development scheme The
surface ash soils at the site and the forest growth currently present on the site will result in
relatively higher site preparation costs Wet site conditions and surface watercourses may
have an impact on construction activities and may increase site development costs due to
increased infrastructure requirements
