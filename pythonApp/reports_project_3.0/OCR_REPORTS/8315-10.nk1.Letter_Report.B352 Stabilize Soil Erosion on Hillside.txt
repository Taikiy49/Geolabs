PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located near Building 352 and the adjacent auxiliary building at 
the NAVFAC Makalapa facility on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. We understand that a 
section of the existing slope above the electrical substation has experienced significant 
ongoing erosion. Concrete debris has been exposed on the slope face while eroded soil 
has built up behind the lower retaining wall. We understand that the concrete debris was 
used as fill material in the upper slope section. We also understand that soil material 
has overtopped the lower retaining wall causing blockage to a concrete drainage swale 
running near the electrical substation.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae became extinct 
while Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau 
lava banking against its eastern flank.  
After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion incised deep 
valleys into the Koolau Shield Volcano along with the accumulation of the terrigenous 
and lagoonal deposits along the coastal areas, volcanic activity returned to portions of 
the Island of Oahu as a series of localized lava flows followed by explosive cinder and 
tuff cone formations. These late eruptions belong to the Honolulu Volcanic Series and 
are believed to have occurred between about 30,000 and 800,000 years ago.  
The scattered eruptions emanated from about 37 vents distributed mainly over 
the south and east sides of the older Koolau Shield Volcano (Gramlich, Lewis, & 
Naughton, 1971). Multiple cinder and tuff cone vents belonging to the Honolulu Volcanic 
Series erupted easterly of the Pearl Harbor East Loch in the Salt Lake area. The more 
prominent Salt Lake area volcanic vents still retain some visible remnant crater 
structures as represented by the subaerial topography of the Aliamanu and Makalapa

Craters. These post-erosional (i.e., following the cessation of shield building) events 
were contemporaneous with some of the late Pleistocene sea level fluctuations. 
These late eruptions were explosive in character due to the rising magma’s 
interaction with groundwater that resulted in violent steam explosions that expelled large 
quantities of pyroclastic materials generally composed of volcanic ash and cinder with 
some embedded blocks of basaltic rock. These deposits consolidated to form volcanic 
tuff, locally referred to as “mudrock.” 
The oldest of the eruptions (at Aliamanu Crater) caused the initial large diversion 
of Halawa Stream to the north of its original location into what are now the Southeast 
and Magazine Lochs of Pearl Harbor during the Kaena (+95 feet) high stand of the sea. 
The Aliamanu tuff is a water-laid pyroclastic deposit resulting from the higher stand of 
the sea at the time of deposition.  
Following the deposition of the Aliamanu tuff, stream erosion and the deposition 
of alluvial soils continued prior to the next phase of pyroclastic eruptions at Salt Lake. 
The later Salt Lake area eruptions were essentially simultaneous and have been 
assigned to the stand of the sea that was between about 40 and 60 feet below the 
present sea level. The last Salt Lake area pyroclastic eruptions deposited a mantle of 
volcanic tuff over much of the Makalapa-Salt Lake area. 
In summary, the project site is situated along the northwestern rim of Makalapa 
Crater. In general, the subsurface materials underlying the site consist of man-made fills 
overlying volcanic tuff and older alluvium with increasing depth.  
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
one boring, designated as Boring No. 101, extending to a depth of about 48 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
Our boring generally encountered a surface fill layer approximately 5 feet thick 
consisting of very stiff to hard silty clay. Underlying the fill, volcanic tuff formation was 
encountered to a depth of about 37 feet. The upper 5 feet of volcanic tuff was generally 
highly weathered and soft to medium hard. The tuff was then graded to moderately to 
slightly fractured, slightly to moderately weathered, and medium hard to hard. Below the 
tuff, older alluvium consisting of stiff silty clay was encountered to the maximum depth 
explored of about 48 feet below the existing ground surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other 
factors.

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is located above the electrical substation adjacent to 
Building 352 at the NAVFAC Makalapa facility on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. An eroded 
slope section has caused the accumulation of soil behind a lower retaining wall. Heavy 
rain events cause water and debris to overtop the retaining wall and deposit near the 
electrical substation. The site layout, the extent of the eroded slope section, and 
location of the lower retaining wall are presented on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
A visual site reconnaissance was conducted on November 2, 2022 by our project 
engineer. The purpose of our reconnaissance was to review the existing conditions of 
the top-of-slope, slope face, bottom-of-slope, and lower retaining wall to evaluate 
possible erosion control and slope stabilization systems. Site reconnaissance 
photographs depicting the observed conditions at the project site are presented on 
Plates 3.1 through 3.5. 
Based on our field observations and the topographic information provided, the 
lateral extent of the erosion scar is approximately 35 feet long measured along the 
top-of-slope. The existing slope west of the erosion scar has a slope inclination of about 
one and two-tenth horizontal to one vertical (1.2H:1V), and the existing slope to the east 
of the erosion scar has slope inclinations of about 1H:1V to 0.7H:1V. The slopes 
adjacent to the erosion scar are generally covered by short grass. Several large trees 
were observed at the top-of-slope to the west of the erosion scar (Photograph No. 9, 
Plate 3.5). 
The eroded area was observed to be filled with broken up reinforced concrete 
debris and pieces of reinforcing steel (Photograph Nos. 1 through 5, Plates 3.1 through 
3.3). It appears that this debris was used as slope backfill material. Subsequent rain 
events have eroded the soil cover, exposing the man-made debris and creating steep 
side slopes within the erosion scar. Based on the topographic survey map provided, the 
slope inclination within the scar averages about 1.4H:1V but becomes near-vertical in 
the upper 5 feet of the erosion scar. The eastern and western side slopes of the scar 
expose fill material consisting of grayish brown silty sand with some gravel with pieces 
of corroded rebar (Photograph No. 6, Plate 3.3). Below the erosion scar, soil debris was 
observed to be accumulated behind the lower retaining wall. Near the bend in the 
retaining wall alignment, the soil accumulation had reached the same level as the top of 
the wall (Photograph Nos. 7 and 8, Plate 3.4). Therefore, based on the topographic 
information provided, the thickness of soil accumulation behind the retaining wall ranges 
from about 1 to 3 feet in height.  
The western half of the slope has slope inclinations generally ranging from about 
1.3H:1V to 2H:1V. Low-lying grass vegetation was observed on most of the slope faces. 
Distinct rock outcrops were observed at approximately mid-height, extending across 
most of the western half of the slope. The exposed rock outcrops were observed with 
slope face inclinations from about 0.5H:1V to near-vertical. The eastern half of the slope 
was generally observed to have a gentler slope inclination of about 2H:1V. Rock

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration program, our boring drilled near the top-of-slope 
generally encountered a relatively stiff fill layer approximately 5 feet thick overlying 
volcanic tuff formation extending to a depth of about 37 feet. Below the tuff, stiff older 
alluvium was encountered to the maximum depth explored of about 48 feet below the 
existing ground surface. Based on our field observations, it appears that the existing top 
of the slope was built out using man-made debris and on-site soils. Therefore, the 
existing slope face is likely composed of fill and man-made debris overlying the in-situ 
volcanic tuff formation. 
Based on our field observations and the topographic survey map provided, we 
understand that the existing shallow drainage swale at the top of the slope does not 
extend to above the erosion scar (Photograph No. 10, Plate 3.5). However, we believe 
that stormwater is overtopping the slope in this area, causing the slope to erode. 
Therefore, we recommend that drainage improvements be installed at the top of the 
slope area, such as extending the existing drainage swale to above the erosion area 
and discharge the stormwater to a drainage structure. 
Two-dimensional cross-sections of the erosion scar area were used for our slope 
stability analyses. The purpose of our analyses was to evaluate the stability of the 
existing slope and back-calculate the soil properties of the in-situ fill material observed 
on the slope face. Our models were also used to evaluate the performance of the 
recommended erosion control and slope stabilization measures. Our analyses were 
performed using the latest version of the software program Slide2 by Rocscience. 
Slide2 is a two-dimensional limit equilibrium analysis program that can model complex 
slope stability and seepage conditions. The Bishop’s Simplified Method, Spencer’s 
Method, and GLE/Morgenstern-Price Method were used to evaluate the stability of the 
existing slope. 
Based on our analyses, the factor of safety against sliding for the existing erosion 
scar area is less than the required factor of safety of 1.5. Therefore, a suitable slope 
stabilization measure will be required to increase the global slope stability factor of 
safety to 1.5 or greater.  
Because the existing erosion scar has side slope inclinations of 1.4H:1V to 
near-vertical, the eroded area will need to be backfilled. Based on the topography of the 
slope and the erosion scar, we do not believe the erosion scar can be properly 
backfilled using conventional backfill placement and compaction. Therefore, the erosion 
area will need to be backfilled with concrete, fiber-reinforced shotcrete, or grouted 
rubble paving (GRP).

Based on our field observations, we believe creating formwork for the erosion 
scar area may be infeasible. Therefore, an erosion control and slope stabilization 
system using cast-in-place concrete was not considered for this project. Due to the 
existing erosion scar having a global slope stability factor of safety of less than 1.5, soil 
nails would likely be needed in addition to fiber-reinforced shotcrete to adequately 
stabilize the area. Because of this, this would likely be the most expensive alternative 
and take the longest to construct. Therefore, we recommend an erosion control and 
slope stabilization system consisting of GRP with a bottom key. The bottom key should 
be a minimum of 2 feet thick by 2.5 feet deep. The front of the key should also be 
setback a minimum of 5 feet from the back of the lower retaining wall to prevent the 
additional load being imparted to the lower retaining wall. Details of the GRP erosion 
control and slope stabilization system are presented on Plate 4. 
The slope erosion has exposed in-situ concrete and steel debris that was 
originally used to build out the existing slope. To facilitate the erosion control and slope 
stabilization work, the concrete, steel, loose rocks, man-made debris, surface 
vegetation, and other deleterious debris should be cleared from the erosion scar area 
and 5 feet laterally beyond the edges of the erosion scar and properly disposed of 
off-site. The large tree on the western edge of the erosion scar, as well as the 
horizontally growing tree trunk, should also be removed to facilitate erosion control and 
slope stabilization work (Photograph No. 9, Plate 3.5).  
The GRP system should be well-drained to reduce the potential for build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of 2-foot-wide strips of a 
prefabricated drainage composite product placed on the face of the cleared and 
grubbed erosion scar. The prefabricated drainage composite product should extend 
from the top of the erosion area to the base of the erosion area and should be 
hydraulically connected to a weephole at the base of the wall. The weepholes should be 
spaced no more than 5 feet apart and should be hydraulically connected to 
prefabricated drains. Permeable backfill material, such as open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), wrapped with non-woven filter fabric should be placed 
around the weephole system. 
To reduce the potential for future erosion at the edges of the GRP system, 
permanent turf reinforcing matting (North American Green C-350 or equal) should be 
provided on the existing adjacent slopes extending a minimum horizontal distance of 
5 feet on both sides of the GRP system. 
The existing soil debris behind the lower retaining wall should be removed to 
prevent future rains from causing the material to overtop the wall. The loose debris 
should be properly disposed of off-site.
