Project Considerations
The Hawaii Resort Ko‘Olina project site is within the existing Ko’Olina Resort
development in the District of Ewa on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. The project site
encompasses two parcels of land (Lot Nos. 13 and 15) fronting Lagoon No. 1 of the

Resort & Spa (to the northwest). The two parcels are located to the southwest of
Ali inui Drive, which is the main entry road into the resort.
The proposed project generally consists of constructing a hotel development at the
project site. We understand the preliminary plans call for developing three tower structures
of about 11 to 13 stories high. We also understand that a two to three-level basement
parking structure, conference center, restaurants, and other support facilities will be
required. Further details of the proposed development were not available at the time this
letter report was prepared.
We understand that Walt Disney Imagineering, the potential purchaser for this
property, would like to proceed with a due diligence study to evaluate the feasibility of the
proposed project. Therefore, a limited field exploration consisting of drilling and sampling
of two borings was conducted for this due diligence study.

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Koolau Volcano reached the
end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago.
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau
generated sediments, which were transported to the coast. In the vicinity of the project site
and to the southeast, these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine
sediments and coral/algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of
the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway
to over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu.
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present.
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level.
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys
in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous
reef materials. Placement of man-made fills and commercial development of the area
within the last 20 years have brought the Ko’ Olina area to its present form.
Site Description
As mentioned previously, the project site is within the existing Ko’Olina Resort in
the Ko’Olina area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project site (Lot Nos. 13 and 15) is
along Ali inui Drive between Olani Street and Kamoana Place. Lagoon No. 1 fronts the
property, as shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2.
Based on our site reconnaissance, the project site generally is graded to a
near-level condition. The majority of the project site is grassed with an irrigation network.
Based on the available maps and our observations, the ground surface elevations at the
project site range from about +20 to +30 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).

Subsurface Conditions
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project by drilling and sampling
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, at the approximate locations shown on
the Site Plan, Plate 3. The borings were advanced to depths of about 80.5 to 81.3 feet
below the existing ground surface. In addition, research and review of available borings
(in-house information) in the project vicinity was conducted to supplement the data from
the limited field exploration conducted.
Based on the limited filed exploration and a review of available in-house
information, native soils at the project site generally consist of coralline detritus and coral
formation. Coralline detritus generally consist of medium dense to dense silty sands and
gravel with some cementation (varies from slight to moderate).
Our in-house information and site observations indicate that the majority of the
project site was graded to a fill condition during the original mass grading of the Ko’Olina
Resort. We estimate that fills of up to about 16 feet in height may have been placed at the
western portions of the project site. In general, fill materials placed during the initial mass
grading operations consisted of on-site materials generated from excavations at other
areas within the Ko’Olina Resort. Therefore, we anticipate that the near-surface materials
may consist of mixture of alluvium and coralline deposits. In areas with deeper fills, rock
fills consisting of coralline boulders and cobbles also may have been placed.
The eastern portion of the project site generally was mass graded to a cut
condition. Therefore, we anticipate that coralline detritus and/or coral formation may be
encountered near the ground surface at this portion of the site.
Interbedded layers of coralline detritus and coral formation generally are anticipated
below the surface fill materials. It should be noted that based on our experience in the
project vicinity, coralline detritus and coral formation are highly variable in terms of
hardness and quality. Pockets of voids and cavities are commonly encountered in coralline
formations in this area. In addition, cavities in the coral formation could be filled with soft
and/or loose coralline detritus and alluvial deposits.
Alluvium consisting of very stiff to hard silty clays was encountered below the
coralline deposits in our borings from about 40 and 65.5 feet below the ground surface in
Boring Nos. 1 and 2, respectively. The alluvium was underlain by clinker basalt formation
from about 63 feet in Boring No. 1. The alluvium extended to the maximum depth explored
of about 81.3 feet in Boring No. 2. Therefore, the depth to rocky subsurface conditions
varies considerably across the project site, as evidenced by the two borings drilled.
Shallow groundwater levels should be expected due to the relatively low elevations
at the project site. In general, we envision that the groundwater levels will typically range
from about -1 to +1 foot MSL. However, due to the proximity of the project site to the

Discussions and Recommendations
Based on our review of the available information, we believe that the proposed
hotel development project is generally feasible from a geotechnical engineering

Deep Foundations
We understand that details of the proposed structures have not been determined at
the time this letter report was prepared. However, we understand that the main structures
may range from 11 to 13 stories high. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at
the project site, we believe that the proposed tower structures should be supported on a
deep foundation system consisting of cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts or augered
cast-in-place concrete piles.
Based on our experience at the Ko’Olina Resort, we envision that there may be
concerns regarding excess noise and vibrations generated during the typical pile driving
operations. Therefore, we believe that drilled shaft foundations or augered cast-in-place
(ACIP) piles may be desired for support of the proposed structures. Considering the
subsurface conditions (coralline detritus and/or coral formation), there is a likely potential
for cost savings when comparing drilled shaft foundations and augered cast-in-place piles
to the traditional driven pile foundations for support of the new structures in addition to the
benefits of less noise and vibrations.
We envision that the new structures may be supported on 36-inch diameter drilled
shafts, similar to most of the recent high-rise building developments within Ko’Olina
Resort. It is anticipated that each column would be supported on a single drilled shaft
designed to support the structural loads of the columns. For preliminary estimating
purposes, we envision that the drilled shafts would need to extend to depths of about 50 to
80 feet below the bottom of footing (cap) elevation in order to achieve an allowable
compressive load capacity of about 1,500 kips per drilled shaft.

It should be noted that cavities and voids are commonly encountered in the
coralline materials at the project vicinity. Therefore, the actual volume of concrete required
to fill the drilled shaft foundation may be appreciably more than the theoretical concrete
volume. Based on our experience at the Ko Olina Resort area, we envision that concrete

required for the drilled shaft foundations.
Our limited field exploration encountered varying subsurface conditions from the
makai to mauka portions of the project site. Therefore, we recommend evaluating the
subsurface conditions further by conducting a design-level field exploration program
consisting of drilling and sampling additional borings during the design phase of the project
when the type and distribution of the proposed structures are better defined.
Expansive Soils
Based on our experience, the on-site silty clay alluvial soils may exhibit moderate
shrink/swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. As mentioned previously,
silty clay alluvial soils have been used as fill materials during the initial mass grading
operations for the Ko’Olina Resort development. In addition, our borings encountered the
silty clay soils near the existing ground surface. .
At this time, the finished floor elevations of the various slab levels have not been
determined. The expansive potential of the alluvial soils at the site at the slab subgrade
level should be evaluated further by performing laboratory swell tests during the design
phase. Where expansive soils are encountered, over-excavation of the alluvial soils may
be necessary under foundations, slabs-on-grade, and/or pavements. We estimate that the
over-excavation depth below the foundations, slabs-on-grade, and pavements may be on
the order of about 18 to 36 inches in areas underlain by the expansive soils. The resulting
over-excavation would need to be backfilled with non-expansive select granular fill
(on-site coralline soils or imported fill).
Cobbles and Boulders
As mentioned previously, rock fills consisting of coralline and basaltic cobbles and
boulders were placed during the initial mass grading operations of the resort. In general,
we believe that rock fills at the project site were limited to the deep fill areas only.
Therefore, the potential for presence of cobbles and boulders exists at the mass graded
areas along the western portions of the project site. Excavations for basements, building
foundations, and/or utility lines may encounter some difficulties due to the presence of
these potential obstructions in the subsurface.

Site Preparation
Grading plans were not available at the time this letter report was prepared. In
general, the excavated on-site materials may be used as a source of fill materials,
provided that they are processed to meet the gradation requirements of the various fill
types. Due to the potential for presence of expansive soils, special attention should be
given to the site preparation aspect of the proposed construction. Over-excavation of the
subgrades under structures and roadways may be necessary depending on the expansive
nature and distribution of the on-site clayey soils. Fill areas may require placement of
non-expansive select granular material below future improvements, such as building slabs,
sidewalks, and/or pavements.
Boulders (i.e., rock fragments larger than 12 inches in maximum size but less than
2 feet in dimension) encountered during site grading may be used in fills, provided that
several provisions are followed. In general, the boulders should be placed a minimum of
4 feet below the finished subgrades and at least 6 feet laterally away from slope faces. In
addition, fills consisting of cobbles and boulders should be properly “choked-off’ at the
surface to reduce the potential for migration of fines through the voids between the
boulders.
Limitations
It should be noted that it is not possible to observe all the site conditions based on a
brief reconnaissance of the project site and a review of literature. Variations of subsurface
conditions from those described in this report may occur, and the nature and extent of
these variations may not become evident until further geotechnical engineering exploration
is conducted at the site or construction is underway. A design-level geotechnical
engineering exploration should be conducted to confirm and/or modify the preliminary
recommendations provided herein. Our services on this project were performed in
accordance with generally accepted standards of geotechnical engineering practice; no
warranty is expressed or implied.

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling two borings, designated as
Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 80.5 and 81.3 feet below the existing
ground surface. The approximate locations of the borings drilled at the site are shown on
the Site Plan, Plate 3. The borings were drilled using truck-mounted drilling equipment
equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who observed the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
Core samples of coral formation and/or rock materials encountered at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling
is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral and/or rock formation.
The coral and/or rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered
for examination and description.
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of soil and/or rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material
recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if
3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent
and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

quality of coral and/or rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that

drilling-induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5-foot
core run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the coral
and/or rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths.
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits for soil classification. The test results are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1.
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D 1883) was performed on a
bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-2.
One modified Proctor Compaction test (ASTM D 1557) was performed on a bulk
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships. The test results are presented on Plate B-3.
