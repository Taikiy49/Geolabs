This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Interstate Route H 1 Waiawa to Keehi Interchange
Contraflow and Shoulder Lane Project in Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The
general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and _ geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations for the
proposed overhead signs only In addition borings were drilled at the electrical line
crossings to provide basic subsurface information for installing the electrical lines using a
trenchless method such as directionai drilling The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed Contraflow project site is approximately 14 miles in length
stretching along Interstate Route H 1 from about one half mile west of Waikele Bridge to
Keehi Interchange on the south side of the island of Oahu as shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1 The proposed project consists of the creation of an
additional lane for Contraflow inbound traffic In general one of the two existing
outbound lanes will be utilized for the Contraflow lane and the other lane will be used as
a buffer between the Contraflow lane and opposing traffic A specially designed movable
traffic barrier and vehicle will be used to partition the Contraflow lane known as the
Zipper lane on a daily basis
There will be three crossover areas to allow traffic to enter and exit the Contraflow
H 2 Interchange and the third will be near the Pearl Harbor Interchange to allow the
Contraflow Zipper lane back onto the inbound shoulder area In the Waiau area the
existing shoulder area adjacent to the median barrier is limited Therefore the inbound
lanes will require re striping and the creation of additional traffic lanes on the mauka side
of the highway This may involve paving the existing mauka shoulder
In support of the Contraflow Zipper lane project sixteen electric powered signs
two of which will be variable message signs or VMS and twenty one non powered signs
are planned along the Contraflow Zipper lane route The various sign support schemes
are as follows

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be of late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau
referred to as the Schofield Plateau
The Koolau Volcanic Series is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of
lavas These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt
ith interbedded thin clinker The tt of i in shield
building phase about 2 million years ago For the last 1 to 2 million years in situ _
chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred forming a mantle of residual
soils which grade to saprolite with depth In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty
material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to
basaltic rock formation with depth
As previously indicated the Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the
Koolau Volcano ponded against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the
late Pleistocene Epoch The dips of the lava beds are near horizontal between 3 to
5 degrees from horizontal The lava flows on the plateau have weathered to depths of
50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors of the soil The deep narrow
canyons such as Waikakalua Kipapa and Kaukonahua are the result of this
weathering
As the main shield building on the island of Oahu drew to a close during the
Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of continental
glaciation As the sea levels fluctuated fringing coral algal reefs began to grow on the

seaward margins of the island After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as
the Honolulu Volcanic Series Subsequently a thick sedimentary wedge known as the
Honolulu Coastal Plain started to accumulate along the southern shores of Oahu The
Honolulu Coastal Plain is composed of intercalated coral algal reef deposits terrigenous
and marine sediments and volcanics of the Honolulu Volcanic Series

Tertiary Koolau Volcanic Basalt Tkb
The tertiary Koolau volcanic basalt generally consists of gray with multi colored
mottling highly vesicular slightly to completely weathered soft to hard basalt
This rock formation and the soil material above completely weathered basalt
were formed by pahoehoe and a a lava flows Clinker beds created from the a a
lava flows cover approximately 60 percent of the Koolau Range In lower
elevations and areas of lower rainfall the upper basalt weathered to a red lateritic
soil In higher elevations and areas of higher rainfall the upper basalt weathered
to a dark gray to brown clay The weathering profile may extend to great depths
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3778 00 Page 10
The weathering was also non uniform in various areas thus creating spheroidally
weathered boulders in random areas
Based on the borings drilled for the cantilever signs Boring Nos 1 through 5 7
and 8 and for the electrical line crossings Boring Nos 101 through 104 and 111
through 118 it appears that the upper basalt generally consist of weathered to
very stiff red brown silty clays The lower basalt was generally found to be
orange gray vesicular moderately fractured highly to moderately weathered and
ranged from soft to hard rock In Boring No 118 gray highly fractured soft
volcanic tuff was encountered below the silty clay The surface layer may consist
of the pavement section and or topsoil
Quaternary Alluvium Qa
The quaternary alluvium or older alluvium generally overlies the basalt rock
formation and consists of terrigenous sediments which have been transported by
stream action from the upper reaches of the streams The older alluvium is
generally located at the foot of mountain ranges and steep slopes and at the
heads of large eroded valleys The material is more granular in nature when
compared to recent alluvium and consists of alluvial and colluvial fans talus and
debris flows of material generally consisting of sand gravel cobbles and boulders
with clays
Based on the borings for the cantilever signs Boring and 15 and for
the electrical crossings Boring Nos 105 and 108 through 110 it appears that the
older alluvial soils generally consist of very stiff light brown to red brown silty clays
and clayey silts with some basalt boulders The underlying rock material generally
consists of soft to medium hard orange and gray slightly to highly weathered
basalt and cinder rock The surface layer may consist of the pavement section
and or topsoil

Quaternary Honolulu Volcanic Tuff Qht
The quaternary Honolulu Volcanic Tuff generally consists of gray to brown highly
fractured soft to hard volcanic tuff The fracturing generally follows a block pattern
parallel and perpendicular to the dips of the bedding This tuff material is generally
the result of Koolau and post Koolau basalt In some areas limestone coral is
found embedded within the tuff material cemented together in a breccia matrix
Based on the borings for the cantilever signs and sign bridges Boring Nos 10
through 13 and 16 and for the electrical line crossings Boring Nos 119 through
124 it appears that the tuff material generally consists of highly fractured soft to
medium hard material In various locations layers of coral tuff breccia were
found embedded within the tuff material At Boring and 124
above the volcanic tuff material The fill material was composed of processed tuff
material mixed with clayey material The surface layer may consist of the
pavement section and or topsoil
Recent Fill Rf
The recent fill generally consists of various quality fill material placed over soft to
very soft brown clay with varying amounts of white marine deposits such as shells
coral and other calcareous marine organisms These soils are generally the result
of filling of marshes and low elevation areas around the Pearl Harbor vicinity
Based on Boring No 9 for a cantilever sign it appears that the recent fill material
generally consists of very dense gray brown silty gravel with sand white coral
gravel and debris followed by very soft to medium stiff gray clay The surface
layer may consist of the pavement section and or topsoil

Recent Alluvium Ra
The recent alluvium or younger alluvium generally consists of soft to medium stiff
brown to dark gray clayey silts and organic silts with varying amounts of sand and
infrequent pockets of gravels and cobbles These soils are generally the result of
sedimentation in a low energy environment such as a lake marsh lowland and
coastal areas Frequently the soils assigned to this geologic unit accumulated
very rapidly under water and may not have been exposed to desiccation
Therefore there is a tendency to encounter under consolidated sediments which
are still settling under their own weight or the weight of minimal overburden
Based on the borings for the electrical line crossings Boring Nos 106 and 107 it
appears that the recent alluvial soils generally consist of medium stiff to very stiff
fractured hard light brown gray basalt The surface layer may consist of the
pavement section and or topsoil

Drilled Shaft Foundations
Based on the loading conditions provided and the subsurface conditions
encountered we recommend that the poles for the cantilever sign and sign bridges be
supported by drilled shaft foundations As previously indicated two foundation types
Anchor Bolt on Concrete Drilled Shaft and Anchor Bolt onto Elevated Pedestal are
planned for support of the proposed pole structures resulting in about the same loading
conditions on the drilled shaft foundations Therefore we have not made distinctions
between the two types of foundations
Based on the bottom diameter of the steel poles 24 inches at the base the
available auger core barrel sizes from the local drilling companies and the shaft lengths
anticipated drilled shaft diameters of 3 feet were considered in our foundation analyses
The major considerations in our foundation analyses included the lateral loads and
a Hine moments shattt tations
In general lateral load resistance for drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of
the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of the shaft
and induced moment in the shaft Based on a maximum horizontal deflection of inch at
the top of the drilled shaft subjected to ground line overturning moments and lateral
forces we have evaluated the required drilled shaft lengths using the computer software
program LPILE plus for Windows This program is a microcomputer adaptation of a finite
difference laterally loaded pile program originally developed at the University of Texas at
Austin The program solves for deflection and bending moment along a drilled shaft
under lateral loads as a function of depth The analysis was carried out with the use of
internally generated non linear p y curves to represent soil moduli The lateral deflection
was then computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths The analyses
were performed for a laterally loaded 3 foot diameter cast in place concrete drilled shaft

Torsion Resistance
In general sign foundation wi be subjected to torsional moments resulting in
torsion on the drilled shaft foundations Torsion may be resisted by the side shear along
the drilled shaft surface and adjacent soils In our foundation analyses we have checked
the drilled shafts subjected to the torsional moments provided by the Project Structural
Engineer Based on our analyses we believe that the 3 foot diameter drilled shafts
extending to the lengths recommended should be able to resist the torsional moments
without significant movements We wish to emphasize that the sides of the drilled holes
should be relatively free of loose materials and the concrete for the drilled shaft should
have close contact to the in situ soils after the drilled shaft has been constructed to
provide the torsional resistance required
Construction Considerations
method of construction and construction procedures As a result we recommend that a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii be present to observe the installation of drilled shafts
during construction In our opinion the following may have a significant impact on the
effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in some of the borings drilled for
the project we anticipate that drilling through dense boulders or rock formation will be
required The drilled shaft contractor will need to have the appropriate equipment and or
coring tools to drill through the boulders and rock formation Appropriate measures by the
contractor will also be needed to avoid dislodging any boulders into the drilled shaft
excavation prior to placement of concrete
In addition soft loose soils may be encountered during construction of some of the
drilled shaft foundations To reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes
temporary casing of the drilled holes may be needed during construction The contractor
should be made aware that temporary casing of the drilled holes may be needed at no
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3778 00 Page 20
additional cost to the State In addition care should be exercised during removal of the
temporary casing to reduce the potential for necking of the drilled shaft Therefore a
minimum 5 foot head of concrete should be maintained above the bottom of the casing
and or groundwater level during removal of the casing
The load carrying capacity of drilled shafts depends to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the
shaft holes and in placing concrete in the holes The bottom of all drilled shafts should be
cleaned of any soft or disturbed material prior to constructing the foundation The bottom
should also be fairly level
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is generally not recommended _ If
construction methods using a tremie pipe will be required A low shrink concrete mix with
high slump 6 to 9 inch slump range should be used to provide close contact between
the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils The concrete should be placed in a suitable
manner to reduce segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix
Considerations for Electrical Line Crossings
As previously mentioned we envision that a trenchless method such as
directional drilling will be used to install the electrical line crossings To reduce the
potential for significant ground surface settlement we recommend that the directional
drilling be located deep enough such that at least two borehole diameters of undisturbed
soil exist above the directional drilling borehole Generally arching of the soil cover
should occur at a depth of about two borehole diameters or more above the directional
drilling borehole

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the cantilever sign and sign bridges were explored by
drilling and sampling 16 borings to depths ranging from about 20 to 38 5 feet below the
existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans Plates 2
through 34 The subsurface conditions at the electrical line crossings were explored by
drilling and sampling 24 borings to depths ranging from about 12 5 to 16 5 feet below the
existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans Plates 2
through 34 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of borings Fiates A T roug
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description The
core barrel was advanced using water as a drilling fluid
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100

would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Thirteen Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil
classification The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plates B 1 and
B 2
Nine sieve analysis tests ASTM D 422 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils Graphic presentations of
the test results are provided on Plates B 3 and B 4
_________ Two unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on selected
samples to evaluate their unconfined compressive strength characteristics Results of the
tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The test
results and stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 5 and B 6
Two unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression tests TXUU ASTM D 2850
were performed on selected samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the
cohesive soils The approximate in situ effective overburden pressure was used as the
applied confining pressure Results of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths The test results and stress strain curves are presented
on Plates B 7 and B 8
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate their shear strength characteristics The test results and shear stress versus
normal stress plots are presented on Plates B 9 and B 10
One consolidation test ASTM D 2435 was performed on a selected soil sample to
evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the on site soils The test results and
consolidation curve are presented on Plate B 11
