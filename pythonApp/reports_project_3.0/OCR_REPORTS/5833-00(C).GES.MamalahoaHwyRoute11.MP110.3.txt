This letter report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering study
performed for the Mamalahoa Highway Earthquake Repairs Route 11 Milepost 110 3
project in the Captain Cook area of South Kona on the Island of Hawaii The project
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This letter report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our site reconnaissance review of slope stabilization
alternatives and engineering analyses These recommendations are intended to assist in
hazard reduction and or the design of the stabilization of the existing rock cut slope The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the
end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project is in the vicinity of Milepost 110 3 on Mamalahoa Highway Route 11 in
the area of Captain Cook in South Kona on the Island of Hawaii A near vertical rock cut
slope of about 15 to 20 feet in height has experienced accelerated erosion along the
bottom of the slope for a distance of approximately 150 lineal feet alongside the highway
Erosion and raveling of the exposed basaltic clinker materials on the lower slope face has
resulted in some undercut and overhanging rock conditions
To reduce the potential for additional undermining of the rock slope which could
produce future hazardous rockfall conditions it is desired to investigate possible options
for hazard reduction and or stabilization of the undercut slope face

The purpose of our geotechnical engineering services was to obtain an overview of
the existing surface conditions to develop a generalized soil and or rock data set to
formulate geotechnical recommendations for the rockfall hazard reduction and or the
stabilization of the slope face To accomplish this we conducted a program consisting of
the following tasks and work efforts

around the project site
2 Reconnaissance of the project site and mapping of the exposed rock face by

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers
an area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of the
following five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during recent
geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea
both still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Mauna Loa near its geological
contact with the southern flank of Hualalai The project site appears to be underlain by
basaltic lava flows of the Kau Volcanic Series of Mauna Loa which were deposited during
the Holocene and Pleistocene Epochs Stearns and Macdonald 1946 Several large

In the project vicinity the Kau Volcanic Series rock is typically composed of basaltic
lava flows which are mantled in places by a relatively thin layer of silty and sandy volcanic
ash The near surface lava formation exposed at the project site appears to represent a a
lava flow type consisting of a dense rock lava flow core and bounding weak clinker layers
The surface of a a lava is typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular
flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and
boulders Typically a more dense and layered lava rock material is contained within the
lava flow core Cavities are most commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas Cavities form
when lavas are still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and
blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes form when molten lava drains from
the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large
longitudinal distance along the flow Blisters and pockets inter flow cavities are generally
smaller in horizontal extent
The a a lava flow exposed at the project site consists of a gray colored weakly
vugular dense lava flow core composed of massive to slightly fractured and slightly
weathered basaltic rock which is bounded by upper and lower clinker layers The exposed
clinker layers may be characterized as a weak medium hard to soft rock reddish brown
colored rock mass consisting of welded basalt rock fragments typically ranging in size from
gravel to cobbles The dense basaltic rock was observed to contain some open joints and
fractures extending through the core of the flow in places The clinker materials may be
picked apart with a hammer with little effort and were observed to be actively eroding from
beneath the overlying dense rock material
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is along the mauka shoulder of Mamalahoa Highway Route 11
near Milepost 110 3 in the area of Captain Cook in South Kona on the Island of Hawaii
The Route 11 intersection with Napoopoo Road bounds the project site a short distance
toward the west
The section of Mamalahoa Highway fronting the project site consists of a two lane
asphaltic concrete highway with 10 foot travel lanes The existing highway is
superelevated on the makai side of the roadway and forms a right hand curve heading in
the Kailua Kona inbound direction An existing paved shoulder ranging in width between
about 5 and 9 feet resides mauka of the white lane stripe The edge of pavement along
the paved shoulder is bounded by a continuous length of asphaltic concrete curbing of
approximately 0 5 feet in height which appears to assist in the control of surface drainage
toward a downstream catch basin inlet

The rock cut slope reported to have experienced accelerated erosion bounds the
mauka side of the highway for a distance of approximately 150 lineal feet as shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 An unpaved shoulder of variable width is between the edge of the
pavement and the toe of the rock cut slope The width of the existing unpaved shoulder
appears to range on the order of between about 1 to 13 feet
Based on our observations a significant volume of rocky debris has accumulated in
the unpaved shoulder along the toe of the existing rock cut slope The debris appears to
consist mainly of loose basaltic clinker materials derived from erosion of the weak rock
clinker layer residing along the bottom of the cut slope The loose debris generally consists
of gravel and cobble sized rock fragments with some boulders up to about 2 feet in
diameter
Photographs of the existing slope conditions are presented in Appendix B
Potential Rockfall Conditions
It appears that the eroding and deteriorated clinker layer has resulted in the
development of some undercuts within the clinker along portions of the lower rock cut
slope The clinker undercuts have generated some overhanging rock conditions for the
dense massive basaltic rock above the clinker exposure The typical undercuts extend
approximately 2 to 5 feet in depth into the slope measured from the slope face with a
localized area of maximum undercut extending approximately 10 feet deep The maximum
area of rock formation undercut appears to be related to a possible lava tube feature near
the clinker dense rock contact at about Profiles of the existing slope condition
at selected station intervals are presented in Appendix A
The dense basalt rock above the basaltic clinker layer appears to be generally
stable however several very large blocks of dense rock were observed to be bounded by
wide open relatively planar smooth rock joints indicating some potential for future block
separation from the parent outcrop may exist The jointed blocks are in contact with each
other and appear to range in size on the order of about 5 to 12 feet in diameter with a
depth approaching about 6 to 8 feet Continued undermining of the basaltic clinker layer
and loss of support may initiate the separation of the jointed rock blocks from the cut slope
face
Based on our observations the loose clinker accumulation has formed a mantling
debris slope ranging between about two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V and 3H 1V It
appears that the clinker layer comprising the lower portion of the rock cut slope probably
extends from the upper contact with the dense basalt down toward the toe of the cut slope
at the unpaved shoulder elevation However due to the loose clinker accumulations we
believe the possibility exists that some dense rock may be encountered buried underneath
the clinker debris accumulations

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our rockfall hazard evaluation conducted for the project it appears there
may be four potentially viable options for rockfall hazard reduction and remediation of the
slope erosion and potential rockfall hazard problem The remedial options include the
following
Option 1 Roadway Realignment Stabilization of Existing Slope
Option 2 Stabilization of Existing Slope Only
Option 3 Cut Slope Setback
Option 4 Roadway Realignment Partial Slope Setback
Option 1 Roadway Realignment Stabilization of Existing Slope
This remedial option entails the realignment of the existing highway in the makai
direction away from the existing rock cut slope to reduce the risk for encroachment of
erosion debris and potential rockfall on the northbound travel lane We understand that the
purpose for the roadway realignment is to improve the existing roadway intersection with
Napoopoo Road The roadway realignment would also develop a new widened shoulder
buffer zone suitable for the retention of future clinker debris and potential rockfall materials
adjacent to the edge of pavement We estimate the minimum width of unpaved buffer
zone extending from the existing cut slope toe toward the new edge of pavement should
be approximately 20 feet wide A minimum 20 foot wide catchment zone is desirable due
to the large rock block size observed comprising the existing cut slope face
To reduce the potential for additional erosion of the existing basaltic clinker layer
and further undercutting of the basalt rock above the loose clinker debris mantling the
clinker layer should be removed The remaining clinker exposure should be stabilized by
placement of Grouted Rubble Paving GRP or concrete to backfill the undercut void and
improve the support of the overlying dense rock material The GRP backfill should provide
a uniform natural appearance on the slope face compared to concrete backfill
Several very large blocks of dense rock with wide open rock joints were observed
We recommend installing rock bolts through the blocks of rock to reduce the potential for
block separation from the parent rock outcrop The rock bolts should consist of a 3 inch
diameter drilled hole filled with cementitious grout with a galvanized steel reinforcing bar
We envision utilizing rock bolts of about 10 to 20 feet long
As indicated previously we understand a private developer and the County of
Hawaii has preliminary plans to realign the roadway and reconfigure the intersection with
Napoopoo Road at the project area Based on our review of the preliminary realignment
plan it appears the planned realignment is favorable with respect to the development of a
shoulder buffer zone along the section of cut slope addressed by our site evaluation The
available distance from the edge of the new roadway to the existing cut slope would range

Primary advantages of Option 1 include the following 1 improvement of roadway
safety by increasing the site distance and providing additional lanes for turns
2 development of a rock catchment zone along the mauka shoulder fronting the existing
rock cut slope 3 stabilization of the undercut and basaltic clinker layer to reduce the
potential for future large block rockfall from the cut slope and 4 securing open jointed
blocks of rock with rock bolts to reduce the potential for future block separation from the
parent rock outcrop A conceptual sketch of Option 1 is presented on Plate 3 1
The estimated construction cost of the stabilization of the existing lower cut slope
and the rock bolting is about 170 000 The estimated construction cost does not include
cost for traffic control permits and construction management Because the roadway
realignment to improve sight distance at the project area is currently in design the
roadway realignment cost was not included A breakdown of the estimated construction
cost is provided on Plate 4
Option 2 Stabilization of Existing Slope Only
This remedial option entails keeping the existing roadway alignment stabilizing the
lower cut slope and rock bolting the open jointed blocks of rock The purpose for the slope
stabilization is to remove the loose clinker debris and backfill the undercut voids to reduce
the basaltic clinker erosion and provide better base support for the overlying dense rock
formation In addition the rock bolts should assist in reducing the potential for block
separation from the parent rock outcrop The lower cut slope stabilization method would
be the same as discussed for Option 1 A conceptual sketch of Option 2 is presented on
Plate 3 2
A primary advantage of Option 2 is the lower cost to stabilize the existing cut slope
only A primary disadvantage is that a designated rock catchment zone would not be
developed for the retention of potential large block rockfall which could occur as a result of
another large earthquake despite the stabilization of the cut slope The estimated
construction cost of the stabilization of the existing lower cut slope and the rock bolting is
about 170 000
Option 3 Cut Slope Setback
This remedial option entails keeping the existing roadway alignment and developing
a setback of the existing cut slope face for a minimum distance of about 150 linear feet
along the roadway between about and as shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The purpose for the slope setback is to develop a new cut slope face with a more
stable slope inclination and remove the existing undercut condition and local region of
open jointed rock which may be susceptible to future large block rockfall

Based on our observation of the existing slope and the location of existing rock
joints it appears that a minimum setback of about 15 feet measured horizontally from the
existing bottom exposure of dense rock material is desirable to improve the stability of the
dense rock portion of the slope It may also be desirable to extend the cut slope setback
toward the north to encompass an adjacent section of steep rock cut slope on the existing
roadway curve The length of cut slope encompassed by the extended slope setback is
estimated at about 230 lineal feet New exposures of weak clinker materials along the
slope toe should be stabilized with GRP to reduce the potential for appreciable future
clinker erosion and undermining A conceptual sketch of Option 3 is presented on
Plate 3 3
Primary advantages are stabilization of both the lower basaltic clinker layer and the
overlying dense basalt rock development of a widened shoulder and improvement of the
site distance along the existing road curve Primary disadvantages include higher cost and
the need to extend the roadway Right of Way or acquire a slope easement to
encompass the new slope setback limits Because of the extended time needed to obtain
the necessary Right of Way or slope easement we believe Option 2 should be
performed for the interim remediation The estimated construction cost of increasing the
cut slope setback is about 620 000 The cost for obtaining the additional Right of Way
or slope easement should be added to this option
Option 4 Roadway Realignment Partial Slope Setback
This remedial option consists of the roadway realignment as previously discussed
for Option 1 In addition portions of the existing cut slope would be set back at varying
depths of about 5 and 15 feet where dense rock undercut conditions occur Where no
undercut condition exists the slope would not be set back except to create a cut slope
transition between adjacent new cut slope segments The purpose for the partial slope
setback with varying depth is to remove only the sections of existing cut slope where the
undercuts and open jointed rock is most pronounced
We anticipate a minimum 15 foot cut slope setback between about and
about where loose clinker dense rock undercuts a lava tube feature and open
jointed rock conditions exist Furthermore we anticipate a minimum 5 foot cut slope
setback between about and about where loose clinker and some
limited dense rock undercuts exist The horizontal slope setback depth should extend into
the slope from the existing undercut location toe of existing dense rock layer as depicted
on Plate 3 4
The purpose of the partial slope setback is to remove only the segments of cut
slope where existing clinker erosion dense rock undercuts and open jointed dense rock
conditions are observed A relatively short length of existing cut slope measuring
approximately 25 lineal feet along the roadway does not appear to require a remedial
setback However a cut slope transition may be necessary to connect the adjacent slope
setbacks

Preliminary Recommendations
We believe that Options 1 3 and 4 are viable long term remedial solutions for the
project area We believe that Option 2 should be considered only for interim remediation
Because of the lower construction cost and the roadway realignment planned for the area
the recommended remedial solution is Option 1 followed by Option 4 Because Option 2 is
part of Option 1 we recommend implementing Option 2 for interim remediation until the
construction of the new roadway based on the preliminary plans reviewed
Based on the available topographic plan we anticipate that a construction
easement will be necessary to perform the slope stabilization for Options 1 and 2 because
portions of the existing slope needing repair are outside of the existing County
