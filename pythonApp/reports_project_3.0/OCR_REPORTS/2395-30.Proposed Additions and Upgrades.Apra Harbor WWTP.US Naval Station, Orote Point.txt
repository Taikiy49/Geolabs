INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed additions and upgrades of the existing Apra Harbor
Wastewater Treatment Plant at the U S Naval Station Orote Point on the island of Guam
Mariana Islands The general location and vicinity of the site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommen
dations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
The recommendations presented herein are intended for foundation design input and may
not be appropriate to form a basis in the preparation of construction cost estimates nor
for construction bidding purposes

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The project site is located on the western central portion of the island of Guam the
largest and southernmost of the Mariana Islands The approximate location of the project
site is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The foundation of the island is composed of volcanic rocks including pyroclastic
materials which were extruded during early to mid Tertiary time Volcanic activity ceased
during the Oligocene epoch Following the cessation of the volcanic activity extensive
coral algal reef deposits were laid down on the island from the Miocene through
Pleistocene epochs
Tectonic uplift has raised these old reef deposits and subsequent marine activity
created lagoonal environments where fine grained sediments accumulated During the
recent century development of the military facility has shaped the existing land by grading
and filling

Liquefaction
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In this
process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such as during
an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in
pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build
up of pore water pressure the effective stress internal strength of the soils is reduced
Under sustained vibration the pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden
pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as
a viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both
horizontal and vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant
deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that liquefaction
will not occur if the deposit is greater than 50 feet below the ground surface In deeper
deposits the greater overburden pressure is generally sufficient to prevent liquefaction
from occurring The major factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of a soil are
as follows
1 Grain_Size_ Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2395 30 Page 9
3 Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake
In general the subsurface information obtained from the drilled borings appears
to indicate that the site is underlain by medium dense to dense sandy coral and limestone
gravel at the various depths sampled Based on our laboratory test results the
underlying granular soils are generally moderately to well graded Therefore on the basis
of our exploration data and laboratory test results we believe that the subsoils at this site
are not highly susceptible to liquefaction

Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy of
at least 45 000 foot pounds The hammer should be equipped with an energy level
control such that driving within the loose soft soil may be controlled at a lower
energy to reduce the tensile stress development within the piles which could
damage or cause cracking of the piles
The contractor should forward pile and driving equipment data forms to Geolabs
Hawaii prior to construction for evaluation Geolabs Hawaii should review the
submittal and assess the driveability of piles with the use of the selected hammer
Due to possible variation in the length of the piles unit prices should be obtained
during bidding for add ons shorter piles etc
Pile Driving Criteria
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption with the exception of
splicing if needed A pile may be rejected or its design capacity may be reduced
when the driving resistance is interrupted for more than four hours and the pile
cannot be driven to the required depth
During soft driving i e when the pile tip is within soft loose soils the hammer
stroke or energy should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile
stresses do not develop within the pile
Because local variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the
bearing soils the supporting capacity of each pile must be confirmed in the field
by our office during driving This can be accomplished by using a driving

Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using

D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core
run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory tests were performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in evaluating pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in evaluating certain properties such
as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight
of the tested sample and dry unit weight are shown at the appropriate sample depths on
the Boring Logs
Atterberg Limits tests ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 are used to evaluate the
plasticity of the fine grained portion of soils The Liquid Limit and Plasticity Index of the
material are used to classify the soil under the Unified Soil Classification System The
results of these tests are shown on the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
and are summarized on Plate B 5
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of the soil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining
to the performance or suitability of a soil
Three Unconfined Compression tests ASTM Test Method D 2166 85 were
performed on selected cohesive soil samples A summary of the compressive strengths
is presented on Plate B 1
Two organic content tests ASTM Test Method D 2974 87 were performed to
evaluate the amount of organic matter in peats The test results are summarized on Plate
B 1
Six sieve analyses were performed on selected samples to evaluate the gradation
characteristics of the soils The test results are plotted on Plates B 3 and B 4
Two consolidation tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate its
compressibility characteristics The test results are plotted on Plates B 6 and B 7
