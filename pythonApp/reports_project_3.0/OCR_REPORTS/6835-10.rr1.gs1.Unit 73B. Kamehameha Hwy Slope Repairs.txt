SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our exploratory borings at the project site generally encountered a surface fill layer 
of about 1-foot thick overlying alluvial deposits to 8.5 feet deep.  The alluvium was 
underlain by residual and saprolitic soils, and basalt rock formation extending to the 
maximum depth explored of 45.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The fill layer 
consisted of about 5 to 11 inches of asphaltic concrete, 5 inches of Portland cement 
concrete, and dense sandy gravel.  The alluvium was composed of medium stiff to hard 
silty clay/clayey silt with some gravel.  The residual and saprolitic soils consisted of stiff to 
hard silty clay/clayey silt with sand and gravel.  The basalt rock formation was medium 
hard to hard.  We encountered groundwater in two of the drilled borings at depths of about 
29.8 and 33.7 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration.  
The groundwater depths correspond to elevations of about -0.3 feet and +8 feet Mean Sea 
Level, respectively. 
Roadway distresses consisting of longitudinal and alligator cracking, and 
depressions were observed along the makai traffic lane of Kamehameha Highway. Since 
the existing lower slopes at the distressed area are relatively steep with inclinations of up 
to about one and three-tenths horizontal to one vertical (1.3H:1V) within the silty 
clay/clayey silt soils, we believe that the potential cause of the observed roadway surface 
distresses may be attributed to the downslope creeping of the clay subgrade soils.  In 
addition, surface water entering into the cracks on the pavement surface softens the clay 
subgrade soils and increases the downslope creep of the roadway.    
Remedial measures consisting of the installation of a permanent wall system is 
recommended. The wall heights were estimated to be about 5 to 7 feet high.  Several wall 
systems were evaluated and included the installation of conventional walls with shallow 
spread footing, retaining walls supported by micropiles and a Geosynthetic Reinforced Soil 
(GRS) wall system. Because of the limited space available for construction of the new wall, 
we believe that the installation of a retaining wall supported by micropiles or a GRS wall 
system should be considered for the project. 
The retaining walls supported by micropiles include a relatively narrow pile cap 
(footing). Therefore, the amount of excavation is reduced compared to conventional 
retaining walls. The critical loading condition for the micropiles is due to lateral loading 
from the earth and traffic surcharge loading. Based on our preliminary analysis, two rows 
of micropiles spaced at about 5 feet on-centers and with a minimum diameter of 5.5 inches

Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Rehabilitation of City Streets, Unit 73B Kamehameha Highway - 
Slope Repairs project in the Kaneohe area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes our findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations resulting from our field exploration, laboratory testing, field 
observations, and engineering analyses for the proposed project. The recommendations 
presented herein are intended for the repair of the distressed roadway section and 
pavement design purposes only. The findings and recommendations presented herein 
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project is part of the Rehabilitation of City Streets Unit 73 Kamehameha 
Highway, Haiku Road to Kahekili Highway project. The project site is along 
Kamehameha Highway near He`eia State Park in the Kaneohe area on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1 and the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The existing Kamehameha Highway at the project site consists of an asphaltic 
concrete paved roadway with one traffic lane in each direction and is located on a 
relatively steep slope.  The makai and mauka sides of the roadway slopes downward 
and upward, respectively.  
The major area of distress was observed along the makai side of the roadway for 
a distance of about 460 linear feet. Longitudinal and alligator cracks and depressions 
were observed on the makai lane pavement surface. In addition, some slope erosion 
was observed at the downslope face.  The ground surface slopes downward beyond the 
edge of the makai side pavement at inclinations ranging from about two horizontal to 
one vertical (2H:1V) to about 1.3H:1V.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island.  The 
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) 
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  The Koolau 
Volcano reached the end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation.  As the continental glaciers advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below the present 
stand.  When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to above the present level.  
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations.  The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas 
and fans of terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef 
deposits at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine 
sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused 
streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the 
sediments and calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and 
lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials.  In addition, renewed 
subaerial erosion of the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial 
soils under relatively high energy conditions. 
In some of the mountainous regions of Hawaii, chemical weathering has altered 
the basaltic flows to a soil-like condition.  Erosional processes, consisting of detachment 
of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and transport downslope toward the axis 
of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium, have also altered the natural condition of 
the volcanic flows.  Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a

When alluvial/colluvial deposits are left in place for long periods of time, chemical

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The roadway remediation project site is adjacent to the Heeia Pond area on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The distressed area along Kamehameha Highway is about 
460 linear feet in length.  The distressed area is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
The existing Kamehameha Highway within the project site is generally about 
20 feet wide and consists of one traffic lane in each direction with an asphaltic concrete 
paved surface. Numerous cracks were observed on the pavement surface at the 
distress area.  In addition, settlement of the pavement surface was observed.  The 
surface cracks and ground settlement were observed within the makai lane near the top 
of the lower slope.  
The existing ground elevations along the roadway vary from about +25 feet Mean 
Sea Level (MSL) to +32 feet MSL at the project site.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our borings, the project site is generally underlain by a thin surface fill 
layer overlying alluvial deposits to 8.5 feet deep.  The alluvium was underlain by 
residual and saprolitic soils, and basalt rock formation extending to the maximum depth 
explored of about 45.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  Groundwater was 
encountered in the drilled borings at about 29.8 and 33.7 feet depth.  
Roadway distress was observed along the downslope (makai) side of 
Kamehameha Highway. Remedial measures consisting of the installation of a 
permanent wall system is recommended.  Several wall systems were evaluated for the 
permanent repair and included the installation of conventional retaining walls with 
shallow spread footing, retaining walls supported by micropiles, and geosynthetic 
reinforced soil (GRS) wall system. 
Detailed discussions of these items and our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Potential Causes of Distressed Roadway 
The distress area exhibited longitudinal cracks and depressions at the pavement 
surface near the top of the slope along the makai lane of the roadway.  The existing 
lower slope inclination ranged from about two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) to 
1.3H:1V.  Based on the borings, silty clay/clayey silt soils were encountered near the 
upper area of the lower slope. 
Since the existing slopes at the distressed area are relatively steep with 
inclinations up to 1.3H:1V, we believe that the potential cause of the observed surface 
cracking may be attributed to the shallow downslope creeping of the near-surface 
clayey soils.  In addition, surface water entering into the cracks on the pavement 
surface softens the clay subgrade soils and increases the downslope creep of the 
roadway.

3.2 
Remedial Measures of Distressed Roadway 
Since surface water entering into the cracks on the pavement surface may cause 
softening of the clayey soils and increase the rate of downslope creep of the roadway, 
we recommend filling the existing surface cracks and subsequent cracks with sealant 
until final repair construction.  We anticipate that the roadway needs to be open to traffic 
at the end of the workday.  Because of the limited construction area available, flattening 
of the existing slope to a more stable slope is not feasible, and the installation of a 
permanent retaining wall system is recommended for the distressed roadway area. The 
permanent wall system will be installed in a cut condition.  Several potential retaining 
wall systems were evaluated for the distress area.  These wall systems included the 
installation of conventional retaining walls, walls supported by micropiles, and a 
geosynthetic reinforced soil (GRS) wall system.  Discussion of these potential retaining 
wall systems for use as remedial measures are provided below.

3.4 
Retaining Wall System Supported on Micropiles 
The width of the wall footing may be reduced with the installation of micropiles 
below the wall footing.  The wall loading is resisted primarily by the micropiles instead of 
the footing. 
In general, the micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter (usually 
less than 12 inches), drilled and grouted, pile with steel reinforcing.  The micropile 
foundation is typically constructed by drilling a hole (with or without casing), placing 
reinforcing steel in the hole, and grouting the hole.  Micropiles are desirable because 
they can be installed readily in access restrictive environments and in numerous soil 
types and ground conditions.  In addition, installation of the micropiles generally causes 
minimal disturbance to adjacent structures, the adjacent soils, and the environment. 
The distressed area was divided into two wall type sections (Wall Types I and II). 
Wall Types I and II have retaining wall heights up to 5 and 7 feet, and about 160 and 
300 linear feet in length, respectively. The extent of the two wall type sections is shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

3.5 
Geosynthetic Reinforcement Soil (GRS) Wall System 
The GRS wall is a composite wall system, which utilizes high-density 
polyethylene, or other reinforcing elements, to reinforce the backfill zone and improve 
the shear strength of the reinforced soil zone. This composite system essentially forms 
a gravity wall structure with an ability to tolerate significant total and differential 
settlements. In addition, GRS walls also are desirable due to the flexibility of the wall, 
ease of construction, high load carrying capacity, and economy.  The GRS wall system 
typically utilizes a Concrete Masonry Unit (CMU) facing. 
GRS technology consists of closely spaced layers of geosynthetic reinforcement 
and compacted granular fill material.  The components of a GRS system generally consist 
of: (1) a row of blocks (the facing elements), (2) a layer of compacted granular fill to the 
height of the facing blocks, and (3) a layer of geosynthetic reinforcement.  A sketch of the 
proposed GRS wall system is presented on Plate 4. 
The facing is not a structural element of a GRS mass. The most commonly used 
facing element for GRS walls is the concrete masonry unit (CMU) with nominal dimensions 
of 8 inches by 8 inches by 16 inches.  CMU blocks are lightweight, easy to place, and 
ensure compaction at every 8-inch lift before placement of the next geosynthetic layer.  
The geosynthetic reinforcement extends directly beneath each layer of CMU blocks as a 
frictional connection.  The CMU should have a minimum compressive strength of 
2,000 pounds per square inch (psi) and a water absorption limit of 5 percent.  The top 
three courses of the CMU blocks should be pinned with an epoxy-coated No. 4 rebar and 
grouted. 
The wall subgrades should be proof-rolled and recompacted to a firm and 
unyielding surface. Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the GRS

3.6.3 Pavement Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the subbase and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface 
should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface 
water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. Surface water ponding 
should not be allowed during or after construction. Development of good shoulder 
drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage structures is 
strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or 
premature failure of the pavements.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to depths of about 31.5 to 
45.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped 
with continuous flight augers and coring tools.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550/D3350M, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil sample 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material

shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Four Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-2 through B-5. 
Three Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) 
were performed on selected in-situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear 
strengths of the soils.  The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressures were 
used as the applied confining pressures for the relatively “undisturbed” soil samples.  
The test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-6 through B-8. 
Two Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were 
performed on selected rock cores to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of 
the rock formation encountered. Test results are presented on Plate B-9. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio Test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a 
bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils.  The test results are presented on Plate B-10.
