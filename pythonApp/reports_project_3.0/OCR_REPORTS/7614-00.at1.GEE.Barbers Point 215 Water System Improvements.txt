SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our drilled borings along the Old Farrington Highway (Borings 1 through 6) 
generally encountered 2 to 10 inches of AC overlying about 1 to 3.5 feet of fill material. 
The fill material was generally underlain by alluvial deposits to 15.5 feet depth. It should 
be noted that Boring No. 6 encountered a basalt formation at about 6.5 feet depth, 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 21.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. Our drilled borings near the existing water tanks (Boring Nos. 7 and 8) generally 
encountered 1 to 8.5 feet of fill material underlain by colluvium and residual soils to a 
depth of 17 feet below the existing ground surface.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, perched 
groundwater, and other factors. 
We anticipate new water and drain line inverts will be about 5 to 12 feet below the 
existing ground surface. Installation of the proposed water and drain lines will involve 
excavation into the underlying fill, alluvium, colluvium, residual soil, and hard basalt 
formation. In general, we anticipate that the in-situ soils along the proposed water and 
drain line alignments may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. 
However, trench excavations into the underlying cobbles and boulders and/or basalt rock 
formation may require chipping and/or the use of hoerams to remove the materials and 
to achieve the design invert elevations. 
In general, the excavated on-site soils encountered during our field exploration may 
be used in the upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the pavement subgrade, provided that the materials are free of vegetation, deleterious 
materials, organic material, clay lumps, and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in 
maximum dimension. 
Design of metallic substructures, such as waterline pipes or other metallic piping, 
should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. Based on 
the laboratory resistivity results, the subsurface soils at the project site have resistivity 
values ranging from about 900 to 3,000 ohm-cm and are considered moderately to 
severely corrosive in accordance with the Board of Water Supply Water System External 
Corrosion Control Standards dated July 28, 2021.

1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Barbers Point 215 Water System Improvements project in Ewa on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses for the water system improvements project. These recommendations are 
intended for the design of trench excavation, backfilling, pavement restoration, drainage 
structures and thrust block/vault design, and corrosion potential only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The Barbers Point 215 Water System Improvements project is located mauka of the 
Old Farrington Highway and west of the Wet ‘n Wild Water Park in the Ewa District on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project consists of the installation of approximately 425 linear 
feet (LF) of water mains from the Old Farrington Highway to Reservoir 1 and then to 
Reservoir 2. In addition, we understand that approximately 796 LF of drain line along the 
Old Farrington Highway is planned to carry the runoff water from the project site to the 
nearby Awanui Gulch. The drainage system will include new manholes, vaults, and outfall 
structures. Based on the provided project plans, we believe that the water line inverts range 
from 8 to 12 feet below the existing ground surface.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. 
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice 
Age) in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. As volcanic 
activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked against its 
eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau known as Schofield Plateau. 
Following the extrusion of lavas in the early Pleistocene Epoch, the island 
underwent a long cycle of erosion and weathering forming the prominent ridgelines and 
summits as we know today. During the erosion period, the Island of Oahu began to slowly 
subside by more than 1,200 feet in elevation, resulting in the drowning and sedimentation 
of the valleys and the formation of the steep Koolau Pali. Coral reefs continued to grow 
in the surrounding shallow waters of the island. 
From the mid to late Pleistocene Epoch, the sea level rose and repeatedly fell in 
response to global glaciation and the availability of surface waters to sustain the oceans. 
The various sea level elevations and their representative deposits are known as “stands” 
and include from oldest to youngest:  the Kahuku, Kahipa, Kaena, Laie, Waialae, Waipio, 
and Waimanalo stands. Geologic deposits associated with the various sea level stands, 
including marine sediments and coral reefs, were deposited and subsequently altered or 
removed by later sea level fluctuation. Therefore, depositional records reflecting the 
changes in sea level and the occurrence of emerged coral reef deposits are often 
incomplete. 
The project site is situated on the Ewa Plain to the southeast of the Waianae 
Mountains. The Ewa Plain is a gently sloping alluvial plain formed by the deposition of 
alluvial clays and silts derived from the weathering of basalt rock further up-slope. The 
alluvial deposits accumulated and the materials interbedded with marine sediments and 
coral/algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of the 
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located north of the Old Farrington Highway and west of Wet ‘N 
Wild Water Park. There are three existing water tanks within the project limits that are 
connected to the Old Farrington Highway with a paved access road. The access road and 
the old Farrington Highway are paved with asphaltic concrete (AC) pavement. 
In general, the topography of the project site slopes downward from north to south. 
Based on the topographic survey map provided, the existing ground surface elevations 
range from approximately +184 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) near Reservoir 2 to about 
+137 feet MSL at the outfall to Awanui Gulch.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The proposed 24 and 30-inch waterlines and 24-inch drain line alignment will 
traverse areas underlain by fill, alluvium, colluvium, residual soils, and basalt rock 
formation. Boulders and cobbles were encountered within the colluvium. Basalt rock 
formation was encountered in our boring near the proposed outfall location at a depth of 
about 6.5 feet and extended to the maximum depth explored of about 21.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the 
time of our field exploration.  
We understand that the installation of the water and drain lines will generally 
consist of trench excavation, pipe placement, trench backfill, and pavement restoration. 
Detailed discussions for the design of the water system improvements project, including 
trench excavation, trench excavation support, drainage structures, pavement restoration, 
thrust block, corrosion protection, and other geotechnical aspects of the project are 
presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Trench Excavation 
Based on the information provided, we understand that the invert elevations of the 
new water and drain lines will be about 5 to 12 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Installation of the proposed water and drain lines will involve excavation into the 
underlying fill, alluvium, colluvium, residual soils and basalt formation. In general, we 
anticipate that the in-situ soils along the proposed water and drain line alignments may 
be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. However, it should be noted that 
cobbles and boulders and basalt formation were encountered. Therefore, difficult 
excavation into cobbles and boulders and basalt rock formation should be anticipated. 
Trench excavations into the cobbles and boulders and basalt rock formation may require 
chipping and/or the use of hoerams to remove the materials and achieve the design invert 
elevations. The contractor must exercise care to avoid over-ripping, which would disrupt 
the structure of the rock formation and may result in a potential loss of bearing strength 
for foundations in its vicinity.

3.2 
Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate that excavations up to about 5 to 12 feet below the ground surface 
will be required for the installation of the new water and drain lines and manhole 
structures. Deeper excavations may be required where the water and drain lines traverse 
existing underground utilities. Where deeper excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) 
are planned, temporary shoring or sloping and benching should be implemented for the 
trench excavations.  
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and 
safety of the excavation and shoring installation. The contractor’s representative should 
be on-site at all times during excavation and construction work for the opportunity to 
promptly observe changing or unforeseen conditions, such as high groundwater, 
inappropriate construction sequence or techniques, etc., which may affect the shoring 
stability. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for 
excessive ground movement. 
It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the 
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction. The properly braced shoring is 
essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent ground 
into the excavation, which may result in potential settlement or distress to adjacent 
structures or other improvements. 
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the 
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. Due 
to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the magnitude

3.3 
Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the underlying fills, alluvium, colluvium, residual soils, 
or basalt rock formation, a minimum of 6 inches of granular cushion material, such as 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) should be provided below the pipe. 
Where soft and/or loose soils are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, a 
stabilization layer consisting of an additional 18 inches of open-graded gravel, such as 
No. 3B Fine gravel, wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) 
should be provided below the cushion layer for uniform support. 
The granular cushion layer should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width 
of one-fourth (¼) of the outside pipe diameter or trench width, whichever is greater and 
should extend 12 inches above the pipe. Before the placement of cushion material, a 
Geolabs representative should observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm 
materials are exposed at the bottom of the trench or whether a stabilization layer should 
be provided below the cushion layer. 
3.4 
Trench Backfill 
In general, the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the 
Water System Standards, State of Hawaii 2002. The first zone of backfill extending from 
the cushion material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes (or 12 inches above 
the groundwater level, whichever is greater) should consist of select free-draining 
granular material (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) to reduce the compaction effort required 
and resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is critical to use this free-draining material

to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide 
adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill 
settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the pavement subgrade or finish grade should consist of the excavated on-site soils or 
imported fill materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, organic material, 
and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. It 
should be noted that the fill requirements presented herein are intended as guidelines 
only and may be modified based on Geolabs additional laboratory testing and field 
observations on the available fill materials. 
Imported fill materials used for trench backfill should be well graded from coarse 
to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension. The material should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material 
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell 
potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Geolabs 
should test the imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations. 
Aggregate base course materials required for the flexible pavement restoration 
section should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates and should conform to Section 31 
of the City and County of Honolulu Standard Specifications (September 1986). Aggregate 
subbase materials required for the rigid pavement restoration section should conform to 
Section 30 of the City and County of Honolulu Standard Specifications (September 1986). 
Geolabs should test the imported fill materials for conformance with these 
recommendations. 
3.5 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfill materials consisting of the on-site soils should be moisture-conditioned to 
at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches 
in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The 
upper 3 feet below the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction.

Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course and subbase 
materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed 
in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of 
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established 
in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by 
dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed. 
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during backfilling to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper 
moisture content. Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort 
should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the 
specified compaction.

 Backfilling Underground Utility Structures 
The underground utility structure excavations will need to be properly backfilled to 
reduce the potential for subsidence at the ground surface. The excavated on-site 
soils or imported fill materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and 
clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension may 
be used as backfill up to the finished subgrades.  
Underground utility structure backfills should be moisture-conditioned to at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 
8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction. Where structures are located below future pavement areas, the upper 
3 feet of the underground utility structures backfill below the pavement grade should 
be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum 
dry density as determined by ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

3.9 
Settlements 
Primary settlement of the water mains is normally caused by the difference in the 
unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the 
underlying subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our engineering analyses, 
settlement on the order of less than ½ inch is anticipated for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could occur if 
improper trench support is used. 
3.10 Corrosion Potential 
Design of metallic substructures, such as waterline pipes or other metallic piping, 
should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. Five sets of 
laboratory corrosion tests, including pH and minimum resistivity, were performed by our 
office to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site soils encountered. The test results are 
summarized in Appendix B. The soil resistivity values were determined in general 
accordance with ASTM G51. 
Based on the field resistivity test results, the subsurface soils at the project site 
have resistivity values ranging from about 900 to 3,000 ohm-cm. The Board of Water 
Supply recently published an updated version of the Water System External Corrosion 
Control Standards, dated July 28, 2021. The updated version is effective as of the 
publishing date for Oahu only.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling ten borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 8 and 1A and 3A, extending to depths of about 5 to 
21.5 feet below the existing pavement surface at the approximate locations shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2 We used a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight 
augers to advance the borings. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock samples were described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the 
Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-10. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration 
sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.

would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material 
is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe 
the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test results 
is 2`provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
The graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B-2. 
One Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression test (ASTM D2850) was 
performed on selected in-situ soil sample to evaluate the undrained shear strengths of 
the older alluvium soils.  The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressures were 
used as the applied confining pressures for the relatively “undisturbed” soil samples.  The 
test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plate B-3. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested.  Results of the direct 
shear test is presented on Plate B-4 
Four Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were performed on 
selected intact core runs to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the basalt 
formation encountered. The test results are presented on Plate B-5. 
One one-inch Ring Swell test was performed on relatively undisturbed sample to 
evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils under different surcharge loads.  
The test results are summarized on Plate B-6. 
Five sets of corrosion tests, including pH (SW 9045W), minimum resistivity 
(EPA 120.1), chloride content (EPA 325.2), and sulfate content (EPA 375.2), were 
performed by E.L. Pacific on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration.  
The results of these tests are summarized on Plate B-7. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-8 and B-9.
