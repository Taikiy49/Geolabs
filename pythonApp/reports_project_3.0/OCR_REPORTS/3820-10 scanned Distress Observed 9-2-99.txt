Dear Mr Aiello
We understand that the lower portion of the slope may be covered with shotcrete
to reduce the potential for rock fall hazards at Lot and 108
To reduce rock fall hazards from the upper slope which will not be shotcreted we
recommend that either a rock fall protection netting material be provided at the upper
slope or a 5 foot high chain link fence be provided at the slope bench Otherwise the
entire slope should be covered with shotcrete
We believe that the installation of a chain link fence would be easier to construct
and be more cost effective Installation of a rock fall protection netting will likely be
difficult and expensive as installation of rock anchors will be necessary Placement of
shotcrete over the upper slope will likely be expensive as embedment into the soil
material at the top of the slope will be necessary to allow proper anchoring and to reduce
the potential for water infiltration Embedment at the top of the slope will be more difficult
due to the presence of concrete swales at the tops of the slopes

For the option of placing shotcrete at the lower slope and installing a chain link
fence on the slope bench we offer the following recommendations A typical section view
is shown on Plate 1
1
Prior to placing shotcrete we recommend that the rock surface be shot with
water to remove the loose rock pieces The remaining loose rock pieces
should be picked off by the contractor
The shotcrete should be placed 2 to 3 inches thick with fibermesh mixed
into the concrete material Four inch diameter weepholes spaced 8 feet
on center horizontally as well as vertically should be placed on the rock
surface and held in place by the shotcrete on the outside surface of the
pipe The opening adjacent to the rock slope should be covered with a
geotextile fabric AMOCO 4546 or similar These weepholes should help
to drain water within the rock reducing the development of high pore
pressures which may cause a pop out or spalling of the shotcrete
The shotcrete may be colored to improve its appearance The coloring can
be added either into the shotcrete mix or stained onto the finished product
It should be noted that it may weather with time either discolor or wash
away
To reduce the possibility of water infiltration behind the shotcrete and to
properly anchor the shotcrete from peeling off we recommend that the
shotcrete be embedded a minimum of 12 inches at the toe and 6 inches
thick at the slope bench Wire fabric reinforcement should be used in the
concrete on the slope bench
The poles for the chain link fence should be a minimum of 3 inches in
diameter and should be embedded a minimum of 30 inches below the
finished ground surface The 12 inch thick concrete may be considered as a
part of the embedment depth The poles should be spaced no more than
5 feet apart and should be at least 36 inches away from the top of slope
The chain link fencing material should be placed on the inside face of the
poles so that the poles are exposed Colored fencing may be considered
to improve its appearance Note that impact forces from rocks up to
24 inches in diameter were used in the fence design

Site Observations and Soil Testing

Dear Ms Courtney
In accordance with your request a site visit was made to the above referenced lot
on February 16 to observe the existing site conditions and obtain subsurface soil
samples to evaluate the reported house movements This letter presents the results of
our laboratory testing and recommendations to reduce the potential future house
movements
Site Observations
The subject house is a_ single story steel framed structure with concrete
slab on grade foundations During a brief visit inside the house small hairline cracks
were observed in the drywall at various locations The cracks seemed more prevalent
along the side walls of the house than the interior walls A slightly larger crack was noted
in the garage slab where the thickness of the concrete changes for the landings at the
door
The ground adjacent to the house concrete slab appeared to slope properly away
from the house foundations Ponding of water near the house was not evident A strip of
gravel was observed adjacent to and around the house concrete slab The gravel was
contained by a plastic edging strip which separated the gravel from the lawn
Exploration
A hand dug test pit was excavated adjacent to the perimeter footing on each side
of the house The footing depths were measured and observed to be about 12 inches
below the ground surface in accordance with the plans The soils encountered consisted
of medium stiff to stiff brown silty clay in a moist to very moist condition
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory swell tests were performed on soil samples obtained below the footing
bottom in the test pits The swell tests consisted of placing a 2 4 inch diameter by one
inch high soil sample in a testing apparatus with a surcharge load of 55 psf to simulate
the field conditions The samples were then submerged in water to measure the swelling
potential under extreme conditions The measured swell of the samples were 0 3 and
0 7 percent which are considered low

March 7 2001
Another swell test was conducted by first drying the soil then compacting it to
simulate the field conditions after site grading The sample was surcharged with a load of
55 psf and allowed to air dry overnight then saturated The swell measured in this test
was 4 5 percent which is also considered low
Findings and Recommendations
The following is a summary of our opinions and recommendations based on our
observations exploration and laboratory test results
e The drywall cracking observed inside the house was small and does not
compromise the structural integrity of the house
e The drywall cracking may be due to differential heaving of the slab caused
by changes in moisture content of the soils under the house probably from
landscape watering and rainfall
e The soils under the house appear to be moist and near equilibrium
