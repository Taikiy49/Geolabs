REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These include the following Kohala which has been long extinct Mauna Kea
which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801
and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the eastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield Volcano
which has been built up by basaltic lava flows These flows are of the Pliocene to
Pleistocene Epochs and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series
The lava flows were subsequently covered by volcanic ash deposits known locally as
Pahala Ash

The proposed project is located within the property of the Hawaii National Guard
on the southeastern side of Circle Avenue southwest of Earl s Avenue in Hilo on the
Island of Hawaii The site is located southwest of a fuel storage shed a fuel pump a ramp

manicured grass The site is fairly flat with a ground elevation of about 69 to 71 feet

FIELD PERCOLATION TESTING
Falling head percolation tests were performed in each of the two borings to evaluate
the permeability of the subsurface materials The results of our percolation tests are
presented in Appendix C of this report
Based on the results of our field percolation tests we recommend that the drywell be
corresponds to a disposal rate of about 7 9 x 10 cfs per square foot 510 gallons per square
foot per day For the purpose of design we recommend that only the bottom area of the
drywell be used to determine the capacity The design depth of the drywell should be based
on the anticipated amount of water retained prior to the water percolating into the ground
It should be noted that some siltation may occur and therefore the permeability of the
soil will reduce over time We therefore recommend that this occurrence be accounted for
in the design of the drywell and some type of maintenance program be implemented

Site Preparation
Site preparation operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Inc
It is important that a representative from our office observe the site preparation operations
to evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation process
and to confirm that the exposed soil conditions are similar to those encountered in our field
exploration
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation organic debris construction debris rubbish
old pavements and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly
off site or stock piled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation
should be properly backfilled with on site fill material placed in 6 to 8 inch level loose lifts and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction However the upper 2 feet from
finish subgrade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Utilities to be abandoned in place under the proposed slab should be backfilled by pumping
lean concrete under low pressure
Soft and yielding areas encountered should be over excavated to expose firm natural
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with fill placed in 12 inch
maximum loose lifts moisture conditioned to at least the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction ASTM D 1557 91 The excavated
GEOLABS INC
W O 4182 00 C Page 7
soft soils should be disposed of properly off site and or used in landscape areas where
appropriate
At the finish subgrade the existing ground should be scarified to a depth of 10 inches
and moisture conditioned to at least the optinum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Saturation and subsequent yielding of the
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation
of the soft areas and replacement with fill placed in 12 inch maximum loose lifts
moisture conditioned to at least optimum moisture content and compacted to at least
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
The fill material should consist of well graded low expansive materials less than
3 inches in largest dimension The fill material should also be free of vegetation and
deleterious materials In general the on site silty basalt gravel and basalt gravel material
should be suitable for fill material
Imported fill should consist of non expansive granular material such as crushed coral
mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with
no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory
CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 Imported fill materials should be tested
by Geolabs Inc for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project
site for its intended use
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the subgrade soils are critical
elements of earthwork observations and soil density tests should be performed by
Geolabs Inc during the site preparation operation to assist the contractor in obtaining the

required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content Where compaction is less
than required additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture
content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction
Slabs On Grade
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by
near surface silty basalt gravel and basalt gravel soils with low swelling potential The area
adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab and graded to divert water
away from the slab to reduce the potential of water ponding around the slab
For the design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 275 pounds per
square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted subgrade material
Based on the anticipated vehicle loads and the soil conditions we recommend a minimum
slab thickness of 8 5 inches Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the
slab joints which are subjected to vehicular traffic The thickened edges of slabs adjacent
to any unpaved area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent
grade
Subgrade soils should be prepared in accordance with the recommendations
presented in the Site Preparation section of this report

Asphaltic concrete pavements are anticipated for access roads and possible parking
areas Based on the on site silty basalt gravel and basalt gravel subgrade materials the
following pavement section may be used for preliminary budgeting purposes until the finish
subgrade material is verified and laboratory testing of this material is conducted

Subgrade soils should be prepared in accordance with the recommendations
presented in the Site Preparation section of this report Base course materiais should
consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to not less than 95 percent relative
compaction ASTM D and density tests should be performed on the actual
subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above

Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all surface
water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere on the site during
or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent
to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs extend a minimum of 2 inches into the
subgrade soil to reduce migration of landscape water into the pavement section
Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect excessive water from
landscaping irrigation For long term performance we recommend a subdrain system be
constructed adjacent to the paved landscaped area
Utilities
It is anticipated that some utilities may be constructed for the proposed project The
methods and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the contractor
subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavation should comply with all
applicable local state and federal safety requirements Trench shoring design and
installation should be the responsibility of the contractor Trench shoring and bracing should
conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under
the pipes The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should consist of
free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the
pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining granular material be
used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to
provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
GEOLABS INC
W O 4182 00 C Page 10
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
finish subgrade should consist of the approved on site soils or select granular material The
backfill material should be moisture conditioned to at least the optimum moisture content
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence The
upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to not
less than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical compaction equipment should be used
to compact the materials at the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding should not
be used to compact the backfill material
It is our understanding that an 8 inch diameter cast iron pipe is planned for the area
with only a 12 inch thick soil cover For those lines near anticipated vehicular traffic routes
we recommend that the pipes be encased above and below pipe with either concrete or
CLSM low strength concrete or flowable fill If it is desired to allow possible future
excavation with normal construction equipment then CLSM with a compressive strength of
50 to 250 psi should be utilized Note that the weight of the pipe should be greater than the
bouyant force to prevent movement of the pipe during backfill placement
In lieu of encasement of the pipes 36 inch wide 6 inch thick concrete slab could be
constructed at the ground surface above the pipe to serve as a mat that spreads out the
vehicle loads

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations ASTM D 2937 were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
One 1 Sieve Analysis Test ASTM D 136 C 117 and D 1140 was performed on a
selected sample of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in
soil classification Results of this test are presented on Plate B 1
One 1 California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement
subgrade support Results of the CBR test are presented on Plate B 2
One 1 Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on the
near surface soils Results of this test are presented on Plate B 3

APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
The falling head percolation testing was performed in general accordance with the
Auger Hole method This method is a variation of the borehole method in which water is
added to fill an auger hole at a given level and then the fall of the water level is observed
with time The permeability is based on the assumption that a unit hydraulic head gradient
causes the flow through the bottom and sides of the hole This method is expected to give
only a very approximate indication of the actual permeability of the soil
Prior to conducting the falling head percolation tests the holes were filled with water
in an attempt to saturate the surrounding soils The saturation phase was continued until the
amount of head drop per a unit of time became fairly constant
