2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene
to early Pleistocene in age
The project site is situated on the Ewa Coastal Plain which extends seaward off
the southeastern flank of Waianae and the southwestern flank of Koolau This plain
was formed by the accumulation of a thick wedge of sediments and coral reef
formations on the flanks of the two volcanoes
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that
the sea level rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of
the calcareous reef materials
The soils and rocks underlying the project site were described by H T Stearns
and K N Vaksik as presented below

2 4 Site Description
The project site is to the south of the City of Kapolei in the Kapolei area on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii The project site is former sugar cane plantation land and is
bordered approximately by the southwestern terminus of the existing Kapolei Parkway

Barrette Road to the east and the proposed historic Pearl Harbor Trail and Kalaeloa

generally slopes down toward the southeast with existing ground surface elevations
ranging from about 45 feet Mean Sea Level MSL in the former location of the

northwestern corner of the project site

Based on the subsurface conditions that our work at the site has encountered it

3 3 Slabs on Grade
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend
placing a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below the slabs The open graded gravel cushion fill
would serve as a capillary moisture break and would provide more uniform support of
the slabs To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration through the slab and
subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier is recommended
on top of the No 3B Fine gravel cushion layer Flexible floor coverings such as carpet
or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking It
is also recommend that interior walls be designed to incorporate some flexibility to
accommodate a small amount of possible ground movements
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as garage floor slabs
we recommend providing a 6 inch layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs in lieu of
the 4 inch thick gravel cushion layer The moisture barrier may also be omitted for
these slabs The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basalt aggregate
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction For the design of structural
slabs supported on aggregate subbase a modulus of subgrade reaction of about
250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted
aggregate subbase

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation

3 6 Basic Utility Installation
We anticipate that basic utility installation for the proposed project may consist
primarily of water sewer and drain lines to provide backbone infrastructure to support
future subdivision development In general good construction practices should be utilized
for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities at the site The
contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used for trench excavation
subject to practical limits and safety considerations In addition the excavations should
comply with the applicable federal state and local safety requirements The contractor
should be responsible for trench shoring design and installation
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that much of the utility
trench excavations will likely encounter engineered fill overlying coral formation which
may pose some excavation difficulties during the trench excavation In addition the
contractor should anticipate significant over break of the sides of the trench excavations
considering the nature of the materials at the site This over break condition may be
minimized by the use of trenching machines
In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended below the pipes Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should
be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the backfill thus
reducing the possibility of pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill should consist of granular material
generally less than 3 inches in maximum size compacted to at least 90 percent relative
compaction Where trenches will be located in paved areas the upper 3 feet of the
trench backfill below the pavement finished grade should be compacted to no less than
95 percent relative compaction Where the utility line crosses beneath the footings the
utility pipes should be concrete jacketed

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the drilling and sampling
four borings each to a depth of approximately 21 5 feet below the existing ground
surface The borings were drilled with a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger
equipment at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the field exploration operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general
conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log
Legend Plate A 0 1 Logs of the materials encountered are presented on Plates A 1
through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 and D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler and a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler
with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler
the second and third 6 inches of an 18 to 24 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
