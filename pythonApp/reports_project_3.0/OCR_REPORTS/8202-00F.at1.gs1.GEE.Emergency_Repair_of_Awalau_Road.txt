SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered a surface fill layer approximately 1 to 2-foot 
thick overlying alluvial deposits to depths of about 18 to 19 feet. The alluvium was 
underlain by residual soils to the maximum depth explored of about 22 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The surface fill layer generally consisted of very stiff 
clayey/sandy silt with some gravel. The alluvial deposits were composed of soft to stiff 
sandy/clayey silt with gravel; cobbles and boulders; and dense gravelly sand. The 
residual soil consisted of stiff to very stiff sandy silt. 
We encountered groundwater in the drilled borings at depths of about 5 and 5.5 
feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. Considering the 
project is located adjacent to the existing stream, the groundwater levels may vary in 
response to the water level in the stream. In addition, it should be noted that the 
groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, seasonal precipitation, surface 
water runoff, and other factors. 
A ford crossing structure will be constructed for the east bridge approach and will 
consist of a Portland Cement Concrete pavement section along Awalau Road with a 
4H:1V fill slope covered with GRP on the north side of the roadway. The following rigid 
pavement section is recommended. 
Rigid Pavement Section 
  6.5-Inch Portland Cement Concrete 
  6.0-Inch Aggregate Subbase Course (95% Relative Compaction) 
12.5-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade 
In addition, a 4-foot deep cutoff wall should be provided at the bottom of the GRP 
slope to reduce the potential for distress of the GRP slope from scour. 
For the construction of the 1H:1V slope at the west end of the ford crossing, we 
recommend a soil-nailed slope system consisting of a series of individual reinforcing bars 
grouted into drilled holes and a shotcrete facing to stabilize the steep slope face. The 
5.5-inch diameter soil nails should have a minimum embedment length of 12 feet and be 
installed at an inclination of about 15 degrees from the horizontal. The soil nails should 
be spaced at 5 feet on-center horizontally and vertically.

performed for the Emergency Repair of Awalau Road project located in the 
Kaupakalua/Makawao area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of the 
remedial measures for the project. The findings and recommendations presented herein 
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The emergency repair project is located on Awalau Road near the Awalau Gulch 
crossing in the Kaupakalua/Makawao area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii.  Based on the 
information provided, we understand that the emergency project involves repairing the 
east approach of the Awalau Bridge with a ford crossing structure and the existing Awalau

Voids are located within the existing concrete rubble masonry (CRM) channel walls

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes:  the older West Maui (Tertiary 
Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene Epoch). 
The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain between these two volcanoes. The 
project site is located on the northern flank of Haleakala. 
The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows from Haleakala ponding on 
West Maui. It is comprised of alluvium washed from the slopes of West Maui and 
East Maui (Haleakala). The erosional processes are dominated by the detachment of soil 
and rock masses from the mountain walls, and the soil materials are transported 
downslope toward the isthmus primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once these materials 
reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become dominant, 
and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium.  
In general, stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the stream 
flows transmit large volumes of water for a very short duration. Because of this, the 
transport of sediments is intermittent, and the bulk of the stream's hydraulic load consists 
of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines. When the 
erosional base levels change, these sediment loads are left as deposits. 
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time, chemical processes begin 
to alter the materials simultaneously, causing a breakdown or weathering of the materials. 
Chemical processes also cause induration, or cementation, of the coarse-grained portion 
of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate. 
Simultaneously, erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in 
headwaters. This continued erosion generates material, which is transported downslope 
covering the older alluvial soil deposits. Depending on the local base level and rate of 
transport, these newer sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time. In 
addition, their consistency and density are generally less than those of the older, partially 
consolidated deposits.

Underlying the alluvial soil deposits are overlapping lava from the West Maui and 
East Maui Volcanoes. The bulk of the Haleakala Shield Volcano was built during the late 
Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows of the 
Honomanu Volcanic Series. During the Pleistocene Epoch, the characteristics of the lava 
changed to very hard, thickly bedded flows of andesitic composition. These lavas have 
been grouped as the Kula Volcanic Series. Typically, the basalt rock formation consists 
of thinly to thickly bedded a’a and pahoehoe type lava flows. The development of the 
areas surrounding Kahului in the past several decades has brought the project site to its 
present conditions.

Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is along Awalau Road at the Awalau Gulch crossing in the 
Kaupakalua/Makawao area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. At the time of our field 
exploration, Awalau Road was cleared of debris, and concrete jersey barriers were placed 
near the washed-out roadway embankment section. Within this area, approximately 
80 linear feet of damaged roadway embankment was observed. The eroded embankment 
slope inclination was observed to be near vertical and exposed cobbles, boulders, and 
silty fill/alluvial materials.  
Based on our field observations and the topographic survey information provided, 
the roadway slopes gently from east to west with elevations ranging from about +1,240 
to +1,220 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).

3.3 
Temporary Bridge Structure 
We understand that a temporary bridge structure is planned to be constructed over 
the drainage way instead of a culvert for the project. Details of the new bridge structure 
were not available at the time of this report. 
We understand that the temporary bridge structure will be an Acrow bridge. Based 
on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we believe that shallow 
spread and/or continuous footings bearing on the recompacted in-situ soils may be used 
for support of the proposed bridge structure. Bearing pressure of up to 3,000, 4,000, and 
9,000 psf may be used for the Service Limit State, Strength Limit State, and Extreme 
Event Limit State, respectively, to design shallow foundations bearing on the 
recompacted stiff to very stiff silty clay.  
Footing subgrades should be recompacted to at least 95 percent relative 
compaction to provide a relatively firm and unyielding surface prior to the placement of 
reinforcing steel or concrete. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by 
dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of footing excavations 
should be over-excavated until stiff/dense materials are encountered or to a maximum 
depth of 2 feet. The over-excavation may be backfilled with select granular materials

moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a

In general, the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finished grade. Footings located adjacent to slopes or on 
slopes should be embedded to a sufficient depth to provide a minimum horizontal setback

Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 
1H:1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as 
deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging 
adjacent below-grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the 
potential for appreciable foundation settlement. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed as recommended herein, we 
estimate the total settlement of footings supported on the recompacted on-site soils to be 
on the order of 1 inch or less. Differential settlements between adjacent footings 
supported on similar materials should be on the order of about 0.5 inches or less. 
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed between 
the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure acting 
against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of 0.35 
and 0.28 may be used for the Extreme Event Limit State and Strength Limit State, 
respectively, for footings bearing on the recompacted in-situ soils.  Resistance due to 
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 290 and 
145 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf) for the Extreme Event Limit State and 
Strength Limit State, respectively. This assumes that the soils around the footings are 
well-compacted. Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the 
upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected.

3.4 
Retaining Structures 
We anticipate that retaining walls may be required for grade separation for the 
temporary bridge structure. In general, retaining wall foundations should be designed in 
accordance with recommendations in the “Temporary Bridge Structure” section herein. 
Wall footings should be at least 18 inches wide and should be embedded a minimum of 
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades. 
Retaining structures should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressures due 
to the adjacent soils and surcharge effects. The recommended active lateral earth 
pressure for a level backfill condition is 38 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). 
For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the structure, a rectangular 
distribution with a uniform pressure equal to 36 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure 
acting over the entire height of the structure, which is free to deflect (cantilever), may be 
used in the design. 
Retaining walls should be well-drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures. A proper drainage system should be provided behind the walls.

 Soil-Nailed Slope 
The soil-nailed slope system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars 
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize the steep slope. The reinforcing bars provide 
both additional tensile and shear strengths to the in-situ slope soil materials and 
reinforce the sidewall of the slope.  
The design of the soil-nailed slope systems will need to consider both the internal 
and external stability of the reinforced mass. The design of the internal stability 
includes establishing the size, spacing, orientation, and length of the grouted 
reinforcing bars. The external stability includes the slope stability of the reinforced 
mass. 
The soil nails should be installed by drilling a minimum 5.5-inch diameter hole with 
an inclination of approximately 15 degrees from horizontal. The soil nail bar should 
consist of ASTM A615 Grade 75 threaded bar with a minimum bar diameter of 
1.25 inches. Due to the proximity of the soil-nailed wall to the existing stream, we 
recommend using a double corrosion protection system for the nails. Epoxy coated 
bars surrounded by neat cement grout or sand-cement mixture with a minimum 
28-day compressive strength of 4,000 pounds per square inch (psi) may be 
considered. 
Based on our soil nail analysis, we recommend using a design nail length of 12 feet 
for nails extending into the alluvium and residual soil materials encountered in our 
field exploration. The soil nails should be spaced 5 feet on-center horizontally and 
vertically. The first nail should be installed about 2 feet below the top of the slope.

Based on the above design parameters, the soil-nailed slope system should have a

the static loading condition considering a surcharge load of 250 pounds per square

 Soil Nail Installation 
Potentially difficult drilling conditions may be encountered during the installation of 
the soil nails due to the presence of cobbles and boulders within the fill and alluvial 
materials encountered in the borings drilled. Utilizing a temporary casing may be 
required to maintain an open hole for the soil nail installation. 
 Soil Nail Testing 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated behind the soil-nailed slope, we 
recommend performing a minimum of one pre-production verification test on a 
sacrificial soil nail. In addition, proof tests should be performed on at least 
10 percent of the production soil nails during construction to confirm the bond 
stresses used in the design. 
The verification tests should be loaded gradually to 150 percent of the design load 
for the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of about 200 percent 
of the design load. In addition, the load at every loading increment should be held 
for at least 10 minutes, and creep tests should be held for at least 60 minutes. The 
proof tests should consist of subjecting the soil nail to at least 150 percent of the 
design load, and the load should be held for at least 10 minutes (until stable). The 
proof test nails may be incorporated into the permanent soil-nailed slope provided 
that the nails satisfy the test criteria. Pullout tests and creep tests on the soil nails 
are an integral part of the design of the soil-nailed slope system. Therefore, a 
Geolabs representative should observe the pullout tests and creep tests.

 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be cleared 
and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site or 
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the 
excavated materials.  
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be 
over-excavated to expose firm natural material, and the resulting excavation should 
be backfilled with well-compacted engineered fill. In general, the excavated soft and 
wet soils may not be reused as a source of fill and backfill materials. 
After clearing and grubbing, the existing ground surface should be scarified to a depth 
of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to 2 percent above the optimum moisture, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. For pavement 
subgrades, the compaction requirement should be a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed 
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in

 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the on-site excavated soils should be suitable for use as general fill 
materials provided that the maximum particle size is less than 3 inches in largest 
dimension. The excavated on-site materials generated from excavations into the 
near-surface materials may be used as general fill or backfill materials provided that 
they are screened of over-sized materials and/or processed to meet the gradation 
requirements (less than 3 inches in largest dimension). In addition, fill materials 
should be free of vegetation and deleterious materials. However, the excavated soft 
and wet soils may not be reused as a source of fill and backfill materials. 
Imported materials required for site filling should consist of non-expansive granular 
material, such as crushed coral, basalt, or cinder sand. The select granular fill should 
be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest 
dimension. In addition, the material should also contain between 10 and 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value 
of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less when 
tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Imported fill materials should be tested for 
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the 
intended use.

Fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to 2 percent above the optimum

Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to a depth of about 22 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers and coring tools.  
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test results 
is presented on Plate B-1. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested.  Results of the direct 
shear tests are presented on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) were 
performed on selected in-situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of 
the soils.  The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the applied 
confining pressures for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample.  The test results and the 
stress-strain curve are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the 
soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-6 and B-7.
