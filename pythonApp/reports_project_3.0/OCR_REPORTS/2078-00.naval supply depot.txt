INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of a geotechnical engineering
exploration for the proposed Cold Storage Warehouse FY90 MCON Project
P 259 at the Naval Supply Depot Subic Bay Republic of the Philippines
The general location of the project is shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 2

PURPOSE AND SCOPE OF WORK
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of
subsurface conditions at the project site to provide pertinent soil data
such as shear strength compressibility swelling potential and soil
classification for the site grading and foundation design of the proposed
Cold Storage Warehouse structure and related site improvements
The scope of the exploration was performed in general accordance with
our revised proposal dated May 25 1988 and included a review of
available geologic literature and previous borings in the area field
reconnaissance the drilling and sampling of seven 7 borings laboratory
soil testing engineering analysis of the field and laboratory data and
report preparation
The boring logs as well as the results of the field and laboratory
test data are presented in the appendices of this report

SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is situated within the Naval Supply Depot southwest
of the intersection of Rizal and Argonaut Highways
The site was currently used as an open storage yard and is surrounded
by Building Nos 1034 to the north 1003 and 1006 to the east 1000 to the
south and 1027 to the west
Topographically the area is flat with a ground elevation of about 11
feet above Mean Sea Level
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The Naval Facility is located within the Western Volcanic Intrusive
Province of Luzon and comprises Recent to Pliocene Quaternary Volcanic
deposits The E W Trending San Antonio Fracture Zone is probably the
closest identified major fault zone located about 8 to 10 miles northerly
of the site
At the project site the underlying volcanic deposits are primarily
of pyroclastic origin and consist of lithic tuffs volcanic breccia
agglomerate and volcanic mudflows lahar believed to have been
generated by explosive eruptions from Mount Natib Mount Santa Rita and
Mount Balakibok three of the major volcanic centers which comprise the
Zambales Bataan Volcanic Highlands
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2078 00 Page 4
Dk No 65
Examination of the geomorphology of the area leads to the conjecture
that the site is located on a marine deltaic flat at the toe of a rugged
mountainous terrain The area was cut during low stands of the sea by
streams originating in the mountainous areas to the east of the site
Numerous glacial eustatic sea level variations caused the emergence
and submergence of the land mass that produced marine deltaic soil
deposits at various levels both above and below the present sea level
These low energy deposits are usually soft and highly compressible From
the geology map and older 1955 air photos of the site it appears that
the area was submerged at one time then later artificially filled for the
development of the Naval base
Subsoil Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by
drilling and sampling seven 7 borings to depths of 60 to 126 5 feet
below the ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site
Plan Plate 3
The borings encountered relatively uniform subsurface conditions
which may be generalized as follows
1 Surface fill layer of dense silty sand 8 to 9 feet thick
2 Soft clay at the water level 3 to 5 feet thick
3 Loose sand 12 to 30 feet thick The sand was found to be
thicker under the northern portion of the site
4 Highly weathered tuff consisting of stiff clayey sand 15 to 30
feet thick
5 Weathered agglomerate rock formation at depths varying from
about 40 to 70 feet below the existing ground surface

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Poor soil conditions were encountered below the building site The
compressible soft loose soils were found to be as much as 35 feet thick in
the northern portion of the site with decreasing thickness towards the
south These compressible soils have a major impact on the site grading
and foundation design of the project
Static Ground Settlement
The floor of the new building will be raised about 4 feet above the
existing grade to accommodate the loading dock
Ground settlements are anticipated from the weight of the new site
fill as well as structural loads due to compression of the soft clay and
loose sand layers below the site Estimated settlements due to the
various loading conditions anticipated are as follows
1 Four 4 feet of new fill 2 to 3 inches
2 Storage or live loads of 1000 p s f 8 inches
3 Structural columns loads of up to 150 kips less than 1 inch
Due to the limited thickness of the clay layer and the relatively
high permeability of the loose sand it is anticipated that the
settlements will occur relatively rapidly We estimate that 90 of the
settlements may occur within about 3 months

Liquefaction Settlements
The borings indicate that the project site is underlain by very loose
silty fine sand The thickness of this layer varies from about 12 to 30
feet Generally structures on the base are currently designed to resist
a design earthquake which has a 50 probability of exceedance in 50
years This design earthquake has previously been determined to have a
probable magnitude of 6 5 on the Richter Scale Because of the very
shallow water table and the extremely loose consistency of the sand layer
we believe that it is very likely that during a strong earthquake M
6 5 occurring at close distance to the site 10 to 20 miles this layer
of loose sand will liquefy
Liquefaction of the underlying sands could cause extensive ground
settlements on the order to 6 to 18 inches These settlements would
probably occur unevenly across the site To minimize liquefaction
potential densification of the loose sands below the site would be
required To achieve the required densification we believe special deep
compaction methods such as those listed below could be considered
a Vibrating Probe A 30 inch O D open ended pipe pile is vibrated
by a vibratory pile driver into the loose sands causing
densification of the surrounding sands
b Dynamic Consolidation Heavy Tamping Heavy weights ranging
from 10 to 40 tons are dropped from a height of 50 to 130 feet
causing localized liquefaction and subsequent settlement
densification of the loose sands
c Compaction Piles Displacement piles are driven into the ground
at close spacing Granular backfill is then introduced into the
hole and compacted as the pile is withdrawn
GEOLABS HAWAII

Dk No 65
We believe that the method selected would depend primarily on the
cost effectiveness of each alternative as well as the availability of
specialty contractors equipment in the Philippines and the results of a
test program to verify the effectiveness of each densification method
Our analysis indicates that densification of the underlying soil to about
70 relative density would provide a factor of safety against liquefaction
of about 1 1 In our judgement it is probable that each of the above
densification methods could increase the relative density of the site sand
to about 70 A field exploration program should be conducted after
densification to verify that the selected methods have achieved the
required densification
It should be realized that any stabilization method used would be
very costly In light of the present uncertainties associated with
prediction of earthquakes and liquefaction analyses an alternative design
procedure could be to accept the risk of liquefaction during a large
magnitude earthquake and to accept the consequence of having to repair
broken utilities and repair other ground surface damages which may occur
These are discussed below
Foundation Alternatives
Spread Footing Surcharge
Shallow spread and wall foundations may be used for the proposed
building if provisions are made to limit static and dynamic
settlements The static settlements may be reduced by first placing
the 4 feet of permanent fill with allowance for anticipated
settlement and a surcharge fill with a weight equivalent to the
storage loads The settlements of the fill and surcharge should be
monitored to determine when the settlements have essentially stopped
and building construction may begin Some settlement of the areas
adjacent to the fill and surcharge should be expected since the zone
of influence of the new fill weight will extend beyond the fill
limits

In order to reduce the dynamic liquefaction settlements one of the
densification methods mentioned above may be considered If the risk
of ground settlements and probable building damage following a large
earthquake is acceptable then the densification procedures may be
omitted
Spread and wall foundations may be designed utilizing an allowable
bearing value of 3000 p s f with an one third increase allowed for
temporary wind or seismic loads
The footings should be embedded a minimum of eighteen 18 inches
below the lowest adjacent grade
Pile Foundation With Structural Slab
In light of the potential static and dynamic settlements at the site
pile foundations may be utilized to support the structure The use
of a structural slab system would eliminate the need for fill and the
corresponding ground settlements The pile supported structure would
also be resistant to liquefaction settlements Another advantage
would be the elimination of potential frost heave below the freezer
slab since the structural slab system could provide a 3 to 4 foot
high open space between the slab floor and the ground
Based on the boring data the anticipated pile length varies from
about 45 to 70 feet below the existing ground surface with an
average length of about 60 feet
If the site is subjected to a strong earthquake settlement of the
loose sand may occur The downward movement would result in negative
skin friction forces generally known as downdrag force on the
pile shaft The magnitude of the downdrag force generally depends on
the thickness of compressible layer adhesion between the soil and
pile and the pile perimeter area

Dk No 65
Calculations indicate that a 14 inch square prestressed concrete pile
bearing into the weathered agglomerate rock may be designed for
70 ton net allowable load carrying capacity after taking the downdrag
force into account Net allowable pile capacities for 12 and
16 inch square piles are 65 and 90 tons respectively
Pile Foundations With Slab On Grade
In order to reduce the pile foundation costs a slab on grade system
in lieu of the structural slab system may be considered Fora
slab on grade the site would first have to be filled and surcharged
as described in the Spread Footing Surcharge section above With
this foundation system the pile supported structure would probably
remain intact even if liquefaction occurs However the
slab on grade would probably suffer severe damage due to the
settlements
Pile Foundation
Following discussions of the foundation alternatives with the design
team it was decided that a pile foundation with a structural slab
would be the most appropriate system for the facility
The piles should be driven with a hammer with a rated energy of at
least 28 000 foot pounds The hammer should be equipped with an
energy level control so that driving within the soft deposits may be
achieved at a reduced energy level to minimize the tensile stress
within the pile which could cause damage to the pile
The recommended pile driving criteria are as follows
1 The piles should be cast in one continuous section
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2078 00 Page 10
Dk No 65
The driving process should be continuous without interruption for
each pile A pile may be rejected or its design capacity
modified when the driving process is interrupted for more than
four 4 hours
During easy driving when the pile tip is still within soft
soil the hammer stroke should be carefully controlled reduced
energy so that excessive tensile stresses are not transmitted to
the pile
The pile must be driven to the bedrock layer which is about 50
to 75 feet below the existing ground surface at the southern and
northern portions of the building respectively
The pile driving may be terminated when the blow counts steadily
increase with depth and reach 10 blows per inch for each of the
last 3 inches or when aggregated blow counts are 120 blows for
the last foot
This criteria is based on the use of a diesel driving hammer with
a rated energy of 28 000 foot pounds to drive a 14 inch square
concrete pile with a design load of 100 tons This driving
criteria should be modified according to the actual energy of the
hammer used

Pavements
It is our understanding that a concrete truck apron as well as an
asphaltic concrete driveway are planned in front of the loading dock area
to the north of the cold storage warehouse
The traffic loading is not well defined at this time For design
purpose we have selected a medium highway traffic load with Design Index
of 3 according to NAVFAC Design Manual 5 4 This traffic load consists of
21 to 75 daily repetitions of 18 kip equivalent axle load
Based on our borings the existing upper fill is coral sand and
gravel which generally provides good subgrade support For the new
pavement construction it is recommended that at least 6 inches of the
existing fill be overexcavated the then exposed subgrade and the
subsequent backfill should be recompacted to a minimum of 95 relative
compaction per the ASTM D 1557 78 maximum density
