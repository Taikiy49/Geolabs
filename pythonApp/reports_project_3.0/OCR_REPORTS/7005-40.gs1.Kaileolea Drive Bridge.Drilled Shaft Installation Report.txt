Dear Mr. Kwan: 
As requested, Geolabs, Inc. provided geotechnical engineering services during 
the drilled shaft foundation installation for the new Kaileolea Drive Bridge. In general, 
the purpose of our geotechnical engineering services was to observe the drilled shaft 
foundation installation for general conformance with the project plans and specifications. 
This letter report summarizes our geotechnical observations. 
Geolabs, Inc. performed a geotechnical engineering exploration and our 
recommendations were presented in the report entitled "Geotechnical Engineering 
Exploration, Kaileolea Drive Bridge, Ewa, Oahu, Hawaii," dated February 26, 2015, and 
our Addendum report dated April 29, 2015. 
As part of the project, one trial shaft and one instrumented load test were 
performed at the bridge site prior to production drilled shaft installation. The results of 
the load test program were presented in the report by Loadtest, Inc. entitled “Report On 
Drilled Shaft Load Testing (Osterberg Method), TS 1 – Kaileolea Road Bridge, Ewa, HI 
(LT-1580-1)” dated July 12, 2017. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The proposed project involves the construction of a new bridge structure 
extending from Kaileolea Drive over the existing water feature of the golf course in the 
Ocean Pointe development on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project is located within 
the south-central portion of the development, south of the golf course, and north of 
Papipi Road.  The project location and vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1. 
The new one-span bridge will be about 44 feet wide and 150 feet long and 
supported by abutments at both ends. Each abutment structure will be supported on 
four 36-inch diameter drilled shafts. Based on the loading provided by the project

Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site and the structural 
loading, a deep foundation system consisting of 36-inch diameter cast-in-place concrete 
drilled shafts was utilized for foundation support of the bridge structure. Based on the 
drilled shaft load test results, we recommended production shaft tip elevations of -52.5 
feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) for Abutment #1 and -57.2 feet MSL for Abutment #2. 
The drilled shafts were designed to derive vertical support primarily from skin 
friction. The end-bearing component of the drilled shafts was discounted in the design 
due to difficulties associated with obtaining a clean bottom below the groundwater level 
during construction. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The borings at the bridge site generally encountered a surface layer of extremely 
weathered coral ranging from about 4 to 5 feet thick underlain by coral formation to 
depths of 20 to 25 feet.  The coral formation was underlain by lagoonal and coralline 
detritus deposits to about 45 feet deep, followed by coral formation extending to the 
maximum depths explored of about 51.5 feet below the existing ground surface.    
The extremely weathered coral consisted of medium dense to very dense silty 
sands and gravels with cobbles and boulders. The coral formation was moderate to 
highly weathered and soft to medium hard. The lagoonal deposit generally consisted of 
very loose to dense clayey/silty sands and gravels with traces of shell fragments. The 
coralline detritus deposit was composed of medium dense to very dense coral gravel 
with sand, and silty/clayey sands and gravels.  
We encountered groundwater in the drilled borings at depths from about 1.7 to 
2.7 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration.  These 
depths correspond to about Elevations +0.8 to +1.6 feet MSL.

DRILLED SHAFT INSTALLATION 
Foundation International installed the trial shaft, load test shaft and production 
shafts. A 36-inch diameter trial shaft was installed between May 17 and 19, 2017. A 
Watson 3100CM drill rig was used in the construction of the trial shaft.  In addition, a 
42-inch diameter temporary steel casing was also used.  Crosshole Sonic Logging 
(CSL) testing was performed on the Trial Shaft on May 26, 2017.  Based on the results 
of the CSL testing, it was recommended that concrete integrity coring be performed and 
consist of one core hole extending to a depth of about 10 feet below the top of the Trial 
Shaft.  The results of the integrity coring were satisfactory.  
A 36-inch diameter load test shaft, designated as Load Test Shaft 1, was 
installed between June 21 and 27, 2017.  Since the shaft was off-plumb from the 
vertical, Load Test Shaft 1 was abandoned and backfilled with Controlled Low Strength 
Material (CLSM).  The replacement load test shaft was designated as Load Test Shaft 2 
and was installed between June 23 and 29, 2017.  Load testing was performed on Load 
Test Shaft 2 on July 6, 2017 by Loadtest.  The results of the load testing confirmed the 
design shaft lengths for the project. 
Eight 36-inch diameter production drilled shafts, designated as #1-1 through #1-4 
for Abutment No. 1 and #2-1 through #2-4 for Abutment No. 2, were installed at the 
Kaileolea Drive Bridge site between July 31 and August 23, 2017. The approximate 
drilled shaft locations are shown on the Drilled Shaft Identification Plan, Plate 2. A 
Geolabs representative observed the shaft excavation, placement of the steel 
reinforcements, and concrete placement operations during the installation of the 
production drilled shafts. 
The production shafts were excavated using a Watson 3100CM drill rig equipped 
with 36 and 42-inch diameter augers; 36 and 42-inch diameter core barrels; 36 and 
42-inch diameter digging buckets; and a 36-inch diameter cleanout bucket. The 
production shafts were constructed using temporary steel casing. The production shafts 
were excavated to elevations ranging from about -52.0 to -57.5 feet MSL. 
After the shaft holes were drilled to their designated tip elevations, the reinforcing 
bar (rebar) cages were placed into the drilled holes. Rebar cage spacers were used to 
provide the clearance associated with a minimum concrete cover of 4 inches between 
the sidewalls of the drilled hole and the sides of the rebar cage. 
The shaft concrete was placed after placement of the rebar cage using a 
4-inch ID steel pipe by tremie methods. During concrete placement, the bottom of the 
tremie pipe was immersed in at least 5 feet of concrete, as required in the 
specifications. 
CSL testing was performed on the production shafts and the results are 
presented in our technical memorandum entitled “Production Shafts CSL Testing, 
Kaileolea Drive Bridge, Ewa, Oahu, Hawaii” dated September 7, 2017.  Based on the

Based on our field observations, CSL testing, and integrity coring, we believe that 
the drilled shafts for the Kaileolea Drive Bridge have been installed in general 
conformance with the project plans and specifications and within the limits of generally 
accepted construction practices. The location of the top of the shafts should be 
measured using optical survey equipment to verify the shaft position is within the 
tolerance in the project specifications. Based on our observations of the drilled shaft 
installation, it is our professional opinion that the drilled shafts have been installed to 
sufficient depths to provide the design load capacity.
