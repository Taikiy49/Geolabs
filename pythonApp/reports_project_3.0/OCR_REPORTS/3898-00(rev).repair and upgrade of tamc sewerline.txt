SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration and literature research the
existing gravity sewerline alignments are generally underlain by
highly variable subsoil conditions ranging from soft marsh and
organic deposits at the GTE Baseyard and Moanalua Gardens
premises to stiff dense alluvial soils at the Fort Shafter SPS
and to hard tuff and basalt rock formation at the TAMC complex
Relatively shallow groundwater levels were encountered along
the downstream side of the sewerline alignments at depths of
about 5 4 to 14 2 feet below the existing ground surface at the
time of our field exploration
TAMC
Based on our literature research we believe that the new
sewerlines within the TAMC area may be underlain by surficial
soil deposits overlying mudrock at shallow depths and blue
rock at greater depths For preliminary cost estimation
purposes the basalt rock formation may be anticipated at
depths of about 8 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface
GTE Baseyard
Because of the highly compressible marsh deposits underlying
the GTE Baseyard site we recommend that the new sewerline
segment within the baseyard premises be supported by driven
pile foundations For preliminary design and cost estimation
purposes we believe that 12 inch square precast prestressed
concrete piles with a compressive load capacity of up to 30 tons
per pile may be used An estimated pile tip elevation on the
order of about 120 feet MSL is recommended for the driven
piles

Moanalua Gardens
Soft organic deposits are anticipated at the invert elevations of
the new sewerline pipes and manhole structure within the
Moanalua Gardens premises Therefore we recommend that
a stabilization layer consisting of 24 inches of No 2 Rock
wrapped in a filter fabric be provided under the bedding layer for
more uniform support Special precautionary measures such
as sewing the filter fabric for underwater placement and use of
light compaction equipment should be implemented during
construction to reduce the potential for significant pipeline
settlements
Fort Shafter SPS
Based on the competent subsoil conditions encountered at the
sewage pump station site we recommend that shallow
foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings
bearing on the surficial weathered tuff rock be used to support
the proposed new generator building extension For preliminary
evaluation and cost estimation of the new overflow dry well we
believe that a mat foundation bearing on the medium dense
alluvial soils underlying the site at greater depths may be used
for the design of the underground structure
In general we believe that interlocking steel sheet piling may be
used for temporary shoring for excavations in materials other
than the hard tuff or basalt rock formation especially where
dewatering of the excavations will be necessary Due to the
potential for presence of cobbles and boulders we believe that
predrilling through these obstructions would be necessary in
order to facilitate the sheet pile installation operations For
shallow excavations and excavations in open areas we believe
that a cantilever type of sheet piling may be considered For
deeper excavations and excavations located adjacent to
buildings utilities and pavements we recommend that
interlocking steel sheet piling with horizontal bracing be used for
excavation support in order to reduce the potential for adjacent
ground movement

INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Repair and Upgrade of Tripler Army Medical Center TAMC Sewerline and
Fort Shafter Sewage Pump Station SPS project in Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii
The general location and vicinity of the site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses The
recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of foundations pipe
installation shoring and dewatering and site preparation only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3898 00 Rev Page 7
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by
thinly bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased approximately
2 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicate that the island of Oahu has
subsided by as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity
Macdonald and Abbot 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed behind
the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the lagoons
Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available
to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially stable
during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to the
present sea level of Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of terrigenous
sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at correspondingly higher
elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters protected by
fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and
reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3898 00 Rev Page 8
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action extended
the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal sediments
During these stable periods the growth of fringing coral algal reefs formed distinct ledges of
reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea during that period
About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that rise the
valleys in the project area were submerged In the last 10 000 years or so the sea level has
adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to
accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits of
soft loose sediments along the coast in areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways
The TAMC side of the existing sewerline alignment is geologically situated on the
northeastern flank of the Aliamanu vent and Salt Lake vent complex of the Honolulu Volcanic
Series As the molten magma ascended through these vents it reacted with the
groundwater generating steam This steam caused explosive eruptions which deposited low
cones of ash material that have consolidated to form tuff locally referred to as mudrock
The Aliamanu tuff is an older water laid ash deposit that was laid down when the sea
level was higher than its present level The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its material
over alluvium originating from Halawa Stream After this phase of volcanic activity ceased
sedimentary deposits accumulated over the Aliamanu tuff before the next phase began
Then the Salt Lake eruptions began when the sea was at about 40 to 60 feet below its
present level The Salt Lake eruptive event deposited its material over earlier tuffs from the
older Aliamanu events The water that accumulated in the crater was believed to originate
from depressional springs and rainwater During periods of little rainfall the shallow ponded
water was reduced by evaporation and over the years the mineral salt content of the ponded
water increased resulting in the name Salt Lake

The Fort Shafter side of the existing sewerline alignment is geologically situated on
the southwest flank of the Koolau Volcanics These volcanics are composed of basaltic lava
flows of early Pleistocene Age The basalt is overlain by a complex interlayered system of
terrestrial and marine sedimentary deposits During the later Pleistocene Age the sea level
rose and fell several times as a result of glacioeustatic fluctuations resulting in local swamp
conditions which were later superimposed by more recent deposits of terrestrial detritus of
sand gravel silt and clay eroded from the older rocks of the area In more recent time the
efforts of man have shaped the low lying land to its present form by placement of fill in low
areas

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our literature research and field exploration the existing gravity sewerline
appears to be underlain by highly variable subsoil conditions ranging from soft marsh
deposits at the Baseyard to stiff dense alluvial soils at the Fort Shafter
SPS and to hard tuff and basalt rock formation at the TAMC complex In addition shallow
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3898 00 Rev Page 13
groundwater levels were encountered along the downstream side of the alignment at depths
of about 5 4 to 14 2 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration
For the installation of the new sewerline pipes and manhole structure underlain by
soft loose subsoils within the Moanalua Gardens premises we recommend that a
stabilization layer consisting of 24 inches of No 2 Rock wrapped in a filter fabric be provided
under the bedding layer for more uniform support see Plate 6 Special precautionary
measures such as sewing the filter fabric in lieu of overlapping for underwater placement
and use of light compaction equipment should be implemented during installation of the
sewerline pipes and associated manhole structure due to the presence of soft loose soil
deposits
Based on our field exploration at the Baseyard thick compressible
soft marsh deposits were encountered to a depth of about 90 feet below the existing
pavement surface Because of the highly compressible marsh deposits encountered and
past problems associated with settlement at the site we believe that new sewerlines installed
at the Baseyard premises could be subjected to appreciable settlements
resulting from consolidation of the soft soil deposits due to previously placed fill loads As
a result we recommend that driven piles with a concrete cradle be used for support of the
new sewerline to be installed at the baseyard premises
Based on our field exploration and engineering analyses we recommend that 12 inch
square precast prestressed concrete piles embedding in the stiff dense alluvial soils
encountered at greater depths be used to support the proposed new sewerline pipes A net
allowable compressive load capacity of up to 30 tons per pile with a preliminary pile
embedment depth of about 130 feet below the existing ground surface may be used for the
recommended pile foundation design Since boulder fills were encountered in our drilled
boring near the existing ground surface we believe that predrilling at the proposed pile
locations should be performed prior to pile installation to facilitate the pile driving operations

settlements of about inch We believe that most of the settlements should occur
during the construction period
Because of the potential for presence of loose soft subsoils near the foundation
subgrade level some movement of soils around the new overflow dry well excavation
should be anticipated due to changes in the earth stresses during and after

adjacent structures in the future may result in some settlement of this underground
dry well structure Therefore we recommend that some flexibility in the connection
of the piping network to the overflow dry well structure be incorporated in the design

Site Preparation
We anticipate that the majority of the site grading work will involve excavations for the
construction of the sewer manhole structures and installation of the new sewerline pipes
Site grading and earthwork operations should be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office be present to observe
the grading operations to evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the
excavation process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions at the invert elevations are
similar to those encountered in our exploration
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris and other deleterious and organic
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Existing pavements that are
to be demolished should be completely removed Any over excavation resulting from
demolition should be backfilled with compacted select granular fill material Existing utilities
to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly
backfilled with select granular fill material placed in 8 inch loose lifts and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities that are to be abandoned in place under
the proposed structures should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure
After clearing grubbing and demolition areas at grade or are designated to receive
fill should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding
to the maximum dry density
Based on our field exploration the near surface excavated on site soils excluding the
soft clays and other saturated materials below the water table may be re used as a source
of general fill and backfill material Some of the near surface on site clayey soils are
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3898 00 Rev Page 35
moderately expansive particularly if compacted dry of the optimum moisture content
Therefore excavated on site clayey soils should not be used as fills within the upper 3 feet
from the finished grades in structural and pavement areas However the excavated clayey
material may be used in landscaping areas if appropriate and the surplus should be
disposed of properly off site
Fill and backfill materials required for the project construction should be placed in level
lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Compaction requirements for fills and backfills within 3 feet of the finished pavement grades
should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Where needed
imported materials should consist of non expansive select granular fill materials such as
crushed coral and basalt gravel The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with
no particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a
California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or
less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It should also contain
between 10 and 30 percent passing the No 200 sieve Fill materials should be tested and
approved prior to hauling to the project site
Fill or backfill below the water level should consist of free draining granular material
such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation up to a minimum of 12 inches
above the highest anticipated groundwater level If fill or backfill below the water level is
placed on clayey materials we recommend that the No 3B Fine gravel be wrapped in a filter
fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent
Excavation
Our field exploration disclosed that the existing gravity sewer is generally underlain
by highly variable subsoil conditions ranging from soft marsh deposits at the GTE Hawaiian
Tel Baseyard to stiff dense alluvial soils at the Fort Shafter SPS and to hard tuff and basalt
rock formation at the TAMC complex In addition shallow groundwater levels were
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3898 00 Rev Page 36
encountered along the downstream side of the alignments at depths of about 5 4 to 14 2 feet
below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration Therefore we envision
that temporary shoring of the sewerline and manhole excavations would be required for the
project construction except in the hard tuff and basalt rock formation
In general we believe that interlocking steel sheet piling may be used for temporary
shoring purposes especially where dewatering of the excavations will be necessary For
shallow excavations and excavations in open areas we believe that cantilever type sheet
piling may be considered For deeper excavations and excavations located adjacent to
buildings utilities and pavements we recommend that interlocking steel sheet piling with
horizontal bracing be used for excavation support in order to reduce the potential for adjacent
ground movement
A monitoring program should be developed and implemented by the contractor to
detect ground movement and or subsidence adjacent to the sewerline and manhole
excavations especially in soft soil deposits which may result in damage to nearby
structures and pavements It should be noted that during and after installation of the sheet
piles minor settlements may occur Therefore it is recommended that a qualified
geotechnical engineer be retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the shoring
system used
Excavation Method
It is anticipated that conventional earthmoving equipment may be used to excavate
the sewer trench and manhole excavations based on the subsurface conditions
encountered However it should be noted that hard tuff and basalt rock formation
may exist at shallow depths in the vicinity of the TAMC complex and in other localized
areas that would require heavy equipment for excavation or removal In addition
cobbles and boulders should be anticipated in the excavations at the stream
crossings Moanalua Gardens premises and Baseyard premises
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the
contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3898 00 Rev Page 37
The excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal
to the depth of the excavation measured from the edge of the excavation in order to
reduce the potential for ground movement In addition the excavated soils should be
stockpiled at least 15 feet away from underground utilities to reduce the potential for
ground movement or subsidence which may damage the underground utility lines
Excavation Support
We anticipate that excavation depths up to about 20 to 30 feet may be required in
localized areas along the gravity sewerline alignment Based on the subsoil
conditions encountered in our borings we believe that shoring of the sides of the
excavations will be necessary to protect personnel working in the excavations
The requirement for shoring in hard tuff and basalt rock formation should be evaluated
on a site specific basis
Temporary shoring of the excavations using sheet pile shoring especially where
dewatering of the excavations will be necessary may be considered However it
should be noted that the existing gravity sewerline alignment may likely traverse other
utility lines therefore care should be exercised to avoid truncating the existing utility
lines if sheet piles are to be used as shoring If sheet pile shoring is used it would
also serve as a cut off wall for the dewatering operations Therefore the sheet piles
should be driven with a suitable hammer to a sufficient depth to reduce the potential
for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the amount of dewatering within the
excavations due to the high permeability of the subsoils It should be noted that the
excavations at Moanalua Gardens and Baseyard will likely
encounter soft loose soil deposits at the bottom of the excavation There is a strong
potential for bottom heave in these soft loose soil conditions Therefore the
contractor should carefully evaluate the potential for bottom heave and design the
shoring system accordingly A minimum factor of safety of 1 5 should be provided in
evaluating shoring stability and bottom heave of the excavation
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3898 00 Rev Page 38
It is important that adequate sheeting be installed prior to excavation and maintained
tight against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation The properly
braced sheeting is essential to reduce lateral movements of the adjacent ground into
the excavation which may result in potential settlements or distress to adjacent
structures or other improvements such as roads or utilities
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His her representative who should be required to be
continuously present on site during excavation and construction work will have the
best opportunity to promptly observe changing conditions during construction such
as unforeseen subsoils unexpectedly high groundwater table inappropriate
construction sequence or techniques etc which may affect shoring stability
However it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation
Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to accurately estimate the
magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship
such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed on the
subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation In addition
it should be noted that settlement of the new pipeline may occur as a result of the
vibrations generated during the extraction of the sheet pile shoring Therefore
special attention should be given by the contractor during the sheet pile removal
process to reduce the potential for pipeline settlement
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical if necessary and that the adjacent ground should
be continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of movements with
instruments

Dewatering
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater level is
above the bottom of the proposed excavation Groundwater was encountered at depths
ranging from about 5 4 to 14 2 feet below the existing ground surface in the borings drilled
during our field exploration Based on the groundwater levels anticipated and the nature of
the soils to be encountered during the excavations dewatering will be required at the
excavations along the downstream side of the gravity sewerline alignment Therefore
dewatering provisions will need to be included in the contract documents for the proposed
construction Since the excavation will involve discharge of groundwater a National Pollutant
Discharge Elimination System NPDES permit may be necessary The contractor should
consult their independent consultant for the latest regulations and information pertaining to
the permit application
Based on the anticipated depths of the sewerline pipes and manhole structures and
the relatively shallow groundwater table dewatering with cut off walls such as interlocking
sheet piles and or tremie concrete plugs may be considered However sumps will be
needed to collect water that percolates up into the base of the excavation or infiltrates
through the sheet piles The sheet piles should be driven to a sufficient depth to reduce the
potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the amount of dewatering within the
excavations in areas underlain by granular subsoils with generally high permeability Use
of an interlocking sheet pile shoring support system with dewatering with tremie concrete
plug is relatively watertight which should allow the groundwater levels outside the
excavations to be maintained close to the original pre construction levels Therefore some
type of groundwater control requirement should be specified in the contract documents
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and he she should
be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during construction
depending on the conditions encountered The dewatering method selected should have
minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the proposed excavation The
dewatering operations should be coordinated with the trench shoring support such that the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3898 00 Rev Page 40
stability of the excavations is not jeopardized The operations should be carried out without
softening the bottom of the excavations
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents should
be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater table
and or to exclude water from the excavations to allow construction of the proposed structures
under safe and dry conditions These works or systems may include but are not limited to
pumping
Dewatering Considerations
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of a
suitable method of dewatering
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or damage
to existing buildings roads and environment
b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide
safe and dry working conditions in the excavation
Cc The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering
It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained by the
contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His her qualified representative who should be
continuously present on site during dewatering activities will have the best
opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and to
implement as soon as possible necessary precautionary or remedial measures
including but not limited to stopping or slowing down the dewatering operations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3898 00 Rev Page 41
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils may
be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations should
be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the excavation
bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be performed without
pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and should be coordinated with the
shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not adversely affected
Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in a blowing or heaving
of the excavation bottom or sides
Groundwater drawdown outside the excavation will cause additional settlement
resulting from consolidation of the loose soft compressible soils Therefore the use
of a deep well system outside the excavations to draw down the groundwater level
should not be allowed
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches connected
to excavations If this occurs the granular bedding and or backfill in the utility
trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater drawdown
resulting in settlements and potential damage to utility lines and or other existing
adjacent structures

the project construction may create potential hazards to these utility lines which if damaged
could disrupt the operations of the existing business and or facilities Therefore we
recommend that all existing utilities along the existing gravity sewer and future sewerline
replacement alignments be accurately located by the contractor prior to construction The
contractor has the sole responsibility to devise and implement an effective program to protect
the utilities during construction

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the existing gravity sewerline alignment and sewage
pump station were explored by drilling and sampling four borings Boring Nos 1 through 4
extending to depths ranging from about 31 5 to 176 5 feet below the existing ground surface
at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 3 The borings
were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers hollow
stem augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer geologist who observed the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last
12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs
of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would
be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical
Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
