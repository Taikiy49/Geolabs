that a minimum shaft diameter of 60 inches should be used in our engineering analyses for

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is within the existing Kapaolono Community Park in the Honolulu 
area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on 
the Project Location Map, Plate 1. The park is generally bordered by 11th Avenue to the 
west and 12th Avenue to the east. Both streets slope downhill towards the south with 11th 
Avenue slightly lower in elevation and 12th Avenue slightly higher in elevation relative to the 
site.  It is also bordered by Maunaloa Avenue to the north and by Kilauea Avenue to the 
south, which slopes downhill towards the west.  
The site is a relatively flat, well-maintained field with a softball amenity. On the east 
side of the field, a two-tier Concrete Rubble Masonry (CRM) retaining wall supports the 12th 
Avenue roadway with several monkeypod trees planted between the retaining walls. Each 
row of the CRM retaining wall is about 5 to 6 feet in height. On the west side, the field is 
supported by a grass slope with a 5-foot retaining wall at its base that wraps around the 
south side of the field and extends almost to 12th Avenue, parallel to Kilauea Avenue. At the 
top of the concrete stairs from Kilauea Avenue in the southwest corner of the field is another 
large monkeypod tree. In the northwest corner of the field is the softball diamond oriented 
with the home plate located in the northwest corner. There is also a comfort station and a 
pool in the northwest corner of the park, and basketball and tennis courts in the northeast 
corner of the park. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling five borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to depths of about 40 and 45 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by a relatively 
thin surface fill layer, about 2.5 to 4.5 feet thick, underlain by basalt formation with clinker 
layers extending to the maximum depth explored of about 45 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The fill materials encountered generally consisted of stiff to very stiff silty clay. 
Basalt formation encountered consisted of hard, massive to severely fractured, and slightly 
to moderately weathered basalt rock. Clinker materials encountered generally consisted of 
loose to dense silty sand and silty gravel.  
Voids or cavities, ranging from 0.5 to 3 feet deep, were encountered in three of the 
drilled borings. It should be noted that lava tubes, blisters and/or cavities are commonly 
present in basalt formations. Therefore, the potential for the presence of lava tubes, blisters, 
and/or cavities exists at the project site.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration.  However, it must be noted that groundwater levels may fluctuate depending on 
surface runoffs, seasonal precipitation, and other factors.

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the information provided, six 60-foot tall light poles with 18 maximum light 
fixtures will be installed to replace the existing lighting system at the project site. Based on 
the loading demands provided by the project structural engineer and subsurface conditions 
encountered during our field exploration, we recommend supporting the new light poles on 
single cast-in-place drilled shaft foundations. Detailed discussions and recommendations 
for foundation design are presented in the following sections. 
Drilled Shaft Foundations 
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances, the proposed 
new light pole structures may be supported by a foundation system consisting of 
cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts. Based on the pedestal dimensions for the new light 
pole structures and the subsurface soil conditions encountered at the project site, we 
recommend using a shaft diameter of 60 inches for the proposed drilled shaft foundations. 
Based on the anticipated structural loads and the subsurface conditions encountered at the 
project site, we recommend using drilled shaft foundations with an embedment length of no 
less than 15 feet below the design finished grades for the new light pole structures planned 
for the project. This corresponds to a drilled shaft length of no less than 14 feet with the top 
of the drilled shaft at 1 foot below the adjacent design finished grades.  
For the new light pole located behind the existing CRM retaining wall, we recommend 
using a drilled shaft foundation with an embedment length of no less than 20 feet below the 
design finished grade.  This corresponds to a drilled shaft length of no less than 19 feet with 
the top of the drilled shaft at 1 foot below the adjacent design finished grade. 
To reduce potential damages from the surcharge loads on the existing CRM retaining 
wall due to the laterally loaded light pole, an outer steel casing of about 72 inches in diameter 
may be provided for the upper 6 feet of the drilled shaft foundation. The annular space 
between the drilled shaft and the outer casing may be backfilled with compressible materials 
such as injected foam. 
The cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally from 
skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding basalt formation.  A net allowable 
compressive load capacity of up to 125 kips per shaft may be used for dead-plus-live loads 
and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as wind or seismic forces.

Lateral Load Resistance 
The lateral load resistance of drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of the 
surrounding soil, the stiffness of the drilled shaft, allowable deflection at the top of the 
drilled shaft, and the induced moment in the drilled shaft. The lateral load analyses 
were performed using the program LPILE-plus for Windows, which is a 
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference, laterally loaded deep foundation 
program originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin. The program 
solves for deflection and bending moment along a deep foundation under lateral 
loads as a function of depth. The analysis was carried out with the use of non-linear 
“p-y” curves to represent soil moduli. The lateral deflection was then computed using 
the appropriate soil moduli at various depths.

Foundation Settlement 
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundation will result from elastic compression of the 
shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the soils and rock 
formation encountered at the site. The total settlements of the drilled shafts are 
estimated to be less than 0.5 inches. We believe that a significant portion of the 
settlement will be elastic and should occur as the loads are applied. 
Construction Considerations 
Drilled shafts are desirable for light pole foundations because of the significant 
increase in lateral load capacities when compared to shallow foundations. However, the 
performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor's method of 
construction and construction procedures. In our opinion, the following conditions would 
have an impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations.

Voids/Cavities 
Based on our field exploration, the proposed new light pole locations are generally 
underlain by basalt formation below the surface fills. It should be noted that voids 
and/or cavities commonly present in the basalt formation could be encountered within 
the depth of the drilled shaft foundations. In order to reduce the potential for 
excessive overruns of concrete volume of the drilled holes, permanent steel casing 
should be provided for the drilled shaft foundation construction work. 
Confined Area 
For the new light pole to be installed in the terrace area of the two-tier CRM retaining 
walls, the terrace area would not be accessible to the drilled shaft rig. A specially 
fabricated platform may be required to support the drilled shaft rig positioned in front 
of the lower retaining wall to construct the subject drilled shaft foundation. As an 
alternative, the drilled shaft rig can be positioned behind the upper retaining wall to 
construct the subject drilled shaft foundation provided that portions of the upper 
retaining wall can be temporarily removed to provide access to the drilled shaft rig 
and reinstalled after the drilled shaft installation. Therefore, a temporary works item 
should be included for the installation of the subject drilled shaft foundation.  
The terrace area is planted with monkeypod trees, some tree roots and difficult 
drilling may be encountered during the initial drilling of the subject drilled shaft 
foundation. Proper root-cutting tools would be required to clear the obstruction. In 
addition, some tree branch trimming may be necessary to provide space for the 
drilled shaft construction.  
Workmanship 
The load-bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend, to a large extent, on the contact 
between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils and basalt formation. Therefore, 
proper construction techniques are important. The contractor should exercise care in 
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes. 
Based on our field exploration and the estimated length of the drilled shaft, 
groundwater is generally not expected in the drilled hole during the shaft 
installation work. Due to the relatively short length of the drilled shaft and dry hole 
conditions anticipated, concrete placement using the free fall method should be 
acceptable. In the event of seasonal rainfall and/or perched groundwater, water 
may be encountered in the drilled hole, and concrete placement by tremie method 
would be required. 
A low-shrink concrete mix with high slump (6 to 9-inch range) should be used for the 
concreting operation. The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce 
the potential for segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix. If pre-cast

Utility Trench 
We envision that new electrical duct lines will be required to service the proposed 
new lighting system. In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) is recommended under the duct lines. If 
loose or soft soils are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, a stabilization layer 
consisting of an additional 18 inches of open-graded gravel wrapped in a non-woven filter 
fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below the bedding layer for uniform 
support. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as clean sand or No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 
6 inches above the duct lines to provide adequate support around the pipes, and to reduce 
the compaction effort of the backfill, thus reducing the possibility of pipe damage. It is critical 
to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the 
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper 
backfill material and placement procedures could result in backfill settlement and pipe 
damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the duct lines 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on-site soils or imported select 
granular fill material. The backfill should be placed in maximum 6-inch level loose lifts, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and mechanically compacted 
to no less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground 
subsidence. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content corresponding to the maximum dry 
density.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling five borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to depths of about 40 and 45 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped 
with continuous flight augers and coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock materials were described in general accordance 
with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The 
Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods for the 
Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International Society for 
Rock Mechanics (March 1977). Graphic representations of the materials encountered are 
presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-5.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in 
general accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the 
field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive 
strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material 
is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe 
the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties 
of Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).
