SUMMARY OF RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration disclosed that the proposed waterline
alignment is generally underlain by loose to very loose
coralline deposits in the southern portion of the site and
very dense coralline deposits in the northern portion of the
site For the Ala Wai Canal crossing the proposed
waterline alignment is underlain by a dense coralline
deposit
Groundwater was encountered at elevations above the
proposed waterline invert elevations along most of the
alignment
Due to the anticipated depths of excavation and the
relatively shallow groundwater levels we recommend that
the excavated trenches be properly supported with
adequately designed continuously interlocking _ steel
sheetpiling and cross bracing to retain the very loose
subsoils behind the sheet piles and to protect adjacent
ground and existing structures against potential subsidence
Dewatering will be required prior to installation and
backfilling of waterline pipes below the natural groundwater
table
We believe that the new water lines should be designed
with sufficient flexibility at the joints to tolerate the relatively
small amounts of differential settlement approximately 1
inch in 200 feet anticipated

specific design recommendations

INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the design of the proposed water transmission main project located on
the island of Oahu Hawaii The exploration was performed in accordance with our
proposal dated and our revised proposal dated
The general location of the project site is shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings resulting from our exploration and our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analysis based on the
data obtained from the field exploration and laboratory testing These
recommendations are intended for design input and are not intended for preparation
of construction estimates or bids

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
It is our opinion that the proposed waterline may be constructed along the
proposed alignment provided our geotechnical engineering recommendations as
presented in this report are followed
Poor to good soil conditions were encountered along the project site Through
the southern two thirds of the alignment very loose coralline deposits were
encountered at the proposed invert elevations Therefore trench shoring with
continuously interlocking steel sheetpiling and bracing will be needed to reduce lateral
movement of the ground and potential damage to existing facilities In the northern
third of the alignment very dense coral formations were encountered Hard
excavation may be encountered in this area The need for shoring will depend on the
depth of excavation and actual conditions encountered and therefore the shoring
requirement must be determined by the contractor in accordance with the OSHA and
other safety requirements
For the Ala Wai Canal crossing a dense coral formation was encountered at an
elevation of about twenty 20 feet below MSL To reduce potential future damage to
the new waterline the new waterline should be placed within a trench in the coral
formation and be provided with a reinforced concrete jacket
Along most of the site the groundwater levels were above the proposed invert
elevations Therefore dewatering will be required for most of the alignment
Corrosion property tests of selected soil and water samples were performed in
the laboratory The results are presented in Appendix B

Earth Pressure Loads on Pipe
Loads on buried conduits are influenced by the width of the trench the size of
the pipes the unit weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance between the
backfill material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads on the buried
water pipes an average unit weight of 120 p c f for backfill material and a coefficient
of friction of 0 25 are recommended Earth forces upon the pipe increase rapidly with
the width of the trench and therefore the width of the waterline trench should be kept
to a minimum
Traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be considered since the
proposed waterline alignments are located within travelled ways
Bearing Capacity and Settlement
The very loose and very dense coralline deposit encountered at the proposed
invert elevations have generally poor and good bearing capacity respectively
The new waterline construction in general will not materially increase the
vertical loading upon these underlying soils The net difference in loading between the
weights of the excavated material and the subsequent water pipe and backfill will be
essentially small Therefore settlements should be small provided good construction
methods are followed
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great effect upon the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important
design consideration
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 10
For pipes bearing on the very dense coral formation a minimum of six 6
inches 10 inches for 30 inch pipes of compacted granular bedding material should
be placed below the pipe The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe
sides a minimum width of one fourth the outside pipe diameter and it also should
extend half way up the pipe sides The bedding material should consist of clean
granular material such as 3B Fine gravel or ASTM C 33 No 67
For pipes bearing on the very loose coralline deposits an additional 18 inch
layer of clean granular material should be provided below the pipe and bedding layer
to provide more uniform support for the pipe
For the concrete jacketed pipes below the Ala Wai Canal crossing a granular
bedding is not required
The recommended bedding detail is illustrated on Plate 3 Typical Trench

Trench Support
It is Our opinion that the trench walls in the very loose coralline deposits with
shallow groundwater level conditions throughout the southerly two thirds of the
alignment will require continuous sheetpiling support It is important that adequate
interlocking steel sheetpiling be installed prior to the excavation and maintained tight
against the trench walls with proper bracing to reduce lateral movements of the
adjacent ground into the trench excavation which would result in potential settlements
adjacent structures This is particularly critical since some of the existing structures
adjacent to the alignment may be supported on spread footings bearing on the upper
subsoils
The steel sheetpiling should be driven properly with a suitable hammer to a
depth sufficient to maintain the stability of the trench bottom and to reduce the
possibility of bottom heaving and of piping through the subsoil or quick conditions
which may be created by upward seepage pressure during dewatering
The recommended minimum depths of penetration of the sheeting to reduce
the potential for upheaval and piping are presented on Plate 4 Recommended

Penetration of Sheet Piling The penetrations shown do not include a safety factor A
minimum factor of safety is recommended for determination of the penetration
required
The required depths of penetration are also affected by the method selected for
dewatering operations The curves presented on Plate 4 are based on the assumption
that there is no drawdown of the groundwater table outside of the sheeting i e
pumping is done within the sheeting The use of a deep well system outside the

in the surrounding area possibly resulting in ground settlement
The need for support of the trench walls in the northerly third of the alignment
where the underlying coralline deposits are more dense may be less critical and may
be governed by applicable safety regulations and requirements This determination
should be made the sole responsibility of the contractor
The sheetpiling and bracing systems used throughout the project must comply
with all applicable safety regulations and requirements The adequacy and safety of
the sheetpiling and bracing installation operations should be made the responsibility of
the contractor His representative who should be required to be continually present
on site would therefore have the opportunity for observing changing conditions
which might affect the trench stability during construction

pressure diagram presented on Plate 5 Apparent Pressure Diagram may be used as

Appropriate loads due to surcharge traffic or sloping ground conditions
adjacent to the trench excavations should also be added to the recommended earth
pressures for design purposes
Even if good construction procedures are followed some movement of the
trench support bracing system may be anticipated because of changes in earth
stresses during excavation Movements depend greatly on workmanship e g how
quickly and tightly the support is installed It also depends on the types of subsoils
the depth and width of excavation and the type of support In view of the complexity
of the problem it is difficult to accurately predict the amount of movement to be
anticipated of a bracing system and the adjacent ground Therefore it is important to
realize that the trench support should be installed as early as practicable and that
measurements of vertical and horizontal movements are required especially in areas
where ground movements may result in damage to the existing adjacent structures
The ground adjacent to the trench excavations should be continuously
monitored for cracks or dips and other indications of movements and possible
sloughing of the trench walls Such monitoring is particularly critical for this project
which traverses intensely developed areas along heavily trafficked roadways
Dewatering
Dewatering will be necessary for the majority of the proposed alignment since
the existing groundwater levels in most areas are generally above the proposed
waterline pipe inverts The dewatering operations should be coordinated with the
trench shoring support so that the stability of the excavations are not jeopardized
The operations should be carried out without softening the bottom of the excavations
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O Page 14
Relatively large quantities of groundwater may have to be extracted from the
excavations since the natural groundwater table may be as high as ten 10 feet above
the deepest excavation bottom and the underlying subsoils may consist of highly
permeable coralline soils
Method of Dewatering
The method and equipment to be used for the excavation dewatering should
be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety
considerations
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents
should be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural
groundwater table and to exclude the water from the excavations to permit
construction of the proposed waterline structures under safe and dry
conditions These works or systems should therefore include but not be
limited to pumping only
We suggest that the following three 3 basic criteria be considered in selecting
a suitable method of dewatering
1 The method should result in the least disturbance or damage to the
adjacent existing structures
2 The method should maintain stability of and also provide safe and
dry working conditions in the excavations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 15
3 The method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modifications to
accommodate various ground conditions
Based on these criteria it appears that sump pumping inside the excavations
may be used if the steel sheetpile shoring is driven sufficiently deep to prevent
piping and or heaving of the excavation bottoms
Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of the
excavations is generally not recommended even though this method could
reduce the required sheetpile penetration The resultant areal depression of the
natural groundwater table could induce consolidation of subsoils resulting in
potential ground settlements affecting the existing proximate roadways and
structures
lf however the use of a well point system is elected recharging by timely
injection of water back into the subsoils should be considered to balance the
groundwater table and minimize areal drawdown in areas near the existing
roadways and structures We believe however that the risk of causing
settlements to adjacent development will generally be higher by using the well
point method than using the sump pumping method
Construction Precautions
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the
natural groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to
dewatering It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be
retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O Page 16
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His qualified representative who should be
required to be continuously present at the site will have the best opportunity to
promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and to
implement as soon as possible necessary precautionary or remedial measures
including but not limited to stopping or slowing down the dewatering operations
Where encountered at the excavation bottoms permeable granular subsoils
may be susceptible to piping and quick conditions Therefore the project
dewatering operations should be done without pumping out soil fines pumping
clear water only and should be coordinated with the shoring installation so that
the excavation stability is not jeopardized The dewatering operations should be
carried out without creating a quick condition or softening of the excavation
bottoms
Extra caution should also be taken by the contractor to avoid dewatering utility
trenches that connect to the excavations If dewatering occurs within previously
backfilled utility trenches the granular bedding and or backfill in the utility
trenches could act as subdrains and this could cause significant areal
settlements and damage to the utility lines and or other existing adjacent
structures due to water level draw down outside of the braced sheetpile
excavation
Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be constructed in accordance with the
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction of September 1986
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O Page 17
The first zone of backfill extends from the bedding material to at least twelve
12 inches above the top of the waterline pipes Select free draining well graded
granular material is recommended for the first zone of backfill to minimize the
compaction effort required and resulting stresses to the pipe The granular material
should be well graded less than one 1 inch in size and have no more than 10
passing No 200 sieve Compaction by water ponding or jetting may be allowed
provided the granular material is self draining
The trench backfill from twelve 12 inches above the top of the pipes to within
two 2 feet of the existing ground elevation may consist of the excavated on site soils
except organic clays and silts The backfill should be placed in thin lifts with each lift
mechanically compacted to a minimum 90 of its maximum dry density as determined
in accordance with ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
The trench backfill up to a level within two 2 feet of the design pavement
surface elevation in paved areas should consist of select borrow compacted to a
minimum 95 of its maximum dry density as determined in accordance with ASTM
Test Method D 1557 78
A typical section of the pipe trench is presented on Plate 3 Typical Trench
Detail
Precautions should be taken against the occurrence of pipeline floatation before
and during trench backfilling due to possible influx of large quantities of groundwater
into the trenches The uplift buoyant forces due to groundwater influx may lift empty
or even partially filled water pipes

If practicable the trenches should be kept free of water during backfilling
Should backfilling under water be necessary granular material such as 3B Fine

Joints and Connections
Due to the varying subsoil conditions along the proposed waterline alignment
we recommend that flexible pipe joints be used to connect the water pipes to allow for
some amount of differential settlement
Existing Utility Lines
Since the general project site is in a developed urban area it is anticipated that
many existing utility lines may be encountered during construction of the water line
Therefore we recommend that all existing utility lines along the proposed
waterline alignment be located accurately prior to construction and that an adequate
program be devised to protect the existing utility lines during construction
Pavement Replacement
After trench excavation waterline placement and trench backfilling have been

one that is equal or better

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling twenty three 23
borings to depths of 9 4 to 50 0 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 A truck mounted rig was used
to drill the borings
The soils encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who continuously monitored the drilling
operations The classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and testing
in the laboratory All soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A summary of the materials encountered are presented on the
Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 23 2
Soil samples were obtained from the borings by driving a 2 inch standard split
spoon sampler or a 2 4 inch D split barrel sampler with a 140 pound hammer free
falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12 inches are shown
on the Boring Logs at the approximate sample depths
Core samples of soil and rock materials encounteed at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a
rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock
material left in the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
classification and testing
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of the rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material
recovered from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would
be shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses This designation is only applicable to core retrieved from in
situ rock formations RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material that is in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities
discounting any drilling induced fractures or breaks

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to identify appropriate tests
to aid in determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight
Determinations are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in determining
certain engineering properties The moisture content expressed as a percentage of
the dry weight of the tested sample is shown at the appropriate sample depths on the
Boring Logs
Sieve Analysis test ASTM Test Method D 422 63 is performed to determine the
grain size distribution of the soil The grain size distribution is used for classification in
the Unified Soil Classification System and to provide indices on other engineering
properties The result of the test is summarized on Plate B 5
Corrosion Property Tests
Soil resistivity tests ASTM G 57 78 and pH tests ASTM G 51 77 were
performed on selected samples as an aid to evaluate their corrosion potential The
water soluble sulfate and chloride content of selected soil samples and the sulfate
content of selected water samples were also measured to aid in evaluating their
corrosion potential The results of these tests are summarized on Plates B 1 through
B 4
