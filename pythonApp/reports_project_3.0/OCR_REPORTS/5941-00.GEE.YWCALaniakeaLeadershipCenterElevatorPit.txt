performed for the proposed elevator pit at the YWCA of Oahu Laniakea Facility in the

vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This letter report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations

recommendations are intended for design of foundations slabs on grade and retaining
structures only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report
Please note that the soil samples recovered during our field exploration
remaining after testing will be stored for a period of two months from the date of this
report The samples will be discarded after that date unless arrangements are made for
a longer sample storage period Please contact our office for alternative sample storage
requirements if appropriate

FIELD EXPLORATION
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling one boring
designated as Boring No 1 extending to a depth approximately 35 5 feet below the
existing ground surface The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
Materials encountered in the boring were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operation on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate 3 Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Log of Boring Plates 4 1 and 4 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the boring drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the boring in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the boring drilled at the site we recommend supporting the proposed
elevator pit on a shallow foundation system The elevator pit foundations likely would bear
on the very dense limestone encountered in the boring at the foundation subgrade
elevation
The planned excavation for the proposed elevator pit would extend below the
basement level which could expose the foundations of the existing building Therefore
underpinning of the existing building footings may be required in localized areas unless the
existing footings are bearing directly on the limestone formation cemented materials
Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of foundations slabs on grade
and retaining structures are presented in the following sections herein

Foundations
Based on the generally competent subsurface conditions encountered we
recommend supporting the new elevator pit on a shallow foundation system bearing on
the very dense limestone encountered in the boring An allowable bearing pressure of
up to 4 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used to design the footings bearing on
the very dense limestone This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces
Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the foundation
excavations should be over excavated to expose the underlying dense materials The
over excavation should be backfilled with free draining gravel such as ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation materials due to the presence of groundwater at the foundation
subgrade elevation Alternatively the bottom of footing may be extended down to bear
directly on the underlying competent materials
If foundations are next to utility trenches easements or existing footings the
new footings should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or the existing footing Alternatively
the new footing may be embedded to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines or
the bottom of the existing footing This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the
potential for appreciable foundation settlement
Special attention should be given to the locations and depths of the existing
building footings around the new elevator pit location New footings adjacent to the
existing building footings should be embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the
existing building footings The new footings adjacent to the existing footings should be
designed to resist the additional structural loads
If the foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with our
recommendations we estimate that total settlements of footings supported on the very
dense limestone should be on the order of about 1 inch or less
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed between
the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure acting
against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 45
may be used for footings bearing on the very dense limestone Resistance due to passive
earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per
square foot per foot of depth pcf above the groundwater and 150 pcf below the
groundwater This assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted and or
properly confined For design purposes the groundwater level may be assumed at 2 feet
MSL taking into consideration normal tidal fluctuations

Slabs On Grade
Because the floor slab for the elevator pit will be below the groundwater level we
recommend placing a 6 inch layer of free draining gravel such as ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation material below the concrete floor slab for uniform support Soft and or loose
materials encountered at the slab subgrade elevation should be over excavated to
expose the underlying dense materials The over excavation should be backfilled with
free draining gravel such as ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation material due to the
presence of groundwater

Excavation
Based on our field exploration the project site generally is underlain by very dense
limestone below the basement level In general we believe that conventional excavation
equipment may be used to excavate the limestone underlying the proposed elevator
structure However excavations into localized areas consisting of dense and or hard
limestone may require the use of hoe rams or chipping
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the
contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavated soils should
not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal to the depth of the excavation
measured from the edge of the excavation in order to reduce the potential for appreciable
ground movement
Because the planned excavation will extend below the basement level by about
6 feet we believe that underpinning of the existing building foundations may be required
in localized areas during the project construction unless the existing footings are bearing
directly on the limestone encountered in the boring The contractor must exercise care to
avoid over ripping the limestone which would disrupt the structure of the limestone
resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for the existing building footings in its
vicinity because the new excavation is very close to the existing footings and will likely
extend below the bottom of the existing footings
Dewatering
Based on our field exploration groundwater was encountered between about
Elevations 0 7 and 0 8 feet MSL We understand that the elevator pit excavation may
extend to a depth of about 6 feet below the basement level corresponding to an elevation
of about 2 3 feet MSL Groundwater will be encountered in the elevator pit excavation
therefore dewatering will be required to construct the elevator pit

Based on our experience in the project vicinity the permeability of the subsurface
materials especially the dense limestone may be considered moderately to highly
permeable However the actual subsurface permeability may range broadly and also vary
locally in terms of orders of magnitude Therefore the contractor should pay special
attention to the site specific dewatering plan for the proposed excavations
Dewatering for construction is the contractor s responsibility The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and he she
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on the conditions encountered The dewatering method selected
should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the proposed
excavation As indicated previously the underlying limestone may be moderately to highly
permeable and is capable of transmitting moderate to high quantities of water into the
excavation
Because the project is in a developed area the dewatering operation should be
conducted in such a manner that the dewatering will not cause areal ground subsidence
which may cause potential damage to the existing nearby structures and underground
utilities Therefore consideration should be given to a dewatering system that includes a
cut off wall to reduce the infiltration of groundwater from the sides of the elevator pit
excavation due to the moderate to high permeability of the dense limestone extending
below about 2 feet MSL
Earthwork
We anticipate the majority of the earthwork for the project will consist of excavation
of the elevator pit and backfilling behind the elevator pit walls At the on set of earthwork
the area within the excavation limits should be thoroughly cleared Construction debris
deleterious materials and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed
properly off site or in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials with the spoils Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing
should be over excavated to expose firm material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with free draining granular material ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation The
excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed off site Contract
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and fill
placement to account for variations in the over excavation quantities
The excavated soils may be re used as a source of backfill materials behind the
elevator pit walls provided it is free of deleterious materials and screened for particles
greater than 3 inches in largest dimension It should be noted that excavation into the
limestone encountered below the basement level may generate some over sized
material The over sized material should be removed prior to using the excavated
material as fill or backfill

Imported fill and backfill materials if required should consist of non expansive
select granular material such as crushed coral or basalt The material should be well
graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension In
addition the material should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and
should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with
ASTM D 1883 Geolabs should test imported fill materials for conformance with these
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use
Fill and backfill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 6 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Where groundwater is
encountered within the excavations backfill materials should consist of free draining
granular materials ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation The free draining granular materials
should be used up to a level about 12 inches above the groundwater level to facilitate
compaction of the fill materials
A Geolabs representative should monitor earthwork operations during construction
to observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the earthwork
operations and to confirm whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those
encountered in our field exploration
