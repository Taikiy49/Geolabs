Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau known as Schofield
Plateau
The project site is situated on the Ewa Plain to the southeast of the
Waianae Mountain Range The Ewa Plain is a gently sloping alluvial plain formed by the
deposition of alluvial clays and silts derived from weathering of the basalt rock formation
further up slope The alluvial deposits were laid down and are inter bedded with marine
sediments and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness
of the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1
Highway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves
as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern
Oahu Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the
present
The project site is situated over the relatively thick alluvial clay soils The
coralline and marine deposits are believed to underlie the site but at some depth
beneath the alluvium Agricultural development within the last 100 years has brought
the project area to its present form

Seismic Design Considerations
Based on the International Building Code 2003 Edition IBC 2003 as amended
the project site may be subject to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will
need to be addressed The following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity
and soil profile type for seismic design
Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic
activity therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments However some of
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate
damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii
over 90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of
Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than
Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of
Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have
occurred in the County of Maui The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands
of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu For example small
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the HUD Building site is generally underlain by
stiff to very hard clay extending to the maximum depth explored of about 26 5 feet
below the existing ground surface Two of the borings encountered a coralline deposit
from about 15 5 to 18 5 feet deep Our laboratory tests indicate that the near surface
clayey soils exhibit high shrink swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the
soil moisture contents Therefore special attention should be given to the site
preparation recommendations for the site grading and foundation design to account for
the highly expansive soil conditions at the site
Based on the generally stiff subsurface soil conditions encountered at the project
site we believe that a shallow foundation system slabs on grade with thickened edge
footings and spread footings may be used to support the proposed building structure
Due to the highly expansive soil conditions at the site we recommend capping the
expansive clayey soils with a minimum of 36 inches of non expansive select granular
fill material below building slab areas We also recommend providing 36 inches of
non expansive select granular fill material below the walkway and lanai slabs In
addition special attention should be given to the recommended procedures for
subgrade soil preparation below slabs to reduce the potential shrink swell effects of the
near surface clayey soils
Field observations during our exploration indicated that shrinkage cracks have
developed on the existing ground surface across the project site The existing
shrinkage cracks if left unattended may increase further in size under prolonged dry
weather conditions Shrinkage cracks adjacent to the building foundations are
potentially detrimental since water can readily infiltrate into the subsurface through the
crack openings and soften the subsoils around the building foundations thus affecting
their performance Therefore we recommend scarifying the existing ground surface
moisture conditioning and recompacting during the site preparation operations In
addition appropriate maintenance measures should be implemented to reduce the
potential for recurrence of the shrinkage cracks around the building structures

Slab On Grade Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe
that slabs on grade with thickened edge footings may be used to support the building
structure planned Our exploration indicated that the site is generally underlain by
moderately to highly expansive clayey soils at or near the existing ground surface
Unless slab on grade foundations supported by these expansive soils are properly
designed there is a potential for future distress to the building structure resulting from
wetting and subsequent swelling of the expansive clayey soils
To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from the swelling of the
on site expansive soils we recommend providing non expansive select granular fill
material capping fill for a minimum depth of 36 inches below the building slab cushion
The capping fill should also extend beyond the perimeter of the building slab a minimum
of 2 feet The perimeter thickened edges of the slabs should be embedded a minimum
of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent exterior grade The embedment of the interior
thickened edge footings may be reduced to a minimum depth of 12 inches below the
adjacent finished grade The base of the thickened edge should have a minimum width
of 12 inches
The slab edges should incorporate a sufficient amount of top and bottom
longitudinal steel reinforcement and the top and bottom steel reinforcing bars should be
connected by stirrups In addition the concrete slab should be reinforced with

reinforcing steel bars The project structural engineer should be consulted for design
details of this thickened edge which is intended to function both as a moisture barrier

Prior to placement of the non expansive select granular fill material the soil
subgrade below the slabs should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches

The slab subgrades including the select granular fill should be kept moist until
the placement of cushion fill and concrete The select granular fill material should
provide a capping layer of moist soil above the underlying potentially expansive
subgrade soil thereby reducing the loss of moisture from the clayey subgrade soils In
our opinion the reduction in potential drying and wetting of the subgrade soils should
reduce the impact of shrink swell effects on the slab due to potential heaving of the
underlying expansive clayey soils when wetted
We recommend incorporating a minimum 4 inch layer of cushion fill below the
concrete slab The cushion fill should consist of free draining materials such as
No 3 Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation which would also serve as a capillary
moisture break The slab subgrade should be thoroughly wetted prior to concrete
placement to allow the select granular fill layer to remain in a moist condition To
reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor

Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered on the slab
since they can better mask minor slab cracking It is also recommended to incorporate
some flexibility into the interior wall design to accommodate small amounts of possible
ground movements

Shrinkage Cracks
Our field observations indicated that substantial shrinkage cracks are present at
the existing ground surface of the project site In general shrinkage cracks will start to
develop in the ground due to the lack of maintenance of the soil moisture content as the
soils start to dry out under the sun and dry weather conditions The development of
shrinkage cracks in the ground was apparently intensified by the prolonged dry climatic
conditions in the Kapolei area resulting in large fluctuations of the soil moisture content
Detailed discussions of the shrink swell behavior of the on site soils and the mechanism
of shrinkage crack development are presented below

Based on the laboratory test results performed for the project the on site clayey
soils generally have a moderate to high shrink swell potential when subjected to
fluctuations in the moisture content and may be classified as expansive soils In
addition it will likely lose substantial strength when saturated and will shrink
appreciably when subjected to drying resulting in shrinkage cracks In general the
mechanism of shrink swell in expansive soils is a complex phenomenon and is
influenced by a number of factors including soil characteristics and environmental
factors
Expansive soil characteristics can generally be measured by engineering properties
such as soil plasticity and density to evaluate the shrink swell potential of soils In
general the soil plasticity reflects the capacity of the clay minerals in the soil to
retain large amounts of water while maintaining a coherent structure Therefore a
highly plastic soil such as those encountered at the project site will have a greater
potential for volume change shrink and swell due to the loss or absorption of
moisture under cycles of drying and wetting In addition increased soil densities
through compaction or natural depositional history such as desiccation generally
have greater shrink swell potential
Environmental factors include the initial moisture content of the soil and moisture
variations over time The initial moisture content generally impact the potential of
the soil to absorb or expel water from the soil system In general a dry soil will
have a higher capacity to absorb water and swell while a wet soil will lose water
more readily upon exposure to dry conditions and will shrink more Moisture
variations can generally be caused by climate surface drainage vegetation
permeability etc
Shrinkage Crack Development Mechanism
Due to the high plasticity of the on site clayey soils shrinking of the near surface
soils starts to develop as soil moisture evaporates from the soil system under the
prolonged dry climatic conditions characteristic of the Kapolei area These
shrinkage cracks may close up to a certain degree upon wetting as the soils
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
W O 5260 40
GEOLABS INC
Page 18
Hawaii California
expand during periods of wet weather or heavy rains However shrinkage of the
on site soils and subsequent cracking of the ground surface will recur when the dry
weather returns Therefore the development of shrinkage cracks will recur when
the on site clayey soils are subjected to prolonged dry climatic conditions which
causes large fluctuations in the moisture content unless some measures are taken
to maintain the soil moisture content at a relatively constant level
In areas where ponding water occurs the wet ground will have a higher potential
for shrinkage cracks to develop since the wet soils will lose water more readily upon
exposure to dry conditions and will shrink more Therefore it should be recognized
that this phenomenon will recur and continue unless measures are taken to
maintain the soil moisture content relatively constant in order to reduce the
shrink swell behavior of the on site clayey soils due to moisture fluctuations

Recommendations for Site Preparation
If the existing ground cracks are left unattended for an extended period of time
and are allowed to penetrate deeper below the surface the ground cracks could
adversely affect the performance of the future building foundations The ground cracks
could serve as conduits for water to percolate into the foundation soils and cause loss of
strength of the foundation soils resulting in potential settlement of the building structure
In addition migration of the foundation soils into the openings of the shrinkage cracks
could undermine the foundation system and result in potential settlement of the building
structure Therefore remedial work to correct or mitigate the observed ground cracks
should be implemented prior to construction of the proposed buildings
Based on our field observations shrinkage cracks up to about 1 inch wide and
12 to 18 inches deep were observed at the project site Therefore we recommend
scarifying the existing ground surface to a minimum depth of 12 inches
moisture conditioning to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacting to at least 90 percent relative compaction prior to commencement of the
site work grading operations to remove the zone with shrinkage cracks

Drainage

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Five one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test
results are summarized on Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Four Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the shear strength parameters of the soils The test results are presented on
Plates B 3 through B 6
Two Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on selected
in situ cohesive soil samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the
soils The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depth and graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B 7 and B 8
