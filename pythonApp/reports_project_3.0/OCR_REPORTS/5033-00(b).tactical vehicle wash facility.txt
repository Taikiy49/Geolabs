2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island
The younger volcano Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island As the
volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau banked against its eroded
eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau The
project site is located on the Schofield Plateau
The Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded
against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene
Epoch The lava flows on the plateau have undergone in situ weathering to depths of
15 to 30 meters 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors of the soil The
deep narrow canyons such as Waikakalaua Kipapa and Kaukonahua are the result of
this in situ weathering
This in situ chemical weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils
that grade to saprolite with depth In general residual soils are composed mainly of
silty clay and clayey silt materials and are typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic
rocks The less weathered saprolite is composed mainly of silty and sandy material with
remnants of the parent rock structure Spheriodally weathered boulders are typical in
the Schofield Plateau The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with depth

3 2 Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the structural loads
anticipated for the project we believe that shallow foundations consisting of spread
and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed building structures
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 000 psf may be used for the design
of foundations bearing on the stiff in situ residual soils or compacted fill material The
bearing pressure provided above is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
33 percent for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
For footings bearing on the stiff in situ soils the bottom of the footing excavations
should be scarified to a depth of at least inches moisture conditioned to at
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and recompacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction to provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density

No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials
such as No 3B Fine gravel should also be used for the trench backfill up to about
inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and
to reduce the compaction effort on the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging
the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the
sides of the pipes Improper backfill material and placement could result in settlement of
the backfill and damage to the pipes

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level inches above
the pipes to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select on site
soils or select fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum inch
level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are
below pavement areas the upper feet of the trench backfill below the
pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
3 8 Drainage
The finished grades outside the tactical vehicle wash facility structures should be
sloped to shed water away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It
is also advised that gutter systems be installed around the buildings and that the
discharge be diverted away from the foundation areas Excessive landscape watering
near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations
should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water
infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting
of the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration Drainage swales should be
provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface runoff away
from the slab and footing foundations
