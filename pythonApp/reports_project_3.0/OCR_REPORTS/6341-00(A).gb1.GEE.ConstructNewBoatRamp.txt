PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is at the southern tip of the Pearl City Peninsula between V 1 Dock
and V 2 Dock on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project scope consists of constructing
a new boat ramp adjacent to the existing boat ramp
The new boat ramp will be located to the west of the existing boat ramp In
addition to the boat ramp a 10 foot wide by 52 foot long concrete pier will be constructed
along the west side of the new boat ramp The top of concrete pier is planned at
approximately 3 5 feet Mean Sea Level MSL

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
We understand that the new concrete pier will be approximately 10 feet wide and
about 52 feet long Based on the design layout the new concrete pier will be supported
by three bents Each of the bents will be supported by two precast prestressed concrete
piles
Based on the information provided the maximum compressive and lateral loads
imposed on a single pile is approximately 35 Dead Load 20 kips and Live Load 15
kips and 10 kips respectively Based on the structural load demands provided the depth
of water to the mudline and the subsurface conditions encountered in our drilled boring
we recommend supporting the new concrete pier on driven 20 inch square precast
prestressed concrete piles The concrete piles would derive its vertical support mainly from
skin friction between the pile shaft and the surrounding clayey soils and some end bearing
on the clayey soils To achieve an allowable compressive load capacity of 35 kips we
recommend the piles extend to an elevation of about 65 feet MSL Due to possible
variation in the length of the piles unit prices should be obtained during bidding for
add ons shorter piles etc Detailed recommendations pertaining to the driven pile
foundations are presented in the following table

Predrilling
As previously mentioned a 3 foot thick coral ledge was encountered at a depth of
about 18 5 feet below the water level The presence of the coral will likely impede the pile
driving process and may cause damage to the piles during driving In order to facilitate the
pile driving operations through the coral ledge and potential obstructions we recommend
predrilling at the proposed pile locations to about 25 feet MSL
We wish to emphasize that the diameter of the predrilled holes should be limited to
the diagonal dimension of the pile to provide the driven piles with sufficient soil contact for
lateral load resistance
Downdrag Loads
The loose silty sand may be susceptible to liquefaction in the case of a seismic
event producing a peak horizontal ground acceleration of about 0 15g at the site In the
event of seismically induced liquefaction settlement of the loose silty sand will occur at the
site When the loose silty sand move downward relative to the pile the downward
movement will result in negative skin friction forces acting on the pile shaft The negative
skin friction forces are generally known as downdrag forces The magnitude of the
downdrag force depends on the thickness of the liquefiable layer relative density of the
potentially liquefiable deposits and the pile perimeter
Based on our foundation analyses we estimate that the downdrag loads imposed
on the vertical piles due to the potential for liquefaction of the loose silty sand may be on
the order of about 58 kips per pile The allowable compressive load capacity has already
taken into account the downdrag loading on the pile
Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on the concrete pier may be resisted by the lateral load
capacity of the 20 inch square concrete piles Lateral load resistance of driven piles is a
function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the pile allowable deflection
at the top of the pile and the induced moment in the pile The lateral load analyses were
performed using the program LPILE plus for Windows which is a microcomputer
adaptation of a finite difference laterally loaded pile program originally developed at the
University of Texas at Austin The program solves for deflection and bending moment
along a pile under lateral loads as a function of depth The analysis was carried out with
the use of non linear p y curves to represent soil moduli The lateral deflection was then
computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths

Pile Foundation Settlements
Settlements of the driven pile foundations will primarily result from elastic
compression of the pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total settlement
of the pile supported foundations to be less than 0 75 inches with differential settlements
between bents supported on piles not exceeding about one half of the estimated total
settlement We believe that these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as
the loads are applied
Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy of
about 30 000 to 40 000 foot pounds of energy per blow The hammer should be equipped
with an energy control level such that driving within the loose soft soils may be controlled
at a lower energy to reduce the potential for tensile stress development within the piles
which could damage or cause cracking of the piles
Consideration should be given to the matching of the pile hammer with the weight
of the piles to be driven when determining driving equipment to be used This will result in
the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy to the top of the pile and also
reducing potential damage to the piles Geolabs should review the pile driving equipment
proposed to evaluate whether the equipment is capable of installing the piles to the
recommended capacity
The contractor should forward pile and driving equipment data forms for review
prior to construction Geolabs should review the submittal and assess the driveability of
piles with the use of the selected hammer A wave equation analysis should be performed
after selection of the pile driving equipment to assess the driveability of the piles and to
establish the preliminary pile driving criteria for the pile installation program
Pile Driving Criteria
For preliminary design of the 20 inch square concrete piles pile driving may be
terminated when the estimated pile tip elevation of 65 feet MSL has been achieved The
pile driving may also be terminated when the aggregate blow counts are at least 30 blows

Field Exploration
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling one test boring
designated as Boring No 1 extending to a depth of about 67 5 feet below the water level
The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 We drilled the boring
using a skid mounted drill rig equipped with rotary coring tools
Our field engineer classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1
Graphic representations of the materials encountered and other observations such as the
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Pocket penetrometer test results are
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description Rock cores were
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the
Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2 The Rock Description System is based on the publication
Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses by
International Society for Rock Mechanics March 1977
Recovery REC may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the
relative quality of rock masses where appropriate Recovery is defined as the actual
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt
For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
mechanical and handling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on
the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the

ROCK FRACTURE CHARACTERISTICS
The following terms describe general fracture spacing of a rock
Massive Greater than 24 inches apart
Slightly Fractured 12 to 24 inches apart
Moderately Fractured 6 to 12 inches apart
Closely Fractured 3 to 6 inches apart
Severely Fractured Less than 3 inches apart
DEGREE OF WEATHERING
The following terms describe the chemical weathering of a rock
Unweathered Rock shows no sign of discoloration or loss of strength
Slightly Weathered Slight discoloration inwards from open fractures
Moderately Weathered Discoloration throughout and noticeably weakened though not able to break by hand
Highly Weathered Most minerals decomposed with some corestones present in residual soil mass Can be broken by hand
Extremely Weathered Saprolite Mineral residue completely decomposed to soil but fabric and structure preserved
