SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our geotechnical exploration consisted of drilling and sampling four borings 
extending to depths of approximately 82.5 to 114 feet below the top of deck.  Mudline was 
encountered at depths of about 10 to 14 feet below the existing wharf deck.  Our borings 
encountered a layer of soft/loose harbor and lagoonal deposit overlying stiff alluvial soils.  
Below the stiff alluvial materials, we encountered weathered volcanic tuff formation and/or 
coralline deposits. 
We believe that the loose granular harbor deposits at the project site have factors 
of safety of less than 1.0 against liquefaction based on the design earthquake magnitude.  
Therefore, the sandy harbor deposits are susceptible to soil liquefaction during the design 
earthquake event.  Due to the soil liquefaction, the effect of the settlement would induce a 
downdrag load imposed on the new concrete piles.  However, the effect of lateral 
spreading onto the new concrete piles may be ignored due to loose consistency of the 
liquefied soil profile. 
We recommend using 20-inch square precast, prestressed concrete piles to 
support the new Building 797.  To achieve an allowable compressive load capacity of 
150 kips, we recommend extending the concrete piles to a tip elevation of approximately 
-77.5 feet MLLW, bearing on the competent soil/rock stratum.  The concrete piles should 
be spaced a minimum of 5 feet from center-to-center, to avoid reduction in the vertical 
compressive load capacity. 
Existing composite piles consisting of 18-inch square concrete piles and 18-inch 
timber piles installed in 1942 support the existing quaywall structures.  We believe that the 
existing composite piles may be capable of providing an ultimate compressive load 
capacity of 50 kips (25 tons), as indicated in the RFP documents without considering the 
downdrag force induced by the soil liquefaction.  Lateral load capacity of the existing 
composite piles was analyzed in the group model for the lateral spring stiffness, to support 
the structural evaluation. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Repair Quaywalls S14A, S16A and S18A project in the Pearl Harbor 
area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project location and general vicinity are shown 
on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. This report summarizes the findings from our field 
exploration and presents our geotechnical recommendations for design of the proposed 
waterfront improvements. 
The recommendations provided herein are intended for design of new 
foundations and evaluation of existing foundations only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The design-build project for repair Quaywalls S14A, S16A and S18A is located at 
the head of Magazine Loch between Wharf S-14 and Wharf S-19 in the Pearl Harbor 
area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The existing quaywalls S14A, S16A and S18A (also 
designated as Boat Landings 1, 2 and 3) were originally constructed in 1942 and 1953. 
The three boat landing wharves are approximately 165 feet long by 13 feet wide, 
supported on a total of 98 vertical and 44 battered composite piles. 
The project will require demolishing and reconstructing the entire existing decks, 
inboard walls/guardrails, fender systems, landing shelter, Building 797 and other 
utilities.  Based on the RFP requirements, the new quaywalls will be supported on the 
existing composite piles except the new Building 797 will be supported on new concrete 
piles. 
We understand that the new concrete pile foundation supporting Building 797 
requires engineering evaluation for bearing capacity, liquefaction and slope stability 
analyses.  However, the existing composite pile foundation supporting the new quaywall 
structures requires bearing capacity analyses only.  Seismic analysis for the new 
quaywall structures is not required.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island.  The 
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) 
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae Volcano 
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was partially 
buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank.  The Koolau Volcano 
reached the end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
The geologic conditions underlying the project site are the result of a complex 
series of deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio-eustatic sea level 
changes and post-erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch. Following 
the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial soil materials were deposited in 
a deltaic or estuarine environment in the Pearl Harbor area. 
The sea level fluctuated several times, receding perhaps 300 feet below and 
rising about 100 feet above the present sea level. Concurrent with a rise in sea level 
during the late Pleistocene era, eruption of the Salt Lake-Makalapa member of the 
Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash, 
which consolidated to form volcanic tuff (locally known as “mudrock”) layers. 
Subsequent erosion dissected and thinned mantles of volcanic tuff. 
The magazine loch of Pearl Harbor represents tributary stream valleys.  During 
the recent epoch, as sea level rose to its current stand, soft/loose harbor sediments 
were deposited underlain by recent alluvium.  Pearl Harbor Naval Base was developed 
during the middle portion of this century, comprising removing some of harbor 
sediments by dredging and placing man-made fill to create reclaimed land.  The project 
site is generally located near stream valleys, underlain by thick compressible harbor 
sediments and recent alluviums overlying highly weathered volcanic tuff materials.

2.3 
Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the ASCE 7-10, the project site may be subjected to seismic activity, 
and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed for the project. 
Based on the RFP documents, a site-specific ground motion study is not 
required.  The RFP documents also require Site Class “E” (Soft Soil Profile) to be used 
from a seismic analysis standpoint.  Based on Site Class E, the following spectral 
response parameters may be used for the seismic analysis.  In addition, we understand 
that seismic analysis and liquefaction assessment are not required, except for 
Building 797 at S14A, as specified in the Amendment 0002 to RFP for Task Order. 
The peak ground acceleration (PGA) using Maximum Considered Earthquake 
(MCE) for Site Class E is determined to be approximately 0.36g in accordance with 
ASCE 7-10 for 2 percent probability of exceedance in 50 years (2,475 years return 
period).  Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the design peak ground 
acceleration, liquefaction may occur resulting in appreciable settlements at the ground 
surface (mudline).  Therefore, the effects of potential liquefaction should be taken into 
consideration in the design of the Building 797.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings encountered mudline at depths of about 10 to 14 feet below the 
concrete deck.  The subsurface conditions consisted of about 40 to 70 feet of soft/loose 
harbor/lagoonal deposits over stiff alluvium and weathered volcanic tuff formation and/or 
coralline deposits extending to the maximum depth explored of about 114 feet below the 
deck.  Thickness of the soft/loose harbor/lagoonal deposits varied significantly from 
60 to 70 feet at S16A, to 40 to 50 feet at S14A and S18A.  A thin layer of volcanic tuff 
rock about 5 feet thick was encountered at a depth of about 27 feet below the deck at 
S14A. 
We recommend installing 20-inch square precast prestressed concrete piles to 
support the new Building 797.  The driven pile would derive its support from end bearing 
on the very stiff alluvium or weathered volcanic tuff formation and from skin friction 
between the pile and the surrounding stiff alluvial deposits.  We recommend extending 
the driven piles to a tip elevation of about -77.5 feet MLLW, to achieve an allowable 
bearing compressive load capacity of up to 150 kips. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings and as-built 
information included in the RFP documents, it is our opinion that the existing composite 
piles consisting of 18-inch square precast concrete pile over 18-inch timber pile may be 
capable of providing an ultimate compressive load capacity of 25 tons (50 kips), 
excluding the downdrag force induced by the soil liquefaction. 
Detailed discussions and specific design recommendations for the waterfront 
improvement project are presented in the following sections.

Considering the potential liquefaction occurring within the upper loose harbor 
deposit during the design earthquake event, ground settlement may occur at the site 
inducing ground downward movement relative to the pile. The downward movement will 
result in negative skin friction, generally known as downdrag forces, acting on the pile 
shaft.  The magnitude of the downdrag force depends on the thickness of the liquefiable 
soil layer, magnitude of the earthquake, and the pile size. 
Based on our foundation analyses, we estimate that the downdrag loads 
imposed on the vertical piles due to the potential liquefaction may be on the order of 
about 25 kips per pile.  It should be noted that the ultimate compressive load capacity of 
the piles (375 kips) is a net value; therefore, the ultimate compressive load capacity has 
already been reduced by the expected downdrag loads in the pile foundation analyses. 
Based on review of the RFP documents, we anticipate significant variation of the 
weathered volcanic tuff rock under Building 797.  The tuff rock may be present at 
elevations between -40 and -80 feet MLLW based on the subsurface conditions 
encountered in the vicinity of Quaywall S14A.  Consideration may be given to designing 
the concrete pile reinforcing to account for this variation. 
We recommend conducting a test pile program consisting of driving test piles and 
dynamic load testing during the test pile installation to establish the actual production 
pile lengths and to confirm the design pile compressive load capacity.  Due to possible 
variation in the length of the piles, unit prices should be obtained during bidding for 
add-ons, shorter piles, etc. 
3.1.1 Predrilling 
We recommend predrilling each pile location to an elevation of about -30 feet 
MLLW, to facilitate the pile driving operations and to clear the pile driving path from 
obstruction and/or shallow volcanic tuff rock, as encountered in Boring No. 101 at 
S14A.  The predrilled holes should have a diameter of no more than 28 inches for 
the 20-inch square piles.  The required predrilling size and depth should be verified 
and/or modified by Geolabs during the test pile driving operation based on the 
conditions encountered and pile driving equipment used.

3.1.2 Lateral Load Resistance of Pile Group Foundation 
Based on the structural loading combinations provided, we envision a pile group 
consisting of ten 20-inch square concrete piles may be designed to resist the 
structural loads of the new building. 
The lateral load resistance of driven piles is a function of the stiffness of the 
surrounding soil, the stiffness of the pile, the geometry of the pile group, allowable 
deflection at the top of the pile, and the induced moment in the pile.  The lateral 
load analyses were performed using the computer program GROUP. The pile 
group geometry, pile properties, soil properties, and loading conditions were input 
into the GROUP program based on subsurface conditions encountered in the 
borings, structural loads provided by the project structural engineer, and our 
experience.  Lateral load analyses were performed by modeling the lateral loads 
applied along the longitudinal (parallel to the waterfront face) and the transverse 
direction (perpendicular to the waterfront face), to assess the pile reactions in axial 
compressive force, shear force and induced bending moment. 
It should be noted that the structural loads were transferred to the central pile group 
at the bottom of the pile cap near the top of the pile (assumed at about 2 feet below 
the top of the cap).  Therefore, it would be critical to increase rigidity of the pile cap 
structures, so that the horizontal forces could be distributed across the structure 
onto the piles. 
The program solves for deflection and bending moment along each pile under 
vertical and lateral loads as a function of depth.  The analysis was carried out with 
the use of non-linear “p-y” curves to represent soil moduli.  The lateral deflection 
was then computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths. 
Results of the lateral load analyses are summarized in the table below, presenting 
the pile reactions including lateral deflection, maximum axial compressive force, 
maximum induced bending moment, flexural length, and maximum shear force 
under each load combination.  The analyses were performed assuming either 
fixed-head or pinned-head boundary conditions between each pile and concrete 
pile cap.

Uplift loads imposed on the pile foundation may be resisted by a combination of the

of about -77.5 feet MLLW supporting the Building 797.

shear resistance distributed on the entire pile length.  Therefore, mechanical

3.1.4 Pile Foundation Settlements 
Settlement of pile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression of the 
pile member and the subgrade response.  We estimate that the total settlement of 
the pile-supported foundation to be less than 0.5 inches.  Differential settlements 
between adjacent piles should not exceed about one-half of the total foundation 
settlement.  We believe that these settlements are essentially elastic and should 
occur as the loads are applied. 
3.1.5 Pile Hammer Selection 
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering energies in the 
range of about 60,000 to 80,000 foot-pounds in the field.  The hammer should be 
equipped with an energy control level such that driving within the soft and/or loose 
soils could be controlled at a lower hammer energy setting to reduce the potential 
for tensile stress development within the piles, which could damage or cause 
cracking of the piles. 
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into 
consideration the "matching" of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be 
driven.  This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy 
to the top of the pile and also reducing potential damage to the piles.  Geolabs 
should review the proposed pile driving equipment to evaluate whether the 
equipment is capable of installing the piles to the recommended capacity. 
The contractor should forward pile and driving equipment data forms to Geolabs for 
review prior to construction.  Geolabs should review the submittal and assess the 
driveability of the piles with the use of the selected hammer.  A wave equation 
analysis should be performed after selection of the pile driving equipment to assess

3.1.6 Pile Driving Criteria 
For preliminary design of the 20-inch square, prestressed precast concrete piles, 
pile driving may be terminated when aggregate blow counts are at least 60 blows 
per foot for the last foot or 6 blows per inch for the last 6 inches.  The above 
preliminary driving criteria should be confirmed and/or modified after selection of 
the pile driving equipment and performance of wave equation analyses. 
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption.  A pile may be rejected 
or its design capacity may be reduced when the pile driving is interrupted for more 
than 4 hours and the pile cannot be driven to the required depth.  During soft 
driving, such as when the pile tip is within soft soils, the hammer stroke (or energy) 
should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile stresses do not develop 
within the pile during the pile driving operation. 
Because variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the bearing 
soils, the supporting capacity of each pile must be confirmed in the field by a 
Geolabs representative during pile driving.  It should be noted that the pile design 
was developed from our pile analysis using the field exploration data.  Therefore, a 
test pile program should be carried out to confirm our preliminary pile design and to 
establish the pile driving criteria.  The production pile driving criteria may be revised 
based on the test pile program.

Corrosion Protection 
One set of laboratory corrosion tests, including pH, minimum resistivity, chloride 
content, and sulfate content, was performed by TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. to 
evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site soils encountered.  Levels of the chloride content 
of the on-site soils encountered near the existing mudline are moderately high on the 
order of 2,500 parts per million (ppm), while the sulfate content is relatively low, less 
than 15 ppm.  Based on the chloride and sulfate content tests performed on the on-site 
soils, we believe that either Type I or Type II cement may be used for the concrete in 
contact with the ground planned for the project.  However, considerations should be 
given to increasing concrete cover of the pile and/or specifying 28-day concrete strength 
of 5,000 pounds per square inch (psi) with water-cement ratio of 0.4 in accordance with 
ACI 318, since the pile foundation would be installed in the sea water environment.  It 
may be appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test 
results and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion protection.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 104, extending to depths of about 
82.5 to 114 feet below the top of deck.  The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted or 
a track-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools.  
The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  Soils, where encountered, were classified in general 
conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.1.  Symbols and abbreviations used in the boring logs are also 
described on the Soil Log Legend.  Deviations made to the soil classification in 
accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil Classification Log Key, 
Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the 
Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-4.3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In addition, we obtained 
some samples from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts needed to 
drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties.  The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Nine (9) Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid limit (LL), plastic limit (PL), and plasticity index (PI), as an 
aid in classifying the soils. The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths.  Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on 
Plate B-1. 
Two (2) Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 and C136) were performed to 
evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification. Graphic 
presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B-2. 
Five (5) Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests 
(ASTM D2850) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained 
shear strength of the silty and clayey soils encountered. The approximate in-situ 
effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the 
relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test results and the stress-strain curves are 
presented on Plates B-3 through B-7. 
One Unconfined Compression test (ASTM D7012) was performed on a selected 
rock core sample to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the rock formation 
encountered.  The test results are presented on Plate B-8. 
One set of Corrosion tests, including one pH (SW 9045C), Minimum Resistivity 
(SM 2510B), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) tests, 
were performed by TestAmerica Laboratory, Inc. on a selected soil sample obtained 
from our field exploration.  The test results are summarized on Plate B-9 and in 
Appendix C.
