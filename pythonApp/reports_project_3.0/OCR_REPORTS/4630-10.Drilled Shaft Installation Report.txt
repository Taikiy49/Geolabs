entitled Geotechnical Engineering Exploration Kamehameha V Highway Emergency
Replacement of Kawaikapu Bridge FAP No ER 12 4 Island of Molokai Hawaii dated
In addition a test program consisting of installing and testing a sacrificial
instrumented trial shaft was conducted at the beginning of the drilled shaft foundation
installation work for the proposed bridge replacement The load test results are
summarized in the report prepared by LoadTest Inc entitled Report on Drilled Shaft Load
Testing Osterberg Method dated
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The replacement bridge project site is along Kamehameha V Highway across
Kainalu Gulch on the Island of Molokai Hawaii It was proposed to demolish the existing
bridge and construct a single span concrete girder bridge structure approximately 61
feet long across the stream The general contractor was Brown s Trucking Inc and the
drilled shaft contractor was CMZ of Hawaii Inc
To allow local and tourist traffic across the stream during construction the new
bridge structure was constructed in two phases In the first phase the northwestern half
of the existing bridge structure was retained to allow for vehicular access while the
southeastern half of the new bridge structure was constructed In the second phase
vehicular access was routed onto the newly constructed southeastern half of the new
bridge structure while the northwestern half of the new bridge was constructed

Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the structural loads
imposed on the foundations 48 inch diameter cast in place concrete drilled shafts were
designed to support the new single span concrete bridge structure To accommodate
the structural loading demands the drilled shaft foundation was designed to extend to
elevation 84 5 feet Mean Sea Level MSL with a length of 82 feet to achieve
compressive load capacities of 680 and 375 kips for extreme event limit state and
strength limit state respectively
It should be noted that the cut off elevation of the drilled shaft was designed at
about elevation 2 5 feet MSL with bottom of cap elevation about 2 98 feet MSL
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Based on the borings drilled the project site is underlain by fills and or alluvial
soils extending to depths of about 4 5 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
fills and or alluvial soils are underlain by very soft and very loose alluvial and or lagoonal
soil deposits extending to depths of about 67 5 to 73 5 feet below the existing ground
surface The very soft and very loose alluvial and or lagoonal soil deposits are underlain
by colluvial and or alluvial deposits consisting of hard to very hard basaltic boulders and
cobbles with some gravel extending to the maximum depths explored of about 70 to
88 feet below the existing ground surface
We encountered groundwater in our borings at depths of about 4 to 7 5 feet
below the existing ground surface Due to the proximity of the project site to the
Pacific Ocean groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on tidal fluctuations
seasonal precipitation groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other factors
PRE PRODUCTION TESTING PROGRAM
As part of the specification requirements a sacrificial trial shaft 48 inch
diameter was constructed to examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment
proposed by the contractor for actual subsurface conditions Following satisfactory
completion of the trial shafts static load test utilizing a bi directional load cell in the shaft
along with instrumentation by embedment strain gages was performed to confirm or
modify the production drilled shaft length
An engineer from LoadTest Inc installed the load cell in the shaft and performed
the load test utilizing the bi directional load cell The load test results are presented in
the reports entitled Report on Drilled Shaft Load Testing Osterberg Method dated
as previously referenced A representative from our office observed the
installation and testing of the load test shaft
Based on the load test results the original design drilled shaft length was
maintained

PRODUCTION DRILLED SHAFT INSTALLATION
A total of 12 production drilled shafts excluding two abandoned shafts were
installed between May 10 2005 and for the project The locations of the
production drilled shafts are presented on the Drilled Shaft Location Plan Plate 1 A
Geolabs engineer observed the installation operations
The production drilled shafts were excavated using a_ crane mounted
drill rig equipped with auger digging bucket clean out bucket
and or core barrel Temporary 50 inch I D Inside Diameter steel casing about 42 feet
long was used during excavation to prevent the drilled hole from cave in in the soft
and or loose soil layers A Summary of Drilled Shaft Installation is presented on Plates
2 Details of the shaft installation are summarized in Plate 2 Summary of Drilled Shaft
Installation and presented in Appendix Drilled Shaft Installation Logs
The drilled shaft contractor used polymer slurry to keep the drilled uncased hole
open during excavation The polymer slurry was prepared in the hole by
sprinkling sifting the dry polymer Shore Pac onto flowing water and further mixed by
rotation of the drilling tool in the hole in accordance with the polymer manufacturer s
recommendations The polymer slurry was removed from the hole by centrifugal pump
during concrete placement into a holding tank for recycling
Concrete was placed through a 10 inch O D steel gravity tremie pipe starting
from the bottom of the hole During concrete placement a minimum of 5 feet of
concrete head was maintained above the bottom of the tremie pipe In order to provide
good quality concrete at the top of the drilled shaft additional concrete was placed in
the drilled shaft to allow some over pouring at the top In general concrete was
over poured until sound concrete was evident at the top of the drilled shaft
The approximate volume of concrete placed in the shaft was monitored and is
presented in the attached Appendix The theoretical and actual concrete volume placed
in the shafts is summarized on Plate 2
Non destructive Integrity testing by Cross hole Sonic Logging CSL was
performed on all the production drilled shafts and concrete coring was conducted on
selected production shafts The results of the integrity testing indicated that poor
concrete and soil intrusion occurred at depths between 56 and 62 for Shaft No 4 and
depths between 58 and 76 feet for Shaft No 8 Extensive repair effort was carried out
by coring pressure washing and pressure grouting However the repairs for Shaft Nos
4 and 8 were not successful based on the CSL tests Consequently Shaft Nos 4 and 8
were abandoned and Shaft Nos 4R and 8R were designed and installed for
replacement

Discussion of Results
Increases in first arrival times FATs were less than 10 in all profiles for shafts 7 and 11
The energies were fairly uniform in all profiles In shaft 8 an anomaly defined by increases in FATs
between 5 and 60 was identified in all six profiles at a depth of around 58 to 60 feet below the top of
the tubes Another anomaly defined by increases in FATs between 20 and 60 in all six profiles was
also identified over the lower few feet of the tubes Reductions in relative energies were also associated
with these FAT increases
With regard to CSL testing an anomaly can be defined as an increase in FAT or a reduction in
relative energy As a general guideline increases in FATs of 10 or less can be considered
insignificant Increases in FATs between 10 and 20 are usually minor but have been verified as
defects in some cases and not as defects in other cases Increases in FATs greater than 20 usually

CROSSHOLE SONIC LOGGING CSL
Introduction
Cross hole sonic logging CSL or sonic coring is used to evaluate the condition
of the concrete within cast in place concrete piles caissons or drilled shafts It can also
be used to evaluate the concrete integrity of slurry or diaphragm walls This method
involves using a piezo electric transducer to generate a signal that propagates as a sound
compression wave within the concrete Another transducer is used to detect the signal
The typical source receiver arrangement for a drilled shaft is shown in Figure 1
Procedure
CSL testing will performed using the PISA testing equipment manufactured by
Piletest com in Herzlia Israel The PISA is a compact digital computer that can record
convert and process analog signals to digital signals
Each transducer is placed into a vertical PVC or steel tube filled with water
These water filled tubes are attached to the reinforcement cage prior to or immediately
after pouring the concrete and act as a coupling medium between the transducer and the
tube The source and receiver transducers are lowered to the bottom of their respective
tubes and placed such that they are in the same horizontal plane The emitter transducer
generates a sonic pulse on the order of 10 per second which is detected by the receiver
in the opposite tube While the sonic pulses are generated the two transducers are
simultaneously raised within the tubes at a rate of around 1 foot per second until they
reach the top of the drilled shaft This process is repeated for each possible tube
combination Figure 1b shows the six tube combinations that can be tested logged using
a configuration of 4 tubes within the drilled shaft Obviously as the number of tubes
increases the number of combinations increases thereby increasing the resolution of the
testing zone
Theory
The principle essentially relies on the relationship between wave speed modulus
and density as presented in equation 1
In homogeneous good quality concrete the wave speed is typically around 11 000 to
13 000 feet per second If the condition of the concrete is compromised either by a void
soil inclusion or necking within the shaft then the wave speed will be reduced by some
percentage from the sound concrete value The existence of a flaw or defect will
increase the time it takes for the signal to propagate The quantity that is actually
measured is the propagation time between the transducers Figure 2 presents a typical
ultrasonic signal The vertical axis is the signal amplitude microvolts and the horizontal
axis is the time milliseconds The point where the signal amplitude begins to rapidly

Advantages to this method include direct assessment of pile integrity which can

Discussion of Results
Increases in first arrival times FATs were less than 10 in all profiles for shafts 1 The
energies were fairly uniform in all profiles In shaft 3 the combinations of profiles using tube 3 were
not tested due to a blockage at 10 below the top of the access tube Increases in FATs between
approximately 20 and 55 were identified over the lower 18 inches of the three tested profiles In
shaft 4 there was an anomaly identified in all six profiles at a depth of around 56 to 58 feet below the
top of the tubes defined by increases in FATs ranging between approximately 30 and 150
Reductions in relative energies were also associated with these FAT increases
With regard to CSL testing an anomaly can be defined as an increase in FAT or a reduction in
relative energy As a general guideline increases in FATs of 10 or less can be considered
insignificant Increases in FATs between 10 and 20 are usually minor but have been verified as

indicate anomalies worthy of consideration and further evaluation particularly if they occur over several
feet in depth and over several different profiles Changes in relative energy can sometimes indicate
defects depending on the degree of the reduction and the associated increase in FAT
In general the compression wave velocity for cast in place concrete typically ranges between
11 000 and 13 000 feet per second The average apparent compression wave velocity can be
determined from the CSL results by dividing the average tube distances by the average first arrival times
for each tube combination in a shaft The average apparent compression wave velocity computed in this
manner cannot be used as an absolute number e g the tube distances below the top of the shaft are not
exactly known and must be used with caution However it can be used as a guide for evaluating the
overall condition of the concrete in a shaft compared to other shafts As shown in Table 1 the average
apparent compression wave velocities for the shafts range between approximately 11 300 to 11 700 feet
per second

We have reviewed the CSL test results for the retest performed on Shaft 4 on March 7 2006
During the initial CSL test on an anomaly was identified in all profiles defined by
increases in FATs between 30 and 150 with associated reductions in energies at approximately 56 to
58 feet below the top of the concrete During subsequent coring the anomaly was confirmed to be a
defect Recent remediation activities via flushing and grouting were completed on
The CSL results obtained within the repair zone during the retest indicate similar FAT increases and
energy reductions as those obtained during the initial test only a marginal improvement was observed
Minor anomalies were also noted in the upper portion of the shaft during the retest
We believe that the minor anomalies in the upper portion of the shaft are due to debonding
between the tubes and the concrete as a result of the long period of time elapsed between the initial test
and the retest The anomaly within the repair zone may be a result of
1
partial or incomplete repair of the defective zone or
2
refraction and or attenuation of the signals as they pass through around the grout concrete
interface within the repair zone
We have experienced both of these situations in our projects involving similar repair methods in
the past Other possible solutions to determine if the repair was successful are
1
Retest the shaft again to evaluate whether additional grout curing time was needed
2
Perform pulse echo method PEM integrity testing to corroborate the CSL results The
PEM however may also be affected by the grout concrete interface or
3
Core the shaft to evaluate the effectiveness of the repair

Discussion of CSL Results
All tubes were accessible over their full length No significant increases in first arrival times
FATs or weaker energy levels were detected in the profiles FAT increases less than 10 Table 1
summarizes the CSL testing results including the average apparent compression wave velocity for the
shaft concrete and comments on the CSL results Appendix B contains the CSL testing data summary
and the individual CSL logs for each tested tube combination including plan views showing the tube

With regard to CSL testing an anomaly can be defined as an increase in FAT or a reduction in
relative energy As a general guideline increases in FATs of 10 or less can be considered
insignificant Increases in FATs between 10 and 20 are usually minor but have been verified as
defects in some cases and not as defects in other cases Increases in FATs greater than 20 usually
indicate anomalies worthy of consideration and further evaluation particularly if they occur over several
feet in depth and over several different profiles Reductions in relative energy can sometimes indicate
defects depending on the degree of the reduction and the associated increase in FAT
In general the compression wave velocity for cast in place concrete typically ranges between
11 000 and 13 000 feet per second The average apparent compression wave velocity can be
determined from the CSL results by dividing the average tube distances by the average first arrival times
for each tube combination in a shaft The average apparent compression wave velocity computed in this
manner cannot be used as an absolute number e g the tube distances below the top of the shaft are not
exactly known and must be used with caution However it can be used as a guide for evaluating the
overall condition of the concrete in a shaft compared to other shafts As shown in Table 1 the average
apparent compression wave velocity for the shaft concrete was 12 000 feet per second

Increases in first arrival times FATs were less than 10 in all profiles for shafts 12 The

8R and 9 except for isolated tube combinations that indicated increases of FATs up to 15 In shaft

feet below the top of the tubes defined by increases in FATs up to 20 In general significant
reductions in relative energies were not associated with these FAT increases
With regard to CSL testing an anomaly can be defined as an increase in FAT or a reduction in
relative energy As a general guideline increases in FATs of 10 or less can be considered
insignificant Increases in FATs between 10 and 20 are usually minor but have been verified as
defects in some cases and not as defects in other cases Increases in FATs greater than 20 usually
indicate anomalies worthy of consideration and further evaluation particularly if they occur over several

In general the compression wave velocity for cast in place concrete typically ranges between

manner cannot be used as an absolute number e g the tube distances below the top of the shaft are not

overall condition of the concrete in a shaft compared to other shafts As shown in Table 1 the average
