PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
As part of Task Order No 0010 Infrastructure Improvements for the Unexploded
Ordnance Clearance Project for Kaho olawe island Reserve several cement stabilized soil
test sections were constructed for evaluation purposes Five sections of Portland cement
stabilized soils and two sections of chemically stabilized soils were constructed in the
Royal Kunia Subdivision site in the Kunia area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii These test
sections were constructed to evaluate the Portland cemeni stabilized soils and chemically
stabilized soils for use as pavements
Initially a site in Mililani was selected and planned for the soil cement test sections
Therefore laboratory testing of the Mililani soils stabilized by Portland cement was
conducted However due to heavy rains in the week preceding the test section
construction a new site was selected for the test section construction The proposed test
section location in Mililani was too muddy for proper soil cement construction Due to the
last minute change in the test section location no cement stabilized soil testing were
conducted for the Royal Kunia site
However based on the available index tests for the Royal Kunia Subdivision site
we believe that the soils existing at the Royal Kunia Subdivision site is closer in properties
to the typical soils found on the Island of Kaho olawe

CONSTRUCTION OF THE TEST SECTIONS
Each of the test sections generally measured about 4 9 meters wide by about
6 1 meters long Several test sections with varying cement contents using fresh water and
seawater supply were constructed to evaluate the properties of these test sections A
schematic sketch of the test sections constructed is provided as Plate 1 of this letter report
for reference The following procedures were generally used to prepare the site and to
construct the test sections
1 After clearing the test section areas the subgrade soils were scarified to a
depth of about 150 to 200 mm moisture conditioned to within 5 percent of
the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction The optimum moisture content and maximum dry density were
determined based on ASTM D 698 test procedures consistent with the
project specifications The maximum dry density of the soil is m
with the corresponding optimum moisture content at 25 percent
2 Pulverization tests were conducted on the materials to be stabilized If the
material was dry of the optimum moisture content the amount of particles
greater than retained on the No 4 sieve varied from about 12 to
17 percent The maximum percent particles greater than allowed
for soil cement construction is 20 percent If the soil was wet the soil would
not meet the pulverization requirement
3 Because of the relatively limited size of the test sections 4 9 meters by
6 1 meters in plan dimensions the methods for applying the cement and
mixing it into the soils were relatively crude utilizing manual labor In order to
obtain increased uniformity the cement was applied and mixed in two lifts to
obtain the desired 150 mm and 200 mm compacted thickness sections
4 The cement used for mixing was Type 1 ll cement produced by Hawaiian
Cement in 42 kilogram bags The bags were somewhat evenly spaced over
the area cut open and the contents placed on the soil The cement was
then raked over the surface Considerable variation in cement spread
should be expected by using this method
5 The cement was then mixed with the use of a comparatively small Kubota
420 roto tiller At least three passes of the mixer and usually more were
used for each lift During the mixing operation laborers removed the visible
lumps greater than inch in size There were some variations of
color of the mixture indicating some non uniformity of the cement spreading
and mixing

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our observations of the test section construction we have the following
comments and recommendations for further consideration
1
The appearance of the constructed test sections after the 7 day curing
period was generally good with only minor cracking cracks less than 5 mm
wide of the soil cement pavement observed However significant
shrinkage cracking cracks on the order of 10 to 15 mm wide of the
chemically stabilized soil test section was observed Vehicular traffic has not
started to traverse over the test sections at this time

2 Based on the test sections constructed we believe that the methods
employed in the construction of the test sections are quite crude and should
not be used as an indication of the actual construction process for full scale
production of Portland cement stabilized soils on the Island of Kaho olawe
In general the methods employed to spread and mix the soil cement
materials should be greatly improved for the full scale production on the
Island of Kaho olawe Therefore an appropriate cement spreader to obtain
more uniform spreading and a more powerful mixer such as a cross shaft
rotary mixer should be used to spread and mix the soil cement
3 Additional effort will need to be expended prior to construction on
determining the depth of loose soil cement mixture needed to attain the
desired final thickness of the soil cement pavement This may be
accomplished by the construction of test sections on the Island of
Kaho olawe as required in the specifications for the project
4 The compaction effort needed to achieve the maximum field density with the
full size equipment will also need to be established based on the test section
on the Island of Kaho olawe This may be accomplished by rolling the
soil cement mixture and field testing the wet density of the soil cement after
each two passes of the roller until the wet density increase is less than
5 kg m
5 Eliminate the normal crown currently shown on the plans for the soil cement
pavement surface since obtaining the required finished surface would be
difficult to control regardless of the method employed to construct the
soil cement pavement Additional tolerance should be allowed in the project
specifications to account for the difficulty in obtaining the finished surface

7 Due to the variability in the soils and considering the difficulty in achieving
