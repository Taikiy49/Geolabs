Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Waianae Volcano became
extinct while the Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried
below the Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas
formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
The project site is located within the Schofield Plateau The Schofield Plateau was
formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against the already eroded slopes
of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch The dips of the lava beds are near
horizontal between 3 to 5 degrees from horizontal The lava flows on the plateau have
undergone in situ weathering to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the
reddish color of the soil
This in situ weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils that grade
to saprolite with increasing depth In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty
material while residual soils are more clayey Both residual and saprolitic soils are typical
of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The residual and saprolitic soils grade to
basaltic rock formation with increased depth Spheroidally weathered boulders within the
residual and saprolitic soils are typical in the Schofield Plateau

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Non expansive
select granular fill materials should be placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted
to at least 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or
other types of acceptable compaction equipment Water tamping jetting or
ponding should not be allowed to compact the fills Where compaction is less than
required additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of
moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction

Underground Water and Drain Lines
We understand that 12 and 16 inch diameter DIP water lines and 30 to 84 inch
diameter RCP drain lines will be required for the project In general good construction
practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new
utilities The contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used for trench
excavation subject to practical limits and safety considerations In addition the
excavations should comply with the applicable federal state and local safety
requirements The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design and
installation
In general we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
open graded gravel ASTM C33 No 67 gradation under the pipes for uniform support
The granular bedding layer should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of one
fourth ¼ the outside pipe diameter or trench width whichever is greater Free draining
granular materials such as open graded gravel ASTM C33 No 67 gradation also
should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to
provide adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material
to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in backfill
settlement and pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated of on site soils or
select granular fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 8 inch level loose
lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce
the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence The upper 3 feet below the
finished grade in planned roadway areas should be compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined
in accordance with ASTM D1557

Underground Utility Structures
Based on the information provided we anticipate new underground utility structures
such as drain manholes and valve vault structures will be required for the project Based on
our field exploration we anticipate the new underground utility structures will be constructed
in the stiff to hard residual soils and or compacted general fills at the project site Based on
the subsurface conditions encountered the following general guidelines may be used for
the design of the drainage structures at the project site
Underground Utility Structure Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered an allowable bearing pressure
of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be utilized for design of the
underground utility structures bearing on the stiff to hard residual soils and or
compacted general fills at the project site To provide uniform bearing support for
the new structures we recommend providing a minimum 6 inch thick layer of No 3
Fine gravel ASTM C33 No 67 gradation below the bottom of the drainage
structures
Foundation subgrades should be recompacted to a firm and unyielding surface
prior to the placement of the No 3 Fine gravel Soft and or loose materials
encountered at the bottom of the excavations should be over excavated to expose
the underlying firm materials or maximum 2 feet below the bottom of the footing
The over excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill materials
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Based on our engineering analyses we believe that the net increase in bearing
pressure on the underlying stiff to hard residual soils and compacted general fills
resulting from the underground utility structure construction would be minimal
Settlements on the order of less than 1 inch are anticipated for the drainage
structures

Corrosion Potential
Design of metallic substructures such as waterline pipes or other metallic piping
should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure Five sets of
laboratory corrosion tests including pH and minimum resistivity were performed by our
office to evaluate the corrosivity of the on site soils encountered The test results are
summarized on Plate B 7 in Appendix B The soil resistivity values were determined in
general accordance with ASTM G51
In addition a total of 60 field resistivity surveys were conducted at 30 locations
Model 400 resistance meter manufactured by Nilsson Electrical Laboratory Inc Tests
were taken at each location using 10 and 20 foot pin spacings A second set of
tests was taken orthogonal to the first set at each location The approximate field
resistivity test locations are shown on the Overall Site Plan Plate 2 and the Site Plans
Plates 3 1 through 3 6
The Wenner 4 Pin Method utilizes four surface probes or pins electrodes to
evaluate the average resistivity an indication of corrosivity of the underlying soil mass
The field resistivity survey was used to evaluate the average resistivity values of the soil
mass at the approximate survey locations Results of the field resistivity surveys are
presented on Plates C 1 through C 15 It should be noted that the field resistivity survey
may be influenced by temperature weather conditions soil moisture existing
underground utilities and other factors
Based on the field resistivity survey results the subsurface soils at the project site
have resistivity values ranging from about 2 509 to 33 177 ohm cm corresponding to
corrosion ratings of 2 to 5 respectively based on the previous Board of Water Supply

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling ten borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 106 and 201 through 204 extending to depths of
about 6 5 to 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations
shown on the Overall Site Plan Plate 2 and the Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 6 The
borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
In addition four bulk samples of the near surface soils designated as Bulk 1 through
Bulk 4 were obtained to evaluate the support characteristics of the near surface soils
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described
on the Soil Classification Log Key Plate A 0 2 Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 10
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples
were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586 Penetration
Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration
sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler
the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration
Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The penetration
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the
specific sampler type used The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the
Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer test results are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D2216 and Unit Weight determinations ASTM D2937
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Four Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B 1
Three Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D2166 were performed on selected
in situ samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on site clayey
soils The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Individual stress strain curves of the unconfined compression tests are presented on
Plates B 2 through B 4
Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests ASTM D2850 were
performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the in situ
soils The approximate in situ effective overburden pressure was used as the applied
confining pressure for the relatively undisturbed soil sample The test results and the
stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 5 and B 6
Five sets of corrosivity tests including and Minimum Resistivity
ASTM G57 tests performed by our office Chloride EPA 300 and Sulfate Content
EPA 300 tests performed by Eurofins TestAmerica Laboratories Inc and Bicarbonate
Content SM 2320B performed by Enthalpy Analytical were performed on selected soil
samples obtained from our field exploration The test results are presented on Plate B 7
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D1557 Method A were performed
on selected bulk soil samples to evaluate the relationship between the moisture content
and the dry density of the near surface soils The test results are presented on Plates B 8
and B 9
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 10 through B 13

Field Resistivity Survey
Geophysical survey equipment i e resistance meter was mobilized to the project
site on July 27 through August 4 2021 for the field resistivity survey testing Sixty soil
resistivity surveys were conducted at 30 locations designated as R 1 through R 30 using
the in Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured
by Nilsson Electrical Laboratory Inc The method utilizes four surface probes or pins
electrodes to evaluate the average resistivity of the underlying soil mass The average
resistivity values of the soil mass extending from the existing ground surface to the test
depth was assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations shown on
the Overall Site Plan Plate 2 and the Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 6
The soil resistivity surveys were performed along the existing ground surface at the
approximate survey locations shown on the Site Plan The average soil resistance along the
proposed alignment was evaluated at each survey location with the electrodes aligned at
equal spacing of generally 10 and 20 feet The depth of penetration of a resistivity
sounding is a function of the electrode spacing
The resistance meter Nilsson Model 400 provides a voltage between the outer
electrodes causing an electrical current to flow between them The voltage drop between
the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive built in voltmeter As a result the soil
resistance in ohms is obtained from the voltmeter dial reading The soil resistivity in ohm
cm which is directly related to both resistance and spacing can then be computed The
evaluated soil resistances and computed resistivity values in tabulated format and graphical
format are presented on the following plates At the time of resistivity surveying the
temperature was about 84 to 87º F The existing ground surface was dry to moist It should
be noted that the soil resistivity may be influenced by temperature weather conditions
existing underground utilities and other factors
