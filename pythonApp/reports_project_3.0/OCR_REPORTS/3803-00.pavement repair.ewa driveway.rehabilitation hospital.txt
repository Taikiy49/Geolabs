INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Pavement Repair Ewa Driveway project within the premises
of the existing Rehabilitation Hospital of the Pacific in the Nuuanu area of Honolulu on the
island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and _ geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of pavements and site
preparation only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the existing Rehabilitation Hospital of the Pacific
along Kuakini Street in the Nuuanu area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii Our
site visit indicated that extensive pavement failures such as alligator pattern cracking
depressions and potholes were noted in the pavement structures along the ewa
driveway and in the service courtyard area The majority of the pavement distress
appears to be concentrated alongside the garbage dumpsters and at the driveway
entrance exit areas Due to the deteriorated condition of the existing pavements we
understand that it is desired to evaluate and formulate remedial measures pertaining to
the pavement repair work at the subject site
Based on our past experience in the vicinity of the project site we anticipate that
subsurface conditions will likely consist of highly expansive adobe clays overlying basalt
rock formation at greater depths Special attention was given to the pavement support
characteristics of the clayey subgrade soils

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
of middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level The processes of erosion and
deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic sea level fluctuations When the sea
level was low the erosional base level was correspondingly lower and valleys were
carved to depths below the present sea level When the sea level was high the erosional
base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reach
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3803 00 Page 5
the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium In general stream flows in Hawaii
are intermittent and flashy such that the stream flows transmit large volumes of water for
very short duration Because of this transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of
the stream s hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles
gravel sands and fines When the erosional base levels change these sediment loads
are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin
to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material
Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion
of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate
Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in
headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is transported downslope
covering the older alluvial deposits Depending on the local base level and rate of
transport these newer sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time In
addition their consistency and density are generally less than those of the older partially
consolidated deposits
The project site is located in Nuuanu Valley an amphitheater headed valley
carved by erosion into Koolau Basalt Filling the base of this valley is a thick sequence of
alluvium interbedded with deposits of lava and pyroclastics of the Honolulu Volcanic
Series

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Severe pavement distresses and failures including alligator cracking depressions
and potholes were observed along the ewa driveway and service courtyard areas
Based on our field exploration the existing pavements are generally underlain by highly
expansive clayey subgrade soils at shallow depths In addition our drilled borings
indicated that a compacted base course layer is not evident below the asphalt concrete
paving across the project site Laboratory testing on the highly expansive clayey
subgrade soils indicated that the material exhibits poor pavement support characteristics
with a laboratory CBR value of 1 6 and a swell of 11 6 percent We believe that the
pavement distresses and failures observed at the project site were a result of heavy truck
traffic loads over an inadequately designed and or constructed pavement section
underlain by highly expansive subgrade soils with poor pavement support characteristics
Therefore it is our opinion that the existing deteriorated pavements should be removed
and replaced with a new pavement structural section constructed on a properly prepared
subgrade Special attention should be given to the drainage requirements since water
infiltration into the underlying highly expansive clayey subsoils may result in subsoil
softening and loss of bearing support Detailed discussion of these items and our
geotechnical engineering recommendations are presented in the following sections of this
report

Pavement Design
Based on our field exploration performed for the subject project the existing
pavement structure is generally underlain by expansive clayey subsoils with poor
pavement support characteristics at shallow depths Considering the heavy truck traffic
anticipated for the ewa driveway and service courtyard it is our opinion that the following
new pavement sections may be used for the subject project
Flexible Pavement Section
3 0 Inches Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inches Basaltic Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
18 0 Inches Select Borrow 95 Percent Relative Compactio
27 0 Inches Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade
Rigid Pavement Section
7 0 Inches Portland Cement Concrete
12 0 Inches Select Borrow 95 Percent Relative Compaction
19 0 Inches Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade
The pavement subgrades should be prepared in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Site Preparation section of this report The base
course and select borrow subbase for the flexible pavement section should consist of
crushed basalt aggregate compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
CBR and field density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils
encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above sections
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and current scope of work we anticipate that
minimal site grading work will be required for the proposed pavement reconstruction
project At the on set of earthwork the areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All asphalt concrete paving vegetation debris rubbish
and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3803 00 Page 9
and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below any future pavement
areas should be over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft
and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site or used in landscaping areas
if appropriate
After clearing grubbing and demolition the future pavement areas should be
excavated where necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the select
borrow layer The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in
place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum
dry density If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the pumping
subgrade may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for pumping
subgrade conditions As a guide one sack of cement may be used for approximately
25 square feet of subgrade area Alternatively the subgrade may be proof rolled and a
woven geotextile fabric such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent should be placed on the
subgrade prior to placement of the select borrow layer
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after preparation of the subgrade we
recommend that the soil be re scarified moisture conditioned and recompacted as
recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due
to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas
and replacement with well compacted engineered fill at no additional cost to the owner
Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation
and engineered fill placement to account for variations in the over excavation quantities

Select borrow and base course materials required for the new pavements should

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base course subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement
surface should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all surface water off
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should not be
allowed anywhere on site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to
isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs
be extended a minimum of 2 inches into the subgrade soil below the base and subbase
course layers to reduce migration of landscape water into the pavement section
Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect excessive water from
landscaping irrigation For long term performance we recommend that a subdrain
system be constructed adjacent to paved landscaped areas

Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the near surface soils to evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil
classification The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depth Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
One sieve analysis ASTM D 422 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on Plate B 2
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 3 through B 5
Three Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 6 through B 8
