FIELD EXPLORATION
We explored the subsurface by drilling and sampling two borings designated as
Boring Nos 1 and 2 each to a depth of about 41 5 feet below the existing ground surface
using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers Boring No 1 was
drilled approximately 8 5 feet away from the existing Electric Pole No 3 19 and Boring
No 2 was drilled approximately 14 feet away from the existing Electric Pole No 2 19 The
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488 Standard

presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock formation encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description Rock cores were
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the
Rock Log Legend Plate 3 3 The Rock Description System is based on the publication
Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics March 1977
Recovery REC may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the
relative quality of rock masses where appropriate Recovery is defined as the actual
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt
For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any

Subsurface Conditions
As mentioned above two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 were drilled
and sampled for this project extending to depths of approximately 41 5 feet below the
existing ground surface
Boring No 1 was drilled near the existing Electric Pole No 3 19 Our field
exploration disclosed that this location is underlain by fill materials below 3 5 inches of
concrete The fill materials generally consist of loose sandy gravel with some clay and
extend to approximately 5 feet below the ground surface The fill layer is underlain by
colluvium which consists of some cobbles and boulders in a clay matrix More cobbles
and boulders were encountered from 17 5 feet below the ground surface during our field
exploration The colluvium layer extends to the maximum depth explored of about 41 5
feet below the ground surface
Boring No 2 was drilled near the existing Electric Pole No 2 19 Our field
exploration generally encountered 7 feet of fill below 4 inches of concrete The fill
materials consist of loose silty sand with gravel and grade to sandy clay from 4 to 7 feet
below the ground surface Colluvium consisting of clay with cobbles and boulders was
encountered below the fill layer and underlain by basalt The basalt was encountered at
about 15 5 feet and extends to the maximum depth explored of about 41 5 feet below the
ground surface It is generally medium hard to very hard
We did not encounter groundwater at the time of our field exploration However it
should be noted that groundwater levels may vary due to seasonal precipitation and other
factors

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the information provided a new electric pole will be installed to replace
the existing leaning Pole No 3 19 at the project site We understand the current design
concept involves installing a steel pole up to 55 feet long Based on the loading conditions
provided by the project structural engineer and subsurface soil conditions encountered
during our field exploration we recommend supporting the new electric pole on a single
cast in place drilled shaft foundation Detailed discussions and recommendations for
foundation designs are presented in the following sections
Drilled Shaft Foundations
Based on the structural loads and subsurface conditions we recommend using a
3 foot diameter drilled shaft extending to a minimum depth of 25 feet below the ground
surface to support the new electric pole The drilled shaft may be designed with an
allowable compressive load capacity of 80 kips The allowable compressive load capacity
for the drilled shaft is for dead plus live loads The compressive load capacity may be
increased by one third ⅓ when considering transient loads such as wind or seismic
forces A factor of safety of 2 5 was used for the allowable compressive load capacity
Lateral Load Resistance
The lateral load resistance of a drilled shaft is a function of the stiffness of the
surrounding soil the stiffness of the drilled shaft allowable deflection at the top of
the drilled shaft and the induced moment in the drilled shaft The lateral load
analyses were performed using the program LPILE plus for Windows which is a
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference laterally loaded deep foundation
program originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin The program

We understand that the steel pole will be fully inserted into the drilled hole before

Foundation Settlement
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundation will result from elastic compression of the
shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the soils encountered
at the site We anticipate that the total settlements of the drilled shafts are
estimated to be less than 0 25 inch We believe that a significant portion of the
settlement will be elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
In general the performance of drilled shafts depends significantly upon the
contractor s method of installation and construction procedures The load bearing
capacities of drilled shafts depend on the frictional resistance between the shaft and
the surrounding soil rock Therefore the contractor should exercise care in drilling
the shaft hole and in placing concrete into the hole As a result of these potential
variations a Geolabs representative should be present to observe installation of the
drilled shaft during construction In our opinion the following may have a significant
impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundation
1
To reduce the potential for significant caving in of the drilled hole
temporary casing may be required during drilled shaft installation Care
HECO
Page 7

should be exercised during removal of the temporary casing to reduce the
potential for necking of the drilled shaft concrete
2
Boulders and cobbles were encountered in the borings during our field
exploration Difficult drilling conditions will likely be encountered at the
project site and should be expected The drilled shaft contractor will need
to have the appropriate equipment and tools to drill through these types of
natural obstructions in the subsurface Appropriate measures also will be
needed to avoid dislodging boulders into the drilled shaft hole during the
drilling and shaft installation process
3
We did not encounter groundwater at the time of our field exploration
However due to the relatively wet weather condition in the Manoa area
some water may be expected in the drilled hole during the shaft
installation If the drilled hole is wet the shaft concrete should be placed
by tremie methods During concrete placement a minimum of 5 feet of the
concrete head should be maintained above the bottom of the tremie pipe
A low shrink high slump 7 to 9 inch slump range concrete mix should be
used to provide close contact between the drilled shaft and the
surrounding soils The concrete should be placed promptly after drilling to
reduce the potential for softening and or caving in of the sides of the drilled
hole In addition although the drilled shaft is primarily designed based on
skin friction the bottom of the drilled hole should be relatively free of loose
materials prior to concrete placement
Due to the proximity of the existing house and cobbles and boulders anticipated
some vibrations induced by the drilling may be felt by the residences Therefore we
recommend setting up monitoring points around the house and conducting a damage survey
before and after construction
It is imperative that a Geolabs representative is present at the project site to observe
the drilling and installation of the drilled shaft during construction Geolabs observation of
the drilled shaft installation operations is necessary to confirm the assumed subsurface
conditions and make a prompt adjustment if needed
