INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Reservoir and Access Road Liliuokalani Trust in the area
of Kailua Kona of the North Kona district on the island of Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design
input only and may not be appropriate to form the basis for construction cost estimates
nor for construction bidding purposes

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had
activities during recent geologic time Hualalai which erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa
and Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central part of the island The lava formation encountered at the
site appears to be of pahoehoe flow which spread and ponded as it approached the
ocean Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an
internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in
pahoehoe lavas These cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state
and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as
blisters and pockets Lava tubes intra flow cavities are formed when molten lava
drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for
a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller
in horizontal extent

SITE DESCRIPTION
The proposed water reservoir and access road development of Liliuokalani Trust
is located on the northeast side of Kailua Kona in the District of North Kona on the
island of Hawaii The subject site is located to the northeast of the existing Mamalahoa
Highway as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The subject site is situated in a relatively undeveloped area Fine grained soil
and ash and Lava flow from previous volcanic activities in the area were observed on
the existing ground surface creating a very rugged ground surface The area is heavily
vegetated with Keawe trees and grasses The existing ground surface slopes down
from the northeast to southwest with elevations ranging from 1750 feet MSL along the
northeast limit to about 1500 feet MSL along the southwest limit
The newly drilled water well site had been graded to a relatively flat surface for
the purpose of well drilling A gravel road is currently present at the project site to
provide access to the water well Asphaltic concrete pavements were noticed at five
sections of the gravel road with total length of about 800 feet
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling six borings to depths
ranging between 5 and 25 feet below the existing ground surface Three of the borings
were drilled for the proposed water reservoir and three borings were drilled along the
access road alignment The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the
Site Plans Plates 2 and 3
In general our field exploration encountered volcanic rock consisting of medium
hard to very hard basalt with occasional clinker layers from the surface to depths
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3269 00 Page 7
ranging from 5 to 25 feet below the existing ground surface The basalt ranged from
very closely to moderately fractured and ranged from virtually unweathered to
moderately weathered The rock formation encountered included dense and vesicular
porous basalt The clinker layers encountered ranged from 1 to 3 5 feet thick and
consisted of rock fragments with variable amounts of soil content
Cavities were encountered at different depths within the basalt formation in 4 of
the 6 borings drilled at the site As previously mentioned cavities are commonly
encountered in the pahoehoe flows In general it appears that cavities ranging from
about to 1 feet in height were encountered in our borings at various depths It
should be emphasized that the vertical and lateral extent of these cavities are unknown
and they could be highly variable
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field
exploration However groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on factors such as
seasonal rainfall groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other factors
For detailed description of the rock conditions encountered please refer to the
Logs of Borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 presented on Plates A 1 1
through A 6 1 of Appendix A

consist generally of basalt rock formation with some cavities and clinkers Based on
our evaluation of the subsurface conditions with respect to the proposed development
the principal geotechnical engineering considerations for the proposed water reservoir
development include the following

Foundations
Based on the preliminary grading plan we understand that the proposed water
reservoir has finished floor elevation of 1683 feet MSL Thus the entire reservoir will
be in a cut area Based onthe subsurface conditions encountered at the project site
we believe that the on site basalt rock and or clinkers would provide suitable foundation
support for the proposed water reservoir structure A continuous ring footing may be
used to support the proposed reservoir structure The ring footing may be placed
directly on the exposed basalt rock
It should be noted that if clinker layers are exposed at the bottom of footing
elevation the clinker should to be over excavated and be replaced with lean concrete
Based on our borings the clinker layers appear to be thin between 1 and 3 5 feet
If backfilling with lean concrete is not desirable the clinker needs to be crushed by
scarifying at least 12 inches moisture conditioned and recompacted to at least 90
percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3269 00 Page 10
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 The basalt rock
partially present at the foundation level should be over excavated or scarified by at
least 12 inches and compacted as engineered fill to provide a more uniform foundation
support
Further information regarding the proposed chlorination facility design was not
available at the time this report was prepared If it is located in cut areas the above
mentioned recommendations for the water reservoir may be applied to the chlorination
facility If it is located in fill areas we recommend that a minimum of 2 feet of 6 inch
minus well graded granular material be used under the footings If the structure
straddles the transition of cut and fill we recommend that all footings bear on
engineered fill to provide more uniform support for the foundation bearing on both cut
and fill and to reduce the potential for differential settlements To provide engineered
fill beneath footings in cut area the subgrade in cut area should be over excavated or
scarified by at least 12 inches and compacted as engineered fill Details regarding
engineered fill are presented in the Site Grading section
For foundations bearing directly on basalt rock an allowable bearing pressure
of up to 6 000 pounds per square feet p s f may be used for design An allowable
bearing pressure of 3 500 p s f may be used for foundation placed on compacted
clinker or engineered fill These bearing values are for dead plus live loads and may
be increased by one third Vs for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade If the footings are located adjacent to a slope the footings

should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum of 8 feet setback distance
measuring from the outside edge of the footings horizontally to the slope face
Due to the presence of cavities as encountered in our borings we recommend
that a program of probing and grouting at footing locations be implemented during
construction A representative of Geolabs Hawaii should observe the probing and
grouting operation to evaluate the extent of cavities Special attention should be given

If the footings are designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total settlement of footings is estimated to be on the order of 1 2 inch with
differential settlements on the order of 1 4 inch
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings

A coefficient of friction of 0 4 may be used for footings bearing on engineered fills
Resistance to the lateral loads due to passive pressure may be calculated using

Slab On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed water
reservoir structure The slab on grade will bear directly on the basalt rock in cut area
We recommend that the rock subgrade be proof rolled at least 4 passes with heavy
equipment such as a D 9 bulldozer to assist in detecting and possibly collapsing near
surface cavities
For the chlorination facility if the slab on grade will bear on basalt rock cut the
subgrade should be proof rolled at least 4 passes with heavy equipment such as a D 9
bulldozer to assist in detecting and possibly collapsing near surface cavities If the
slab on grade will bear on engineered fills the fill should be moisture conditioned and
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction If the slab on grade will
straddle the transition between cut and fill the basalt rock in cut areas should be over
excavated or scarified by at least 12 inches and compacted as engineered fills The
scarification process is to provide more uniform subgrade support for the slab across
the cut and fill transition and also to assist in detection of potential cavities near the
surface
Foundation Probing
As previously indicated our field exploration encountered cavities in four of the
six borings drilled at this site The cavities encountered in the borings ranged from
about to 1 feet in height To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support
resulting from collapse of the cavities below a footing we recommend that a program
of cavity probing and grouting be conducted during construction We recommend that
the probing and grouting program be conducted for all footings located in cut areas and
the footings located in fill areas with fill thickness less than 5 feet
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3269 00 Page 13
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each spread
footing location and 10 foot on centers for continuous footings The probe holes should
be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned
bottom of footing If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the
probing operation additional probes should be drilled at closer spacings to help
delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and
any cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to
moderate pressures A typical sand cement grout would consist of 1 part sand 1 part
cement and 0 7 parts water
Because of the strong potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at
the site we recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained
during bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing
and grouting operation is complete Also the contractor should be made aware that
a longer lag time between probing grouting operations and foundation construction is
likely to be required in the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation
of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the
presence of cavities and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess
grout take and or changed conditions are observed

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to
reduce potential contamination of the excavated materials Based on our field
exploration surface stripping on the order of about 6 inches may be needed to
remove the vegetation
In areas to receive fill the subgrade should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches
if possible and compacted to 90 percent relative compaction Otherwise the
subgrade should be proof rolled with a D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy equipment
minimum 4 passes to help detect and collapse near surface cavities Any
loose areas or cavities disclosed during clearing operations should be backfilled
with compacted engineered fill

Fill and Backfill Materials
The fill and backfill materials to be used as general fill material should consist
of well graded granular materials less than 12 inches in largest dimension The
on site cut material if less than 12 inches in maximum dimension may be used
as fill or backfill materials Fill materials with boulders greater than 12 inches in
diameter may be used as rock fill provided they are placed at least 4 feet below
the finish subgrades In general placement of boulders in fills should be in
accordance with the procedures recommended in the Boulder Disposal
subsection
The engineered fill recommended beneath footings and slabs on grades should
consist of well graded granular material of 6 inches in maximum dimension with
sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass If the
excavated rock material does not contain sufficient fines to produce a well
graded material off site cinders or non expansive soil may be mixed into the
excavated material to increase the percentage of fines to provide a well graded
material for compaction and increase the stability of the fills Any imported
material if necessary should be observed and or tested by Geolabs Hawaii for
its suitability prior to being transported to the site
Boulder Disposal
Boulders i e rock fragments larger than 12 inches in maximum size
encountered during site grading may be used in the rock fill provided that the
following recommendations are followed
1 Boulders should be spread out and must not be nested together They
should be placed such that compaction equipment will be able to compact
between and around them
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3269 00 Page 16
2 Boulders or portions of boulders should not encroach within 4 feet of the
finish subgrade elevation
3 Boulders larger than 2 feet in its largest dimension may be utilized as rock
fills or boulder fills where proposed fills are greater than 5 feet thick For
boulders larger than 4 feet in maximum dimension it may be necessary
to reduce the size of these larger boulders or dispose of them off site
4 Care must be exercised to avoid placement of boulders in the proposed
utility alignment and within the depths of the proposed utility lines to
reduce the potential for encountering the boulders during excavation for
the utility trenches
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding
12 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rock boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the
number of passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of
compaction should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project
under the observation of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii On the basis
of this testing program the number of passes may then be used as the field
criterion for adequate compaction

Excavation
The field exploration disclosed that the site is underlain by very closely to
moderately fractured volcanic rock ranging from medium hard to very hard It
is anticipated that the volcanic rock may be excavated with normal heavy
excavation equipment such as ripping with large bulldozers where the rock is
near the existing ground surface However excavations into the deeper
formations may require the use of hoerams chipping or blasting
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are
based on our visual observation of the existing rock formations and field data
from the borings performed at the site We recommend that all contractors
proposing to bid on this project be encouraged to examine the site conditions
and the boring data to make their own interpretation

APENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling six
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 at the approximate locations shown
on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3 The depths of our borings range between about 5
and 25 feet below the existing ground surface They were drilled using truck mounted
drill rigs equipped with rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A of Appendix A Graphic presentation
of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 1
through A 6 1
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring
Logs as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
