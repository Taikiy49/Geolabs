SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Lanai is an extinct shield volcano built by eruptions at the summit
dome and along three rift zones more than 1.20 to 1.46 million years ago. The principal
rift zone trends northwestward as a broad ridge, and is responsible for the conspicuous
elongation of the island in that direction. A less conspicuous bulge on the southern side
of the island is a result of volcanic extrusion on the southwest rift zone. The rift zones
intersect at the island summit. The summit of the shield volcano collapsed to form a
caldera from which a shallow graben, bordered by an echelon of step faults, extends
south-southeast toward Manele Bay. The caldera was largely, but not completely filled
by lava flows, and the present Palawai Basin at the south central portion of the island is
an alluvial filled remnant of the ancient caldera.
The Island of Lanai was built by the extrusion of thin-bedded a’a and pahoehoe
tholeiitic basaltic flows that are generally inclined at about 6 to 15 degrees from
horizontal (where not disturbed by faulting). Volcanic rocks on the Island of Lanai are
grouped as the Lanai Volcanic Series. The near-surface developed soils generally
consist of well-drained, fine-textured and moderately fine-textured soils derived from
volcanic ash and the in-situ weathering of the igneous rocks. The formation of the Island
of Lanai was also the result of large changes in sea-level that caused submergence and
re-emergence of the land mass through geologic time. Faulting and stream erosion in
conjunction with changes in sea-level are also responsible for the high sea cliffs along
the southern and western coastlines.
The project site is northerly from the Palawai Basin and is at the margin of the
upland plateau and foothills comprising the central mountain portion of the island. Most
of the soils in the project area are residual and saprolitic soils, derived from the in-situ
weathering of volcanic ash and igneous rock. In general, the residual and saprolitic soils
grade to basaltic rock formation with depth.

2.2 Site Description
The Well No. 3A Pump Outfitting project is about 1 mile east of Lanai City on the
Island of Lanai, Hawaii. The project site is accessed via a dirt road on the outskirts of
town and resides at about the 1,800-foot elevation on the side slope of Kapano Gulch
as shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
A new water well, identified as Well No. 3A, was recently constructed adjacent to
the existing Well No. 3. Ground surface elevations range between about 1,830 feet
Mean Sea Level (MSL) at the entrance road to about +1,850 feet MSL at the well-head.
Based on our observations, the existing well-head site is rough-graded and is generally
level to very slightly sloping towards the south. At the time of our exploration, some
existing shed structures and concrete well pads were observed at the well-head.
Based on our observations, some existing steep and exposed cut slopes on the
order of about 12 to 25 feet high were observed adjacent to the existing shed structures
at the well-head and at the well-head entrance as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The
cut slopes expose highly erosive saprolitic soils with some embedded highly weathered
rocks (cobble size materials). The existing cut slopes show signs of distress and slope
instability including open cracks and talus accumulations of fallen material as a result of
the slow raveling of friable soil and rock materials and some looming larger slope
failures involving localized blocks of saprolitic soil material.

2.4 Seismic Design Considerations
Based on the 1997 Uniform Building Code (UBC) (current code used by the
County of Maui), the Island of Lanai is located within Seismic Zone 2B with an effective
peak ground acceleration of 0.20g. Therefore, the project site may be subjected to
seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed for the
project. The following sections provide discussions on the seismicity of the Island of
Lanai and the soil profile for seismic design.
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but
most are so small that they can only be detected by instruments. However, some of
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate
damage.
W.O. 6141-00 GEOLABS, INC. Page 5

In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes directly associated with the movement of magma are
concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island
of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) are
related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking
diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii
has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); however,
earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of Hawaii.
To a lesser degree, the Island of Lanai has experienced earthquakes greater than
M5+. An earthquake with a Magnitude 6.8 occurred on February 20, 1871 near the
Island of Lanai. As a result of the earthquake, portions of the island fell into the sea.
Huge boulders were hurled from the mountainsides, and ravines were filled with
debris of rocks and trees. In addition, several large cracks opened on different parts
of the island.
2.4.2 Site Classification
In general, the test pits encountered stiff to hard residual and saprolitic soils. We
believe that residual and saprolitic soils and basalt rock may be extrapolated to
depths of about 100 feet below the ground level. Based on the above, seismic
design of the proposed structures may be based on a Soil Profile Type Sp in
accordance with Table 16-J of the Uniform Building Code (1997).

SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the competent subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we
recommend supporting the proposed building on a shallow foundation system
consisting of spread and/or continuous footings. As an alternative, the foundations may
also consist of thickened-edge slab footings. The footings should be embedded a
minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades. The soils encountered
at the bottom of footing excavations should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction.
We anticipate that concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the floors of the
proposed building. Due to the relatively high moisture content of the in-situ clayey soils,
we recommend providing a minimum of 6 inches of non-expansive select granular fill
material below the slabs to serve as a protective layer or working platform.
In addition, we recommend properly preparing the subgrade soils by scarifying
the subgrade soils to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioning the subgrade

minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The underlying subgrade soils should be
wetted and kept moist until the final placement of slab concrete or pavement. Where
shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we recommend
preparing the soils again as recommended.

3.2  Slabs-On-Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the floors of the new
pump control building. Due to the relatively high moisture content of the in-situ clayey
soils, we recommend providing a minimum of 6 inches of non-expansive select granular
fill material (capping fill) below the slabs to serve as a protective layer or working
platform. The capping fill should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction.
For the interior building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic), we recommend
providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill below the slabs for uniform
support. The cushion fill should consist of open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67
gradation) and would also serve as a capillary moisture break. Where slabs are
subjected to vehicular traffic or sustained vibrations, a minimum of 6 inches of
compacted aggregate subbase course should be provided below the slabs in lieu of the
4-inch thick cushion fill layer. The aggregate subbase course layer should be
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.
To reduce the potential for excess moisture infiltration and subsequent damage
to floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel
cushion layer. Flexible floor coverings should be considered above the floor slab
because they can better mask minor slab cracking.

3.3 Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography, we anticipate minor grading at the site. At the
on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be cleared and

additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and engineered fill placement to

compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers
to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum
dry density.

recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above. Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor
drainage may require over-excavating the soft areas and replacing these areas with

3.4 Soil Resistivity
A resistivity test was performed to evaluate the corrosivity of the subsurface soils
encountered at the project site. The resistivity was determined in general accordance
with ASTM G 57.
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In
general, the lower the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried
metallic structure. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will
contribute to corrosion to metallic objects. Our test indicate that the soils at the site have
a resistivity on the order of about 2,900 ohm-cm. Based on the results of the resistivity
test, the soils at the site may be considered very corrosive.
3.5 Utility Line Trenches
We understand that new water and drain lines are planned for the project. In
general, good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling
of the trenches for the new utilities. The contractor should determine the method and
equipment used for trench excavation, subject to practical limits and safety
considerations. In addition, the excavations should comply with the applicable federal,
state, and local safety requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench
shoring design and installation.
In general, for utility line support, we recommend using a granular bedding,
consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), under the
pipes. Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33,
No. 67 gradation), should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the
compaction effort of the backfill, thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes. It is

critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes.
Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage.
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on-site soils
with a maximum particle size of 3 inches or select granular fill material. The backfill
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in maximum
8-inch level loose lifts, and mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence. Where trenches are
below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement finish
grades should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction.
3.6 Pavements
We understand flexible pavements will be constructed for the project. In general,
we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the access road would be light and would
consist primarily of passenger vehicles with some light trucks only. Therefore, we have
assumed generally light traffic loading conditions for pavement design purposes. On this
basis, we recommend using the following pavement design for the project.
Flexible Pavement Section
2.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction)
8.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
The subgrade soils under the pavement areas should be scarified to a minimum
depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture, and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction.
CBR tests and/or field observations should be performed on the actual subgrade
soils during construction to confirm the above design section is adequate. The
aggregate base course should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates compacted to a
W.O. 6141-00 GEOLABS, INC. Page 13
Hawaii ° California

minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. In addition, the pavement surfaces should
be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off the
pavements. Surface water ponding should not be allowed on-site during or after
construction.
3.7 Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding. It is also advised to
install gutter systems around the building and to divert discharge away from the
foundation and slab areas. Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
slabs should also be avoided. Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the
subsoils. Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to
drain surface run-off away from the slabs and foundations.
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent loss of strength and/or heaving of the near-surface clayey
soils. The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings
immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration.
