Based on our field exploration, the project site is underlain by volcanic ash with
clinker materials and dense basalt formation extending down to the maximum depth
explored of about 25 feet below the existing ground surface. The thickness of volcanic
ash with clinker ranged from about 5 feet in Boring No. 6 to the maximum depths
explored of 16 feet in Boring Nos. 2 and 3. It should be noted that dense basalt
formation was encountered in Boring Nos. 1 and 6 only. We did not encounter
groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field exploration.
In general, we envision that most of the cut slopes would expose volcanic ash
with clinker materials. In general, we believe that the cut slopes planned at the site
should be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or
flatter. The new infiltration basin is located in a sloping area with limited space.
Therefore, considering the site limitations and intended use, we believe that cut slopes
may be steepened to 1H:1V or flatter for the infiltration basin site only. Provisions to
protect the slope face by diverting surface water away from tops of cut slopes and
placing geotextile fabric or slope planting should be considered.
A total of four constant head percolation tests were conducted in selected
borings drilled to provide data for design of the proposed infiltration basin. Based on
our field percolation tests, we believe that an average hydraulic conductivity of about
0.01 feet per minute may be assumed in the subsurface materials encountered at a
depth of about 16 to 25.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The hydraulic
conductivity value roughly corresponds to an infiltration rate of about 100 gallons per
day per square foot of surface area at the location and depths tested. It should be
emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the on-site materials is highly variable due
to the presence or absence of significant voids in the lava or the presence of embedded
clinker seams, which greatly affect the disposal capacity of the infiltration basin.

1.1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed South Kohala Wastewater Treatment Plant Improvements
project within Mauna Kea Resort in the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii.
The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map,

geotechnical recommendations for the design of the proposed facility improvements
only.
The recommendations provided herein are intended for the design of site
grading, foundations, slabs-on-grade, retaining structures and underground utilities only.
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted
at the end of this report.
1.2 Project Considerations
The South Kohala Wastewater Treatment Plant (WWTP) is within the existing
Mauna Kea Resort in the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii. The project
involves upgrading the existing wastewater treatment system facility including the
following:
e Installation of a packaged filter unit (dual units) mounted on a depressed
concrete pad (vault) down to as much as 5 feet below ground level. The filter
steel tanks will be sheltered by an open sided metal roofed structure.

about 30 feet long to house ultraviolet lamps for R-1 effluent disinfection.
The top of the channel will be a foot above the existing ground.
° Construction of an infiltration basin to dispose effluent from the facility.
Based on the preliminary grading plan, we envision that site grading for the
construction of infiltration basin will generally consist of cuts and fills of up to about 6 to
8 feet. to achieve design grades at the project site.

2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an
area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of five
shield volcanoes, which include the following: Kohala (long extinct); Mauna Kea (activity
during recent geologic time); Hualalai (last erupted in 1801); and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea (both still active).
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Mauna Kea, which has
formed the west-central portion of the island. The project site appears to be underlain by
basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea, which were
deposited during the Pleistocene Epoch (Stearns and Macdonald, 1946).
The Hamakua Volcanic Series are typically composed of basaltic lava flows,
which are mantled by a relatively thin layer of palagonitized ash, locally referred to as
Pahala Ash. The ash soil at the project site differs from Pahala Ash found in other
portions of the Island of Hawaii in that the in-situ moisture content is generally lower,
and the soil exists in a dry, friable state. The soils at the site have been subjected to
wind erosion, resulting in the formation of a desert pavement on the surface. This desert
pavement is a surficial layer of cobbles and gravel, which were left behind as the finer
ash was blown away.
The lava formation encountered at the site appears to be of both a’a and
pahoehoe type flows, which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean. A’a
lava is typically characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface resembling
a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders. Pahoehoe is
characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular (porous) rock.
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas, especially pahoehoe type
lava. Cavities form when the lava is still in a molten state, and represent both lava tubes
(intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form when
W.O. 6017-00 GEOLABS, INC. Page 4

molten lava drains from the cooling flow, leaving a hollow tube-like structure which may
extend a large longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets are generally
smaller in horizontal extent.
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions. In general, water is
transmitted through the porous rock matrix, along joints, fractures and _ inter-flow
contacts, cavities, and along clinker layers. Clinker and cavities serve as the primary
water transmission features because of their high permeability.

2.4 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in
the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to
volcanic activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small that
they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of the earthquakes
are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate damage.
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the
Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent)
are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking

3.1.1 Site Preparation
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site to
reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials to be used as
embankment fill materials. The contractor should exercise caution while clearing
and grading near collapsed lava tube features and/or cavities, which may represent
potential lava tubes or cavities that may further collapse under the load of heavy
construction equipment.
Finish subgrades in cut areas and areas to be filled should be proof-rolled with a
minimum 20-ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes to assist in
detecting and possibly collapsing near-surface cavities. The vibratory drum roller
used for the proof-rolling operations should be operated at a speed of about
300 feet per minute.

3.1.2 Fills and Backfills
In general, we recommend capping fill areas with a minimum 2-foot thick layer of
structural fill materials. Structural fill materials should consist of well-graded
granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with
sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass. In
addition, structural fill materials should contain less than 30 percent particles
passing the No. 200 sieve by weight. In general, the excavated on-site materials
may be re-used as a source of structural fill materials provided that the materials
are processed to meet the maximum particle size and gradation requirements
presented above.
Fill and backfill materials below the minimum 2-foot thick structural fill layer may
consist of general fill materials. General fill and backfill materials should consist of
well-graded granular materials, the majority of which are less than 12 inches with
an absolute maximum dimension of 18 inches. Materials between 12 and 18 inches
in particle size should be limited to about 15 percent or less of the total volume of
general fill materials. The excavated on-site materials, if less than 18 inches in
maximum dimension, may be used as a source of general fill or backfill materials
(except for trench backfill), provided that they are processed to meet the above
gradation requirements for general fill. If the excavated materials do not contain
sufficient fines to produce a well-graded material, off-site borrow or on-site rock
crushing of larger rock fragments or boulders should be considered to provide the
required gradation and particle sizes.

3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. General fills and backfills
should be placed in level lifts of about 18inches in loose thickness,
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water

content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.
Compaction should be accomplished by using sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers,
or other types of acceptable compaction equipment.
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of
rocks, boulders, and/or cobbles. Instead, a testing program should be conducted at
the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a Geolabs

3.1.5 Cut and Fill Slopes
In general, we envision that most of the cut slopes would expose volcanic ash with
clinker materials. In general, we believe that the cut slopes planned at the site
should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. We understand that
the new infiltration basin is located in a sloping area with limited space. Therefore,
we understand that the use of steeper cut slope inclination is desired in this area.
In general, we believe that cut slopes may be steepened to 1H:1V or flatter for the
infiltration basin site only, considering the site limitations and intended use. A
Geolabs representative should observe and map the cut slopes during construction
to evaluate whether the exposed cut slope materials are consistent with our
assumptions.
It should be noted that some raveling and erosion of steepened cut slopes may
occur over time. Therefore, provisions to protect the slope face by diverting surface
water away from tops of cut slopes and placing geotextile fabric or slope planting
should be considered.
In general, permanent fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V
or flatter. This assumes that the fill slopes will be constructed using well-graded
granular materials (structural and general fills). In addition, fills placed on slopes
steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched. A typical keying and benching
detail is provided on the Slope Detail, Plate 3.
In general, the filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in
level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement
recommendations. Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back
to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. The fill slope face
should be finished to a relatively smooth and well-compacted surface. In addition,
surface water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes, and slope planting
should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for significant
erosion of the finished slopes.

3.2 Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and anticipated grading
activities, we recommend using shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to
support the new lightly-loaded structures (one to two-stories) planned. As an alternative,
foundation support for the new structures may consist of thickened-edge slab footings.
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used
for the design of footings bearing on either the compacted structural fill or on-site
materials. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by
50 percent for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces.
In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the
lowest adjacent grade. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on slopes should
be embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance measured from the
outside edge (at the base of the footing) to the face of the slope is no less than 6 feet.
In addition, foundations next to other foundations, utility trenches, easements or
other retaining walls should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the structure or utility trench, or the footings
should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement.
Soft and/or loose materials that may be encountered at the bottom of footing
should be over-excavated until dense materials are exposed in the footing excavation.
The over-excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill materials,
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Alternatively, the bottom of the footing may
extend down to bear directly on the underlying competent material.
If the foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with our
recommendations, we estimate the total foundation settlements to be less than 1 inch.

Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on similar materials may
be on the order of about 0.5 inch or less.
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed

3.3. Slabs-On-Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the floors of the new
structures. For the interior building slabs (not subject to vehicular traffic), we
recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill below the slabs for
uniform support. The cushion fill should consist of open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33,
No. 67 gradation) and would also serve as a capillary moisture break. Where slabs are
subject to vehicular traffic or sustained vibrations, a minimum of 6 inches of compacted
aggregate base course should be provided below the slabs in lieu of the 4-inch thick
cushion fill layer. The aggregate base course layer should be moisture-conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction.
To reduce the potential for excess moisture infiltration and subsequent damage
to floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel
cushion layer. Flexible floor coverings should be considered above the floor slab
because they can better mask minor slab cracking.

3.4.4 Drainage
In general, retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the potential for
build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a
12-inch wide zone of permeable material, such as open-graded gravel
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), placed directly around a_ perforated pipe
(perforations. down) at the base of the retaining wall. The perforated pipe should
discharge to an appropriate outlet or weepholes.
As an alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or
EnkaDrain, may be used instead of the permeable drainage material. The
prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a
perforated pipe at the base of the retaining wall. Unless covered by concrete or

3.6 Percolation Tests
A total of four constant head percolation tests were conducted in selected
borings to provide data for design of the proposed infiltration basin planned at the
project site. The percolation tests were performed at depths of about 16 to 25.5 feet
below the ground surface.
Based on our field percolation tests, the subsurface materials generally have
coefficient of permeability (k) of about 7.8 x 10° to 1.8 x 107 feet per minute, which
correspond to about 4.0 x 10° and 9.1 x 10° centimeters per second at the locations
tested. Based on the field percolation test results, we believe that an average hydraulic
conductivity of about 0.01 feet per minute may be assumed in the subsurface materials
encountered at a depth of about 16 to 25.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The
hydraulic conductivity value roughly corresponds to an infiltration rate of about
100 gallons per day per square foot of surface area at the location and depths tested.
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the on-site materials is
highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids in the lava or the
presence of embedded clinker seams, which greatly affect the disposal capacity of the
infiltration basin. In general, the greater the number of clinker seams and/or voids

The finished grades outside the buildings and other structures should be sloped
to shed water away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for

away from the foundations and slabs.

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling six borings,
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending to depths of about 5 to 25 feet below
the existing ground surface. We drilled the borings using a truck-mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools. The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving
a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.
Some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample
depths.
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113,
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description.
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock

APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
As part of our field exploration program, we conducted four constant head
percolation tests at the project site. In general, the drilled borings had a diameter of
about 3.5 inches and extended to depths of about 16 to 25.5 feet below the ground level
at the time of the test. A water truck was used to pump clear water into the boreholes
for the constant head test method. The flow rate was recorded by timing and
calculating the volume as a relatively constant flow rate was achieved. The percolation
test results are presented on Plates C-1 through C-4.
