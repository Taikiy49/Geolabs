1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the FY02 Replace Hydrant Fueling System project located within Hickam Air
Force Base in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of
the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses
for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of
foundations slabs on grade retaining structures site preparation and pavements only
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at
the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is located to the south of the existing nine hole golf course in the
central portion of Hickam Air Force Base AFB in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
The project site is approximately bounded to the north by Moffet Street to the east by

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from
two separate submarine shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano
Waianae Shield is estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in age 2 7 to 3 4 million years
ago and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The Koolau Shield is
estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age 2 2 to 2 5 million years ago and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that the Waianae
Volcano became extinct while the Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank is
partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu After a long period of volcanic
inactivity during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic
activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations
These more recent volcanic eruptions are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of
depositional and erosional events due to glacio eustatic sea level changes and
post erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch The Koolau Shield was
built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows Following
the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano alluvial materials were deposited in an
estuarine and deltaic environment in the Pearl Harbor area The sea level fluctuated
several times receding perhaps 90 meters below and rising about 30 meters above the
present sea level Concurrent with a rise in sea level during the late Pleistocene Epoch
eruption of the Salt Lake Makalapa member of the Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the
older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash which consolidated to form volcanic tuff
locally called mudrock layers Subsequent erosion dissected and thinned those
mantles of tuff formation
During the Recent Epoch as the sea level rose to its current stand the area of the
project site was covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of soft and loose

2 4 Soil Profile for Seismic Design
Our field exploration at the site generally encountered dense surface fills and hard
coral formation overlying tuff rock formation and medium dense to dense coralline deposits
at greater depths The surface fills generally consisted of dense to very dense sandy
gravel on the order of about 1 to 1 5 meters thick The coral formation and tuff rock
formation encountered in our borings were generally severely fractured and ranged from
medium hard to hard soft rock is harder than very stiff to hard soil
Based on the average penetration resistance N values of the subsurface
materials encountered below the foundation subgrade level we believe that the project
site may be classified as a Stiff Soil Profile Therefore we believe that the seismic
design of the building structure at the project site may be designed based on a Soil Profile
Class D in general accordance with Table 3 1 of Seismic Design For Buildings

3 1 Foundations
Based on the existing site topography and the generally dense subsurface
conditions encountered at the site we believe that shallow foundations consisting of
isolated spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the new structures
planned at the project site Considering the critical nature of the fuel storage tanks and the
potential for localized soft loose zones in the upper fill materials we recommend that a
300 mm thick layer of compacted aggregate subbase material be provided below the
bottom of footings and slab subgrades for uniform support
For the fuel storage tanks an allowable bearing pressure of up to 200 kilopascals
kPa may be used for design of the perimeter ring footings bearing on 300 mm of

anticipated at the project site The 300 mm compacted aggregate subbase course

in the tank farm area These bearing values are for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 when considering transient loads such as those caused by

the footings for the structural loads

3 7 Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the light pole foundations because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations
However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the
following conditions would have an impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft
foundations
Dense Coral Formation
Based on our field exploration the proposed new light pole locations are generally
underlain by surface fills overlying a medium hard to hard coral formation at
relatively shallow depths Therefore coring into the potentially hard coral formation
should be expected during the drilled shaft construction Appropriate drilling
equipment and coring tools should be utilized by the drilled shaft contractor to
install the drilled shafts to the depths and dimensions recommended herein
Workmanship
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 150 to 225 mm range should be used
to provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soil The
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for
segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix If pre cast concrete pole
bases are used the gap between the pole base and the surrounding soils should
be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete A representative from Geolabs should

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 extending to depths of approximately
9 5 to 22 3 meters below the existing ground surface A bulk sample designated as
Bulk 1 was obtained of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics The approximate locations of the borings and bulk sample are shown on
the Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 3 The borings were drilled using truck mounted
drilling equipment equipped with hollow stem augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 75 mm OD Modified California sampler with a 63 5 kilogram hammer
falling 760mm In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and
Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 50 mm OD standard penetration sampler using
the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last
300 mm of a 45 mm drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the coral formations encountered at the site were obtained using
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the hollow core of the
bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if of

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 100 mm in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 750 mm of sound material is recovered from a core run
the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock

RQD value would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous
methods of excavation

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the logs of borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Three sets of corrosivity tests including minimum resistivity
chloride contents and sulfate contents tests were
performed by E L Pacific on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration to
evaluate the corrosivity of the in situ soils to buried metallic and concrete substructures
The results of these tests are summarized on Plate B 1
Six sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 ASTM D 1140 were performed on
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on
Plates B 2 and B 3
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a
bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The test results are presented on Plate B 4
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationship The test results are presented on Plate B 5

APPENDIX D
Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the in situ soils was evaluated by performing two falling head
permeability tests in Boring No 3 drilled at the proposed pump house building location
The drilled boring had a diameter of about 100 mm and the tests were conducted at
depths of about 3 0 and 4 6 meters below the existing ground surface at the time of the
test
The falling head method was performed by filling the boreholes with water and
allowing the water to infiltrate into the ground The holes were cased to a depth of about
1 5 meters at the time of the test The water levels and corresponding times were
recorded for the calculation of subsurface soil permeability The results of the permeability
tests conducted are presented on Plates D 1 and D 2
