In accordance with our fee proposal dated April 20, 2011, we have provided 
geotechnical engineering services in support of the design of the Apple Store Ala Moana 
Center Renovation project in the Ala Moana Shopping Center in Honolulu, Hawaii.  The 
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This letter report summarizes the work performed on the project and presents our

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
Based on the information provided, we understand that the renovation requires 
removal of three interior columns in the store, which will result in increased loading on the 
six surrounding columns. The existing columns extend from the mall level to ground level 
parking and are supported on 16.5-inch octagonal or 12-inch square precast and 
prestressed concrete piles. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that the six involved columns 
are either supported on three or four pile groups. Based on the information presented in 
the geotechnical report by Dames & Moore, dated August 3, 1964, each concrete pile was 
designed for an allowable bearing capacity of 100 tons for a 16.5-inch octagonal concrete 
pile or 75 tons for a 12-inch square concrete pile end-bearing on the coral ledge. Pile 
installation records were not available at the time of this report preparation. 
Based on the information provided by the project structural engineer, we 
understand that the original design column load is about 600 kips. An additional 100 kips 
of structural load will be generated due to the column removal.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the majority of the western one-third of the 
island. The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene

(Ice Age) in age and forms the bulk of the eastern two-thirds of the island. After a long 
period of volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the 
Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and 
tuff cone formations. These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano and on the coastal 
plain of Southern Oahu. Therefore, the geomorphology and subsurface conditions in the 
area are directly related to the glacio-eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during the 
Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age) and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain. The coastal 
plain was built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano, which forms the eastern 
two-thirds of the Island of Oahu. The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of 
alluvium derived from erosion of the volcano, interbedded with coral reefs and associated 
deposits. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. Most of the coastal plains were developed during the 
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly. As the glaciers grew and 
advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell 
below the present stands of the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of 
water became available such that the sea levels rose to above its present level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
The subsurface conditions in the Ala Moana/Kaka’ako area are dominated by 
fossil Pleistocene Age coral/algal reef deposits and related deposits, which are 
intercalated with terrigenous sediments, laid during the higher stands of the sea. 
Therefore, the project site is generally underlain by deposits consisting of calcareous 
sediments and lagoonal deposits. A surface layer of man-made fill was placed over these 
deposits for the development of the Ala Moana/Kaka’ako area within the last century. 
FIELD EXPLORATION 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3. The borings extended to about 
65 feet below the existing ground surface using a skid-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous-flight solid stem augers and HQ casing. The boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by 
testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D 2487,

Graphic representations of the materials encountered and other observations, such as

Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D 1586, Standard Test Method for Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of 
Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer 
falling 30 inches. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 
6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of rock and/or hard materials encountered at the project site were 
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation.  Core drilling is a rotary 
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation.  The rock and/or hard 
material left in the hollow core of the bit are mechanically recovered for examination and 
description. 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of soil/rock masses.  Recovery is defined as the actual length of material 
recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.  For example, if 
3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 
74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) also is a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses.  RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting 
drilling-induced fractures or breaks.  If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5-foot core run, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of 
Borings as RQD = 50%.  Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of 
the rock, based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and 
Minerals." 
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the 
coral/rock, its relative quality, brittleness, and fissile characteristics.  A dense rock 
formation with a high RQD value would be very difficult to rip and would probably require 
more arduous methods of excavation.

LABORATORY TESTS 
Samples recovered from our field exploration were visually examined and 
classified in our laboratory in Honolulu, Hawaii. Selected samples were tested to evaluate 
the physical characteristics and engineering properties of the soils encountered. The 
laboratory testing was formulated with the emphasis on evaluating the moisture, density, 
plasticity, gradation, and shear strength of the soils encountered. 
Moisture Content tests were performed on selected soil samples to aid in defining 
the interface between the various soil layers encountered and correlating the layers 
between the borings. The moisture content tests were performed in general accordance 
with ASTM D 2216. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths in Appendix A. 
To provide information on the particle size distribution of the soils encountered, 
three Sieve Analysis tests were performed to evaluate the gradation characteristics and to 
aid in soil classification. The tests were performed in accordance with ASTM C 117 and 
C 136. Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B-1 of Appendix B.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
three borings, each located adjacent to columns at row/grid lines 73/E2, 73/E3 and 75/E3, 
as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings extended to depths of about 65 to 70 feet 
below existing ground surface. 
In general, the subsurface conditions at the site consist of about 2 to 8 feet of 
surface fill and about 12 to 20 feet of loose/soft lagoonal deposit overlying a coral ledge 
(coral/limestone formation) and coralline deposit extending to the maximum depth drilled of 
approximately 70 feet below the existing ground surface. 
It should be noted that the coral ledge encountered at the site is highly variable in 
thickness and in hardness.  The coral ledge was encountered within depths ranging from

The coralline deposits encountered at the site consist of medium dense silty 
coralline sands and gravel with localized loose and dense zones extending to the

Groundwater was encountered at a depth of about 2 feet below the ground surface

water levels are expected to vary with tidal fluctuations.  It should be noted that water 
levels also may vary with seasonal precipitation, storm surge conditions, groundwater 
withdrawal, and other factors. 
MICROPILE FOUNDATIONS 
As previously mentioned, it is proposed to remove three existing interior columns 
for the store renovation.  Therefore, additional structural loads from the removed columns 
will be redistributed onto adjacent columns. Based on the structural evaluation, the 
unfactored column loads including dead, live and seismic loading on each column will 
increase by about 100 kips.  
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings and tight access 
conditions with limited headroom available, we recommend using a deep foundation 
system consisting of micropiles for support of additional column loads. The capacity of the 
micropile foundation should be derived primarily from skin friction between the micropile 
shaft and the coral/limestone and coralline materials encountered in the borings. 
Due to highly variable subsurface conditions and lack of existing pile foundation 
installation records, we recommend installing the micropiles at each adjacent column to 
support the additional column loads generated by the interior column removal.  Therefore, 
the existing concrete piles under the existing columns would not be over-stressed due to 
increased column loads. 
A micropile consists of a small diameter (usually less than 12 inches) drilled and 
grouted pile with steel reinforcing.  The micropile foundation typically is constructed by 
drilling a borehole, placing reinforcing steel in the hole, and grouting the borehole. 
Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed readily in access restrictive 
environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions.  In addition, installation of 
the micropiles generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent structures, the adjacent 
soils, and the environment. 
Based on the availability of local material inventory, we envision that a micropile 
system with a minimum grout bulb diameter of either 5.5 or 7 inches may be used.  We 
recommend designing each micropile based on an allowable compressive load capacity of 
50 and 64 kips for 5.5 and 7-inch bulb diameter, respectively.  The allowable compressive 
load capacity for the piles is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third

(1/3) for transient loads, such as wind or seismic forces.  Based on the subsurface 
conditions encountered at the site, we recommend extending the micropiles through the 
loose lagoonal deposits and that they derive load bearing support from the medium dense 
to dense coral/limestone and coralline materials encountered at greater depths. 
Based on the subsurface conditions at the site and our experience with similar 
conditions, we believe that an ultimate bond stress of about 2,000 pounds per square foot 
(psf) may be developed at the interface between the grout bulb and the adjacent dense 
materials.  Therefore, to achieve the allowable compressive load capacity with a factor of 
safety of 2, we believe that each micropile would need to extend to a minimum depth of 
about 55 feet below bottom of pile cap.  The 55-foot depth is based on a 35-foot deep load 
bearing grout bulb developed within the coral/limestone and coralline materials with a 
20-foot grout zone (neglected) within the lagoonal deposits. 
To facilitate the micropile drilling and ensure the quality of the grouting, we 
recommend advancing steel casing to the bottom of the micropile during the drilling 
operation. The steel casing may be withdrawn during grouting operation while a minimum 
of 5 feet grout is maintained above the bottom of the casing at all time.  To reduce risk of 
micropile “necking” within the loose/soft lagoonal material, 20 feet of permanent steel 
casing should be left in-place at the top of each micropile. 
It should be noted that the bond stress between the grout bulb and the soil is highly 
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the 
contractor to install the micropile.  Therefore, the bond stress between the grout bulb and 
the soil may vary considerably between different contractors and micropile foundation 
systems. 
A pullout test (proof test) should be performed on a selected micropile during 
construction to confirm the load carrying capacity of the installed micropile. The pullout test 
should consist of subjecting the micropile to at least 200 percent of the design loads and 
the load should be held for at least 15 minutes.  Pullout test on the selected micropile is an 
integral part of the design of the micropile foundation system.  Therefore, we recommend 
conducting the pullout test under the observation of a Geolabs representative. 
A specialty contractor experienced in the construction of a micropile foundation 
system (minimum five projects) should perform installation of the micropiles. Due to the 
specialized nature of the micropile foundation construction, observation and testing of the 
micropile foundation system should be designated a “Special Inspection” item.  Therefore, 
a Geolabs representative (Special Inspector) should be present to observe the 
geotechnical aspects of the micropile foundation construction and testing. 
It should be noted that the micropile foundation system provides low lateral load 
resistance due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile.  Therefore, the lateral load 
resistance contributed by the micropiles should be neglected.

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted in this letter report are based, in part, 
upon information obtained from our review of the available information contained in the 
Dames & Moore report and our field borings conducted for the store renovation project. 
Variations of subsurface conditions from those assumed in our design may occur, and the 
nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is 
underway.  If variations then appear evident, Geolabs should be contacted to re-evaluate 
the recommendations presented herein. 
This report has been prepared for the exclusive use of Apple, Inc. for specific 
application to the Apple Store Ala Moana Center Renovation Project in accordance with 
generally accepted geotechnical engineering principles and practices.  No warranty is 
expressed or implied.  This letter report has been prepared solely for the purpose of 
assisting the architects and engineers in the design of foundations for the proposed 
project only. 
The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated soil conditions are commonly 
encountered.  Unforeseen subsurface conditions, such as perched groundwater, soft 
deposits, hard layers, or loose fills, may occur in localized areas and may require 
additional probing or corrections in the field (which may result in construction delays) to 
attain a properly constructed project.  Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund is 
recommended to accommodate these possible extra costs. 
This geotechnical exploration conducted at the project site was not intended to 
investigate the potential presence of hazardous materials existing at the site.  The 
equipment, techniques, and personnel used to conduct a geo-environmental exploration 
differ substantially from those applied in geotechnical engineering.
