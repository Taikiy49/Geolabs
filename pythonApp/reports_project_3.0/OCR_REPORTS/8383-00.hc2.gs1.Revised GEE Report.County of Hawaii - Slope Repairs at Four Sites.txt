SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the borings drilled at the slope sites within the 
project limits generally encountered a thin surface fill layer underlain by residual soils, 
saprolite and/or basalt formation to the maximum depths explored of about 60 to 167 feet 
below the existing ground surface. Groundwater was encountered at a depth of about 
52 feet below the existing ground surface in one of the drilled borings at the time of our 
field exploration. It should be noted that groundwater levels encountered in the drilled 
borings are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface 
water runoff, and other factors. 
We understand that heavy rainfall events during Hurricane Lane in 2018 caused 
shallow landslides at four sites along Old Mamalahoa Highway, and it is desired to repair 
the distressed slopes at these four sites. The heavy rainfall may have saturated the upper 
portion of the materials on the slope face causing a reduction in the shear strength of the 
materials.  In addition, the heavy rainfall runoffs will have the potential for high erosive 
power on the slope surfaces.  We believe that a combination of the above factors may 
have caused the shallow landslide to occur. 
Several remedial measures consisting of a soil nailed slope, a gabion retaining wall, 
and a mechanically stabilized earth (MSE) wall were evaluated for the project.  The 
constraints of the project consisted of limiting the improvements within the current 
right-of-way and above the ordinary high-water mark. 
The new gabion retaining wall and MSE wall remedial measures would require a 
deep excavation at the slope toe for the wall installation. This excavation may destabilize 
the existing slope unless the excavation is properly shored and would be very costly.  
Because of the above, the soil nailed slope remedial measure is recommended for the 
proposed repairs of the distressed slopes.  
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and 
recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the “County of Hawaii – Slope Repairs at Four Sites, Various Locations, 
Island of Hawaii” project from Papaikou to Hakalau on the Island of Hawaii. The project 
locations and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses. These recommendations are only intended for the design of slope repairs. 
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted 
at the end of this report.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii, the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago, covers an area of 
approximately 4,000 square miles.  The island was formed by the activity of five shield 
volcanoes, including the following:  Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (some activity 
during recent geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 and is considered to be 
dormant), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both are still active). 
The project site is located on the eastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield Volcano, 
which had been built up by the successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and 
pyroclastic materials. The most recent lava flows that comprise the ground surface and 
shallow subsurface at the project site are believed to be Pliocene to Pleistocene in age 
(approximately 1.2 million years to 2 million years before the present time) and belong 
to the upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series. 
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project sites are located along Old Mamalahoa Highway at various locations 
on the Island of Hawaii. The project locations and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. The approximate locations of each site are shown on the 
Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.4. The following subsections provide a brief description 
of the existing conditions at each site location. 
2.2.1 Site 1 - PW40 Site #222942 
This site is generally located along Old Mamalahoa Highway near the Hawaii 
Tropical Botanical Gardens. The site is located on an “S” curve with three slope 
failure locations, each about 30 feet long by 10 feet wide by 30 to 40 feet high.  Two 
failed slopes are upslope adjacent to the roadway and one failed slope is on the 
downslope side of the roadway. The slope faces are heavily vegetated.  Large trees 
with exposed roots near the top of the upslopes were observed.  The upslope faces 
generally exposed saprolite and weathered basalt.

The subsurface condition near Site 1 was explored by drilling and sampling 
one boring, designated as Boring No. 1, on Mamalahoa Highway extending to a 
depth of about 60 feet below the existing ground surface.  
Our field exploration indicated that the project site was generally underlain by a 
surface fill layer, residual soils, saprolite and basalt formation extending to the

surface fill layer was underlain by residual soils consisting of very stiff and hard 
clayey/sandy silt and clayey silt extending to a depth of about 29 feet below the

saprolitic materials consisting of silty sand and sandy silt were encountered to 
about 46.5 feet deep, followed by hard basalt formation extending to the maximum 
depth explored of about 60 feet below the pavement surface. Groundwater was not 
encountered in the boring at the time of our field exploration. 
2.3.2 Site 2 - PW40 Site #222941 
The subsurface condition near Site 2 was explored by drilling and sampling one 
boring, designated as Boring No. 2, on Mamalahoa Highway extending to a depth 
of about 61.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  
Based on our boring, the project site was generally underlain by a surface fill layer, 
residual soils, and saprolite extending to the maximum depth explored of 61.5 feet 
below the existing pavement surface.  The surface fill was about 1.5 feet thick and 
consisted of 6 inches of asphaltic concrete and very dense silty sand with some 
gravel.  The surface fill layer was underlain by saprolite and residual soils extending 
to the maximum depth explored of 61.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  
The residual soils consisted of hard clayey silt with gravel and the saprolite was

stiff to very stiff clayey silt. Groundwater was encountered at a depth of about 
52 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration.  The 
groundwater depth corresponds to an elevation of +424 Mean Sea Level (MSL).

3.1 
Slope Preparation 
At the on-set of construction, surface vegetation, trees, loose material, and 
deleterious material should be removed from the slope face and adjacent areas. Slope 
scaling should also be performed to remove loose rock fragments on the existing slope 
face. Care should be taken in removing the material so that the stability of the existing 
slope is not compromised. 
3.2 
Soil Nailed Slope 
A soil-nailed slope system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars 
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize the existing slope. A shotcrete facing is generally 
applied to the face of the slope providing the appearance of a conventional concrete 
retaining wall. To provide a natural rock appearance, the shotcrete facing may be 
sculpted and stained to match the appearance of the surrounding environment. The 
reinforcing bars provide additional tensile and shear strength to the wall. The reinforced 
soil mass behaves in a manner similar to a gravity retaining wall but lacks a footing. 
The soil nails should be installed by drilling a minimum 5.5-inch diameter hole 
with an inclination of approximately 15 degrees from horizontal. The inclination of the 
nails may be increased to avoid existing underground structures. The soil nail bar 
should consist of an ASTM A615 Grade 75 threaded bar with a minimum bar diameter 
of 1.125 inches. Due to the proximity of the soil-nailed wall to the Pacific Ocean, we 
recommend using a double corrosion protection system for the nails consisting of 
epoxy-coated bars surrounded by neat cement grout or sand-cement mixture with a 
minimum 28-day compressive strength of 4,000 pounds per square inch (psi). 
Based on the existing site conditions and subsurface conditions encountered, we 
recommend using design soil nail lengths ranging from 25 to 42 feet extending into the 
fill, residual soil, saprolite, and basalt formation encountered in our field exploration. The 
first nail should be installed about 2 feet below the top of the slope. The preliminary 
design soil nail lengths and spacings are summarized in the following table:

3.3 
Soil Nail Installation 
To facilitate the installation of the soil nails through the existing slope face, a 
shotcrete flash coat should first be applied to the face of the existing slope. Soil nail 
installation activities should avoid causing instability to the existing slopes.  
Potentially difficult drilling conditions may be encountered during the installation 
of the soil nails due to the presence of cobbles and boulders in the residual and 
saprolitic soils, and basalt rock formation. Utilizing a temporary casing may be required 
to maintain an open hole for the soil nail installation. 
3.4 
Soil Nail Testing 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated behind the soil-nailed slope, we 
recommend performing a minimum of one pre-production verification test on a sacrificial 
soil nail installed near the top of the slope at each of the four repair sites. In addition,

proof tests should be performed on at least 10 percent of the production soil nails during 
construction to confirm the bond stresses used in the design. 
The verification test should be loaded gradually to 150 percent of the design load 
for the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of about 200 percent of 
the design load. In addition, the load at every loading increment should be held for at 
least 10 minutes, and creep tests should be held for at least 60 minutes. The proof tests

the load should be held for at least 10 minutes (until stable). The proof test nails may be 
incorporated into the permanent soil nailed slope system provided they satisfy the test 
criteria. Pullout tests and creep tests on the soil nails are integral components of the 
design for the soil nailed slope system. Therefore, we recommend conducting pullout 
tests and creep tests in the presence of a Geolabs representative. 
3.5 
Shotcrete Facing 
Shotcrete placement should be performed by an experienced nozzleman. Prior to 
production shotcreting, it is recommended that unreinforced test panels of shotcrete be 
constructed for inspection. We understand that the shotcrete facing may be sculptured. 
Therefore, adequate thickness of the final facing should be provided to accommodate 
the sculptured surfacing. 
3.6 
Drainage 
The soil-nailed slope should be well-drained to reduce the potential for the 
build-up of hydrostatic pressures. Drainage elements consisting of 2-foot wide strips of 
a prefabricated drainage composite product may be used on the face of the slope 
before the application of a shotcrete flash coat used for temporary stabilization. The 
prefabricated drainage composite product should extend from the top of the slope to the 
base of the slope and hydraulically connected to a weep hole near the base of the 
soil-nailed slope.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths ranging from 
about 60 to 167 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.4. The borings were drilled 
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools. 
 Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock cores were described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested 
Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the 
International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-4.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch O.D. Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch O.D. standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description.  
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.

For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting 
any drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used 
to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical 
Properties of Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Nine Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. Test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
test results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Fifteen Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification.  Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plates B-2 through B-4. 
Seven Unaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012) were performed on selected 
core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the rock formation 
encountered. Unconfined compression test results are presented on Plate B-5. 
Three Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) 
were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of 
the soils. The undrained shear strength test results along with the shear stress-strain 
curves are presented on Plates B-6 through B-8. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D 3080) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the in-situ soils. The test results are 
presented on Plate B-9. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a 
bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-10.
