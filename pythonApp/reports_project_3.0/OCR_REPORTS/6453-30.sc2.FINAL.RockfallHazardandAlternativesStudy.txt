REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and
Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age 4 million years ago and forms the bulk of the western one third of
the island The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age 5 million years ago and forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Waianae and Koolau shields volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations mainly at
the southeastern portion of the Island of Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
filled pre existing depressions and accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and at the heads of valleys erosional
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock materials from the steep valley
walls and transportation down slope toward a valley axis primarily by gravity as colluvial
earth materials Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley
stream flow processes become dominant and the sediments are transported and
deposited as alluvial earth materials
The project site is on a knoll on the lower south facing flanking slope of Puu Mailiilii

Site Reconnaissance Method
A ground surface reconnaissance of the study slope was conducted on May 2 and
17 2012 The reconnaissance was performed under fair and dry weather conditions by a
two person team consisting of our senior geologist and geologist assistant In the absence
of a topographic map of the study slope we established a temporary baseline across the
top of the slope to serve as a basis for locating the approximate position of the boulder
deposits on the slope
The reconnaissance was performed by a foot traverse of the study slope area
Vegetation clearing had been performed by others prior to our site visit which permitted
good visibility of the character and setting of the rock deposits on the slope
In general the purpose of our site reconnaissance was to develop an inventory of
potential rockfall hazards that could affect the adjacent roadway along the toe of the slope
The slope was systematically traversed and potential rockfall hazards involving loose
unattached or precariously positioned rocks larger than about 2 feet in largest dimension
were documented for this report Field information including descriptions and photographs
were recorded for each individual boulder or boulder group identified as having some
potential for initiation of movement with a hazardous consequence
A visual assessment of the potential for rock movement low moderate or high
potential hazard was conducted based on the overall physical character of the deposit
We supplemented our assessment of potential rock movement by performing some
modeled rockfall simulation using the computer based Colorado Rockfall Simulation
Program CRSP to obtain a statistical review of the potential rockfall conditions
Furthermore we performed a visual assessment of the potential for a dangerous
consequence assuming the rock is released from the slope low moderate high potential
for dangerous consequence In general the number of rocks rock size and the location
of the hazards the relative level of potential hazard and the potential for a dangerous
consequence resulting from rock release provides useful information for the formulation of
appropriate rockfall mitigation controls

The CRSP is a computer program that is a widely accepted engineering tool used
to estimate potential rockfall behavior by simulating rockfall activity based on input
parameters that are assigned on a site specific basis The input parameters for this project
were assigned based on our observations and measurements collected in the field The
program provides a statistical analysis of potential rockfall activity based on the
topographic profile and other specific input information such as rock size shape and other
parameters used to quantify the typical ground surface conditions Information obtained
from the CRSP analysis includes the potential for rockfall initiation and the predicted
falling rock velocity bouncing height kinetic energy and roll out distance The output
information is useful to assist in the understanding of potential rockfall processes that may
occur and the site specific design of various rockfall mitigation schemes such as rockfall
impact barrier fences rockfall catchment ditches or other rockfall containment and control
systems The schematic slope profiles and the summarized data obtained from the CRSP
analysis are presented in Appendix B
Because a topographic survey was not available for our use we developed
schematic slope profiles based on limited measurements collected in the field The slope
profiles used in our analyses are presented in Appendix A Our rockfall simulation analysis
was performed using spherical and tabular shaped boulders of 3 4 and 5 feet in diameter
initiating from the specified positions on the modeled slope Numerical input coefficients
were used which approximated the observed typical ground surface relief and roughness
conditions For each rockfall simulation run 100 simulated source rocks of each rock
diameter were utilized to develop a statistical distribution of the results
It should be noted that our rockfall protection criterion for the design of various
falling rock interception systems rockfall impact barrier fence ditch berm structures is
defined as the probable interception and catchment of 90 percent of possible rockfall
hazards assessed by the computer rockfall simulation analysis CRSP The 90 percent
interception catchment criteria is a target that is commonly used in engineering practice for
evaluation criteria that can be quantified by a statistical and probability analysis using
model data for natural occurrences such as rockfall activity Since the analyses are based
on statistics and probability a 100 percent criterion is typically impractical to achieve
Based on the results of the CRSP analyses simulated falling rock is shown to have
a low potential for reaching the roadway due to the low gradient of the slope within the
rock release zone and the initial rock acceleration assuming an at rest condition prior to
movement Based on our analysis of the site conditions a slope steeper than about
2H 1V or some initial velocity acceleration expressed as momentum such as struck by
object or earthquake acceleration is needed to allow the simulated falling rock to continue
rolling on the slope to encroach at the roadway It should be noted that the results of the
CRSP analysis should be used to interpret potential rockfall conditions in conjunction with
experienced judgment and visual evaluation

Falling Rock Hazards
Rockfall is a type of natural mass wasting process that is in part responsible for the
evolution of mountain slopes Rockfall is a common natural hazard due to the steep
volcanic terrain comprising the mountains and valleys of the Islands of Hawaii Rockfall
activity involves the detachment of a rock mass from the slope face and the fall of rock
materials under the force of gravity Rockfall may involve a single unstable block of rock
outcrop or it may involve a rockslide which encompasses multiple rock components
Falling rock behavior can be complicated by the varying character of the ground
surface and topography encountered along the rock fall line Rocks may either roll
bounce or have combined movement vectors Falling rock may also hang up temporarily
or permanently on the slope without reaching the bottom under the effects of terrain
roughness topography vegetation and the presence of natural features such as berms
and terraces formed by other rock outcroppings
Under the current state of engineering practice combined with the use of proven
rockfall control methods it is still impractical to mitigate 100 of all potential rockfall
activity at a site However with careful engineering and quality construction during the
installation of appropriate rockfall mitigation controls the risk for rockfall activity can be
substantially reduced
For this project the identified potential rockfall hazards represent boulders that
appear to have been placed on the slope during early site development The rocks
represent surface boulders that are NOT part of the natural in situ coral rock formation
present at the site Therefore traditional evaluation of the character of the parent rock
outcrop such as weathering and fracturing are of less importance to the stability of the
deposit Thus the focus of our evaluation is based on the probable stability of the
deposits with respect to visual evaluation of the particular rock setting on the slope and the
relation of the boulder with other adjacent boulder components nesting or inter related
positioning of the boulders

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our field reconnaissance we identified a number of
potential rockfall hazards of relatively moderate to high risk that should be mitigated to
reduce the potential for a dangerous rockfall event In addition we identified a number of
potential rockfall hazards of relatively low risk that could also be mitigated if desired or
monitored for signs of future rock instability
We conducted computer rockfall simulation in an effort to evaluate the statistical
potential for rocks to reach the adjacent roadway Based on our computer rockfall
simulation results the existing slope conditions are not very conducive to sustaining falling
rock momentum assuming the rock begins to move from an at rest condition However
our experience suggests that idealized computer rockfall simulation results should not be
relied on as a primary method of hazard evaluation The computer simulation results
should be used in conjunction with experienced field judgment to obtain a more realistic
evaluation of the potential hazard conditions Thus even though the rockfall simulation
suggests a low potential for modeled rocks to reach the roadway our conclusions based
on combined visual assessment and the results of the simulation indicate that mitigation
should be performed A single rock reaching the roadway would present a dangerous
condition for the public and because many of the larger rocks are nested together the
potential exists for multiple rocks to reach the roadway as a result of the failure of a key
rock component For this reason we believe that in the interest of public safety rockfall
mitigation at the site should be performed to reduce the potential for a dangerous rockfall
event to affect the roadway

1 Boulder Demolition Removal
This option is the engineers preferred alternative and is considered to be a
comprehensive and permanent mitigation alternative The option generally consists
of the controlled demolition removal and disposal of potentially hazardous
boulders The work would involve the installation of temporary safety controls
around the work area to facilitate the safe and systematic demolition of boulders
marked for removal by the engineer Typically the boulders are reduced by
mechanical effort to manageable size rock fragments that are swept to the bottom
of the hill for collection and off site disposal Typically in difficult access settings
the boulders are broken up by a hydraulic rock splitting device inserted in
pattern drilled holes in the boulder Other methods may include pneumatic drilling
and chipping along pre existing rock joints fractures the use of a chemical
expansion compound in drilled holes and small controlled explosive charges set in
drilled holes Where site access conditions are favorable heavy construction
equipment such as a track excavator and hoe ram may be used to reduce the rocks
for removal and transportation
Advantages
Permanent mitigation option hazard elimination
Provide for a clean and stable appearing slope fronting the facility
Future site slope improvements such as slope landscaping could be
implemented without concern for boulder deposit stability

render the boulder removal as inconsequential to underlying material
