SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the existing pavement structure along Hawaii Kai 
Drive generally consisted of about 2 to 11 inches of asphaltic concrete underlain by 4.5 to 
15.5 inches of base/subbase material.  The average thicknesses of the asphaltic concrete 
and base/subbase material are about 2 to 3 inches and about 6 to 8 inches, respectively.  
The existing pavement structure appears to be thicker between Lunalilo Home Road and 
Kealahou Street, which ranges from about 4.5 to 11 inches of asphaltic concrete over 
about 5 to 15.5 inches of base/subbase material.  
Our field observations indicated that the pavements along Hawaii Kai Drive exhibits 
medium to high severity pavement distress.  We anticipate that the new finished pavement 
would need to match the existing concrete curbs and gutters; therefore, the rehabilitation 
of Hawaii Kai Drive should consist of milling (cold-planing) the existing asphaltic concrete 
and replacing with new asphaltic concrete and base.  
Based on the traffic data provided and the subgrade conditions along the 
alignment, the new flexible pavement sections for Hawaii Kai Drive should consist of the 
following: 
2.0 inches of AC on 6.0 inches of ACB (Kalanianaole Hwy. to Keahole St.) 
3.0 inches of AC on 6.0 inches of ACB (Keahole St. to Wailua St.) 
2.0 inches of AC on 5.0 inches of ACB (Lunalilo Home Rd. to 
Kealahou St.) 
2.0 inches of AC on 3.0 inches of ACB (Wailua St. to Kanoenoe St.) 
4.0 inches of AC (Kamilo St. to Lunalilo Home Rd.) 
It should be noted that the pavement from Kanoenoe Street to Paila Street appears 
to be in relatively good condition; therefore, we believe that pavement rehabilitation may 
not be needed between Kanoenoe Street and Paila Street.  If it is desired to conduct 
rehabilitation between Kanoenoe Street and Paila Street, then the flexible pavement 
section between Wailua Street and Kanoenoe Street should be used.  The text of this 
report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific pavement design 
recommendations.

1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering analyses 
performed for the Rehabilitation of City Streets, Unit 71 Hawaii Kai Drive project in the 
Hawaii Kai area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our 
analyses for the roadway rehabilitation project. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The Rehabilitation of City Streets project is part of an overall Pavement Survey 
Project covering various locations across the City and County of Honolulu. Generally, 
the Unit 71 project will involve rehabilitation of approximately 4 miles of Hawaii Kai Drive 
from Kalanianaole Highway to Kealahou Street in the Hawaii Kai area of Honolulu on 
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The roadway alignment of the pavement rehabilitation 
project is shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on the traffic information provided and considering the City Bus routes for 
Hawaii Kai Drive, we have divided Hawaii Kai Drive into five sections for pavement 
evaluation, which are the following: 
Kalanianaole Highway to Keahole Street 
Keahole Street to Wailua Street 
Wailua Street to Kanoenoe Street 
Kamilo Street to Lunalilo Home Road 
Lunalilo Home Road to Kealahou Street 
It should be noted that the pavement from Kanoenoe Street to Paila Street 
appears to be in relatively good condition; therefore, we believe that pavement 
rehabilitation may not be needed between Kanoenoe Street and Paila Street.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age.  After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations.  These series are 
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series.  The project site is located at the southern 
flank of the Koolau Mountain Range. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea.  When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations.  When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level.  
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and in the heads of valleys, the erosional 
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls 
and are transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as 
colluvium.  Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, 
alluvial processes become dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited 
as alluvium.

After removal of the existing asphaltic concrete layer to accommodate the new 
pavement structural sections, the exposed base materials or asphaltic concrete 
remnants should be proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton (static weight) drum roller for a 
minimum of eight passes to compact the exposed base materials. The drum roller 
should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. If broken up asphaltic 
concrete is exposed after the milling process, consideration should be given to complete 
removal of the broken up asphaltic concrete to expose the underlying intact asphaltic 
concrete or aggregate base materials before proof-rolling the base materials. 
If soft soils are exposed after the milling process, we recommend providing a 
stabilization layer in lieu of proof-rolling the soft subgrade, which may induce pumping 
condition.  The stabilization layer should consist of 8 inches of aggregate base course 
over a layer of geogrid (such as Tensar Triax TX 160 or equivalent) underlain by a 
woven geotextile fabric (such as Mirafi HP370 or equivalent) as shown on Plate 5. The 
aggregate base course should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T-180 
(ASTM D 1557). Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
In general, the contractor should vacuum-sweep the pavement areas 
immediately after cold-planing to remove gravel-sized materials and dust that was not 
picked up by the cold-planing operation. It should be noted that the vacuum-sweep 
should not preclude the dust control operation during cold-planing. In general, the 
contractor should begin paving no later than three working days after cold-planing 
begins. Finally, vacuum sweeping of cold-planed surfaces should also be performed 
immediately prior to paving.

3.6 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

Field Exploration - Borings 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway rehabilitation by drilling 
and sampling twenty borings, designated as Boring Nos. 301 through 320, extending to 
depths ranging from about 3.5 to 5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The 
approximate boring locations for this project are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 
through 3.18. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers and coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-20. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Field Exploration – Pavement Cores 
The boring exploration was supplemented by coring the existing pavement section 
to depths of about 1 to 1.7 feet below the existing pavement surface at 30 selected 
locations, identified as C-301 through C-330.  The approximate coring locations for this 
project are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.18. The corings were drilled 
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the corings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Corings, 
Plates B-1 through B-30.

Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Tests 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) testing was performed in the 20 boring 
locations (Boring Nos. 301 through 320) to obtain pavement support characteristics of 
the in-situ soils. The tests were performed using a Salem Dual Mass Dynamic Cone 
Penetrometer to a depth of about 2.5 to 5 feet below the existing pavement surface. 
The DCP device consists of two 16mm-diameter stainless steel rods with the 
lower rod containing an anvil, a replaceable 60o pointed tip and depth markings. The 
upper rod holds a 17.6 lb drop hammer with a 22.6 inch drop distance. At the beginning 
of a test, the cone is “seated” by dropping the hammer until the widest part of the cone 
is just below the testing surface at which point the initial penetration is recorded as 0. 
The operator then lifts and drops the hammer one or more times depending on the 
strength of the soil at the test location.  The number of blows for a minimum of 1-inch 
penetration is recorded.  The process is continued until the desired depth of testing is 
reached or the full length of the lower rod is buried. 
Data from our DCP testing indicates the penetration index (PI) which is simply the 
penetration distance of the cone for each drop of the hammer expressed as millimeters 
per blow. The penetration indices obtained from our tests were correlated with common 
pavement design parameters such as California Bearing Ratio (CBR). Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the DCP Logs, 
Plates D-1.1 through D-20.1.
