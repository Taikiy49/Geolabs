Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and
percolation testing performed for the FY16 OMA Repair by Replacement Cesspools with
Septic Systems project at Pohakuloa Training Area in the Pohakuloa area on the Island
of Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations based
on our field exploration field percolation testing laboratory testing and engineering
analyses The recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of trench
excavation pipe installation backfilling and leach field design only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

The project involves renovation of the existing Quonset huts Buildings
and 188 at the Pohakuloa Training Area PTA on the Island of Hawaii This project was
conducted as a modification to the work performed under our previous task orders and
reports entitled Percolation Testing Renovate Buildings Pohakuloa

Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from five shield
volcanoes Kohala Mauna Kea and Kilauea which have
coalesced to form one large island Of these five volcanoes Mauna Loa and Kilauea
are considered to be active and Hualalai is believed to be in a dormant stage with its
last activity in about 1803
Pohakuloa Training Area PTA is situated in the north central portion of the
Humu ula Saddle a broad plateau between Mauna Kea and Mauna Loa Based on an
available geologic map USGS 2007 the PTA area is underlain by rocks of the
Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea The map indicates that these Hamakua rocks
may be mantled by recent alluvium that would be outwash from the slopes of
Mauna Kea to the north Since these outwash materials are generally cindery in nature
it is difficult to differentiate between the alluvial soils and airlaid cinder from volcanic
activity from eruptions from adjacent cinder cones from Mauna Loa
In terms of engineering properties there is essentially no difference between the
cindery alluvium and airlaid cinder deposits Both behave as granular cohesionless
soils and are generally well drained

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
In general our field exploration indicated that the project site is underlain by
asphaltic concrete and or surface fills placed over medium dense to dense volcanic ash
medium dense to very dense volcanic cinder sands dense to very dense clinkers and
hard to very hard basalt rock formation at variable depths We did not encounter
groundwater within the depths of the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration
We anticipate that the new sewer lines would have invert depths ranging from
about 4 to 14 feet below the existing ground surface Based on the subsurface soil
conditions encountered we believe that conventional excavation methods may be used
for installation of the new sewer lines and proposed leach fields However excavations
into the dense clinkers and hard basalt rock formation may require the use of hoerams
and or chipping
Where excavations greater than 5 feet in depth are planned temporary shoring
or sloping and benching should be used unless stable rock formation is encountered
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by cinder sands
and clinkers over basalt rock formation at greater depths It is our opinion that the
cinder sands and clinkers may be classified as a Type C Soil for excavation purpose A
slope inclination of one and one half horizontal to one vertical may be used
for temporary cut slopes into the cinder sands and clinkers for open cut excavations
Where open cut excavations are not feasible trench walls would have to be cut
vertically requiring the use of shoring during construction For trench excavations
located adjacent to existing utility poles we recommend that a minimum setback
distance of 5 feet should be provided between the edge of the trench excavations and
the existing utility poles

Trench Excavation
Based on the plan and profile drawings provided we anticipate that the new
sewer inverts would be on the order of about 4 to 14 feet below the existing ground
surface Installation of the proposed sewer system will involve excavations into the
medium dense to dense volcanic ash medium dense to very dense volcanic cinder
sands dense to very dense clinkers and hard to very hard basalt rock formation
anticipated at variable depths It is anticipated that the volcanic ash and cinder sand
deposits may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such as a large
backhoe excavator However based on experience in the area and with similar
materials excavations into the dense clinkers and hard basalt rock formation may
require the use of hoerams and or chipping The contractor must exercise care to avoid
over ripping which would disrupt the structure of the basalt formation resulting in a
potential loss of bearing strength for improvements in the vicinity
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials are
based on our experience in the project vicinity and the field and laboratory data from the
borings drilled at the project site Contractors should be encouraged to examine the site
conditions and the subsurface data to make their own reasonable and prudent
interpretation
The excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance
equal to the depth of the excavation measured from the edge of the excavation in order
to reduce the potential for ground movement In addition the excavated soils should be
stockpiled at least 15 feet away from underground utilities to reduce the potential for
ground movement or subsidence which may damage the underground utility lines

Trench Excavation Support
We anticipate that excavation depths of up to about 15 feet below the ground
surface will be required for the installation of the new sewer system Deeper
excavations may be required where the sewer line will traverse existing underground
utilities Where excavations greater than 5 feet in depth are planned temporary shoring
or sloping and benching should be used unless stable rock formation is encountered
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by cinder
sands and clinkers over basalt rock formation at greater depths It is our opinion that
the cinder sands and clinkers may be classified as a Type C Soil profile for excavation
purpose Where space is available an open cut excavation may be used for the sewer
system construction A slope inclination may be used for temporary cut slopes
into the cinder sands and clinker materials Because of the granular nature of these
materials sloughing of the sides of the excavations may still be experienced during
construction This should be further evaluated by a qualified geotechnical engineer
during construction

providing a minimum setback distance of 5 feet between the edge of the trench
excavations and the existing utility poles

It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction The properly braced shoring is
essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent
ground into the excavation which may result in potential settlement or distress to
adjacent structures or other improvements
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation
Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to accurately estimate the
magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship such
as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed the subsurface
conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation
It is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed properly
and as early as practical The adjacent ground should be continuously monitored for
cracks dips and or other indications of movements with instruments until the sewer line
excavation is finally backfilled It should be noted that during and after installation of the
shoring system minor settlements may be possible Therefore it is recommended that
the contractor retain a qualified geotechnical engineer to design and evaluate the
shoring system used

Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important design
consideration For this project a minimum of 6 inches of granular bedding material
such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C33 No 67 gradation should be provided below
the pipe
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of
one fourth 1 4 the outside pipe diameter and should extend half way up the sides of the
pipe Prior to the placement of bedding material a Geolabs representative should

Trench Backfill
The first zone of backfill extending from the bedding material to at least 12 inches
above the top of the pipes should consist of select free draining granular material
ASTM C33 No 67 gradation to reduce the compaction effort required and resulting
stresses on the pipe In addition it is critical to use this free draining material to reduce
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in
backfill settlement and pipe damage
In pavement areas the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to
the pavement subgrade i e bottom of the aggregate base course layer should consist
of satisfactory materials per UFGS Section Satisfactory materials are
classified per ASTM D2487 as
SW SM and 2 inch minus Imported materials should be tested for conformance with
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use
As an alternative to facilitate the backfilling and reduce the time required for
applying compactive effort Controlled Low Strength Material CLSM may be used to
backfill the trenches The use of CLSM would also reduce the potential for future
settlement of the trench backfill under the repeated wheel loading from traffic on the
base roadways

Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements
Backfills should be moisture conditioned to 2 percent above the optimum
moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted
to at least 90 percent relative compaction The upper 3 feet of backfill excluding the
free draining granular material below the finished pavement grade should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557 Optimum

Settlements
Primary settlement of sewer lines is normally caused by the difference in the unit
weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill
material placed over the pipes The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the
underlying subsoils below the trench invert Based on our calculations settlement on
the order of less than 0 5 inch is anticipated for the project
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good
workmanship will be engaged during construction Additional settlement could occur if
improper trench support is used
3 7
Pavement Restoration
We envision that flexible pavement will be used for pavement restoration for this
project In general we anticipate the vehicle loading for the pavement will primarily
consist of traffic generally equivalent to residential use by passenger vehicles Based on
the subsurface conditions encountered in our exploration we recommend matching the
existing asphaltic concrete minimum 3 inches and aggregate base course minimum
8 inches for pavement restoration
The aggregate base course materials should consist of crushed basalt aggregate
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction Aggregate base course
materials should meet the material requirements of Section 703 06 in the Hawaii
Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction 2005

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the new sewer line alignments were explored by
drilling and sampling fifteen borings designated as Boring through SB 18
with the exception of 12 and SB 16 extending to depths ranging from about
10 5 to 16 feet below the existing ground surface In addition the subsurface conditions
at the proposed leach field areas were explored by drilling and sampling fifteen borings
extending to depths of about 11 to 19 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were
drilled using a truck mounted drill rig Mobile B 53 equipped with continuous flight
augers and coring tools
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are
described on the Soil Classification Log Key Plate A 0 2 Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 30
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description Rock cores were
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the

Recovery REC may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the
relative quality of rock masses where appropriate Recovery is defined as the actual

For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative

sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting
any drilling mechanical and handling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run in rock the RQD would be 50 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Four 4 one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on remolded samples to
evaluate the swelling potential of the volcanic ash soils The test results are
summarized on Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentation of the test
results is provided on Plate B 2
Eleven Grain Size Distribution tests were performed to
evaluate the gradation characteristics of selected soil samples and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plates B 3
through B 5
Eleven Uniaxial Compression tests ASTM D7012 Method C were performed on
selected core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the basalt
formation encountered The test results are presented on Plate B 6
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 7 through B 9
