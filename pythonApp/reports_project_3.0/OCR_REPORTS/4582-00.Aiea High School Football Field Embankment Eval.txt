GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING EXPLORATION
AIEA HIGH SCHOOL FOOTBALL FIELD EMBANKMENT EVALUATION
Dear Mr Miura
In accordance with our fee proposal dated we have conducted
a geotechnical engineering exploration at the embankment fill for the Aiea High School
Football Field in Aiea on the Island of Oahu Hawaii This report presents our preliminary
findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for the
subject project
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located near the intersection of Ulune Street and Kaamilo Place
on the existing football field of Aiea High School in the area of Aiea on the Island of Oahu
The general project site and vicinity is shown on the Project Location Plan Plate 1
It is our understanding that the football field will be improved by the addition of
material to form a crown in order to allow proper drainage for the turf growth However there
is a concern that the instability of the embankment fill on the southern side of the field may
jeopardize the proposed football field improvements The signs of instability include a large
crack in the ground adjacent and parallel to the slope distortion of the chain link fence along
the top of the slope and presence of water as evidenced by localized green grass along
portions of the slope
The scope of our work included the exploration of the embankment slope along the
southern side of the athletic field to evaluate the overall stability of the slope and to provide
recommendations for structural improvements to increase the stability of the slope if
required
Our scope of work also included review of as built plans drilling of three borings along
the top of the embankment laboratory soil testing analysis of the slope stability conceptual
design of remedial measures and report preparation Final design of the remedial work and
future improvements to the football field are not included in our scope of work

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program for the project consisted of drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about 26 5 feet
below the existing ground surface along the edge of the track in the southeastern portion of
the field The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration disclosed that the project site is underlain by stiff to
very stiff reddish brown silty clay with gravel extending to the maximum depth drilled
Seepage or high moisture zones were not detected in the borings
Groundwater levels were not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration It should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to
seasonal precipitation temperature groundwater withdrawal and other factors
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on
the Logs of Borings in Appendix A
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on a selected
sample to evaluate its peak and residual shear strength characteristic The test results and
shear stress versus normal stress plots are presented on Plates B 1 and B 2
BRIEF SITE HISTORY
Based on a review of available construction drawings it appears that the football field
was constructed in about 1963 by cutting material from the northwestern end of the field and
filling the southeastern end Fills of up to about 20 feet high were required to meet the
design finished grades The embankment fill slope was designed at a ratio of up to one and
one half horizontal to one vertical A CRM retaining wall up to about 6 feet high
was constructed at the toe of slope along Ulune Street
The flood lights for the field were installed in about 1963 However due to leaning of
the poles remedial work was done in about 1980 The remedial work generally consisted
of adding piles to increase the lateral resistance of the poles Four of the poles are located
close to or on the embankment fill slope under study
POSSIBLE CAUSES OF SLOPE MOVEMENTS
The slope movements observed at the site were generally small and did not seem
to indicate mass instability Most of the movements were indicative of slow slope creep
type movements

Slope Ratio
The material used for the embankment fill consisted of silty clay residual soils Fine
grained soils such as these are normally limited to fill slope ratios of about 2H 1V The
original design utilized steeper slope ratios of 1 5HV Field observations indicate that
portions of the existing slope may have been constructed at even steeper ratios up to about
1H 1V
Fine grained clayey soils on slopes will tend to creep downhill over time due to
gravitational forces The creep tendency will increase with the steepness and height of the
slopes It is our opinion that the embankment slope grades are too steep for the material
used
Water Conditions
Water or moisture has a significant effect on the performance of soils Clayey soils
in particular have a tendency to absorb water and weaken Additionally water can erode
soil materials and cause isolated steepening of slopes
The embankment fill slopes exhibit signs of surface erosion resulting in localized
areas of steepened slopes which in turn increases the creep potential
The vegetation cover on the slope is sparse except for an approximately 35 foot long
portion of the slope along the southeastern end of the field It was reported that the grass
in this area is usually green indicating possible subsurface seepage or surface runoff This
portion of the slope is about 15 feet above the CRM wall at sidewalk level The slope surface
is irregular with signs of shallow slumping
It is our opinion that surface and subsurface water in this area are primary causes of
the slope instability
POTENTIAL REMEDIAL MEASURES
It appears that the primary causes of the slope instability are the steep slope ratio and
localized subsurface and surface water Therefore remedial measures should be directed
toward addressing these items
Slope Flattening
Flattening the existing slopes to ratios of 2H 1V or flatter would increase the overall
Stability of the embankment However due to the existing track facilities this is not a viable
alternative

Increasing Embankment Soil Strength
The stable slope ratio of a soil is largely dependent on the internal soil strength of the
material Therefore if the material strength is increased it may be built at a steeper slope
ratio The strength of the existing embankment fill may be increased by the addition of
geosythetic reinforcement The reinforcement may consist of synthetic geogrids or
geotextiles placed in horizontal layers between the soil to increase the tensile strength of the
soil mass A subdrainage system is also included to collect and drain seepage water
All or a portion of the existing embankment fill may be removed and recompacted with
the geosnythetic reinforcement This would allow the embankment to be reconstructed to
the existing grades The advantage of this alternative is that the appearance of the site would
be restored to the current condition The disadvantage of this alternative is that significant
excavation would be required The length of the geosynthetic reinforcement is generally
about equal to the height of the slope The existing infrastructure including the drain line and
light poles would restrict excavation in some areas
A schematic section through the highest portion of the slope is shown on Plate 3
GRP Facing
Since the boring data and visual observations indicate that the slope movements are
probably due to near surface soil movements and not mass instability a GRP facing may
help to resist the slope creep movements The GRP would provide some lateral support to
the slope face and help to minimize contraction and expansion of the near surface soils
caused by changes in moisture content
The GRP should have a minimum thickness of 12 inches with a 24 inch deep and
24 inch wide socket at the bottom and at mid slope Weepholes should be provided at
regular intervals to allow subsurface water to drain A schematic section illustrating this
alternative is presented on Plate 4
Retaining Walls
Retaining walls may be constructed to reduce the slope ratios and heights Generally
three locations for the walls were evaluated First a new retaining wall may be placed at the
sidewalk level along Ulune Street This would result in wall heights up to about 20 feet Due
to the anticipated high costs and aesthetic considerations this location is not recommended
A second location for the wall would be at the top of the embankment slope This
would probably result in the shortest wall height however it appears that the existing light
pole foundations would conflict with this wall alignment Additionally the wall may need to
extend deep into the slope to avoid movements caused by the instability of the existing slope
below
