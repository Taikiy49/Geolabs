INTRODUCTION
This subsurface investigation was undertaken to define sub
surface conditions along the proposed utility alignments
and to evaluate the soil conditions within the previously
filled sluice area California Bearing Ratio tests along with the
accompanying density tests were performed to evaluate the
pavement subgrade support and to establish recommendations
for the pavement design and construction
Site Description
The site of the proposed container yard expansion is at Berth
51 B on Sand Island Honolulu Hawaii The approximately
12 9 acre site is located in the mauka Ewa portion of Sand
Island adjacent to Kapalama Basin of Honolulu Harbor
The present unpaved gravel surface is essentially level at
approximately elevation 9 feet and is being utilized for
container storage
A drawing of the container yard expansion entitled Existing
Conditions dated October 1972 shows a small inlet
designated as the sluice area approximately 200 feet
west of the eastern property line In 1972 this inlet was
approximately 360 feet by 60 feet and had a bottom level
extending to approximately elevation 9 feet
W 1975
The same drawing indicates numerous stockpiles of debris
including areas of reinforced concrete rubble This mate
rial was apparently utilized to fill the sluice area Little
surface evidence remains of the previous iniet
A subsequent site drawing entitled Seatnzain Whang Extension
H C 1765 dated gives only a shoreline
indication of the inlet The inlet area at that time had
been filled to between elevations 8 and 10 The inlet fill
therefore has been in place a minimum of 18 months
Proposed Construction
It is anticipated that the proposed container yard expansion
will be at approximately the existing grades Minor site
grading will be required to obtain a uniformed surface and
to provide surface drainage The improvements will include
an asphalt pavement and storm drains
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The present Sand Island is essentially a man made island
built upon a shallow coral reef The island was initially
much smaller consisting of a shallow water coral reef formed
within a few feet of sea level
W 1975
The portion fronting the ocean consisted of a breaker zone
in which more resistant coral formations developed Behind
this coral zone the original island was composed of sand and
coral debris deposited in calmer water behind the surf zone
These natural reef deposits presently appears as the light to
dark gray soils below the light brown fill
The upper fill originated from dredging operations for the
harbor and for the seaplane runways constructed adjacent to
the Ewa end of the island This upper fill material is
generally composed of light brown or tan coral sand with
some silt Numerous localized areas of miscellaneous fill
and building rubble have been placed upon the upper coral
fill
Subsurface Explorations
A total of eighteen shallow borings were utilized to define
the subsurface conditions within the proposed container yard
expansion This investigation consisted of eight primary
borings and ten secondary borings The secondary borings
drilled adjacent to primary and 8 were utilized
for additional definition of the materials deposited within
the sluice area The test boring locations have been super
imposed on the enclosed General Plan Plate 1
W 1975
A detailed description of the field exploration methods and
accompanying laboratory testing is given in the appendix
along with the boring logs and laboratory test results
Soil Conditions
The subsurface explorations and Jaboratory testing divided
the site into two areas of similar subsurface profiles
1 the predominant area outside of the sluice fill consisting
of relatively competent sand and coral fill and 2 the
sluice area which has been backfilled with concrete rubble
metal wood and other debris
The site in the vicinities of and 8C
is underlain by approximately 7 to 11 feet of medium dense
te dense light brown silty sand and coral gravel This is
the dredged fill which was placed upon the underlying natu
ral deposits of loose to medium dense gray silty sand and
coral The upper fill was generally covered by three inches
of gravel In some areas a thin layer of soft gray silty
clay separated the upper fill and the natural deposits
Within the sluice area the upper fill varied from 1 2 to 8 0
feet and included a layer of loose to dense black cinder
sand and coral This upper fill blankets a lower fill com
posed of a heterogenous mixture of boulders concrete rubble

metal wood miscellaneous junk and other debris Borings
4A through 4E 5A through 5E 8A and 8B were drilled within
this material Due to the concrete rubble obstructions the
borings penetrated a maximum of ten feet into the lower fill

Groundwater was encountered between elevations 0 4 and 1 8
Due to the close proximity of the ocean and Honolulu Harbor
it is expected that this groundwater level will vary with

Site Preparation
Sluice Area The rubble fill in the sluice area was appar
ently placed upon the underlying compressible soil without
the necessary engineering controls to assure a stable fill
The intervening eighteen month period since the placement of
the fill should be sufficient for the consolidation of the
underlying soft materials due to the fill loadings Addi
tional settlements if any would be in the form of secondary
compression and should not be excessive
The rubble fill contains some areas of open matrix which
could resuit in shallow surface depressions if the overlying
soils migrate into the open spaces
It is therefore recommended that the sluice area be undercut
a minimum of two feet below the existing surface proof
rolled with a large vibratory compactor and backfilled with
compacted granular fill
The proof rolling of the undercut surface should consist of
ten passes with a vibratory compactor such as the Raygo 4048
a Hyster C620A or other similar equipment capable of
delivering a minimum total applied force of 30 000 pounds
W 1975
The vibratory compactor should collapse near surface voids
and densify the surface materials The two feet of compacted
backfill will create a layer of soil sufficiently strong to
bridge minor areas of limited support
Following the proof rolling operations the area should be
backfilled in maximum 12 inch lifts utilizing a non cohesive
select borrow compacted to 95 percent of its maximum density
The select borrow should be crusher run waste mudrock
coral sand or cinders with Jess than 15 percent passing the
200 mesh seive and a minimum CBR of 55 percent
Utilizing the above procedures the possibility of signifi
cant settlements should be materially reduced Minor settle
ments can more economically be corrected by additional
surface paving in lieu of the costly undercutting required
to obtain complete assurance of no future settlements
General Site Preparation
While the test borings indicate that the upper fill is
generally competent it was apparently placed without suffi
cient engineering control to assure uniformity It is there
fore recommended that the proposed paving areas be proof
rolled with the previously specified vibratory compactor to
delineate areas of uncompacted or unsuitable pavement support
W 1975
This proof rolling would consist of two passes of the com
pactor and should be monitored by a soils engineer who would
delineate the areas of unsuitable subgrade support
These areas if encountered should be undercut to remove the
unsuitable materials to a maximum depth of three feet and
replaced with compacted select borrow as previously specified
Periodic in place density determiniations should be made to
assure that the subgrade has been uniformly compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent of its maximum density
Pavement Design
Representative samples of the upper fill was obtained during
our subsurface investigation and tested to evaluate its sub
grade support capacity A California Bearing Ratio test was
performed in accordance with American Society for Testing
and Materials Test Designation D1883 utilizing a ten pound
surcharge The test gave a corrected CBR of 55 percent
which represents an excellent subgrade material The CBR test
results are summarized on Page 11
Based upon the above CBR value single axle loads of 30 to 50
kips and 100 psi tires it is recommended that the pavement
section consist of three inches of asphaltic concrete placed

Utilities
With the exception of the filled sluice area the remaining
materials will provide adequate support for utility lines
located above mean sea level
Utilities located below this level in the natural deposits
may encounter localized areas of soft materials requiring
removal and replacement with a 12 inch crushed rock bedding
The extent of these localized areas must be established
during construction
W 1975
Trench Excavations
It is anticipated that trench excavations through the upper
fill can be accomplished with conventional methods Special
excavating methods such as blasting or percussion equipment
should not be required
The filled sluice area should be avoided if possible due to
the concrete rubble which would hamper the excavation opera
tions and would not provide uniform support for the pipes
The soils are essentially granular and will be stable with
side slopes of 1 to 1 If the contractor elects to utilize
braced excavations the shoring should be designed based upon
the enclosed pressure distribution shown on Plate 2
Excavations below approximately elevation 2 will require
some dewatering It is anticipated that the dewatering can
be accomplished with Sump pumps and similar equipment to
approximately elevation 1 Excavations below this level
will require sheeting well points or other similar methods
to assure a stable dry excavation
Construction Inspections
It is recommended that the general site grading and the under
cutting proof rolling and backfilling of the sluice area be
W 1975
inspected to assure adequate compaction of the in place
materials and to assure that the recommended compaction
level is consistently achieved
It is recommended that the trench excavating pipe placement
and backfilling compaction be inspected to assure that the
recommendations are correctly implemented
The approximate extent of the Sluice area has been outlined
on the General Plan based upon the information obtained from
the previous site drawings and our subsurface explorations
This estimated extent should be utilized for estimating pur
poses the actual extent must be established during the con
struction operations
The area of the proposed container yard expansion is man made
land which is generally not uniform Conditions quite
different from those encountered in the subsurface explorations
may therefore be disclosed during the construction operations
The construction inspections Should also verify that the sub
surface conditions are consistent with the findings of the
invéstigation If unforeseen or undetected soil conditions
such as concrete rubble pockets of soft cohesive soils or
unusual water conditions are encountered corrective measures
should be made in the field as they are detected
