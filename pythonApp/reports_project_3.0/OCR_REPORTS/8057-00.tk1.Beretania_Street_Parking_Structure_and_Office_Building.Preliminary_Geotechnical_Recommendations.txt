ANTICIPATED SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Based on the field exploration conducted by Masa Fujioka & Associates (MFA) in 
2012, we envision the project site is likely underlain by loose to dense weathered 
volcanic ash sands, partially welded volcanic ash, and/or cinder sands extending to 
depths ranging from about 6.5 to 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  The 
surface soils are generally underlain by medium hard to very hard pahoehoe basalt 
formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about 25.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. 
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of the field 
exploration conducted by MFA in 2012.  However, it should be noted that groundwater 
levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface 
water runoff, and other factors.  
SEISMIC DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS  
Based on the subsurface materials anticipated at the project site and the geologic 
setting of the area, we anticipate the project site may be classified from a seismic analysis 
standpoint as a “Very Dense Soil and Soft Rock” site.  Therefore, we believe the seismic 
design of the building and retaining structures may be designed based on a Site Class C 
soil profile based on Chapter 20 of the American Society of Civil Engineers (ASCE) 
Minimum Design Loads for Buildings and Other Structures, 2010 Edition (ASCE 7-10).

and may be used for seismic analysis of the project.

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS  
Based on the available soils boring data, we envision basalt formation may be 
encountered at variable depths ranging from about 6.5 to 16.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface across the project site.  These depths generally corresponded to the top 
of basalt formation at about Elevations +31.5 to +35.5 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  
Based on the schematic drawings provided, we understand that the design finished floor 
elevation of the lowest basement parking level is set at about +18.5 feet MSL.    
Therefore, we envision excavations on the order of about 13 to 17 feet into the basalt 
formation may be required to achieve the design finished floor elevation of the lowest 
basement parking level.  Further discussions and preliminary recommendations for the 
project design are presented in the following sections.   
Shallow Foundations 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions at the project site and the design 
finished floor elevation as described above, we envision the proposed parking structure 
and office building structure may be supported on shallow foundations consisting 
of spread and/or continuous strip footings bearing on the basalt formation underlying the 
site at deeper depths.  
An allowable bearing pressure on the order of about 6,000 pounds per square 
foot (psf) may be used for the preliminary design of footings bearing on the basalt 
formation. The allowable bearing pressure should be reduced to 3,000 psf for footings

In general, the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 30

extending the footing down to bear directly on the basalt formation, the footing excavation 
may be backfilled with lean concrete or flowable fill from the basalt formation up to the 
design bottom of footing elevation. 
Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground should be 
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of 6 feet 
measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of the slope. Foundations 
next to other subsurface structures, including but not limited to, existing foundations, 
utility trenches, and/or easements, should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary 
plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the subsurface structure, or the 
proposed footings should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. 
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent subsurface structures 
with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation 
settlement.  
If the foundations for the proposed structure are designed and constructed in 
strict accordance with our recommendations, the total settlement of the foundations is 
estimated to be approximately 1 inch or less with differential settlements on the order of 
about 0.5 inches.  
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction between the 
base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure 
developed against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of the foundation. An 
coefficient of friction of 0.50 and 0.35 may be used for foundations bearing on the basalt 
formation and in-situ soils/compacted fills, respectively. Resistance to lateral loads due 
to passive earth pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 500 
pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf) for foundations embedded in the basalt 
formation. The passive earth pressure value assumes that the concrete for the footings 
is cast neat against the basalt formation.  A reduced value of 350 pcf may be used if the 
footings are cast against soil backfill materials, which should be compacted to a

Foundation Probing 
Based on our experience in similar basalt formation, cavities and/or voids are 
commonly encountered in the basalt formation underlying the project site. To reduce the 
potential for loss of foundation support resulting from the collapse of cavities and/or lava 
tubes below 
foundations 
supported 
on 
the basalt formation, 
we 
recommend 
implementing a program of cavity probing and grouting at the foundation locations 
during construction.  
Generally, we recommend drilling probe holes at each isolated spread footing 
location (one probe hole for every 50 square feet of footing area) and at 10 feet on-
center along wall foundations or continuous strip footings. The probe holes should be at 
least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned 
bottom of foundation elevation.  
If cavities and/or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing 
operation, additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating 
the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and/or void. The probe holes and cavities 
discovered should be backfilled with sand-cement grout or pumpable controlled low 
strength materials injected (pumped) at low to moderate pressures. We recommend 
utilizing a low strength sand-cement grout or pumpable controlled low strength 
materials with a slump range of 7 to 10 inches for the grouting operations.   
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and/or voids at the project site, 
we recommend obtaining unit prices for additional probing and grouting during bidding. 
In addition, the probe drill should be available on-site until the probing and grouting 
operations are completed. The contractor should be made aware that a longer lag time 
between probing/grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in 
the construction schedule.  
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of 
a Geolabs representative. This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of cavities 
and/or voids and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take 
and/or changed conditions are observed. Observation of the probing and grouting 
operations during construction should be designated a full-time “Special Inspection” 
item.

anticipated subsurface conditions and the design finished floor elevation, we envision the 
slabs-on-grade for the basement parking level generally will be supported on the in-situ

For the interior building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic), we recommend 
providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. The open-graded gravel cushion fill would 
serve as a capillary moisture break and would provide for uniform support of the slabs. To 
reduce the potential for excessive future moisture infiltration through the slab and 
subsequent damage to floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended 
on top of the open-graded gravel cushion layer. Interior walls should be designed to 
incorporate some flexibility to accommodate a small amount of possible ground 
movements. 
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic, such as the basement parking 
level, we recommend providing a 6-inch layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs in lieu 
of the 4-inch thick gravel cushion layer. The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed 
basaltic aggregates compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
For the design of structural slabs supported on 6 inches of aggregate subbase, a 
modulus of subgrade reaction of about 250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection 
(pci) may be used for wheel loads acting on the floor slab supported on the compacted 
aggregate subbase. Where slabs are intended to function as rigid pavements for trucks, 
such as at loading dock areas, a minimum slab thickness of 6 inches may be used for 
preliminary design purposes. In addition, provisions should be made for proper load 
transfer across the slab joints that will be subject to vehicular traffic. 
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at 
least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade. It should be emphasized that the areas 
adjacent to the slab edges should be backfilled tightly against the edges of the slabs with 
relatively impervious soils. These areas also should be graded to divert water away from 
the slabs and to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs. 
Exterior concrete slabs, such as walkways, also will be required for the project. In 
general, we recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick cushion fill layer consisting of 
aggregate subbase below exterior concrete slabs that will not be subjected to vehicular 
traffic. The aggregate subbase supporting the exterior concrete slabs should be 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. To reduce the potential for 
significant slab cracking due to concrete shrinkage, crack control joints should be provided 
at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right-angle 
intersections.

Retaining Structures 
Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions at the project site, it is our opinion 
that the following preliminary guidelines may be used to design retaining structures 
planned for the project. 
Wall Foundations 
In general, we believe retaining wall foundations may be designed in accordance 
with the recommendations and parameters presented in the “Shallow Foundations” 
section. In addition, retaining wall foundations should be at least 18 inches wide 
and should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent 
finished grades. For sloping ground conditions, the footing should extend deeper to 
obtain a minimum 6-foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside 
edge of the footing to the face of the slope. Wall footings oriented parallel to the 
direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings.

Drainage 
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic 
pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of 
permeable material, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), 
placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of 
the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, a 
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used 
instead of the drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product also should be 
connected hydraulically to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of compacted on-site 
materials or free-draining compacted fills, where specified by the designer. Unless 
covered by concrete slabs, the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of 
low-expansion, relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for excessive 
water infiltration behind the walls.
