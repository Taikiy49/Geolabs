INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Proposed Satellite Communications Antenna Tower located on the
southwest face of the Joint Intelligence Command Pacific Building 352 on the island of
Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design
level input only and may not be appropriate to form the basis for construction cost
estimates nor for construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed satellite communications antenna tower was originally located at
the southeast face of the Joint Intelligence Command Pacific Building 352 in Makalapa
on the island of Oahu Hawaii A field exploration program consisting of drilling and
sampling one boring Boring No 1 to a depth of about 40 feet below the existing
ground surface has been conducted in mid February to obtain an overview of the
subsurface soil conditions We understand that the proposed antenna tower has
subsequently been relocated to the other side of the original corner against the
southwest wall of Building 352 and adjacent to Room 453 A subsequent boring
Boring No 2 was drilled at the new location as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Under the current design concept the proposed tower will be a 5 story open
structure approximately 75 feet high with a plan dimension of 13 feet by 13 feet The
tower will be constructed of structural steel with a total weight of approximately 125 kips
supported by 4 legs A proposed finish grade of 110 feet Mean Lower Low Water

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
On the basis of our geotechnical engineering exploration we believe that the
proposed antenna tower at the new site may be supported on mat foundations bearing
on the very stiff in situ silty clay soils encountered at shallow depths We estimated
that the total foundation settlements under the anticipated design load of 125 kips to
be less than inch with differential settlements between the center and the corner of
the mat to be on the order of inch Detailed discussion of our geotechnical
engineering design recommendations are presented in the following sections of this
report
Mat Foundations
Based on the generally very stiff to hard near surface soil conditions encountered
at the project site we believe that a mat foundation bearing on the very stiff near
surface soils may be used to support the proposed tower structure
Based on our field expioration an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000
pounds per square foot p s f may be used for design of shallow foundations bearing
on the in situ very stiff silty clay material encountered at shallow depths This bearing
value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 for transient
loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces For mat foundation design a
modulus of subgrade reaction of 200 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection
p c i may be used for mat foundation bearing on the very stiff silty clays The
subgrade modulus is the modulus of vertical subgrade reaction for a 1 foot square test
plate
The bottom of the mat foundation should be embedded a minimum of 3 feet
below the lowest adjacent finish grade We estimate the total foundation settlements
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 8
under the anticipated design load of 125 kips to be less than inch with differential
settlements between the center and the corner of the mat to be on the order of inch
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional
resistance and adhesion developed between the bottom of the foundation and the
bearing soil Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This
assumes that the soils around the mat are well compacted or undisturbed Where
needed a shear key may be considered to provide additional passive resistance to
lateral loads A coefficient of friction of 0 4 may be used between the mat and the in
situ very stiff clayey silt material In addition an adhesion value of 100 p s f between
the mat foundation and the bearing soils may be used where needed
Foundation excavations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs
Hawaii prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm foundation
bearing conditions and the embedment depths

Based on the current design concept we anticipate that site grading for the
subject project will involve approximately 5 feet of excavation into the existing ground
for the construction of the mat foundation

At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of off site
All areas beneath future tower slab and 2 feet beyond should be over excavated
to the future foundation subgrade level If zones of soft loose or saturated soils are
encountered at the over excavated subgrade level deeper over excavation may be
required to expose firm material The clayey material resulting from the over
excavation should be removed and properly disposed of off site
Foundation subgrade should be scarified to a depth of 8 inches moisture
conditioned to at least 2 percent above optimum moisture content and compacted to
at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 97
Select granular fill material needed to backfill over excavation should be placed
in level lifts not exceeding 9 inches in loose thickness and compacted to not less than
90 percent relative compaction The select granular fill material should consist of
imported granular material such as crushed coral basalt gravel or cinder sand The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles greater than three
inches in largest dimension The material should have a laboratory California Bearing
Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of not more than one percent
when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It should contain less
than 20 percent passing the No 200 sieve Any imported fill material should be tested
and approved for its intended use by our firm prior to delivery to the project site

Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Also it is advised that gutter
systems be installed around the tower and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations should also
be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms
and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils Drainage swales should be
provided as soon as possible especially along the retaining wall and should be
maintained to drain all surface run off away from the founaation siab
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the foundation slab immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling two
2 borings to depths of 31 and 39 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
portable drilling equipment
The material encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer geologist who monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and
by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
nia sampier with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The biow counts needed to
drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of four inches 4 in length without any discontinuities discounting
any drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from
a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring
Logs as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of
the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
