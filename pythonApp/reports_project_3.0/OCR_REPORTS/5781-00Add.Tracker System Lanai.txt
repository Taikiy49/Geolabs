Additional Field Exploration
Our initial field exploration consisted of excavating three test pits designated as
Test Pit Nos 1 through 3 each extending to a depth of about 5 feet below the existing
ground surface In order to investigate the entire project site additional field exploration
was conducted and consisted of excavating six additional test pits designated as Test Pit
Nos 4 through 9 extending to depths of about 3 and 6 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate test pit locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 1
In general both field explorations indicate that the project site is underlain by
residual soils consisting of medium stiff to very stiff clayey silts on the order of about 2 to
6 feet The residual soils were underlain by hard saprolite consisting of clayey silts with
sand and gravel The saprolite extended to the maximum depth explored of
approximately 6 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was not observed on the test pits at the time of our field explorations
However groundwater levels can vary significantly depending on rainfall temperature
surface runoff groundwater withdrawal and other factors

Uplift Load Resistance
In general uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead weight of the
drilled shaft and by shear along the shaft surface and the adjacent soils
Considering that the drilled shafts are designed based on adhesion between the
shaft and the surrounding soils we believe that an ultimate uplift load capacity of
7 kips per drilled shaft may be used in the design for array pedestal drilled shafts
and 30 kips per drilled shaft for motor drive pedestal drilled shafts The uplift load
capacities are based on the recommended drilled shaft lengths The uplift load
capacities provided include the weight of the drilled shaft
The ultimate uplift load capacities provided were computed assuming that the
entire lengths of the drilled shafts will be reinforced to provide proper load
transfer to the surrounding soils
The ultimate uplift load capacities provided above should be used for transient
loads only For sustained uplift loads acting on the foundations the ultimate uplift
load capacities should be reduced by a factor of safety of 2 0 The project
structural engineer should check the structural capacity of the shaft member in
tension
Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on the structures may be resisted by a combination of the
lateral load capacity of the drilled shafts The passive earth pressure acting against
the near vertical faces of the foundation caps and or grade beams were ignored in
our engineering analyses However passive earth pressure against the
near vertical faces of the foundation caps and or grade beams may be estimated
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 per square foot per foot of depth pcf for
above groundwater conditions If groundwater is encountered during the drilled
shaft construction Geolabs should be notified immediately to re evaluate the
foundation design

In general lateral load resistance of the drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of
the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of
shaft and induced moment in the shaft In general we recommend spacing the
drilled shafts at a minimum of 2 5 times the diameter of the shaft from
center to center when installed in a group Therefore the effect of group action
was considered in our lateral load analyses by including an efficiency factor in the
direction of loading
The lateral load analyses were performed using the program LPILE plus for
Windows which is a microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference laterally
loaded drilled shaft program originally developed at the University of Texas at
Austin The program solves for deflection and bending moment along a drilled
shaft under lateral loads as a function of depth The analysis was carried out with
the use of non linear p y curves to represent soil moduli The lateral deflection
was then computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths
The lateral loads 2 5 and 20 5 kips acting at the top of the drilled shaft the

Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
In general the performance of drilled shafts depends significantly upon the
contractor s method of installation and construction procedures The following
conditions would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled
shaft foundations
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a significant extent on the
frictional resistance between the shaft and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques especially during the drilling operations are important The
contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete
into the drilled holes
The drilled shafts are to be embedded into the residual soils and saprolite materials
encountered in the borings drilled The drilled shaft contractor will need to
demonstrate that the proposed drilling equipment and coring tools where
appropriate will be capable of installing the drilled shafts to the recommended
depths and dimensions by the performance of a trial shaft program
We do not anticipate groundwater will present within the depths of the drilled shaft
excavations We require that the bottom of the drilled hole be free of loose
materials prior to the placement of concrete If groundwater is encountered during
the drilled shaft excavations Geolabs should be notified immediately to re evaluate
the foundation design
It is imperative that a representative from Geolabs be present at the project site to
observe the drilling and installation of drilled shafts during construction Therefore
observation of the drilled shaft installation operations by Geolabs is necessary to
confirm the assumed subsurface conditions and should be designated a Special
Inspection item
