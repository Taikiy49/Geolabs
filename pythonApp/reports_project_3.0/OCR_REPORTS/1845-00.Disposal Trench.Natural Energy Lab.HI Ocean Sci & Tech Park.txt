INTRODUCTION
The purpose of this exploration and testing was to provide subsurface
permeability data for use in the design of the proposed seepage trench for
the disposal of spent ocean water from the Natural Energy Laboratory of
Hawaii NELH and the leach field for disposal of sanitary wastes from the
proposed beach restroom facilities at Hawaii Ocean Science and Technology
HOST Park

The exploration consisted of the drilling of five 5 test wells and
four 4 observation wells by a contract driller under the supervision of
our firm Upon completion of the drilling 4 inch well casings were
installed in the test wells and piezometers were installed in the
observation wells After the installation of the casings and piezometers
the wells were completed and developed as appropriate
Subsequent to the completion of the wells permeability testing was
performed to obtain data for the design of the disposal systems
The results of the exploration and testing which were used in the

SITE DESCRIPTIONS
The project sites are located in the area of Keahole Point in North
Kona on the west coast of the island of Hawaii
The disposal trench site is located to the north of the existing NEIH
facility in a relatively barren pahoehoe lava field The ground surface
is hummocky but in general has relatively low relief Surface
elevations vary between about 8 and 14 feet above Mean Sea Level

The proposed restroom facility site is located at the northern end of
Wawaloli Beach along the west side of the HOST NEIH access road The
ground surface is relatively level with an elevation of approximately 6 0
feet above Mean Sea Level The southern portion of the site is covered
with coral beach sand and the northern portion is a pahoehoe lava flow
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is currently proposed to excavate a seepage trench for the
disposal of spent ocean water from the NEIH facility This spent ocean
water will be composed of a mixture of warm and cold water which has been
cycled through the ocean thermal energy conversion project and other
projects at the laboratory It is anticipated that the ultimate rate of
disposal will be on the order of 22 MGD
It is also proposed to construct a public restroom facility at
Wawaloli Beach This facility if constructed will utilize a septic tank
and leach field for the treatment and disposal of sanitary wastes
GENERAL SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The project sites are located on the west flank of Hualalai about 5
miles south and west of the northwest rift zone Hualalai is one of the
five volcanoes which built the island of Hawaii and was last active in
approximately 1801 1803 The project sites are underlain by Hualalai
basalts which consist of interbedded pahoehoe and a a lava flows
originating from the northwest rift zone
At the project sites the basalts encountered were relatively fresh
and unweathered and are estimated to be of an age on the order of
hundreds of years The lavas exposed at and near the project sites were
generally of the pahoehoe form with some clinkery areas The exposed
surfaces of the flows were hummocky and had numerous pressure ridges

These features appear to indicate that the lava at time of deposition
was moving slowly over a relatively level surface
Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project sites were explored by
drilling four 4 test wells and four 4 observation wells at the the
proposed disposal trench location and one 1 test well at the location of
the proposed restroom facility at Wawaloli Beach Details of the field
exploration are presented in the appendices of this report
In general at the proposed trench site the drilled wells
encountered a relatively thick flow unit of basalt which extended to a
depth of about 12 feet below the existing ground surface This flow unit
which was vesicular pahoehoe on the surface graded with depth to
relatively dense basalt which appears to indicate that the lava flow had
ponded Below this flow unit the wells encountered gravel to
cobble sized basalt fragments representing an a a clinker stratum This
stratum varied in thickness between 2 and 11 feet in the drilled wells
Below the clinker the wells encountered relatively dense basalt to the
maximum depths drilled
At the proposed restroom facility site the surface soil consisted of
loose tan beach sand approximately three 3 feet deep overlying a layer
approximately 3 feet thick of relatively dense basalt underlain by a thin
1 foot stratum of basalt clinker which was in turn underlain by
relatively dense basalt to eight 8 feet below the existing ground
surface the maximum depth drilled at this site
Well Drilling and Testing
The test and observation wells were drilled utilizing truck mounted
rotary drilling equipment The test wells were drilled using a inch
diameter core barrel which provided samples for visual examination The
wells were then reamed to a minimum diameter of six 6 inches and slotted
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1845 00 Page 5
Dk No 003
PVC well casing was placed in the wells at appropriate depths to provide
representative tests of the different strata encountered After the
installation of the casing the wells were completed with sand packing
bentonite and grout seals and then developed by airlifting
The observation wells were drilled using an NX core barrel which
provided samples for visual examination and comparison to the samples
obtained from the test wells Upon completion of the drilling the
observation wells were finished by installation of a piezometer sand
packing bentonite and grout seals
The test wells were tested by the injection of water and where
possible the withdrawal of water Wells were tested by either the
constant head method or the falling head method as appropriate For the
constant head tests injection and withdrawal rates were monitored by the
use of a paddle wheel flow sensor which gave direct reading of the flow in
gallons per minute In the falling head tests the wells were filled toa
datum level and the drop in water level was recorded with respect to time
DISCUSSTON AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the proposed NEIH trench disposal site
encountered a thin semi confined aquifer at an average depth of
approximately twelve 12 feet below the ground surface This aquifer
consists of coarse basalt clinker and has an average thickness of ten 10
feet Our field injection permeability testing indicates that this strata
has an average net permeability ranging from about 100 to approximately 450
feet per day and that the overlying semi confining layer of dense basalt
has an average net permeability of about 2 feet per day
Tt should be noted that the test wells or cbservation wells did not
encountered significant cavities or voids although numerous large cavities
and cracks were observed on the surface of the exposed lavas at the
proposed disposal sites Any such gross permeability features should
enhance the efficiency of any disposal system if encountered during
construction

NELH Disposal Trench
Based upon our analyses using an average permeability k value of 175
feet per day as derived from our field testing we estimate that a ten 10
foot deep trench with a base width of ten 10 feet is capable of
infiltrating approximately 16 000 gallons per day per lineal foot of trench
with a build up of ten 10 feet of head in the trench Therefore
approximately 250 lineal feet of trench will be required to handle the
anticipated start up flows of approximately 4 0 million gallons per day
This does not include any allowance for clogging air entrainment surge
flows or excessively high ground water conditions
The simplified model also assumes a uniform geometry with the upper
dense basalt and the underlying clinker both being ten 10 feet in
thickness whereas the logs of the drilled test and observation wells
indicate a more complex subsurface geometry The actual thickness of the
upper confining layer encountered in the drilled borings varied from 11 to
13 feet below the existing ground surface while the permeable clinker
stratum varied from 2 to 11 feet in thickness in the drilled borings

A disposal trench excavated to a depth of 10 feet below the existing
ground surface may not completely penetrate the dense surface layer of
basalt Therefore the final trench design should call for a minimm
excavation depth of 15 feet with a further requirement that clinker be
encountered before the termination of excavation
Our analyses indicate that the thickness of the clinker stratum is the
limiting factor in surface disposal Therefore little advantage is gained
by increasing the width or depth of the trench
If multiple trenches are to be constructed on the site we recommend
that they should be separated center to center by a minimm distance of 5
times the nominal trench width to reduce the interference between the flow
regimes induced by the individual trenches
The permeability of the clinker zone and the rock formation below it
may be improved by loosening the material This may be accomplished by
overexcavation and or blasting However it is not possible to precisely
quantify the increase in permeability which will be obtained by loosening
of the material
The calculated injection permeability of the clinker layer ranged from
70 to 450 feet per day This is well within the range of variability
expected due to variations in the stratum as well as testing limitations
However the actual capacity of the trenches will be directly proportional
to the actual permeability Therefore a two fold increase in permeability
will result in a doubling of the trench capacity For this reason it is
our recommendation that a full scale testing program be developed and
implemented during the construction of the trench es to evaluate the
actual disposal capacity This program should consist of step testing the
absorption rate of the trench es during excavation i e introducing
water into the trench es at different rates and observing the build up of
the water level in the trench es with respect to time This testing
program will provide the actual capacity of the trench es as constructed

and provide data for the design of future expansion of the disposal
system The testing could also allow testing of the potential improvement

It is also recommended that the disposal system include a one hundred
per cent backup capacity This capacity should be a redundant system in
the form of a completely separate disposal component e g additional

allow for periodic maintenance and will also provide lead time in the event
of sudden failure of one of the systems
Construction Considerations
The drilling data as well as previous experience indicate that hard
excavation utilizing hoe rams and probably blasting will be needed for
efficient excavation
The rock formation is generally hard and may be cut at a slope ratio
of about H V
Dewatering of the excavation will not be required However it is
anticipated that some diving observations may be required to verify that
the excavation has reached the permeable clinker layer
It is our understanding that the disposal trenches will be left open
with no pipe or conduit and a high fence will be constructed around the
facility for safety

Environmental Monitoring
Due to the large quantity of water which will be disposed of through
the trench system it is our opinion that a groundwater monitoring
program be developed and implemented specifically for the trench
system This program should consist of a series of monitoring wells
placed both upgradient and downgradient of the disposal system The
actual monitoring of these wells could be carried along with the
monitoring already proposed for the entire HOST NELH complex
Wawaloli Beach Restroom Facility
Based upon our field permeability testing the basalt formation in the
vicinity of the proposed restroom facility has an average net permeability
of 5 feet per day However leach fields for septic systems are normally
designed utilizing the rate of fall occurring in a falling head test in a
drilled boring This rate of fall is expressed in terms of minutes per
inch our field testing yielded an average rate of fall of five 5 inches
per minute or 0 2 minutes per inch This rate exceeds the highest rates
used for septic system design in Public Health Service Publication No 526
Manual of Septic Tank Practice In this publication it is recommended
that the maximum rate of application of sewage to a leaching system should
not exceed five 5 gallons per day per square foot of trench bottom It
is our recommendation that this guideline should be adhered to in the
design of the proposed leach field
It is our further recommendation that the trenches for the proposed
leach field be overexcavated to reach the clinker material at a depth of
approximately six 6 feet below the existing ground surface and that the

overexcavated trench be backfilled with clean granular material such as
3B Fine This overexcavation will allow more rapid seepage of the
effluent and the length of the trench may be reduced in accordance with the

Due to the close proximity of the proposed restroom facility to the
beach and the relatively high public usage of the beach we suggest that
the septic system effluent be disinfected prior to its disposal through the
leachfield

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based
in part upon information obtained from field data points such as borings
probes test pits and or observation wells Variations of conditions
between the field data points may occur and the nature and extent of
these variations may not become evident until construction If variations
then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the
recommendations given in this report
The location of the field data points were approximately determined
by taping from points surveyed by R M Towill Corporation Elevations of
the surveyed points were provided by the surveyors and elevations of the
field data points were set by hand level at the time of our exploration
The physical location and elevation of the field data points should he
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the
field data points depict the approximate boundaries between soil types
and as such may denote a gradual transition The strata lines shown on
profiles or cross sections are based upon interpolation between field data
points and may not represent actual subsurface conditions
Water level data from the field data points was collected at the
times given on the graphic representations and or in the text of this
report This data has been reviewed and interpretations made in the
formulation of this report However it must be noted that fluctuation
may occur due to variations in rainfall tides temperature and other
factors
This report has been prepared solely for the purpose of assisting the
engineer in the preliminary design evaluation of the proposed project
Therefore this report may not contain sufficient data or the proper
information for use in contract bid estimation A contractor wishing to

APPENDIX B
Permeability Testing
Gravity injection tests were performed in three 3 test wells
installed to evaluate the permeability of the subsurface Pumping
withdrawal tests were performed in the same three 3 tests wells to
further evaluate the subsurface permeability Falling head percolation
tests were performed in two 2 wells to evaluate the permeability of the
shallower subsurface formation
Following the construction of the test wells preliminary percolation
testing was performed on the wells The wells were first developed by
airlifting with an air compressor After development gravity injection
Constant Head tests were performed using water from a nearby aquaculture
facility These test data are presented on Plates B 1 through B 3
Pumping withdrawal tests were performed by pumping the wells using a
2 inch or 3 inch centrifugal pump and monitoring drawdown These data are
shown on Plates B 4 through B 6
Percolation Falling Head tests were performed in the two 2
shallower test wells by filling the well to a known level and measuring
the fall in water level with respect to time These data are shown on
Plates B 7 and B 8
