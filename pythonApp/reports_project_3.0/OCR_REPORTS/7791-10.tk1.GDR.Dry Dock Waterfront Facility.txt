SECTION 1.  
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The proposed project site is located in the vicinity of Dry Docks 1 and 2 of the Pearl Harbor 
Naval Shipyard and Intermediate Maintenance Facility (PHNS & IMF) in Pearl Harbor on the Island 
of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1.  Based on the information provided, it is desired to perform a site analysis and utilities 
study to support an update of the Installation-PWD 1391 for the FY22 MCON P-109, Dry Dock 
Waterfront Facility project. 
Based on the information provided, the proposed new dry dock waterfront facility will be 
a multi-story waterfront ship services support building in the Controlled Industrial Area (CIA) 
between Dry Docks 1 and 2. It is envisioned that the building will be constructed of concrete with 
a concrete pile foundation. The facility will include production shop spaces, meeting/conference 
rooms, break rooms, restrooms, administrative and engineering areas, and project management 
and project team spaces. In addition, built-in equipment will include passenger and freight 
elevators, a bridge crane on the first floor, and emergency generators with accessories. A 
design-level field exploration and laboratory testing program was undertaken to assist the design 
team in the detailed design of the project. 
A summary of the field exploration program conducted for the project is presented below. 
Detailed information and discussions for each specific work item are provided in the respective 
appendices of this geotechnical data report.

SECTION 3.  
SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
3.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield volcanoes, 
Waianae and Koolau. The older volcano, Waianae, is estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in 
age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger shield, Koolau, is 
estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and forms the majority of the 
eastern two-thirds of the island. The Koolau shield reached the end of its main shield-building 
phase about 2 million years ago. 
The geologic conditions underlying the project site are the result of a complex series of 
deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio-eustatic sea level changes and 
post-erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch. Following the cessation of 
volcanic activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial soil materials were deposited in a deltaic or 
estuarine environment in the area of Pearl Harbor. 
The sea level fluctuated several times, receding perhaps 300 feet below and rising about 
100 feet above present sea level. Concurrent with a rise in sea level during the late Pleistocene 
era, eruption of the Salt Lake-Makalapa member of the Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the 
older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash, which consolidated to form volcanic tuff 
(locally known as “mudrock”) layers.  Subsequent erosion dissected and thinned those mantles 
of volcanic tuff.  
During the recent epoch, as sea level rose to its current stand, the area was covered with 
soft and loose lagoonal marine deposits.  These deposits were subsequently covered with 
man-made fills placed during the middle portion of this century for land reclamation projects 
during construction of the exiting Pearl Harbor Naval Shipyard.  The waterfront facility project 
site is located at the edge of the former shoreline Pearl Harbor. The subsurface conditions of the 
project site generally consist of fill materials overlying coralline deposits and highly weathered 
volcanic tuff materials.

3.2 
Subsurface Characteristics 
In general, we observed two main stratigraphic units across the project area within the 
proposed dry dock waterfront facility. The stratigraphic units consist of soil and rock materials of 
varying character and engineering properties. The two main stratigraphic units are listed below 
in the general order of geologic age, beginning with the younger unit and ending with the older 
unit: 
1. 
Quaternary Honolulu Tuffs (Qht) 
2. 
Coral Limestone (Qls) 
The following sections briefly describe the general characteristics of each of the above 
stratigraphic units based on our literature research, past experience with similar materials, and 
the field exploration and laboratory testing program conducted at selected locations across the 
project site. The approximate delineation of these stratigraphic units at the ground surface of the 
project vicinity is presented on the Geology Map, Plate 3. 
3.2.1 Quaternary Honolulu Tuffs (Qht) 
Tuff from vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series consists of volcanic ash, which 
consolidated or hardened to form a medium hard rock that resembles siltstone or 
sandstone, locally called “mudrock”. The tuff is frequently found overlying coralline 
deposits or older alluvium units. This formation has relatively low permeability, and 
groundwater generally is not encountered in this formation. When it does occur, it is 
usually in the form of seepage along joints. 
3.2.2 Coral Limestone (Qls) 
This unit consists of fossil remnants of coral-algal reefs and their related detrital debris 
deposits. In Hawaii, the detrital deposits tend to be the dominant portion of any coralline 
rock assemblage. The detrital deposits are weakly to strongly cemented coralline debris 
consisting of gravel, sand, and silt-sized clastic fragments. Some of the coralline fragments 
were dissolved, and the solution cavities were filled with cemented and/or uncemented 
fine sands and silts. The following subunits are used in this geotechnical data report.

Coralline Detritus 
This subunit consists of granular detritus broken from reef formations. The soils 
generally consist of medium dense to dense sandy gravel and gravelly sands. Silts 
and clays are only present in minor or trace amounts. Detritus also may be weakly 
cemented with occasional moderate to strong cementation. 
Coral Formation 
This subunit is composed of coral and calcareous algae in their original growth 
positions or detrital deposits, which have become moderately to well cemented. 
Sandstone 
Sandstone is a sedimentary rock composed mainly of cemented sand-sized 
particles (clasts) consisting of individual mineral particles or grains of weathered 
parent rock. The sedimentary particles comprising sandstone typically are derived 
from the breakdown of parent material into sedimentary particles. The 
sedimentary particles typically are eroded, transported and sorted by water or air, 
and deposited as layers of sedimentary materials. Over time, the sediments may 
be buried by other sediments or geologic layers, and the burial pressure combined 
with a cementing agent act to consolidate the grains and cement the pore space 
between the grains to form hardened sandstone. Typical cementing agents may 
be calcium carbonate (common in calcareous sand deposits of Hawaii) or silica and 
some clays.  
Most sandstones in Hawaii are light brown in color and calcareous being derived 
from the breakdown of parent coralline and reef materials into sand-sized 
particles. The cementing agent in most calcareous sandstone is calcium carbonate. 
Inclusions of basaltic rock fragments, marine shells, and other fragments of broken 
coral may be cemented in the sandstone layers. Sandstones containing such clast 
inclusions commonly are referred to as beachrock in Hawaii, having been derived 
from available shoreline geologic materials.

The strength (hardness) of sandstone generally depends on the grain size, degree 
of sorting, and degree of cementation of the sedimentary materials. The 
weathering and decomposition of calcareous sandstone may be accelerated by 
exposure to acidic water, which attacks the calcareous cement binding the sand 
grains and causes a slow exfoliation of the outermost exposed layers of the 
sandstone outcropping. Sandstone may or may not contain visible bedding 
surfaces. Aeolian (wind derived) sandstone may exhibit cross-bedding structures 
that developed during the initial deposition of the sand layers. 
3.2.3 Groundwater 
Groundwater level measurements were taken during the advancement of the boreholes 
drilled for the project during our field exploration program. We encountered 
groundwater level in the drilled borings at depths ranging from about 5.6 to 11.2 feet 
below the existing pavement surface. The groundwater levels measured generally 
correspond to about Elevations -0.2 to +1.4 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). It should be noted 
that groundwater levels are subject to change due to tidal fluctuations, storm surge 
conditions, rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other 
factors. 
We explored the subsurface conditions across the project site by drilling and sampling 
eight borings, designated as Boring Nos. B-1, A-2, B-3, B-4, A-3, B-6, B-7, and B-8, extending to 
depths ranging from about 13 to 102.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  A bulk sample 
of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1, was obtained to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are 
shown on the Boring Location Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, our field exploration generally encountered 3 to 6 inches of asphaltic concrete 
(with the exception of B-7 with 8 inches of Portland cement concrete) underlain by fill materials 
consisting of loose to medium dense silty sands and stiff clayey silts extending to depths of about 
1 to 7 feet below the existing pavement surface.  The surface fill materials were underlain by 
interbedded layers of coralline detritus consisting of loose to medium dense  coralline sands and

GEOTECHNICAL EXPLORATION PROGRAM 
The geotechnical exploration program conducted for the Dry Dock Waterfront Facility 
project generally consisted of the following field and laboratory work components. A brief 
description of each work component is presented below.  Detailed discussions, findings, and test 
results of the various components are provided in the respective appendices herein. 
4.1 
Soil Borings, Rock Coring, and Associated Field Testing 
Soil and rock sampling methods and field testing were performed in accordance with the 
scope of services outlined in our revised fee proposal dated August 10, 2018 and the 
Subconsultant Agreement entered into on September 26, 2018. The field exploration is described 
in more detail in the introduction to Appendix A. The following discussion provides an overview 
of the methods and procedures. 
Typically, soil borings were advanced with 4-inch diameter continuous-flight solid-stem 
augers (SSA) to the first encounter with groundwater and then continued with PQ-size wireline 
coring methods to advance the PQ-size casing from the ground surface. In general, auger drilling 
was used in areas where stiff to hard cohesive soils (older alluvium) exist and in locations where 
rock coring was not anticipated. Additionally, SSA drilling was switched over to the wireline coring 
method when hard clays, boulders, and/or rock formation were encountered or when soft and/or 
loose soils that caved in were encountered. 
Sampling of the soils was performed at about 5-foot intervals. Drive samples were 
obtained using the modified California sampler (ASTM D3550) and the Standard Penetration Test 
(SPT) split-spoon sampler (ASTM D1586).  
Where the borehole was advanced utilizing the PQ core barrel in the soil formations, 
samples of the soils recovered from the PQ core barrel also were retained in core boxes for visual 
observation. Intervals where the borehole was advanced utilizing the PQ casing only as a rotary 
wash drilling method, there were no soil samples collected. The borings were advanced into the 
rock formations with the PQ-size wireline coring methods. The PQ-size wireline coring methods 
produce rock core samples of 3.35 inches (85 mm) in diameter.

In-situ testing of the subsurface materials in the borings included the SPT, which was 
performed in accordance with ASTM D1586. Standard Penetration Test (SPT) sampling provides 
a measure of the resistance or consistency of a soil and is reported as the uncorrected N-value, 
which is defined as the number of blows required to drive a standard split-spoon sampler 
12 inches with a 140-lb hammer, after 6 inches of initial seating. The penetration resistance of 
the modified California sampler was recorded in a similar manner, and the modified California 
sampler blows per 12 inches can be approximately related to the SPT N-value through 
correlation. 
Rock core recovery (REC) and rock quality designation (RQD) were measured as described 
in Appendix A. ASTM D6032 - Standard Test Method for Determining Rock Quality Designation 
(RQD) of Rock Core states that “Core sizes from BQ to PQ with core diameters of 36.5 mm 
(1.44 in.) to 85 mm (3.35 in.), respectively, are normally acceptable for measuring RQD as long as 
proper drilling techniques are used that do not cause excessive core breakage or poor recovery, 
or both. The NX-size (54.7 mm [2.16 in.]) and NQ-size (47.5 mm [1.87 in.]) are the optimal core 
sizes for measuring RQD. The RQD is also useful for large core diameters provided the core 
diameter is clearly stated.” The rock core photographs are included in Appendix E for information 
and reference. 
4.2 
Seismic Shear Wave Velocity Testing 
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling was performed at a completed borehole (Boring 
No. A-2) using seismic cone penetration testing (SCPT) equipment. Procedures for the test are 
discussed in more detail in Appendix B. In brief, the completed borehole was tremie-filled with 
time-release bentonite pellets and bentonite chips in order to provide a medium through which 
the seismic cone tip could be pushed. The sleeve and tip resistance through the bentonite-filled 
borehole is not meaningful because the cone is pushed through the bentonite backfill; however, 
the seismic wave travels from the ground surface through the surrounding soils, allowing 
measurement of the average seismic shear wave velocity. 
The seismic cone tip contains a geophone sensor. At a succession of depths, the 
advancement of the cone tip was halted, a seismic pulse was generated at the ground surface by

4.3 
Field Permeability Testing 
Field permeability tests (either constant head or falling head methods) were performed 
at depths of about 17 and 16.5 feet below the existing pavement surface in Boring Nos. B-6 and 
B-8, respectively, to obtain data pertaining to the dewatering operations anticipated for 
below-grade structures and underground piping.  Details of the permeability test procedures and 
the calculated hydraulic conductivities are provided in Appendix C.  
4.4 
Laboratory Testing 
Laboratory testing was performed on soil and rock samples to determine the physical and 
engineering properties. Laboratory tests performed included moisture content, unit weight, 
Atterberg Limits, grain size distribution, ring swell, uniaxial compressive strength of rock, 
Unconsolidated-Undrained (UU) Triaxial Compression, Direct Shear, Modified Proctor, laboratory 
California Bearing Ratio (CBR), and corrosion potential tests (pH, minimum resistivity, chloride 
content, and sulfate content). The laboratory test results are provided in Appendix D.

Test Boring Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions across the project site by drilling and sampling 
eight borings, designated as Boring Nos. B-1, A-2, B-3, B-4, A-3, B-6, B-7, and B-8. The borings 
extended to depths ranging from about 13 to 102.5 feet below the existing pavement surface 
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous-flight augers and rotary coring tools. 
The field exploration was conducted from March 6 through 26, 2019. In addition, a bulk sample 
of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1, was obtained for laboratory CBR and Modified 
Proctor tests.  The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Boring 
Location Plan, Plate 2. 
Soil borings were advanced with 4-inch diameter continuous-flight solid-stem augers 
(SSA) to the first encounter with groundwater and then continued with PQ-size wireline coring 
methods to advance the PQ-size casing from the ground surface. In general, auger drilling was 
used in areas where stiff to hard cohesive soils (older alluvium) exist and in locations where rock 
coring is not anticipated. Additionally, SSA drilling was switched over to the wireline coring 
method when hard clays, boulders, and/or rock formation were encountered or when soft and/or 
loose soils that caved in were encountered.   After the borings were advanced to the maximum 
depth of the boreholes, the bored holes generally were backfilled with cement-grout with the 
exception of Boring No. A-2. 
Boring No. A-2, selected for seismic shear wave velocity testing was backfilled with 
0.25-inch diameter time-release bentonite pellets (below the groundwater level) and bentonite 
chips (above the groundwater level). The temporary casing from the coring operations was used 
as a tremie pipe to place the time-release bentonite pellets starting from the bottom and 
advancing upward. Backfilling above the static groundwater table were performed in stages by 
adding water to limited amounts of bentonite chips to ensure hydration before additional 
bentonite chips were placed in the borehole. The top 3 feet of the borehole was then grouted 
with Quikcrete concrete mix. No additives were added to the drilling water during our drilling 
operations. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and textural 
examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for 
Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the 
laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for 
Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on 
the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with 
ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations 
of the materials encountered and other observations, such as groundwater levels, artesian

Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general 
accordance with ASTM D3550, Standard Practice for Thick Wall, Ring-Lined, Spilt Barrel, Drive 
Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer 
falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general 
accordance with ASTM D1586, Standard Test Method for Penetration Test and Split-Barrel 
Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer 
and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 
18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by using 
diamond core drilling techniques, consisting of a PQ wireline coring system with double tube core 
barrel (core size of 3.35 inches), in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond Core Drilling 
for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the 
rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for 
examination and description. The coring rates indicated on the Logs of Borings refer to the time 
required to complete that particular core run at that depth. Rock cores were described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The 
Rock Description System is based generally on the publication “Suggested Methods for the 
Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by International Society for Rock 
Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring attempt 
versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings 
as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality of 
rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the total core run in rock that is sound material 
in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling, mechanical, 
and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 
5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of 
Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock 
based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. 
Carmichael (1989).

Seismic Shear Wave Velocity Test 
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling of the subsurface materials was performed using 
Seismic Cone Penetration Testing (SCPT) equipment. Seismic shear wave velocity testing using 
seismic cone penetration testing equipment was performed at a selected boring location, 
designated as Boring No. A-2(SW), as shown on the Boring Location Plan, Plate 2. 
The seismic shear wave velocity profiling was performed at approximately 3.3-foot 
intervals extending to a depth of about 102.9 feet below the existing pavement surface. In order 
to conduct the seismic shear wave velocity test in the boring, the test boring generally was 
advanced utilizing rotary coring methods to the maximum depth of the boring. Logs of the 
materials encountered in the boring are presented on the Logs of Boring in Appendix A. After the 
boring was advanced to the maximum depth of the borehole, the bored hole was backfilled with 
0.25-inch diameter time-release bentonite pellets. The temporary casing from the coring 
operations was used as a tremie pipe to place the bentonite pellets starting from the bottom and 
advancing upward. When the bentonite pellets are in contact with the groundwater in the 
borehole, the pellets started to hydrate slowly. As the bentonite pellets hydrate, they swell and 
soften. Backfilling above the static groundwater table was performed in stages by adding water 
to limited amount of bentonite chips to ensure hydration before additional bentonite chips were 
placed in the borehole. The probe was then pushed through the softened bentonite extending 
to a depth of about 102.9 feet below the existing pavement surface using the seismic cone 
penetration testing equipment (SCPT). 
The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10-ton steel 
electronic subtraction cone into the bentonite backfilled borehole. The cone has an apex angle 
of 60 degrees and a projected surface area of 1.55 square inches (10 square centimeters) 
equipped with an electronic depth sensor attached to the cone rod. The seismic cone carries a 
uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the arrival of a shear wave generated and propagated 
from the ground surface. Seismic measurements are made when the SCPT has stopped and a 
shear wave is sent into the subsurface. A shear wave is generated at the surface by striking a 
loaded plank with a switched hammer. The propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow 
to the cone was measured at each discrete depth interval. The vector difference of these depths 
divided by the time difference for the shear wave to arrive at the various depths provided the 
average shear wave velocity over the depth interval. 
The seismic shear wave velocities measured, and the average seismic shear wave velocity 
calculated for the top 100 feet of the soil profile at the boring location are presented on 
Plates B-1.1 through B-1.4. The weighted average seismic shear wave velocity for the upper 100 
feet of the soil profile was calculated based on the average shear wave velocity  method 
described in Section 1615.1.5 of the International Building Code (2006 Edition).

Field Permeability Tests 
The permeability of the in-situ soils was evaluated by performing one constant head at 
Boring No. B-6 and one falling head test at Boring No. B-8 at depths of about 17 and 16.5 feet below 
the existing pavement surface, respectively. 
The constant head test was performed by pumping water into the borehole until the water 
surface has stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time. The water height 
and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which to base calculation of the 
subsoil permeability. The test results of the constant head permeability test are presented on 
Plate C-1. 
During the falling head testing, the borehole was carefully filled with water to the top of the 
borehole.  The time intervals and water drops were recorded to provide data upon which to base 
calculation of the permeability values of the subsoils. The test results of the falling head permeability 
test are presented on Plate C-2.

Laboratory Tests 
Samples recovered from our field exploration program performed for the project were 
visually examined and classified in our laboratory in Honolulu, Hawaii. Selected samples were 
tested to evaluate the physical characteristics and engineering properties of the soils 
encountered. The laboratory testing program was formulated with the emphasis on evaluating 
the moisture, density, plasticity, gradation, shear strength, and corrosivity of the soils 
encountered. 
The laboratory tests performed included determination of dry density and natural 
moisture content, Atterberg Limits, sieve analysis, expansion, unconfined compression, triaxial 
compression, direct shear, CBR, Modified Proctor, and corrosion tests. Results of the tests 
performed are presented on Plates D-1 through D-13. Detailed descriptions of the various tests 
performed for this exploration are presented below. 
Moisture and Density 
Moisture Content and Dry Density tests were performed on selected soil samples to aid 
in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and correlating the layers 
between test borings. The moisture content tests were performed in general accordance with 
ASTM D2216. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. 
Atterberg Limits 
Two Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the liquid 
limit (LL), plastic limit (PL), and plasticity index (PI), as an aid in classifying the soils. The Atterberg 
Limits tests were performed in general accordance with ASTM D4318. The test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of 
the test results is provided on Plate D-1. 
Sieve Analysis and Hydrometer 
To provide information on the particle size distribution of the soils encountered, 15 Sieve 
Analysis tests were performed to evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil 
classification. The tests were performed in accordance with ASTM C117 and C136. Graphic 
presentations of the test results are provided on Plates D-2 through D-4. In addition, eight tests 
were conducted on selected soil samples to evaluate the amount of fines content. The test was 
performed in accordance with ASTM D1140.

Ring Swell 
Two one-ring Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed or remolded 
samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils.  The test results are 
summarized on Plate D-5. 
Uniaxial Compressive Strength – Rock Cores 
Two Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D7012 Method C) were performed on 
selected rock cores to evaluate their unconfined compressive strength of the rock formation 
encountered. Uniaxial compressive strength test results for the selected rock cores are presented 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths and presented on Plate D-6. 
Unconsolidated-Undrained Triaxial Compression 
To evaluate the undrained shear strength of the clayey soils, two Unconsolidated 
Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests were performed on selected relatively undisturbed 
soil samples in accordance with ASTM D2850. The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the relatively undisturbed samples. The 
approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was estimated using the wet density of the 
stratigraphic units and the depth of the groundwater table. The test results and the stress-strain 
curves are presented on Plates D-7 and D-8. 
Direct Shear 
To evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the in-situ soils, two strain-controlled, 
consolidated-drained Direct Shear tests were performed on relatively undisturbed soil samples 
in accordance with ASTM D3080. The test results are presented on Plates D-9 and D-10.  
Modified Proctor 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557) was performed on a bulk sample of 
the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content relationships.  The test 
results are presented on Plate D-11. 
California Bearing Ratio 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a bulk 
sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils. 
The test results are presented on Plate D-12. 
Corrosion Potential 
Two sets of pH (ASTM G51), minimum resistivity (ASTM G57), chloride (EPA 300), and 
sulfate (EPA 300) content tests were performed by our office or TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. 
on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. The test results are presented on 
Plate D-13.
