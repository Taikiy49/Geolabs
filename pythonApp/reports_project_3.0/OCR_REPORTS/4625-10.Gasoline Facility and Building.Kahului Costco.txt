2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes the older West Maui Tertiary
Epoch and the more recent East Maui also known as Haleakala Pleistocene Epoch
The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain located between these two
volcanoes The project site is located at the northern portion of this gently sloping plain
The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows from Haleakala ponding on West
Maui It is comprised of alluvium washed from the slopes of West Maui and East Maui
Haleakala The erosional processes were dominated by the detachment of soil and
rock masses from the mountain walls and the soil materials were transported
downslope toward the Isthmus primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials
reached the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes became
dominant and the sediments were transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream
flows transmit large volumes of water for very short duration Because of this situation the
transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists
of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the
erosional base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin
to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the materials
Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion
of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate
Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in
headwaters This continued erosion generates materials which are transported
downslope covering the older alluvial soil deposits Depending on the local base level and
rate of transport these newer sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time
In addition their consistency and density are generally less than those of the older
partially consolidated deposits

Underlying the alluvial soil deposits are overlapping lava flows from the West Maui
and East Maui Volcanoes The bulk of the Haleakala Shield was built during the late
Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows of the
Honomanu Volcanic Series During the Pleistocene Epoch the characteristics of the lava
changed to very hard thickly bedded flows of andesitic composition These lava flows
have been grouped as the Kula Volcanic Series Typically the basalt rock formation
consists of thinly to thickly bedded a a and pahoehoe type lava flows

be used for support of the open canopies and building addition planned at the site An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for

transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces

Due to the potential for presence of soft and or loose fill soils at shallow depths as
encountered in our field exploration we recommend that a 12 inch thick layer of structural
fill material be provided below the bottom of footings for uniform support Because the
footings for the open canopies may extend to depths of over 5 to 6 feet deep below the
existing ground surface we anticipate that cobbles and boulders will likely be encountered

fill material recommended above would also serve to reduce the potential for concentrated
loading resulting from footings bearing on sharp points of the cobbles and boulders The

Prior to placement of the structurai fili material the bottom of the footing
over excavations at the open canopies should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
where possible moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
recompacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction If soft and or loose soils are
encountered at the over excavated subgrade levels deeper over excavation may be
required to expose the underlying firm material Alternatively the bottom of the
over excavation may be compacted to a firm condition and lined with a layer of filter fabric
such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent prior to placement of the structural fill The soft and or
loose fill soils anticipated below the structural fill at the building addition remodel area

materials should meet the requirements for imported fill materials presented in the Site
Preparation section of this report Structural fills should be moisture conditioned to above

thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative

maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test

procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight

We anticipate that the pump island canopies may be subjected to uplift forces
created by wind conditions Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the following
1 dead weight of the structure 2 weight of backfill material above the footing where

Contribution of dead weight from the backfill may be estimated using a unit weight of
110 pounds per cubic foot pef For side shear between the sides of the footings and the
adjacent soils a shear value of 150 psf may be used
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 40 may be used for footings bearing on the 12 inch thick structural fill material
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the
soils around footings are well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the

3 5 Excavation
In order to install the new underground fuel storage tanks we anticipate that an
excavation on the order of up to about 20 feet below the existing ground surface will be
required Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we believe that the upper fill
soils within 10 to 15 feet of the existing ground surface may be excavated with
conventional excavation equipment However excavation into the underlying dense
boulders and hard basalt rock formation may require the use of hoerams and chipping
The above discussions regarding the excavation characteristics of the on site soil rock
deposits are based on field data obtained from the project site We recommend that
contractors bidding on this project be encouraged to examine the site conditions and
subsurface data to make their own interpretation
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the
contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavated materials
should be stockpiled no closer to the edge of the excavation than a horizontal distance
equal to the depth of the excavation in order to reduce the potential for appreciable
ground movement due to surcharge loading
Based on our field exploration the project site is underlain by variable nature fill
soils over dense boulders and basalt rock formation Therefore if construction space is
available it is our opinion that an open cut may be used for the excavation of the
underground fuel storage tanks A slope inclination ratio of one horizontal to one vertical
1H 1V or flatter may be used for the temporary cut in the upper fill soils and dense
boulder layer We believe that excavation into the underlying hard basalt rock formation
may be left unsupported at a near vertical slope for temporary construction purposes
If the open cut method is not practical for the excavation we recommend that a
temporary shoring system be used to support the excavation The temporary shoring

Subsurface Soil Permeability
Based on our borings dense boulders and hard basalt rock formation may be
anticipated below the groundwater level The actual subsurface soil rock
permeability may range broadly and also vary locally in terms of orders of
magnitude The permeability of the subsoils rock formation at the site will be
highly dependent on the tightness compactness of the boulders and the
fracturing of the basalt rock formation Therefore the contractor should pay
special attention to the site specific dewatering plan for the excavations
Dewatering Method
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection
of equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and
he she should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be
required during construction depending on the conditions encountered The
dewatering method selected should have minimal impact on the groundwater
level surrounding the excavation As previously indicated the boulder layer and
basalt rock formation at this site may be highly permeable depending on the
tightness and fracturing and are capable of transmitting moderate quantities of
water
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents should
be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater
table and or to exclude the water from the excavations to allow construction of the
structures under safe and dry conditions These works or systems may include but
are not limited to grouting cut off walls tremie concrete plugs or any combination
of the above and or other possible methods
It should be noted that the subsurface conditions within the excavation depths at
the site consist of boulders and rock formation which are capable of transmitting

moderate quantities of water Because the project is located in a developed area
the dewatering operation should be conducted in such a manner that the
dewatering will not cause areal ground subsidence which may cause potential
damage to the existing structures and utilities Therefore consideration should be
given to a dewatering system that includes a cut off wall It should be noted that
disposal of the dewatered effluent would also be problematic at the site considering
the generally low permeability of the near surface soils at the site
Dewatering Considerations
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of a
suitable method of dewatering
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or
damage to existing structures roads and environment
Db The dewatering method should maintain stability of and provide
safe and dry working conditions in the excavation
Cc The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to
dewatering It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be
retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His or her qualified representative who should
be required to be continuously present on site during dewatering activities will
have the best opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during

dewatering operations

3 9 Drainage
The finished grades outside the new structures should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding In addition excessive
landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next
to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the
potential for appreciable water infiltration into the subsurface Drainage swales should be
provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the
slabs and foundations
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface silty and clayey soils
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately
after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration into the
subsurface

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Three one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples of the soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils
The results of the tests are summarized on Plate C 1
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio Test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a
bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The test results are presented on Plate C 2
Two sets of corrosion tests including minimum resistivity
chloride content and sulfate content were
performed by Oceanic Analytical Laboratory inc on selected soil samples obtained from
our field exploration The results of these tests are summarized on Plate C 3
