REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two main shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau beginning in the mid Pliocene Epoch The older volcano
Waianae was the first to develop closely followed by the development of Koolau Volcano
For a period of time Oahu was comprised of two islands formed by each of the volcanic
centers The volcanoes continued to erupt and banked against the slopes of each other with
GEOLABS INC
W O 2418 20 Page 6
eee ED
Waianae terminating its eruptive activity prior to the cessation of Koolau The main shield
building activities ceased approximately 3 5 to 2 5 million years ago
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by extrusion of the lavas
of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed
dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical weathering of
the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The weathering process
has formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic soils In general saprolite is composed mainly
of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite
grades to basaltic rock formation with increased depth Fills placed during the initial
development of the campus may underlie the ground surface at portions of the project site

Concrete Retaining Wall Foundations

In general wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches Wall

Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the
retaining structures The perforated pipe should discharge to an appropriate outlet
or weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as
MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the drainage material The
prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a
perforated pipe at the base of the retaining structure Unless covered by concrete
slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious material
compacted on site soils to reduce the potential for water infiltration behind the
retaining structures

retaining wall system be used as an alternate solution to the conventional concrete retaining
wall supporting the major fill planned on the western and southern portions of the proposed
athletic field The MSE retaining wall system is a composite wall system which utilizes
welded wire mats galvanized steel strips high density polyethylene or other reinforcing

and economy

retaining wall must satisfy four stability conditions 1 bearing failure 2 translational sliding
3 overturning stability and 4 overall slope stability Geotechnical design parameters to
evaluate these stability conditions are presented in the following subsections Some of the
geotechnical parameters necessary in evaluating the internal stability of the MSE retaining
wall are presented in the Reinforced Fill and Backfill Materials subsection The external
and internal stability of the MSE retaining wall system will need to be evaluated by the design
engineer We have evaluated the overall slope stability for the MSE retaining wall and the

the underlying potentially expansive subgrade soil thereby reducing the loss of moisture from
the clayey subgrade soils In our opinion the reduction in potential drying and wetting of the
subgrade soils should reduce the impact of shrink swell effects on the slab
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation be incorporated below the building slabs as a cushion fill layer To reduce
the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture vapor barrier membrane is recommended on top of the gravel cushion
fill layer Two inches of sand may be considered above the plastic membrane to provide
for protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Provision of the
2 inches of fine sand above the vapor barrier will be left up to the discretion of the project
structural engineer
We anticipate that exterior concrete flatwork and sidewalks will be constructed for the
proposed project Where exterior concrete flatwork and sidewalks are planned we
recommend that the exterior flatwork and sidewalks be supported on at least 12 inches of
select granular fill The select granular fill required under the slabs should extend to the
outside edge of the exterior flatwork and sidewalk slabs The concrete for the exterior
flatwork and sidewalks may be placed directly on the 12 inch thick select granular fill
Concrete for the bleachers may be placed directly on the compacted excavated
subgrades in lieu of the 12 inch thick select granular fill due to the difficulty in placing the
select granular fill on the relatively steep slope However it should be noted that the

Light Pole Foundations
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistance the proposed new
light pole structures may be supported by a foundation system consisting of cast in place
concrete drilled shafts Based on the anticipated structural loads and the subsoil conditions
encountered at the project site we recommend that drilled shafts with a minimum diameter
of 30 inches be used to support the proposed 70 and 90 foot high light pole structures
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site a drilled shaft
embedment length of not less than 10 and 12 feet below the design finished grades may be
used for the 70 and 90 foot high light pole foundations at the subject site respectively
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally from
skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable compressive load
capacity of up to 20 and 30 kips per shaft may be used for the 70 and 90 foot high light
poles respectively These compressive load capacities are for dead plus live loads and may
be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces The
end bearing component of the drilled shafts has been discounted in our analyses

Based on our field exploration weathered basalt formations and or boulders may

soldier piles to the depths and dimensions recommended herein

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
seven borings designated as Boring Nos 101 through 107 extending to depths of about
31 5 to 57 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 7
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would
be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Twelve one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of the tests are summarized on
Plates C 1 and C 2
Nine Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of the
tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentations of the test results are provided on Plates C 3 and C 4
Seven unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on selected
samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the on site silty and clayey
soils The test results are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Results of the unconfined compression tests are summarized on Plate C 5
Two unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression tests were performed on
selected in situ soil samples in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 2850 to
evaluate the undrained shear strength of the silty and clayey soils The approximate in situ
effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the relatively
undisturbed samples The test results and the stress strain curves are presented on
Plates C 6 and C 7
Six direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics Results of the direct shear tests are
presented on Plates C 8 through C 13
Three California Bearing Ratio CBR ASTM D 1883 tests were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement
subgrade support Results of the CBR tests are presented on Plates C 14 through C 16
Three Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on the
bulk samples obtained to evaluate the relationship between the moisture content and the
dry density of the near surface soils The test results are presented on Plates C 17
through C 19
