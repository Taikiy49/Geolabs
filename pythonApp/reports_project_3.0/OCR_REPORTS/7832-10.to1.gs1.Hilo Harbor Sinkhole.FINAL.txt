SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a surface fill layer, about 1 to 15 feet 
thick, underlain by lagoonal deposits, coralline detritus, clinker, and basalt rock formation 
extending to the maximum depth explored of 23 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
surface fill layer consisted of 2 to 12 inches of asphaltic concrete, loose to dense sandy/silty 
gravel and gravelly/silty sand with some cobbles, and very soft to hard clayey silt.  The 
lagoonal deposits were composed of soft sandy silt and loose silty sand with some gravel.  
The coralline detritus consisted of loose to very dense silty/gravelly sand and sandy gravel.  
The clinker was composed of loose to dense sandy gravel.  The basalt rock formation was 
hard to very hard. 
Voids/cavities ranging in height of about 1.5 to 9 feet were encountered in four of the 
drilled borings. Soft/loose sediments were encountered in some of the voids.  We 
encountered groundwater at depths of about 3.5 to 8 feet below the existing ground surface.  
It should be noted that the groundwater levels are subject to change due to tidal fluctuations, 
seasonal precipitation, and other factors. 
Based on our field exploration and the draft report by Kai Hawaii, Inc. dated 
October 2019, potential causes of the sinkholes were developed.  The potential causes of 
the sinkholes consisted of the loss of ground behind the existing bulkhead due to openings 
in the bulkhead or concrete apron, isolated areas of lower consistency and density of the 
near-surface subsoils, and cavities formed within the clinker and basalt rock formation along 
the old shoreline. 
The recommended remedial options by Kai Hawaii, Inc. for the loss of ground behind 
the bulkhead consisted of pressure inject polyurethane chemical grout into the cavity on the 
land side of the bulkhead, excavate behind the bulkhead to expose and fill the cavity with 
concrete and install new concrete slab behind the bulkhead, and drive steel sheet piling 
behind the Pier 2/3 bulkhead.  We agree with the options suggested.  In addition, we 
recommend checking the condition of the existing sewer, water, and/or drain lines, if present 
in the area, for leakage. 
For the areas behind and between bulkheads where cavities were not encountered 
in the borings, we recommend that the isolated sinkholes be excavated down to a minimum 
of 2 feet or until firm and dense soil is encountered and backfilled with compacted aggregate 
base course and asphaltic concrete.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Hilo Harbor Sinkholes, Commercial Harbors CE Support Phase 4 
Statewide project in Hilo on the Island of Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the remedial 
measures of the observed depressions/sinkholes. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located within Hilo Harbor in the Hilo area on the Island of 
Hawaii.  Numerous depressions (sinkholes) were identified by the Hawaii Department of 
Transportation (HDOT) Harbor personnel and observed in several areas within the Harbor 
facility.  The identified sinkholes were from Pier 3 extending to near the future Matson 
Container Yard Expansion at the eastern end of the harbor. 
We understand that determining the potential cause and remedial measures for 
the sinkholes are desired.  A boring was drilled near each of the identified sinkhole 
locations and is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
A draft report entitled “Structural Assessment of Bulkheads at Piers 1, 2, and 3”, 
dated October 2019, was submitted by KAI Hawaii, Inc.  The draft report reported their 
findings and recommendations for the bulkheads.  
1.2 
Purpose and Scope 
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain information on the subsurface 
conditions to develop an idealized soil/rock data set to formulate geotechnical engineering 
recommendations for remedial measures of the observed depressions/sinkholes. The 
work was performed in general accordance with our fee proposal dated March 20, 2019.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an area 
of approximately 4,030 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the following 
five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during recent geologic 
time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both still 
active). 
The project site is within the Hilo Harbor area. Hilo Harbor lies at the intersecting 
slopes of the Mauna Loa and Mauna Kea Volcanoes. The lava flows of the Mauna Loa 
Volcano are relatively “young” in terms of geologic time and maybe on the order of only 
750 to 1,500 years old. Below the basaltic lava flows of the Mauna Loa Volcano, the 
underlying lava flows of the Mauna Kea Volcano are believed to be Pliocene to 
Pleistocene in age and belong to the upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series. 
The land surrounding the Hilo Harbor area was reclaimed from the bay by the 
placement of fill over the lagoonal deposits and/or coralline detritus. The project site is 
generally underlain by lagoonal deposits and deposits of calcareous sediments (also 
known as coralline detritus).  Basalt formation from the recent Mauna Loa lava flows may 
be encountered at greater depths below the coralline detritus deposits.  
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located within Hilo Harbor on the Island of Hawaii. Hilo Harbor 
is bounded by Puhi Bay to the east and Kuhio Bay to the west. Generally, the project site 
spans across areas near Piers 1 through 3 and near the future Matson Container Yard 
Expansion area.  
Numerous underground utilities are located along the west side of Pier 3 and along 
Pier 4.  The harbor area with the sinkholes is relatively flat with ground surface elevations 
of about +8 to +9 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  The harbor area is covered with asphaltic 
concrete pavement.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS

fill layer underlain by lagoonal deposits, coralline detritus, clinker, and basalt rock

3.2 
Potential Causes of the Sinkholes 
Based on our field exploration and the draft report by KAI Hawaii, Inc. dated 
October 2019, potential causes of the sinkholes were developed.  The boring locations 
and potential cause of the sinkholes are provided below. 
Behind Bulkhead at Piers 2 and 3 (Boring No. 3) 
Based on Boring No. 3, our field observations, and draft report by Kai Hawaii, we 
believe the potential cause of the sinkhole is due to loss of ground behind the 
bulkhead from material going through the bulkhead and concrete apron.  An 
existing sewer line was noted in the area.  In addition, sewer odor was noted from 
the sinkhole.  The existing sewer line may have been damaged and facilitated the 
loss of ground condition with the sewer discharge.

Behind and Between Bulkheads at Piers 1, 2 and 3 (Boring Nos. 1, 4, 5, 6, 7, 16 &

The borings performed in these areas did not encounter voids or cavities.  In 
addition, loose/soft soils were not encountered near the ground surface.  It should

Along Old Shoreline (Boring Nos. 2, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14 & 15) 
Four of the borings performed in this area encountered cavities.  In addition, loose 
and very soft materials were encountered in some of the borings.  Based on the 
old drawings (1926) of the original shoreline prior to construction of the pier 
structures, the reported sinkholes and our borings are situated along the original 
shoreline as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  The original shoreline consisted of 
the old lava flow.  Typically, lava tubes and cavities are present within the clinker 
and basalt rock formation at this location.  Therefore, we believe that the sinkholes 
are caused by the loss of material into the existing cavities.  In addition, some of 
the sinkholes may be due to the surface settlement from the underlying loose and 
very soft soils.     
3.3 
Recommended Remedial Measures 
Based on the potential causes of the existing sinkholes described above, remedial 
measures were developed.  The recommended remedial measures are provided below. 
Behind Bulkhead at Piers 2 and 3 (Boring No. 3) 
In Kai Hawaii, Inc.’s draft report dated October 2019, three remedial options were 
provided and consisted of the following from the least costly to the most costly: 
1. Pressure inject polyurethane chemical grout from the land side. 
2. Excavate behind bulkhead to expose and fill cavity with concrete and 
install new concrete slab behind the bulkhead. 
3. Drive steel sheet piling behind the Pier 2/3 bulkhead.

We agree with the remedial options provided above.  It should be noted that 
potential boulders may be encountered in the backfill behind the existing bulkhead 
that would impede sheet pile installation and require predrilling that would 
significantly increase the cost.  In addition, we recommend checking the condition 
of the existing sewer line, and water or drain lines, if present in the area, for 
leakage.  Damaged sewer, water and/or drain lines should be repaired. 
Behind and Between Bulkheads at Piers 1, 2 and 3 (Boring Nos. 1, 4, 5, 6, 7, 16 & 
17) 
The isolated sinkhole areas should be excavated to a minimum of two feet or until 
firm and dense soil is encountered.  The excavation should be backfilled with 
aggregate base course compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction.  
Should groundwater be encountered in the excavation, free-draining granular 
materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), wrapped on 
all sides with non-woven filter fabric should be used. The free-draining granular 
materials should be used up to a level of about 12 inches above the groundwater 
level to facilitate compaction of the backfill materials. 
New asphaltic concrete pavement should be placed at the surface of the 
excavation.  The thickness of the new asphaltic concrete should match the existing 
asphaltic concrete thickness.  
Along Old Shoreline (Boring Nos. 2, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14 & 15) 
Based on our field exploration, the following remedial options are provided:  
1. Installation of a stabilization platform 
2. Cavity probing and grouting 
3. Structural slab on deep foundation 
The installation of a stabilization platform may be considered to help bridge over 
the existing cavities and loose/soft soils.  The stabilization platform should be a 
minimum 5 feet thick and consists of aggregate base course, geogrids (Tensar 
Triaxgrid TX-7 or equal), and a separation geotextile (Mirafi N180 or equal).  At

least three layers of geogrids should be used in the stabilization platform.  The 
aggregate base course should be compacted to a minimum 95 percent relative 
compaction.  This option is the least costly but has a higher risk of settlement 
compared to the other options. 
The second option consists of the implementation of a cavity probing and grouting 
program.  The extent of the existing cavities should be determined by probing using 
a drill rig.  The probe holes should be drilled at about 5-foot spacing and extend to 
the anticipated cavity bottom depth.  The probe holes should be backfilled with 
Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) or grout to fill the existing cavities and 
probe holes.  Additional probe holes may be required should the probe holes take 
an excessive amount of CLSM or grout.  We believe this option would be more 
costly than the above option.  We anticipate that the drilling will be slow and 
difficult. 
The last option is constructing a structural slab to span over the existing cavities.  
The structural slab will be supported by a deep foundation system consisting of 
drilled shafts or micropiles.  The installation of the deep foundations into the 
underlying basalt rock formation will be slow and expensive.  This option is the 
most reliable and most costly compared to the other options.  The structural slab 
foundations should extend beyond the bottoms of the cavities.   
A Geolabs representative should monitor the remedial work to observe the 
presence of additional cavities and/or voids in the subsurface and to allow for additional 
recommendations to be made if changed conditions are observed.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field borings and bulk samples. Variations of the subsurface 
conditions between and beyond the field borings and bulk samples may occur, and the 
nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is 
underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the 
recommendations presented herein. 
The field boring and bulk sample locations indicated herein are approximate, 
having been estimated by taping from visible features shown on the Topographic Survey 
Map transmitted by G70 on September 27, 2019. Elevations of the borings were 
estimated from contours shown on the same plan. The field boring locations and 
elevations should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods 
used. 
The stratification breaks shown on the graphic representations of the borings 
depict the approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. These data have 
been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. It should be 
noted that the groundwater levels are subject to change due to tidal fluctuation, rainfall,

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
17 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 17, extending to depths of about 10 to 
23 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, seven bulk samples of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 through 7, were obtained to evaluate the pavement 
support characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and HQ coring.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock samples were described in general accordance 
with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates 
A-1 through A-17. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler 
using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second 
and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the 
Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. 
The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) 
blow counts.  
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). “Suggested Methods for

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material is 
recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown 
on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality 
of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting drilling induced 
fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, 
the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. 
Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock, based on the

ROCK FRACTURE CHARACTERISTICS
The following terms describe general fracture spacing of a rock:
Massive: Greater than 24 inches apart
Slightly Fractured: 12 to 24 inches apart
Moderately Fractured: 6 to 12 inches apart
Closely Fractured: 3 to 6 inches apart
Severely Fractured: Less than 3 inches apart
DEGREE OF WEATHERING
The following terms describe the chemical weathering of a rock:
Unweathered: Rock shows no sign of discoloration or loss of strength.
Slightly Weathered: Slight discoloration inwards from open fractures.
Moderately Weathered: Discoloration throughout and noticeably weakened though not able to break by hand.
Highly Weathered: Most minerals decomposed with some corestones present in residual soil mass. Can be broken by hand.
Extremely Weathered: Saprolite. Mineral residue completely decomposed to soil but fabric and structure preserved.
HARDNESS
The following terms describe the resistance of a rock to indentation or scratching:
Very Hard: Specimen breaks with difficulty after several "pinging" hammer blows.
Example: Dense, fine grain volcanic rock
Hard: Specimen breaks with some difficulty after several hammer blows.
Example: Vesicular, vugular, coarse-grained rock
Medium Hard: Specimen can be broked by one hammer blow. Cannot be scraped by knife. SPT may penetrate by
~25 blows per inch with bounce.
Example: Porous rock such as clinker, cinder, and coral reef
Can be indented by one hammer blow. Can be scraped or peeled by knife. SPT can penetrate by
~100 blows per foot.
Example: Weathered rock, chalk-like coral reef
Very Soft: Crumbles under hammer blow. Can be peeled and carved by knife. Can be indented by finger Plate
pressure.
Example: Saprolite A-0.3

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test results is 
provided on Plate B-1. 
Fifteen Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plates B-2 through B-4. 
Seven laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-5 through B-11.
