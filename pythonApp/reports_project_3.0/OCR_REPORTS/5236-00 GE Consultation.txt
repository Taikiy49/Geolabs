SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
A preliminary rockfall potential evaluation was conducted for three undeveloped
hillside properties located in the Punchbowl/Papakolea area. Based on our limited site
reconnaissance, we believe that the parcel identified as TMK: 2-2-053: 007 may pose
moderate risk of potential rockfall which may affect the existing homes located at the base
of the hillside in Pauoa Valley. Additional study of the potential rockfall hazards is
recommended to evaluate the distribution of potential rockfall hazards and remedial
measures that may be considered to protect the existing homes. Future development on
the upper hillslopes is not anticipated to significantly increase the risk of potential rockfall,
provided some protective measures are implemented during the construction activity on
the upper slopes.
We believe that the future development of the parcel identified as
TMK: 2-5-022: 001 may be of low risk for potential rockfall hazards due to the limited
occurrence of rock outcrops and surface boulders encountered mainly on the lower
hillslopes at the site. We believe that the potential rockfall hazard rnay be reduced further
by removing the potentially hazardous rocks during mass grading of the site.
The Parcel identified as TMK: 2-5-019: 016 has separate regions of low risk and
moderate to high risk for potential rockfall hazards. The region of higher risk is located on
the northwesterly facing slopes above Kanaha Stream where many rock outcrops and
surface boulders were observed. Additional study is recommended to evaluate the
potential hazards and develop mitigation considerations for development of the site.
The text of this report should be referred to for more detailed discussions and
specific design recommendations.

SECTION 2.0 - SITE CHARACTERIZATION
a | Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two main
shield volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older shield volcano (Waianae Volcano) is
estimated to be middle to late Pliocene Epoch in age and forms the bulk of the western
third of the island. The younger shield volcano (Koolau Volcano) is estimated to be late
Pliocene Epoch to early Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age) in age and forms the majority of
the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Waianae Volcano became extinct while the
Koolau Volcano remained active. Therefore, the older Waianae Volcano’s eastern flank
was partially buried below the younger Koolau Volcano lavas that banked against the
Waianae Volcano’s eastern flank.
The end of volcanic activity associated with the Koolau Volcano was followed by
a long period of landmass erosion whereby deep stream valleys were carved into the
dome shaped Koolau Shield Volcano. The erosional valleys grew larger by the
processes of stream incision and mass transfer (landslides and rockfall) emanating from
the steepened valley sidewalls. In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the mass transfer
process typically dominates erosion. Mass transfer is essentially the detachment of soil
and rock masses from the steep valley walls and transportation downslope as colluvium
toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravitational force. The mass transfer process is
also a contributing mechanism responsible for the generation of some rockfall activity.
Once these transitory colluvial materials reach a flowing stream, typically on the floor of a
valley, alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments may be re-mobilized for
further transportation and deposition as alluvial deposits. Therefore, the deeper valley
floors were filled with accumulations of alluvial sediments and colluvial landslide
materials as the island eroded and slowly depressed beneath the weight of the new
landmass.
Approximately 850,000 to 1,000,000 years ago, some limited volcanic activity
resumed with the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series from secondary vents
scattered across the eastern portion of the Island of Oahu. The eruptions occurred

along the established secondary rift zones of the Koolau Volcano. Some of the more

some of the Honolulu Volcanic Series eruptions occurred in the Tantalus area, which

2.2 Local Site Geology
The project site is situated on various hillside slopes that comprise the northeast
to southwest trending Tantalus/Pauoa divide ridgeline associated with the Koolau
Mountain Range. Therefore, the slopes are primarily composed of sequentially layered
basaltic lava rock formation and resultant secondary weathered by-products including
in-situ weathered saprolitic soils (extremely weathered rock formation). Other surficial
transient deposits located on the middle and lower elevation slopes include eroded
alluvial soils and colluvial materials such as boulders and broken rock fragments. Due
to the relatively close proximity of several younger volcanic vents associated with the
eruption of the Roundtop and Sugarloaf pyroclastic deposits, portions of the site may
also contain some surface veneer deposits consisting of silty and sandy volcanic cinder
materials.
Parcel 1 (TMK 2-2-053: 007) encompasses a portion of the Pauoa/Tantalus
ridgeline and some extensive northwesterly facing slopes located above Pauoa Valley.
Parcel 2 (TMK 2-5-022: 001) encompasses a portion of the Pauoa/Tantalus ridgeline
divide and a portion of the southeasterly facing hillslopes located above the
Kanaha Stream valley. Parcel 3 (TMK 2-5-019: 016) encompasses a portion of the
Kanaha Stream valley floor and the adjacent southwesterly facing Makiki hillslopes.
Because the parcels reside on moderately sloping hillside terrain, heavy surface water
runoff and erosion of the near-surface soil and rock materials may pose potential
hazards to development located in downslope regions. The Site Plan, Plate 2 shows
the location of the three land parcels comprising the project site in relation to the

Parcel 1 is roughly rectangular in shape and encompasses approximately 34 acres
of undeveloped land, which shares a common boundary with the existing DHHL

elevations along the ridgeline and upper slopes within Parcel 1 range between

Most of the ground surfaces within Parcel 1 are covered by generally thin silty and

Surface rock outcrops are typically expressed as exposed parallel trending rock
ledges, which outcrop intermittently on the hillside. The rock ledge outcrops
observed during our field reconnaissance, generally consist of moderately

SECTION 3 — DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
3.1 General Findings
Based on our site reconnaissance, it was observed that deeper weathering and
decomposition of the geologic materials has locally penetrated the existing surface and
near-surface rock formations underlying portions of the project site. The deeper basalt
rock weathering, which essentially transforms the parent hard rock material into
saprolitic soil by-products, is most visibly pronounced at the high elevations of the
project site such as along the summit ridgeline and on the upper elevation hillslope
segments. Thus the higher elevation regions have generally fewer hard rock surface
outcroppings and the existing low relief ground surfaces appear to be mainly composed
of highly to extremely weathered basalt rock consisting of clayey, silty, and sandy soils
with some embedded rock fragments. As a result of the limited occurrence of hard rock
outcrops, the generation of colluvial boulders (potential rockfall) from the upper hillslope
regions is believed to be generally low.
However, as the middle elevation hillslopes are approached, the occurrence of
surface rock outcroppings becomes generally more numerous. The outcroppings are
typically near continuous parallel trending ledge features that protrude from the slopes
as sub-vertically inclined exposures of hard rock material. The exposed ledges, which
were observed to typically range in height between 3 and 25 feet, represent individual
layers of more erosion resistant rock. Between the higher relief rock ledges are flatter
inter-slope regions with generally fewer surface rock exposures. The inter-slope
regions are typically covered with soil deposits and highly weathered clinker materials,
which erode more easily to form the gentler slope inclinations.
In general, transient surface boulders and rock fragments generated by the
weathering and deterioration of the parent rock formation were encountered mainly at
the middle to lower elevation portions of the hillslopes located within the project site.
The observed boulders and rock fragments appear to have broken from the parent rock
formation, which become cyclically unstable with the passage of time and effects of
erosion. Some of the more precariously positioned loose boulders and rock fragments
may become re-mobilized downslope under the effect of a movement triggering
mechanism and the force of gravity (rockfall initiation). However, most of the observed
W.O. 5236-00 GEOLABS, INC. Page 14
Hawaii ¢ California

transient boulders and rock fragments appear to have come to rest in relatively stable
settings on the flatter inter-slope segments. Some existing potentially unstable and/or
precariously positioned boulders may be re-initiated to roll downslope, especially where
slope inclinations may be steeper than about 3H:1V or where ledges may exist that
promote rapid gains in rolling energy.
3.2 Potential Rockfall Mechanisms
Individual boulders that are positioned at rest on the ground surface typically
require an external force or significant change in environmental conditions to induce
toppling and/or rolling movement. The toppling or rolling motion may be initiated by a
variety of external factors. The induced motion may dissipate or increase depending on
the steepness of the hillslope, the presence or absence of obstacles on the hillslope,
and the shape and angularity of the rock fragment. In general, hillslopes that are flatter
than about 3H:1V are not very conducive to the initiation of rolling rock motion unless
the rock mass is already moving at a significant velocity. Some factors that may
contribute to the initiation of rolling rock motion include the following:
e Natural erosion of the surrounding ground surface
e Disturbance by human or animal activity such as excavation or land grading
e Significant alteration or removal of the surrounding natural vegetation
e Alteration of the existing surface drainage patterns
e Heavy surface runoff associated with flooding conditions
e Earthquake shaking or other severe ground vibration
e Impact from upslope falling rock
e Intentional boulder movement or acts of vandalism
The mixed a’a and pahoehoe lava flows that comprise the hillsides within the
project site form generally low to high relief rock linear trending outcrops separated by
lower gradient soil covered slope segments at the ground surface. The basalt rock
outcrops observed at the site generally have about 3 to 6 feet of vertical to sub-vertically
W.O. 5236-00 GEOLABS, INC. Page 15
Hawaii ¢ California
SECTION 3 — DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
inclined relief with occasional high ledge outcroppings extending up to about 25 feet in
vertical height. The rock formation outcrops tend to create a stepped hillside character
with sporadic bare rock ledges or scarps interspersed with flatter terrace features
covered with surface soils. The lava flows are typified by the presence of hard rock
layers interbedded with clinker seams that are composed of weaker, more erosive
fragmented rock materials. The bounding clinker seams are generally thinner than the
more massive hard rock layers that represent the core of the individual lava flows.
The hard rock layers usually form more erosion resistant ledge outcroppings with
near-vertical scarp faces. The underlying clinker seam is usually more susceptible to
erosion and weathering. Therefore, the weaker clinker seams erode faster than the
overlying hard cap rock creating undercut ledges and cave features. As the erosion of
the clinker seam progresses, the hard rock ledge becomes increasingly unsupported
and is susceptible to breakage. If the undercut becomes pronounced, the hard rock
ledge may fracture from the parent outcrop and fall to the ground thus initiating
downslope movement as a loose boulder. Other possible causes of rockfall boulder
generation include failure along open fracture discontinuities within the rock formation
and the presence of voids and lava tubes, which may contribute to instability in the
overlying rock materials.
It should be emphasized that rockfall activity is a natural form of mass transfer
erosion acting on steepened slopes and is a fundamental part of the geologic evolution
of hillslopes. It remains very difficult, if not impossible, to predict when rockfall may
occur as many variables have an effect on the initiation of rockfall events. However,
experience has shown that rockfall hazards are heightened most during and following
periods of heavy rainfall, where strong surface runoff is produced, and when other
disturbance to the slope surfaces has occurred.
3.3. Rockfall Protection Alternatives
Based on our limited reconnaissance and observation of the existing site
conditions at the project site, we present some conceptual alternatives, which may be
considered to reduce the potential hazard associated with potential rockfall activity. The
W.O. 5236-00 GEOLABS, INC. Page 16
Hawaii ¢ California
SECTION 3 — DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
various alternatives may not be suitable for all situations and some alternatives require
future inspection and maintenance for optimal performance. The possible alternatives
may include:
e Rockfall Impact Barrier Fence
e Catchment Area
e Wire Mesh Drapery
e Rock Removal
The rockfall impact barrier is a specialized fence structure specifically designed
and constructed to catch and retain large higher velocity rolling boulders and rock
fragments that may emanate from a wide variety of non-point source regions on a
hillside. The impact barrier is typically utilized where it is not be practical or feasible to
remove the individual potential rockfall hazards from the slope due to the wide coverage
of the rocks, difficult access, and potential for causing significant disturbance to the
slope. The impact barrier is typically constructed along the lower portion of a hillslope
above the structures or roadways that need to be protected from potential rockfall
hazards. The rockfall impact barrier is considered to be a comprehensive rockfall
protection system, which is designed for various load capacities depending on the
site-specific slope conditions and typical size and location of potential rockfall hazards
on the hillside. Rockfall impact barriers may require some periodic inspection and
maintenance especially after having sustained a very large impact or when a significant
buildup of rock debris is experienced. In addition, some components of the fence
system may require replacement after 15 to 25 years of performance.
The catchment area is essentially a physical obstruction designed and
constructed to allow for the dissipation of falling rock or rolling rock energy. The
catchment area may be constructed where sufficient space may allow for the grading
and development of a flattened buffer zone or ditch excavation located between the
base of the slope and the features that need to be protected from potential rockfall
hazards. Catchment areas are most feasible when they are located adjacent to steep
rock cut slopes and smaller area hillsides where lower velocity rockfall and limited rock
bouncing is anticipated. The catchment area is generally a low maintenance,
W.O. 5236-00 GEOLABS, INC. Page 17
Hawaii ¢ California
SECTION 3 — DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
natural-looking protective system, which may require periodic removal of accumulated
debris to maintain the desired rockfall protection effectiveness.
Wire mesh drapery is typically installed on hillslope surfaces where potential
rockfall hazards may exist or develop over time such as on a rock cut slope or slope
surface containing many rock outcroppings with minimal vegetation. The wire mesh
drapery is anchored to the slope face and is designed to control the fall of rock and rock
fragments down toward the base of the slope where the material may be collected or
removed. Wire mesh drapery is typically used for the control of smaller diameter rocks
and rock fragments generally less than about 3 to 4 feet in dimension. Wire mesh
drapery requires some periodic inspection maintenance to maintain the optimal
performance of the system depending on the frequency and size of impacts sustained.
In addition, some components of the wire mesh drapery may require replacement after
15 to 25 years of performance.
Rock removal consists of the destruction and removal of identified potential
rockfall hazards (individual boulders and/or surface rock outcroppings) from the hillside
area. Rock removal may be performed on a larger scale by heavy machinery during
site mass grading or by portable hydraulic/pneumatic equipment using manual labor in
difficult/sensitive access conditions or where very localized hazards exist. Rock
removal is considered to be a long-term minimum maintenance scheme for protection
from potential rockfall hazards, provided that the potential rockfall hazards are
completely identified and removed. The rockfall protection scheme may be a viable
alternative for new development and may be incorporated into the site grading plan.
