INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed single family and multiple family housing development
Kekuilani Subdivision Village 4 of the Villages of Kapolei Development The location of
the site and its vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommen
dations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for foundation pavement and site
grading design The findings and recommendations presented herein may not be
appropriate to form the basis for preparation of final construction cost estimates nor
for construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is situated in the District of Ewa on the island of Oahu Hawaii

multiple family developments

work This mass grading involved the placement of about six feet 6 of fill The fill

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two 2 shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is
estimated to be of the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of
the late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae ended
lava flows from Koolau banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad
plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating sedi
ments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and
further south these sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments
and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the H 1 Freeway to over 1 000 feet
at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining forma
tion or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of
sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present

Soil Conditions
In general our field exploration with the exception of Boring Nos 11 and 12
encountered a surface layer of fill consisting of very stiff to hard brown to reddish
brown silty clay This fill extended from the existing ground surface to depths ranging
between about four 4 to six feet 6 In Boring Nos 11 and 12 the surface fill
consisted of medium dense tan sandy gravel and silty sand
Under the fill our exploration generally encountered with the exception of
Boring No 12 very stiff to hard brown alluvial silty clay soil to the maximum depth
explored of 11 5 feet In Boring No 12 our boring penetrated a very stiff to hard
brown clayey silt with coral gravel
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings during the time of our
field exploration In general the site is at an elevation several tens of feet above the
highest basal water table in the area However since the alluvial silty clay soils
underlying the site have relatively low permeability and alluvial deposits frequently
contain lenses of gravel or sand there is a possibility that perched groundwater could
be encountered

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
It is Our opinion that the development project is feasible from a geotechnical
engineering point of view provided that our recommendations presented herein are
followed
In general our current field exploration indicates that the project site is underlain
by a surface fill layer of very stiff to hard silty clays Based on our laboratory test
results the on site clay soils have swell potentials that range from moderate to high
It is our opinion that spread and or continuous footings may be used to
support the proposed structures if the foundations and subgrades are properly
designed and constructed
Site Preparation
Prior to the commencement of earthwork the site should be thoroughly cleared
and grubbed of the existing pavement vegetation and other deleterious materials
Deleterious materials should be removed from the site and disposed of properly
Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing operations should be
over excavated to firm ground and the resulting depression should be backfilled with
well compacted engineered fill

Slab On Grade Foundations
It is our understanding that the structures proposed for the residential develop
ment will be supported by slab on grades with thickened edge footings
Our exploration indicates that the site is underlain by moderately to highly
expansive silty clay soils Unless foundations supported by these soils are properly
designed there is a potential for future distress to structures resulting from the
swelling of these soils The following recommendations present alternatives for
reducing the potential of such structural distress
Generally the alternatives may be considered to be equivalent in their capability
to reduce the effects of the expansive soils Selection of either alternate will depend
upon economics site preparation procedures and ease of construction
Alternate No 1
To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from the swelling of the
on site expansive soils we recommend that non expansive off site select
material be used for a minimum depth of 24 inches below slab subgrade The
capping should extend at least two feet 2 beyond the perimeter of the
proposed structure
The select material should be non expansive granular material such as select
borrow crusher waste crushed coral mudrock or cinder The material should
be well graded with no particles greater than three inches 8 in size with a
laboratory CBR swell value not exceeding one 1 percent and CBR value of 20
or more It should not contain large percentages of boulders organic or other
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3016 00 Page 11
deleterious material It should be capable of forming a competent stable mass
when compacted
Prior to placement of the capping material the underlying soil should be
scarified to a depth of ten inches 10 moisture conditioned to about three 8
percent above optimum moisture content and recompacted to between 85 and
90 percent of its maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Method D
1557 78 The select capping fill should be placed in maximum of twelve inch
12 thick horizontal lifts moisture conditioned to slightly above optimum
moisture content and compacted to between 85 and 90 of its maximum dry
density as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
The select capping material should be placed as soon as possible after the
scarification and recompaction of the existing subgrade soil This will create an
additional cap layer of moist soil above the underlying potentially expansive
subgrade soil and thereby reduce the loss of moisture from the subgrade soils
The reduction in drying will reduce the potential swelling of the underlying
expansive clayey soils
Conventional slab on grade construction may then be utilized for proposed
structures constructed on the 24 inch thick capping fill However it is recom
mended that flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl be utilized to
mask any minor slab cracking It is further recommended that interior walls be
designed to incorporate some flexibility to accommodate some _ possible
movements

Infrastructure
It is anticipated that the infrastructure for the development will consist primarily of
water drainage and sewerage for the housing area In general good construction
practices should be utilized for the installation and trench backfill of the proposed
infrastructure
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that in general
conventional excavation methods may be utilized for utility line trenching at the site
The subsurface materials encountered at the site are generally competent
However areas of loose materials may be encountered Trench shoring design and
installation should be the sole responsibility of the contractor Trench shoring and bracing
should conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3016 00 Page 15
Utilities should be placed on a six inch 6 cushion of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM
C 33 No 67 gradation The self compacting granular backfill such as No 3B Fine
should be used around the pipe and extend twelve inches 12 above the top of the pipe
It is critical to use this self compacting material to minimize the potential of formation of
voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the
pipes which could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes The use
of on site clayey soils as backfill around utility pipes is not acceptable
Trench backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding eight inches 8 in
loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of its maximum dry density
as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 In roadway and structural areas the
upper two feet 2 of backfill below finished subgrade should be compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent
Mechanical compaction equipment should be used to compact the materials
encountered at the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be used
to attempt to compact the on site soils
Drainage
The finished grades outside footings slabs and pavements should be shaped to
shed water away from foundations to avoid ponding conditions Also it is advised that
gutter systems be installed around the structures and that the gutter run off water be
diverted as far away from the slab on grade foundations and pavements Excessive
landscape watering near the foundations and pavement section should be avoided
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
recommended foundation systems since ponded water could cause excessive
subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving of the soils or loss of strength
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3016 00 Page 16
Foundation excavations should be properly backfilled tight against the footings and slabs
immediately after setting of the concrete to prevent ponding conditions
Planters close to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to prevent water from entering the foundation and slab subgrade Planting of
landscaping or gardens adjacent to foundations should also be avoided
Drainage swales must be provided as soon as possible and maintained to divert
surface run off away from the foundations and slabs

Subdrainage
It has been our experience with projects in the Ewa area and with projects having
similar soil conditions to find that when a granular select material is used as subbase
course or as a Capping over clay soils there is a tendency for water to accumulate at the
interface between the select material and the underlying clay soils
It appears that the granular select material is relatively permeable and that the clay
soils are effectively impermeable Precipitation and landscape irrigation water infiltrate
through the topsoil into the select material and form a perched water table on the clay
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3016 00 Page 18
Problems have occurred when this water reaches the subgrade of pavement
section prisms and when the select material daylights on top of the clay When the water
seeps into road subgrades it can soften the subgrade soils and weaken the pavement
section Where the seepage daylights continuously wet areas can develop
To minimize the potential of accumulation of water along the select material and
clay soil interface we recommend that subdrains be installed to intercept and divert water
to appropriate drainage locations such as drain inlets Generally the worst problems
have occurred where the water moves into road subgrades Therefore we recommend
that subdrains be installed along the sides of the road prisms The subdrains could either
be of the conventional type i e a perforated pipe embedded in free draining gravel or
one of the newer synthetic types e g MiraDrain or ConTech 100

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate
tests to aid in determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil and materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and unit weight
determinations are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in determining
certain properties such as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a
percentage of the dry weight of the tested sample is shown at the appropriate
location on plates summarizing test results and at the appropriate sample depths on
the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 12
Atterberg Limits tests ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 are used to determine the
plasticity of the fine grained portion of soils The Plasticity index of the material is used
to classify the soil under the Unified Classification System Two 2 Atterberg Limit test
were performed The results of these tests are shown on Plate B 1
Sieve analysis tests ASTM Test Method D 422 63 are performed to determine
the grain size distribution of the soil The grain size distribution is used for
classification in the United Classification System and to provide indices on other
engineering properties Three 3 sieve analysis were performed and the results of
these tests are summarized on Plate B 1
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical
parameters of a soil These physical parameters may used in engineering analyses
pertaining to the performance or suitability of a soil
Expansion tests are performed to evaluate the swelling potential of soils under
structural loads These tests aid in evaluating the materials for use as foundation
support and embankment Twenty one 21 one inch ring swell tests were performed
on undisturbed or remolded samples of the clay soils to evaluate the swelling potential
