SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered a thin layer of surface fill materials
under the existing pavement along Liliuokalani Avenue consisting of medium dense silty
sand of approximately 2 feet in thickness underlain by loose to very loose beach sand and
lagoonal deposits to a depth of approximately 30 feet below the existing ground surface A
layer of medium dense coralline detritus consisting of unconsolidated to moderately
cemented coralline gravel and sands with varying amounts of silt was encountered from
approximately 30 to 40 feet below the existing ground surface This coralline formation is
underlain by a layer of very loose lagoonal deposits comprised of silty sands and coral
fragments from approximately 40 to 45 feet below ground surface followed by a thin layer
of alluvial deposits consisting of dark brown silty clay between approximately 45 to 47 feet
below the ground surface This thin layer of alluvium rests on top of a layer of medium
dense to dense coralline detritus from approximately 47 feet to the maximum depth drilled
at 52 feet below the existing ground surface We encountered groundwater in the boring at
a depth of 6 5 feet below the ground surface at the time of our field exploration
Based on the relatively poor subsurface conditions encountered we believe that
the relatively thin fill crust and the loose and or soft lagoonal deposits encountered at the
project site would not provide adequate foundation support for the proposed new
McDonald s Building without appreciable settlements under the structural column loads In
addition the project site could be subjected to appreciable ground settlement in the event
of liquefaction during a moderate earthquake Therefore we recommend supporting the
proposed McDonalds Building on a deep foundation system
Based on the anticipated column loads the deep foundation system may consist of
micropiles to support the new structure We recommend that the micropiles be extended
through the lagoonal deposits and derive load bearing support from the medium dense
coralline detritus materials below the lagoonal deposits The micropiles would derive its
support primarily from skin friction between the pile and surrounding coralline detritus
materials
A concrete slab on grade may be used for the ground floor slab of the new building
with the understanding that appreciable settlement may occur during moderate seismic
events As an alternative to the concrete slab on grade a structural slab may be used to
reduce the potential for damage during earthquake events

Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The
Koolau Volcano reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years
ago
The project site is on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano and its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio eustatic
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the
Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated
by fossil Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high
stands of the sea which are intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the
stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age many sea level changes occurred
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level on Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid
rise in sea level occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area were
drowned In the last 10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand
Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy
estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits of loose and or soft
sediments along the coast in the areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways
In the early part of this century the Waikiki area consisted of low marshy areas
As the City of Honolulu grew and the Waikiki area was urbanized man made fills were
placed to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons The fill was placed upon lagoonal
deposits consisting of silty sands with coral fragments The lower clayey material was

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most of the earthquakes are so small that they may only be detected by sensitive
instruments However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and
a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii
over 90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of
Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than
Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of
Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have
occurred in the County of Maui The effects of earthquakes occurring on the
Islands of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu For example
several small landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui
Earthquake of In addition some houses on the Island of Oahu were
reportedly damaged as a result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 M7
Over the last 150 years of recorded history we are not aware of reported
earthquakes greater than Magnitude 6 occurring on the Island of Oahu We

Liquefaction Potential
Based on the International Building Code 2006 the project site may be subject to
seismic activity and the potential for soil liquefaction at the project site will need to
be evaluated Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and a design peak
ground acceleration of 0 18g liquefaction of the loose and saturated lagoonal
deposits at the site may occur resulting in appreciable settlements at the ground
surface Therefore the effects of potential liquefaction at the site should be taken
into consideration in the design of the proposed new two story McDonald s building
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by

earthquakes In this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected
to vibration such as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease
in volume causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to
occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective
stress internal strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the
pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially
reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid
During liquefaction the soil acquires mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and
vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that
liquefaction will not occur if the deposit is greater than 40 to 50 feet below the
ground surface In deeper deposits the greater overburden pressure is generally
sufficient to prevent liquefaction from occurring The major factors affecting the
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows
1
Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2
Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
3
Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake

Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we
recommend using a deep foundation system to support the proposed fast food chain
structure We envision that the column loads for the proposed two story structure will

than 12 inches drilled and grouted piles with steel reinforcing The micropile
foundation is typically constructed by drilling a borehole placing reinforcing steel in the
hole and grouting the borehole Micropiles are desirable because they can be readily
installed in access restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and ground
conditions In addition micropile installation causes minimal disturbance relative to
adjacent structures the adjacent soils and the environment

7 5 inches may be used to support the new building structure We recommend

for the micropile The allowable compressive load capacity for the piles is for

wind or seismic forces Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site we

Micropile Load Test Program
It should be noted that the bond stress between the grout bulb and the soil is highly
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the
contractor installing the micropile Therefore the bond stress between the grout
bulb and the soil may vary considerably between different contractors and micropile
foundation systems In order to determine whether the contractor s methods of
micropile installation are adequate and to determine the ultimate grout to soil bond
stress we recommend performing at least one pre production load test on
sacrificial micropile s Considering the smaller size of the micropile it may be
difficult to maintain the compressive test loading centralized during the load test
Therefore it is our opinion that the tensile load test may be performed to evaluate
the developed grout to soil bond stress In general the purpose of the
pre production load test on a sacrificial micropile is to fulfill the following objectives
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed by the
contractor to install the micropiles to the depths required
To confirm or modify the estimated minimum depths of the micropiles by
determining the ultimate grout to soil bond stress
To assess the contractor s method of drilling and grout injection
In general the pre production load test should be performed in accordance with
ASTM D 3689 Based on our experience we believe that the load test should not
be conducted earlier than seven days after completion of the micropile installation

Lateral Load Resistance
It should be noted that the micropile foundation system provides low lateral load
resistance due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile Therefore the
lateral load resistance contributed by the micropiles should be neglected Lateral
loads imposed on the building structure may be resisted primarily by passive
pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and grade beams and
by shear acting along the sides of the pile caps and grade beams Passive
pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and grade beams may
be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per
foot of depth pcf above the groundwater level and 175 pcf below the groundwater
level Additional resistance to lateral loads may be provided by shear resistance
between the sides of the pile caps and grade beams and the adjacent soils A side
shear resistance of up to 150 psf may be used for transient loads such as wind or

In general corrosion protection is one of the concerns for the micropile foundation
system Depending on the micropile foundation systems used there is a potential
for having inconsistent grout cover around the reinforcing steel of the micropile We
believe that the potential for inconsistent grout cover could be significantly reduced
if good installation practices and good quality control measures are implemented
We believe that a permanent steel casing for the micropile may provide sufficient
corrosion protection A permanent steel casing with a minimum diameter of
7 inches should be installed to the top of the dense supporting layer In addition
grout with a water cement ratio of about 0 40 to 0 45 should be used
As mentioned previously the performance of the micropile foundations is highly
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the
contractor The consistent implementation of production quality control measures
will result in a better micropile product We believe that a metering device to
measure and record the flow rate of the grout pumped into the micropile should be
provided and working at all times Grout should be produced with high speed
high shear mixers to reduce the potential for segregation of the neat cement grout
The grouting equipment should be equipped with a pressure gauge to monitor grout

Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling
one boring extending to a depth of about 52 feet below the existing ground surface
The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The boring was
drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers rotary
wash drilling and rotary coring equipment
The materials encountered in the boring were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils and rocks were classified in general conformance with
respectively Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on
the Log of Boring Plate A 1
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of coral rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a
hollow bit to cut into the coral rock formation The coral rock material left in the hollow
core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of coral rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
