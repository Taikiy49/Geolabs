for the proposed Lot 7 Crossroads project in the Kailua Kona area on the Island of Hawaii
The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map

project These recommendations are intended for the design of building foundations
slabs on grade retaining walls site preparation and pavements only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which had activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea which
are both still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain which makes up
the west central part of the island The lava formations encountered at the site appear to be
of a a and pahoehoe basalt type flows which spread and ponded as they approached the
ocean A a is typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling
a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders Typically a more
dense and layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core
Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface with an internal
structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas
These cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and they represent both
lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may
extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters pockets inter flow Cavities
are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt formations are considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit
water quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is
transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts
cavities and along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high
therefore they serve as the major water transmission features

Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
that the proposed office building be supported on a shallow foundation system consisting of
spread and or continuous footings We understand that the finished floor will be set at an
elevation of about 245 feet MSL Based on the proposed finished floor elevation we
anticipate that the north and north central portions of the proposed building footprint will
straddle a cut and fill transition Therefore we anticipate that the building foundations will bear
on either compacted fills or basalt rock formation Where encountered at the bottom of footing
elevation we recommend that the basalt rock formation be over excavated by a depth of
about 12 inches to provide a relatively uniform bearing surface and to reduce the potential for
structural distress resulting from the building foundations bearing on differing subgrade
materials The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with compacted structural fill
In general we anticipate that the basalt rock formation may be encountered at the bottom of
footing elevation at the north and north central portions of the office building footprint
Based on these conditions we recommend that an allowable bearing pressure of up
to 4 000 pounds per square foot psf be used for the design of footings bearing on compacted
structural fill This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
50 percent for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces In general
the footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished
grade
Where foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements the foundations
should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom
edge of the utility trench or they should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the
GEOLABS INC

utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable
foundation settlement
Based on the existing topography and the proposed finished floor elevation portions
of the office building may bear on the existing sandy basaltic gravel fill Therefore the
subgrade soils at the bottom of footing excavations should be moisture conditioned and
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to the placement of
reinforcing steel or concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the footing subgrades the soft and or
loose materials should be over excavated until dense materials are exposed in the footing
excavation The over excavation should be backfilled with structural fill materials
moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction or the bottom of footing may be extended down to bear directly
on the underlying competent material
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our recommendations
we estimate the total settlements to be less than 1 inch Differential settlements between
adjacent footings supported on similar materials should be on the order of about 0 5 inches

Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area voids and or cavities may be present in the basalt
rock formation encountered at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of foundation
support resulting from collapse of cavities below a footing we recommend that a program of
cavity probing and grouting be implemented during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that a probe hole be drilled at each spread
footing location and at 10 feet on center along wall foundations The probe holes should be
at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of
footing elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing
operation additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the
vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered
should be backfilled with controlled low strength material CLSM injected at low to moderate
pressures As an alternative the probe holes and cavities discovered may be backfilled with
sand cement grout with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches injected at low to moderate pressures
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that the probe drill be available on site until the probing and grouting operations
are complete It should be noted that a longer lag time between probing grouting operations
and foundation construction might be required in the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of cavities

Slabs On Grade
We understand that concrete slabs on grade are planned for the proposed office
building In general we recommend that a cushion fill layer consisting of 4 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be provided below the slab for uniform
support and to serve as a capillary moisture break A moisture membrane should be
incorporated above the capillary moisture break to reduce the potential for damage to
moisture sensitive floor coverings by moisture infiltration Two inches of moist fine sand may
be considered above the plastic membrane to provide protection of the membrane and to aid
in curing of the slab concrete Provision of the 2 inches of moist fine sand above the vapor
barrier will be left up to the discretion of the project structural engineer
The subgrade soils below the slabs should be scarified to a minimum depth of about
8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density If basalt rock
formation is encountered at the slab subgrade level the basalt rock formation should be proof
rolled with a minimum 15 ton vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes to help detect
and collapse near surface cavities and or voids
Exterior concrete walkways planned for the proposed project should be supported on
4 inches of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation In
addition crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways
with expansion joints at right angle intersections

Dynamic Lateral Earth Forces
Since the project site is located on the seismically active Island of Hawaii Seismic
Zone 4 in accordance with the Uniform Building Code dynamic lateral earth forces will
need to be considered in the design of retaining structures The dynamic lateral earth
force due to seismic loading a x 0 40g may be estimated using 16H pounds per
foot of wall length for level backfill conditions where H is the height of the wall in feet
The dynamic lateral earth force should be assumed to act at the mid height of the
retaining structure The dynamic lateral earth force presented above is in addition to
the static lateral earth pressures An appropriately reduced factor of safety may be
used when dynamic lateral earth forces are accounted for in the design of the retaining
structures
Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
A typical drainage system should consist of about 1 cubic foot of permeable material
such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped with non woven
filter fabric at each of the weephole locations The weepholes should be spaced not
more than 8 feet apart Backfill behind the retaining walls beyond the 3 inch minus
zone may consist of structural fill materials 6 inch minus granular materials Unless
covered by concrete or asphaltic concrete the upper 12 inches of backfill should
consist of relatively impervious materials such as the well graded on site soils to
reduce the potential for significant water infiltration behind the walls

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The compaction
requirement for fills or backfills below areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be
increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of rock
and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of passes of a
compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be conducted at
the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a representative
from Geolabs On the basis of this testing program the number of passes may then
be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction

recommended below the pipes to provide for uniform support
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes
It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper
backfill material around the pipes and improper placement could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes For the proposed water lines the granular bedding and the
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the water line pipes should consist of 1 inch
minus well graded granular materials The granular bedding and the initial trench backfill for
the water lines should be moisture conditioned and compacted to not less than 90 percent
relative compaction

Drainage
The finished grades outside the office building structure should be sloped to shed
water away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also
advised that gutter systems be installed around the office building and that the discharge be
diverted away from the foundation and slab areas Excessive landscape watering near the
foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided
or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration into
the subsurface
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab
edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce potential for significant water
infiltration into the subsurface In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations
and slabs

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the proposed project site were explored by drilling and
sampling four borings extending to depths of approximately 5 to 16 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with rotary
coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring
attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
