INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration conducted
for the proposed Makai Campus Drainage Improvements at University of Hawaii Manoa
The general location of the project site and its vicinity is shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
This report also summarizes the findings of our exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from analyses of the data obtained
during the field exploration The recommendations presented herein are for design input
and are not intended for construction estimation or bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a new drain line in the southwesterly corner of the Manoa
campus of University of Hawaii Approximately 1 200 lineal feet of trench excavation pipe
installation and trench backfill are anticipated for the project The proposed construction
will also include new manholes along the new drain line
Based on the available plans and sections provided to us the new drain line will
be constructed of reinforced concrete pipe ranging from 24 to 60 inches in diameter The
pipe will be installed at depths of approximately 10 to 16 feet below the existing ground
surface or at invert elevations of approximately 56 to 50 feet above Mean Sea Level
MSL The drain line will replace two existing drain lines running from University Avenue
to the existing drain system near Andrews Amphitheater

SITE DESCRIPTION
The alignment of the proposed drainage improvement extends from University
Avenue near Sinciair Library across the southwestern corner of UH campus down to an
existing manhole near Andrews Amphitheater

Ground surface elevations range from about 68 feet MSL at the northwesterly end

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built up during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene by
the activities of two basaltic shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau Koolau is the
younger of the two and reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million
years ago The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2967 00 Page 5
Koolau was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from northwest to southeast
trending rift zones These lava flows are interbedded pahoehoe and aa flows with
pahoehoe being the dominant form
After the cessation of the main shield building phase of Koolau there was a long
period of erosion The project site is situated in Manoa Valley which is an amphitheater
headed valley which was deeply eroded into the Koolau massif
During the Pleistocene Epoch there was a renewal of volcanic activity with the
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series Along the crest of the ridge along the
westerly side of Manoa Valley there are the Tantalus Roundtop and Sugarloaf vents
When these vents were active one or more lava flows from these vents spilled
down the westerly wall of the valley and moved across the valley floor These lavas
ponded and filled the lower portion of the valley The ponding allowed the lava to densify
as it cooled forming dense massive rock
Other eruptive events of the Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled these lava flows with
pyroclastic material particularly sand sized cinder Subsequent fluvial action has
transported minor amounts of alluvial soils onto the site
During the development of the University campus man made fills were placed to
grade the area

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
It is our opinion that the proposed drain line is feasible provided the geotechnical
engineering recommendations presented in this report are closely followed
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2967 00 Page 7
Since the proposed alignment of the drain line passes close to existing buildings
and facilities it is imperative that appropriate construction procedures good workman
ship and a proper construction monitoring program be employed in the construction of
the project These requirements are vital to reduce the potential of causing distresses or
damages to the adjacent existing facilities
Earth Loads on Drainage Pipes
Earth loads on pipes normally depend on trench wiath pipe diameter unit weight
of the backfill material and friction between the trench walls and the backfill Earth loads
on pipes will increase rapidly as the trench width increases Therefore the width of
trenches excavated for the installation of the new drain line should be kept to a minimum
To estimate the vertical loads on the buried drainage pipes for design purposes
a minimum unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot for the backfill material and a
maximum frictional coefficient of 0 25 may be used
Trench Excavation
Based on the plans and profiles provided to us the inverts of the future drain line
will vary from about 10 to 16 feet below the existing ground surface along the alignment
Therefore trench depths of up to about 17 feet including over excavation for pipe
bedding and rock cushion are anticipated
Based on extrapolation of the subsoil conditions encountered in our field
exploration conventional excavation methods may be used from to about
It is anticipated that excavation in this portion of the alignment will encounter
medium dense to dense silty sand or gravel for most of the trench depth
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2967 00 Page 8
It is anticipated that excavation from about to the end of
the drain line will be more difficult Our exploration encountered highly weathered basalt
formation from about four feet 4 below the existing ground surface down to the
estimated invert elevation
Since hard basalt rock formation was encountered close to the drain line invert
elevations in the borings and the surface of the rock formation may vary hard excavation
may be required for portions of the trench excavation Hard excavation is generally
considered to include chipping splitting hoerams and other methods of excavation in
consolidated or rock materials
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be at the choice of
the contractor within practical limits and safety considerations
The width of the drain line trench excavation should be kept to a minimum to
reduce the potential of significant ground settlement in addition excavated material
should not be stockpiled within 15 feet of the trench in order to reduce the potential
ground movement or subsidence which may cause damage to existing adjacent
structures
Trench Support
Safety regulations require that excavations be supported or sloped to minimize the
potential of collapse of the excavation walls The design and methodology of the
excavation support system is the responsibility of the contractor
The following comments are offered to aid the civil engineering consultant in
developing plans and specifications for the execution of the work
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2967 00 Page 9
Portions of the proposed drain line will be adjacent to existing buildings and other
site improvements In order to reduce the potential of inducing distresses or damages
to these existing buildings and improvements we recommend that these portions be
positively supported by sheeting bracing or other means
We recommend that continuous steel sheeting horizontally braced with cross
struts be used to support the trench excavations from to 4 80 and
from to 11 08 in order to reduce the potential of adjacent ground
movement or subsidence This consideration is critical since this portion of the
drain line is close to existing structures or roads Some of these facilities may be
supported on spread footings which are sensitive to movement of the upper soils
For estimating earth loads acting on the horizontal trench bracing or struts the
apparent pressure diagram presented on Plate 3 may be used The pressure
distributions illustrated in the pressure diagram do not include a factor of safety
Appropriate loads due to surcharge traffic or sloping ground conditions adjacent
to the trench excavations should be added to the earth pressure distributions for
design
Construction Precaution
The sheeting and bracing system used must comply with all applicable
safety requirement The adequacy and safety of the design and installation
of the sheeting and bracing system used should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His qualified representative who should be
GEOLABS HAWAIi
W O 2967 00 Page 10
required to be continuously present on site will have the best opportunity
to promptly observe any changing conditions during construction such as
unforeseen subsoils unexpected groundwater unsuitable construction
sequence or techniques etc which may affect the trench support stability
Even with good construction procedure some movement of the trench
support system may still occur due to changes in earth stresses which will
normally occur during excavation The magnitude of movement will depend
greatly on workmanship for example how quickly and tightly the trench
support is installed It also depends on the types of subsoils the depth and
width of excavation and also the rate of excavation
In view of the complexity of the problem it is difficult to accurately predict
movement of a trench support system and the adjacent ground Therefore
it is important to realize that the trench support should be installed properly
as early as practicable and that the adjacent ground be continuously
monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of movements such as
trench wall sloughing

be cut to a steeper ratio as determined by the project geotechnical engineer

Settlements
Settlement of the drainage pipes should be relatively small since the net increase
in vertical loads on the competent subsoils are small Based on the resuits of the field
standard penetration tests it is estimated that the total long term settlements may be on
the order of 0 5 inches
The above estimates assume proper construction procedures and good
workmanship Additional settlement could occur as a result of improper trench backfill
The backfill settlements after the installation of the drainage pipe is a function of
future trench backfill and the amount of backfill and compaction efforts If the trench is
properly backfilled with compacted lifts to 90 minimum compaction the future trench
settlement would be minimum
Pipe Support
Pipe bedding is an important design consideration since distribution of stresses
against the bottom of the pipe has a great impact upon its load support capacity
We recommend that clean granular material such as ASTM C 33 No 67 3B
Fine gravel be used as bedding material for the proposed drain line A minimum of six
inches 6 of granular bedding material should be placed below the pipes
Granular self compacting material such as 3B Fine gravel clean sand or clean
cinder should be used as backfill around the pipe from the bedding to a level of at least
one 1 foot above the top of the pipe This material is needed to allow the backfill to be
in contact with the pipe under its haunches and to provide lateral support for the pipe
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2967 00 Page 12
Backfilling
The trench excavations should be backfilled as soon as the drain line pipes are
properly installed inspected and tested in general the trench backfill requirements
should follow the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction City and County
of Honolulu September 1986 Section 11
The first zone of backfill extends from the bedding material to at least twelve inches
12 above the top of the drain pipes Select free draining well graded granular material
is recommended for use in this first zone of backfill to minimize the compaction effort
required and the resulting compaction stresses on the pipes The granular material should
be self compacting material such as 3B Fine gravel clean sand or clean cinder
Compaction by water ponding or jetting may be allowed provided the granular material
is self draining
The trench backfill from twelve inches 12 above the top of the pipes to the
finished grade in open areas or to two feet 2 below the road or parking pavement
subgrade may consist of the excavated on site sand and gravel The material should be
well graded less than three inches 3 in maximum size and have no more than 9
passing the No 200 sieve The backfill should be mechanically compacted to a minimum
90 of its maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
The trench backfill within two feet 2 below the road pavement subgrade should
consist of select borrow compacted to a minimum 95 of its maximum dry density as
determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78

Manhole Structures
It is anticipated that the manholes will be constructed on competent subgrades of
either rock formation or dense gravel To provide uniform support it is our opinion that
an eight inch 8 thick working platform of 3B Fine gravel should be provided below the
proposed manhole structures An allowable bearing capacity of 3 000 p s f may be used
for the design of the manhole structures bearing on the eight inch 8 gravel platform
The lateral earth pressure acting on the proposed underground structures will
depend on the type of backfill used the extent of backfill and the compaction effort on
the backfill material around the structures Therefore granular material such as select
on site sand and gravel is recommended for use as backfill around the structures to
reduce the compactive effort required
We recommend that the new manholes be designed to resist the following lateral
earth pressure provided granular backfill is used
Above water level 580 p c f equivalent fluid pressure
The above value assumes that the backfill around manhole structures is properly
placed and compacted Surcharge loading due to traffic or other applicable sources
should be added to the above values for design

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration and Laboratory Testing
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling three 3 borings to
depths ranging from 25 5 to 30 0 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate location shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 A truck mounted drilling rig was
used to drill the borings
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Spilt Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a standard spilt spoon sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The
blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12 inches or for fraction of a foot where a full
foot of penetration was not practicable are shown on the Boring Logs at the appropriate
sample depths
Moisture content determinations were performed on selected samples The results
of these tests are presented on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 3 at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 and would be shown on the Boring Logs as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
