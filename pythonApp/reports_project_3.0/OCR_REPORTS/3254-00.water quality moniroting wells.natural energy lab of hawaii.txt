PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is situated adjacent to the Natural Energy Laboratory of
Hawaii Hawaii Ocean Science and Technology Park NELH HOST property at Keahole
Point in the district of North Kona on the island of Hawaii The subject monitoring wells
were installed within Keahole Airport property hydraulically upgradient of all commercial
tenants within the NELH property
In keeping with the current groundwater monitoring program in place at the facility
it was desired to design and install groundwater monitoring wells to replace an original
control or background well set identified as W 3 During recent expansion of the
Cyanotech facility the original well set W 3 was eliminated
It was then desired to construct a replacement well set identical to the original W 3
wells to monitor the groundwater below the site and to aid in monitoring the impacts on
the adjacent coastal waters The new well set also named W 3 will serve as a control
point to obtain background groundwater samples upgradient from all development within
the NELH property
Since the site is underlain by a thin stratified Gnyben Herzberg lens it was
originally recommended that the monitoring wells be constructed as clusters with varying
completion depths to allow discrete monitoring of different intervals of the lens This was

similar to the existing monitoring wells currently being used in the NELH HOST complex
In keeping with the original W 3 well cluster design the new W 3 cluster was designed
to duplicate the monitoring of discrete groundwater zones
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our work on the project was to design drill and install replacement
groundwater monitoring wells for the original W 3 well cluster and to provide
documentation of the construction of the wells for inclusion in the NELH HOST
groundwater monitoring program
In order to accomplish this we have provided an exploration program consisting
of the following tasks and work efforts
1 Review of the groundwater conditions encountered in our previous efforts
at the site as well as the design of the original W 3 well cluster to aid in the
design of the replacement W 3 monitoring wells
2 Mobilization and demobilization of a truck mounted drill rig water truck
compressor and operators to and from the project site
3 Drilling and installation of three monitoring wells at the project site to depths
ranging between about 25 and 65 feet below the existing ground surface
The well locations were chosen by NELH personnel and marked in the field
prior to our mobilization
4 Coordination of the field exploration logging of the wells and supervision
of the well installation by a Field Geologist from our firm

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The project site is located on the western coastal flank of Mt Hualalai one of the
five basaltic shield volcanoes comprising the island of Hawaii Shield volcanoes are
characterized as being low dome shaped mountains built by the quiescent eruptions of
very fluid lavas The general slope on the flanks of the dome range from about 5 to 15
degrees above a horizontal plane Hualalai is generally considered to be the third oldest
of the five volcanoes having an age ranging from the late Pleistocene to Recent with its
most current activity occurring around the year 1801 Since 1801 Hualalai has been
dormant but occasional earthquakes suggest it remains capable of erupting to this date
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3254 00 Page 4
Hawaiian volcanic activity is typified by an abundance of flank eruptions along rift
zones or zones of weakness in the earth s crust The NELH HOST Park project site is
situated about 7 miles to the south and west of the main northwest southeast trending rift
zone of Mt Hualalai This rift zone forms a structural and hydrologic boundary which is
expressed physically at the surface as a line of small cinder and spatter cones trending
to the northwest from the summit area
Project Area Site Description
Most of the surficial basalt flows exposed at the ground surface in the vicinity of
the new monitoring wells are of the Pahoehoe type which is characterized by a relatively
smooth or ropy rock surface with numerous hummocks tumuli pressure ridges and
cracks In some nearby areas the pressure ridges and cracks form sharp topographic
relief Percolation of rainwater is thought to be very rapid due to the presence of well
developed cracks of several inches in diameter located near the new monitoring wells
The project site is barren rocky land with very sparse tufts of grass growth
Elevations at the site were unknown at the time of our field exploration though it was
estimated to be approximately 18 feet above Mean Sea Level judging from the depth of
the observed water table in Boring W 3
Subsurface Geologic Conditions
In general the initial cored boring W 3 66 feet in depth penetrated about 37 feet
of relatively dense to very dense pahoehoe basalt This upper flow unit appeared to be
widely fractured with a few thin strongly vesicular or clinkery zones Beneath this dense
surficial layer the boring generally encountered clinker or severely fractured pillow lavas
and black sand littoral scree deposits of high porosity These deposits extend from a
depth of approximately 37 to 66 feet the maximum depth explored below the existing
ground surface This sandy loose to moderately hard clinker zone is believed to have

formed by molten lava entering the ocean and causing steam explosions which broke the
lava at the flow front into sand and gravel sized fragments This pillow lava and scree
deposit then formed a base for the lava flow which continued to advance seaward

Hydrologic Conditions
Groundwater moves from areas of recharge to areas of discharge in response to
the driving force of the hydraulic head imposed by the recharge Generally the project
site is considered to be in a discharge area since the groundwater moves from the
recharge area on the upper slopes of Hualalai to discharge at the coastline
Tidal fluctuations observed in previously drilled wells at the project site indicate that
the subsurface rocks underlying the site are highly permeable and that the flux of the
groundwater below the site is rapid
Generally in volcanic aquifers groundwater flow and solute transport is
stratigraphically controlled Preferential flow paths occur in voids within and between the
lava strata in fractures and clinker strata Installation of monitoring wells with continuous
screened intervals may yield spurious data and may also cause cross contamination

between discreet flow tubes within the aquifer Therefore we proposed to construct a
cluster of wells with short screened intervals at the monitoring site This would allow
more discreet sampling of the individual stratigraphically controlled flow paths
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Well Locations
The general locations of the monitoring wells were chosen by representatives of
NELH HOST Subsequently the sites were adjusted somewhat in the field to improve
accessibility of the monitoring wells during installation and during the later monitoring
program The approximate locations of the wells are marked on the Site Plan Plate 2
The wells were installed in an alignment progressing approximately from south to north
beginning with W 3 then W 3A and finally W 3B The actual locations should be
surveyed to establish their true horizontal and vertical locations as well as elevations
Well Drilling and Installation
Our effort consisted of installing three groundwater monitoring wells identified as
W 3 W3 A and W3 B to respective depths of 64 34 and 24 feet below ground level to
monitor separate and distinct zones within the groundwater column These three wells
were installed as a cluster approximately 4 feet apart The ground surface elevations at
the three well locations appeared to be nearly identical
W 3 the first well installed was drilled using a truck mounted rotary drilling
machine advancing HQ wireline diamond coring tools By utilizing this method
continuous rock core samples were recovered to aid in the logging of subsurface
materials encountered This pilot hole was then reamed larger utilizing 4 inch diameter
casing to facilitate the installation of the 2 inch diameter PVC monitoring well The 4 inch
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3254 00 Page 7
casing was necessary to keep the boring open and clear through the loose caving clinker
and sand encountered below a depth of about 37 feet
The remaining monitoring wells W 3A and W 3B were drilled entirely by the air
percussion method previously outlined
The wells were drilled and constructed in general accordance with current EPA
guidelines governing the installation of monitoring wells The wells were also constructed
in such a manner as they are compatible with the existing monitoring program and
sampling equipment
Studies of monitoring well construction have shown that monitoring wells may be
contaminated during construction and thereby provide false or spurious base line data
Potential sources of contamination are drilling fluids and the materials and methods used
in development Therefore clean water and compressed air were used as drilling fluids
to minimize the potential introduction of contaminants during drilling The compressed
air was routed through an in line air filter to reduce the potential of contamination from the
compressor itself The drilling tools were decontaminated by steam cleaning prior to
mobilization to the site and washed with a trisodium phosphate solution followed by a
clean water rinse prior to the commencement of drilling
At the well cluster location an initial well W 3 was cored to a depth of about 66
feet below the ground level to obtain rock core samples for description of the formation
characteristics The two additional shallower wells of variable depths were drilled next to
each of the deeper pilot holes Two inch PVC well casings were installed with the
screened portions isolated in discrete zones in order to sample variable flow paths within
the lens For a sketch of the constructed well characteristics refer to Appendix B Plate
B 1 for a sketch of the constructed well characteristics

All three wells were packed with clean No 3 silica sand and sealed using bentonite
and cement grout Wellheads with steel locking caps were constructed for each well to
provide protection from potential tampering or vandalism
Well Development
It is recommended that prior to implementing any groundwater sampling procedure
or pump installation the wells should be developed by manual bailing to purge the well
casing and surrounding filter pack of any fine sediment material which may have been
introduced during the drilling and well installation procedure
Generally development of the wells by bailing is considered complete when
extracted water is visibly clear and free of suspended solids
In addition prior to the collection of water samples for testing purposes the wells
should be purged of a minimum of three to five well volumes to actively flush and move
fresh formation water into the well casing to assure a representative sample is collected
The purging effort should be completed a minimum of 24 hours prior to the actual water
sampling collection
Additional Monitoring Wells
We recommend that additional monitoring wells be added to the groundwater
monitoring program as the development of the HOST Park NELH complex continues in
the future Particular attention should be given to potential point sources of groundwater
contamination such as disposal trenches and tenants who mobilize large volumes of
water through their facilities Attention should also be given to tenants who develop high
tech facilities which may use solvents or other chemicals and materials that may have
potential impacts if released into the environment

LIMITATIONS
The analysis and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon
information obtained from field data points such as borings and or observation wells
Variations of conditions between the field data points may occur and the nature and
extent of these variations may not become evident until the actual monitoring program is
implemented If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the
findings and recommendations given in this report
The location of the field data points were determined by NELH personnel prior to
site mobilization Elevations of the data points were unknown at the time of our well
installation The physical location of the field data points should be considered accurate
only to the degree implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the field data points
depict the approximate boundaries between rock soil types and as such may denote
a gradual transition
Water level data from the field data points was collected at the times given in the
logs of borings in this report This data has been reviewed and interpretations made in
the formulation of this report However it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due
to variations in rainfall tides temperature and other factors

APPENDIX A
Field Exnloration
The borings identified as W 3 W 3A and W 3B were drilled either by advancing
HQ wireline coring tools and 4 inch diameter casings or a 6 inch diameter down hole air
percussion hammer Rock core was obtained from Boring W 3 and the material
recovered from the boring was classified by visual and textural examination in the field by
our geologist who monitored the drilling operations cn a continuous basis Samples
were not recovered from Borings W 3A and W 3B although a rough log of the boring was
kept by monitoring the drilling rate fall of the rods through voids and observation of rock
chip cuttings returned to the surface by the air circulation from the percussion hammer
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotery drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74 percent
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50 percent Generally itie following is used to cescribe the relative quality of the
rock based on the Practical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
