INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the grading foundation and pavement design of the three 3 proposed
reservoirs for the Kaupulehu Huehue Joint Utility Corridor

SITE DESCRIPTION
The three 3 project sites are located upslope easterly of Kaupulehu and Queen
Kaahumanu Highways and downslope westerly of the Mamalahoa Highway on the
Island of Hawaii Hawaii
The three sites are generally staged along an existing improved access roadway
which traverses the property limits between the Kaupulehu Resort and Huehue Ranch
from Queen Kaahumanu Highway to Mamalahoa Highway The area is characterized by
topography sloping slightly to moderately toward the west which retains the original
structure of the volcanic flows which have covered the site Both a a and pahoehoe flows
are evident throughout the area The reservoir site at 1 307 foot elevation is characterized
by pahoehoe lava on the surface the two lower reservoir sites are characterized by a a
lava on the surface Cinder and spatter cones collapsed lava tubes and pahoehoe
surface blisters are also observable within the limits of the project sites The areas are
sparsely vegetated

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five 5 shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both still active
The project sites are located on the northern flank of Hualalai mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island and consists partly of the prehistoric
basaltic lava flows of the Hualalai Volcanic Series which were most likely deposited during
the late Pleistocene epoch Stearns and MacDonald 1946
Hawaiian volcanic activity is typified by an abundance of flank eruptions along rift
zones or zones of weakness in the earth s crust The project sites are situated in the
northwest rift zone of Hualalai
The lava flows at the sites are a a basalt types with dense zones of hard rock and
loose zones of clinker overlying and intermixed with older pahoehoe flows The clinker
phase is highly permeable Lava tubes are generally not encountered in a a flows but
are not uncommon in the pahoehoe flows Cinder cones underlain and surrounded by
depositional scoriaceous cinder and spatter cones are located within the project area

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
In general our field reconnaissance indicates that the proposed water reservoir
sites are underlain by volcanic rock formations Our borings at the water reservoir sites
encountered relatively competent basaltic lava formations with some pockets of loose
clinker The volcanic rock formations extended to the maximum depths explored
Based on our findings construction of the proposed water reservoirs is feasible
from a geotechnical point of view provided that our recommendations are followed The
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2790 00 Page 7
underlying volcanic rock formations should provide adequate bearing for the proposed
structures Due to the possible existence of cavities within the volcanic formations we
recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting of the foundation subgrades
be conducted during construction This program is discussed in more detail below
Site Preparation
Prior to the commencement of any earthwork all areas within the grading limits
should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious materials The resulting stripped and
processed material should be removed from the site and disposed of properly Any voids
or yielding areas disclosed during the clearing operations should be overexcavated to firm
ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered
fill
Based on our exploration the majority of the cut and materials generated from cuts
will be volcanic rocks including clinker material The on site excavated rock materials
should be suitable for use as engineered fill if it properly broken down processed to a
12 inch minus miximum particle size placed and compacted If additional imported
material is required the material should generally conform to the requirements stipulated
in Section 16 Borrow material of the Standard Specifications for Public Works
Construction dated September 1986
If the volcanic rock materials encountered at the sites are processed and reduced
to a relatively well graded material with an average maximum size of about six 6 inches
and an absolute maximum size of twelve 12 inches the material may be utilized as
structural fill up to two 2 feet below the tank foundations Upper two foot portion of the
above structure fills or backfills below footings and slabs for the reservoirs should consist
of well graded granular material with a maximum dimension of three 3 inches This
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2790 00 Page 8
upper fill material will also act as a choking layer to the underlying 6 to 12 inch cobble
size fill embankment
In areas to receive fills the existing surface should be proofrolled with a vibratory
drum roller with a minimum static weight of 20 tons to expose and collapse any near
surface voids Any exposed voids should be backfilled as described below
Generally fills required should be placed in level lifts not exceeding twelve 12
inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of the maximum
dry density as determined in accordance with procedures described in ASTM Test
Method D 1557 78 Trench and structural backfills should be placed in level lifts not
exceeding eight 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
of the maximum dry density determined in accordance with procedures described in per
ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 The upper three 3 feet of fills and backfills below road
and footing or slab subgrade level should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent of
the maximum dry density
Any fills to be placed on slopes having ratios steeper than 5 1 horizontal to
vertical should be keyed and benched into the underlying soil or basalt formation to
lessen potential instability of the new fill A schematic representation of keying and
benching is shown on the attached sketch Plate 5
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
excavated volcanic rock material Instead a testing program to determine the number
of passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project On the basis of this testing
program the number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate
GEOLABS HAWAII

compaction In general six to eight passes would be adequate to compact each rock
fill lift using a sufficiently heavy vibrary compaction
Excavation
The borings indicate that the sites are generally underlain by materials which may
be excavated by conventional methods such as ripping with a D 9 bulldozer In some
localized instances hard excavation utilizing a hoe ram may be required
The preceding statement regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials is
based on our observations of the existing rock outcrops in the area and visual evaluation
of the cores
We recommend that all prospective contractors bidding on this site should examine
the rock outcrops and the boring data to make their own determination regarding the
rippability of the material
Slopes
Cuts in hard basalt rock less than twenty 20 feet in height may be designed with
dense materials such as loose clinker cinder or soils and fill slopes should be designed
with two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V slope ratios
Cut and fill slopes in excess of twenty 20 feet in vertical height should have an
eight 8 foot wide bench constructed at the middle of the slope
Soil slopes should be protected as soon as possible after construction to minimize
the potential for erosion

Surface water from areas above cut slopes should be diverted away from the cut
and prevented from flowing across the face of the slope
Structure Foundations
Based on the data obtained during our field exploration the structure foundations
will bear on in situ volcanic formations ranging from moderately hard basalt rock to loose
clinker Due to the likelihood that conditions at the finish floor elevations will vary special
consideration should be given to subgrade preparation at locations where foundations
bear on more than one type of material
Where the structures will span between differing subgrade conditions i e hard
basalt grading to loose clinker or placed fill the slab and footing areas should be ripped
to a depth of three 3 feet below the bottom of the slab or footing and recompacted to
a minimum of 95 percent of their maximum dry density to lessen the potential for
differential settlement across the structure On site materials processed to form a well
graded material with maximum size of three 3 inches may be utilized as select material
The material selected for use should be tested and approved by the geotechnical
engineer prior to placement
Providing that these recommendations are followed an allowable bearing pressure
temporary wind or seismic loads are considered
Footings should be embedded a minimum of twelve 12 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade Footings located next to utility trenches should be extended
down to an elevation equal to or deeper than that of the trench bottom The trench
backfill which should be completed prior to foundation construction should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent of its maximum dry density as determined in
GEOLABS HAWAII

accordance with procedures described in ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 or filled with
concrete
All footing excavations should be properly backfilled and compacted to 95 percent
density as indicated above
The site grading should be designed to divert surface water away from the tank
foundations
Settlement
Based upon the boring data and the anticipated reservoir loads the estimated
settlements would generally be on the order of less than one half of an inch total and
one fourth of an inch differential
Lateral Resistance
For passive earth pressure an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 p s f per foot of
depth may be used A friction factor of 0 4 may be utilized to determine the sliding
resistance of the tank footings
Cavity Probing and Grouting
To reduce the possibility of footing settlement due to collapse of loose pockets or
cavities in clinker or lava tubes in the underlying lava rock the footing excavations should
be probed at approximately ten 10 feet on centers with a minimum of one probe drilled
at each spread footing location If a cavity is located below a footing additional probe
holes may be made to delineate the horizontal extent of the cavity The probe holes
should have a minimum diameter of three 3 inches and extend below the bottom of the
footing to a depth of at least ten 10 feet The probe holes should be grouted with a
cement sand mixture from the bottom of the hole using low pressures The probing and

these recommendations are carried out properly and to locate potential voids near the
footing excavations as well as to recommend modifications to the above grouting
procedures which may be required to accommodate unusual conditions

Design Review
Preliminary and final drawings and specifications for the proposed construction
should be forwarded to the geotechnical engineer for review and written comment prior
to construction This review is needed to determine adherence to the earthwork
foundation and pavement design recommendations given herein If this review is not

misinterpretation of the recommendations
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the geotechnical engineer be retained to provide
geotechnical engineering services during construction of the grading and foundation
phases of the work This is to observe compliance with the design concepts
specifications or recommendations and to allow design changes in the event that
subsurface conditions differ from those anticipated at the time of preparation of this
report The recommendations given in this report are contingent upon such observations
If actual exposed rock soil conditions encountered during construction are different
from those assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made

APPENDIX A
The subsurface conditions at the proposed water reservoir sites were explored by the
drilling and sampling of five 5 borings to depths ranging between thirty 30 and thirty nine 39
feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans
Plates 2 3 and 4 The borings were drilled by a truck mounted rotary drill rig advancing
continuous core drilling tools
The materials encountered in the borings were examined and described and classified
in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2488 84 Description and Identification of
Soils Visual Manual Procedure by a geologist from our firm The logs of the brings are shown
Core samples of soil and rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which uses
a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow portion of the bit
is mechanically recovered for examination and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring
attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as REC
74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality of
rock masses This designation is only applicable to cores retrieved from in situ rock formations
RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material that is in excess of four
4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced fractures or
breaks if 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50
and would be shown on the Boring Logs as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to
describe the relative quality based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks
and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The rippability or foundation support characteristics of a rock mass is a function of the
relative hardness of the rock its relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense
basalt with a high RQD would be very difficult to rip and would possibly require blasting or
splitting for excavation
