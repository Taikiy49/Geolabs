approximate alignment planned. Our field exploration at the project site is generally

and design.

geotechnical recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This data report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering 
exploration performed for the Microgrid Feeder Grid and Bio-Fuel Line Routing project at 
Joint Base Pearl Harbor–Hickam (JBPHH) on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings of the site characterization resulting from our 
field exploration and laboratory testing for the project. These findings are intended for the 
preliminary design of underground fuel pipeline installation. The findings presented herein 
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
We understand that it is proposed to construct a new biogas energy power plant 
facility with approximately 20,000 linear feet of the underground transmission pipeline 
system in the JBPHH area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. This data report provides limited 
preliminary geotechnical site characterization in support of the transmission pipeline 
installation pricing estimate. 
A detailed transmission pipeline design was not available at the time this report 
was prepared. We envision that a detailed geotechnical exploration program will be 
performed after the pipeline alignment plans are defined and approved under a separate 
task order.

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age (2.7 to 3.4 million years ago) and forms the bulk of the western 
one-third of the island. The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age (2.2 to 2.5 million years ago) and forms the majority of 
the eastern two-thirds of the island. The end of volcanic activity associated with the 
Koolau Volcano was followed by a long period of landmass erosion whereby deep stream 
valleys were carved into the dome-shaped Koolau Shield Volcano. 
Approximately 850,000 to 1,000,000 years ago, some limited volcanic activity 
resumed with the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series from secondary vents 
scattered across the eastern portion of the Island of Oahu. The eruptions occurred along 
the established secondary rift zones of the Koolau Volcano. Some of the most notable 
landmarks located on the Island of Oahu, such as Punchbowl and Diamond Head Craters, 
including Aliamanu Crater, were created by the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
filled pre-existing depressions and accumulated at higher elevations. 
The rock formations are mainly inclined down towards the central volcanic vent. 
The volcanic tuff rock comprising Aliamanu Crater was created by the fall-out deposition

of super-heated volcanic ash and cinders containing some entrained blocks of basaltic 
lava rock, which were emitted from the Aliamanu volcanic vent. 
The northeastern part of the site lies near the southwestern flank of the Aliamanu 
vent and Salt Lake vent complex of the Honolulu Volcanic Series. As the molten magma 
rose through these vents, it reacted with the groundwater generating steam that 
developed into explosive eruptions depositing low cones of volcanic ash, which 
consolidated to form tuff, locally referred to as “mudrock”. 
The “Aliamanu tuff” is an older water-laid ash deposit that was laid when the 
seawater level was higher than the present level. The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited 
its material in alluvium that originated from the Halawa stream. 
After this phase of volcanic activity ceased, sedimentary deposits accumulated 
over the Aliamanu tuff before the resumption of the next phase of volcanic activity. The 
Salt Lake eruptions occurred when the sea level was about 40 to 60 feet below its present 
level. The Salt Lake eruptive events deposited its materials over earlier tuff from the older 
Aliamanu events. 
Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial soil materials 
were deposited in an estuarine and deltaic environment in the Pearl Harbor area. The sea 
level fluctuated several times, receding perhaps 300 feet below and rising about 100 feet 
above the present sea level. 
During the Recent Epoch, as the sea level rose to its current stand, the area was 
covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of soft and loose lagoonal marine 
deposits. Land development and reclamation projects in the past 40 to 60 years have 
added man-made fills, bringing the site to its present configuration.

Older Alluvium (Qa) 
Older alluvial deposits are generally very stiff to hard silty and clayey soils that 
originally were deposited in alluvial environments, buried over long periods of time 
by more recent deposits to become somewhat consolidated, and then 
subsequently uncovered at the ground surface by erosion or other geologic 
processes. This unit generally overlies the Koolau Basalt and consists of 
terrigenous sediments, which have been transported by stream action from the 
upper reaches of the streams. 
Gravel lenses representing buried stream channels are sometimes encountered. 
Portions of the Older Alluvium unit contain some weathered basaltic cobbles and 
boulders. These coarse-grained parts of the unit also may contain artesian 
groundwater from the basal freshwater system or as a sub-aquifer. The deposits 
may resemble saprolite soil deposits but are clearly differentiable by the layered 
characteristics. 
Older Alluvium materials generally exhibit low permeability (with the exception of 
the coarse-grained basaltic cobbles and boulders) and high strength with generally 
low settlement characteristics. Frequently, these materials show a moderate to 
high shrink/swell potential when subjected to fluctuations in moisture content.

Coral Limestone (Qls) 
This unit consists of fossil remnants of coral-algal reefs and their related detrital 
debris deposits. In Hawaii, the detrital deposits tend to be the dominant portion of 
any coralline rock assemblage. The detrital deposits are weakly to strongly 
cemented coralline debris consisting of gravel, sand, and silt-sized clastic 
fragments. Some of the coralline fragments were dissolved, and the solution 
cavities were filled with cemented and/or uncemented fine sands and silts. 
Due to the granular nature of the majority of the coral-algal deposits and the 
variable degree of cementation frequently present, this formation generally has 
high to moderate in-situ permeability and strength characteristics. The following 
subunits are typically included in this unit: 
Coralline Detritus 
This subunit consists of granular detritus broken from reef formations. The 
soils generally consist of medium dense to dense sandy gravel and gravelly 
sands. Silts and clays are only present in minor or trace amounts. Detritus 
also may be weakly cemented with occasional moderate to strong 
cementation. 
Coral Formation 
This subunit is composed of coral and calcareous algae in their original 
growth positions or detrital deposits, which have become moderately to well 
cemented. 
Sandstone 
It is a sedimentary rock composed mainly of cemented sand sized particles 
(clasts) consisting of individual mineral particles or grains of the weathered 
parent rock. The sedimentary particles comprising sandstone typically are 
derived from the breakdown of parent material into sedimentary particles. 
The sedimentary particles typically are eroded, transported, sorted by water 
or air and deposited as layers of sedimentary materials. Over time, the 
sediments may be buried by other sediments or geologic layers, and the

burial pressure combined with a cementing agent act to consolidate the 
grains and cement the pore space between the grains to form hardened 
sandstone. Typical cementing agents may be calcium carbonate (common 
in calcareous sand deposits of Hawaii) or silica and some clays. 
Sandstones containing such clast inclusions commonly are referred to as 
beachrock in Hawaii, having been derived from available shoreline geologic 
materials. 
It should be noted that this coral limestone unit is highly to extremely 
variable in terms of thickness, consistency and hardness. Typically, dense 
and hard coral formation is present in the upper coral ledge and extends 
deeper with interbedded pockets of coralline detritus and/or sandstone. 
Consistency of the coralline detritus may vary from very loose/soft to 
stiff/hard. 
Quaternary Honolulu Tuffs (Qht) 
Tuff from vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series consists of volcanic ash, which 
consolidated or hardened to form a medium hard rock that resembles siltstone or 
sandstone, locally called “mudrock.” The tuff is frequently found overlying coralline 
deposits or alluvium units. It should be noted that it is quite common to see a 
coarsening from accidental detritus and/or agglomeration toward the bottoms of 
the ash beds. 
Because the formation is a medium hard rock, it has very high strength 
characteristics. Structurally, the tuff is very competent. This formation has relatively 
low permeability, and groundwater generally is not encountered in this formation. 
When it does occur, it is usually in the form of seepage along joints. 
Recent Alluvium (Ra) 
These soils are generally the result of sedimentation in a very low energy 
environment, such as an estuary or bay. Frequently, the soils assigned to this unit 
accumulated very rapidly underwater and might not have been exposed to air. 
Therefore, there is a tendency to encounter under-consolidated sediments, which

settle under the weight of minimal overburden or structural loading. Recent 
alluvium is generally found within some stream channels and bordering some 
stream banks, at the mouths (outlets) of streams, and within broad valley floors 
and basin features surrounded by hills. These soils also may be found in proximity 
to swamp and tidal flat environments. 
The soils assigned to this unit generally are considered to have low in-situ 
permeability and low strength characteristics and the tendency for excessive 
settlement to occur in structures overlying soils of this unit. The following subunits 
are typically included in this unit. 
Recent Alluvium 
This subunit generally consists of tan to brown silty clays with varying 
amounts of sands and infrequent pockets of gravel and cobbles. The 
consistency is generally in the range of very soft to medium stiff. 
Lagoonal Deposit 
This subunit generally consists of light gray to medium gray sandy silts or 
silty sands in a very soft to soft consistency and/or very loose to loose 
relative density. Gravel-sized fragments are rare. Frequently, the silts are 
organic in origin and may have a mild to strong organic odor. 
Estuarine/Marsh Deposit 
This subunit generally consists of dark gray and brown silts and clays with 
fibrous organic materials in a very soft to soft consistency. This soil unit 
contains traces of fine to medium-grained sands. They are characterized by 
a moderate to strong organic odor and may contain small fragile mollusk 
shells. 
Recent Fill (Rf) 
This stratigraphic unit represents low-lying areas, which were filled during 
urbanization. The majority of these low-lying areas are underlain at relatively 
shallow depths by recent alluvium and were originally marsh areas. Some of the 
filled lands may be underlain by older alluvial soil deposits. In areas underlain by

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling six borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending to depths drilled of about 51 to 52.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
General Site Plan, Plate 2 and Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.6. 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by surface 
fills over harbor/lagoonal deposits and/or volcanic tuff, and coralline materials extending 
to the maximum depth drilled of about 52.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
surface fills consisted of 2 to 6 inches of asphaltic concrete pavement, medium stiff to stiff 
silts, and loose to medium dense sands and gravels extending to depths between about 
1 and 9 feet below the ground surface. Below the surface fills, we encountered loose/soft 
harbor/lagoonal deposits extending to about 26 feet below the ground surface overlying 
generally hard weathered tuff formation at Boring No. 1 location, while medium hard to 
hard weathered tuff formation was encountered in other locations (Boring Nos. 2 
through 6) extending to about 18 to 51 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Underlying the weathered tuff formation, coralline material was encountered 
consisting of loose to medium dense coralline detritus, medium dense to very dense 
weathered sandstone, and medium hard to hard coral formation extending to the 
maximum depths explored of about 52.5 feet below the existing ground surface. It should 
be noted that a layer of soft lagoonal deposit was encountered in Boring No. 2 between 
tuff rock and volcanic cinder sand at about 19 to 16 feet below the ground surface.

2.4 
Groundwater 
Elevations taken from Google Earth show majority of the pipeline alignment in the 
Joint Base Pearl Harbor - Hickam area to be at about +5 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  
Typically, the groundwater is present at the mean sea level (MSL) elevation of 
+0.0 feet and fluctuates between +2 and -1 feet MSL with the tide period. Occasionally, it 
raises up to +3.0 feet MSL during the king tide period. 
Therefore, groundwater along the proposed microtunneling alignment may be 
generally anticipated to be encountered at a shallow depth (less than 10 feet) as a basal 
water table near sea level through various parts of the site.  It should be noted that 
groundwater was encountered at about 13 to 23 feet below the ground surface, equivalent 
to -8 to -18 feet MSL, which is substantially lower than the mean sea level of +0.0 feet. 
Based on the review of the boring logs and core photographs, we believe the dense 
volcanic tuff rock formation may cap the basal water preventing it from discharging quickly 
into the boreholes after the rotary drilling and coring operation. 
Although artesian water is not anticipated in the vicinity of the proposed pipeline 
limits, it was encountered in the borings drilled for the Honolulu High-Capacity Transit 
Corridor Project in the Waipahu and Pearl City areas. 
In addition, the Pearl Harbor artesian aquifer basins are located near the project 
site where Koolau Basalt occurs at about Elevation +20 feet MSL or lower. Artesian

2.6 
Corrosion Potential 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower 
the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects.

On the basis of the laboratory resistivity and pH results, the subsurface soils along 
the proposed pipeline alignment have resistivity values ranging from approximately 
400 to 10,000 ohm-cm and pH values of between 7.5 and 9.0 within the upper 5 feet of 
soils, corresponding to corrosion ratings of 1 (Extremely Corrosive) to 4 (Moderately 
Corrosive) based on the guidelines provided by the City & County of Honolulu – Board of 
Water Supply. 
Levels of the chloride content of the on-site soils encountered near the existing 
ground surface are relatively low, on the order of 250 parts per million (ppm) or less, while 
the sulfate content is relatively low, on the order of about 400 ppm or less. 
Based on the chloride and sulfate content tests performed on the on-site soils, we 
believe that either Type I or Type II cement may be used for the concrete in contact with 
the ground plan for the project. It may be appropriate to consult with a professional 
corrosion engineer to review the test results and provide detailed recommendations for 
corrosion protection.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
six borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending to the maximum depths 
explored of about 51 to 52.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2, and Site Plans, Plated 3.1 through 
3.6. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight 
augers and coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-6.2.  
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). “Suggested Methods for 
the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International Society 
for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material is 
recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown 
on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality 
of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting drilling induced 
fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, 
the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. 
It should be noted that RQD does not apply to coral material. However, the RQD values of 
the coral material are included in the boring logs for rough characterization of the coral 
material. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock, based 
on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals."

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Five Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentation of the test results 
is provided on Plate B-1. 
Nine Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
In addition, three hydrometer tests (ASTM D422) were conducted on the samples to further 
evaluate the finer grain materials of the soils. Graphic presentations of the grain size 
distributions are provided on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Five Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-4 through B-8. 
To evaluate the undrained shear strength of the soils, two Unconsolidated 
Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests were performed on selected in-situ soil 
samples in accordance with ASTM D2850. The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” sample. The 
test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-9 and B-10. 
Nine Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were performed on 
selected core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the tuff/coral
