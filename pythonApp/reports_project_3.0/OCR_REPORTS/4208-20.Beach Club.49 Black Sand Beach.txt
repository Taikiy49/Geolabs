INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Beach Club at the 49 Black Sand Beach development located within the
Mauna Lani Resort in the Anaehoomalu area of the District of South Kohala on the Island
of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations based on our
review and analyses of the site conditions the design finished grades and the subsurface
information obtained for this project The geotechnical recommendations are intended for
the design of site grading structure foundations retaining structures underground utilities
and pavements for the subject project only The findings and recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the northern flank of Hualalai Mountain fronting the
shoreline A large portion of the project site is underlain by beach sand deposits The beach
sand deposits were derived from shoreline processes and deposited by wind and wave
action The lava formation encountered below the beach deposits and at the ground surface
at other portions of the site appears to be of a a type flow which spread downslope from
vents further up on the mountain flank A a lavas are typically characterized by a porous
rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments
including cobbles and boulders Typically a denser and layered lava rock material is
contained within the lava flow core
GEOLABS INC
W O 4208 20 Page 5
Cavities such as lava tubes or other void spaces are quite commonly encountered
buried within a a lavas Many of these cavities were formed when the still molten lava
created the structures by means of flow and ponding of the lava The cavities represent both
lava tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava
tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like
structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow
cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
The a a basalt lava rocks and clinkers encountered at the site are generally
considered to be permeable rock and may transmit water readily in both horizontal and
vertical directions Water is transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints
fractures inter flow contacts cavities and along embedded clinker layers The permeability
of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as the major water transmission
features

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions at the project site generally
consist of beach sand deposits of varying thickness across the project site The beach sand
deposits are underlain by a a basalt formation extending to the maximum depths explored
of about 16 5 to 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface One boring drilled at the
eastern portion of the project site encountered a a basalt extending from the ground surface
to the maximum depth explored of about 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface It
should be noted that our borings encountered about 5 to 13 feet of beach sand deposits at
the site The beach sands graded from very loose near the ground surface to medium
dense dense with increasing depth Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the
time of our field exploration
As indicated above the beach sand deposits encountered at the ground surface were
generally in a loose and dry state The loose and dry near surface beach sands would not
provide suitable foundation support for the proposed structures due to lack of confinement
and moisture In addition the loose and dry beach sands would pose some difficulties to
heavy construction equipment working in the area Therefore the near surface beach sands
will need to be over excavated and the exposed sandy surface should be capped with
structural fill to provide the confinement needed to support the heavy equipment during the
construction activities and the proposed structures
GEOLABS INC

In order to provide a firm base for construction equipment to work in the area we
recommend that the loose near surface beach sand deposits be over excavated minimum
of 24 inches below existing ground surface to expose the medium dense beach sands and
be capped with structural fill materials well graded 6 inch minus granular fill materials As
a minimum we recommend that a pad of 12 inches of well compacted structural fill be
provided at the finished subgrades finished mass graded surface prior to commencement
of building construction In addition foundations for the proposed structures except the
swimming pool located above the beach sand deposits should bear on at least 12 inches
of structural fill
Prior to the over excavation of the loose near surface beach sand deposits we
recommend that the entire sandy area be thoroughly wetted and soaked to slightly densify

by about 24 inches below the existing ground surface It should be emphasized that the
sandy soils should be kept moist and wet proof rolled and should be promptly capped with
a minimum of 12 inches of structural fill The excavated beach sands should not be re used
as a source of general fill or structural fill materials unless the sands are mixed with the basalt
rock fragments to provide a well graded fill material

Site Grading
Based on the existing topography and the design finished grades we anticipate that
the proposed site grading may consist of cuts and fills of about 10 feet relative to the existing
ground surface At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should
be thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials including the stockpiles of chopped kiawe logs should be removed and disposed
of properly off site
After clearing and grubbing the entire sandy area should be thoroughly wetted and
soaked to slightly densify the loose sands and to provide some apparent cohesion to the
sands After thorough wetting of the sands we recommend that the loose beach sand
deposits be over excavated by about 24 inches below the existing ground surface to expose
the underlying medium dense beach sands It should be noted that further moisture
conditioning of the sands might be required to keep the exposed sands moist and wet
The exposed sandy soil subgrades should then be proof rolled with a vibratory drum
roller a minimum of six passes prior to the placement of new compacted fills It may be
necessary to limit the size of the vibratory drum roller to about a 10 ton vibratory roller or
smaller to reduce the potential for inducing a pumping subgrade condition in the sandy soils
After the proof rolling operations the compacted beach sands should be promptly capped
with a minimum of 12 inches of structural fill The structural fill should be compacted with the
vibratory drum roller a minimum of six passes to obtain a well compacted mass If the beach
sand is allowed to dry out before placement of the structural fill the beach sand should be
re moisture conditioned and compacted as recommended above
In the other areas that are to receive fill and finished subgrades in cut areas exposing
a a basalt we recommend that the subgrades be proof rolled with a 20 ton vibratory drum
roller for a minimum of six passes to help detect and collapse near surface cavities and
voids and to provide a firm base for the placement of fills The proof rolling operations would
also serve to consolidate the porous a a basalt formation surficial clinker materials exposed
at the ground surface

Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the retaining
structures discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
GEOLABS INC

instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the retaining structure
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of well graded granular
fill material less than 3 inches in maximum dimension Unless covered by concrete
slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious material
to reduce the potential for water infiltration behind the retaining structure
Considering the relatively permeable nature of the beach sand deposits and the a a
basalt formations we believe that the build up of hydrostatic pressures behind the
swimming pool walls should be negligible without a drainage system Therefore the
typical drainage system behind retaining structures may be omitted for the swimming
pool walls only
Underground Utilities
It is anticipated that most of the trenches for utilities will be excavated in the a a basalt
compacted fill or beach sand deposits It should be noted that trench excavations made in
the beach sand deposits may cave in quite readily due to the nature of the beach sands
Therefore care should be taken by the contractor to avoid significant undermining of the
adjacent structures within proximity to the trench excavations In addition shoring of the
trenches may be necessary during the utility line installation Shoring of the utility trench
excavations should be the responsibility of the contractor and should conform to all
applicable local state and federal regulations
In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation is recommended below the utility lines for uniform support Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel should be used for the initial trench backfill
up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to
reduce the need for compaction of the backfill thus reducing the potential for damaging the
pipes The upper portion of the trench backfill should consist of granular materials generally

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 at the approximate locations shown on
the General Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of our borings ranged from about 11 5 to
21 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted
drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown
as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD wouid be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
