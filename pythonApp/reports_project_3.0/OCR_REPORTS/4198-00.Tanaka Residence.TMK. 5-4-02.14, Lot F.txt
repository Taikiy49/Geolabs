REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Hawi and Pulolu Volcanic
Series of Kohala which were deposited during the middle to late Pleistocene Epoch
Stearns and Macdonald 1946 These series are typically composed of basaltic and
andesitic lava flows which are mantled by a relatively thin layer of palagonitized ashy soils
locally referred to as Pahala Ash The ashy soils around the project site are generally
silty materials with relatively high in situ moisture contents The soil encountered at the
project site may also be a product of in situ weathering of the lavas rather than an air laid
ash
In general the majority of the lava flows underlying the ashy soils at the project
site are in a a form which is characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly surface of
fragmental clinkers Typically these a a flows are underlain by a dense core of massive
basalt which is frequently interbedded with thinner less continuous clinker layers

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions at the project site
generally consist of stiff to very stiff clayey silts with varying amounts of weathered basalt
fragments extending from the surface to the maximum depth explored of approximately
10 feet below the existing ground surface The clayey silt ashy soils were generally in a
moist to very moist condition The test pits generally indicated that the amount of
weathered basalt fragments in the ashy soils increased with depth
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site foundations
consisting of spread footings may be used to support the new elevated residence An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for
the design of footings bearing on the stiff to very stiff clayey silt soils with weathered
gravel and cobbles encountered at shallow depths Due to the post and pier type
construction the footings should be embedded a minimum of 36 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grade
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report
House Foundations
We understand that the proposed residence will be elevated with a post and
pier type construction Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project
site we recommend that foundations consisting of spread footings be used to support the
new residence An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for the
design of footings bearing on the stiff to very stiff clayey silt soils with weathered gravel
W O 4198 00 Page 7
and cobbles encountered at shallow depths This bearing value is for dead plus live
loads and may be increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by
wind or seismic forces
Due to the post and pier type construction the footings should be embedded a
minimum of 36 inches below the lowest adjacent finish grade For footings constructed
near the tops of slopes or on slopes the footings should be embedded deep enough
such that the horizontal distance measured from the outside edge at the base of the
footing to the slope face is not less than 8 feet
Where foundations are located next to retaining structures utility trenches and or
easements they should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the retaining structure
foundations utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural
loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing
excavations they should be over excavated to expose firm materials The
over excavation should either be backfilled with granular material compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom should be extended
down to underlying competent material Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with procedures described in ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91
If the foundations are designed in accordance with our recommendations we
estimate total settlements to be less than 1 inch with differential settlements on the order
of inch

Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on compacted on site soils
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the
soil around footings is well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Retaining Structures
Due to the slightly to moderately sloping ground conditions at the proposed house
location we understand that a short retaining wall of up to about 5 feet in height is
planned The following general guidelines may be used for preliminary design of retaining
structures
Retaining Structure Foundations
In general we believe that retaining structure foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the House Foundations
section of this report The footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches In
addition foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum
depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade For sloping ground
conditions the footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 6 foot set back
distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of
the slope Footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be
constructed in stepped footings

Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the
retaining structures discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an
alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain
may be used instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product
should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the
retaining structures
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of on site and or
granular fill material less than 3 inches in maximum dimension Unless covered by
concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively
impervious material to reduce water infiltration behind the retaining structures

that the proposed site grading may primarily consist of cuts of up to about 5 feet and

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
After clearing and grubbing the areas within the proposed house and pavement
limits should be excavated where necessary to the design finish subgrades In
areas to receive fill the subgrade should be scarified to a minimum depth of
8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction The excavated
materials may be reused as a source of fill materials provided that the materials
meet the gradation requirements presented in the Fills and Backfills subsection
of this report Loose and or soft spots encountered at the excavated subgrade
levels should be removed and replaced with granular fill materials compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

Fills and Backfills
In general we believe that the excavated on site soils may be re used as a source
of general fill material at the site provided that the materials are free of vegetation
deleterious materials clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 6 inches in
maximum dimension In addition the fill material should be moisture conditioned
and compacted as recommended in the following subsection entitled Fill
Placement and Compaction Requirements
Imported material should be well graded granular material with no particles greater
than 6 inches in largest dimension Imported material should be observed and or
tested by Geolabs Hawaii for its suitability prior to being transported to the site
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches
in loose thickness moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Where fills are placed under pavements the upper 3 feet of the fill below the
pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM
Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage
by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rock boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number
of passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction
should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the
observation of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii On the basis of this testing
program the number of passes may then be used as the fieid criterion for
adequate compaction

Excavation
We understand that an excavation of up to about 5 feet in depth may be necessary
for the construction of the retaining wall Based on our field exploration the
proposed residential site is underlain by weathered basalt gravels and cobbles in
an ashy soil matrix at shallow depths It is anticipated that the near surface soils
may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such as large
backhoes However excavations in localized hard areas may require the use of
hoerams or chipping
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based
on our visual observation of the existing site and field data from the test pits
excavated at the site We recommend that the contractors proposing to bid on this
project be encouraged to examine the site conditions and the test pit data to make

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the test pits it appears that
most of the cut slopes would expose the weathered basalt gravel and cobbles in
ashy soil matrix In general we believe that cut slopes may be designed with a
slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter
Permanent fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or
flatter Fills to be placed on slopes with inclinations steeper than 5H 1V should be
keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against
sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in
level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement
recommendations Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back
to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face Water should be
diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as
soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes

Carport Slabs and Concrete Driveways
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the carport slab and
concrete driveway The subgrades for slabs on grades should be scarified to a depth of
at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D155791 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
A 6 inch layer of select borrow subbase over compacted moist subgrade is
recommended for more uniform support The select borrow subbase should consist of
crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
We recommend that the concrete slab be designed for the intended loads For design of
structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per cubic inch pci may
be used for the compacted select borrow subbase Provisions should be made for proper
load transfer across the slab joints subjected to vehicular traffic The subgrade soils
beneath the garage and driveway slabs should be prepared in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Site Preparation subsection of this report
Utility Lines
We envision that new utility lines i e water lines will be required for development
of this residence It is anticipated that most of the trenches for utilities will be excavated in
the surficial ashy soils with weathered basalt fragments In general a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is
recommended below the pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for the trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce
compaction of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes

The upper portion of the trench backfill should consist of granular material
generally less than 6 inches in maximum size as recommended in the Fills and Backfills
subsection compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Where trenches will be
located in paved areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade
should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the residential structure should be sloped to shed
water away from foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also
advised that gutter systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be
diverted away from the foundation and slab areas Excessive landscape watering near
the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should
be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the
subsoils
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab
edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce water infiltration In addition
drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to
drain surface run off away from the foundations and slabs

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by excavating four test pits
designated as Test Pit Nos 1 through 4 to depths of approximately 6 to 10 feet below the
existing ground surface The approximate locations of the test pits are shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2 The test pits were excavated using a rubber tired backhoe and operator
from James H Luke Sons Inc
The materials encountered in the test pits were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the excavation operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Test Pits Plates A 1 through A 4 of

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 tests were performed on selected soil samples
as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests
are presented on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of
the test are summarized on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depth
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
One direct shear test ASTM D 2166 was performed on a selected soil sample
to evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plate B 2
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed
on a near surface soil to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soil
Results of the test are presented on Plate B 3
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a
near surface soil sample Results of the test are presented on Plate B 4
