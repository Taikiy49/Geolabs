INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Installation of Street Lights project along Waokanaka Street in Honolulu on
the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations derived from
our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These recommendations
are intended for the design of light pole foundations only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located to the northwest of Pali Highway in the Nuuanu area on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii It is proposed to install new lights along approximately 3 360 lineal
feet of Waokanaka Street We understand that the new light poles will be installed along the
top of the slope on the east side of Waokanaka Street In general the new light poles will
be approximately 27 5 feet in height above the ground level The structural loads that are
expected to be imposed on the light pole foundations which were provided by the project
structural engineer are as follows
e Vertical load 500 pounds
e Lateral load at the base 816 pounds
e Overturning moment at the base 22 400 foot pounds
Based on the topographic survey map provided Waokanaka Street varies in elevation
from about 730 feet Mean Sea Level MSL near the northeastern end of Waokanaka Street
to about 535 feet MSL near the intersection of Waokanaka Street and Pali Highway In
general the existing ground between Waokanaka Street and Pali Highway slopes down at
a slope inclination varying from about three horizontal to one vertical 3H 1V to as steep as
one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V

at the site to develop a soil rock data set for the formulation of geotechnical engineering

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion
incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available
to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When
the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was correspondingly
lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level When the sea level
was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher
elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and the soil materials are
transported downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once
these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium In general stream
flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy in that the stream flows transmit large volumes
of water for very short duration Because of this transport of sediments is intermittent and
the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders
cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional base levels change these sediment
loads are left as deposits

When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin to
alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material
Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion of
the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate Simultaneously
erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in headwaters This
continued erosion generates material that is transported downslope covering the older
alluvial deposits Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer
sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time In addition their consistency and
density are generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered dense silty sand and gravel underlain by
stiff silty clay Groundwater was encountered in two of the four borings at depths of about
15 feet below the ground surface at the time of field exploration In order to develop the
required bearing and lateral load resistances for the new light pole structures we recommend
that drilled shaft foundations be used to support the proposed lighting system Based on the
anticipated structural loads and the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we
recommend that drilled shafts with a minimum diameter of 18 inches be used for the
proposed 27 5 foot high light pole structures The drilled shaft foundations should have an
embedment length of not less than 8 feet below the design finished grades Based on the
length of drilled shafts recommended the drilled shafts will generally be embedded in the
dense silty sand and gravel with some of the drilled shafts extending into the stiff silty clay
It should be noted that the performance of the drilled shafts depends significantly upon
the contractor s method of construction construction procedures and workmanship
Therefore the recommendations and general guidelines presented in this report should be
followed by the contractor during the foundation construction work Detailed discussions and

recommendations for design of foundations and other geotechnical aspects of the project are

Light Pole Foundations
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances the proposed
new light pole structures should be supported by a foundation system consisting of
cast in place concrete drilled shafts Based on the anticipated structural loads and the
subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we recommend that drilled shafts with a
minimum diameter of 18 inches and an embedment length of not less than 8 feet below the
design finished grades be used to support the proposed light pole structures
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally from
skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable compressive load
capacity of up to 10 kips per shaft may be used The compressive load capacity is for dead
plus live loads and may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as wind or
seismic forces The end bearing component of the drilled shafts has been discounted in our
analyses
Lateral loads imposed on the light pole foundations are resisted by resistance of the
shaft soil system to deflect Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is a function of the
stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable deflection at the top of
shaft and induced moment in the shaft Based on the information provided each drilled
shaft will be subjected to a lateral load of 816 pounds with an associated overturning moment
of about 22 400 foot pounds at the ground surface The anticipated settlements of the light
pole foundations are estimated to be on the order of less than 0 25 inches We believe that
the settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the light pole foundations because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations
However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
GEOLABS INC
pots

method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the following conditions
may have a substantial effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the contact
between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper construction
techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft holes and
in placing concrete into the holes A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch
range should be used to provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the
surrounding soil The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce segregation
of the aggregates from the concrete mix If precast concrete pole bases are used the gap
between the pole base and the surrounding soils should be backfilled with concrete or lean
concrete
It should be noted that the upper dense silty sand and gravel may be susceptible to
some caving in during the drilling operations If the surrounding soils are disturbed or
over excavated during construction the disturbed soils should be removed and backfilled
with lean concrete This precautionary measure will aid in maintaining lateral support of the
drilled shaft foundation since the foundations are located at or near the top of a slope A
representative of Geolabs should be present to observe the installation of drilled shafts
during construction to confirm the subsoil conditions and embedment depths

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations presented in this report are based in part upon
information obtained from field borings Variations of subsurface conditions between and
beyond the field borings may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not
become evident until construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will be
necessary to re evaluate the recommendations presented in this report
The locations of the field borings are approximate having been estimated by taping
from visible features shown on a topographic map provided by Ronald N S Ho Associates
Inc Elevations of the borings were also obtained from the same plan The locations and
elevations of the field borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by
the methods used
The stratification breaks shown on graphic representations of the borings depict the
approximate boundaries between soil rock types and as such may denote a gradual
transition Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the
graphic representations and or presented in the text of this report These data have been

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings to depths of about 26 3 to 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
trailer mounted and a portable drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer tests provide an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the soil samples Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths
