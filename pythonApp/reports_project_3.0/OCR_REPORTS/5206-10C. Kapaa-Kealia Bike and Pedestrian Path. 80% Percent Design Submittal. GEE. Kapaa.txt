SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our exploratory borings drilled at the proposed Moikeha Stream Bridge
improvement site generally encountered a surface fill beach layer to depths of 4 to 6 feet
The surface fill beach layer consisted of medium dense sand and silty sand medium stiff
sandy clay and boulders The fill oeach layer was underlain by lagoonal deposits to about
140 to 163 feet below the existing ground surface The lagoonal deposits generally
consisted of very loose to very dense sand and silty sand very soft to stiff sandy silt and
clayey silt and loose clayey gravel Below the lagoonal deposits an alluvial deposit was
encountered to the maximum depth explored of 190 5 feet below the existing ground
surface The alluvial deposit generally consisted of medium stiff to very hard clayey
silt sandy silt with organic material cobbles and boulders and dense to very dense
sandy clayey gravel Groundwater was encountered in the borings at depths from about
5 1 to 5 6 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration
Based on the highly compressible lagoonal deposits encountered at relatively
shallow depths and the moderate to high structural loads anticipated for the bridge
structure we believe that appreciable settlements will occur if a shallow foundation system
is used to support the proposed bridge structure Therefore we recommend that the
proposed bridge structure be supported on a deep foundation system consisting of
16 5 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles
The driven piles will extend through the highly compressible lagoonal deposits and
into the underlying stiff and dense alluvial deposits The driven piles will derive support
primarily from side resistance between the sides of the pile and the surrounding soils
Preliminary pile lengths of 164 and 172 feet may be used for the abutments and piers
respectively
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific
recommendations for design of the bridge improvement project

This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Moikeha Stream Bridge for the Kapaa Kealia Bike
Pedestrian Path project in Kapaa on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The general location

engineering analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of bridge
foundations retaining structures and site grading only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene
Epoch more than 2 4 million years before present Following the cessation of this
main shield building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of
basaltic lavas of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition
of alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation
The majority of the Island of Kauai is covered by lavas of the Waimea Canyon
Volcanic Series These lavas consist of four distinct formations Napali Olokele
Haupu and Makaweli These formations are comprised of thin bedded a a and
pahoehoe flows to massive basalt flows that ponded in calderas and graben
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of
Kauai These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded
from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various locales Associated with
the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at the vent
During the Pliestocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted
global sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Kauai
remained essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in
nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both
higher and lower relative to the present sea level of Kauai
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenious sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at

The basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lavas of the Koloa Volcanic Series are

2 2 Existing Site Conditions
The bridge improvement project site is located along the portion of the
Kapaa Kealia Bike and Pedestrian Path at the Moikeha Stream crossing in Kapaa on
the Island of Kauai Hawaii The existing bridge is a six span concrete bridge structure
approximately 114 feet in length and about 12 feet in width The existing pile section
that was visible consisted of a 14 inch square concrete pile section The existing bridge
is currently being used for pedestrian traffic
The stream banks adjacent to the existing bridge structure were lined with
dumped and grouted riprap boulders Water was noted flowing within Moikeha Stream
at the time of our field exploration
Based on available plans the existing ground surface elevations range from
about 7 to 8 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the existing bridge abutment structures
In general the existing ground surface slopes down towards the stream at about
two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V inclination at the bridge site

Uplift Load Resistance
Uplift loading of the foundation is typically encountered during a seismic event
Since seismic analysis need not be performed for the Island of Kauai because of
the low design ground acceleration we understand that the piles are not subject to
uplift loading Therefore uplift load resistance analysis was not performed

Foundation Settlements
Settlement of the driven pile foundations will result primarily from elastic
compression of the pile and the subgrade response We estimate that the total
settlement of the driven pile supported foundation to be less than 0 75 inches with
differential settlements between piles not exceeding about 0 5 inches We believe
that these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are

Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering energies in the
range of about 30 000 to 70 000 foot pounds in the field The hammer should be
equipped with an energy level control such that driving within the loose soft soils
may be controlled at a lower energy to reduce tensile stress development which
could damage or cause cracking of the piles
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy
to the top of the pile and also reducing damage to the piles Geolabs should review
the pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate if the equipment is capable of
installing the piles to the recommended capacity The contractor should forward
pile and driving equipment data forms to Geolabs prior to construction for

Test Pile Program
Based on the planned bridge construction we recommend that a test pile program

following objectives

2 To estimate the production pile lengths

the base of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an
alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may
be used instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product
should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of Type A Structure
Backfill Material conforming to Section 703 20 of the HSS a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction Unless covered by concrete slabs or pavements the upper
12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively impervious material to reduce the
potential for water infiltration behind the walls In addition the backfill below the
drainage outlet or weepholes should consist of the relatively impervious material
to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the footing subgrade The relatively
impervious material should be compacted to not less than 90 percent relative

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the proposed bridge improvement site were
explored by drilling and sampling two borings designated as Boring Nos M 1 and M 2
extending to depths of about 162 and 190 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight
augers and coring tools The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 3
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 1
through A 2 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
