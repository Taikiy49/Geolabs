In accordance with our fee proposal dated this letter report provides
retaining wall alternatives and recommendations for the Maile Way and University Avenue
Repair project located in the Honolulu area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering

selection and the design of the retaining wall foundations only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located in the Honolulu area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
project site is located near the intersection of Maile Way and University Avenue as shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 The project consists of the removal and replacement of
approximately 50 linear feet of the existing Concrete Rubble Masonry CRM retaining wall
located adjacent to the Mid Pacific School baseball field
The existing CRM wall has a retaining height of about 9 feet with a concrete
sidewalk at the top of the wall area Based on visual observations of the existing wall face
it appears that the existing wall has moved out horizontally In addition it appears that the
existing concrete sidewalk and curb at the top of the wall has settled
The area behind the existing CRM wall concrete sidewalk and curb is University
Avenue There are two traffic lanes leading into Manoa Valley in this area with one of the
traffic lanes merging into the other lane In the other direction exiting Manoa Valley there
is one traffic lane that expands to two traffic lanes A significant amount of vehicular traffic
was observed on University Avenue near the project area during our field exploration

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
one boring designated as Boring No 1 to a depth of about 31 5 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate location of the boring is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration generally encountered a surface fill layer about 9 feet in
thickness The surface fill layer generally consisted of stiff to very stiff clay and loose to
medium dense sands and gravels The surface fill layer was underlain by medium stiff to
very stiff clay to about 31 5 feet below the existing ground surface the maximum depth
drilled
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled boring at the time of our field
exploration It should be noted that groundwater levels at the project site may fluctuate
depending on seasonal precipitation and other factors Detailed descriptions of the
materials encountered in the boring are presented on the Log of Boring Plate A 1 in
Appendix A Results of the laboratory tests performed on selected soil samples are
presented in Appendix B
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our exploration the project site is generally underlain by stiff
clay soils Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled boring at the time of our field
exploration
Our laboratory testing on the clay materials indicated a high expansion potential
We believe that the wall structure may be supported on shallow foundations provided that
a minimum of 36 inches of low expansive select granular fill material is provided below the
wall footings to reduce the effects of the expansive on site soils The wall foundations
should bear on compacted granular fill material
Alternatively a concrete wall structure supported on a drilled shaft foundation may
be considered to reduce the amount of excavation required during wall construction The
drilled shaft foundation consists of 2 foot diameter shafts with shaft lengths of 15 feet and
spaced at about 6 foot intervals
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report

CRM Wall Foundations
One of the wall alternatives being considered is replacing the existing wall with a
similar CRM wall system These walls are generally gravity type walls that primarily rely
on the wall weight for lateral resistance Retaining wall recommendations are provided
below
Wall Foundations
Our laboratory testing on the clay materials indicated a high expansion potential
To reduce the potential for differential movement of the wall we recommend that a
minimum of 36 inches of compacted granular fill material be provided below the
bottom of the wall footing The subgrade material at the bottom of the granular fill
layer should be scarified a minimum depth of 8 inches and moisture conditioned to
at least 2 percent above the optimum water content and compacted to at least 90
percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557
Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the site we
believe that an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot
psf may be utilized for the design of the retaining wall foundations bearing on
properly compacted granular fill material Wall footings should have a minimum
width of 18 inches and should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the
lowest adjacent finished grade
We estimate that settlements for footings bearing on properly compacted granular
fill as recommended herein to be less than one 1 inch total Differential
settlement is estimated to be less than one half 1 2 inch
lf soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing
excavations the materials should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials The over excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill material
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom
may be deepened to the underlying firm materials
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded
below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward
from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation
settlement

Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure

Select granular fill and imported fill should consist of non expansive granular
material such as crushed coral basalt or cinder sand The material should be
well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and
should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 Imported fill materials should be tested by
Geolabs Inc for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the
project site for its intended use
Fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system consists of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around the perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a
prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used
in lieu of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be
hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall Unless
covered by concrete the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of impervious
material such as the on site clay material to reduce water infiltration behind the
wails
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative from
Geolabs Inc prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation
bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
As indicated above the CRM wall system generally relies on the wall weight to
resist the lateral earth pressure Because of the high lateral earth pressure we anticipate
that a wide CRM wall base may be required to provide enough lateral resistance
Therefore the excavation required for wall construction will probably extend into the
existing traffic lanes Alternatively a concrete wall supported on drilled shafts may be
considered to reduce the amount of wall excavation required
Drilled Shaft Supported Concrete Wall
This alternative consists of a reinforced concrete wall supported by a drilled shaft
foundation system Utilizing a drilled shaft foundation system will reduce the footing width
of the wall and subsequently reduce the amount of excavation required for wall

construction A sketch of this wall alternative is shown on the Concrete Wall Supported on
Drilled Shafts Plate 3 In general the lateral load resistance of the drilled shafts is a
function of the stiffness of the surrounding ground the stiffness of the shaft allowable
deflections at the top of the shaft and induced moments in the shaft
We understand that the moment at the base of the wall is about 24 foot kips per
linear foot of wall and the vertical load is about 7 kips per linear foot of wall Based on the
above loading we recommend that drilled shafts with a nominal diameter of 2 feet be used
to provide lateral resistance and support for the concrete wall The drilled shafts should
have a minimum length of 15 feet and should be spaced about 6 feet on centers Lateral
load analyses using the LPILE program was conducted Based on our analyses the
maximum induced moment to the shaft is about 144 foot kips The maximum lateral
deflection at the top of the shaft is less than 1 inch
The performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures As a result of these potential
variations we recommend that a representative from Geolabs Inc be present to observe
the installation of the drilled shafts during construction In our opinion the following may
have a significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations
Based on our field exploration a stiff clay deposit was encountered below the
existing wall footing level Based on our past experience we believe that cobbles and
boulders may be present in the clay deposit Therefore the drilled shaft contractor will
need to have the appropriate equipment and tools to drill through these obstructions
where encountered
The load carrying capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the
shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes
Sidewalk
We anticipate that new sidewalk slabs on grade will be required for the project To
reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from shrinking and swelling of the
on site clay soils a minimum of 24 inches of select granular fill material should be
provided below the bottom of the new sidewalk slab on grades The subgrade soils below
the sidewalk should be kept moist prior to concrete placement
For these concrete slabs we recommend a minimum 4 inch thick slab thickness
Control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the sidewalks and at
right angle intersections
