SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a relatively thin cover of surface fills 
overlying coralline deposits at the project site. The surface fills generally were about 0.2 to 
6 feet thick and consisted of various materials, including sands, gravels, silts, and clays. 
The coralline deposits beneath the fills consisted of coralline detritus, coral formation, 
sandstone and siltstone, and coral-algal limestone and varied from medium dense to very 
dense with some loose pockets or zones and stiff to hard. In a few borings, stiff to hard 
alluvial materials were encountered within the coralline deposits. Groundwater was not 
encountered in the borings at the time of our field exploration.  
Based on the subsurface materials encountered at the project site and the geologic 
setting of the area, the project site may be classified from a seismic analysis standpoint 
as being a “Very Dense Soil and Soft Rock” site. Therefore, we believe the seismic design 
of the building structures may be based on a Site Class C soil profile in accordance with 
the International Building Code, 2012 Edition. Based on the subsurface conditions 
encountered, the phenomenon of soil liquefaction is not a design consideration for this 
project site.  
A shallow foundation system consisting of spread footings and/or continuous strip 
footings bearing on the recompacted on-site soils or new fills may be used for foundation 
support of the new structures. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be 
used for the design of shallow foundations. The bottom of footings should be embedded 
a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade. 
Cavities and/or voids are commonly encountered in the coralline deposits at the 
project site. To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from the 
collapse of cavities below foundations, we recommend implementing a program of 
proof-rolling the building structure subgrades prior to placing fill materials. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age (2.7 – 3.4 million years ago) and forms the bulk of the western 
one-third of the island. The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age (2.2 – 2.5 million years ago) and forms the majority of 
the eastern two-thirds of the island. 
The project site is situated on the Ewa Coastal Plain, which extends seaward off 
the southeastern flank of Waianae Volcano and the southwestern flank of Koolau 
Volcano. This plain was formed by the accumulation of a thick wedge of sediments and 
coral reef formations on the flanks of the two volcanoes.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous 
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous 
reef materials.

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code (2012 Edition), the project site may be 
subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed. 
The following sections provide discussions on the seismicity, soil profile type for seismic 
design, and the potential for liquefaction at the project site. 
2.3.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused largely by shifts 
in the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to 
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or 
during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island 
of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most of the 
earthquakes are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. 
However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause 
minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent 
of earthquakes) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of high seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the earthquake 
epicenter. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of 
Hawaii. 
It should be noted that some earthquakes in Hawaii are caused by the burden of the 
island masses overlying the lithosphere, which cause the lithosphere to sag beneath 
the immense weight. The isostatic adjustment of the Pacific lithosphere beneath the 
islands’ overburden causes some moderate to strong intensity earthquakes. The

addition, some areas on the Island of Oahu, including the Tantalus, Iwilei, and 
Tripler areas, reported more intense ground shaking, severe enough to have 
cracked reinforced concrete.

2.3.3 Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the International Building Code (2012 Edition), the project site should be 
evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction. Soil liquefaction is a condition where 
saturated cohesionless soils near the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of 
strength due to the build-up of excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic 
stress applications induced by earthquakes. When the loose saturated sand deposit 
is subjected to vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and 
decrease in volume, causing an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is unable 
to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective

water pressure build-up could equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing 
the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During 
liquefaction, the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and 
vertical movements, and if not confined, will result in significant deformations.

and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the liquefaction

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the onset of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be cleared 
and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other unsuitable 
materials should be removed and disposed of properly to reduce the potential for 
contaminating the excavated materials to be used as embankment fill materials. 
After clearing and grubbing, areas designated to receive fills should be scarified to a 
depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction (per 
ASTM D1557). Relative compaction, in this report, refers to the in-place dry density 
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content 
(percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. Compaction 
should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other types of 
acceptable compaction equipment. 
Soft and/or loose, weak, or yielding areas (or cavities) disclosed during site 
preparation operations should be over-excavated to expose firm ground, and the 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with fill materials (well-graded 3-inch minus 
materials) compacted to a minimum 90 percent relative compaction. Saturation and 
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrades due to inclement weather and poor 
drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
compacted fill materials. 
Abandoned asphaltic concrete roadways, concrete walkways, sewer and waterlines, 
and sewer manhole structures are present at the project site. To reduce the potential 
for differential settlement, the existing roadway pavement and concrete walkways 
should be demolished and removed. Based on the topographic survey plan provided, 
the abandoned sewer lines range from about 2.5 to 7 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  We anticipate that the abandoned waterlines will be relatively shallow at 
about 3 feet below the existing ground surface.  These abandoned sewer and 
waterlines should be removed or backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material 
(CLSM) or cement grout.

3.1.2 Excavation 
Excavation for this project generally would consist of excavations for the building 
foundations, building slab, infiltration basins, and other infrastructure. Some of the 
grading and infrastructure work likely will encounter the underlying coral 
formation/limestone. It is anticipated that these coralline deposits may be excavated 
readily with heavy excavation equipment, such as a large backhoe excavator. 
However, coral formations typically contain localized hard and crystallized zones that 
might require additional efforts in localized areas.  
Therefore, we anticipate that some difficult excavation conditions may arise in 
localized areas during construction. It is possible that the excavation for a portion of 
deep utility line trenches may require the use of hoerams or chipping. The contractor 
must exercise care to avoid over-ripping, which would disrupt the structure of the 
coral formation or sandstone, resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for 
improvements in the vicinity.

3.1.4 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Select granular 
fill materials should be placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to at least 
90 percent relative compaction. 
3.1.5 Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, cut and fill slopes planned at the site should be designed with a slope 
inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. The filling operation 
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers 
in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations. Fill slopes should be 
constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a 
well-compacted slope face. Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes, 
and slope painting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for 
erosion of the finished slopes.

Structure Foundations 
In general, we recommend supporting the proposed residential dwelling structures 
on a shallow foundation system bearing on the recompacted on-site soils and/or new 
compacted fills. Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions, we recommend using 
shallow isolated spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the proposed 
structure. In addition, the thickened-edges of slabs also may be used to support the 
structures. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) 
may be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted on-site 
soils and/or new compacted fills needed to achieve the finished grades. This bearing 
value is for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for 
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. In general, footings 
should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades. 
Bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent 
relative compaction. Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of footing 
excavations should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm materials. The 
over-excavation may be backfilled with the on-site soils compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction, or the bottom of footing may extend down to bear directly 
on the underlying competent materials. 
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 
45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench 
or they should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This 
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with 
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation 
settlement. 
We estimate footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings 
bearing on subgrades prepared as recommended herein to be less than 1 inch total with 
differential settlements on the order of 0.5 inches or less. 
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed between 
the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure acting 
against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of

3.3 
Proof-Rolling 
Based on our experience in the area, cavities and/or voids are commonly 
encountered in the coralline deposits in the project vicinity. To reduce the potential for 
loss of foundation support resulting from the collapse of cavities below foundations, we 
recommend implementing a program of proof-rolling the building structure subgrades 
prior to placing fill materials or constructing the final improvements, such as the floor 
slabs. Generally, the exposed subgrade should be proof-rolled utilizing a minimum 10-ton 
vibratory drum roller a minimum of eight passes to help detect the presence of 
near-surface cavities and/or loose zones. Proof-rolling operations should be conducted 
under the continuous observation of a Geolabs representative. 
If cavities and/or voids are encountered, soft and/or loose materials encountered 
at the bottom of cavities and/or voids should be over-excavated to expose the underlying 
firm materials. The over-excavation may be backfilled with the on-site soils compacted to 
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction, or the bottom of footing may extend down 
to bear directly on the underlying competent materials.

Utilities 
We envision new underground utilities, such as sewer, drainage, water, and

3.7 
Corrosion Potential and Chemical Attack to Concrete 
Minimum resistivity, pH, chloride content and sulfate content tests were previously 
performed to evaluate the corrosivity of the in-situ soils at the site. We performed the pH 
and minimum resistivity tests on selected soil samples in general accordance with 
ASTM G51 and ASTM G57, respectively. The chloride and sulfate content tests were 
performed by Test America Laboratories, Inc. in general accordance with the EPA 300.0 
test methods.  
Resistivity generally is recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. Generally, a lower 
resistivity results in a greater potential for corrosion of buried metallic objects. Conversely, 
a higher resistivity means the soil is less likely to contribute to corrosion of metallic 
objects. Based on the resistivity values of the in-situ soils encountered, the subsurface 
materials may be classified as very corrosive to extremely corrosive for buried metallic 
structures based on the Water System External Corrosion Control Standards by the 
Board of Water Supply, City and County of Honolulu dated 1991. A corrosion engineer 
should be consulted for detailed recommendations on corrosion protection of metallic 
substructures, if appropriate. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent in part on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in 
the soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of equal or more than 300 parts per million 
(ppm), sulfate content of more than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of less than 5.0 may be 
considered “extremely corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures based

Previous Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were previously explored by drilling and 
sampling 14 exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6 and 101 through 
108, extending down to depths of about 11.2 to 23.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
In addition, 26 shallow borings (P-101 through P-126) also were drilled to depths of about 
8 to 12 feet below the ground surface in support of infiltration testing for LID design. The 
borings were drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch solid-stem augers 
and PQ Coring tools. The borings were drilled at the approximate locations shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-40. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch O.D. 
standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch O.D. modified 
California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Please note that the efficiencies of the safety hammers used for this project were 
measured in May 2015 and the resulting energy transfer ratios are shown on the Logs of 
Borings. The soil consistencies and densities presented in the boring logs have been 
adjusted based on the energy transfer ratio, as appropriate.

the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were
