Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered fill material overlying
near surface volcanic ash soils underlain by basalt rock formation at greater depths The
near surface volcanic ash soil layer may be on the order of about 6 to 10 feet thick

recommend that foundations for the proposed building be extended through the volcanic
ash soils and bear on the underlying basalt rock formation

during construction to reduce the potential for loss of foundation support due to collapse of
cavities and or voids below the footings

2 1 Regional Geology
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These include the following Kohala which has been long extinct
Mauna Kea which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last
erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the northeastern flank of the Mauna Loa Shield
Volcano which has been built up by basaltic lava flows These lava flows are of the
Pleistocene Epoch and Recent Age and belong to the Ka u Volcanic Series The lava
flows were subsequently covered by volcanic ash deposits locally referred to as
Pahala Ash Pahala Ash is an air laid deposit of volcanic ash from the Hamakua
Volcanic Series of Mauna Loa Mountain during the Pleistocene Epoch Pahala Ash
generally has high in situ moisture contents and low in situ densities The ash is
characterized as having thixotropic properties i e it is susceptible to significant loss of
strength when remolded under equipment vehicular or seismic loads
The lava formation encountered at the site appears to be of pahoehoe flow
which is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal
structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe
lavas Cavities are formed when lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities blisters and pockets Lava tubes are
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like
structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow
cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent

3 1 Methods of Construction
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site
two alternative methods of construction were considered and are presented below
Alternative No 1
According to our field exploration results the project site is generally underlain by a
near surface layer of soft clayey silt volcanic ash Volcanic ash is generally a
thixotropic soil i e the soil loses significant strength temporarily when remolded
and or disturbed especially during a seismic event We believe that appreciable
settlement may occur when the soft volcanic ash is subjected to earthquake
loading resulting in cracks in the slabs on grade Due to the thixotropic nature of
the volcanic ash soils encountered at the project site we recommend that the
near surface ash soils encountered within the building footprint be entirely removed
down to the rock formation and replaced with well compacted structural fill We
recommend that the building footings be supported by well compacted structural fill
We believe that the slabs on grade may be constructed on the structural fills for the
proposed building construction
The advantage of this alternative construction method is that it ensures that both
building footings and slabs on grade be supported on firm bearing materials and
as a result minimize the potential for ground settlements The disadvantages of
this method are the relatively high costs and significant earthwork
Alternative No 2
This alternative construction method requires limited removal and replacement of
the on site soft volcanic ash materials We recommend that the slabs on grade

3 4 Foundation Probing
Based on our field exploration and previous experience in the area cavities
and or voids may be present in the basalt rock formation encountered at the project site
To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities
below a footing we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be
implemented during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probeholes be drilled at each
spread footing location one probehole for every 50 square feet of footing area and at
10 feet on center along wall foundations The probeholes should be at least 3 inches in
diameter and should extend at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of footing
elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing
operation additional probeholes should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating
the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probeholes and cavities
discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate
pressures We recommend that a low strength sand cement grout with a slump range
of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the grouting operations In lieu of the sand cement grout
fluid lean concrete such as controlled low strength material flowable fill may also be
used for the grouting operation
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site
we recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition we recommend that the probe drill be available on site until the
probing and grouting operations are complete It should be noted that a longer lag time
between the probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be required
in the construction schedule

they can better mask minor slab cracking

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
nine borings to depths ranging from about 5 5 to 20 5 feet below the existing ground
surface Two bulk samples designated as Bulk 1 and Bulk 2 of the near surface
materials were obtained to evaluate the pavement support characteristics The
approximate locations of the drilled borings and bulk samples are shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped
with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 9
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral formation The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

time The water heights and the rates of pumping were then measured to provide data
upon which to base calculation of the subsoil permeabilities
