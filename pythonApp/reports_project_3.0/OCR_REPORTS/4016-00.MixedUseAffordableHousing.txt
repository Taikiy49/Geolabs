REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age The project site is situated at the intersection of Pauoa and
Nuuanu Valleys as they open onto the southeastern Oahu Coastal Plain The coastal plain
was built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano which forms the eastern third of the
Island of Oahu The project site is located on an accumulation of alluvium derived from
erosion of the volcano interbedded with coral reefs and associated deposits
After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep
valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed
by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic
Series The Honolulu Volcano Series which began less than a million years ago
MacDonald and Abbott 1970 produced numerous cinder and tuff cones and basalt flows
which became inter layered with the coastal plain deposits The nearby Punchbowl Hill
Puowaina is a tuff cone near the center of Honolulu built against the end of a spur of the
Koolau Range The tuff is mostly brown palagonitized vitric ash and lapilli with scattered
fragments of coral limestone and Koolau basalt
Most of the coastal plain developed during the Pleistocene Epoch when the sea level
experienced fluctuations related to the glacial stages As the glaciers grew and advanced
less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present
stands of the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became
available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level The processes of
erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea level fluctuations When
the sea level was low the erosional base level was correspondingly lower and valleys were
carved to depths below the present sea level When the sea level was high the erosional
base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher elevations
GEOLABS INC
W O 4016 00 Page 8
The advances and retreats of the sea level produced reef deposits at varying levels
including the one immediately underlying the site which was created during the
Waimanalo Stand of the sea first described by Stearns and Vaksvik 1935 Based on our
review of the geologic map and field exploration the Block J Development site lies at the
interface between a buried erosion basin to the west and the adjacent cinder materials to the
east A surficial layer of man made fill was placed over these deposits for the development
of the parking lot at the site

Seismic Soil Coefficient
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration and the
Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts we believe that the project site may be
classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as being a stiff soil site The soil profile
encountered at the site may be classified as a Type D Sp soil profile in accordance with the
1997 Edition of the Uniform Building Code
Mat Foundation
As previously indicated we anticipate that the basement excavation will likely expose
the stiff to very stiff alluvial soil deposits and or dense coral detritus deposits at the
GEOLABS INC
W O 4016 00 Page 12
basement subgrade level at about Elevation 2 to 9 feet MSL The underlying
foundation materials below the basement subgrade elevation will comprise generally of the
stiff to very stiff alluvial soils encountered in our borings Based on these subsurface
conditions we recommend that a mat foundation be used to support the basement base
structure complex and the two residential high rise tower structures An allowable bearing
pressure of up to 8 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for design of the mat
foundation bearing on the stiff alluvial soil deposits and or dense coral detritus deposits This
bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by as much as 50 percent
when considering short term transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces The allowable bearing pressure provided above is a net value therefore the weight
of the mat foundation may be discounted
Based on the results of our in situ pressuremeter tests conducted and our foundation
analyses a modulus of subgrade reaction of 75 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection
pci may be used in the design of the mat foundation for long term loading conditions based
on dead loads or gravity loads For short term loading conditions such as those caused by
wind or seismic forces in addition to the dead and live loads a modulus of subgrade reaction
of 200 pci may be used in the mat foundation design The lower modulus of subgrade
reaction for the long term loading conditions is due to the anticipated differential foundation
settlements between adjacent columns
As mentioned earlier we anticipate that the mat foundation subgrade will likely consist
of a combination of the stiff alluvial soils silts and clays and the dense coral detritus deposits
sands and gravel In order to provide the mat foundation with more uniform support and
to aid in detecting large areas with soft subgrade conditions we recommend that the
foundation subgrade be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton drum roller a minimum of six
passes The drum roller should be operated at a speed not faster than 300 feet per
minute The foundation proof rolling operations should be performed in the full time
presence of a representative from Geolabs Yielding areas and or soft areas disclosed
during the proof rolling operations should be backfilled with well compacted fill materials

Lateral loads imposed on the building foundation may be resisted by frictional

Uplift Load Resistance
Based on our field exploration we encountered groundwater at varying depths
between about Elevation 1 9 and 5 5 feet MSL In general we believe that the static
groundwater level at the site should vary between about Elevation 0 and 3 feet MSL based
on the groundwater measurements taken from Boring Nos 2 and 4 through 11 It appears
that the groundwater levels measured in Boring and 12 fluctuated more than
normally anticipated Based on our experience in the area we believe that the groundwater
levels measured at elevations higher than 3 feet do not represent the static groundwater
level and may be considered to be transient groundwater conditions Therefore we believe
that a static groundwater level of 3 feet MSL may be used in the design of the basement
mat foundation and walls for hydrostatic uplift purposes
Resistance to uplift loads imposed on the mat foundation may be mobilized by the
dead weight of the structure and the mat foundation In addition the weight of soils placed
above the mat foundation system may also be used to resist uplift loads Contribution of
dead weight from compacted soils above the mat foundation may be estimated using a unit
weight of 110 pounds per cubic foot pcf

anchors For schematic design purposes we believe that low to moderate capacity tie down

5 to provide the stress strain relationship of the in situ soils at the site for mat foundation

Slabs On Grade
We envision that some of the floor slabs for this project such as the access ramps
and some other slabs may consist of slabs on grades In general we envision that the
fill materials supporting the new slabs on grades will consist of the on site granular
materials In addition we anticipate that the slabs on grade will be subjected to vehicular

the slabs subjected to the dynamic loads due to the vehicles The aggregate subbase

compaction For design of structural floor slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pci
may be used for the compacted aggregate subbase We recommend that the concrete

made for proper load transfer across the slab joints that will be subjected to vehicular
traffic
Slab subgrades below the aggregate subbase should be scarified to a depth of
about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to
at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is
the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
For slabs on grade with moisture sensitive surfacing we recommend that the

Excavation Support
The basement construction for the Block J Development will involve a large
excavation in a well developed urban area In addition the basement excavation will
abut several 2 to 3 story building structures and an electrical transformer facility
Therefore the planned excavations should be adequately shored to reduce the
potential for damage to the surrounding buildings utility lines and streets caused by
earth movements toward the basement excavation Excavated materials should not
be stockpiled adjacent to the planned excavation within a horizontal distance equal
to the depth of the excavation to reduce the potential for ground movements The
excavation support and shoring system used is the responsibility of the contractor and
must comply with all applicable Federal State and local safety requirements
Based on the stiff and dense subsurface conditions encountered at the project site
we believe that use of steel sheet pile shoring will likely encounter difficulties due to
the potential for penetration refusal during the sheet pile installation operations
In lieu of steel sheet piling we believe that a soldier pile and lagging with tiebacks
shoring system or soil nail earth retention system may be used for shoring support of
the proposed basement excavation

Tied Back Shoring System
For temporary shoring exceeding about 12 feet in height we recommend that a
restrained shoring system consisting of soldier pile and lagging with temporary
tiebacks be considered for shoring purposes A tied back wall is a structural system
that uses an anchor embedded in the ground to secure a tendon which applies a
lateral force on the retaining structure Lateral load resistance can be mobilized
through the post tensioned tiebacks and through the use of passive pressure acting
on the foundation members extending below the bottom of the basement excavation
Tiebacks are advantageous since they are external to the excavation and permanent
construction may proceed without hindrance from internal bracing Tiebacks have
been employed primarily for temporary excavations generally utilizing soldier piles
with lagging for soil retention or mixed in place soil wall Since tiebacks will encroach
into and below the adjacent properties permission for encroachment from the owners
of the adjacent properties should be obtained prior to the tieback design installation
For a restrained shoring system with braced or tied back conditions the apparent
pressure diagram presented on Plate 5 may be used for preliminary design purposes
Appropriate loads due to surcharge building foundations equipment traffic or
sloping ground conditions adjacent to the excavations should be added to the
recommended earth pressures for design purposes An appropriate safety factor
should be included by the shoring designer
Soil Nail Earth Retention System
As an alternative to the tied back wall system a soil nail earth retention system may
be considered for shoring of the basement excavation A soil nail earth retention
system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars grouted into drilled holes to
GEOLABS INC
W O 4016 00 Page 22
stabilize an excavation or slope A shotcrete facing is generally applied to the face
of the excavation to provide the appearance of a conventional concrete retaining wall
The reinforcing bars provide both additional tensile and shear strengths to the soil
and reinforce the sidewall of the excavation The reinforced soil mass behaves in a
manner similar to a gravity retaining wall except that it lacks a footing
If a soil nail earth retention system is planned for shoring the basement excavation
special attention should be given to the groundwater levels encountered at the site
The presence of groundwater at the site can significantly impact the design and
installation of the soil nail earth retention system In addition proper drainage of the
temporary soil nail earth retention system should also be incorporated into the design
details of this system
Underpinning of Existing Structures
The proposed basement excavation is located within about 8 feet of the existing
2 to 3 story building structures located at the eastern portion of the site Based on the
spatial relationship between the basement excavation and the existing structures we
recommend that the existing building structures on the eastern portion of the site be
underpinned to transfer their foundation loads to the soils below the basement
excavation Underpinning of the existing structures may be accomplished using
drilled underpinning piers or bracket piles extending below the lowest basement
elevation The underpinning piers or bracket piles should be designed to support

embedded in the stiff alluvial soils Geolabs should be consulted for the design of the

Percolation Tests
Percolation tests were performed in Boring and 12 to evaluate the
permeability of the subsurface soils at the site to evaluate the dewatering
characteristics The percolation tests were conducted using the constant head and
falling head test methods After the borings were drilled to the desired depth for
percolation testing water was injected into the boring The water was maintained at
a certain level and the water pumping rate was measured for the constant head test
After the constant head test was completed the water source was shut off and the
drop in the water level within the boring was measured for the falling head test The
tests indicated that the subsurface soils have a coefficient of permeability k in the
range of about 0 0005 to 0 002 centimeters per second cm s Results of the
percolation tests are presented in Appendix D

Dewatering Considerations
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents should
be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater
table and or to exclude water from the excavations to allow construction of the
proposed structures under safe and dry conditions These works or systems may
include but are not limited to well point pumping and sump pumping at the edges
of the basement excavation We suggest that the following three basic criteria be
considered in selection of a suitable method of dewatering
1 The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or damage to
existing buildings roads and environment
2 The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide safe and
dry working conditions in the excavation
3 The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modifications to
accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering
It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained by the
contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His her qualified representative who should be
continuously present on site during dewatering activities will have the best
opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and to
implement as soon as possible necessary precautionary or remedial measures
including but not limited to stopping or slowing down the dewatering operations
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils may
be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations should be
carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the excavation bottoms

out soil fines pumping clear water only and should be coordinated with the shoring

APPENDIX A

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling twelve
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 12 extending to depths ranging from about
58 5 to 101 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the
borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled with a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with hollow stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineers who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown
as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the

Seven sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 ASTM D 1140 were performed on
selected samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid
in soil classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on
Plates B 1 and B 2

liquid limit LL and plastic limit PL as an aid in classifying the soils and interpreting the
test results The Atterberg Limits tests were performed in accordance with ASTM Test

appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on
Plate B 3
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the shear strength parameters Results of the two direct shear tests are presented
on Plates B 4 and B 5
Two unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression TXUU tests were performed
on selected in situ soils to evaluate the undrained shear strengths in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 2850 The approximate in situ effective overburden pressure
was used as the applied confining pressure for the relatively undisturbed samples The
test results and the stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 6 and B 7

compressibility characteristics of the underlying foundation soils These tests were
performed in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 2435 and the test results are
plotted on Plates B 8 and B 9

APPENDIX C
Pressuremeter Testing
The pressuremeter test basically consists of placing an inflatable cylindrical probe in
a predrilled hole and expanding the probe while measuring the changes in volume and
pressure in the probe The purpose of pressuremeter tests is to characterize the
stress strain relationship of the in situ soil
The pressuremeter tests were performed in general conformance with Procedure A
of ASTM Test Designation D 4719 87 An NX size single cell pressuremeter manufactured
by Roctest was used in the tests The pressuremeter probe has a length to diameter ratio
of above 6 0 and the volume of the uninflated probe V is 75 cubic inches in The
borehole was prepared by driving a 3 inch OD split barrel sampler with a 140 pound hammer
free falling 24 inches Approximately equal pressure steps were used to inflate the
pressuremeter probe in such a way that approximately 9 to 27 increments were obtained for
the test For every pressure step the probe volume readings were taken at 30 and
60 seconds after the pressure increment was applied The volume difference between the
30 second and 60 second readings is referred to as the creep volume V
The test results were corrected for pressure loss due to the stiffness of the probe
membrane and hydraulic head Figure 1 depicts a typical pressuremeter test curve The test
curve is based on the 60 second voiume readings The creep curve is a plot of V versus
the pressure applied to the borehole wall P The P reading is believed to be associated with
the total in situ lateral stress The creep pressure P is related to the in situ yield stress
Above P plastic deformations become prevalent The test curve typically shows an almost
straight line section within the range of low volume increase In this range a constant soil
deformation modulus called pressuremeter modulus E can be evaluated as follows
