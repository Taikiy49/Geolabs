REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be 
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during 
which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity 
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These 
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano, and its 
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio-eustatic 
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the 
Honolulu Coastal Plain. The subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by 
fossil Pleistocene Age coral/algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high 
stands of the sea that are intercalated with terrigenous sediments. The base of the 
stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt. 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred 
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. These 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower 
relative to the present sea level on the Island of Oahu. About 15,000 years ago, a 
relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred. During that sea level rise, the valleys in the 
project area were drowned. In the last 10,000 years or so, the sea level has adjusted to

In the early part of this century, the Waikiki area consisted of low, marshy

alluvial debris, which apparently smothered the coral reef development. Land 
development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought the 
Waikiki area to its present form. It should be noted that much of the resulting fill 
materials placed are of poor quality in terms of supporting heavy structural loads. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling one boring, designated as 
Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 102 feet below the existing ground surface. 
The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The project site is generally underlain by surface fill materials, beach sand and 
lagoonal deposits over coralline deposits. In general, surface fills consisting of medium 
dense to dense silty and sandy soils extended down to about 2 feet below the ground 
surface. The surface fill layer is underlain by loose beach sands and soft lagoonal 
deposits. The beach sands generally extended to about 25 feet below the existing 
ground surface, while the lagoonal deposits extended to about 40 feet deep. It should 
be noted that the beach sands and lagoonal deposits encountered at the project site are 
compressible when subjected to structural loads. 
Below the compressible lagoonal deposits, coralline materials, consisting of 
interbedded soft to medium hard coral formation and medium dense to dense sandy 
and gravelly coralline detritus, were generally encountered and extended to the 
maximum depth explored of about 102 feet below the ground surface. It should be 
noted that loose silty sand and silty gravel pockets were encountered in the coralline 
detritus. 
We encountered groundwater at a depth of about 5.5 feet below the existing 
pavement surface, corresponding to about Elevation +1.1 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). 
Due to the proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean, water levels are expected 
to vary with tidal fluctuations. It should be noted that water levels also may vary with 
seasonal precipitation, storm surge conditions, groundwater withdrawal, and other 
factors.

SEISMIC DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition) (IBC 2006), the project site 
would be subjected to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to be 
addressed. Our field exploration generally encountered a surface fill over loose beach 
sand and soft lagoonal deposits extending to depths of about 40 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Below the highly compressible lagoonal deposits, we generally 
encountered coralline reef formation with interbedded hard coral and coralline detritus 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 102 feet below the ground surface. 
Based on our geotechnical analysis, the loose beach sand (about 23 feet thick) 
present at the site appear to have a factor of safety of less than 1.0 with regard to potential 
soil liquefaction. Therefore, we believe the project site could be subjected to appreciable 
ground settlements (on the order of up to about 12 inches) in the event of liquefaction 
during a moderate earthquake (M6+). However, we believe that lateral spreading 
associated with liquefaction would not be a significant design consideration based on the 
relatively flat site topography. 
Because the project site is underlain by liquefiable soils, the project site will need to 
be classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as being Site Class F based on 
Table 1613.5.2 of IBC 2006. Therefore, the site coefficients (Fa and Fv) will need to be 
determined based on a site-specific response spectrum for seismic design of the project. 
In accordance with Chapter 20, Site Classification Procedure for Seismic Design 
contained in ASCE 7-10 (Minimum Design Loads for Buildings and Other Structures), a 
site-specific elastic response spectrum (site-specific design ground motion) was 
developed using the Shake 2000 computer program for the one-dimensional wave 
propagation analysis.

MICROPILE FOUNDATION RECOMMENDATIONS 
A geophysical surveying exploration was conducted using ground penetration 
radar (GPR) and high frequency reflection testing by Global Geophysics. The 
geophysical survey was performed along grade beams between existing columns at the 
basement level and street level fronting Kalakaua Avenue. The results of the GPR and 
the high frequency reflection testing suggested that the existing building columns may 
be supported by 12-inch square concrete piles with 6 feet square pile caps. However, 
the length of the existing pile foundation could not be determined. Details of the 
geophysical survey exploration are contained in the report entitled “Report for 
Geophysical Surveys from Foundation Investigation at Kowa Waikiki Beachside Hotel, 
Honolulu, Hawaii” dated October 24, 2014 by Global Geophysics. A copy of the report is 
included in Appendix D for ease of reference. 
Due to the lack of as-built information and uncertainty of the existing pile 
foundation capacity, we understand that additional structural loads from the renovation 
will be supported by a new foundation system, to avoid surcharging the existing pile 
foundation. Details of the additional structural loads were not available at the time of this 
report prepared. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the boring and tight access 
conditions with limited headroom available, we recommend that a deep foundation 
system consisting of cased micropiles be used for support of additional structural loads.  
We recommend that the capacity of the micropile foundation be derived primarily from 
skin friction between the micropile shaft and the coral/limestone and coralline materials 
encountered in the boring. 
A micropile consists of a small diameter (usually less than 12 inches) drilled and 
grouted pile with steel reinforcing. The micropile foundation typically is constructed by 
drilling a borehole, placing reinforcing steel in the hole, and grouting the borehole. 
Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed readily in access restrictive 
environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions. In addition, installation

Based on the availability of local material inventory, we envision that a micropile 
system with a minimum grout bulb diameter of 5.5 inches may be used.  We 
recommend designing each micropile based on an allowable compressive load capacity 
of 75 kips.  The allowable compressive load capacity for the micropiles is for dead-plus-
live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as wind or 
seismic forces.  Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we 
recommend that the micropiles extend through the loose beach sands and soft lagoonal 
deposits and derive load bearing support from the medium dense to dense 
coral/limestone and coralline materials encountered at greater depths, as a bonded 
zone. 
To achieve the allowable compressive load capacity with a factor of safety of 2.0, 
we estimate that each micropile would need to extend to a depth of about 60 feet below 
bottom of pile cap.  The 60-foot depth is based on a 20-foot deep load bearing grout 
bulb (bonded zone) developed within the coral/limestone and coralline materials with a 
40-foot grout zone (neglected) within the beach sands and lagoonal deposits. 
To facilitate the micropile drilling and ensure the quality of the grouting, it is 
critical to advance the steel casing to the bottom of the micropile, defined as “Casing 
Method,” during the drilling operation. The steel casing would be withdrawn during the 
grouting operation while a minimum of 5 feet grout head is maintained above the bottom 
of the casing at all times.

In general, the micropiles should have a minimum center-to-center spacing of 
about 24 inches to avoid reduction in vertical load capacity due to group action and to 
facilitate drilling of the micropiles. 
Downdrag Loads 
In the event of seismically induced liquefaction, settlement of the compressible 
deposits will occur at the project site. When the loose soil deposits consolidate, or 
move downward relative to the micropiles, the consolidation or downward 
movement will create negative skin friction forces, generally known as downdrag 
forces, acting on the micropile shafts. The magnitude of the downdrag force 
depends on the thickness of the liquefiable layer, relative density of the potentially 
liquefiable deposits, and the micropile size. 
The allowable compressive load capacity of the micropiles has been reduced by 
the expected downdrag loads in the micropile foundation analyses. Based on our 
foundation analyses, we estimate the downdrag load imposed on the micropiles 
due to settlement of the beach sands may be on the order of about 6 kips for the 
5.5-inch diameter micropiles. 
In addition, placement of fill materials required to achieve design grades also 
may induce settlement of loose compressible deposits. The ground settlement 
would result in downdrag forces acting on the micropile shaft supporting the new 
structures. If additional fill is required to achieve design grades, the micropile 
recommendations presented herein should be re-evaluated.

Lateral Load Resistance 
It should be noted that the micropile foundation system provides low lateral load 
resistance due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile.  Therefore, the 
lateral load resistance contributed by the vertical micropiles should be neglected.  
Lateral loads imposed on the new structure foundations may be resisted primarily 
by passive earth pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and 
grade beams and by shear acting along the sides of the pile caps and grade 
beams. 
Passive pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and grade 
beams may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 250 pounds per 
square foot per foot of depth (pcf) above the groundwater level and 150 pcf below 
the groundwater level.  Additional resistance to lateral loads may be provided by 
shear resistance between the sides of the pile caps and grade beams and the 
adjacent soils.  A side shear resistance of up to 150 pounds per square feet (psf) 
may be used for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces only. 
Micropile Foundation Settlement 
Settlements of the micropile foundations will result primarily from elastic 
compression of the pile member and subgrade response.  We estimate the total 
settlement of the micropile foundations to be less than 0.5 inches.  We believe that 
these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied. 
Micropile Load Test Program 
It should be noted that the micropile load capacity is highly dependent on the 
drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the contractor to 
install the micropile.  Therefore, the micropile load capacity may vary 
considerably between different contractors and micropile foundation systems.  In 
order to determine whether the contractor’s methods of micropile installation are 
adequate and to determine the ultimate load capacity, we recommend performing 
one pre-production load test. In general, the purpose of the pre-production load 
test on a sacrificial micropile is to fulfill the following objectives: 
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed 
by the contractor to install the micropiles to the depths required

In general, the pre-production load test should be performed in accordance with 
ASTM D1143. Based on experience, we believe the load test should be conducted 
no earlier than 7 days after completion of the micropile installation to allow the grout 
adequate time to cure. Two additional micropiles may be used for reaction during 
the compressive load testing of the pre-production load test micropile. The reaction 
micropiles may be installed to depths as deep as the load test micropile to provide 
adequate reaction in uplift (to be determined by the contractor). 
The load test micropile should be loaded gradually to at least 200 percent of the 
allowable design load in compression. We recommend holding the maximum test 
load (200 percent of the design load) for a minimum of one to two hours depending 
on the recorded movements of the load test micropile. After successfully holding 
200 percent of the design load, we recommend testing the pre-production micropile 
to failure, to examine the ultimate micropile load capacity. The pre-production load 
test is an integral part of the design of the micropile foundation system. Therefore, 
we recommend a Geolabs representative observe the pre-production load test. 
We recommend that the pre-production load test micropile should consists of 
20-foot bonded zone (grout bulb) and 40-foot unbonded zone (non-grout) with steel 
casing. Upon completion of the load testing, the steel casing may be removed after 
the unbonded zone is fully grouted. 
In addition to the pre-production load test, we also recommend performing pullout 
tests (proof tests) on selected micropiles during construction to confirm the load 
carrying capacity of the installed micropiles. Based on the micropile drilling and 
grouting records, Geolabs should select test micropiles after completion of the 
production micropile installation. We recommend testing a minimum 10 percent of 
the total number of micropiles or minimum of two micropiles, whichever is greater, 
for pullout. The pullout tests should consist of subjecting the micropile to at least 
150 percent of the design load. The micropile should be loaded in 12.5% design 
load increments, and each load should be held for at least 5 minutes. The 
maximum test load should be held for a minimum of 10 or 60 minutes. Pullout 
testing on selected micropiles is an integral part of the design of the micropile 
foundation system. Therefore, we recommend conducting the pullout tests under 
the observation of a Geolabs representative.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling one borehole, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 
102 feet below the existing pavement surface.  The approximate boring location is 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  The boring was drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary wash drilling/coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the boring by visual and 
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  Soils, where encountered, were classified in general 
conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.1.  Symbols and abbreviations used in the boring logs are also 
described on the Soil Log Legend.  Deviations made to the soil classification in 
accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil Classification Log Key, 
Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the 
Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In addition, we obtained 
some samples from the drilled boring in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts needed to 
drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation.  Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation.  The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description.  Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3.  The Rock Description System is based on the 
publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in 
Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate.  Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.  
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses.  RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks.  If 2.5 feet of sound material 
is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%.  Generally, the following is used to describe 
the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of 
Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

Seismic Shear Wave Velocity Test 
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling of the subsurface materials at the project 
site was performed using Seismic Cone Penetration Testing (SCPT) equipment. The 
purpose of the seismic shear wave velocity profiling of the subsurface materials was to 
more closely analyze the seismic design considerations for the project. Shear wave 
velocity testing was performed at a boring location, designated as B-1 on the Site Plan 
(Plate 2). The seismic shear wave velocity profiling was performed at various depths 
extending to a maximum depth of about 103 feet below the pavement surface. 
In order to conduct the shear wave velocity test in the boring, the test boring was 
advanced utilizing rotary coring methods to the maximum depth of the boring. A log of 
the materials encountered in the boring is presented on the Logs of Boring in 
Appendix A. After the boring was advanced to the maximum depth of the borehole, the 
bored hole was backfilled with 0.25-inch diameter coated bentonite pellets. The 
temporary casing from the coring operations was used as a tremie pipe to place the 
bentonite pellets starting from the bottom and advancing upward. When the bentonite 
pellets are in contact with the groundwater in the borehole, the pellets start to hydrate 
slowly. As the bentonite pellets hydrate, they swell and soften. The probes were then 
pushed through the softened bentonite extending to a depth of about 102 feet below the 
existing pavement surface. 
The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10-ton 
steel electronic subtraction cone with an apex angle of 60 degrees and a projected 
surface area of 1.55 square inches (10 square centimeters) into the ground at a rate of 
approximately 20 mm/second (+5 mm/second). The depth of the cone penetration was 
recorded at about 2-inch (50-mm) intervals using an electronic depth sensor attached to 
the cone rod. The values of the tip resistance, friction ratio (sleeve friction over tip 
resistance), and pore pressure measurements in this instance are irrelevant. 
The cone carries a uniaxial horizontal accelerometer geophone to detect the 
arrival of a shear wave generated and propagated from the ground surface. The seismic 
measurements were made when the SCPT had stopped and a shear wave was sent into 
the subsurface. A shear wave was generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank 
with a switched hammer. The propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the 
cone was measured at each discrete depth interval. The vector difference of these depths 
divided by the time difference for the shear wave to arrive at the various depths gave 
provided the average shear wave velocity over the depth interval. 
The shear wave velocities measured in the boring are presented on Plate C-1. 
The weighted average shear wave velocity calculated for the top 100 feet of the soil 
profile is 982 feet per second. The weighted average shear wave velocity was 
calculated based on the average shear wave velocity method described in 
Section 1613.5.5 of the International Building Code (2006 Edition).

This letter report presents the results of the geophysical survey performed by Global 
Geophysics on October 9, 2014 at Kowa Waikiki Beachside Hotel, Honolulu, Hawaii. 
The objectives of the studies are to locate potential voids. 
METHODOLOGY, INSTRUMENTATION AND FIELD PROCEDURES 
Ground penetrating radar (GPR) and high frequency sonic reflection were used for this 
project. GPR was used to detect the footing dimensions above watertable. The sonic 
reflection was used to determine the dimension of pile under the footing. 
Ground Penetrating Radar 
The GPR method uses electromagnetic pulses, emitted at regular intervals by an antenna 
to map subsurface features.  The electromagnetic pulses are reflected where changes in 
electrical properties of materials occur such as changes in lithology or where 
underground utilities are present. The reflected electromagnetic energy is received by an 
antenna, converted into an electrical signal, and recorded on the GPR unit.  The data is 
recorded and viewed in real time on a graphical display that depicts a continuous profile 
or cross-section image of the subsurface directly beneath the path of the antenna. 
The depth of penetration of the GPR signal varies according to antenna frequency and the 
conductivity of the subsurface material.  The depth of subsurface penetration with GPR 
decreases with an increase in the frequency of the antenna and an increase in soil 
conductivity.  Low frequency antennas (50 to 500 MHz) provide the best compromise 
between obtaining good subsurface penetration and resolution.

Seismic reflection  
A high resolution seismic survey is to provide an image of the subsurface that is as 
detailed as possible, within the limits imposed by the nature of acoustic wave propagation 
in the earth. The 2D seismic method entails propagation of the acoustic waves through 
the earth and reflects back to the receivers. The survey procedure entails collection of a 
seismogram.
