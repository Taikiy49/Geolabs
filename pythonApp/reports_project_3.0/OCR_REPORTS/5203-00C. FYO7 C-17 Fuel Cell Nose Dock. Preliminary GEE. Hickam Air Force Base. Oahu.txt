exploration performed for the FY07 C 17 Fuel Cell Nose Dock project located in Hickam
Air Force Base on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the

engineering analyses These preliminary recommendations are intended for planning
and the preliminary design of hangar foundations hangar slabs on grade site grading
and vehicle pavements only The findings and preliminary recommendations presented

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during
which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These
series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The underlying geologic site conditions are the result of a complex series of
depositional and erosional events due to glacio eustatic sea level changes and
post erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch The Koolau Volcanic
Shield was built by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows during the early Pleistocene Epoch
Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano alluvial soil materials
were deposited in an estuarine and deltaic environment in the Pearl Harbor area The
sea level fluctuated several times receding about 90 meters below and rising about
30 meters above the present sea level
During the Recent Epoch as the sea level rose to its current stand the area was
covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of soft and loose lagoonal marine
deposits Land development and reclamation projects in the past 40 to 60 years have
added man made fills of both documented and undocumented placement bringing the
site to its present configuration

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes occurring throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma which comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most of the earthquakes are so small that they may only be detected by sensitive
instruments However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a
few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes directly associated with the movement of magma are
concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island
of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent of
earthquakes are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and degree
of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii
The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than
Magnitude 5 M5 However earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of
Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have
occurred in the County of Maui The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands

Site Classification
Our field exploration generally encountered fill materials about 1 to 2 meters thick
The fill materials were underlain by coral and volcanic tuff formations extending to
the maximum depths explored of about 6 6 meters below the existing ground
surface Based on our experience in the area of the project site we believe that the
subsurface conditions encountered in our borings may be extrapolated to depths of
about 30 meters below the ground surface Based on the average penetration
resistance N values of the subsurface materials encountered we believe that the
project site may be classified as Class D in accordance with Table of the
IBC 2000 The Class D site classification represents deposits of soils with a
weighted average Standard Penetration Test SPT blow count between 15 and
50 blows per 300 mm for the soil profile within the upper 30 meters of the ground

3 1 Building Foundations
Based on the competent subsurface conditions encountered we believe that the
hangar foundations may be supported on a shallow foundation system consisting of
spread and or continuous footings
For spread and or continuous footings bearing on the engineered fill materials
an allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for design of

It should be noted that groundwater may be encountered during construction of

3 2 Foundation Probing
Our experience at the project vicinity suggests that cavities and or voids in the
underlying coral formations are commonly encountered If the hangar foundations are
designed to bear on the fill material we believe that the potential for loss of foundation
support resulting from collapse of the cavities below the foundations is relatively small If
it is desired to further reduce the potential for loss of foundation support then a program
of cavity probing and grouting at the foundation locations may be implemented during
construction
However if the foundations are designed to bear on the coral formation then the
potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities below the
foundations would be increased Therefore a program of cavity probing and grouting at
the foundation locations should be implemented during construction to reduce the
potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities and or
sinkholes below foundations

spread footing location one probe hole for every 5 square meters of footing area and
at 3 meters on center along wall foundations The probe holes should be at least 75 mm

3 3 Slabs On Grade
We understand that the hangar floors will consist of concrete slabs on grade For
slabs on grade not subjected to equipment operation and vehicular traffic we
recommend that a cushion fill layer consisting of 100 mm of open graded gravel

coverings by moisture infiltration
For concrete slabs on grade subjected to equipment vibration or vehicular forklift

aggregate subbase for uniform support of the slabs in lieu of the 100 mm of
open graded gravel For design of structural floor slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction

The on site fill materials generated from the cut areas may be reused as a source

3 8 Drainage
The finished grades outside the proposed structures should be sloped to shed
water away from foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding
Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting
of the concrete to reduce water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be
provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface runoff away
from slabs and foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings extending to depths ranging from about 6 1 to 6 6 meters below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings
were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers
and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 75 mm OD Modified California sampler with a 63 5 kilogram hammer falling 760 mm
In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
50 mm OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 300 mm of an 450 mm drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of the coral and volcanic tuff formations encountered at the site
were obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if
1 220 millimeters of material is recovered from a 1 525 millimeter core run the recovery
would be 80 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 100 millimeter in length without any discontinuities discounting
any drilling induced fractures or breaks If 760 millimeters of sound material is
recovered from a 1 525 millimeter core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to
