REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the majority of the western one third of the island
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Ice Age in age and forms the bulk of the eastern two thirds of the island After a long
period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau
Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff
cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano and on the coastal
plain of Southern Oahu Therefore the geomorphology and subsurface conditions in the
area are directly related to the glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during the
Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The coastal
plain was built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano which forms the eastern
two thirds of the Island of Oahu The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of
alluvium derived from erosion of the volcano interbedded with coral reefs and associated
deposits
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation Most of the coastal plains were developed during the
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly As the glaciers grew and
advanced less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below
the present stands of the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water
became available such that the sea levels rose to above its present level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
The subsurface conditions in the Ala Moana Kaka ako area are dominated by fossil
Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits which are intercalated with
terrigenous sediments laid during the higher stands of the sea Therefore the project site
is generally underlain by deposits consisting of calcareous sediments and lagoonal
deposits A surface layer of man made fill was placed over these deposits for the
development of the Ala Moana Kaka ako area within the last century

slightly under consolidated and highly compressible soft soil approximately 4 to 7 feet
thick may be the primary reason for pavement depression settlement We recommend

Possible Causes of Pavement Distress
Our laboratory testing indicated that soft silty and clayey soil encountered in Area 1
and Area 2 is slightly under consolidated and highly compressible Over many years of
pavement reconstruction the asphaltic concrete pavement thickness has increased from
1 2 inches to 18 inches in some areas In addition the Arena renovation has introduced
heavy traffic loading on the existing parking lot The original pavement design may not
have considered the heavy traffic loading
We observed wet subgrade conditions in the borings in Area 2 Excessive water
would likely soften and reduce the strength of the subgrade soils increasing the
susceptibility to bearing failure
We understand that there was speculation of an underground stream existing
below portions of the Area 2 Flowing water could cause loss of the finer material resulting
in ground settlements However our field exploration did not encounter evidence of the
underground stream
We believe that highly compressible soft soil may be the primary reason for
pavement depression settlement in Area 1 and Area 2 Heavy traffic loading and
excessive water also contribute to the pavement depression and bearing failure
The root system of large trees can cause uplifting of the pavement This appears to
be the case in Area 3 where the pavement and concrete curb has raised and buckled due
to the tree roots

The affected area is approximately 15 feet by 50 feet in plan in the longitudinal
direction along the driveway between the Concert Hall and Exhibition Hall as
shown on the Site Plate 3 1 The width of the driveway is about 30 to 35 feet The
depression did not extend beyond the paved driveway
We recommend removal and replacement of soft compressible soils with structural
fill to reduce the potential for future settlement The structural fill should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum 90 percent relative compaction ASTM D 1557 until a depth of 3 feet
below the ground surface Compaction requirements for the upper 3 feet of backfill
should be increased to 95 percent relative compaction
The limits of the excavation should extend beyond the depression into firm stiff
materials For the purpose of preparing cost estimates and bidding the limits of the
excavation may be quantified by including the entire driveway area and to
approximately 7 feet below the existing ground surface as shown on Site Plan
Plate 3 1
As previously mentioned groundwater was encountered at a depth of 6 5 feet
below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration Therefore
groundwater would likely be encountered near bottom of the excavation
Free draining granular materials ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for
submerged backfill to a minimum of 6 inches above the groundwater The free
draining granular material should be wrapped in a non woven filter fabric such as
Mirafi 180N or equivalent

diagonally approximately 65 feet by 90 feet in plan as shown on the Site Plan

the Arena and roadway sides

structural fill to reduce the potential for future settlement Due to the speculated
underground stream we recommend using a non woven filter fabric to wrap the
