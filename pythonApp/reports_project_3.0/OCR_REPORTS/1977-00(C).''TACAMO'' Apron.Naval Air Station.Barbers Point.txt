INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering
evaluation performed for the proposed airfield pavement improvements of
the TACAMO Apron at the Naval Air Station in Barbers Point Ewa Oahu
Hawaii The general location of the project site is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1 The exploration was performed in general
accordance with the Revised Amendment No 3 dated May 3 1988 to the
Revised Statement of Architect Engineer Services dated
Contract N62742 87 D 0073
This report summarizes our findings from the field exploration and
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses of data
obtained from the field exploration and laboratory testing These
recommendations are intended for design input and are not intended for
construction bidding estimation purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project area includes approximately 135 000 square yards of
pavements generally bordered by Taxiways K P M and T and also
includes an area between Hangars 110 and 111 Both concrete and asphaltic
concrete pavements are involved
Portions of the existing pavements have generally deteriorated as
evidenced by cracking and bird bath conditions throughout the area It
is proposed to evaluate the present load capacity of the pavements and to
strengthen if necessary in order to meet the present and future aircraft
loading requirements

Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built by two basaltic shield volcanoes
Waianae and Koolau Waianae is the older of the two and reached the end
of its activity before Koolau

As volcanic activity on the island of Oahu drew to a close the
island began to subside slowly While the island was sinking fringing
coral algal reefs began to grow on the seaward margins of the island
During the Pleistocene or Ice Age global sea levels rose and fell
in response to the great continental glaciers As the glaciers grew the
global sea level fell below the existing level and as the glaciers melted
and retreated the sea level rose to heights above the current stand of
the sea
Locally the fringing coral algal reefs continued to grow during the
high stands of the sea leaving fossil reefs which are now exposed above
sea level At the project site and in its vicinity there are exposures
of such fossil reefs which most likely grew during the Kaena 95 feet
stand of the sea Interbedded with these coral algal materials are
alluvial sediments which were transported from the nearby Waianae Range

Radar Survey
Previous experience indicated that the aircraft pavement area at the
site is underlain by coral formation at relatively shallow depths
Coral and coral algal limestones are the result of the growth of
marine organisms which secrete calcium carbonate The formations
constructed by these organisms are frequently discontinuous in nature
and have voids where the organisms did not grow Often these voids
are filled with detritus However the voids also frequently act as
lines of weakness for the formation of solution cavities caves and
sinkholes
A ground penetrating radar survey was conducted at the site in order
to attempt to detect subsurface voids
The survey was conducted after the initial borings were drilled in
order to have a reference for correlation of the radar data
The ground penetrating radar system utilizes impulse radar technology
to obtain a continuous high resolution profile of the subsurface
The radar profiling system consisted of two separate antennas five
feet apart mounted on a wooden platform The antenna array is
connected by an electrical cable to the processing and display
equipment and is towed along the ground at low speeds
The dual antenna system allows running two parallel lines
simultaneously thus reducing the number of transects required and
reducing the possibility of missing anomalies of large areal extent
Selected subsurface radar profiles and interpretations are presented
in Appendix D

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The field exploration generally encountered competent subgrade
material although it was not possible to distinguish between the base
course and select borrow subgrade layers Most of the soils consisted of
silty coral sand and gravel derived from excavated coral formation The
soils generally contained about 20 to 30 percent fines and would be
classified as SM or GM according to the Unified Soil Classification
System The field and laboratory tests indicated that the on site coral
materials beneath the present pavement slabs can provide relatively high
values of subgrade strength CBR and modulus of subgrade reaction values
The borings disclosed that the thicknesses of the existing asphaltic
concrete pavement range from 3 5 to 5 0 inches and cement concrete
pavement ranges from 6 0 to 7 0 inches for reinforced concrete and up to
about 10 5 inches for non reinforced concrete Our laboratory tests
recorded high values of flexural strength for the existing concrete
pavement
Based on the construction history of the airfield supplied to us the
project area was first constructed between 1943 and 1966 and major
repairs were performed in 1960 s Thus based on the exploration results
which encountered competent subgrade it appears that the observed
deterioration of the pavements may be mainly due to aging rather than
structural distress
The current analyses of aircraft pavement thicknesses were performed
in accordance to NAVFAC Design Manual DM 21 Airfield Pavements June
1973 NAVFAC Design Manual DM 21 3 Flexible Pavement Design for
Airfields August 1978 and Military Handbook 1021 4 Rigid Pavement
Design for Airfields Air Force Manual 88 6 Rigid Pavements for
Airfields Other Than Army August 1979 was also used as a reference

Pavement Joints
Joints in concrete pavements should be provided to 1 allow
contraction and expansion caused by temperature and moisture changes in
the pavements 2 relieve warping and curling stresses caused by
temperature differential within pavements 3 prevent irregular or random
cracking of pavements from unequal subgrade support or other causes 4
control shrinkage and cracking and 5 expedite construction
NAVAFAC Type Specification TS 02614 latest revision Joints
Reinforcements and Mooring Eyes in Concrete Pavement should be referred
to for joint details and construction requirements Joint sealing
compounds used should be jet fuel resistant and blast resistant and should
follow the recommendations of the same specification
Pavement Drainage
Surface runoff from the project and pavement areas should be
collected and drained to a suitable discharge point Water should not be
allowed to pond immediately adjacent to the pavement areas The subject
site should be graded during either a rehabilitation program or during new
pavement construction to provide positive drainage away from the pavement
areas

It is our understanding that the inlet structure may empty into a dry

Subsurface Cavities
Based on the radar survey and probing results cavities exist at
random locations below the pavement areas The cavities appear to be
generally located deeper than 3 feet below the existing pavement surface
and are generally small 1 to 2 foot high
There were no observable differences in the pavement performance at
the areas where the cavities were detected at the time of our field
exploration
A preliminary stress analysis was performed to evaluate the effect of
the aircraft wheel load over a typical cavity about 1 foot high and 3
feet below the pavement level and extending laterally for about 9 feet
Based on our preliminary analyses it does not appear that the
detected cavities pose imminent concerns under the design aircraft
However the long term stability of the cavities cannot be forecast since
the cavity roof may weaken under the continued traffic loads
To reduce the concerns of potential cavity collapse the cavity areas
may be filled with grout However due to the large areal extent of the
pavements and random nature of the cavities grouting of the entire area
would be very costly An alternative may be to grout the suspected cavity
areas only under selected travel paths within the apron area and or those
at shallow depths below the pavement surface

Site Preparation
No major site grading is anticipated
For new pavement section construction the area within the contract
limits should be thoroughly cleared at the on set of earthwork All
existing pavements debris and unsuitable materials should be removed and
disposed properly off site in order to prevent contamination of the
excavated material Conventional equipment may be used for excavation
The excavated soil can be used for backfilling as subgrade The
compaction requirements are detailed below
Subgrade Preparation
The surface of the subgrade for new pavements should be compacted to
a minimum of 100 of its maximum dry density per ASTM D 1557 78
Soft spots encountered at the subgrade level should be removed and
replaced with sandy soil compacted to a minimum of 100 maximum dry
density per ASTM D 1557 78
The subgrade soils encountered by our exploration program consisted
mainly of tan and white silty sand and coral gravel The maximum dry
densities determined by ASTM D 1557 78 on bulk soil samples from test pits
varied from a low value of 98 p c f for silty sand to a high value of 137
p c f for silty gravel
Based on the results of our recent field density tests taken on the
compacted with small compaction equipment backfill at the test pits it
appears that the subgrade soils can be compacted to 100 of its maximum
dry density as established by ASTM D 1557 78 without unusual difficulties
Groundwater was not encountered during our field exploration Also
the subgrade soils generally fall into the category of GM and SM based on
the Unified Classification System and may be classified as permeable
therefore subsurface drainage problems are not anticipated

New Utility Lines
New drain lines may be planned with the pavement improvement A

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level one foot

maximum dry density per ASTM D 1557 78 The upper portions of the

In sixteen 16 borings drilled in place CBR tests were performed on
the near surface soils to evaluate their strength characteristics for
pavement subgrade subbase base support The test results are summarized
on Plates B 1 through B 3
The field in place CBR tests were carried out in general accordance
with ASTM D 4429 84 Load penetration curves of the individual in place
CBR tests are presented on Plates B 4 through B 34
A truck mounted drilling rig with approximately seven 7 tons of
dead weight was used for the load reaction The truck was placed over the
boring so that one of the I beams at the rear end was located directly
above the center of the boring A hydraulic jack was then used to jack
against this I beam with its piston pipe extensions and penetration piston
connected and resting on the soil in the boring
A dial gauge graduated in units of 0 001 inch was used to measure
the deflection and the pressure gauge attached to the hydraulic jack was
used to measure the applied load
Field density tests were performed on the backfills of the four test
pits In general the backfills were compacted to a minimum of 98
compaction per ASTM D 1557 78 A summary of the field density tests is
presented on Plate B 35

APPENDIX D
Ground Penetrating Radar
The ground penetrating radar system utilizes impulse radar technology
to obtain a continuous high resolution profile of the subsurface A
pulse is transmitted from a special antenna through the subsurface until
it reaches a soil interface void or imbedded object Then depending on
the electrical characteristics of the soil interface or object a portion
of the transmitted pulse is reflected back to the receiver of the same
antenna The received signal is then processed and a subsurface profile
is displayed on the built in graphic recorder An example of the signal
structure and profile is shown on Plate D 1
The radar profiling system consists of two antennas separated by five
feet and mounted on a wooden platform The antenna system was towed along
the ground and is connected by an electrical cable to the processing and
display equipment located in the vehicle The dual antenna system allows
the operator to run two parallel lines simultaneously thus reducing the
number of transects required and reducing the possibility of missing
cavities of large lateral extent
Each antenna transmits a radar pulse into the ground at a rate of 500
MHz with a pulse rate of two nanoseconds The vehicle speed was
maintained at approximately 3 to 5 feet per second Therefore
reflections were acquired every inch of travel
The horizontal scale of each profile was determined by measuring off
10 foot intervals along each alignment During a given profile a mark
was placed on the radar graph at the intersection of these 10 foot marks
The vertical scale of the radar graph is 9 9 nanoseconds per division
which corresponds to approximately 1 5 feet per division This was
confirmed by running a transect across a previously drilled boring and
noting the depth to a known obvious reflector
After the square foot radar survey was performed probable
cavity locations were selected and a single antenna system was utilized to
delineate the lateral extent of the suspected cavities Probing locations
were then selected accordingly
A hand held 1000 MHz antenna with a 0 75 nanosecond pulse width was
utilized to detect the presence and orientation of rebar at selected
locations in the floor of Hangar 110 and in the concrete parking apron
Selected subsurface radar profiles and interpretations are shown on
Plates D 2 through D 5
The findings of the radar survey are summarized on Plates D 6 and
D 7
