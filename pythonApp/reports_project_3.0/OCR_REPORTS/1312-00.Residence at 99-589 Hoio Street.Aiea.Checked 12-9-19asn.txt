In accordance with your request we have made a study of the near
surface soil conditions at the above referenced lot in order to provide
foundation recommendations for the proposed residence The study
consisted of excavating three shallow test pits performing laboratory
testing and reviewing the preliminary house foundation plans This
report summarizes our findings and recommendations
PROPOSED CONSTRUCTION
According to the preliminary house plans the proposed residence
will be a one to two story structure approximately 46 by 46 feet in
plan dimension The house will be of wood frame post and beam
construction except for the garage which will be slab on grade on fill
supported by concrete masonry retaining walls

SITE CONDITIONS
The subject lot is located on the eastern side of Hoio Street in
Aiea Oahu Hawaii It slopes down at H V for about 50 feet
from Hoio Street to a more gently sloping area in the back of the lot
At the time of our exploration the site was densely vegetated
with haole koa bushes and grasses which were being grubbed by the
building contractor
SUBSURFACE SOIL CONDITIONS
Three test pits were excavated at the approximate locations shown
on the Site Plan Plate 1 The test pits were excavated utilzing a
rubber tired backhoe provided by the contractor and extended to depths
of 5 0 to 3 5 feet below the existing ground surface The test pit
logs are presented on Plate A 1
In general the test pits encountered a surface layer of fill
consisting of stiff reddish brown clayey silt In Test Pit 2 the fill
was underlain at a depth of 2 0 feet by hard brownish red clayey
silt which appeared to be the native soil of the site
Our laboratory tests included 2 sets of Atterberg Limits tests 3
swell tests under a 1000 p s f surcharge and three shrink swell tests
to examine the swelling potential of the reddish brown clayey silt
soils The results of these tests are presented on Plates B 1 and
B 2 The laboratory results indicate that the on site clayey silt
soils are moderately to highly expansive

No groundwater was encountered in the test pits excavated at the
time of our field exploration
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
It is our opinion that the subject site is suitable for the
proposed development provided that our recommendations are closely
followed These recommendations present means of minimizing swelling
potential of the clayey silt soils that may otherwise heave the
driveway slabs and ways to reduce downslope creep of the foundations
Site Preparation and Fill Placement
The site should first be cleared of vegetation debris slopewash
and other loose overcast soil
Our laboratory test results indicate the on site clayey silt soil
to be moderately to highly expansive Therefore we recommend that the
existing expansive soil within 18 inches below the finished grade of
the driveway be removed This layer of soil is anticipated to be lower
than 18 inches under the garage area Therefore over excavation may
not be required Depending upon the amount of new fill required to
raise the site grade to accommodate the new driveway the amount of
this over excavation could be very small The excavated expansive
clayey silt soil may be reused as fill under garage area only if it is
situated 3 feet below the finished garage slab and away from the back
of the garage retaining wall The over excavation under the driveway
should be backfilled with compacted material

W Page 4
Fills and backfills should be placed in 9 inch loose lifts and
compacted to a minimum of 90 maximum dry density as established by
ASTM D 1557 78 The i11 behind the garage retaining wall should also
be keyed and benched into firm existing materials in order to properly
bond the new fill to the existing slope see Plate 2 for details
The backfill material for driveway over excavations and the fill
for the garage area should consist of select import material such as
crusher waste select borrow or coral to reduce the amount of
compactive effort required These materials should be tested and
approved by the soils engineer prior to its delivery to the site to
assure that it is suitable for the proposed construction
Foundations
Spread and continuous footings may be used at this lot
The portion of the subject lot where it is planned to construct
the proposed residence is relatively steep Additionally the near
surface soils are moderately to highly expansive Therefore it is
critically important that the retaining wall and individual post
footing foundations be embedded sufficiently deep to be situated below
zone of soil swell and shrinkage and to resist downhill Slope creep as
well as to develop the required soil bearing capacity
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 5
Generally individual post footings should be embedded a minimum
of 2 feet below the lowest adjacent finished ground surface so that the
footing would have a minimum 7 feet horizontal set back measured from
the outer footing bottom edge out to the adjacent Slope face An
allowable bearing value of 1000 p s f may be used for the post footing
design
To resist the large lateral earth pressures we recommend that
retaining wall footings be embedded a minimum depth of 4 feet below the
slope surface to develop a minimum horizontal set back distance of 12
feet from the outer edge of the footing to the face of the adjacent
slope In view of the deeper embedment a higher allowable soil
bearing pressure of 1500 p s f may be used for the retaining wall
footing design
The above bearing values are for total dead plus live loads and
may be increased by one third 1 3 for wind or seismic loads
The bottom of all foundations should be cleared of loose material
and bear on firm soil We recommend that the footing excavations be
inspected by a soils engineer to assure that adequate bearing has been
obtained and that loose soils are removed prior to concrete placement
To minimize further drying of the foundation soils during
construction the footing should be concreted and backfilled as soon as
possible after excavation

Retaining Walls
Retaining walls up to 8 feet high are planned along the perimeter
of the garage and driveway to retain fill over which concrete on grade
slabs are scheduled
Well graded granular material such as crusher waste should be
used behind the walls
The recommendations given under FOUNDATIONS should be followed
for the design of the retaining wall foundations
The walls should be designed to resist lateral earth pressures as
follows
Walls unrestrained at top 35 p s f per foot of depth
active condition equivalent fluid pressure
Walls restrained at top 35 p s f per foot of depth
at rest condition equivalent fluid pressure
A base friction factor of 0 4 may be used to resist the lateral
forces on the footing
In addition passive lateral earth pressure of 250 p s f per foot
of depth may be used to resist lateral force on the footings
A drainage system such as weepholes should be used behind the
walls to prevent the build up of hydrostatic pressure

Slab On Grade
Although the on site soils are generally expansive on grade slabs
may be constructed for the driveway and the garage floor provided that
the expansive soils are removed to at least 18 inches below the
finished grade the over excavation backfilled with compacted
non expansive soil and the expansive soil be kept in a moist condition
It is very important that the underlying expansive soils have
adequate soil moisture at the time of concrete placement so that the
post construction heaving potential be kept to a minimum To achieve
this objective the soil surface should be sprinkled moist before
granular fill is placed The granular fill should be placed and
compacted at a moisture content about 3 percentage points wet of the
optimum In addition the compacted granular surface should be kept
moist throughout the period before the slabs are concreted
If either crusher waste or select borrow material is used the
slabs may be placed directly over such a subgrade
Drainage
The finished lot grade outside the on grade slabs and post
footings should be shaped to shed water away from the foundations and
to avoid ponding condition Also it is advised that gutter systems be
installed around the house and that gutter water be diverted far away
from the foundations and walkways Excessive landscape watering near
the foundations and slab should also be avoided
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1312 00 Page 8
Planters next to the foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to prevent water from entering the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper
performance of the above referenced foundations since ponded water
could cause excessive subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving
or loss of strength
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled tight
against footing immediately after setting of the concrete to prevent
ponding condition
Drainage swales must be provided as soon as possible and
maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and footing
foundation
Design Review
Plans and specifications for site grading should be forwarded to
the soils engineer for review and written comments prior to con
struction to see that the intent of our recommendations is included

Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the footing excavations over excavations
and backfilling operations be observed by the soils engineer during
construction This is to see that adequate compaction has been
obtained and the subsurface conditions are consistent with the findings
of our investigation The recommendations given in this report are
contingent upon the above inspections
INVESTIGATION LIMITATIONS
Our services consist of professional opinions and recommen
dations made in accordance with generally accepted soil and foundation
engineering principles and practices This warranty is in lieu of all
other warranties either expressed or implied
The analysis and recommendations submitted in this report are
based on our site reconnaissance soil information from test pit data
and laboratory tests obtained from this soil investigation
We wish to point out that potentials for slab heaving and
foundation movement from soil creep will be greatly reduced if our
recommendations are followed Nonetheless these potentials still
exist due to the inherently higher risks associated with construction
on a hillside slope covered with expansive soil

Unanticipated soil conditions are commonly encountered and cannot
be fully determined by reconnaissance and test pit excavations These
unforeseen conditions such as pockets of organic soils trash fill or

constructed project Some contingency fund is thus recommended to
accommodate these possible costs
