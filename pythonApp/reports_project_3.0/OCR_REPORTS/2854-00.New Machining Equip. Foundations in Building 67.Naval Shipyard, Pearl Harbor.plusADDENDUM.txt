Dewatering
Since the groundwater level was measured to be approximately 8 9 feet below
the existing ground surface at the time of our exploration and the bottom of the
mat footing is about 10 5 feet below the existing ground surface dewatering will
be necessary for the construction of the mat foundation The dewatering
operations should be coordinated with the excavation shoring support so that
the stability of the excavation is not jeopardized The operations should be
carried out without softening the bottom of the excavation
To provide a working platform and to assist in the dewatering a 12 inch
thick cushion layer of 3B Fine gravel is recommended beneath the mat
foundation
Relatively large quantities of groundwater may be extracted from the excavation
since the natural groundwater level is above the excavation bottom and a
permeable coral formation was encountered near the foundation bottom
Method of Dewatering
The method and equipment to be used for the excavation dewatering should
be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety
considerations
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents
should be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural
groundwater table and to exclude the water from the excavation to permit
construction of the proposed mat foundation under safe and dry conditions
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2854 00 Page 4
These works or systems should therefore include but not be limited to
pumping only
We suggest that the following three 3 basic criteria be considered in selecting
a suitable method of dewatering
1 The method should result in the least disturbance or damage to the
adjacent existing structures
2 The method should maintain stability of and also provide safe and dry
working conditions in the excavation
3 The method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modifications to
accommodate various ground conditions
Based on these criteria it appears that sump pumping inside the
excavation may be used Increase in the excavation size may be
required to accommodate the sump pumps
Construction Precautions
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to
dewatering It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer
be retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering
system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the
sole responsibility of the contractor His qualified representative who

best opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during
construction and to implement as soon as possible necessary
precautionary or remedial measures including but not limited to stopping

Where encountered at the excavation bottoms permeable granular
subsoils may be susceptible to piping and quick conditions Therefore
the project dewatering operations should be done without pumping out
soil fines pumping clear water only and should be coordinated with the

The dewatering operations should be carried out without creating a

INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed new machining equipment foundations in Building 67 in the
Naval Shipyard in Pearl Harbor on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location of
the project site and its vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from analyses of the data obtained
during the field exploration for the project These recommendations are intended for
design input and are not intended for construction estimation or bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to install several new machining equipment within Building 67
Some of the heavier new equipment are as follows horizontal boring equipment hydraulic
straight press equipment vertical torque lathe equipment machining and turning center
equipment and a 4 axis vertical machine center equipment

new equipment are as follows

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The field exploration disclosed that the new equipment sites are generally underlain
by two different subsoil conditions The borings drilled in the southern portion of the
building generally encountered dense mudrock near the surface while loose to very loose
coral sands with gravels was encountered in the northern portion of the building
Based on the anticipated bearing stresses we believe that shallow mat foundations
may be used for the proposed equipment foundations bearing on the competent mudrock
deposit or compacted fill
Moderate to hard excavation should be anticipated for the foundations extending
into the medium dense mudrock deposit and the existing three foot thick concrete slab
Foundation Support
Horizontal Boring and Vertical Torque Lathe Equipment
Based on information provided to us the bottoms of the horizontal boring and
vertical torque lathe mat foundations are anticipated to be placed at a depth about
7 5 and 3 0 feet below the top of the existing concrete slab respectively It
appears that the bottoms of the footings will likely be extending into the underlying
dense mudrock deposit encountered in the borings
We believe that shallow mat foundations bearing on the competent mudrock
deposit may be used

Hydraulic Straight Press Equipment
It is planned to place the new press equipment directly on the existing seven inch
thick concrete slab
Based on our field exploration the existing concrete slab is underlain by a medium
dense coral sand with gravel layer overlying dense mudrock and coral
Provided that existing concrete slab is structurally adequate to support the

will not cause excess settlement of the underlying medium dense coral sand and

Based on the information provided to us we anticipate that the bearing stresses

settlements are anticipated to be on the order of less than one fourth inch total

Machine and Turning Center and 4 Axis Vertical Machine Equipment
Based on information provided to us the bottoms of the equipment mat
foundations are anticipated to be placed at depths ranging from 8 to 2 9 below
the existing ground surface
The proposed mat foundations should be embedded just below the existing grades
at the new machine locations If the mat foundations are elevated above ground
the weight of the above ground portions of the foundations would act as additional
loading on the underlying loose subsoils
We believe that shallow mat foundations may be used provided special subgrade
preparation be performed to provide a very dense crust below the footings to
reduce the amount of foundation settlement due to the consolidation of the
underlying loose sandy deposit
To reduce the amount of settlement and to provide uniform subgrade support we
recommend that the foundation subgrade be over excavated a minimum of 24
inches and proof rolled to a firm subgrade at the over excavation bottom The
excavated in situ sandy soils may then be reused to backfill the over excavation
The backfill should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent of its maximum dry
density as determined in accordance with ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 The
over excavation should extend a minimum of 12 inches beyond the edge of the
mat foundation
If additional imported material is required the material should consist of non
expansive select granular materials less than one half 112 inches in maximum size
with less than 20 percent passing the No 200 sieve Any imported fill material
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2854 00 Page 10
should be tested and approved by the geotechnical engineer prior to delivery to
the site
We estimate that the proposed mat foundations will generate less than 1 000 p s f
of direct bearing stresses on the recompacted fill crust Estimated settlement are
anticipated to be on the order of about one quarter inch total
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressures
developed against the buried vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing material
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 250 p s f per foot of depth This assumes that the soil around the foundation
is properly backfilled and compacted A coefficient of friction of 0 4 between the bottom
of the footings and the mudrock or compacted sandy bearing material may be used

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling of five
5 borings to depths of 13 0 to 31 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 One of the borings was also
advanced by continuous penetration without sampling to a depth of 47 0 feet below the
existing ground surface The borings were drilled by a trailer mounted rotary drill rig
advancing continuous flight augers or rotary and core drilling tools as appropriate
The material encountered in the borings were examined and classified in general
accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2488 84 Description and Identification of
Soils Visual Manual Procedure by a geologist from our firm The logs of the borings
are shown on Plates A 1 and A 5
Soil samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM
Standard Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a standard split spoon sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The
blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the boring logs at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of soil and rock materials encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material
left in the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and
testing
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses This designation is only applicable to cores retrieved form in situ
rock formations RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
that is in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks if 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in determining pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 90 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in determining certain properties such
as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight
of the tested sample is shown at the appropriate location on plates summarizing test
results and at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
