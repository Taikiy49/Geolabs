INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of a soils engineering
investigation performed for the earth movement areas in the vicinity of
Kahaloa Drive Kahaloa Place Hulu Place Woolsey Place and Lanikaula
Street in Manoa Valley Oahu Hawaii
Included in this report are our findings in respect to some of the
historical background of the ground movements observations made in
relation to the mechanics and causes of the earth movements and finally
some recommendations for immediate and future remedial and preventive
measures
The general location of the site and its vicinity is shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1

GENERAL SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located on the easterly side of Manoa Valley
along the toe of Waahila Ridge and includes Kahaloa Drive Kahaloa Place
Hulu Place Woolsey Place and Lanikaula Street

The general study area is on a sloping hillside with an average
slope of approximately 3 to 5 horizontal to 1 vertical 33 to 20
slope The area is roughly rectangular in plan with horizontal
dimensions of about 1500 feet in length parallel to the valley ina
northeast to southwest direction and about 300 feet wide between the

The ground elevations over the study area generally varied from

lateral movements were noted in the existing streets curbs and

experienced some structural damages related to earth movements Detailed
discussions related to earth movements are presented in the later part of
this report The major damaged pavement areas along the roads are
indicated on the Site Plan Plate 2 Pictures of some of these areas are

Subdivision Grading
The roadway utility system for the development was completed during
the early 1950 s A review of the plan indicated that the grading
generally involved excavating the uphill side of the Toadway and
probably using the excavated materials for the fill embankment

Geology
The project site is located on the side of a ridge of the Koolau
Range along the eastern slopes of Manoa Valley This ridge is an
erosional remnant eroded from the Leeward flank of the Koolau Range and
consists of steep rocky slopes of relatively unweathered dense basalt
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1350 00 Page 8
The upper rocky slopes consist of massive basalt outcrops forming
vertical cliffs fractured in place basalt and dense boulders some very
large At the foot of the slopes where the site is located extensive
erosion of the upper valley sides has deposited large quantities of
colluviun
The colluvial deposits are made up of rocks that consist of angular
to subangular fragments of basalt rocks that are poorly sorted ranging
in size from 1 to 2 feet to less than 1 inch in a matrix of fine grained
partly weathered material The colluvial material Tepresents the
shedding of erosional debris from the steeper slopes by gravity soil
avalanche or by other means of mass transfer
Some of the colluvium has been partly consolidated cemented into
a fairly coherent rocky mass that may be called a gravel cobble breccia
The site was subsequently covered with a surface layer of young
alluvium consisting of gray clay adobe with cobbles and boulders

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the borings laboratory data instrumentation and
engineering analysis it appears that the ground movements observed are
basically a result of poor subsurface soil adobe clay conditions on
relatively steep hillside slopes at the site These clay soils deposited
on a sloping terrain generally tend to move downslope or creep even if
they are not disturbed by grading Subsequent grading for subdivision
improvements roads houses etc usually result in steeper slopes and
increase water infiltration into the soils due to removal of vegetation
cover and landscape watering etc and thereby increase the potential
for ground movements
The movements that were measured by survey readings prior to our
investigation can generally be classified as relatively slow creep
movements However recent inclinometer readings indicate that the
movements may be accelerating

Findings
l
The field exploration disclosed that the site is underlain by a
surface layer of highly plastic clay soils approximately 9 to 15
feet thick These clay soils which are classified as CH
according to the Unified Soil Classification System locally
referred to as Adobe Clay exhibit high swelling shrinkage
potential and will lose considerable shear strength when
saturated When the CH clay soils experience significant
movement shear surfaces will then develop which will further
reduce the shear strength along the sheared surfaces to a very low
residual shear value
Based on our laboratory soil tests the existing in situ clay layer
is in a very moist to wet condition moisture content of 30 to 503
with the upper 5 feet noticeably wetter than the lower portion
This indicates that a substantial amount of water infiltration has
occurred in the upper portion of the layer and correspondingly
weakened the clay layer We believe the loss of strength might
continue due to additional infiltration and subsequent wetting of
the underlying soil layers
The soils underlying the surface clay consist of Clayey silt with
cobbles and boulders which grade into predominantly boulders in a
soil matrix colluvium These underlying soils generally have
higher shear strength and are generally more stable than the upper
surface clay

friction angle of about 18 degrees and a cohesion value of about
400 p s f for the upper clay soils Previous tests on Similar

Clay soil could lose additional strength and tend to creep or

10 degrees with the horizontal plane

Interpretation of Mechanism of Movement
To date the ground movement appears to be confined to the
relatively thin approximately 10 feet upper clay layer Visual
observations indicate that the movements are confined to relatively small
areas rather than a typical deep seated large slide as experienced in
Palolo and Aina Haina Valleys The past ground movements can probably be
classified as relatively slow downhill creep movements However
recent inclinometer readings indicate that the movements may be
accelerating
Due to the nature of the clay soils and the relatively steep
natural ground slope it is likely that even before development of the
area the near surface soils were slowly creeping downhill Based on
available construction plans it appears that the subdivision grading
GEOLABS HAWAII
generally cut away some of the toe support of the Slope and added weight
additional driving force to the upper areas Site observation
indicates that house construction activity further cut into the toe of
slope for garages and added weight to the upper areas retaining walls

In order to protect the existing City facilities utility lines
road pavements etc in the right of way several options to
improve the stability of the surface clay layer are presented
hereunder for consideration
1 A buttress fill could be constructed within the right of way
The buttress would consist of excavating the low strength clay
soils down to the better colluvium soil 9 to 15 feet deep and
replacing them with high strength compacted granular material
This would increase the shearing resistance of the soil against
ground movement However due to the many existing utilities
and the need to maintain some traffic flow the construction
cost would be high and the buttress fill would need to be
constructed in very small sections Adequate trench shoring
would be required to prevent caving in of the trench walls and
the resultant damage to adjacent properties In addition the
excavation should be backfilled as soon as possible to minimize
accumulation of rain water in the trenches which would further
reduce the stability of tne slopes A schematic section of the
buttress fill is shown on Plate 10
t should be noted that the buttress would probably help to
minimize movements within the downhill side of the road
right of way However if a large slip develops on the uphill
Side the toe of the slip could affect a portion of the
buttress

2 Large diameter caissons cantilever or tie back walls where
rock is encountered could be constructed along the edges of
the right of way to provide additional resistance against slope
movement A schematic section is shown on Plate 10

required in the areas upslope of the right of way in order to

The lateral ground movements at the site could be arrested if the
ground slopes could be flattened to ratios of about 6 1 H V or
less However due to the existing housing development this does

Water Infiltration
The slope inclinometer readings indicate that significant ground
movements are occurring within the upper portion of the Clay layer
Since water infiltration into the underlying clay soils would
increase the potential for slope movement immediate remedial work
should be directed to seal the cracks in the roads and to closely
monitor and repair all utility lines in the area
It is difficult to try to intercept most of the water infiltration
at the surface and to detect broken utility lines immediately
Therefore a trench drain could be installed within the road
right of way and extending down through the clay layer to intercept
and drain subsurface water A typical section of such a trench
drain is shown on Plate 11 The trench drain should be designed to
drain into a suitable outlet in the lower stable areas of the
development The drain would have to be constructed in small
sections to permit through traffic and to minimize the potential
for instability due to excavation
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1350 00 Page 21
It should be noted that the drainage measures would probably help
to reduce the lateral ground movements by minimizing additional
driving force from the water However drainage alone would not
completely arrest the movements since the upper clay soils have
already been saturated and weakened sufficiently to initiate
downhill creep movements
Future Movement Monitoring
The inclinometer readings indicate that the ground movements may be
accelerating However our readings only cover a period of about 3
months In order to better understand the magnitude and rate of ground
movements to assist in the determination of suitable remedial measures
for the area we recommend that monitoring of the inclinometers as well
as survey points be continued

APPENDIX E
COMPUTERIZED SLOPE STABILITY ANALYSIS
Slope stability analyses for this investigation were performed
utilizing Slope II computer program which utilizes the limit equilibrium
theory to analyze the stability of an earth slope This program is a
resident library file in Control Data Corporation s CYBERNET Services
with data imput and output handled by an in house IBM PC micro computer
This comprehensive and flexible program analyzes slope stability by
the method of slices and factors of safety can be obtained for circular
and non circular failure surfaces considering earthquakes and live
loads Cross sections for analyses may consist of a maximum of 15 soil
layers and 1000 slices Various pore pressure conditions may be
incorporated in each analysis with any of the following six analytical
methods
1 The Fellenius or Ordinary method of slices
2 the Simplified Bishop method
3 the Spencer method
4 the Janbu s Simplified method
5 the Janbu s Generalized method
6 the Morgenstern Price method and
7 the Nonvellier method Lowe Karafiath method Corps of
Engineers method and modified Swedish method can be solved as
special cases of the Morgenstern Price method
For our analyses Janbu s Simplified method was utilized with
occasional cross check runs utilizing the Ordinary Method of slices and
the simplified Bishop method
