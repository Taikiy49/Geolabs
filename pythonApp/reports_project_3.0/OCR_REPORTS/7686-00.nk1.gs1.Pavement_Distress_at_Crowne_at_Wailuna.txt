SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling seven borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 7, extending to depths of approximately 5 to 10 feet 
below the existing ground surface. Upon completing the drilling and sampling of each boring, 
the boreholes were converted into groundwater monitoring wells to intermittently observe 
the groundwater levels in the boreholes. 
Our field exploration generally encountered a flexible pavement structure underlain 
by medium stiff to hard silty clay and/or clayey silt extending to the maximum depth explored 
of about 10 feet below the existing ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in 
the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. However, water seepage through the 
existing aggregate base course material was observed in Boring No. 2 during our drilling 
work. 
We understand that the primary mode of distress to the residential side streets is 
apparent water seepage through the asphaltic concrete pavement. Numerous surface 
cracks were observed mainly along the center portion of the roadways. 
Based on the results of our field exploration, the four sets of groundwater level 
readings taken, and our review of the Crowne at Wailuna subdivision as-built drawings, we 
believe that the subsurface seepage water is traveling primarily through the pavement 
aggregate base course material. Therefore, we recommend installing transverse and 
longitudinal subdrains in the existing roadways to reduce the subsurface seepage water 
entering the pavement sections. The subdrains should be connected to nearby drainage 
inlet structures. 
Should it be desired to repair the existing roadways, the roadway repair work should 
be performed during or after subdrain installation. The clayey subgrade should be checked 
for softening. If softening of the subgrade has occurred, the soft subgrade material should 
be removed and replaced with suitable fill material compacted to at least 95 percent relative 
density. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and 
recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Pavement Distress at Crowne at Wailuna project in the Aiea area on 
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, field measurements, and engineering 
analyses for the project. These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended 
for the development of remediation measures for the high groundwater and seepage 
conditions below the existing roadway pavements only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
Project Considerations 
The Crowne at Wailuna subdivision is located at the top of Kaahumanu Street in 
the Aiea area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. We understand that the subdivision was built 
in the mid-1990’s and consists of one and two-story single-family homes, and associated 
asphalt concrete paved roadways. Based on the information provided, we understand 
that water seepage through cracks in the existing asphaltic concrete pavement occurs on 
certain roadways after rain events. We also understand that the water seepage to the 
pavement surface persists for some time after the rain has stopped.  
Based on our review of the available drawings, the project site included filling of 
gullies and drainageways prior to house construction.  Subdrains were installed in the 
gullies and drainageways prior to fill placement.  We anticipate that the on-site silty clay 
and clayey silt soils were used as backfill material to fill the gullies and drainageways to 
finished grades.  
Based on our observations, the existing roadways within the subdivision typically 
slopes toward the middle of the road.  Surface water drained down the middle of the road 
and into drainage inlet structures located in the middle of the roadway.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age.  As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased, lava flows from Koolau 
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, known as 
Schofield Plateau. The project site is located on the Schofield Plateau. 
The project area is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lava of the 
Koolau Volcanic Series.  These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed 
dense vesicular basalt rock formation with inter-bedded thin clinker layers. In-situ 
chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years.  
The project site is located on a planèze, or a triangular, relatively uneroded intravalley 
ridge on the southwest facing flank of the Koolau dome. The weathering process has 
formed a mantle of residual soils which grade to saprolite with depth. In general, saprolite 
is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic 
rocks.  The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with increasing depth. 
Site Description 
The project site is located at the Crowne at Wailuna gated subdivision at the top 
of Kaahumanu Street in the Aiea area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. In general, the 
subdivision is bordered by undeveloped land and by numerous ridges and valleys. A 
Hawaiian Electric Company (HECO) easement with high voltage overhead powerlines 
bisects the property from east to west. An access road leading to a Board of Water Supply 
(BWS) reservoir is located at the end of the northern end of Kaahumanu Street. Based 
on the provided as-built plans, the subdivision was constructed on the pre-existing 
ridgeline. Relatively shallow gullies and/or drainage channels were filled to build the 
existing neighborhood consisting of single-family homes and residential side streets. The 
general layout and as-built contours of the Crowne at Wailuna neighborhood are shown 
on the Overall Site Plan, Plate 2.

Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling seven borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 7, extending to depths of approximately 5 to 10 feet 
below the existing ground surface. Upon completing the drilling and sampling of each 
boring, the boreholes were converted into groundwater monitoring wells. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Overall Site Plan, Plate 2, and Site Plans, 
Plates 3.1 and 3.2. 
Based on the provided as-built plans, Boring Nos. 1, 3, 5, 6 and 7 were located in 
previously cut areas while Boring Nos. 2 and 4 were located in previously filled areas. In 
general, our field exploration encountered approximately 1.5 to 3 inches of asphaltic 
concrete pavement overlying 7.5 to 9 inches of aggregate base course material. 
Underlying the pavement structure in the cut areas, residual soils consisting of medium 
stiff to hard silty clay and/or clayey silt were encountered to the maximum depth explored 
of about 10 feet below the existing ground surface. In one of the fill area borings (Boring 
No. 4), residual soils were encountered from the existing ground surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, water seepage through the aggregate base course material was 
observed in Boring No. 2 during our field drilling work. It should be noted that groundwater 
levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface 
water runoff, and other factors.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a pavement structure underlain by 
medium stiff to hard silty clay and/or clayey silt extending to the maximum depth explored 
of about 10 feet below the existing ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in 
the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. However, groundwater seepage 
through the aggregate base course material was observed in Boring No. 2 during our 
drilling work. 
Based on the results of our field exploration, the four sets of water level readings 
taken, and our review of the Crowne at Wailuna subdivision as-built drawings, we believe 
that the subsurface seepage water is traveling primarily through the pavement aggregate 
base course material. Therefore, we recommend installing transverse and longitudinal 
subdrains in the existing roadways to reduce the severity of subsurface seepage water 
entering the pavement sections. The subdrains should be connected to nearby drainage 
inlet structures. Detailed discussions and recommendations are presented in the following 
sections of this report.  
Observed Distresses 
The existing asphaltic concrete paved subdivision roads typically slope towards 
the middle of the roadway. Surface water flows along the center of the roads and into 
drainage inlet structures located in the middle of the roads. Numerous surface cracks 
were observed mainly along the center portion of the roadways.  
We understand that the primary mode of distress to the residential side streets is 
apparent water seepage through the asphaltic concrete pavement. At the time the boring 
locations were marked, the pavement throughout the neighborhood was dry except for 
areas of known subsurface water seepage, as shown in Photo No. 1 in Appendix C. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that these areas remain wet for 
several days after a significant rain. The borings were generally located near these known 
areas of seepage or in areas where water seepage was expected based on the provided 
as-built plans. In addition, several utility boxes throughout the project site were observed 
to be filled with water as shown in Photo Nos. 2 and 3 in Appendix C.

Groundwater Level Monitoring Program 
As part of the field exploration program, each borehole drilled was converted into 
a groundwater monitoring well to observe the potential for groundwater seepage. The 
groundwater monitoring wells consisted of a 2-inch diameter perforated PVC pipe 
installed and backfilled with sand in each of the 4-inch diameter boreholes. A watertight 
cap was installed at the top of the PVC pipe to prevent surface water infiltration from 
entering the standpipe. The annulus between the outside of the PVC pipe and the 
borehole sidewalls was backfilled with sand and a traffic rated metal cover was installed 
flush with the existing ground surface to protect the monitoring wells and allow access for 
groundwater readings. 
Groundwater level readings in the monitoring wells were taken on four separate 
occasions over an approximately 2-month period. The field measurements were 
conducted either after a significant rainfall event or during extended dryer periods to study 
the characteristics of the subsurface water seepage. The first set of readings, taken on 
March 26, 2018, was conducted after approximately 4 inches of rainfall occurred in the 
Crowne at Wailuna area. The remaining three sets of readings were taken during 
extended dryer periods due to the high groundwater levels observed during the first site 
visit. Water level measurements were taken manually using a portable electronic water 
level meter. The results of the groundwater monitoring program are summarized in the 
table below.

Based on the measured groundwater level readings, increased groundwater levels 
were observed soon after the significant rainfall event and most of the observation wells 
were filled with groundwater to near the bottom of the traffic rated metal well covers, as 
shown in Photo No. 4 in Appendix C.  
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered during our field exploration, the 
monitoring wells were installed in relatively stiff clayey soils. The clayey soils generally 
have a relatively low permeability and, therefore, changes in the groundwater level within 
the clayey soils occur over a longer period of time. Based on the four sets of groundwater 
level readings taken, increases in the groundwater level within the monitoring wells 
appear to rapidly increase after a rainfall event but dissipated slowly during dryer periods. 
Therefore, we believe that the subsurface seepage water is primarily traveling through 
the pavement aggregate base course material. The seepage water is able to infiltrate into 
the monitoring wells at a relatively rapid rate due to the relatively permeable nature of the 
aggregate base course material. Once inside the monitoring wells, the groundwater level 
dissipates slowly due to the relatively low permeability of the in-situ clayey soils. 
Potential Cause of Pavement Distress 
Based on the provided as-built plans of the Crowne at Wailuna subdivision, we 
understand that several pre-existing gullies and/or drainage channels were filled up to 
about 13 feet in vertical height in order to achieve the design finished grades. The largest 
of these filled areas approximately bisects the subdivision longitudinally from northeast to

southwest and runs near the known areas of pavement distress. The pre-existing 
drainage paths prior to subdivision construction are shown on the Overall Site Plan, 
Plate 2, and Site Plans, Plates 3.1 and 3.2. As indicated previously, subdrains were 
installed to collect subsurface water within the existing drainageways prior to fill 
placement. We anticipate that the gullies and drainageways were filled with the on-site

Based on our review of the available drawings and the results of our field 
exploration, we believe the storm water is primarily traveling through the pavement 
aggregate base course material. Since the groundwater takes a long time to drain, 
softening of the underlying clayey subgrade may be occurring.  The pavement distress 
(surface cracks) appear to be caused by the softening of the subgrade and potential loss 
of fines of the clayey subgrade through the cracks in the pavement. 
Recommended Remedial Measures 
Based on our study of the Crowne at Wailuna subdivision as-built drawings, our 
field exploration program, and groundwater level monitoring program, we recommend 
installing transverse and longitudinal subdrains in the existing roadways to reduce the 
subsurface seepage water entering the pavement sections. The recommended locations 
of the transverse and longitudinal subdrains are shown on the Overall Site Plan, Plate 2, 
and Site Plans, Plates 3.1 and 3.2. The purpose of the subdrains is to intercept subsurface 
seepage water flowing through the pavement base course material near known areas of 
pavement distress. The subdrains should be connected to nearby drainage inlet 
structures. We recommend using a 6-inch perforated PVC pipe with a minimum slope of 
0.5 percent for the subdrain system. A typical subdrain detail is shown on Plate 4. The 
drainage plans should be designed by a Civil Engineer. 
Should it be desired to repair the existing roadways, the roadway repair work 
should be performed during or after subdrain installation. The clayey subgrade material 
should be checked for softening.  If softening of the subgrade has occurred, the soft 
subgrade material should be removed and replaced with suitable fill material compacted 
to at least 95 percent relative compaction.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from our test borings. Variations of the subsurface conditions 
between and beyond the test borings may occur and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then 
appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations presented 
herein. 
The test boring locations indicated herein are approximate, having been estimated 
by taping from visible features shown on the Master Grading Plan created by Community 
Planning Incorporated dated November 15, 1993.  Elevations of the borings were 
estimated from the spot elevations and contour lines shown on the same plan. The field 
boring locations and elevations should be considered accurate only to the degree implied 
by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks represented on the Logs of Borings show the approximate 
boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual transition. We did not 
encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration. However, it must 
be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in seasonal rainfall and other factors. 
These data have been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. 
This report has been prepared for the exclusive use of the Crown at Wailuna AOAO 
for specific application to the Pavement Distress at Crowne at Wailuna project as 
described herein in accordance with generally accepted geotechnical engineering 
principles and practices. No warranty is expressed or implied. 
This report has been prepared solely for the purpose of assisting the design 
engineers in the design of the proposed project. Therefore, this report may not contain 
sufficient data, or the proper information, to serve as a basis for detailed construction cost 
estimates.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
seven boreholes, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 7, extending to depths of about 5 to 
10 feet below the existing ground surface. The seven boreholes were then converted into 
groundwater level monitoring wells. The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-7. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007) Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample.  The pocket penetrometer test results 
are summarized on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Eight Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits.  The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.  Graphic presentation of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-1.
