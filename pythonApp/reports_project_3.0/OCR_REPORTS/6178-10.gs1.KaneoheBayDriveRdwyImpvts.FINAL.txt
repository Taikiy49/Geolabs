SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The exploratory borings performed at the project site generally encountered a
surface fill layer about 10 to 20 feet thick underlain by medium stiff to very stiff alluvial
deposit extending to the maximum explored depth of 26 5 feet The fill layer consisted of
asphaltic concrete about 6 to 8 inches thick medium dense to dense sandy gravel soft to
stiff clay and silty clay with gravel and boulders The alluvium was comprised of silty clay
and clay with sand gravel and cobbles We encountered groundwater in two of the drilled
borings at depths of about 12 5 and 21 feet below the existing ground surface at the time
of our field exploration These groundwater depths correspond to approximately
19 5 and 11 feet MSL respectively
Roadway distress was observed at two areas along the downslope Makai side of
Kaneohe Bay Drive Several permanent wall systems including conventional cantilever
concrete and gravity were evaluated for the new wall structure However due to the
presence of the existing 42 inch sewer and desire to keep the road open the installation of
a drilled shaft retaining wall was recommended for the distressed areas
The retaining wall will consist of a pile cap with a short wall facing supported by
drilled shafts The critical loading condition for the drilled shaft foundation is due to lateral
loading from the earth and traffic surcharge loading Lateral load resistance for the drilled
shafts is a function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft
allowable deflection at the top of shaft and induced moment in the shaft We recommend
using 3 foot diameter drilled shafts spaced at 4 feet on center for the new walls The
drilled shafts should have minimum shaft lengths of 15 to 25 feet Non destructive integrity
testing on the production drilled shafts utilizing crosshole sonic logging techniques is
recommended
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific
design recommendations

1 1
Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering study performed
for the Kaneohe Bay Drive Roadway Improvements project in the Kaneohe area on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing field
observations and engineering analyses for the proposed project The recommendations
presented herein are intended for the design of the retaining walls at the distressed
roadway sections only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject
to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2
Project Considerations
The project site is along Kaneohe Bay Drive between Aumoana Place and
Aina Moi Place in the Kaneohe area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The Kaneohe Bay
Drive roadway consists of an asphaltic concrete paved roadway with one traffic lane in
each direction Existing guardrails were observed along the makai side of the roadway
with overhead utility lines along the mauka side of the roadway The project location and
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 and the Site Plan
Plate 2
Two areas of distress were observed along the makai side of the roadway The
first distressed area Distress Area No 1 is about 245 feet long and is between
44 431 and 44 443 Kaneohe Bay Drive Longitudinal and alligator cracks and
depressions were observed on the makai lane pavement surface The ground surface
slopes downward beyond the edge of the makai side pavement at an inclination from
about one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V to about 2H 1V An existing 3 foot wide
drain box culvert crosses Kaneohe Bay Drive near the center of the distress area
The second distressed area Distress Area No 2 is about 230 feet long and is
between 44 397 and 44 409 Kaneohe Bay Drive Numerous longitudinal cracks up to

SECTION 2 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The borings drilled at the project site generally encountered a surface fill layer
about 10 to 20 feet thick underlain by medium stiff to very stiff alluvium extending to the
maximum explored depth of 26 5 feet The fill layer included soft to stiff clayey soils
We encountered groundwater in two of the drilled borings at depths of about 12 5 and
21 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration
Roadway distress was observed at two areas along the downslope Makai side
of Kaneohe Bay Drive Remedial measures consisting of the installation of a permanent
wall system was recommended Several wall systems including conventional
cantilever concrete and gravity were evaluated for the new wall structure However
due to the presence of the existing 42 inch sewer and desire to keep the road open the
installation of a drilled shaft retaining wall was recommended for the distressed areas

Drilled Shaft Retaining Wall
Based on information provided two retaining walls designated as Wall Nos 1
and 2 about 158 and 127 feet long respectively are planned for the project Wall Nos 1
and 2 extend from about Station to 1 99 and from about Station
to 8 02 respectively The retaining wall consists of a pile cap with a short wall facing
supported by drilled shafts
General
Drilled shaft retaining walls typically consist of the installation of a series of closely
spaced drilled shafts along the retaining wall alignment The drilled shafts will serve
as retaining structures and temporary shoring would not be required during
construction
The required width needed for wall construction is relatively narrow for the drilled
shaft retaining wall compared to the conventional retaining wall system Since the
potential disturbance to the existing 42 inch diameter sewer force main is much

The design soil parameters used in the drilled shaft analysis were
back calculated from the observed failure conditions A parametric study

Foundation Settlements
Drilled shaft foundation settlement will result primarily from elastic compression of
the drilled shaft and the subgrade response We estimate that the total settlement
of the drilled shaft supported foundation to be less than 0 5 inch with differential
settlements between drilled shafts not exceeding about one half that amount We
believe that these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads
are applied
Construction Considerations
The performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures As a result of these potential
variations our representative should be present to observe the installation of drilled
shafts during construction In our opinion the following may have a significant
impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations
Based on our field exploration the proposed wall locations may be underlain by
granular fill deposits Due to the granular nature of these materials there is a
potential for caving in of the materials during the drilling operations To reduce the
potential for significant caving in of the drilled holes temporary casing of the drilled
holes may be required during drilled shaft installation Care should be exercised
during removal of the temporary casing to reduce the potential for necking of the
drilled shaft concrete
We encountered cobbles and boulders in the borings at the wall locations at the
time of our field exploration Therefore the drilled shaft contractor should have the
appropriate equipment and drilling tools to drill through these obstructions where
encountered
Workmanship
The load carrying capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes

Non Destructive Integrity Testing
Based on the critical nature of the drilled shaft foundations for the new wall
structure we recommend conducting non destructive integrity testing on the
production drilled shafts for the project Crosshole Sonic Logging CSL is one of
the non destructive integrity testing methods that have been gaining widespread
use and acceptance for integrity testing of drilled shafts
Crosshole Sonic Logging techniques are based on the propagation of sound waves
through concrete In general the actual velocity of sound wave propagation in
concrete is dependent on the concrete material properties geometry of the element
and wavelength of the sound waves When ultrasonic frequencies are generated
Pressure P waves and Shear S waves travel though the concrete If anomalies
are contained in the concrete the anomalies will reduce the P wave travel velocity
in the concrete Anomalies in the drilled shaft concrete may include soil particles
gravel water voids contaminated concrete and highly segregated constituent
particles
The transit time of an ultrasonic P wave signal may be measured between an
ultrasonic transmitter and receiver in two parallel water filled access tubes placed
into the concrete during construction The P wave velocity can be obtained by
dividing the measured transit time from the distance between the transmitter and
receiver Therefore anomalies may be detected if they exist
In general the access tubes should be securely attached to the interior of the
reinforcing cage as near to parallel as possible in the drilled shaft We recommend
casting a minimum of three access tubes into the concrete of the 3 foot diameter
drilled shafts as shown on Plate 5

In addition the access tubes should extend from the bottom of the drilled shaft
reinforcing cage to at least 3 5 feet above the top of the shaft It is imperative that
joints required to achieve the full length of the access tubes are watertight The
contractor is responsible for taking extra care to prevent damage of the access
tubes during the placement of the reinforcing cage into the drilled hole The tubes
should be filled with potable water as soon as possible after concrete placement
but water filling of the access tubes should not be later than 4 hours after the
concrete placement Subsequently the top of the access tubes should be capped
with watertight caps
The Crosshole Sonic Logging CSL test of drilled shafts should be conducted after
at least seven days of curing time but no later than 28 days after concrete
placement In addition the CSL test of drilled shafts should be performed in general
accordance with ASTM D 6760 In the event that a drilled shaft is found to have
significant anomalies and or is suspected to be defective based on the CSL testing
and or field observations the drilled shaft should be cored to evaluate the integrity
of the concrete in the drilled shaft The coring location within the drilled shaft should
be determined by our representative who should be present to observe the
installation of the drilled shafts After completion of the crosshole sonic logging of
the drilled shafts all the access tubes should be filled with grout of the same
strength as the drilled shaft concrete
