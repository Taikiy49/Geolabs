SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the subsurface conditions at the majority of the site 
generally consisted of a relatively thin mantle of stiff to hard clay soils overlying competent 
coralline materials at depths ranging from about 1.0 and 6.0 feet BGS with an average 
thickness of about 3 feet.  The coral formation at the project site appears to be moderately 
to highly weathered and soft to medium hard in nature. However, it should be noted that 
the strength characteristics of coral formation could be highly variable. In addition, the 
depositional nature of the coral formation may create localized areas of hard, crystalline 
coral formation intermixed with soft, severely fractured detritus (debris). 
Based on our test pit exploration, the site is mantled by expansive clay soils. 
These soils have a high shrink-swell potential when allowed to dry and then are 
rehydrated.  This shrink-swell potential can result in structural damages to buildings, 
pavements and other site improvements unless appropriate measures are implemented 
to control the shrinking and swelling.   Care should be taken to allow for a minimum of 
3 feet of select non-expansive material below finish subgrade level in structural and 
pavement areas.  
Based on our exploration and analyses, we recommend using shallow foundation 
systems, such as spread footing foundations or thickened edge slabs to support the lightly 
loaded buildings planned for the proposed development.  An allowable bearing pressure of 
up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of the footings bearing on compacted fill 
placed in accordance with the recommendations presented herein. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age. 
The rocks of the Waianae Volcanic Series are divided into Lower, Middle and 
Upper Members.  The lavas of the Lower Member consist largely of thinly bedded 
pahoehoe flows whereas the Middle Member consists of interbedded pahoehoe and a`a 
flows with a higher occurrence of the a`a form.  The Upper Member consists primarily of 
massive a`a lava flows.  
Erosion deeply dissected the Waianae Shield removing much of the western 
slope of the dome resulting in broad amphitheater headed valleys. During the 
Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of continental 
glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill the 
oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.  When the 
glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of 
the calcareous reef materials. 
The project site is at the foot of Puu o Hulu Kai and Puu o Hulu Uka which are 
elevational prominences on an erosional remnant ridge from the western flank of the 
Waianae Range.  Chemical and mechanical weathering of the volcanic rocks of the 
ridge has resulted in the deposition of an apron of colluvium and alluvium along the 
pediment of the ridge.  These deposits generally consist of remnant boulders and highly 
expansive clay.  In the lower areas where the topographic gradient decreases, the clay 
soils frequently mantle the underlying coral formation.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by excavation of 
12 test pits, designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 through 12, inclusive, extending to depths 
ranging between 1.5 and 7.0 feet below the existing ground surface.  All test pits were 
terminated at refusal on the underlying coralline limestone formation.   The approximate 
test pit locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, our field exploration encountered a surface layer of dark brown 
expansive silty clay soil approximately 2 to 4 feet thick overlying coralline materials 
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 7 feet below the existing 
ground surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the test pits at the time of our field 
exploration.  However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other 
factors.

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code, 2006 Edition, the project site may be 
subject to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed. 
The following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity and soil profile type for 
seismic design. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during

Page 7

volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so 
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of 
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of 
Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of 
Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands 
of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, small 
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of 
1938 (M6.8). Some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a 
result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 (M7+). 
In the last 150 years of recorded history, we are not aware of earthquakes greater 
than Magnitude 6 that have occurred on the Island of Oahu. An earthquake of 
Magnitude 4.8 to 5.0 occurred along the Diamond Head Fault in 1948 on the Island 
of Oahu. The moderate tremor resulted in broken store windows, ruptured building 
walls, and broken underground water mains.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the subsurface conditions at the majority of the 
site generally consisted of a relatively thin mantle of stiff to hard clay soils overlying 
competent coralline materials at depths ranging between about 1.0 and 6.0 feet below 
the existing ground surface (BGS) with an average thickness of about 3 feet.  The coral 
formation at the project site appears to be moderately to highly weathered and soft to 
medium hard in nature. However, it should be noted that the strength characteristics of 
coral formation could be highly variable. In addition, the depositional nature of the coral 
formation may create localized areas of hard, crystalline coral formation intermixed with 
soft, severely fractured detritus (debris). 
The surface expansive silty clay soil has a significant potential for possible 
distresses to proposed structures, pavements and other site improvements.  This 
potential may be reduced by the use of a minimum of 3 feet of capping fill over the 
expansive clay soils.   The capping fill should consist of select non-expansive material, 
such as crushed coral or basalt. 
Based on our exploration and analyses, shallow foundation systems, such as 
spread footing foundations or thickened edge slabs should be used to support the lightly 
loaded buildings planned for the proposed educational development.  An allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of the footings bearing 
on compacted fill placed in accordance with the recommendations presented herein.

surface improvements resulting from the existing expansive surface soils, it is our 
recommendation that the project design allow for a minimum of 3 feet of non-expansive 
select granular fill below finish subgrade level in structural and pavement areas.  
Alternatives for achieving this separation are discussed above in “General Discussion”.

to observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the scarification 
process and to confirm whether the exposed soil and/or rock conditions are similar to 
those encountered in our field exploration.

3.2.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. As discussed above in “Site Description”, there 
are existing boulder piles, refuse and other miscellaneous materials on the site 
which may present difficulties during site preparation. 
Vegetation, debris, deleterious and other unsuitable materials should be removed 
and disposed of properly off-site. Soft and/or yielding areas encountered during 
clearing and grubbing should be over-excavated to expose firm material, and the 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with compacted fills. The cleared and 
grubbed material, generally the upper 12 inches of the soil profile, should be 
properly disposed of off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate.  
Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for 
over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in any 
over-excavation required during the site preparation work. 
After clearing and grubbing, the surface soils should be scarified to a depth of about 
12 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture, 
and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of 
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
If native coralline materials and/or coral formation are exposed at the subgrade 
level, the subgrade should be proof-rolled with a large vibratory drum roller 
(minimum 10 tons static weight, e.g., Bomag 40 Series or Dynapac 250) a 
minimum of eight passes to help detect and collapse near-surface cavities and/or

3.2.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, we believe that excavated on-site clayey soils (without deleterious 
materials and/or over-sized materials) may be reused as a source of general or 
non-structural fill materials. If required, imported general fill materials should consist 
of materials with particle sizes of 6 inches or less in maximum dimension and 
should be free of deleterious materials. The material should have a laboratory 
California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 8 or higher and a swell potential of 
2 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883.  
A minimum of 3 feet of non-expansive, select granular fill materials (structural fill) 
will be required under building slabs, exterior flatwork, and pavement sections 
unless coral formation is encountered at a shallower depth below finish subgrade 
elevation. Excavated on-site coralline materials, if available, may be reused as a 
source of non-expansive, select granular fill materials, if they are processed to meet 
the above requirements. Geolabs should test the imported fill materials a minimum 
of 10 days prior to being transported to the project site for the intended use. 
Imported materials, such as crushed coral or basalt, may be used for structural fill. 
The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no greater 
than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. In addition, the material should

3.2.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fill, structural fills, and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to about 
2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches 
in loose thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D 1557.  Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density.  We wish to emphasize that moisture 
conditioning of the fill materials is an integral part of the fill placement 
recommendations provided herein. Compaction should be accomplished by 
sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction 
equipment. 
Base course, structural fill and select borrow subbase materials should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction.  
Because moisture-conditioning and compaction of the subgrade soils are critical 
elements of earthwork, observations and soil density tests should be performed by 
Geolabs during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree 
of compaction and the proper moisture content.  Where compaction is less than 
required, additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture 
content as necessary, to obtain the specified compaction.  It should be noted that 
the moisture requirement of the fills and subgrades (at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture) is an important requirement for the use of the on-site clayey

3.2.4 Cavities and Voids 
Native coralline materials and coral formations generally are highly variable in terms 
of hardness, weathering, and fracturing. In many cases, cavities and voids in the 
coral formation may be filled with loose coralline deposits and/or clayey alluvial 
soils. In areas where coralline formation is anticipated near the ground surface, 
special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation to reduce the 
potential for distress to the future improvements. 
Based on the field exploration and our experience in the area, cavities and/or voids 
are commonly encountered in the coralline deposits in the project vicinity. To 
reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities 
below foundations, we recommend implementing a program of cavity probing and 
grouting of the structure foundations during construction. 
For estimating purposes, we recommend drilling probe holes at each isolated 
spread footing location (one probe hole for every 50 square feet of footing area) 
and at 10 feet on-center along wall foundations or continuous strip footings. The 
probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 
10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation elevation. 
If cavities and/or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation, 
additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the 
vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and/or void. The probe holes and discovered 
cavities should be backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM). The 
CLSM should be injected at low to moderate pressures (the CLSM should be 
capable of being pumped through a tremie pipe). As an alternate to CLSM, 
sand-cement grout with a slump of about 6 to 9 inches also may be used for the 
grouting operations. 
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and/or voids at the site, the probe 
drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting operations are 
completed. The contractor also should be made aware that a longer lag time

between probing/grouting operations and foundation construction might be required 
in the construction schedule. 
In areas where the excavated subgrade exposes coral formation, the excavated 
subgrade should be proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller 
(e.g., Bomag 40 Series or Dynapac 250) to detect the presence of potential 
near-surface cavities, sinkholes, or loose zones. The vibratory drum roller should 
be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. The proof-rolling operations 
should be performed in the presence of a Geolabs representative. If the 
proof-rolling operations detect a near-surface cavity or loose zones, the area should 
be ripped open, cleaned of the debris, and then backfilled with compacted fill 
materials or lean concrete. Therefore, contract documents should include additive 
and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to 
account for variations in the over-excavation quantities.  
The probing and grouting recommendation is considered to be essential for the 
proposed two-story structure.  It may be considered to be optional for the light 
one-story structures provided that the structural area is proofrolled; and, the owner 
is willing to accept the slight additional risk associated with the possibility of cavity 
collapse. 
3.2.5 Excavation 
The coral formation at the project area appears to be moderately to highly 
weathered and medium hard to hard in nature. It is anticipated that the majority of 
the coral formation may be excavated with heavy excavation equipment, such as 
large backhoe excavator or trenching machines. However, coral formations typically 
contain localized hard and crystallized zones. Therefore, we anticipate some 
difficult excavation conditions may arise in localized areas during construction. It is 
possible that the excavation for a portion of the utility line trenches will require the 
use of hoerams or chipping. The contractor must exercise care to avoid 
over-ripping, which would disrupt the structure of the coral formation, resulting in a 
potential loss of bearing strength for improvements in the vicinity.

3.2.6 Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, we believe that cut slopes in coral may be cut at an inclination of two 
horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or flatter. 
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter.  Slope 
benches are recommended at maximum 20-foot vertical height intervals, if 
applicable.  Fills placed on sloping ground with an inclination of 5H:1V or steeper 
should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new 
fill against sliding.  The filling operations should start at the lowest point and 
continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill 
placement recommendations. 
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Track-rolling of slopes should not be 
accepted.  In the event that over-cutting of a slope occurs, keying and benching 
requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design 
grade with sliver fills.  Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and 
slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for 
erosion of the finished slopes.

Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest

necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional 
structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement.

recommendations, we estimate that total settlements of footings supported on the 
compacted structural fill or coral formation to be on the order of less than one inch.  We 
estimate that the differential settlements between adjacent foundations may be on the 
order of about one half inch.

3.4 
Slabs-On-Grade 
Based on our field exploration, a layer of expansive clayey soils blankets the 
project site at the existing ground surface. Unless slabs-on-grade constructed above 
these expansive soils are properly designed, there is a potential for future distress to the 
lightly loaded slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils 
due to changes in the moisture content. To reduce the potential for structural distress 
resulting from swelling of the subgrade soils, we recommend properly preparing the 
subgrade soils prior to fill placement. In addition, we recommend providing a minimum 
of 3 feet of non-expansive, select granular fill materials below the building slab areas. 
The non-expansive, select granular fill also should extend beyond the perimeter of the 
building a minimum of 24 inches (or extend to the edge of the concrete sidewalk 
surrounding the building). 
In general, we recommend concrete slabs-on-grade be a minimum of 5 inches 
thick. We also recommend lightly reinforcing the building slabs-on-grades with 
reinforcing bars in lieu of the standard welded wire fabric. This is to reduce the potential 
for structural distress due to differential movements of the slabs due to the expansive 
nature of the on-site soils.

For interior building slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery 
vibration), we recommend placing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting 
of open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) below the slabs. The 
open-graded gravel cushion fill would provide uniform support of the slabs and would 
serve as a capillary moisture break. To reduce the potential for future moisture 
infiltration through the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings, we recommend 
placing an impervious moisture barrier on top of the open-graded gravel cushion fill 
layer. Flexible floor coverings, such as carpet or sheet vinyl, should be considered 
because they can better mask minor slab cracking. In addition, we recommend 
designing interior walls to incorporate some flexibility in accommodating a small amount 
of possible ground movements. 
If the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery vibration, such as 
trucks and forklifts, we recommend increasing the slab thickness to a minimum of 
6 inches and providing a 6-inch layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs in lieu of 
the 4-inch thick gravel cushion fill layer. The moisture barrier may be omitted for these 
slabs. The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled 
tightly against the slab edges with low expansion, relatively impervious soils. These 
areas should be graded to divert water away from the slabs and to reduce the potential 
for water ponding around the slabs and foundations.

be used in lieu of the drainage material.  The prefabricated drainage product should

Laboratory Tests 
Four Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to measure the liquid and plastic limits of the near-surface soils and to aid in 
the soil classification and identification. It also provides an indication of the shrink and 
swell potential of the surface soils from changes in moisture content. The test results 
are presented on Plate B-1. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D 1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships.  The test results are presented on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Two California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D 1883) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils.  Test results were also used to evaluate the quality of the materials tested for 
use as fills.  The test results are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5. 
Four one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples 
to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils under a small surcharge 
pressure. The test results are summarized on Plate B-6.
