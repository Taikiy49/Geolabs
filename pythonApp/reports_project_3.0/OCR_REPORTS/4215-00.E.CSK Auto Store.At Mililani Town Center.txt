INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed CSK Auto Store at Mililani Town Center in the Mililani area on the island of
Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our finding and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses The
recommendations presented herein are intended for design of foundations slabs on grade
site preparation and pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project site is located at the Mililani Town Center in the Mililani area on
the island of Oahu Hawaii It is proposed to construct a new one story auto parts store with
associated parking areas around the building Based on the information provided the project
site encompasses an area of approximately 17 000 square feet which includes the new
one story store and parking areas as shown on the Site Pian Plate 2 The site is currently
a parking lot with asphalt concrete pavement
The new store building will have a footprint of about 7 000 square feet Based on the
surface conditions at the site we anticipate that the finished floor of the new store will be
near the existing grades Therefore we envision that minor site grading consisting of cuts
and fills on the order of about 1 foot or less would be needed to achieve the design finished
grades Further details pertaining to the site grading were not available at the time this report
was prepared
Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project site we anticipate that the
subsoil conditions at the proposed project site may consist of some surficial fill underlain by
residual soil and saprolite deposits Special attention will be given to the foundation support
characteristics and expansion potential of the near surface materials

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was
still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava banking against its
eastern flank These banked or ponded lava formed a broad plateau referred to as the
Schofield Plateau on which the project site is located
The project area is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lava of the
Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed dense
vesicular basalt rock formation with inter bedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The
weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils which grade to saprolite with
depth In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical
weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with depth

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the subject site generally encountered very stiff to very hard
residual soils consisting of reddish brown clayey silts near the existing ground surface

Based on our field exploration and laboratory tests we believe that a shallow foundation
system consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the recompacted
near surface soils and or new compacted fills may be used to support the proposed auto
store building
Our laboratory tests indicate that the in situ clayey silts exhibit a slight to low potential
for shrinking and swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations In order to reduce the
potential for structural distress to future slabs on grade resulting from the shrinking swelling
of the in situ clayey silts we recommend that concrete slabs on grade be supported on at
least 6 inches of non expansive select granular fill materials In addition the subgrade soils
below the non expansive select granular fill materials should be properly prepared and kept
moist until covered by the concrete slab Detailed discussion of these items and our
geotechnical engineering recommendations are presented in the following sections of this
report
Foundations
Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the site we
believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the
proposed store building An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 500 pounds per square
foot psf may be used for design of foundations bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or
new compacted fills This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade If foundations are located next to utility ttenches or easements they should
be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
W O 4215 00 E Page 7
For footings bearing on the in situ soils or new fills the bottom of the footing
excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior
to placement of reinforcing steel of concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations
the soft and or loose materials should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials The over excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill materials
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing may be extended
down deeper to the underlying competent material
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings
bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new compacted fills to be less than 1 inch total
with differential settlements on the order of about 0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the building may be resisted by frictional resistance developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of
0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or new compacted
fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils
around the footings are well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the competency
of the foundation bearing materials and the required embedment depths

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed auto store
building As previously indicated our laboratory tests indicate that the clayey silts
encountered in our borings exhibit a slight to low potential for shrink swell when subjected
to moisture fluctuations To reduce the potential for structural distress of the slabs on grade
resulting from swelling of the clayey silt soils we recommend that the subgrade soils
underneath the slab areas be properly prepared and capped with at least 6 inches of
non expansive select granular fill material below the slab cushion The non expansive select
granular fill capping fill should also extend beyond the building perimeter a minimum of
24 inches
The non expansive select granular fill should be placed as soon as possible after the
subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented in the Site
Preparation section of this report The capping fill and the underlying subgrade soils should
be wetted and kept moist prior to final placement of concrete Where shrinkage cracks are
noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soils be thoroughly
moistened to close all cracks or prepared again Saturation and subsequent yielding of the
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation
of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill at no additional cost to the owner
For interior slabs such as the store slab we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick
cushion layer consisting of free draining materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation be placed below the slab above the capping fill to provide more uniform
support of the slab To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent
damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic
membrane is recommended on top of the cushion layer Two inches of moist fine sand may
be considered above the plastic membrane to provide protection of the membrane and to aid
in curing of the slab concrete
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at
least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab should
W O 4215 00 E Page 9
be backfilled tightly against the slab with relatively impervious soils on site soils and graded
to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the
foundations
Site Preparation
We envision that minimal site grading work will be required for the proposed project
construction In general we recommend that the following guidelines be used for the minor
site grading anticipated At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits
should be thoroughly cleared Vegetation debris rubbish asphalt concrete pavements
landscape islands and other unsuitable materials within the grading limits should be
removed and disposed of properly off site
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to
receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or dense
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill
The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site Existing
underground utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation
should be properly backfilled with on site fill materials moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content placed in 6 to 8 inch loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction
After clearing and demolition areas beneath the future slabs should be
over excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 6 inch thick capping layer of
non expansive select granular fill material below the slab subgrade level i e bottom of the
gravel cushion layer The non expansive select granular fill should extend a minimum of
2 feet beyond the perimeter of the proposed building
Prior to placement of the capping fill the subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth
of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction In areas where the finished
grades will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the pavement areas the compaction

requirement should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test
procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend

The capping fill recommended below slabs and imported fill materials if required

to the project site for the intended use

Corrosion Evaluation
One of the subsurface soil samples retrieved from our field exploration at the site was
subjected to chemical analyses for soluble chloride and suifate concentrations The chemical
analysis tests were performed by Environmental Laboratory of the Pacific The results of
these tests are presented on Plate B 5 of Appendix B
Based on the test results the soluble chloride and sulfate concentrations in the soil
sample tested were below the reporting limit as shown on Plate B 5 Therefore the on site
soils tested may be considered to be generally non corrosive to reinforced concrete in
contact with the soils However normal precautions to reduce the potential effects of
corrosion such as minimum concrete cover should be observed for reinforced concrete
substructures
Pavement Design
We understand that asphalt concrete pavements will be required for the parking areas
around the proposed building In general we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the
proposed store will consist primarily of passenger vehicles pick up trucks and occasional
moderately loaded trucks Our laboratory tests performed on the near surface clayey silt
soils encountered beneath the existing pavement appears to have a CBR value of at least
12 with a swell value of less than 3 percent Considering the generally light to medium traffic
anticipated for the proposed project we recommend that the following pavement design be
used for preliminary budgeting purposes
Asphaltic Concrete Pavement
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 percent relative compaction
6 0 Inch Select Borrow Subbase 95 percent relative compaction
14 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Moist Subgrade
The subgrade soils for the new pavement areas should be scarified to a depth of
about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to not
less than 95 percent relative compaction The aggregate base course should consist of
W O 4215 00 E Page 12
crushed basalt aggregate compacted not less than 95 percent relative compaction CBR and
density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during
construction to confirm the adequacy of the above section
Paved areas shouid be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during
or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent
to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be extended a minimum of 2 inches
into the subgrade soils below the aggregate base course to reduce the potential for migration
of landscape water into the pavement section Alternatively a subdrain system is
recommended to collect the excess water from landscape irrigation For long term
performance we believe that a subdrain system should be considered adjacent to the
paved landscaped areas
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines and utility connections may be required for the
proposed project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes for support Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also
be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus
reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material
to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in
settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils The backfill
should be moisture conditioned to above the optinum moisture content placed in about
8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below

Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from
the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems
be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation
and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should
also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms
and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce
the potential for water infiltration
In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain surface water run off away from the foundations and slabs

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling four
borings extending to a depth of about 16 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed on
selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The
results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on the natural samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are presented on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of this
test are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the moisture content and dry density
relationships The results of the tests are presented on Plate B 3
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed
on a bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The results of the tests are presented on Plates B 4
One set of chemical analysis tests including Chlorides EPA Test Method 325 2 and
Sulfates EPA Test Method 375 2 was performed on a selected sample of the soils to
evaluate their corrosion potential The chemical analysis tests were performed by
Environmental Laboratory of the Pacific Results of the tests are summarized on Plate B 5
