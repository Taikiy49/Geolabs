Our borings generally encountered surface fills underlain by basalt formation.  
Surface fills were generally about 5 to 6 feet thick and consisted of dense gravelly cobbles 
with some sand.  Basalt formation was encountered below the surface fills and extended 
down to the maximum depth explored of about 17 feet below the ground surface.  Basalt 
formation is generally medium hard and moderately weathered. It should be noted that 
cavities and/or voids are commonly present in the basaltic lava flows of the vicinity. 
Therefore, the presence of cavities and/or voids should be expected during construction.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the project plans provided, site grading consisting of cuts of less than about 
2 feet deep and fills of up to about 5 feet high will be required to achieve the design finished 
grades at the majority of the lower parking area. After clearing and grubbing, areas to be 
filled should be ripped using a tooth of a large bulldozer to a depth of about 3 feet below 
the existing ground surface at the commencement of grading. The purpose of ripping the 
subgrades is to assist in detecting and collapsing cavities and/or voids contained in the 
near-surface materials encountered at the project site. 
After the fill surfaces have been ripped to a depth of about 3 feet below the surface, 
the ground should be leveled with the use of a Caterpillar D-10 bulldozer or similar size 
bulldozer working the surface a minimum of four passes to provide a relatively level 
platform. After leveling the rugged surfaces with a bulldozer, we recommend proof-rolling 
the fill subgrades with a large vibratory drum roller (minimum 20 tons static weight) for a 
minimum of eight passes to help detect and collapse near-surface cavities and/or voids. 
The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute 
(3½ miles per hour).  Finished subgrades in cut areas should also be proof-rolled with a 
minimum 20-ton (static weight) vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes.

design recommendations.

The recommendations provided herein are intended for the design of site grading, 
underground utility lines, and pavements only. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an 
area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the following 
five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during the recent geologic 
time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both still active). 
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain, which makes up 
the west-central portion of the island. The lava formation encountered at the site appears to be 
of both a'a and pahoehoe type flows, which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean. 
A’a lava typically is characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface resembling a 
jagged accumulation of rock fragments, including cobbles and boulders. Pahoehoe is 
characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular 
(porous) rock. 
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas, especially pahoehoe type lava. 
Cavities form when lavas are still in a molten state, and they represent both lava tubes 
(intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form when molten 
lava drains from the cooling flow, leaving a hollow tube-like structure, which may extend for a 
large longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets generally are smaller in 
horizontal extent. 
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite 
readily in both horizontal and vertical directions. In general, water is transmitted through the 
porous rock matrix, along joints, fractures and inter-flow contacts, cavities, and along clinker 
layers. Clinker and cavities serve as the primary water transmission features because of their 
high permeability.

3.1.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. 
Loose and/or soft soils, where encountered, should be over-excavated to expose 
firm and/or dense materials, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted fills. The contractor should exercise caution while clearing and 
grading near collapsed lava tube features and/or cavities, which may represent 
potential lava tubes or cavities that may further collapse under a load of heavy 
construction equipment. 
After clearing and grubbing, areas to be filled should be ripped using a tooth of a 
large bulldozer to a depth of about 3 feet below the existing ground surface at the 
commencement of grading. The purpose of ripping the subgrades is to assist in 
detecting and collapsing cavities and/or voids contained in the near-surface 
materials encountered at the project site.

3.1.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, we recommend capping the fill areas of the project site with a minimum 
2-foot thick layer of structural fill materials. Structural fill materials should consist 
of well-graded granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum 
dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted 
mass. In addition, structural fill materials should contain less than 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve by weight. The excavated on-site materials, if 
less than 6 inches in maximum dimension, may be used as a source of structural 
fill, provided that they are processed to meet the above gradation requirements for 
structural fill. 
Fill and backfill materials below the minimum 2-foot thick structural fill layer may 
consist of general fill materials. General fill and backfill materials should consist of 
well-graded granular materials, the majority of which are less than 12 inches, with 
an absolute maximum dimension of 18 inches. Materials between 12 and

Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to 
at least 95 percent relative compaction. General fills and backfills should be placed

the optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed 
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage

Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of rocks, 
boulders, and/or cobbles. Instead, a testing program to evaluate the number of 
passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be 
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a 
Geolabs representative. Based on this testing program, the number of passes then 
may be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction.  
For preliminary estimating purposes, compaction may be accomplished by using 
a large vibratory drum roller having a static weight of at least 20 tons traveling at a 
speed of about 300 feet per minute (about 3½ miles per hour). The roller should 
pass over the fill surface at least eight times for structural fill and six times for 
general fill. For general fill, a Caterpillar D-10 bulldozer or similar-size equipment 
may be used in lieu of the vibratory drum roller. 
Select granular fill materials required for the project should be placed in level lifts 
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. The compaction requirement for the last lift of fill (finished subgrade) 
below areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be increased to at least 95 percent 
relative compaction. Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, 
vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water 
tamping, jetting, or ponding should not be allowed to compact the fills.

3.1.5 Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, we believe the cut slopes planned at the site exposing fill materials may 
be designed with a slope inclination of two horizontal to one vertical (2H:1V) or 
flatter. In areas where dense basalt formation is exposed at the cut slope faces, 
the cut slopes may be steepened to inclinations of 1H:1V or flatter.  Where cut 
slopes with inclinations steeper than 2H:1V are planned, provisions for slope 
protection measures, such as placement of grouted rubble paving (GRP) or 
construction of retaining walls, should be considered in the project design in the 
event dense basalt formation is not exposed at the cut slope face.  A Geolabs 
representative should observe the conditions of the finished cut slope face and 
evaluate the need for slope protection measures. 
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the general fill materials recommended herein 
may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. In addition, fills placed 
on slopes steeper than 5H:1V should be benched. The fill slope face should be 
finished to a relatively smooth and well-compacted surface. The fill slope face 
should be free of large boulders (greater than 18 inches in largest dimension) and 
should be free of voids, which would allow erosion and migration of fines to occur. 
The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level 
horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement 
recommendations. Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back 
to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. In addition, slope

Underground Utility Lines 
We envision that new underground utility lines will be required for the project 
development. We anticipate most of the trenches for the utility lines will be excavated in 
the compacted fill and basalt formation encountered in our borings.

(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) should be provided below the pipes for uniform bearing 
support. Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33,

for the formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support 
for the sides of the pipes. Improper backfill material around the pipes and improper 
placement of the backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage.

12 inches above the top of the pipes) may consist of 1-inch minus, well-graded granular

the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well-graded granular materials 
less than 6 inches in maximum particle size. The backfill should be moisture-conditioned 
to above the optimum moisture, placed in about 8-inch level loose lifts, and mechanically 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Where trenches are located in 
paved areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be 
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. 
Some of the trench work for utility installation may involve excavation in the basaltic 
rock formation and may require hard ripping or the use of hoerams. The contractor must 
exercise care to avoid over-ripping, which would disrupt the structure of the rock formation 
and result in a potential loss of bearing strength for footings in its vicinity.

Percolation Testing 
Two percolation tests were performed in selected borings in general accordance 
with the procedures outlined by the Department of Health for the design of individual 
wastewater systems (IWS). 
The percolation tests were performed in our borings at a depth of about 5 feet from 
the existing ground surface. Approximately 1 inch of gravel was placed at the bottom of 
the boring to protect the bottom from scouring and sediments. 
During testing, the test holes were filled with water and refilled as necessary. The 
time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which the 
calculation of the percolation rates was based.  It should be noted that due to the relatively 
high permeability of the subsurface materials, water build-up was not measured in the 
test holes.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling 
six borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 106, extending to depths of about 5 to 
17 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted 
drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis.  These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6. 
Soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number 
of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be 
factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond 
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow 
bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is 
mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3.  The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (September 1977).  
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from 
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of material is 
recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown 
on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.
