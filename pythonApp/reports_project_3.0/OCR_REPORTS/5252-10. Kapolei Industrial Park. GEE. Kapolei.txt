REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to

early Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows
from the Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau
now known as the Schofield Plateau The project site is located on the southwestern flank
of the Waianae Volcano
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau
generated sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project
site and to the south these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine
sediments and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of
the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway
to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the
confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys
in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous
reef materials Placement of man made fills and commercial development of the area
within the last 20 years have brought the project area to its present form

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
As mentioned previously field exploration consisting of drilling and sampling
borings was not conducted for this due diligence study Instead research and review of
available soil and boring data in the vicinity of the project site was performed in order to
evaluate the subsurface conditions at the proposed development site Based on
available information and our experience in the area the native soils across the project
site generally consist of coralline deposits and alluvial soils The limits of the coralline
deposits and alluvial soils are difficult to define due to the irregular depositional nature of
the near surface materials However based on available geologic maps the approximate
original limits prior to mass grading of the site of the alluvium and coralline materials are
shown on the Soil Unit Map Plate 4 The primary surface soils are classified in the Soil
Survey of Islands of Molokai and Lanai State of Hawaii issued
August 1972 by the U S Department of Agriculture Soil Conservation Service and are
described as follows
Coral Outcrop CR
Coral outcrop consists of coral or cemented calcareous sand formed in shallow
ocean water during the time the ocean stand was at a higher level It is exposed
on the ocean shore on the coastal plains and at the foot of the uplands Coral
outcrop makes up about 80 to 90 percent of the acreage The remaining 10 to
20 percent consists of a thin layer of friable red soil material in cracks crevices
and depressions within the coral outcrop
Mamala Stony Silty Clay Loam 0 to 12 Percent Slopes MnC
This type of soil was formed in alluvium deposited over coral limestone and
consolidated calcareous sand It is dark reddish brown in color neutral to mildly
alkaline with moderate permeability Runoff is very slow to medium and the
erosion hazard is slight to moderate
The coral formation in the project area generally appears to be moderately
weathered and medium hard to hard in nature It should be noted that the strength
characteristics of coral formation are highly variable In addition the depositional nature of
the coral formation may create localized areas of hard crystalline coral intermixed with
soft severely fractured detritus debris It should be noted that coral formation is
frequently cavernous or contain pockets of voids Therefore there exists the potential for
the presence of cavities and or voids within the coral formation across the project site In
addition cavities in the coral formation could be filled with soft and or loose coralline
detritus and alluvial deposits

Undocumented Fills and Stockpiles
Based on our field reconnaissance a relatively large stockpile embankment of soils
and or debris was observed within the project limits of the development site In addition
other areas of undocumented fills may be present across the project site and may not be
exposed until additional exploration and or construction is underway
It was not known whether the existing stockpile embankment or other
undocumented fill areas were properly constructed in controlled lifts and compacted with
the required moisture content to the specified degree of compaction However we
envision that the existing stockpile embankment will need to be removed in order to
achieve the finished grades for the proposed development

determined in the field during construction by the project geotechnical engineer The
bottom of the over excavated level should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction prior to the placement of fill materials
Cavities and Voids
As mentioned previously coral formations are generally highly variable in terms of
hardness weathering and fracturing In addition cavities and or voids are commonly
encountered in coral formations in the vicinity of the project site In many cases cavities
and voids in the coral formation may be filled with loose coralline deposits In areas where
coralline formation is anticipated near the ground surface special attention should be
given to the subgrade preparation to reduce the potential for distress to the future
improvements such as floor slabs and pavements
The excavated subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches where
possible moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and proof rolled
with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller to detect the presence of potential
near surface cavities sinkholes or loose zones The vibratory drum roller should be
operated at a speed of about 200 to 300 feet per minute If the proof rolling operations
detect a near surface cavity or loose zones the area should be ripped open cleaned of
the debris and then backfilled with compacted fill materials or lean concrete

Deep Shrinkage Cracks
Our field observations and experience in the area indicate that shrinkage cracks
are commonly present at the existing ground surface underlain by the alluvial soils as
described previously In general shrinkage cracks will develop in the ground due to the
lack of maintenance of the soil moisture content as the surface soils start to dry out
under the sun and dry weather conditions The development of shrinkage cracks in the
ground is apparently intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions characteristic of
the Ewa area resulting in large fluctuations of the soil moisture content
If the existing ground cracks are left unattended for an extended period of time
and are allowed to penetrate deeper below the surface the ground cracks could
adversely affect the performance of the future building foundations The ground cracks
could serve as conduits for water to percolate into the foundation soils and cause loss of
strength of the foundation soils resulting in potential settlement of the building structure
In addition migration of the foundation soils into the openings of the shrinkage cracks
could undermine the foundation system and result in potential settlement of the building
structure Therefore remedial work to correct or mitigate the observed ground cracks
should be implemented prior to fill placement and construction of the proposed
structures
After clearing and grubbing we believe that the proposed building and pavement
footprints should be over excavated by about 3 feet below the existing ground surface
to remove the zone with shrinkage cracks The over excavation also should extend
laterally about 5 feet beyond the building and driveway limits If walkways are planned
around the building structures the over excavation should extend laterally by about
5 feet beyond the outside edge of the walkways
The over excavated subgrades and other on grade areas outside building and
pavement footprints should then be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum
roller for about six to eight passes to assist in detecting and collapsing near surface

Subsequent to the proof rolling operations the subgrades should then be

percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrades we
recommend that the subgrade soil be prepared again as recommended above
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted fill The need for over excavation due to soft subgrade soil conditions
should be evaluated in the field by the project geotechnical engineer
Because the development of shrinkage cracks is largely due to fluctuations in the
moisture content of the on site expansive soils we believe that the efforts should be
directed at maintaining the soil moisture content at a relatively constant level after
preparation and compaction These long term solutions may include landscaping
installation of concrete walkways around the building structures and or placement of
non expansive fills in the yard areas
Expansive Soil Conditions
Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project site the on site alluvial soils
may exhibit moderate to high shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture
fluctuations As mentioned previously the native alluvial soils were observed at the
project site in some areas In addition alluvial soils may have been used as fill materials
during the initial mass grading operations for the Kapolei Business Park development
The lateral extent and thickness of the alluvial soils should be delineated during the
design phase of each individual lot development by conducting a detailed field exploration
program Furthermore the expansive potential of the alluvial soils also should be
evaluated during the design phase Where expansive soils are encountered
over excavation of the alluvial soils may be necessary under foundations slabs on grade
and pavements We estimate that over excavation depth on the order of about 12 to
24 inches may be required in areas underlain by the expansive soils The resulting
over excavation would need to be backfilled with non expansive select granular fill

Lot Drainage
It should be emphasized that proper surface drainage especially within the lots is
critical to the performance of foundations and slabs constructed on expansive soil
conditions Therefore the finished grades outside the building structure should be
sloped to shed water away from the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It
is also advised that gutter systems be installed around the building and that the discharge
be diverted away from the foundation and pavement areas
Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs also should be
avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to reduce the potential for appreciable water infiltration into the subsurface soils
Drainage swales with positive drainage should be provided as soon as possible and
maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation systems because ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled
against the footings immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for
appreciable water infiltration into the subsurface
Additional Exploration

grading and infrastructure installation for the proposed development I n addition a
detailed field exploration and geotechnical engineering analysis should be conducted for
the design of the fine grading and structure foundations during the individual lot
development
