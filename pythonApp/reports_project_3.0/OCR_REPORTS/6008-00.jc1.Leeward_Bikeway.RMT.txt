SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, the proposed gulch/stream crossing sites are underlain by highly variable 
subsurface soil profile, consisting of highly compressible soft marsh/alluvium deposits, 
stiff/dense alluvium and colluvium deposits, and basalt formation.  We believe that excessive 
settlement and bearing failure due to the highly compressible soft soil and scour action are 
critical bridge foundation design considerations. 
We recommend using a deep foundation system consisting of 16.5-inch octagonal 
precast prestressed concrete piles to support the bridge structures.  The driven piles would 
derive its vertical support primarily from skin friction between the pile and the surrounding 
stiff alluvium and from end-bearing on the dense cobbles and boulders and/or basalt 
formation encountered at the project sites.  Based on our analyses, the concrete piles would 
need to extend to depths of 60 to 100 feet below the ground surface in order to achieve an 
ultimate compressive load capacity of 400 kips per pile. 
We believe that the abutment fills will result in appreciable consolidation settlement 
from thick highly compressible soft lagoonal and/or marsh deposits. We recommend 
supporting the concrete approach slabs on the driven pile foundation. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and 
recommendations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu is generally composed of the weathered and dissected 
remnants of two extinct shield (domed) volcanoes – Waianae and Koolau. The older 
Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of the western third of Oahu’s landmass while the 
younger Koolau Volcano forms a major portion of the eastern two-thirds of Oahu’s 
landmass. Generally, it is believed that the Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes began 
erupting on the order of about 20 to 25 million years ago as deep ocean submarine 
volcanoes. Subaerial volcanic eruptions on the Island of Oahu ended approximately 2.2 
to 3.5 million years ago in the Pleistocene Epoch. Furthermore, the Waianae Volcano 
became extinct while the Koolau Volcano remained active; therefore, the eastern flank of 
Waianae Volcano is partially overlapped and buried below the younger Koolau Volcano 
lavas near the central interior portion of the Island of Oahu. 
In addition to the primary volcanic composition of the Island of Oahu, other 
secondary and generally more localized geologic earth materials comprise the lowland, 
coastal, and valley portions of the island. The secondary earth materials include various 
alluvial and colluvial deposits, oceanic and marine sediments, and submerged and 
emerged coralline reef materials. 
The proposed bike path traverses along the Waipahu area, through the lower distal 
flanks of the Koolau Mountain Ranges (Ewa Plain).  The Ewa Plain is a gently sloping 
land surface formed by the long-term accumulation of alluvial sediments consisting mainly 
of terrigenous clays and silts with some embedded cobbles and boulders derived from 
the weathering and decomposition of basaltic rock residing further upslope. Some 
pockets of marine sediments and coralline detritus materials were intercalated within the 
alluvial sediments as a result of past sea-level fluctuations. 
The Ewa Plain is dissected by various drainage gullies, which emanate in the 
upland areas and discharge at the Middle and East Lochs of Pearl Harbor. With the 
passage of time, some of the incised drainage channels meandered in course and were 
filled-in by more recent soft alluvial sediments. The deeper basaltic rock of the Koolau

The subsurface geological conditions along the bike path reflect past glacio-eustatic 
sea-level fluctuations, which occurred most substantially during the Pleistocene Epoch. 
The following paragraphs provide a generalized description of the major processes 
that contributed to the geomorphic evolution of the Island of Oahu. 
The Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes formed during the late Pliocene and 
early Pleistocene Epochs by the sequential deposition of basaltic lava flows. As 
mentioned previously, the main shield-building activity ceased about 2.2 to 3.5 million 
years ago. Evidence from previous deep drilled wells indicates that the Island of Oahu 
has subsided by as much as 1,200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity 
(Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During that period of slow island subsidence, coral-algal reefs grew and fossilized 
along the southern coast of the Island of Oahu. As a result, broad embayments formed, 
which were bounded by fringing barrier reefs whose growth essentially kept pace with the 
slow rate of island subsidence. Thereafter, a series of shallow lagoons formed behind the 
barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated within the lagoons 
(Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (most recent Ice Age), many sea-level fluctuations 
occurred as a result of the waxing and waning of global continental glaciation. As the 
great continental glaciers accumulated, the global level of the oceans fell because less 
water was available to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded 
(melted), the global level of the oceans rose because of the water influx. The landmass 
elevation of the Island of Oahu remained essentially stable during these sea-level 
changes; therefore, the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. The glacio-eustatic 
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were either higher or lower relative to the 
present sea-level conditions.  The following are some generalized examples of the 
geomorphic effects that resulted from the changes in sea level relative to the island 
landmass.

The higher sea-level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and depositional 
fans composed of terrigenous sediment at the heads of old bays. Furthermore, the higher 
sea stands caused accumulation of coralline reef materials at the corresponding higher 
elevations and the deposition of lagoonal and marine sediments in quiet backwaters 
protected from wave action by fringing coral reefs. 
The lower sea-level stands caused streams to incise erosional valleys into the 
existing sediments and reef platforms. In addition, the subaerial exposure of the marine 
sediments and calcareous materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials 
and/or lagoonal deposits and hardening of the calcareous reef materials. Finally, renewed 
subaerial erosion (acting on the upper regions of the volcanic dome) caused the 
mobilization and deposition of terrigenous alluvial soils within relatively high energy 
depositional environments, which is typified by a poorly sorted mix of alluvial materials 
with a greater range in particle sizes. 
During periods of no significant sea level change, continued stream flow extended 
the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and allowed the deposition of alluvium overlying 
pre-existing lagoonal sediments. 
Land development within the last 100 years along the proposed bike path has 
brought the area to its present form. Agricultural land use and other development projects 
have resulted in alterations to the natural terrain including the placement of earth fills, 
which may be of poor quality in terms of supporting large structural loads.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the results of the field exploration, the subsurface conditions underlying 
the bridge crossings consisted of highly variable soil and rock formation, including fill, 
compressible soft marsh/lagoonal/alluvium deposits, stiff alluvium and dense colluvium, 
and basalt formation.  
We believe that excessive settlement and/or bearing failure due to the soft 
compressible soil and scour are critical bridge foundation design considerations.  To 
address these conditions, we recommend using a deep foundation system, such as 
16.5-inch octagonal driven precast prestressed concrete piles, to support the proposed 
bridge crossings. 
Driven concrete piles are recommended to support Bridge Nos. 6 and 7 structures 
at the Waikele and Kapakahi Stream Crossing.  We recommend extending the piles to 
the depths between 60 and 100 feet below the ground to provide an ultimate compressive 
load capacity of about 400 kips.  The recommended pile compressive load capacity from 
the skin friction and end-bearing on the cobbles and boulders and/or weathered basalt 
formation. 
We believe that the abutment fills will result in appreciable consolidation settlement 
from thick highly compressible soft lagoonal and/or marsh deposits. We recommend 
supporting the concrete approach slabs on driven pile foundations. 
Pile Foundations 
Based on the preliminary bridge structural loads provided and the highly 
compressible lagoonal and/or marsh deposits encountered in the borings drilled at Bridge 
Nos. 6 and 7, we believe that a shallow foundation would not be able to provide sufficient 
support for the new bridge structures without appreciable ground settlements. 
To reduce the potential for appreciable damage to the bridge structures resulting 
from ground settlements, we recommend using a deep foundation system consisting of 
driven piles to support the bridge structures.  We recommend using 16.5-inch octagonal

Ground Settlement 
Based on the current design scheme, we understand that concrete approach slabs 
will be designed to achieve grading requirements meeting ADA standards at Bridge Nos. 
6 and 7 sites.  One consideration is to fill the subgrade of the approach slabs.  We envision 
that the amount of the fill may be in the order of 2 to 5 feet. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings, the project sites 
are underlain by 20 to 55-foot thick highly compressible soft lagoonal and/or marsh 
deposits.  The new fill will apply a surcharge load on the thick compressible soft soil layers 
resulting in appreciable consolidation settlement and may adversely damage the 
underground utilities in the vicinity.  Based on our consolidation evaluation, we estimate 
that approximately 5 to 10 inches of the ground settlement may be induced by 2 to 5 feet 
of fill at the Waikele Stream Bridge (Bridge No. 6), while about 6 to 20 inches may occur 
at the Kapakahi Stream Bridge (Bridge No. 7).  Details of the consolidation analysis are

slabs, the upper 12 inches of the backfill should consist of relatively impervious material

retaining structure. In addition, the backfill below the drainage outlet (or weepholes) 
should consist of the relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for water 
infiltration into the footing subgrade. The relatively impervious material should be 
compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 11 through 14, extending to depths of about 116.5 to 150 feet 
below the existing ground surface. We drilled the borings using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis.  These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1.  Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-4.5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in 
general accordance with ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by 
driving a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  
In addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.  The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used.  The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the soil sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are presented on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the coralline formation materials encountered at the project site 
were obtained by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with 
ASTM D2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling 
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the 
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores 
were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by 
the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual length 
of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For 
example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 
74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative quality 
of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any drilling, 
mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is 
recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe 
the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of 
Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties.  The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Sixteen Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Four Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on 
Plate B-3. 
Six Consolidation tests (ASTM D2435) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the on-site soils.  The test results are 
presented on Plates B-4 through B-9. 
Nine 
Unconsolidated 
Undrained 
Triaxial 
Compression 
(TXUU) 
tests 
(ASTM D2850) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear 
strength of the clayey silt soils encountered.  The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil 
sample.  The test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-10 
through B-18. 
Sixteen Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D7012 Method C) were 
performed on selected rock cores to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the 
rock formation encountered. Uniaxial compressive strength test results for the selected 
rock cores are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths and 
on Plate B-19.
