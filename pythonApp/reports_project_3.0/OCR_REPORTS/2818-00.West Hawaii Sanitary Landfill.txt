GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site consists of approximately three hundred 300 acres of
undeveloped lava rock land situated in the Puuanahulu area of the North Kona District on
the Island of Hawaii The site is currently accessed by a single unimproved power line
maintenance road
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2818 00 Page 4
In general the terrain is extremely rugged with some steep slopes locally
approaching a ratio of 1 1 H V The terrain typically reflects the original surface of thick
basaltic lava flows The ground surface elevations range from approximately 120 feet
above Mean Sea Level MSL along the makai boundary of the site adjacent to Queen
Kaahumanu Highway to approximately 225 feet above MSL along the mauka boundary
of the site
The site is generally barren rocky land with isolated stands of vegetation that range
from sparse grasses to scattered kiawe trees
Most of the ground surface is covered with basaltic boulders cobbles and gravel
Clinker with localized exposures of very hard in situ basalt rock formation

It is anticipated that excavations of up to thirty 30 feet in depth for the landfill cells

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five 5 shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is located near the contact of Hualalai and Mauna Loa mountains
in the west central portion of the island and consists partly of the prehistoric basaltic lava
flows of the Mauna Loa Volcanic Series and partly of Hualalai Voicanic Series flows The
rock of both were most likely deposited during the late Pleistocene Epoch Stearns and
Macdonald 1946
The project site has been traversed by the historic basaltic lava flows of the 1850
and 1859 eruptions of Mauna Loa These flows form the surface of the site
At and in the area of the project site the lava flows are primarily in the a a form
which is characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly surface of fragmental clinker underlain
by a dense core of massive basalt

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our exploration and analyses it is our opinion that development of the
proposed sanitary landfill is feasible from a geotechnical engineering point of view The
clinker and basalt formation encountered at the site should provide adequate bearing for
the proposed site improvements The excavated volcanic materials should provide a
suitable source of fill material if reduced to less than 12 inches in size
Shallow spread or strip footing foundations may be used to support structures at
the project site These foundations should bear either on in situ clinker or basalt rock or
bear on fill placed and compacted in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein
Site Preparation
Generally the site is barren with virtually no vegetative cover However there may
be isolated thickets of kiawe or other scrub

W O 2818 00 Page 7
Any organically contaminated topsoil should be stripped and disposed of properly
off site or stockpiled for use in landscaping Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed
during clearing operations should be over excavated to firm ground and the resulting
excavation should be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill
Due to the dry and sometimes windy site conditions moisture conditioning of fill
material will be necessary It is also advisable to institute a dust control program during
clearing and grading operations
Generally the on site rock materials when excavated and properly processed or
broken down should be suitable for use as engineered fill If additional imported material
is required the material should conform to the requirements stipulated in Section 16 of
the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction Import Borrow dated
September 1986
Excavation
It is anticipated that much of the earthwork for the project will consist of excavation
for the landfill cells Based on our field exploration the site is underlain at relatively
shallow depths by very hard and dense basalt rock formation Material of this nature
generally can not be excavated with conventional earthwork methods We anticipate that
excavation at the project site will require hard excavation techniques which may include
but are not limited to chipping splitting and blasting
Generally the excavation method should be left to the contractor Blasting should
be considered to expedite the excavation process Generally consideration should be
given to the economics of blasting to initially reduce the size of the excavated rock
material and to facilitate excavation as opposed to extended periods of heavy ripping at
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2818 00 Page 8
high equipment rates However care should be exercised to avoid overshooting which
could disrupt the clinker or rock formation resulting in a loss of strength
Blasting Considerations
It is our opinion that the clinker and some of the rock at the site could be
excavated without the use of explosives However the progress of excavation work in
basalt formation would generally be slow and inefficient The following general blasting
discussions are presented to the designer and client for their consideration related to
excavation method in basalt formation
Hazards or objectionabie impacts from blasting operations generally originate from
one or more of the following
1 Flyrock
2 Ground Vibration
3 Air Blast
Flyrock
Flyrock is the blaster s term for flying rock or other debris which is launched into
the air during open blasting by the force of the detonating explosives These
projectiles often have tremendous kinetic energy and can crush or destroy objects
in their flight path or at their point of impact
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2818 00 Page 9
Flyrock generated during the earthwork for the proposed grading could potentially
strike nearby features If the flyrock has sufficient kinetic energy it could create
significant damage
Ground Vibration
When an explosive detonates below ground it generates intense stress waves in
the surrounding material These waves crush the rock immediately adjacent to the
explosion and permanently distorts and cracks the rock for some distance
When the intensity of the stress waves attenuates to the point that there is no
longer any permanent deformation of the rock the stress is propagated through
the rock as elastic waves These elastic waves create ground vibration
The potential of the ground vibration to damage a structure is a function of the
intensity of the vibration and the natural frequency of the structure
Air Blast
The air blast from an explosion is a compressional wave in air Noise is the
portion of the air blast with frequencies between 20 and and
concussion is the portion with frequencies below 20 Hz
The effects of air blast can be a consideration for much greater distances
depending upon atmospheric and or other conditions
if blasting is to be used for the earthwork it should be performed in accordance
with the applicable statutes rules and regulations and only by a qualified operator
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2818 00 Page 10
with a proven record We recommend the prequalification of contractors and
blasting operators bidding on the project
We also recommend a program of test shots with instrumentation by seismographs
or other appropriate means be considered by the grading contractor The
program should start with large scaled distances and gradually decrease to
determine the lowest allowable scaled distance
Potential Use of Excavated Rock Material
Based on our visual examination of the rock cores and on the high rock quality
designations of the recovered cores it appears that it may be possible to utilize the
excavated rock material as source of base course or as concrete rock aggregate if the
material is properly crushed and processed It is recommended that additional testing be
conducted to further examine the rock for these potential uses Such additional testing
should include resistance to abrasion specific gravity absorption and sulfate soundness
Fill Placement
In general the excavated rock materials up to one to two foot sizes may be
considered as a source of general embankment fill material provided that a choking layer
is incorporated above the lower rock fill and that the upper two 2 feet of the fill is
capped off with 3 inch maximum size well graded granular materials Also the above 3
inch minus size well graded material should be used as a source of trench and structural
backfill if needed
Serious consideration should be given to mobilizing a crusher to the site to aid in
reducing the size of the anticipated oversized excavated material It is possible that
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2818 00 Page 11
portions of the dense ledges could yield aggregate quality material if properly segregated
and crushed
If the volcanic rock materials encountered at the site are processed and reduced
to a relatively well graded select granular material with an average maximum size of about
six 6 inches and an absolute maximum size of twelve 12 inches the fills and
embankments may generally be considered to be equivalent to earthfill In this case no
special choking layer would be required if some losses of topsoil are considered
acceptable
Generally fills not consisting of rock material required should be placed in level lifts
not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90
maximum dry density as established by ASTM D 1557 78 Trench and structural backfills
should be placed in level lifts not exceeding eight 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 maximum dry density per ASTM Test Method
D 1557 78 The upper two 2 feet of fills and backfills below road subgrade level should
be compacted to a minimum of 95 maximum dry density
Alternately the large boulders or rock fragments less than two 2 feet in diameter
could be utilized as boulder fills or rock fills provided that they are well tracked with a
heavy bulldozer to lock the material into grain to grain contact A choking layer at least
18 inches thick for yard areas and a minimum of 24 inches thick for road or structural
areas should be used to cap the rockfill to reduce the potential of loss of fines from the
upper portion of the fill
The choking layer should consist of a relatively well graded select granular material
with an average maximum size of about six 6 inches and an absolute maximum size of

Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of

Earthwork Quantities
Based on previous experience in the vicinity of the project site it is anticipated that
the rock materials at the project site will experience a volume change when excavated
processed and used as fill material In general the net change will depend upon whether
the material is loose clinker versus dense basalt the average particle size of the fill
material and the amount of void space in the fill
Discussions with contractors whom have worked with similar materials indicate that
the volume change can range from a factor of about 0 75 for loose clinker to as high as
1 5 for massive dense basalt The increase would be the greatest when the excavation
encounters a significant amount of dense basalt and this material is used as boulder fill
Our field exploration indicates that the upper one and one half 1 5 to six and one
half 6 5 feet of the subsurface materials at the site are loose to medium dense dense
clinker Below this depth the basalt formations were generally dense to very hard to the
maximum depths drilled Using our estimate of cuts of up to thirty 30 feet in depth we
anticipate that there will be an increase in the amount of material excavated versus its
yield when used as compacted fill

Foundation Support
For the maintenance facility weigh station and administration building it is our
opinion that relatively shallow spread or strip footing foundations may be used to support
the structures It is anticipated that foundations will bear on either in situ basalt rock
formation on rockfill or on clinker
It is our understanding that the project involves the construction of two 2
reservoirs for the storage of water The locations of the proposed water reservoirs were
not available at the time of our field exploration We recommend that as the sites for the
reservoirs become accessible during grading operations borings be performed at each
reservoir location to verify site specific subsurface conditions to allow for design changes
if necessary
An allowable bearing capacity of up to 4 000 p s f may be used for the design of
foundations bearing on in situ basalt rock formation dense rockfill or compacted clinker
in level areas This value may be increased by one third when temporary wind and
seismic loads are considered
GEOLABS HAWAII

In order to provide uniform support for the foundations and to minimize the
potential of foundation distresses resulting from differential subgrade conditions between
dense rock and rock fill which may exist in the footing subgrades we recommend that
footing excavations in dense basalt rock be over excavated 1 foot below subgrade level
The over excavated area should be backfilled with similar rock fill material in 8 inch loose
horizontal lifts and compacted to a minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density per ASTM
Test Method D 1557 78
Foundations should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade Foundations located on sloping areas should have a minimum
set back of eight 8 feet from the outside edge of the footing to the adjacent slope face
This may require additional embedment to achieve the proper set back distance

increase the foundation embedment depth

Percolation Testing
Falling head percolation tests were conducted in two 2 areas of the project site
to provide data for the design of septic system disposal methods The results of our
percolation tests are presented in Appendix B of this report
GEOLABS HAWAII

These tests indicated that the absorption rates for the on site clinker are relatively
rapid However the underlying basalt rock formation has relatively low absorption
characteristics It is our opinion that the use of a conventional leachfield system at the
site may result in either perching of effluent at shallow depths or migration of the effluent
to other areas
The dry wells proposed for the site improvements should be designed with
consideration to the potential for low absorption in the underlying massive basalt
formation as the rate of percolation will be dependent upon the amount of fracturing of
the in situ rock Generally it is our opinion that any dry wells for the project should
penetrate the dense basalt encountered in our borings to more permeable material

Environmental Considerations
Landfill Liners
It is our understanding that the proposed landfill will be used for the disposal of
municipal solid waste It is our understanding that impermeable liners will be
required for the landfill cells
Clay soils suitable for lining are not available at the site nor are they generally
available on the island of Hawaii Therefore it is anticipated that synthetic
membrane liners will be used
To reduce the potential of punctures in the flexible membranes a cushion layer of
sand is recommended for the bottoms and sides of the cells and should be
installed prior to the placement of the flexible membrane The cushion should be
a minimum of six 6 inches thick

Groundwater Monitoring
To monitor potential impacts on the groundwater below the site which could
potentially affect the coastal waters downgradient of the landfill we recommend
that a network of groundwater monitoring wells be established for the subject
project In general accordance with U S Environmental Protection Agency
regulations the network should consist of a minimum of one upgradient monitoring
point and three downgradient points
Our experience in the West Hawaii area indicates that groundwater is frequently
highly stratified from fresh to saline and that flux through the system is rapid
Optimum monitoring of the groundwater would most likely require multiple well
installations at each monitoring point to intercept the different groundwater flow
paths We recommend that detailed hydrogeologic exploration be conducted to
verify the groundwater conditions and to allow for design of the groundwater
monitoring system

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the drilling and sampling
of eight 8 borings to depths ranging between 15 0 and 39 0 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings
were drilled by a truck mounted rotary drill rig advancing hollow stem continuous flight
augers or rotary core drilling tools as appropriate
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the boring logs at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D
2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and testing
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt vs the length of the core attempt e g if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 and would be shown on
the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material that is in excess of four 4 inches in length without any discontinuities
discounting any drilling induced fractures or breaks if 2 5 feet of sound material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 and would be shown on the
Boring Logs as REC 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative
quality based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100

relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD
would be very difficult to rip and would probably require blasting or splitting for
excavation

The subsurface conditions at the site were also explored by the performance of
eight 8 seismic refraction surveys at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The seismic refraction surveys were conducted using a 12 channel signal
enhancement seismograph with a 12 pound sledge hammer as a seismic energy source
Refraction seismography is based on the transmission of elastic energy
compressional waves through geologic materials Generally the denser or more
consolidated a material is the faster the transmission of the compressional waves These
waves are refracted at interfaces between materials
The refracted waves are received by geophones on the surface and recorded by
the seismograph The recorded data is downloaded to a portable computer either for
processing in the field or for storage and later processing
This technique permits mathematical calculations which will yield approximate
depths or thicknesses of materials below the survey and give their approximate
compressional wave velocities The calculated velocities are average values for the
interval sampled i e the length of the survey Poor surface conditions or irregular
interfaces in the subsurface may distort the data or interfere with interpretation
The calculated velocities may be empirically related to the excavation
characteristics of the materials The following legend explains the terms shown on the
