SUBSURFACE CONDITTONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built by two basaltic shield volcances
Waianae and Koolau Waianae is the older of the two and reached the end
of its activity before Koolau
As volcanic activity on the island of Oahu drew to a close the
island began to subside slowly While the island was sinking fringing
coral algal reefs began to grow on the seaward margins of the island
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2358 00 Page 4
During the Pleistocene or Ice Age global sea levels rose and fell
in response to the great continental glaciers A the glaciers grew the
global sea level fell below the existing level and as the glaciers melted
and retreated the sea level rose to heights above the current stand of
the sea
Locally the fringing coral algal reefs continued to grow during the
high stands of the sea leaving fossil reefs which are now exposed above
sea level At the project site and in its vicinity there are exposure of
such fossil reefs which most likely grew during the Kaena 95 feet stand
of the sea and were subsequently planed to their current elevation during
the Waimanalo 25 feet stand
Subsurface Exploration
Previous experience at Naval Air Station Barbers Point indicates that
the site is underlain by coral formation at relatively shallow depths
Coral and coral algal limestones are the result of the growth of
marine organisms which secrete calcium carbonate The formations
constructed by these organisms are frequently discontinuous in nature and
have voids where the organisms did not grow Often these voids are
filled with detritus However the voids also frequently act as lines of
weakness for the formation of solution cavities caves and sinkholes
A geophysical survey ground penetrating radar was conducted at the
site in order to attempt to detect these voids at relatively shallow
depths below the existing ground surface
The ground penetrating radar system utilizes impulse radar technology
to obtain a continuous high resolution profile of the subsurface The
radar profiling system consisted of two 2 separate antennas five 5
feet apart mounted on a wooden platform The antenna array is connected
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 5
by an electrical cable to the processing and display equipment and is
towed along the ground at low speeds
The dual antenna system allows running two 2 parallel lines
simultaneously thus reducing the number of transects required and
reducing the possibility of missing anomalies of large areal extent
Selected subsurface radar profiles and interpretations are presented in
Appendix D
The radar survey indicated the presence of many cavities within about
5 to 10 feet of the ground surface
Following the radar survey two 2 borings and fourteen 14 probes
were drilled at the site to verify collaborate the findings of the radar
survey
Generally the borings and probes verified the existence of the
cavities as indicated by the radar survey Most of the cavities were
found to be about 1 to 2 feet high except for a few locations where
cavities of 6 to 8 feet in vertical dimension were encountered
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of
our exploration
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
It is our opinion that the site is suitable for the proposed
construction provided special site grading provisions are included to
reduce the possibility of collapse of near surface cavities in the coral
formation

Ground Penetrating Radar
A ground penetrating radar survey of the subject site was conducted
after the initial borings were drilled in order to further explore the
subsurface to locate anomalies which could be cavities directly beneath
the pavements and within the underlying coral algal formation
It is our opinion that the ground penetrating radar survey proved to
be successful and cost effective in locating subsurface cavities and or
loose zones at the project site To confirm the presence of cavities at
suspected locations indicated by the radar survey fourteen 14 probes
were drilled at the site
Open cavities were encountered in nine 9 of the fourteen 14
probes drilled
Based on the radar survey and probing results cavities exist at many
locations below the pavement areas The cavities appear to be located
generally deeper than three 3 feet below the existing pavement surface
and are generally small 1 to 2 foot high although three 3 areas with
suspected large 5 cavities were also found
A preliminary stress analysis was performed to evaluate the effect of
the truck wheel loads over a typical small cavity below pavement level
Based on our preliminary analyses it does not appear that the
smaller cavities detected pose imminent concerns However the long term
stability of the cavities cannot be forecast since the cavity roof may
weaken under continued traffic loads or due to continued erosion
To reduce the concerns of potential cavity collapse the cavity areas
may be filled with grout However due to the large areal extent of the
pavements and random nature of the cavities grouting of the entire area
would be very costly An alternative may be to expose and backfill only
the cavities at shallow depths below the proposed pavement surface
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2358 00 Page 7
Since the anticipated loads from the trucks which will be used at the
facility are relatively small and have limited depth of influence below
the surface generally less than about 2 to 3 feet it appears that a
more practical method to reduce the hazard of collapse due to the near
surface cavities would be to over excavate and recompact the upper soils
at the site The general procedure would be as follows
1 Over excavate and stockpile the existing soils for a depth of 2
feet below the pavement subgrade level and a horizontal distance
of 5 feet beyond the pavement areas
2 Proofroll the excavated grade with a heavy compactor to attempt
to collapse nearby cavities
3 Recompact the excavated soils to a minimm of 95 of the maximm
dry density as established per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
The radar survey and probing indicated that there may be three 3
large surface cavities which could be opened by a backhoe or dozer from
the existing ground surface and backfilled with compacted granular
material These suspected large cavity locations are indicated on the
Site Plan Plate 2

Dry Well Design
By extrapolation of the field percolation test data our analysis
indicated that dry wells in this area with a diameter of 4 feet and a
depth of 10 feet may accommodate a gravity injection of about 500 gallons
per minute with a 2 0 foot head build up We recommend that the dry wells
be field tested following installation to determine the actual capacity
of the wells
Typically surface run off contains suspended solids silt and other
matter which may reduce the potential permeability of the receiving
formation Therefore provisions should be made in the design of the
facility for a long term maintenance program and built in capability for
well redevelopment to extend the useful life of the wells

Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the geotechnical engineer be retained to
provide geotechnical engineering services during construction of the
earthwork and foundation phases of the work In addition the
geotechnical engineer should be retained to provide continuous observation
and consultation services during the proofrolling and site filling phases
of the work This is to observe compliance with the design concepts
specifications or recommendations and to allow design changes in the event
that subsurface conditions differ from that anticipated prior to start of
construction The recommendations given in this report are contingent
upon such observations
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and
approved by the geotechnical engineer prior to hauling to the site
If actual exposed rock soil conditions encountered during
construction are different from those assumed or considered in this
report then appropriate modifications to the design should be made

APPENDIX D
Ground Penetrating Radar
The ground penetrating radar system utilizes impulse radar technology
to obtain a continuous high resolution profile of the subsurface A
pulse is transmitted from a special antenna through the subsurface until
it reaches a soil interface void or embedded object Then depending on
the electrical characteristics of the soil interface or object a portion
of the transmitted pulse is reflected back to the receiver of the same
antenna The received signal is then processed and a subsurface profile
is displayed on the built in graphic recorder An example of the signal
structure and profile is shown on Plate D 1
The radar profiling system consists of two 2 antennas separated by
five 5 feet and mounted on a wooden platform The antenna system was
towed along the ground and is connected by an electrical cable to the
processing and display equipment located in the vehicle The dual antenna
system allows the operator to run two 2 parallel lines simultaneously
thus reducing the number of transects required and reducing the
possibility of missing cavities of large lateral extent
Each antenna transmits a radar pulse into the ground at a rate of 500
MHz with a pulse rate of two 2 nanoseconds The vehicle speed was
maintained at approximately 3 to 5 feet per second Therefore
reflections were acquired every inch of travel
The horizontal scale of each profile was determined by measuring off
10 foot intervals along each alignment During a given profile a mark
was placed on the radar graph at the intersection of these 10 foot marks
The vertical scale of the radar graph is 9 9 nanoseconds per division
which corresponds to approximately 1 5 feet per division This was
confirmed by running a transect across a previously drilled boring and
noting the depth to a known obvious reflector
After the radar survey was performed probable cavity locations were
selected for probing to verify the suspected cavity
A selected subsurface radar profile and interpretation are shown on
Plate D 2
The findings of the radar survey are summarized on the Site Plan
