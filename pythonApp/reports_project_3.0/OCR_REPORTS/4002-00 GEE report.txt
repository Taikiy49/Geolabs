REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Guam is the largest and southernmost of the Mariana Island
archipelago which is an island arc parallel to the trend of the subduction zone forming the
Mariana Trench The Mariana Trench is part of a belt of trenches that extends from
Japan to the Palau Islands and includes the Japan Trench Mariana Trench Yap Trench
and Palau Trench
The Island of Guam can be broadly divided into three geologic provinces
the southern mountains the central mountains and the northern plateau The formation
of the island started during the Eocene Epoch by the eruption of basaltic rhyolite lavas
The mountainous central portion of the island is composed of these Eocene volcanic
rocks and conglomerates tuffs and limestones of Eocene and Oligocene in age
Lava flows and limestone formations of Miocene Epoch make up the majority of the
southern one third of the island The plateaus of the northern portion of the island were
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4002 00 Page 6
formed while this part of the island was still beneath the sea level During the Oligocene
and Miocene Epochs a complex series of coral and algal reefs formed an elongated
platform of limestones and related deposits Tectonic activity has lifted these coral algal
limestones above sea level to form the northern Guam Plateau The project site is
situated in a relatively level portion of the northern plateau which forms the main portion
of Andersen Air Force Base
Weathering and solution working of the limestone has partially recrystallized the
calcium carbonate of the limestone forming massive limestones in some areas and poorly
to well cemented detrital limestones in others Typically the detrital limestones are highly
variable in strength characteristics and are frequently karstic or cavernous in nature
In addition the limestone formations are generally porous Except during heavy storms
the majority of the precipitation naturally percolates through the formation producing little
or no surface water runoff
Typically the surface of the underlying limestone is irregular due to solution
working Frequently formations such as those encountered at the project sites will have
buried pinnacles of massive limestone with spaces between the pinnacles filled with
weathered material and detrital material The surface soil horizon on the limestone
plateau is very thin generally less than 50 mm thick
GEOLOGIC AND SEISMIC HAZARDS EVALUATION
Guam is designated as Seismic Zone 3 by the Uniform Building Code
UBC 1994 However we understand that Seismic Zone 4 criteria will be used for
design of this project Therefore we conducted an evaluation of possible geologic and
seismic hazards that may impact the development of the proposed project An evaluation
of geologic and seismic hazards typically addresses surface fault rupture ground
shaking ground failure liquefaction lateral spreading and ground lurching tsunamis
and seiches and seismically induced flooding
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4002 00 Page 7
As previously discussed Guam is situated adjacent to the subduction zone that
forms the Mariana Trench The island is tectonically active and has experienced
approximately 20 earthquakes exceeding magnitude 7 0 since 1902 Of these events
fifteen occurred prior to 1950 and five have occurred subsequent to that date Several
large faults and fault zones that traverse across the Island of Guam include the following
Pago Bay Adelup Point Fault River Valley Fault Zone
Tamuning Barrigada Fault Pugua Fault and Mount Santa Rosa horst block
The proximity of the project site to faults and fault zones is a major consideration
when evaluating geologic and seismic hazards Of the major faults and fault zones
mentioned above the trace of the Pugua Fault lies closest to the project site at
approximately 8 kilometers to the west of the project site The Pugua Fault stretches from
off shore near Uruno Point and extends south to about Taguac Several minor faults
located about 3 kilometers south of the project site are also present
Based on our evaluation of the project site we do not believe that the geologic and
seismic hazards described above are significant considerations for this project With the
exception of moderate to strong ground shaking we believe that the potential for surface
fault rupture ground failure liquefaction lateral spreading and ground lurching
tsunamis and seiches and seismically induced flooding are considered to be low at this
project site
Based on the subsurface materials encountered in the borings drilled the project
site may be classified as a Type S soil profile based on UBC 1994 A seismic site
coefficient of 1 0 may be used for the project site for seismic analysis of the proposed
structures

The Island of Guam is located in the North Pacific Ocean between 144 degrees
36 minutes and 144 degrees 58 minutes longitude and 13 degrees 13 minutes and

In general the climate of Guam is warm and humid with little variation in temperature
from month to month Based on information obtained from the National Oceanic and
Atmospheric Administration NOAA Guam s mean annual temperature is about
27 degrees Celsius throughout the year Typically annual rainfall averages from about
2 000 to per year The rainy season is from the months of July through
November when monthly rainfall can average over 300 mm per month
Tropical storms and typhoons often pass through or near the Island of Guam
Tropical storms are defined as closed pressure systems with wind speeds ranging from
60 to 120 kilometers per hour Typhoons are similar phenomena with wind speeds
exceeding 120 kilometers per hour During the rainy season tropical storms and
typhoons are common in the vicinity of Guam Tropical storms and typhoons bring high
intensity rainfall when their zone of influence is near Guam In extreme events a typhoon
has produced winds of over 280 kilometers per hour over 3 5 meters of inundation and
650 mm of rainfall in one day

Our field exploration at the project site consisted of drilling and sampling
14 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 14 extending to depths of about 3 1 to
15 4 meters below the existing ground surface In addition two concrete core samples
designated as Boring Nos 101 and 102 were obtained at selected locations along the
taxiway to measure the thickness of the existing concrete pavement The approximate
locations of the borings and concrete cores are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the project site is underlain by limestone formations Surface outcrops
of limestone formations were observed at several locations near the proposed fuel tank
pump house and generator building area Where surface soil was encountered the
materials were generally fills consisting of very stiff sandy silts and dense coral sands and
gravel Surficial fill thickness of up to about 1 meter was encountered in our borings
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4002 00 Page 10
The fills were underlain by soft to medium hard limestone formation extending to the
maximum depth explored of about 15 4 meters below the existing ground surface
The limestone formation generally appeared to be severely fractured and _ slightly
weathered to unweathered It should be noted that strength characteristics of limestone
formations are highly variable The depositional nature of the formation creates localized
areas of hard crystalline limestones intermixed with soft severely fractured detrital
limestones
It should be noted that limestone formations are frequently cavernous or contain
pockets of voids Although cavities were not encountered in our drilled borings the
potential for presence of cavities and or voids exists at the project site

coral aggregate concrete and was underlain by coral sandy gravel
Groundwater was not encountered in our borings at the time of our field
exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change

should be noted that water seepage is commonly encountered in limestone formations

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered limestone formations at or near the
existing ground surface Where surface soils were encountered the thickness of the soils
fills was limited to less than about 1 meter The fills generally consisted of very stiff
sandy silts and dense coral sands and gravel The limestone formations encountered in
our borings were generally severely fractured and soft to medium hard soft rock is harder
than very stiff to hard soil However the borings indicated that localized hard and
crystallized areas are present in the limestone formations
Considering the relatively dense near surface fills and the limestone formations
encountered at the site we recommend that a shallow foundation system consisting of
spread and or continuous footings bearing on the limestone formation and or compacted
structural fill be used to support the proposed structures However it should be noted
that limestone formations are typically karstic or cavernous in nature Therefore to
reduce the potential for possible loss in foundation support from collapse of
underground cavities we recommend that a program of probing and grouting at the
proposed fuel tanks be conducted during construction considering the critical nature of
the tank structures Probing and grouting the foundations of the supporting facilities
such as the pump house and generator building will not be required
We anticipate that a substantial volume of trench excavation will be required for
installation of the new fuel lines As mentioned earlier limestone formations underlying
the project site are typically severely fractured and soft to medium hard Based on our
field exploration we believe that a majority of the limestone formation at the site may be
excavated using large back hoe excavators However due to the presence of localized
hard zones excavation by hoe ramming and or chipping may be required and should
be anticipated

Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe
that the underlying limestone formation or structural fill needed to raise the site would
provide suitable foundation support for the proposed fuel tanks and support facilities
pump house and generator building We believe that a shallow foundation system
consisting of spread footings and or continuous footings may be used to support the fuel
tanks and other structures
An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for
design of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the limestone formation and or
compacted structural fill This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 500 mm below the lowest
adjacent finish grade If foundations are located next to utility trenches they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or the trench should be backfilled with
concrete to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
Due to the potential for presence of cavities as commonly encountered in
limestone formations we recommend that a program of probing and grouting at the
proposed fuel tanks be conducted during construction A qualified geotechnical engineer
should observe the probing and grouting operations to evaluate the extent of cavities if
encountered Probing and grouting requirements are further discussed in the
Foundation Probing section of this report
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with the
recommendations presented herein total settlements of footings are estimated to be on
the order of 25 mm with differential settlements on the order of about 15 mm
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4002 00 Page 13
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 45 may be used for footings bearing on the limestone
formation or compacted structural fill Resistance to lateral loads due to passive earth
pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 55 kilonewtons per
square meter per meter of depth kN m for footings embedded in the limestone
formation or compacted structural fill These values assume that the concrete for footings
is cast neat against the limestone formation or compacted structural fill Unless covered
by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 300 mm below finish grade
should be neglected
Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area voids and or cavities may be present in the
limestone formations encountered at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of
foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities below a footing we recommend
that a program of cavity probing and grouting be conducted during construction for the
proposed fuel tanks considering the critical nature of these tanks
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled along the perimeter
ring foundation spaced at approximately 3 meters on center In addition we believe that
a series of probes with a spacing of about 3 meters on center should be drilled starting at
the center of the tank and radiating out to the perimeter in about 3 meter increments in
radius The probe holes should be at least 75 mm in diameter and should extend to at
least 3 meters below the planned bottom of footing elevation If cavities and or voids are
encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probes should be
drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity
and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with
sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures A typical sand cement grout
should have a slump of about 150 to 200 mm
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4002 00 Page 14
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and
grouting operation is complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer
lag time between probing grouting operations and foundation construction may be
required in the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
qualified geotechnical engineer The probing and grouting program should be monitored
for presence of cavities and additional recommendations should be made if excess grout
take and or changed conditions are observed
Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed pump
house and generator building In general the subgrades for slabs on grades should be
scarified to a depth of at least 200 mm moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
We consider the slabs on grades for the pump house and generator building to be
industrial type slabs Therefore to provide more uniform support under the slabs we
recommend that the concrete slabs on grade be supported on a minimum 150 mm
cushion fill The cushion fill layer should consist of aggregate subbase course compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction For design of structural slabs bearing on
the aggregate subbase course a modulus of subgrade reaction of
per square meter per meter of deflection kN m may be used in the design

Excavation
The proposed project will involve the installation of approximately 2 800 lineal
meters of stainless steel pipes We understand that the average fuel line invert will
be located approximately 2 5 meters below the existing ground surface
Therefore we anticipate that excavations of up to about 3 meters in depth may be
necessary for installation of the new fuel lines
Based on our field exploration the proposed site is underlain by a limestone
formation near the ground surface The limestone formation encountered in our
borings appeared to be severely fractured and soft to medium hard Our borings
indicated that the limestone formation generally breaks down into gravelly sands
and sandy gravel It is anticipated that the majority of the limestone formation may
be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such as excavating with
large back hoe excavators However limestone formations typically contain
localized hard and crystallized zones Therefore we anticipate that difficult
excavation conditions may arise during construction at localized areas It may be
necessary to excavate the trenches in some areas by using hoe ram or by
chipping

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings it appears that
most of the cut slopes would expose the existing fills consisting of sandy silts with
coral gravel or limestone formations In general we believe that cut slopes may be
designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Permanent fill slopes
constructed of controlled fill should also be designed with a slope inclination of
2H 1V or flatter
Fills to be placed on slopes with inclinations steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed
and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against
sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in
level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement
recommendations Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back
to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face Water should be
diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as
soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finish slopes

The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
14 borings extending to depths of approximately 3 1 to 15 4 meters below the existing

The approximate locations of the borings and concrete cores are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment Concrete core
samples were obtained using a rotary coring tool
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 1 through
A 16 1
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 50 mm OD standard penetration sampler or a 75 mm OD split barrel
sampler with a 63 5 kilogram hammer falling 760 mm The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 300 mm of a 450 mm drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the limestone formations encountered at the site were obtained
using core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if of
material is recovered from a core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 100 mm in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 750 mm of sound material is recovered from a core run

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Eight sieve analysis ASTM C 136 tests were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic representations of the grain size distribution are presented on Plates B 1 and
B 2
Two CBR tests were performed on the near surface soils to evaluate the
strength of the in situ materials for pavement support The tests were performed in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 The test results are
presented on Plates B 3 and B 4
One Modified Proctor compaction test was performed on a bulk sample obtained
to evaluate the relationship between the moisture content and the dry density of the
near surface soils The Modified Proctor test was performed in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 The test results are graphically presented on
Plate B 5
Six sets of pH and resistivity tests were performed on the selected soil samples
obtained to evaluate the corrosivity of the in situ soils The results are summarized on
Plate B 6
