Project Considerations
The project involves the construction of a 30 foot high free standing radio
antenna tower at the U S Air Force Long Range Radar Station on Mt Koke e on the
Island of Kauai Hawaii Based on the information provided we anticipate that the
antenna tower will be relatively light in weight but will be subjected to significant uplift
forces and moment at the foundation level Minimal site grading work is anticipated for
the project construction

Geology
The Island of Kauai is the oldest of the islands located within the Hawaiian
Archipelago The Island of Kauai is roughly circular in shape and encompasses a land
area of about 555 square miles making it the fourth largest island in the Hawaiian chain
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano
Mount Waialeale built by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series
beginning about 5 to 6 million years ago The eruption of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic
Series ended about 2½ million years ago and was followed by a long period of landform
erosion The Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series includes the
and Makaweli Formations Following the cessation of this main volcano shield building
phase about 1½ million years ago there was renewed volcanic activity with the
extrusion of basaltic lavas of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the
concurrent deposition of the alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation
The project site is located on Mt Koke e on the northwest margin of an extensive
highland area encompassed by a large lava in filled volcanic caldera The caldera was
filled with thick horizontally bedded lavas of the Olokele Formation which ponded within
the summit caldera The project site is underlain by basaltic lavas of the Napali
Formation which encompass the side slopes of the Mount Waialeale shield volcano
and the majority of the western end of the Island of Kauai The Napali Coastline located
toward the northwest of the project site is a steeply incised coastal sea cliff which
exposes the generally thin bedded basaltic lavas of the Napali Formation Other nearby
major drainage valleys incised by stream erosion extend to depths ranging up to
2 000 feet or more
Because of the relatively old age of the basaltic rock belonging to the Napali
Formation the near surface rock materials are typically deeply weathered In the
vicinity of the project site the deep in situ weathering has produced significant
near surface thickness of silty and clayey residual and saprolitic soils

Site Description
The project site is located within the existing U S Air Force Long Range Radar
Station on Mt Koke e approximately 13 miles north from the town of Waimea on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii The antenna tower site lies east southeast of the existing
AN FPS 93 Radar Dome Structure and north of the existing radio shelter building The
small 9 foot by 9 foot area proposed for the antenna tower is presently a relatively
level grassy patch between the access road for the radar dome building and is adjacent
to an approximately 50 foot downhill slope with a gradient of approximately
three horizontal to one vertical 3H 1V Ground surface elevations were not available
at the time this report was prepared
Geological reconnaissance of this area and the results of our borehole
observations indicate that the site is underlain with medium stiff to stiff brown clayey silt
soils of a residual nature from in situ weathering of its basaltic parent rock
Interviews with present air force personnel yielded information that the proposed
site is located near a previously backfilled 8 foot diameter shaft that served as a refuse
disposal area
2 3
Subsurface Conditions
Our exploration program at the antenna tower site consisted of drilling and
sampling one boring to a depth of approximately 40 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate location of the boring is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by
residual and saprolite soils consisting of medium stiff silty clay to the maximum depth
explored of approximately 40 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled boring at the time of our field
exploration The groundwater level at the project site may be influenced by seasonal
precipitation and subterranean seepage conditions

SECTION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
3 1
Discussion and Recommendations
On the basis of our geotechnical engineering exploration we believe that the
proposed antenna tower may be supported on a mat foundation bearing on the in situ
medium stiff soils encountered at shallow depths similar to the manufacturer s
pre designed foundation system We estimate the total foundation settlements under
the anticipated light structural loads to be less than 1 inch with differential settlements
between the center and the corner of the mat to be on the order of ½ inch Detailed
discussion of our geotechnical engineering design recommendations are presented in
the following sections of this report
3 2
Mat Foundation
Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the site we
believe that a mat foundation bearing on the in situ medium stiff soils may be used to
support the proposed antenna tower structure An allowable bearing pressure of up
to 2 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for design of shallow mat
foundations bearing on the in situ medium stiff silty clay soils This bearing value is for
those caused by wind or seismic forces For mat foundation design a modulus of
subgrade reaction of 100 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be
used for the mat foundation bearing on the in situ stiff soils The subgrade modulus is
the modulus of vertical subgrade reaction for a 1 foot square test plate
The bottom of the mat foundation should be embedded a minimum of 3 feet
below the lowest adjacent finish grade Deeper embedment may be required to provide
the required capacity against uplift and moment reactions at the base of the tower
Additionally the mat foundation should be embedded a sufficient depth to provide a
minimum horizontal setback distance of 8 feet measured from the bottom of the mat to
the slope face

Resistance to uplift loads may be provided by the dead weight of the structure
and the mat foundation Additional resistance to uplift loads may be provided by the
weight of the backfill material placed above the footings if applicable A unit weight of
110 pounds per cubic foot pcf may be used for the on site compacted silty clay
materials
The bottom of the mat footing excavation should be firm and unyielding prior to
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete If soft and or loose materials are
encountered at the bottom of the footing excavation they should be over excavated to
expose the underlying firm materials The resulting over excavation should be
backfilled with the on site soil placed and compacted in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Site Preparation section of this report or the
footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying competent material
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines
We estimate the total foundation settlements under the anticipated light structural
loads to be less than 1 inch with differential settlements between the center and the
corner of the mat to be on the order of ½ inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional
resistance and adhesion developed between the bottom of the foundation and the
bearing soil Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an

Site Preparation
Based on the proposed location of the antenna tower minimal site grading work
is anticipated for the project construction
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Debris unsuitable materials and existing surface
pavements that are to be demolished should be removed and disposed of off site
Areas beneath the future tower mat should be over excavated to the future
foundation subgrade level If zones of soft loose or saturated soils are encountered at
the over excavated subgrade level deeper over excavation may be required to expose
firm material The mat subgrade should be compacted to a minimum of 90 relative
compaction
Generally the on site silty soils should be suitable for use as engineered fill
materials provided that they are properly moisture conditioned and compacted It
should be noted that the on site soils have moderate to high in situ moisture contents
Therefore it may be difficult to achieve the required compaction without some aeration
of the on site soils If imported material is required the material should conform to the
requirements for Select Borrow for Subbase Course stipulated in Section 30 of the
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction dated September 1986
Imported fill material should be tested and approved for its intended use by our firm
prior to delivery to the project site
Fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above optimum moisture content
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures

Drainage
The finished grades outside the antenna tower should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive landscape
watering near the foundations should also be avoided Drainage swales should be
provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface run off away
from the foundation slab
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the foundation slab immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce water infiltration

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
one boring to a depth of approximately 40 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate location shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The boring was drilled using
truck mounted drilling equipment
The materials encountered in the boring were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Log of Boring Plate A 1
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
