2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank Both Waianae and Koolau Ranges have lost most of
their original shield outlines and are now long narrow ridges largely shaped by erosion
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of
lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows
of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred during the last 1 to 2 million years
The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils and saprolite In general
saprolite is mainly composed of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of
volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with depth

Our field exploration indicates that the project site is generally underlain by
residual soil consisting of very stiff to very hard clayey silts extending to depths of about
14 5 to 21 5 feet or more below the existing ground surface Saprolite materials were
encountered below the residual material in some of the borings extending to the
maximum depths drilled of approximately 21 5 feet below the ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration
Based on the competent subsurface soil conditions encountered during our field
exploration we envision that the proposed retail commercial development may be
supported on a shallow foundation system consisting of spread or continuous footings
The near surface soils at the site generally consist of silty clay soils of low to
moderate plasticity Therefore the near surface soils are generally susceptible to
shrinking and swelling when subjected to changes in the soil moisture contents Unless
slabs on grade constructed above these types of plastic soils are properly designed
there is a potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from
shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content As a
result we recommend providing a minimum of 24 inches of non expansive select
granular fill material below the building slab areas The select fill should extend beyond
the footprint of the new slab on grade areas by a minimum of 24 inches
The near surface soils at the site and in the general area are susceptible to
heaving and or loss of strength when saturated Therefore special attention should be
given to the site drainage design Proper surface drainage and reduction of water
infiltration into the subsurface are integral components to the recommendations
provided herein Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade

3 1 Building Foundations
Based on the current grading concept we understand that fills ranging up to
about 1 to 6 feet thick may be required to raise the existing grades to the finished floor
elevations for the new building structures In addition two building pads may be located

3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the design of foundations bearing

The bottom of the footing excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of

Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations should

Keystone Retaining Walls
The Keystone retaining wall system is a composite wall system which utilizes high
density polyethylene or other reinforcing elements to reinforce the backfill zone and
improve the shear strength of the reinforced soil zone This composite system
essentially forms a gravity wall structure with an ability to tolerate significant total
and differential settlements In addition Keystone retaining walls are also desirable
due to the flexibility of the wall ease of construction high load carrying capacity
and economy
Design of the retaining wall system will need to take into consideration both the
external and internal stability of the structure In evaluating external stability the
retaining wall must satisfy four stability conditions 1 bearing failure
2 translational sliding 3 overturning stability and 4 overall slope stability
Geotechnical design parameters to evaluate these stability conditions are
presented in the following subsections Some of the geotechnical parameters
necessary in evaluating the internal stability of the retaining wall are presented in
the Backfill Materials subsection

3 5 Liquefaction Potential
Liquefaction occurs when a build up of excess pore water pressure is induced on
saturated cohesionless soils resulting in a substantial loss of strength The most
common cause of this phenomenon is the cyclic loading due to earthquakes This
process may cause both vertical and horizontal movements in the soil and if not
confined can result in significant deformations Loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion are most susceptible to liquefaction Generally
liquefaction is not likely to occur if the loose deposit is deeper than 50 feet below the
ground surface The greater overburden pressure can be sufficient in preventing
liquefaction in deeper deposits
Based on our field exploration the project site is mostly underlain by very stiff to
hard cohesive soils Additionally the natural groundwater table is greater than 50 feet
below the site Therefore we believe that there is no potential for liquefaction at this
project site

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils under
different surcharge loads The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B 2
Two Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the Direct
Shear tests are presented on Plates B 3 and B 4
One Unconfined Compression test ASTM D 2166 was performed on a selected
sample to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the material tested The test
results are summarized on Plate B 5
