SITE DESCRIPTION
The civic center site has an area of about five 5 acres and is
located in the southern portion of a residential property previously
considered for housing light industrial development by Hawaiian Housing
Authority and Crown Corporation in Waipahu The site is northwest of the
intersection of Farrington Highway and Wailani Stream Kahu Channel The
site is generally bounded by Mokuola Street under construction to the
west Wailani Stream to the east and commercial buildings along Maloalo
Street to the south
The existing ground is generally level with an elevation of around 12
feet Immediately along the northern boundary the ground elevation is
lower at about elevation 7 to 8 feet in areas where previous surcharge
fill was not placed Further north and outside the project site the
previous surcharge fill placed since 1974 appears to be about 5 to 8 feet
thick The Mokuola Street extension has a ground elevation of about 10
feet Excavated material from recent drainline construction was
stockpiled along the northern project boundary as of September 1987 see
the Site Plan Plate 2 for approximate locations
REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The site is located in an area of recent stream and marine deposits
overlying formations of volcanic origin During the Pleistocene epoch
sea level fluctuated reaching as much as 300 feet below the present
level Stream transported sediment consisting mainly of boulders
cobbles and gravel has been deposited near a lower sea level
As sea level began to rise the capacity of the streams was reduced
and finer sands and silts were subsequently deposited At some point the
accumulation of sediments could not keep up with the rising sea and the
area became flooded Very fine grained sediments were then deposited in

General Subsurface Conditions
Generally the subsurface conditions may be generalized by the
following four 4 layers
1 Fill The total thickness of the on site fills is around 8 feet
which consists of around 5 feet of old fill and 3 feet of new
fill The old fill appears denser and consists mainly of reddish
brown silt with some boulders and cobbles while the upper new
fill was probably placed as a surcharge fill which brought the
the site grade to about elevation 12 feet since 1974 The site
development history will be summarized in greater detail later
2 Soft Organic Silt The thickness of this layer on site varies
between 40 to 50 feet The organic silt material is dark gray in
color very soft and compressible Decayed vegetation was noted
near the interface between this layer and the older fill The
soil appears to have been deposited in a marsh environment
3 Old Alluvium This layer consists of stiff to very stiff brown
clayey silts to dense silty gravel
4 Basaltic formations were encountered below the overlying
formations at depths exceeding 120 feet
It is believed that much of the site was used for rice and or taro
cultivation in the past with more recent use as a grazing area for
cattle

Groundwater Conditions
Artesian groundwater conditions were reported in previous soil
reports The hydraulic head was reported to be about 0 7 feet above the
old ground surface old fill in 1974 or at about elevation 10 feet The
upper groundwater level measured in previous borings at the site was
reported to range from elevation 5 to elevation 8 feet
It appears that the underlying basaltic formation is part of the
Pearl Harbor Aquifer This aquifer is confined by overlying deposits of
marine and alluvial sediments with low permeability commonly referred to
as caprock
Groundwater has been heavily developed within the Pearl Harbor
Aquifer Hydraulic head in the vicinity of the site was approximately 33
feet MSL in 1879 23 feet in 1958 and 17 feet in 1978 as published by
the Board of Water Supply 1980 We believe further head declines could
occur and this could initiate more future areal and local ground
settlements due to the artesian head loss

SITE GROUND SETTLEMENTS
An attempt was made to evaluate the settlement pattern of the site by
comparing old and new settlement gauges and general trend of changes in
the topography of the site
Generally the gauges on site indicated a total of 5 to 7 inches of
settlement including three 3 inches between March 1975 and April 1977
and two 2 to four 4 inches between June 1979 and November 1981 Two
additional readings that were taken on November 1977 and May 1978
indicated zero to one third of an inch settlement
Based on the results of our re plotting of some of the settlement
gauge readings between June 1979 and November 1981 using a semi log
scale it appears that the ground settlement may not have been completed
at the end of the monitoring period in November 1981
Based on a recent site reconnaissance for details refer to Appendix
A and comparison of old 1977 and new 1987 topographic maps we
concluded that we can not accurately determine whether or not the site has
undergone an additional amount of long term settlement
In summary based on the available data the current site has
probably settled at least 5 to 7 inches or more to date At this time
the on site surcharge fill placed since 1974 is about 3 feet in thickness
We are not aware of other major earthwork after 1978

Foundation Selection Considerations
Spread or continuous footing foundations are not generally
recommended as large total differential settlements are anticipated unless
special measures are taken Overexcavating the site to compensate future
building loads does not seem appropriate as the site would require a
minimum elevation of 10 feet to avoid flooding and the current elevation
is about twelve 12 feet Removing 2 feet of fill is not sufficient to
compensate for the anticipated loads
The top 3 feet of existing underlying fill was placed as a surcharge
fill since 1974 for light industrial or apartment type of residential
development Although there was a program for fill compaction and
settlement monitoring the program was suspended in late 1974 before
completion and the level of effort of compaction is uncertain Thus a
budget for either fill removal or recompaction of the existing fill should
be considered
Possible foundation designs including the use of spread cont inuous
footings with an additional surcharge program mat and pile foundations
are discussed below
1 Spread Continuous Footing Foundation If spread continuous
footings are desired an additional surcharge and settlement
monitoring program is recommended Since the building loads from
the anticipated development would be much heavier than a
Single story residential building a heavy surcharge program will
probably be required The amount of surcharge fill and surcharge
period should be determined after the second phase exploration is
performed and better estimates of the anticipated building loads
are available Generally the surcharge time would be long due
to the organic nature of the subsoil Furthermore the surcharge
should be restricted to the northern portion of the site in order

Mat Foundation A rigid mat foundation may mitigate problems
resulting from moderate amount of differential settlements within
individual structures Prestress may be applied in a mat
foundation in order to increase the rigidity of the mat
However a large rigid mat foundation could still experience
problems from differential settlement when the underlying
compressible layer was substantial differences in thickness For
example a thicker layer under one end of the mat may tend to
consolidate more than on the other end thus the upper
super structure could be tilted A series of smaller mats may
also be used for each structure with vertical construction joints
to allow for differential settlements The thickness of the soft
organic silt underneath the site should be delineated in greater
detail in the second phase of the geotechnical exploration
However even with mat foundations large long term total
settlements can still occur Frequent maintenance and inspection
of utility connections would also be required in the future
Although a large surcharge fill program may be applied to
initiate the settlements the surcharge period would be much
longer than for the smaller spread footings since the mat
foundation would have a _ greater depth of influence in the
subsoil
Pile Foundation In this Phase I study it appears that pile
foundations may be more appropriate for the Civic Center
development Generally two types of pile foundation support
frictional and end bearing are available

Friction Piles
For friction piles the piles need to penetrate through underlying
fills and soft organic silts into the underlying old alluvium to
develop frictional resistance on the lower pile surfaces Low to
medium pile load capacities in the range of 40 tons to 50 tons may
be expected Based on available borings the thickness of the old
alluvium layer outside the northern boundary of the site varies from
20 to 60 feet The actual subsurface conditions underlying the
proposed development should be verified
End Bearing Piles
Generally for end bearing piles the piles will have to penetrate
through the upper fills compressible soils stiff alluvium and then
bear on the underlying basaltic rock formation Theoretically
relatively high load capacities may be developed if the piles
penetrate through all these upper layers We are not certain about
the practicality of pile driving through the stiff dense alluvium at
this time
Also upward seepage of water may occur due to artesian head Since
the end bearing piles may disturb the confined Pearl Harbor Aquifer
further considerations and consultation with the City Board of Water
Supply and the State Department of Land and Natural Resources may be
necessary Assuming no future fill is placed since the current site
grade is 2 to 2 5 feet higher than the Mokuola Street access road
the down drag force from the existing fill only may be minimal
However this needs to be verified A number of piezometers should
be installed at various levels to monitor the artesian conditions and
their potential impact on pile foundation design

Summary of Recommendations
Surcharge Fill
The upper surcharge fill is in a loose and uncompacted state which
will require either removal or recompaction The underlying old fill
below the upper 3 foot fill layers should also be scarified and
moisture conditioned prior to proof rolling to detect soft zones and
recompacted to obtain a more uniform subgrade Therefore a budget
should be provided for handling these special site grading
preparations
Pile Foundation
Based on available borings at the northern portion of the site it
appears that artesian water conditions may restrict the use of deep
end bearing piles through the caprock layer Therefore moderate
pile capacity of about 40 to 50 tons per pile may be used based on an
estimated pile length of about 75 feet We believe that inch
octagonal prestress concrete piles would be appropriate due to hard
pile driving required through the stiff fills and alluviun
Additional Geotechnical Exploration
We believe that a Phase II exploration is required after the siting
of buildings and facilities is determined At least four 4 new
borings to depths of 120 feet should be drilled and sampled inside
the Civic Center site since previous 1966 borings were located either
near the northern boundary or outside the current development site
The new borings would provide site specific and up dated soil
information under planned facility and obtain current engineering
properties for detailed geotechnical engineering analysis related to
settlement pile down drag bearing capacity pavement design and
site grading requirements

In our current study it was noted that the amount of ground
settlement observed was much less than that predicted by other soils
reports A review on the available consolidation data showed that
the soil at some lower depths may have been overconsolidated
Although this might explain the discrepancy between the observed and
predicted settlements further investigation on the consolidation
characteristics of the compressible soils is warranted
Piezometers should be installed in the Phase IT borings at different
depths to further study the artesian conditions at the site
Triaxial shear tests would also be performed on soil samples from the
new borings to evaluate the in situ strength gain within the upper
organic silts due to the recent 3 foot surcharge fill The
piezometric readings to be obtained together with the proposed
consolidation and shear strength data through the soil profile may
better help us to characterize the soft organic silt and to evaluate
whether or not the site has completed its primary consolidation
