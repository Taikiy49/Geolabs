Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was formed by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger shield
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano became extinct
while Koolau Volcano was still active and the eastern flank of Waianae Volcano was
partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank
Based on our review of available geologic mapping information the majority of
the project site is composed generally of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lavas of
the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks generally are characterized by flows of jointed
dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical weathering
of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The weathering
process has formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic soils In general saprolite
saprolitic soil is composed mainly of silty and clayey materials and is typical of the
tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation
with increased depth Many basaltic boulders or corestones are located throughout
the site due to spheroidal or onion skin weathering of the original lava flows
The area in and around Kalaeokahipa Gulch are underlain by clayey
Recent Alluvium Ra deposits which have been deposited down slope by water
movement These soils generally are mottled brown clayey silts with multi colored sand
and gravel grains interbedded
In addition a localized area of lithified calcareous dune sand and coralline
deposits are present at the north and northeastern portions of the project site The
calcareous dune sands were formed by fine sands blown inland from ancient beaches
and lithified over time

Geologic Site Reconnaissance
A geological and geotechnical engineering reconnaissance of the project site
was conducted in an effort to obtain an overview of the existing field conditions with
respect to potential natural hazards including existing slope instability previous debris
flow activity potential rockfall source areas and flash flooding
The primary geologic hazards in the Hawaiian Islands are volcanic eruptions
earthquakes tsunamis flash floods landslides debris flows and rock falls The Island
of Oahu s volcanoes are long extinct and therefore provide no geologic hazard to this
project site
Earthquakes normally are caused by shifts in the tectonic plates However
earthquakes in Hawaii typically are associated with volcanic activity Very few large
earthquakes throughout history have had epicenters on Oahu although many moderate
ones originate from the Island of Hawaii roughly 150 miles away However there is a
potential for moderate seismic activity at the project site therefore seismic design
standards should be incorporated into the project
Tsunamis or seismic sea waves are a major hazard for all of Hawaii s low lying
coastal areas At Elevation 50 feet Mean Sea Level MSL the lower portion of
Kalaeokahipa Gulch is potentially susceptible to inundation should a large tsunami
strike this area Based on the topographic survey map the lowest wind turbine
generator is WTG No 11 WTG No 11 is at about Elevation 125 feet MSL and nearly
2 miles from the shoreline therefore the likelihood of damage from this geologic hazard
is relatively small
Flash flooding is common throughout the Hawaiian Islands and the area
encompassed by the project site is no exception The project area is transected by
Kalaeokahipa Gulch which is an intermittent stream channel active during flash floods
Inundation and extensive erosion during flooding are the primary hazards for structures
and roads near this area therefore the grading design of the project near the gulch
should consider the flash flooding hazard

Residual Soils
Residual soils are completely weathered volcanic rocks that have lost all visible
rock texture characteristics The residual soils at the project site are typically brown
to reddish brown in color The residual soils may be mottled with various colors
Residual soils are comprised mainly of clayey materials with stiff to very stiff
consistency Typically residual soils occur overlying saprolitic soils or weathered
basalt formation It should be noted that relict boulders of hard rock may be
encountered in residual soils In addition there is potential for encountering hard
basalt rock ledges in residual soils because the volcanic rock may not have
weathered uniformly throughout the rock formation Some relict boulders of hard
rock occasionally may be encountered in residual soils
Saprolitic Soils
Extremely weathered volcanic rock is referred to as saprolitic soils or saprolites
Saprolitic soils represent rock that has been reduced by weathering to soil like
components with weathered rock fragments The soil material retains the remnant
rock texture such as layering vesicularity and some fracture patterns Saprolitic
soils are typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks and are composed of
mainly silty materials The coloring scheme of saprolitic soils depends on the type
of parent volcanic rock from which the soils are derived and the degree of chemical
weathering that the material has experienced Because the parent material does
not weather uniformly throughout the formation relict boulders of hard rock and or
hard rock ledges occasionally may be encountered within saprolitic soils
Basalt Formation
Basalt formations include a broad range of volcanic basaltic rock The basalt rock
material was extruded as sequential layered lava flows from volcanic vents The
basalt rock which frequently contain interbedded porous clinker materials range
from very hard to soft rock Weathering decomposition ranges between highly
weathered and slightly weathered Fracture discontinuities within basalt rock
formations range from severely fractured shattered and broken to relatively
unfractured and massive in character

Recent Alluvium
Recent alluvial materials generally consist of unconsolidated eroded and
transported terrestrial sediments including silts clays sands and some gravel
which are deposited by flowing water Recent alluvial deposits mainly are
encountered adjacent to stream and estuary environments but they also may be
encountered where sediment laden runoff emanating from higher elevation terrain
is deposited on adjacent lower elevation flatter terrain The deposits may be
encountered as wide spread valley fill deposits or as elongated channel filling
deposits The consistency of the recent alluvial deposits at the site generally ranges
from very stiff to hard
Consolidated Calcareous Dunes
Calcareous dune sands consist of thin bedded and cross bedded aeolian limestone
composed of uniform fine calcareous sands The fine sands were blown inland from
ancient beaches Over time the fine sands became lithified resulting in sandstone
with relative hardness ranging between medium hard and hard The sandstone
may contain sufficient solution channels to be permeable
Coralline Detritus
Coralline detritus deposits generally consist of unconsolidated to weakly
consolidated granular marine deposits containing variable coralline reef detritus
debris mixed with finer grain sediments Coralline detritus may include both
grayish marine and brownish terrestrial sediments mixed with various coralline
gravel shell fragments and some sands The materials typically are deposited in
shallow bays lagoons estuaries stream deltas and along outer fringing reefs
where higher wave energy environments exist which assist in generating the
variable detrital materials The deposits typically are encountered as loose granular
materials but they may become medium dense in relative density when buried and
consolidated for longer periods of time Coralline detritus may be encountered
interbedded with lagoonal deposits or as layers or infilling within porous and
cavernous coral reef formation materials

the project site may be subject to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will
need to be addressed The following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic
activity therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so
small that they can be detected only by sensitive instruments However some of
the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate
damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on
the Island of Hawaii Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii
over 90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of
Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than
Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of
Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have
occurred in the County of Maui The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands
of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu For example small
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of

The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10 ton
steel electronic subtraction cone with an apex angle of 60 degrees and an area of
1 55 square inches 10 square centimeters into the softened bentonite backfill The
cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the arrival of a shear wave
propagated from the ground surface The seismic measurements are made when
the SCPT has stopped advancing and a shear wave is sent into the subsurface
A shear wave is generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a
switched hammer The propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the
cone was measured at each depth interval The vector difference of these depths
divided by the time difference provided the average shear wave velocity over the
depth interval The shear wave velocity profiles measured at the probe locations
are presented on Plates B 1 through B 19 in Appendix B

3 13 Underground Utilities
We envision new underground utilities are planned Based on the subsurface
conditions encountered at the project site we anticipate the underground utility trenches
will be excavated in the stiff residual and or saprolitic soils In general the contractor
should determine the method and equipment to be used for trench excavation subject
to practical limits and safety considerations The excavated soils from the trench
excavation should be stockpiled no closer than a horizontal distance equal to the depth
of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for appreciable
ground movement The trench excavation should comply with all applicable local state
and federal safety requirements
In general we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation under the pipes to support the utility
lines The initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of
free draining backfills such as open graded gravel to reduce the potential for pipe
damage from compaction of the backfill It is critical to use a free draining granular
material as the initial trench backfill to reduce the potential for formation of voids below
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Use
of clayey soils as initial trench backfill directly around utility pipes is not recommended
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils having

3 14 Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding considering the
slightly to moderately expansive nature of the on site soils Excessive landscape
watering near the foundations and slabs also should be avoided These drainage
requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above foundation
recommendations because ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or
slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for
appreciable water infiltration into the subsurface In addition drainage swales should be
provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface runoff away
from foundations and slabs

Field Exploration
Our field exploration program at the project site consisted of drilling and sampling
26 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 19 and 101 through 107 extending to
depths ranging from about 5 5 to 52 8 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 We used a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools to
drill the borings
Our geologist observed the drilling operations on a near continuous basis and
classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and textural examination in
the field These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1 Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 26
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description Rock cores were
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the
Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2

Seismic Shear Wave Velocity Tests
Seismic shear wave velocity profiling using seismic piezocone penetration testing
SCPTu equipment was performed in an effort to more closely analyze the seismic
design considerations for the project site Shear wave velocity testing using seismic
piezocone penetration testing SCPTu equipment was performed at nineteen locations
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The shear wave velocity test locations were located at
the test boring locations designated as Boring Nos 1 through 12 and 101 through 107
After drilling and sampling at each test boring location the boring was backfilled
with 0 25 inch coated bentonite pellets Water was placed into the hole to allow the
pellets to swell soften and conform to the borehole sidewalls The bentonite acted as a
coupling medium from the seismic piezocone penetrometer with the surrounding soil
The test was performed to depths of about 46 6 to 52 feet below the existing ground
surface using seismic piezocone penetration testing SCPTu equipment
The seismic shear wave velocity test consists of hydraulically pushing a 10 ton
steel electronic subtraction cone with an apex angle of 60 degrees and an area of
1 55 square inches 10 square centimeters into the softened bentonite backfill The
cone carries a uniaxial horizontal geophone to detect the arrival of a shear wave
propagated from the ground surface The seismic measurements are made when the
SCPTu has stopped and a shear wave is sent into the subsurface A shear wave is
generated at the surface by striking a loaded plank with a switched hammer The
propagation time of the wave from the hammer blow to the cone was measured at each
depth interval The vector difference of these depths divided by the time difference
provided the average shear wave velocity over the depth interval The shear wave
velocity profile measured at the probe location is presented on Plates B 1 through B 19

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Four Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 7012 Method C were performed
on selected core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the basalt
rock encountered Unconfined compression test results are presented on Plate C 1
Fourteen pH AASHTO T 289 and fourteen Minimum Resistivity ASTM G 57
tests were performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration The
test results are summarized on Plate C 2
Eleven Chloride Content and eleven Sulfate Content
tests were performed on selected samples obtained from our field exploration The
chloride and sulfate content tests were performed by Test America and the test results
are summarized on Plate C 3
Eight Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The
test results are provided on Plate C 4
Eight Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 C 136 including four Hydrometer
tests ASTM D 422 were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification Graphic presentations of the
grain size distribution are provided on Plates C 5 and C 6
Nine Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested The test results are
presented on Plates C 7 through C 15
Thirteen Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on
selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength characteristics The test
results along with the stress strain curves are presented on Plates C 16 through C 28
Seven Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates C 29 through C 35

Native Soil We assume the cables are to be directly buried and the native soil may be
used as the cable trench backfill Once the cables are energized the heat from the
cables will dry the soil and the thermal resistivity will increase as shown in the thermal
dryout curves The extent of the dry zone around the cable s will be a function of the

resistivity value of not less than W should be used for the cable rating This
is based on the assumption that the native soil will be installed at the in situ density and
moisture content The cohesive nature and the stiffness of the native soil will make it

the installed density is 90 of the in situ value a thermal resistivity of W
may apply

REPORT FOR SEISMIC REFRACTION AND SURFACE WAVE
SURVEYS FOR A WIND FARM PROJECT KAHUKU HI
This letter report presents the results of the geophysical surveys performed by Global Geophysics
The seismic surveys refraction and surface wave were carried out at seven 7 turbine locations
in September and October 2009 for the First Wind Kahuku project near Kahuku Hawaii The
mat foundations for the structure will be 50 ft by 50 ft in plan dimensions The objective of the
investigation is to determine the lateral and vertical variations of soils at these locations
GEOPHYSICAL METHODS AND FIELD PROCEDURES
Seismic refraction was used to measure compressional wave velocities and surface wave was
used to calculate the shear wave velocities of soil and rock The following paragraphs describe
the methods and field procedures
Seismic refraction
Seismic refraction is the traditional method for determining the rock velocity for rock rippability
using a controlled energy source hammer blank shotgun shells or chemical explosives to
generate a seismic signal The seismic signals are received by a series of geophones 24 for
example that are connected to a seismic cable laid on the ground surface in a linear manner The
geophones evenly spaced along the geophone cable are placed on the ground surface The
seismic energy source is discharged at several places along the array and off both ends
The seismic wavelets travel through the earth to the geophones that convert the acoustic energy in
the ground to an electric signal in the geophone cable The seismograph detects the arriving
electric signals with respect to time and stores the records for future data processing The seismic
data is processed to determine the seismic velocity of the earth material through which the energy
has traveled and to model the subsurface geology This geophysical model depicts the earth in
cross section showing the velocity and thickness of the subsurface layers below the seismic line
The seismic refraction survey was conducted using a Geometrics Geode 24 channel digital
seismograph The sensors were Mark Products vertical geophones and the seismic energy
sources were a 20 lbs sledge hammer The weight drop method appeared to provide sufficient
energy and it was subsequently used for the survey The typical field procedure consisted of

Multi channel Analysis of Surface Wave MASW
The Multichannel Analysis of Surface Waves MASW method determines variations in surface
wave velocities with increasing distances and wavelengths The data from these measurements
are used to model the shear wave velocities of the subsurface This information can then be used
to infer rock soil types stratigraphy and soil conditions
The MASW survey requires a seismic source to generate surface waves and at least 24
geophones to measure the ground response at increasing distances from the source Surface
waves are a special type of seismic wave whose propagation is confined to the near surface
medium The depth of subsurface penetration of a surface wave is directly proportional to its
wavelength In a non homogeneous medium surface waves are dispersive i e each wavelength
has a characteristic velocity stemming from subsurface heterogeneities The relationship between
surface wave velocity and wavelength is used to calculate the shear wave velocity of the medium
with increasing depth
The seismic source can be either active or passive depending on the application and location of
the survey Examples of active sources include explosives weight drops and vibrating pads
Examples of passive sources are drill rigs road traffic micro tremors and water wave action in
near shore environments Geophone measures the arrival time of the various components of the
surface wave train traveling from the seismic source
The surface wave velocity with respect to frequency called the dispersion curve is determined
by measuring the delay time in wave propagation between the geophones The dispersion curve is
then matched to a theoretical dispersion curve using an iterative forward modeling procedure
The result is a profile of shear wave velocity versus depth This shear wave profile can be used
with other parameters such as density to estimate the dynamic shear modulus of the medium as a
function of depth
The MASW data were collected at the same time as the seismic refraction
ANALYSIS AND RESULTS
The lateral resolution is determined by the geophone spacing in p wave profile and by
shot spacing in s wave profile In general the p wave method provides better lateral
resolution but limited to the velocity inversion issue at some of the locations The MASW
method is an inversion modeling process which will not detect velocity changes of thin layers
