2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age
and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae
Volcano became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank is
partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
The project site is on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano and the
geomorphology and subsurface conditions of the area are directly related to
glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the
genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions in the project vicinity
are dominated by fossil Pleistocene age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits
laid during high stands of sea The coral algal reef deposits and related deposits are
inter layered with terrigenous sediments and late series volcanic deposits The base of
the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late Pliocene and
early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building
activities ceased approximately 2 5 to 3 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells
indicates that the Island of Oahu has subsided by as much as 1 200 feet since the
cessation of this early volcanic activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970

During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level
changes as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As
the great continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell because less water
was available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted
global sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu
remained essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in
nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both
higher and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials The lower sea stands caused
streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial erosion of the
upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively
high energy conditions within and along the streams During periods of no significant
sea level changes the continued stream action extended the alluvial deltas and fans
seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal sediments The project site is
generally underlain by coral formation and alluvial deposits consisting of silty clays

backfill material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads on the buried
sewer pipes we recommend using an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic

Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great effect upon the load
supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore pipe bedding is an important design
consideration In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the sewer line pipes
For the new sewer manhole structures a granular bedding consisting of 12 inches
of No 3B Fine gravel is recommended under the manhole base A non woven filter
fabric should wrap around the bedding material with a minimum 24 inches overlap
at the top of the bedding layer

Backfill
In general free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to
about 12 inches above the pipes or 12 inches above the groundwater level to
provide adequate support around the pipes Where soft lagoonal deposits are
encountered a layer of non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be
provided to wrap around the initial trench backfill To reduce the potential for backfill
settlement and pipe damage it is critical to use free draining materials to reduce
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Compaction by water ponding or jetting
should not be allowed
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of approved excavated
on site soils or select granular fill material The backfill should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum
8 inch horizontal loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent
relative compaction
Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill
below the pavement grade should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to
the maximum dry density
In general backfill materials should consist of non expansive granular materials
generally less than 3 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent

Construction Considerations
The settlement estimates presented in the Pipe Settlement subsection assume
that proper construction procedures and good workmanship will be practiced by the
contractor during pipeline installation operations Based on our knowledge and past
experience with similar soft and or loose subsurface soil conditions significant pipe
settlement may be attributed to the construction methodology and equipment used
by the contractor Therefore special attention should be given to the installation
procedures for the proposed sewer lines especially in soft and or loose subsurface
soil conditions to reduce the potential for future settlement distress
As previously recommended the sewer lines near the water table soft deposits
should generally be supported by approximately 6 inches of a No 3B Fine gravel
bedding layer over a subgrade stabilization layer consisting of No 2 Rock wrapped
in a non woven filter fabric The non woven filter fabric is used to provide for
separation between the coarse aggregate No 2 Rock and the fine grained soils
In general a minimum overlap of 2 feet should be provided between the ends of
each roll of the filter fabric placed along the pipe alignment under dry conditions If
the filter fabric is not properly overlapped it is possible that the gravel bedding
material placed may migrate through the openings in the filter fabric and mix with
the underlying soft and or loose soils This may result in pipe settlement due to loss
of cushion materials into the soft and or loose subsoils
Due to the very soft subsoil conditions anticipated near the invert elevations care
should also be exercised during the backfilling operations of the trench excavation
Heavy equipment and vibratory action of the compactor may exert high pressures
onto the pipe and supporting soils during the trench backfilling operations resulting
in potential settlement of the very soft deposits and pipe during construction
Therefore the size of the equipment used to compact the backfill around and above
the pipe should to be limited to less than 4 tons in weight

3 3 Excavation
In order to install the sewer lines excavations below the existing ground surface
will be required for the project construction Based on the planned sewer line inverts
and very soft subsurface soil conditions encountered in our borings we envision that
temporary shoring of the excavations will be required for the majority of the utility line
installation
Our field explorations encountered a surface fill layer over very soft loose
lagoonal deposits from the ground surface to near the sewer line inverts Therefore we
anticipate that conventional excavation techniques using backhoe equipment may be
considered for the planned excavations In general we believe that interlocking steel
sheet piling may be used for temporary shoring purposes especially where dewatering
of the excavations will be necessary For shallow excavations and excavations in open
areas we believe that a cantilever sheet pile shoring system may be considered For
deeper excavations and excavations adjacent to structures utilities and pavements we
recommend using interlocking steel sheet piling with horizontal bracing for excavation
support in order to reduce the potential for significant adjacent ground movement
The contractor should develop and implement a monitoring program to detect
ground movement and or subsidence adjacent to the excavations which may result in
damage to nearby structures and pavements It should be noted that minor settlements
may occur during and after installation of the sheet piles Therefore the contractor
should retain a qualified geotechnical engineer to design and evaluate the shoring
system used

exploration and the available information the surface fill soils and underlying very
soft lagoonal deposits encountered in the borings may be excavated with
conventional earthmoving equipment

Excavation Support
We anticipate that excavation depths up to about 10 feet may be required in

are to be used as shoring
Use of a sheet pile shoring system may also serve as a cut off wall to aid in the
dewatering operations which is further discussed in the Dewatering section of this
report The sheet piles should be driven with a suitable hammer to a sufficient
depth to reduce the potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the
amount of dewatering within the excavations It should be noted that the
excavations will likely encounter very soft soil deposits at the bottom of the
excavation Therefore there is a potential for bottom heave in these very soft soil
conditions The contractor should carefully evaluate the potential for bottom heave
and design the shoring system accordingly
