INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of a geotechnical engineering exploration
performed with respect to a value engineering proposal for Station 0 42 to 4 50 of the
proposed Castle Hills Access Road connecting the northern end of Po okela Street in the
existing Castle Hills Subdivision towards the north to Keaahala Road The site and its
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The site conditions in the southerly portion of the proposed access road alignment
Station 0 42 to Station 4 50 consist of a marshy area and a drainageway Soft and
loose soils were encountered to depths of about twenty 20 to forty 40 feet in the
marshy area and about ten 10 to twenty 20 feet in the drainageway The current
construction contract includes installation of compaction piles to improve the soft soil
conditions of the proposed access road However due to higher construction costs and
uncertainties associated with construction quantity and duration and performance of the
compaction piles a value engineering proposal is being considered to substitute the
compaction piles with wick drains and a surcharge program to serve the same purpose
This report describes an engineering evaluation with site specific geotechnical engineering
exploration and laboratory tests performed to evaluate the proposal
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain subsurface data within the project
site in order to develop a generalized soils data set for the evaluation of a value
engineering proposal pertinent to soft ground improvement of the proposed project

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
It is Our Opinion that it is feasible to improve the soft ground condition by means
of a preloading program with surcharge and wick drains to consolidate and strengthen
the subsoils as proposed in the value engineering proposal Detailed discussion and
recommendations are presented in the following sections
Compactions Piles
The present construction contract for the access road includes provision of
compaction piles to improve the existing soft ground conditions The compaction piles
are to be installed by driving a twelve 12 inch square or round end mandrel into the soft
soils to form a hole The hole is to be backfilled with five 5 foot increments of base
course rock rammed into place by driving the mandrel with a hammer delivering about
10 000 foot pounds energy and reaching a driving resistance of about 20 to 25 blows per
foot The placing and ramming shall be repeated until the hole is filled up to about the
level of a five 5 foot working platform The recommended compaction pile spacing is
three 3 feet on centers on a rectangular grid
It is Our Opinion that the installation of compaction piles entails the following
uncertainties
1 Installation of compaction piles is one of several generally accepted
methods of improving soft or loose ground conditions The method
consists of forcefully introducing granular material sand and or gravel into
the loose soil mass thus attaining improvement by densification However
the effectiveness of the compaction piles to densify soft cohesive soils such
as silt and clay is questionable Besides it is almost impossible to predict
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 6
the degree of densification and improvement of the soft material nor the
overall long term performance of the completed construction
2 There is also an uncertainty regarding how much granular material will be
required to be rammed into the holes to attain the required driving
resistance Therefore construction time and cost requirements are difficult
to predict
3 It is proposed to construct the compaction piles from the outer row closest
to the adjacent residences towards the center to lessen disturbance of
adjoining properties However no additional measures are provided to
reduce potential disturbance or damage such as ground heaving or
slab wall cracking to the adjoining property which may be caused by
ground vibration shaking or squeezing of the soft subsoils during the
installation of the compaction piles
4 The potential of disturbance to previously installed compaction piles due to
the ramming process is difficult to assess
Preloading Program
It is Our opinion that a preloading program can be used to improve the soft ground
conditions encountered approximately between Station 0 42 and Station 4 50 of the
proposed access road construction
Based on our field exploration laboratory test results and previous mass grading
experience in the vicinity of the Castle Hills Subdivision settlement due to embankment
fills of heights proposed for this project may be anticipated to be on the order of two 2
to three 3 feet
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2816 00 Page 7
Our laboratory test results indicated that secondary compression ratios of the soft
subsoils range from about 0 0037 to 0 0109 Based on the boring information and the
laboratory test resuits estimated secondary settlement is anticipated to be in the range
of one half 0 5 to one 1 foot
A preloading program with surcharges of four 4 to eight 8 feet high is
recommended to preconsolidate precompress and to strengthen the soft subsoils in
advance of the construction of the infrastructure and final pavement section We believe
that precompression induced by surcharge can be achieved and can provide an effective
means in eliminating prior to construction of infrastructure and pavement primary
consolidation and a large portion of secondary compression that would otherwise occur
Construction observations with field instruments are required to confirm the design
assumptions regarding the preloading program
Due to construction time constraints vertical wick drains are recommended to
hasten the anticipated precompression The vertical wick drains will provide much shorter
water drainage paths to accelerate the consolidation process and hence result in a
shorter time to reach the anticipated precompression magnitude Based on an average
obtained from the laboratory test results and the wick drain manufacturer s design chart
wick drains with spacings of two 2 to three 3 feet on centers can be used to obtain
ninety 90 percent consolidation within a period of three 3 months An illustration of the
wick drain spacing design is shown on Plate 3
To reduce potential disturbance or damage to the adjoining property an
interlocking steel sheetpile section is recommended along the property line that adjoins
the adjacent residence

SUMMARY OF RECOMMENDATIONS
A preloading program with surcharge is recommended to precompress the in situ
soft subsoils prior to construction of the proposed infrastructure and pavement Due to
construction time requirement only a 3 month period is available to preload the on site
soft subsoils the use of wick drains is recommended to accelerate the required
precompression Detailed recommendations are discussed below
1
To reduce potential disturbance to the adjacent residence an interlocking
steel sheetpile wall approximately twenty five 25 feet deep is recommend
ed along the eastern property limit from Station 0 40 to Station 1 40 Due
to previous subdivision site grading fill placements gravel and boulders may
be encountered at the recommended sheetpile location Therefore a heavy
vibratory hammer should be utilized to drive the recommended sheetpiles
Driving refusal may be encountered at depths of less than 25 feet
depending on the amount and density of gravel and boulders that may be
encountered during the sheetpile installation Over excavation of this
surface bouldery layer should be considered as an alternative in order to
install the sheetpiles down through the upper fill crust
Subdrians with minimum six 6 inch diameter perforated pipes are recom
mended along both the eastern and western property limits The perforated
pipes should be embedded in trenches of about 4 foot wide and 8 foot
deep The trench should be lined with a layer of geotextile fabric such as
Mirafi 140N and backfilled with No 3B Fine gravel or ASTM C 33 No 67
gravel tamped to improve the interlocking resistance

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling seven 7 borings
to depths of 26 5 to 50 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Both truck mounted and hand carried
portable drilling equipment were used to drill the borings
The soils encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who continuously monitored the drilling
operations The classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and testing
in the laboratory All soils were classified in accordance with the Unified Soil Classification
System A summary of the materials encountered are presented on the Boring Logs
Soil samples were obtained from the borings by driving a 2 inch standard split
spoon sampler with a 140 pound hammer free falling 30 inches The blow counts to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample
depths Hand pushed or lightly driven thin wall shelby tube samples were taken at regular
intervals in the soft materials
During the drilling and sampling operation field vane shear tests were performed
by using a rectangular vane with 2 5 inch diameter and 5 inch length The vane was first
pushed into the soft material and then rotated The change of the measured torque with
angular rotation was monitored Maximum torques obtained for both the undisturbed and
remolded soil conditions were recorded The test results are shown on the Boring Logs
and also summarized on Plate A 8 1
