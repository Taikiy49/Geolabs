GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING EXPLORATION 
UPGRADE SOFTBALL FIELD 
MOLOKAI HIGH SCHOOL 
ISLAND OF MOLOKAI, HAWAII 
Dear Mr. Leong: 
This letter report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the upgrade of the softball field within Molokai High School on the Island of 
Molokai, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This letter report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering 
recommendations resulting from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses. These recommendations are intended for the design of the structure foundations 
and site preparation only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject 
to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
Project Considerations 
The planned improvements at the softball field within Molokai High School athletic 
field facility will involve a 13-foot by 13-foot restroom facility, a 15-foot 6-inch-high 
scoreboard, and a 19-foot 5-inch high backstop, and press box/storage.  Our geotechnical 
engineering exploration focused on the foundation support of these proposed structures 
and the site grading and preparation. 
A total of three boreholes were drilled to a depth of 16.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Drilling and sampling was performed using a truck-mounted drill rig. Soil 
samples were collected using split spoon samplers and modified California samplers. A 
percolation test was conducted in one of the borings. One bulk soil sample at the existing 
ground surface was also collected for CBR test. The boreholes were backfilled with 
cuttings after drilling and sampling.

Site Description 
The project site is in the central portion of the Island of Molokai, Hawaii. Based on 
the available topographic survey and our observations, the majority of the existing site is 
generally flat with ground elevations of about 784 to 788 feet above Mean Sea Level 
(MSL) as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The northeast portion of the site has higher 
elevations up to around +800 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL), and the southwest portion of 
the site is lower with elevation of around +780 feet MSL. 
Subsurface Condition 
Our subsurface exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling 
three borings (designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3) extending to a depth of 16.5 feet, 
conducting one field percolation test, and collecting one bulk sample at the existing ground 
surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operation on a 
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified 
Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, other soil 
samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Log of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
In general, hard to very hard residual silty clay was encountered in the top 10 feet, 
underlain by very stiff residual silty clay down to 16.5 feet, the maximum depth drilled in all 
three borings.  
A field percolation test was conducted at a depth of about 5 feet below the existing 
ground surface in Boring No. 2 to evaluate the permeability of the in-situ soil for the design 
of the restroom facility. The test result is presented on Plate A-4.

Laboratory Testing 
The following laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples obtained 
from our field exploration. 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
their physical properties. Test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic 
presentations of test results are provided on Plate B-1. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D 3080) was performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the strength of the in-situ soil. Graphic presentation of test result is presented on 
Plate B-2. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) test (ASTM D 1883) was performed 
on a bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the structural support characteristics 
of the soils. Graphic presentation of test result is presented on Plate B-3. 
Two Ring Swell tests (ASTM D 4546) were performed on selected remolded 
samples to evaluate the swelling characteristics of the soils. Test results are presented on 
Plate B-4. 
Discussion and Recommendations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, we recommend using shallow 
spread footings and/or continuous footings bearing on the re-compacted on-site materials 
to support the restroom facility and press box/storage building planned. An allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design 
of footings bearing on the hard to very hard silty clay. This bearing value is for 
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as 
those caused by wind or seismic forces.  
We recommend supporting the scoreboard and backstop on minimum 18-inch 
diameter drilled shaft foundations in order to develop the required bearing and lateral load 
resistances for the new structures.  Based on the anticipated structural loads and the 
subsoil conditions encountered, the drilled shafts should be embedded a minimum of 
about 8 feet below the bottom of the footing.

Detailed discussions and recommendations for foundation design and site 
preparation are presented in the following sections. 
Minor Structure Foundations 
The minor structures, such as the restroom and storage buildings, may be 
supported on shallow spread and/or continuous footings. An allowable bearing pressure 
of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design of footings 
bearing on the re-compacted on-site materials (minimum 90 percent relative 
compaction). This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by 
one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent 
finished grade. 
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 
45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench, 
or the footings should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed as recommended herein, we 
estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated static design loads for footings 
bearing on the re-compacted on-site soils to be on the order of less than 1 inch. 
Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on similar materials should 
be on the order of about 0.5 inch or less. 
Lateral loads acting on the structures supported on shallow foundations may be 
resisted by a combination of friction between the base of the foundation and the bearing 
materials and by passive earth pressure developed against the near-vertical faces of 
the embedded portion of the footings. A coefficient of friction of 0.40 may be used for 
footings bearing on the re-compacted on-site fill soils. Resistance due to passive earth 
pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per cubic 
foot (pcf) (above groundwater level) for footings embedded in the on-site fill materials. 
This value assumes that the concrete for the footings is cast neat against on-site 
materials. Otherwise, the soils around the footings should be well compacted (minimum 
of 90 percent relative compaction). Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive 
pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches below the finished grade should be 
neglected. 
A Geolabs representative should observe footing excavations prior to placing 
reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions and the 
required embedment depths.

Slabs-On-Grade 
We anticipate that the ground floor slabs for the restrooms and storage building 
will consist of slabs-on-grade. Based on the structural load and existing subsurface 
conditions, we recommend using 4-inch thick concrete slab-on-grade. 
For the interior slabs, we recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of 
cushion fill consisting of open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) below the 
slabs. The open-graded gravel cushion fill would serve as a capillary moisture break 
and would provide uniform support of the slabs. To reduce the potential for excessive 
future moisture infiltration through the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings, 
we recommend an impervious moisture barrier on top of the open-graded gravel 
cushion layer.  
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at 
least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade. It should be emphasized that the 
areas adjacent to the slab edges should be backfilled tightly against the edges of the 
slabs with relatively impervious soils. These areas also should be graded to divert water 
away from the slabs and to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs. 
Scoreboard and Backstop Foundations 
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances, the 
proposed new scoreboard and backstop structures may be supported by a foundation 
system consisting of cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts.  Based on the anticipated 
structural loads and the subsoil conditions encountered, we recommend using concrete 
drilled shaft with a minimum diameter of 18 inches extending a minimum of 8 feet below 
the bottom of the footings into the residual silty clay. The cast-in-place concrete drilled 
shafts would derive vertical support principally from skin friction between the shafts and 
the surrounding soils.

Lateral Load Resistance 
The lateral load resistance of drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of the 
surrounding soil, the stiffness of the drilled shaft, allowable deflection at the top of 
the drilled shaft, and the induced moment in the drilled shaft. The lateral load 
analyses were performed using the program LPILE-V6.0 for Windows, which is a 
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference, laterally loaded deep foundation 
program originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin. The program 
solves for deflection and bending moment along a deep foundation under lateral 
loads as a function of depth. The analysis was carried out with the use of 
non-linear “p-y” curves to represent soil moduli. The lateral deflection was then 
computed using the appropriate soil moduli at various depths.

Foundation Settlement 
Settlement of the drilled shaft foundation will result from elastic compression of 
the shaft and subgrade response of the foundation embedded in the soils 
encountered. We anticipate that the total settlements of the drilled shafts are 
estimated to be less than 0.5 inch. We believe that a significant portion of the 
settlement will be elastic and should occur as the loads are applied. 
Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations 
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend, to a significant extent, on 
the friction between the shaft and the subsurface hard to very hard residual silty 
clay. In general, the performance of drilled shafts depends significantly upon the 
contractor's method of installation and construction procedures. Therefore, 
proper construction techniques especially during the drilling operations are 
important.  The drilled shaft subcontractor will need to demonstrate that the

proposed drilling equipment (and coring tools, where appropriate) will be capable 
of installing the drilled shafts to the recommended depths and dimensions. 
To reduce the potential for caving-in of the drilled holes, temporary casing may 
be required during the drilled shaft installation.  Care should be exercised during 
removal of the temporary casing to reduce the potential for "necking" of the 
drilled shaft. Therefore, a minimum 5-foot head of concrete above the bottom of 
the casing should be maintained during removal of the casing.  
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids may have a significant effect on the 
supporting capacity of the drilled shaft; therefore, use of drilling fluids should not 
be used. 
A low-shrink concrete mix with high slump (7 to 9-inch slump) should be used to 
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils. The 
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for 
segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix.  In addition, the concrete 
should be placed promptly after drilling (within 24 hours after drilling of the holes) 
to reduce the potential for softening of the sides of the drilled holes.  
A Geolabs representative should be present to observe the drilling and 
installation of drilled shafts during construction. Although the drilled shafts are 
designed based primarily on skin friction, the bottom of the drilled hole should be 
relatively free of loose materials prior to placement of concrete. Therefore, 
Geolabs’ observation of the drilled shaft installation operations is necessary to 
confirm the assumed subsurface conditions and should be designated a “Special 
Inspection” item in accordance with Section 1704 of the International Building 
Code (2003). 
Concrete Walkways 
We envision exterior concrete walkways and exterior flatwork will be required for 
the proposed project.  In general, we recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick 
aggregate subbase below the exterior concrete slabs.  The aggregate subbase should 
be moisture-conditioned and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction.  The subgrade soils below the subbase should be scarified to a depth of 
about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  To reduce the 
potential for substantial shrinkage cracks in the slabs, crack control joints should be 
provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways (or slabs) with expansion joints 
at right-angle intersections.

Existing structures to be demolished should be completely removed.  The

to be abandoned should be removed, and the resulting excavation should be properly

structure should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete or Controlled Low Strength 
Material (CLSM) under low pressure. 
After clearing, the areas to receive fill and finished subgrades in cut areas should 
be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  The 
compaction requirement for the finished subgrades in areas subjected to vehicular 
traffic should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557.  
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density.   
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft and/or loose areas 
and replacement with well-compacted fill.  A Geolabs representative should evaluate 
the need for over-excavation in the field.

We understand that an emergency vehicle road may be required for the project.  
In general, we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the proposed pavements for this 
project would consist primarily of passenger vehicles with some light trucks and 
occasional heavy trucks.  Therefore, we have assumed generally light traffic loading 
conditions for pavement design purposes.   
We anticipate that the pavement subgrade soils will likely consist of 
re-compacted on-site materials.  On this basis, we recommend using the following 
preliminary pavement design for this project.

Underground Utility Lines 
In general, we recommend using good construction practices for the installation 
and backfilling of the trenches for the proposed utilities. The contractor should 
determine the method and equipment to be used for excavation, subject to practical 
limits and safety considerations. The excavation should comply with the applicable 
local, state, and federal safety requirements. The contractor should be responsible for 
trench shoring design and installation. Trench shoring and bracing should also conform 
to the appropriate health and safety requirements. 
We recommend a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes. Free-draining granular materials such 
as No. 3B Fine gravel should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches 
above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the 
compaction effort on the backfill, thus reducing the possibility of pipe damage. It is 
critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids 
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. 
Improper backfill material and placement could result in backfill settlement and pipe 
damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill, from the level 12 inches above the pipes 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade, should consist of on-site soils or select 
granular fill material. The backfill should be placed in maximum 8-inch level loose lifts 
and mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce 
the potential for future ground subsidence. Where trenches are below pavement areas, 
the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.
