2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and
its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene
Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased
approximately 2 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicates that the island
of Oahu has subsided as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic
activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global
sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and
lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of terrigenous sediments in
the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at corresponding higher
elevations and lagoon marine sediments in the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the volcanic dome of Koolau
During the late Pleistocene Epoch volcanic activity was renewed on Koolau with
eruptions of the Honolulu Volcanic Series In the vicinity of the site eruptions along the

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by Walter Lum
Associates Inc in 1967 and by our office in 1999 The subsurface information from our
previous explorations are included in Appendix B of this report for reference Our current
field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three additional borings
designated as Boring Nos 101 through 103 extending to depths of about 21 5 feet below
the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the drilled borings are shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our current and previous field explorations disclosed that the project site
is generally underlain by approximately 5 to 7 feet of fill materials ranging from stiff clays to
medium dense sands with a mixture of cobbles and boulders Due to the nature of the
undocumented fills encountered at the site we believe that localized zones of loose
and or soft materials may exist within the upper fill layer that are not readily identified in
the borings drilled during our field explorations
The near surface fill materials were generally underlain by in situ soils consisting of
medium dense to very dense silty sands extending to the maximum depth explored of
approximately 24 feet below the existing ground surface Pockets of loose sandy soils
were encountered in some of the borings drilled between depths of about 10 and 15 feet
below the existing ground surface
Based on the Soil Survey of Islands of Molokai and Lanai
State of Hawaii by the United States Department of Agriculture the predominant soil type
in the project area is the Koko series silt loam KsB This soil type is described as dark
reddish brown or dark brown silt loam loam or clay loam that has subangular blocky
structure The substratum consists of cinders and tuff at a depth of approximately 3 to 6
feet below the top of a typical soil profile The substratum appears to correspond to the
dense silty sand deposits encountered in our field explorations performed at the project
site

3 1 Foundations
Based on our field explorations conducted at the project site we anticipate that the
majority of the project site is generally underlain by surface fill materials overlying in situ
silty sand deposits at greater depths In general we believe that slab on grade
foundations with thickened edge footings may be used to support the lightly loaded
three story buildings planned at the site An allowable bearing pressure up to about
may be used for the design of foundations bearing on 12 inches of
non expansive select granular fill materials over the recompacted in situ soils or new
compacted fills This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
The perimeter thickened edges of the slabs should be embedded a minimum of
18 inches below the lowest adjacent exterior grade The embedment of the interior
thickened edge footings may be reduced to a minimum depth of 12 inches below the
adjacent finished grade The base of the thickened edge should have a minimum width of
12 inches Graphic illustration of the recommended foundation conditions is presented on
the Schematic Section Slab On Grade Foundations Plate 3 of this report
The bottom of the footing excavations should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and recompacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with

ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
For footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground the footings
should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of
8 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of the slope Footings
located adjacent to planned retaining walls should be embedded deep enough to reduce
the potential of surcharging the retaining wall foundations or the retaining walls should be
designed to resist the additional structural loads
Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded
below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and
to reduce the potential for foundation settlement If foundations are designed in
accordance with our recommendations we estimate total settlements to be less than 1
inch with differential settlements on the order of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the 12 inch
non expansive select granular fill materials Resistance due to passive earth pressure
may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per
foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils around footings are well compacted
The passive pressure should be reduced for foundations located on slopes Unless
covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil
should be neglected

3 2 Slabs On Grade
Based on the preliminary grading plans provided we understand that the upper
buildings in the sloping grounds to the north may be underlain by differing fill thickness
and or straddle across cut and fill transition on the order of up to about 10 feet thick In
addition dense boulders and soft loose materials may be present in the upper fill layer
in the southern flat portion of the project site alongside Hawaii Kai Drive To reduce the
potential for distress to the new buildings planned for the project we recommend that
the slab on grade foundations be supported on at least 12 inches of non expansive
select granular fill materials The capping fill should extend beyond the new
slab on grade footprint a minimum of 24 inches
For the slab on grade foundations with thickened edge footings the prepared
capping fill and underlying subgrade soils should be kept moist prior to placement of
cushion fill and concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the
subgrade we recommend that the soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted fill To reduce the potential for drying of the subgrade soils and to reduce
the costs of form construction we recommend that the slab edges be cast neat against
the soil excavations The slab edges should incorporate a sufficient amount of top and
bottom longitudinal steel reinforcement The top and bottom steel reinforcement bars
should be connected by stir ups The ends of the stir ups should extend into the floor slab
area and should be tied to the welded wire mesh The welded wire mesh in the
slab on grade should be embedded at the proper height in the concrete to provide
adequate slab reinforcement Blocks or saddles may be used to raise the wire mesh A
stiff grade of wire mesh is thus recommended A structural engineer should be consulted
for design details of this thickened edge which is intended to function both as a moisture
barrier and as a perimeter bearing wall footing
In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill be
placed below the slabs to provide the slabs with uniform support The cushion fill should

3 3 Retaining Structures
Due to the sloping terrain at the northern part of the project site we anticipate
that retaining structures may be required to provide grade separation along some
locations of the project site The following general guidelines may be used for preliminary
design of retaining structures
Wall Foundations
In general we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the Foundations section of
this report Wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches In addition
wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade For sloping ground conditions
the footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 8 foot setback distance
measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope
Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the slope should be constructed in
stepped footings

Backfill behind retaining walls should consist of granular soils or select fill

Over compaction of the retaining wall backfill should be avoided In general an
active condition may be used for walls that are free to deflect by as much as 0 5

Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads within a

distribution equal to 50 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the
entire height of the wall may be used for design Additional analyses during
design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of point loads and line
loads

Fill Materials
General fill materials required to raise the existing ground surface to the proposed
finished subgrades may consist of the excavated on site soils provided that they
are free of vegetation deleterious materials and rock fragments greater than
6 inches in maximum dimension General fill materials should also have a
laboratory CBR value of 12 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent
or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883
The 12 inch non expansive select granular fill required under the building slabs
should consist of select granular material such as crushed coral or basalt The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR
value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when
tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883
Aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials should meet the material
requirements for Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in Sections
703 06 and 703 17 respectively of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road
Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 HSS Imported fill materials should
be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these recommendations prior to
delivery to the project site for the intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The
non expansive select granular fill materials should be placed in level lifts of about

8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction The aggregate subbase
and base course materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers

equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed for

Cut and Fill Slopes
In general permanent cut or fill slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of
two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter Fills placed on slopes steeper than
5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of
the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest point
and continue up in level horizontal compacted Jayers in accordance with the above
fill placement recommendations Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling
and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face It
should be noted that materials greater than 6 inches in largest dimension should
not be exposed at the fill slope face
In the event that over cutting of a slope occurs keying and benching requirements
should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design grade with
sliver fills Water should be diverted away from tops of slopes and slope planting
should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the
finished slopes

Subdrainage
Due to the sloping terrain at the project site seepage conditions may be present at
localized areas across the project site Therefore we recommend that provisions
be incorporated into the construction documents to provide for subdrains in the
following areas
e Atall springs and seepage areas
e In other areas of the site where seepage is observed during and or after
grading or as recommended by the Engineer during construction
In general subdrains should consist of 4 inch diameter perforated pipes with
perforations facing down Subdrains should be surrounded and underlain by at
least 6 inches of drainage material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation or equivalent The No 3B Fine gravel is also equivalent to the
Size 67 coarse aggregate in Section 703 2 of the HSS Table 703 il A non woven
filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent should wrap around the drainage
material In general subdrain trenches should be at least 12 inches wide at least
2 feet deep and should be capped with granular fill A sketch of the Typical
Subdrain Detail is presented on Plate 4 Subdrains should be daylighted into
drainage ditches culverts or other drainage structures for proper discharge
In addition to the relief of subterranean seepage it is our opinion that a surface
drainage system such as interceptor ditches and concrete swales should be
provided along the upper portion of the project site for storm water and surface
run off disposal
Falling Boulder Hazard
Properties adjacent to the upslope areas may be subjected to boulders rolling down
the hillside The falling boulders are a general phenomena occurring on all ridge
slopes on the Island of Oahu Various catchment methods and boulder barriers
can be used to reduce the hazard of falling boulders Some methods that may be
considered include but are not necessarily limited to the following

We envision that new utility lines will be required to serve the proposed

bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be

ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce
the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It
is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of select fill material The backfill
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in maximum
8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative
compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are
below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement finished
grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

APPENDIX C

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One one inch ring swell test was performed on a relatively undisturbed sample to
evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are summarized
on Plate C 1
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plates C 2 and C 3
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates C 4 and C 5
