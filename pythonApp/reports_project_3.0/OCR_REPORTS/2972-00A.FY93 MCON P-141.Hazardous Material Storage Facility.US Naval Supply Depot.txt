SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The project site is located on the western central portion of the island of Guam the
largest and southernmost of the Mariana Islands The approximate location of the project
site is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The foundation of the island is composed of volcanic rocks including pyroclastic
materials which were extruded during early to mid Tertiary time Voicanic activity ceased
during the Oligocene epoch Following the cessation of the volcanic activity extensive
coral algal reef deposits were laid down on the island from the Miocene through
Pleistocene epochs
Tectonic uplift has raised these relatively old reef deposits Subsequent marine
activity created lagoonal environments where fine grained sediments accumulated Within
the last century development of the military facility has shaped the existing land by
grading and filling

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The significant geotechnical engineering considerations for this project are the
presence of a soft and compressible organic clayey deposit below the near surface fill
crust and the presence of a variable groundwater table as encountered in our borings
Significant long term settlements of the organic clayey deposit would occur under the
proposed new fill and building loads Based on our analysis we estimate that a shallow
foundation system bearing on the near surface fill crust would be subjected to settlements
on the order of four inches 4 under the anticipated structural loads Therefore a
shallow foundation system may not be used to support the proposed storage facility
without remedial measures to improve the settlement characteristics of the underlying soft
and compressibie soiis
We considered several alternative measures including surcharging to improve the
ground such that a shallow foundation system may be used to support the proposed
storage facility Surcharging the site to accelerate the anticipated ground settlements was
considered impractical because of the organic nature of the compressible deposit A
significant portion of the estimated settlements would be long term with continued
settlements after completion of construction
Because the compressible layer was encountered at relatively shallow depths from
the existing ground surface about 9 to 12 feet below the surface in our borings we also
considered removal of the compressible deposit and replacement with structural fill
Removai of the compressibie deposit and replacement with structural fill is a viable option
because of the relatively shallow depths of excavation In addition we understand that
this alternative was used successfully at a nearby site if the removal and replacement
alternative is elected the building loads may be supported on a shallow foundation

Fill and Backfill
Structural fill material needed to backfill the excavation and to raise the ground
surface to the proposed finish grades should consist of imported granular material
such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be
well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than three inches 3 in
largest dimension The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more
and should have a maximum swell of less than one 1 percent when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 Imported fill material should
contain a maximum of 20 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve

The near surface fill crust consisting of sandy limestone materials encountered in
our borings may be stockpiled and reused as a source of structural fill material
provided that they meet the above requirements However the excavated on site

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fill and backfill should be placed in level lifts not exceeding ten inches 10 in
loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557 78 test procedures All permanent fill slopes should have
inclinations of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter
All fill or backfill below the water level should consist of clean granular material
such as No 3B Fine gravel or drain rock as recommended above The material
is generally self compacting therefore no mechanical compaction is required
Additional densification may be obtained by use of a vibratory compactor on the
final lift of the material
A filter fabric such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent will be required above the self
compacting backfill prior to placement of fill or backfill material to reduce the
potential migration of fines through the voids of the self compacting material

Drainage
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Also it is advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce water infiltration
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain
all surface run off away from the slab and footing foundations
DEEP FOUNDATION ALTERNATIVE
As an alternative to the above shallow foundation recommendations a deep
foundation system consisting of piles driven through the near surface fill crust and the
underlying soft compressible deposit may be used to support the proposed storage
facility The piles would be driven into the dense limestone formation for bearing support
Since fills on the order of up to two feet 2 are planned within the limits of the proposed
structure and significant structural loads will be imposed on the floor slab future settle
ments of the ground surface are anticipated As a result we recommend that the floor
slab for the proposed storage facility be structurally supported on pile foundations to
reduce the potential for differential settlements between a floor slab supported on grade
and the columns supported on piles if piles are selected for support of the structure

Pile Foundations
Because of the anticipated high column loads for the proposed storage facility we
recommend that pile foundations deriving support principally from end bearing on the
underlying limestone formation be used to support the proposed structure Based on the
data obtained from our field exploration and laboratory testing piles driven through the
upper fill crust and compressible deposits to a depth of approximately 25 to 60 feet below
the surface is recommended to develop the required capacity to support the proposed
structure
Vertical Pile Capacities
We recommend that 16 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles with
an allowable compressive load capacity of 50 tons per pile be used to support the
proposed storage facility structure The piles will derive support primarily from end
bearing in the dense limestone deposit encountered at various depths between 25
and 60 feet below the ground surface A minimum pile spacing of four feet 4
center to center is recommended to avoid reduction in pile capacity due to group
action
An allowable uplift capacity of 20 kips per pile may be used in the design The
recommended pile capacities are for sustained loading For transient loads such
as wind or seismic forces the compressive and uplift load capacities may be
increased by one third
For initial design and cost estimating purposes an average pile length of about 45
feet may be used Production pile lengths should be estimated after completion
of a test pile program as discussed in a following subsection

Foundation Settlements
Pile settlements are estimated to be on the order of less than one inch 1 with
differential settlements between piles not exceeding about one half that amount
Settlements should be elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
Predrilling
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered during our field exploration it is our
opinion that the piles should be able to penetrate through the near surface fill crust
without predrilling This assumption should be confirmed during the test pile
program proposed for the project Therefore we recommend that unit prices for

necessary during the test pile program we recommend that predrilling be included
in the production pile driving operations Recommendations pertaining to
predrilling would be presented after completion of the test pile program
Pile Driving Criteria
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy of
at least 40 000 foot pounds The hammer should be equipped with an energy
control level such that driving within the loose and soft deposits may be performed
at a reduced energy level to reduce the development of tensile stresses within the
piles which could cause damage cracking to the piles
Because local variations in soil strength characteristics are likely to exist in the
bearing soils the supporting capacity of each pile must be confirmed in the field
during driving This will be accomplished by using a driving resistance procedure
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2972 00 A Page 25
such as the Engineering News Record ENR formula or the more sophisticated
Wave Equation Analysis to evaluate pile bearing capacity The driving criteria will
be developed after the selection of the pile driving equipment for production pile
driving Additional pile driving criteria are as follows
1 The driving process should be continuous without interruption for each pile
A pile may be rejected or its design capacity modified when the driving
process is interrupted for more than four 4 hours
2 During easy driving when the pile tip is still within the soft and compressible
soil the hammer stroke should be carefully controlled with reduced energy
levels so that excessive tensile stresses are not induced in the pile
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy
to the top of the pile and also reducing damage to the piles Geolabs Hawaii
should review the pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate if the equipment is
capable of installing the piles to the recommended capacity Due to possible
variation in the length of the piles unit prices should be obtained during bidding
for add ons shorter piles additional welds if used at the connection etc

Floor Slabs
We understand that significant live loading of the floor slabs are planned for the

Slab and the anticipated loading on the siab As a result to reduce the potential
differential settlements between the structure supported on piles and the floor slab
supported on grade we recommend that the floor slabs be structurally supported on pile
foundations Floor slabs should be designed to span between pile foundations
A base course layer beneath the structurally supported floor slab will not be
necessary However the floor slab should be supported on a prepared subgrade The
subgrade soils beneath slabs should be scarified to a depth of eight inches 8 and
compacted to at least 95 percent of the maximum dry density determined in accordance
with ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on pile foundations may be resisted by a combination of the
resistance of the pile soil system to deflect and passive resistance on the front side of pile
caps and grade beams Due to the possible future settlement of the ground surface
sliding friction between the bottom of the slab and the supporting subgrade should be
neglected

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content unit weight and Atterberg Limits tests were performed on
selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The
resuits of these tests are presented on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
One 1 Atterberg Limits test was performed on a selected sample of the soils to
evaluate their liquid and plastic limits The test results are shown on Plate B 1
Three 3 sieve analyses were performed on selected samples to evaluate the
gradation characteristics of the soils The test results are also summarized on Plate B 1
One 1 direct shear test was performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate its
shear strength characteristics The test results are presented on Plate B 2
One 1 consolidation test was performed on a relatively undisturbed soil sample
to evaluate the consolidation characteristics of the soil The test results are presented on
Plate B 3
