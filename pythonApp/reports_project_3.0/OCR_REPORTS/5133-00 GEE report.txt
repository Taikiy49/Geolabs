In accordance with our revised fee proposal dated we have
been providing geotechnical engineering services for the Beretania Line Booster Station
project This letter report summarizes our work on the project and presents our findings
and geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our field exploration
laboratory testing and engineering analyses for the above project The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
letter report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Beretania Booster Station will be located in the courtyard of the Honolulu
Board of Water Supply BWS at the intersection of the Beretania Street and Alapai Street
The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
The Beretania Line Booster Station project consists of a booster pump station with
a maximum capacity of 60 million gallons per day mgd and a 42 inch diameter pipe
connection to the existing distribution system The project involves excavation and
construction for the installation of two vertical turbine pumps and one connector line The
approximate location and layout of the proposed new construction are shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2 The existing utilities in the courtyard will need to be demolished and
relocated
Our geotechnical engineering exploration is limited to the proposed booster pump
station and connector line only Corrosion study along the pipe connection is not included
in our scope of work

FIELD EXPLORATION
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling two borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of about 50 and 13 feet below the
existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped
with continuous flight hollow stem augers The approximate locations of the borings
drilled at the site are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Boring Log Legend Plate 3 Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings
Plates 4 and 5
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface exploration was conducted on Two borings
were drilled at the project site to depths of 50 and 13 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The project site is covered by asphalt concrete and base course In general our

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings we recommend
that concrete walls planned along excavated faces be designed to resist lateral earth
pressure resulting from excavations We recommend that shallow foundations consisting
of isolated spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the new concrete
walls planned for the project An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per
square foot psf may be used for the design of foundations of the new structures bearing
on the recompacted in situ soils or new compacted fills Based on the field exploration
results we anticipate that dewatering may be required for the excavations and
constructions at the project site Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of
wall foundations excavations dewatering and other geotechnical aspects of the project
are presented in the following sections of this letter report

Wall Foundations
Based on the relatively competent subsurface soil condition encountered on the
project site we recommend that the proposed concrete walls be supported on shallow
foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings An allowable bearing
pressure of 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for design of footings bearing
on well compactied structural fill or on site cemented sand The bearing value is for
dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as
those caused by wind and seismic forces
The bottom of the footing excavation should be of loose material prior to the
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete If soft and or loose materials are encountered
at the bottom of footing excavations they should be over excavated to expose the
underlying firm or dense materials The resulting excavations should be backfilled with
concrete or the footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying competent
material Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of the soils as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1557
If the foundation is designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate total settlements of the foundations bearing on the in situ
soils to be less than 1 inch Differential settlements between footings are estimated to be
on the order of 0 5 inch
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and bearing materials and by passive earth pressure
developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings A
coefficient of friction of 0 30 may be used for footings bearing on in situ soils Resistance
due to passive pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf for footings embedded in the
well compacted structural fills or in situ soils This value assumes that the concrete for
footings is placed directly against the well compacted structural fill materials or in situ soils
Unless covered by pavement or slab the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below
finished grade should be neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative from
Geolabs prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the foundation
bearing conditions and the required embedment depths

Subsurface Soil Permeability
Based on our borings soft to very hard cemented sand sandstone layer may be
anticipated below the groundwater level The actual subsurface soil sandstone
permeability may range broadly and also vary locally in terms of orders of
magnitude The permeability of the subsoils sandstone at the site will be highly
dependent on the degree of cementation of the sandstone Therefore the
contractor should pay special attention to the site specific dewatering plan for the
excavations
Dewatering Method
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection
of equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and
he she should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be
required during construction depending on the conditions encountered The
dewatering method selected should have minimal impact on the groundwater
level surrounding the excavation As previously indicated the sandstone layer at
this site may be highly permeable depending on the degree of cementation and
is capable of transmitting moderate quantities of water
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents should
be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater
table and or to exclude the water from the excavations to allow construction of the
structures under safe and dry conditions These works or systems may include but
are not limited to grouting cut off walls tremie concrete plugs or any combination
of the above and or other possible methods
It should be noted that the subsurface conditions within the excavation depths at
the site consist of boulders and rock formation which are capable of transmitting
moderate quantities of water Because the project is located in a developed area
the dewatering operation should be conducted in such a manner that the
dewatering will not cause areal ground subsidence which may cause potential
damage to the existing structures and utilities Therefore consideration should be
given to a dewatering system that includes a cut off wall It should be noted that
disposal of the dewatered effluent would also be problematic at the site considering
the generally low permeability of the near surface soils at the site

Dewatering Considerations
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of a
suitable method of dewatering
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or
damage to existing structures roads and environment
b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and provide
safe and dry working conditions in the excavation
C The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to
dewatering It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be
retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His or her qualified representative who should
be required to be continuously present on site during dewatering activities will
have the best opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during
construction and to implement as soon as possible necessary precautionary or
remedial measures including but not limited to slowing down or stopping the
dewatering operations
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular soils may
be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations
should be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the
excavation bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be
performed without pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and should
be coordinated with shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not
adversely affected Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in
a blowing or heaving of the excavation bottom or sides
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches
connected to excavations If this occurs the granular bedding and or backfill in
the utility trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal
groundwater drawdown resulting in settlements and potential damage to utility
lines and or other existing adjacent structures

Utility Trenches
We anticipate that new utilities may be planned for the new development In
general good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of
the trenches for the new utilities at the site The method and equipment to be used for
trench excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and
safety considerations In addition the excavations should comply with the applicable
federal state and local safety requirements Trench shoring design and installation
should be the responsibility of the contractor
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that conventional
excavation methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation at the site The
upper soils encountered at the site are generally stiff dense However some of the utility
trench excavations will likely encounter the medium hard to very hard cemented sand as
described in the boring logs which may pose some excavation difficulties during the
trench excavation
In general we recommend that granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used for support under the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this
free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill
could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site soils with a maximum
particle size of 6 inches or select granular fill material The backfill should be placed in
maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence
Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
