Project Consideration
The proposed site is located on a recent landfill area developed for
the Honolulu harbor pier facilities It is proposed to construct a cold
storage facility with approximately 25 000 square feet of freezer and
storage area on the vacant lot The facility will include two freezers
located at the south eastern part of the site a chiller at the north a
dry storage area at the northwest a loading zone and an equipment room
at the southeast An employee parking zone is also planned at the
western part of the site

Purpose and Scope
The purpose of this investigation was to explore the subsurface soil
conditions at the site in order to develop soil data for the design of
the site grading foundation and pavement sections for the proposed
structure
The original scope of investigation included drilling and sampling
of four 4 60 foot borings However due to the poor soil condition
down to the 60 foot depth our first boring was extended in an effort to
locate suitable bearing stratum After our boring had extended to 140
feet below the ground surface without encountering a hard bearing
stratum it was felt that due to the potentially high cost of the deep
pile foundation two shallower borings should be made in other portions
of the site to evaluate the uniformity of the upper soils and to
determine if shallow foundations could be utilized As a result the
scope of our investigation was modified to include drilling and sampling
ot the three borings in lieu of four 60 foot borings Also included in
the scope were laboratory testing of samples obtained engineering
evaluation of the field and laboratory data development of design
recommendations and report preparation
Detailed description of our field exploration and laboratory test
results are presented in the appendices of this report

Site Description
The site which was formerly owned by Chevron U S A Inc is
located near the end of the outlet of Kapalama Canal at Pier 38
Honolulu Harbor Oahu Hawaii It is presently bounded to the north by
the Kapalama Canal to the south and west by access roads leading into
the pier and to the east by the storage facilities which consist of two
large concrete fuel tanks still owned by Chevron The total area of the
site is estimated to be approximately 32 500 square feet
The site is flat with fences installed along the property line The
gates located at the southeastern corner provided the only access into
the site Ground cover consists mainly of silty coral gravel

Discussion and Recommendations
General
The project site is underlain by two different compressible layers
a thin about 5 foot thick layer of loose sandy lagoonal deposits and a
thicker layer of very soft clayey harbor sediment Settlement should be
anticipated due to the weight of the new fill and building loads To
induce ground settlements prior to foundation construction we recommend
that the required new fill and possible surcharge be placed as soon as
possible
Ideally the building construction should be delayed until most of
the settlement due to site filling have occurred Then spread footing
foundations may be used for the building support with allowance for some
future settlements due to the structural and live loads If the
anticipated settlement are not tolerable pile foundations would have to
be considered

Our recommendations are discussed in detail and presented in the
following sections
Settlements
Based on the findings we believe that ground settlements will occur
due to 1 the weight of the new fill 2 the building load and 3
additional settlements that may develop if a heavy floor loading is
applied
The anticipated areal settlement due to 41 inches of new fill is
estimated to be on the order of 5 to 7 inches Based on the laboratory
test results we estimate that the time required for 90 of the
settlements to occur would be about 2 years In order to accelerate the
settlements and reduce the waiting time a surcharge fill may be
utilized as described in the subsequent section
Relatively high structural loads are anticipated due to insulation
and maintenance requirements For the anticipated column load of about
97 kips estimated additional settlements are on the order of 2 to 4
inches If practicable efforts should be made to reduce the building
loads
Substantial live loads due to food storage may occur Calculated
settlement under a floor loading of 300 p s f is on the order of about 4
to 6 inches
Surcharge Fill
A surcharge program would minimize ground settlements due to the new
fill and floor loads Also it could help to minimize the possibility of
downdrag caused by the new fill which will reduce the bearing capacity
of piles
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1478 00 Page 6
The length of the surcharge period will depend on the amount of
surcharge used If a 10 foot high surcharge is used estimated surcharge
time would be on the order of 6 months Unfortunately due to the close
proximity of Kapalama Canal the surcharge fill within 40 feet of the
northern boundary of the site would have to be eliminated in order to
minimize the possibility of a slope failure along that boundary
Therefore larger subsequent settlements should be expected or a longer
waiting time required for this portion of the building
Periodic optical survey readings to monitor settlement should be
taken by the project surveyor and transmitted to the soils engineer to
determine the actual surcharge time required
It should be understood that although surcharge can minimize ground
settlement due to the new fill additional footing and slab settlements
due to the building and floor loads would still occur
Foundation Design
Several alternatives for foundation design of the structure were
studied and are presented below
Shallow Spread or Continuous Footing with Surcharge Fill
After completion of the filling and surcharging program shallow
spread or continuous footing foundation may be used As discussed
above a severe limitation to this method would be the limited
surcharge height which could be placed on the northern portion of
the site
In general spread footings should be placed at a level as high as
possible to reduce the footing load applied over the underlying
compressible deposits We believe that a maximum net allowable soil
bearing pressure of 2000 p s f may be used for the design
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1478 00 Page 7
Foundations should not be placed below about elevation 3 feet
otherwise special design may be required due to the close proximity
of the soft subsoils and water level
The anticipated footing settlement under 97 kips of column loading
applied after the new fill has been placed and allowed to settle
is estimated to range between 2 to 4 inches For wall footing 1 75
feet wide settlement with similar magnitude is anticipated
Approximately 70 of these settlement will occur slowly probably
over a number of years after completion of the project
If the settlements are acceptable to the owner then we recommend
that all footing bottoms be embedded at least 12 inches below the
lowest adjacent grade Lateral loads acting on the structure may be
resisted by passive earth pressure developed against the buried
vertical faces of the foundation system and by friction resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundations and the bearing soil
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be calculated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 p c f This assumes that the soil
around the foundation is well compacted The frictional resistance
may be calculated by using a coefficient of friction of 0 4 The
above resistance values are maximum values and do not contain a
factor of safety
Pile Foundation
If the amount of settlement predicted or the settlement waiting
time are not tolerable then pile foundation may be utilized The
piles should be used to support the building loads in conjunction
with a structural floor slab to minimize the fill required
GEOLABS HAWAII

For pile foundation support a suitable bearing stratum is yet to be
explored by drilling additional deeper borings However based on
past experience in the general area and for preliminary cost
estimate purposes it could be assumed that such a stratum would be
located at a depth of 200 feet from the ground surface
In addition we believe that the allowable bearing capacity of the
piles will be affected by the possible downdrag caused by ground
settlement It is estimated that a net capacity of 50 tons per pile
can be developed when a inch octagonal precast concrete pile
is used Additional deep borings would be required to provide
design data for pile foundation
Freezer Unit
Due to the proximity of the water level provisions for a capillary
water break are an important consideration to minimize the possibility of
frost heave under the freezer units
Recommendations for a suitable capillary break can be provided when
more definite plans for the site grading and foundations are available
It should be noted that the use of a structural slab system with a
suitable open space between the slab and the ground level could provide
an effective method to minimize frost heave
Site Grading
Site Preparation
The overall site preparation will include removal of any vegetation
debris and other structures The top 10 inches of the site should
then be scarified and recompacted to at least 90 of the ASTM
D 1557 78 maximum density before new fill is placed

Fill Material
The material below the slab or pavement base course and within 16
inches of the finished grade should be of granular material with a
CBR value greater than 25 and the maximum size of the particle less
than 6 inches
The material below the top 16 inches may consist of the general
borrow type material as required in Section 16 of the Standard
Specification for Public Works Construction City 4 County of
Honolulu May 1975
Fill Placement
All fills and backfills should be placed in horizontal lifts not
exceeding 9 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a dry
density of at least 95 of the maximum ASTM D 1557 78 maximum
density Cohesive materials if used below the top 16 inches plane
may be compacted to 90 minimum
Pavements

be considered whenever appropriate For pavements outside the cold

Utility Lines
The underground utility lines should be placed as late as possible
after the completion of site grading to minimize post installation
settlements In addition flexible joints should be considered
particularly the connections at building should have a higher degree of
flexibility to allow differential settlements
Trench excavations would require proper shoring particularly if
patches of soft organic clays or silts are encountered Since dewatering
may affect adjacent structures a soils engineer should be consulted if
the water level is to be lowered
The materials and compaction requirements for the backfilling of the
trench should be consistent with those recommended under the Fill
Placement section
Generally for backfilling the utility trenches the compacted
density of the backfill from the pipe bed up to 12 inches above the pipe
should be a minimum of 85 of ASTM D 1557 78 The remaining portion
should be 90 If trenches are located under roadways the top 16 inches
of the backfill from the finished grade should be compacted to 95
minimum

Design Review
Plans and specifications for foundation and pavement should be
forwarded to the soils engineer for review and written comments prior to
construction to see that the intent of our recommendations is included
Construction Monitoring
The earthwork operation including excavation fill placement and
compaction should be observed by the soils engineer during construction
to see that adequate compaction has been obtained and that the subsurface
conditions are consistent with the findings of our investigation
We also recommend that the footing excavations be inspected by the
soils engineer prior to placement of concrete or reinforcing steel to
check that adequate bearing has been obtained and that the subsurface
conditions are consistent with the findings of our subsurface
investigation
If the structural columns are to be supported on a pile foundation
the pile driving operations should be monitored by a qualified soils
engineer to verify that the piles have been driven adequately and in
accordance with our recommendations
If conditions encountered during construction are different from
those presented in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made
