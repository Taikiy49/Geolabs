SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling 12 borings and 6 test pits 
within the Ho`opili Development – Phase 17 project site. Our field exploration at the 
project site generally encountered medium stiff to hard cultivated and/or surficial fills 
overlying soft to hard silty and clayey alluvial soils with varying layers of medium dense 
to very dense sandy soils extending to the depths of about 20 to 21.5 feet below the 
ground surface. 
Our laboratory tests indicate that the near-surface clayey soils exhibit low to high 
shrink-swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. 
When considering the lightly loaded structures planned for the Phase 17 development, 
we anticipate that the shrink-swell potential of the on-site soils could result in structural 
distresses to the new buildings and pavements unless special subgrade preparations 
are implemented during construction. 
We recommend the site grading preparation consist of the following procedures 
to create a reworked soil layer within the entire grading limits. 
1. 
Removal of 12 inches of the near-surface cultivated soils after the clearing 
and grubbing operation. These soils should be considered “unsuitable” 
because they contain an appreciable amount of organic materials. These 
soils should be disposed of properly off-site or used in landscaping areas, 
if appropriate. 
2. 
Over-excavate and remove an additional 12 inches of soil and 
moisture-condition to 2 to 4 percent above the optimum moisture content 
for use as general fill, provided the soils are free of vegetation, debris, 
deleterious material, and other unsuitable materials. 
3. 
Prior to replacing the moisture-conditioned soil as fill, the bottom of the 
over-excavation should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches, 
moisture-conditioned between 2 and 4 percent above the optimum 
moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative 
compaction. 
The reworked soil layer would reduce the future soil swelling/heaving potential by 
elevating moisture contents and reducing compaction requirements, as well as 
decreasing the affinity of the clayey soil for water and creating a barrier to reduce water 
infiltration into the underlying dry clayey soil. It should be noted that the 2-foot thick 
reworked soil layer should also be constructed below the finish subgrade and pad grade 
in cut areas.

General fill material should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose

a stiffer foundation system in order to reduce differential movement and resulting in 
angular distortion. We also recommend installing a vapor barrier underneath the 
Waffleboxes to a minimum of 2 feet beyond the slab perimeter to reduce water 
infiltration beneath the slab foundations. 
If conventional concrete slabs-on-grade with thickened edge footings are used 
in lieu of the Wafflemat foundation system, we recommend providing 3 feet of 
non-expansive, select granular fill material beneath the concrete slabs-on-grade and 
extending the material beyond the perimeter of the slab to a minimum of 3 feet. 
The individual homeowners and Homeowners Association should be strongly 
cautioned against over-irrigating or landscaping within 3 feet around the house structures, 
which could adversely affect the proper performance of the foundation system. In addition, 
a qualified geotechnical engineer should be retained to provide recommendations to the 
individual homeowners or the Homeowners Association if improvements or expansions 
are planned beyond the original floor area. 
Actual field infiltration tests were not conducted during our field exploration. Based 
on our experience and the clayey ground condition in nature, we anticipate that the 
infiltration rate of the on-site clayey soils may be in the order of less than 0.5 inches per 
hour to none. Therefore, the use of infiltration systems for the stormwater runoff 
management at the Phase 17 site may be restrictive.

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows 
from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope, forming a broad plateau 
now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the southeastern flank of the 
Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering, followed by erosion of the Schofield Plateau, 
generated sediments that were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to the 
south, these sediments have accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments 
and coral/algal reef formations, forming a sedimentary wedge. The thickness of the 
sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H-1 Highway to 
over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu. 
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to 
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. 
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of 
the calcareous reef materials.

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is generally situated near the central portion of the Ho`opili 
Development in the Ewa area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The site is bordered by 
cultivated agricultural land to the north, west, and east and the Phase 13 Development 
to the south.

preparation for future planting. Several unpaved access roads traverse across the 
Phase 17 site.  
Based on our site observations and Google Earth™, the project site generally 
slopes gently down from north to south. Ground surface elevations range from about +142 
to +101 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) at the northern and southern portions of the project 
site, respectively.

accordance with Chapter 20, Site Classification Procedure for Seismic Design, 
contained in ASCE Minimum Design Loads for Buildings and Other Structures, 2016

maintained at all times during and after the construction to avoid ponding and 
submerging conditions.

3.1.2 Site Perimeter Cut-Off Trench 
Shrinkage cracks have been known to occur in expansive soils in the Ewa/Kapolei 
area during prolonged dry weather conditions and were observed at the existing 
ground throughout the subdivision development. Since shrinkage cracks tend to be 
a conduit for water infiltration into the subsurface soils, we recommend 
implementing special site preparation procedures consisting of installing a cut-off 
trench along the perimeter of the grading limits at the project site bordering an 
undeveloped area. We recommend a cut-off trench at least 3 feet deep and 5 feet 
wide consisting of the on-site clayey soils placed and compacted with elevated 
moisture contents, as illustrated on Plates 5.1 and 5.2. As an alternative, the cut-off 
trench may be backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM). 
The on-site soils used for this cut-off trench should be placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to about 2 to 4 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction. The intent of this cut-off trench is to create a barrier to reduce water 
infiltration into the underlying dry clayey soil from the shrinkage cracks on the 
adjacent properties.

3.2 
Building Foundations 
Based on our field exploration, we recommend the planned building structures be 
supported on a shallow Wafflemat foundation system bearing on the 2-foot reworked 
soil layer, or general fills placed below the finished pad grade. The Wafflemat is a 
post-tensioned slab-on-grade foundation system. This system utilizes a series of 
recycled plastic boxes, “Waffleboxes”, which are placed with the open side down on the 
soil. The Waffleboxes are arranged to form deep ribs or footings throughout the floor 
system, creating a very stiff foundation. A post-tensioned concrete slab is monolithically 
poured between and over the boxes. The empty space beneath the boxes allows for soil 
expansion, reducing the uplift of the slab. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to about 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) 
may be used for the design of the Wafflemat foundations bearing on the compacted 
reworked soil layer or general fills placed in accordance with the recommendations 
presented herein. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased 
by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Generally, minimum subgrade preparation is required for the Wafflemat 
foundation system. Considering expansive soil conditions, we recommend preparing the 
Wafflemat subgrade in accordance with Subsection 3.1.1 – Site Preparation as 
presented herein. Prior to placing the Waffleboxes, the subgrade soil should be 
moisture-conditioned to between 2 and 4 percent above the optimum moisture content, 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction, and covered with a vapor barrier 
to maintain the soil moisture. If the on-site clayey soils dry out and shrinkage cracks 
develop, the subgrade soils should be rescarified, moisture-conditioned, and 
recompacted again.

Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In 
general, the lower the resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried 
metallic structure. Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will 
contribute to the corrosion of metallic objects. 
Based on the results of laboratory corrosivity tests, the near-surface soils at the 
project site exhibit minimum resistivity values ranging from approximately 490 to 
860 ohm-cm, indicating that the near-surface soils are extremely corrosive (Corrosion 
Rating of 1) to buried metallic structures. Therefore, we recommend properly designing 
near-surface metallic substructures (such as piping) for protection against the potential 
for corrosion. 
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH, chloride content, and sulfate content found in the soil. 
In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), sulfate 
content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures.  
Based on the relatively low values of chloride content and sulfate content tested on 
the in-situ materials, we believe the near-surface soils at the project site generally may be 
considered “non-corrosive” and either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement may be used for 
the concrete in contact with the ground. It may be appropriate to consult with a 
professional corrosion engineer to review the test results and provide detailed 
recommendations for corrosion protection.

3.7.3 Design Subgrade Conditions 
Based on our field exploration, the pavement subgrades along the proposed new 
roadway alignments are generally underlain by soft to hard clay. We anticipate that

3.7.5 Site Preparation Below Pavement Areas 
As mentioned previously, the near-surface clayey soils at the project site are dry 
and friable and exhibit low to high shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to 
fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. Prior to placing the 18-inch 
non-expansive, select granular fill, we recommend that the subgrade be properly 
scarified to a depth of at least 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to between 2 and 
4 percent above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted between 85 
and 90 percent relative compaction, as specified in Subsection 3.1.1 – Site 
Preparation herein.

3.7.6 Fill Materials Below Pavement Areas 
The 18-inch capping layer below the pavement sections should consist of 
non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic 
materials. The material should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles 
no larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension and should contain between 10 
and 30 percent of particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have 
a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.  
Aggregate base course and subbase course should meet the material 
requirements for Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in Sections 31 
and 30, respectively, of the Standard Specifications for Public Works 
Construction, City & County of Honolulu, September 1986. Geolabs should test 
imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations prior to 
delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
3.7.7 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
The non-expansive, select granular fill should be placed in level lifts of about 
8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The 
compaction requirement for the final lift of the select granular fill should be 
increased to at least 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course and 
subbase course should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in

(percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
As an additional effort for stability and uniform compaction, we recommend 
proof-rolling the non-expansive, select granular fill material prior to placing the 
aggregate base course (for flexible pavement section) or the aggregate subbase

to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
either well-compacted fill or aggregate base/subbase material. 
3.7.8 Pavement Drainage 
The recommended pavement sections assume that adequate drainage will be 
provided for the paved areas, which should be sloped and drainage gradients 
maintained to carry surface water off-site. Surface water ponding should not be 
allowed on-site during or after construction. When concrete curbs are used to 
isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas, we recommend 
extending the curbs a minimum of 2 inches into the soils below the aggregate 
base or subbase layer to reduce the potential for migration of excessive 
landscape water into the pavement section. Alternatively, a subdrain system 
could be constructed to collect excess water from landscaping irrigation. For 
long-term performance, we recommend constructing a subdrain system adjacent 
to the paved/landscaped areas.

thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the

3.9 
Drainage 
The finished grades outside the building structures should be sloped to shed 
water away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction. It 
is highly recommended to install gutter systems around the buildings and to divert 
discharge away from the foundation and slab areas. Excessive landscape watering near 
the foundations and slabs (within 3 feet of the building perimeter) should be avoided. 
Planters next to foundations also should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and 
drains to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration into the subsoils. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
building foundations and slabs-on-grade because ponded water could cause subsurface 
soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength. The foundation excavations 
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting 
the concrete to reduce the potential for appreciable water infiltration into the subsurface. 
In addition, drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be 
maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
12 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 12, extending to depths drilled of about 
20 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, six test pits, designated as 
Test Pit Nos. 1 through 6, were each excavated to a depth of about 7.5 to 15 feet below 
the existing ground surface. Five bulk samples of the near-surface soils, designated as 
Bulk-1 through Bulk-5, were obtained to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and test pit locations are shown on the Site 
Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers. The test pits were excavated using a Hitachi 450 Excavator. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings and test pits by 
visual and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, 
Standard Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling 
operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further 
reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general 
accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for 
Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with 
ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock samples 
were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are 
presented on the Logs of Borings and Test Pits, Plates A-1 through A-18. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Thirteen Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Seven Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. In addition, two hydrometer tests (ASTM D422) were conducted on the 
samples to further evaluate the finer grain materials of the soils. Graphic presentations of 
the grain size distributions are provided on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
Twelve one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed 
and/or remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test 
results are summarized on Plate B-5. 
Five one-dimensional swell or settlement potential tests (ASTM D4546 Method 
A) were performed to evaluate the swell and/or compressibility characteristics of the 
remolded soil samples under different surcharge pressures. A soil sample is remolded 
to approximately 90 percent relative compaction (based on ASTM D1557) to simulate 
the anticipated field conditions. The remolded soil sample is allowed to settle until it has 
stabilized under a surcharge pressure. Water is then added to the soil sample to 
evaluate its collapse or swell potential in a saturated condition. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-6 through B-10. 
To evaluate the undrained shear strength of the soils, five Unconsolidated 
Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) tests were performed on selected in-situ soil 
samples in accordance with ASTM D2850. The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” sample. The 
test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-11 through B-15. 
Five laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-16 through B-20.
