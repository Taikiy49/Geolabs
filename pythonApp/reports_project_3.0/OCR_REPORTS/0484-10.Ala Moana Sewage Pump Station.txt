INTRODUCTION
The purpose of this investigation was to define subsurface
conditions and provide recommendations for the design and
construction of the foundations for the proposed Ala Moana
Sewage Pump Station and Force Main The findings of our
previous report are included with the current investigation
which was performed to provide detailed design recommenda
tions

Site Description
As shown on the enclosed general location map the proposed
site is adjacent to Fort Armstrong between Ala Moana Boule
vard and the entrance to Honolulu Harbor The essentially
level site is immediately adjacent to the existing pump sta
tion and is presently being utilized as a gravel parking
area for the adjoining Matson Container Terminal It is
bound on the south and west by the container terminal on the
north by the existing pumping station and on the east by
Keawe Street
Proposed Construction
The proposed new pumping facility has plan dimensions of 47
feet by 107 feet and an invert elevation of approximately
23 feet A new 84 inch box inlet will be constructed from
the existing pump station with an invert of approximately
13 feet The combined flow of the pumping stations will be
conveyed by a 78 inch force main to the proposed Sand Island
Treatment Plant
The remaining sections of the force main which includes the
Matson Container Yard section the Honolulu Harbor crossing
and the Sand Island portion are covered by separate subsur
face investigations and reports

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The proposed site is adjacent to Honolulu Harbor which is
part of the submerged coastal plain of ancient Oahu This
coastal plain is built mainly of coral reefs marine sedi
ments and alluvial sediments for the Koolau Volcanic Range
It is apparent that the coral algal reefs developed during
the late Pleistocene Epoch and grew in the shallow slowly
submerging embayment Influxes of non marine alluvial debris
clay and silt apparently smothered most of the reef
development Fluctuations of the sea level gave the harbor
its latest natural configuration Recent fill operations
have given Honolulu Harbor its present outline
The particular site for the proposed pump station appear to
be situated in an area mauka of the main coral reef where
thick layers of loose deposits of reef debris developed in
the protected area They were subsequently blanketed by
shallow water swamp deposits generally consisting of soft
cohesive soils During the recent stages in the site devel
opment the present surface level was obtained by fill
placed upon the swamp deposits
W 1975
Subsurface Explorations
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored in two
stages The initial investigation of 1973 included three
preliminary test borings Seven additional borings were
drilled as part of the current investigation to provide more
detailed information on subsurface conditions in the proposed
building areas
The test boring locations are shown on the enclosed Site
Plan Plate 1 A detailed description of the field explo
ration methods and accompanying laboratory testing is given
in the appendix along with boring logs and laboratory test
results
Pump Test
An additional 12 inch diameter boring was made at approxi
mately the center of the proposed pump station The hole
was cased with a 10 inch diameter perforated plastic pipe
for its full depth and utilized for a pump test to give an
indication of the relative permeability of the subsurface
soil Three inch diameter perforated plastic pipes were
observation wells during the pump test operations The pump
test results are shown on Plate 2
W O
Soil Conditions
Section A A Plate 3 shows a typical subsurface profile as
disclosed by the subsurface explorations The site is blan
keted by a 2 to 12 foot thick upper crust consisting of a
surface fill layer and an underlying layer of cohesive swamp
deposits In Boring 2 eleven feet of cemented sand is also
included within this soil grouping
The fill is generally a dense mixture of silty sand and clay
with some concrete coral and basalt gravel The underlying
Swamp deposits are typically soft to medium stiff clayey
silts with some medium dense sand layers
The upper crust is underlain by 18 to 25 feet of reef debris
consisting of loose sand and coral gravel with some silt
In Boring B 2 this layer had a thickness of only seven feet
It appears that the area near B 2 is a localized condition
not generally indicative of the subsurface conditions beneath
the remaining portion of the site
Hard finger or ledge coral was encountered beneath the reef
debris It is blanketed by a total of 19 to 31 feet of soil
The surface of the coral layer was encountered between ele
vations 19 feet and 28 feet The coral was generally hard
but did contain some sand filled pukas and area of medium to
hard finger coral
W O
The ground water was generally encountered at epproximately
mean sea level but varied from elevations 1 0 to 0 2
The ground water level fluctuates with tidal changes
The findings of the subsurface explorations are summarized
on page 7 Detailed descriptions of the soils and coral
encountered are given in the appended boring logs along with
the laboratory test results
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The subsurface explorations and laboratory testing disclosed
that the site is generally underlain by a thick deposit of
loose reef debris which will not provide adequate foundation
support without large settlements The foundations must
therefore bypass these upper soils and bear upon the under
lying coral
Foundations

of elevation 23 0 feet and assuming a 3 foot thick founda
tion the pump station foundations would be bearing at
approximately elevation 26 0 feet This is generally at or
below the elevation of the coral surface except near Boring
T 5 where the coral surface was not encountered until ele

Retaining Walls
The subsurface portion of the pumping station will essen
tially consist of a rigid non yielding rectangular box For
this type of structure where the walls cannot deflect ade
quately to develop active pressures the retaining walls
should be designed to resist earth pressures at rest equal
to an equivalent fluid pressure of 60 pcf above the water
table and 85 pcf below the water table The structure should
also be designed to resist hydrostatic uplift
Temporary Sheeting
To obtain the most economical design from each contractor it
is preferable to assign the responsibility for the temporary
excavation support to the contractor It is recommended that
the following soil parameters be utilized for the design of
the sheeting and bracing systems

Dewatering
Dewatering will be required to complete the excavation and
construction of the pump station and inlet sewer The exca
vation for the pump station will generally extend into the coral
formation to reach the proposed bearing level A pumping
test was conducted in the vicinity of the proposed pump sta
tion to observe the dewatering characteristics of the sub
surface soils The enclosed Plate 2 gives the test data and
shows the equilibrium water level measured during this pump
test
As can be seen from the test results the soils apparently
contain sufficient silt to reduce the permeability of the
soils and decrease the flow rates through the soils The
draw down curve is relatively sharp indicating a less per
vious strata but also indicating that closely spaced wells
may be required for the dewatering
W O
The surface of the coral may be irregular and may contain
some sand filled pukas The hard coral layer would limit the
penetration of driven sheeting
The possibility of high flows beneath the sheeting or through
sand filled pukas should be considered in the design of the
temporary dewatering system
It is anticipated that the dewatering will be accomplished
utilizing sheeting and open pumping For this type of dewa
tering the sheeting design must consider the seepage forces
and possible loss of soil strength The bottom of the exca
vation should be covered with a properly graded granular
filter blanket to facilitate the construction and pumping of
seepage water
Subsidence due to this method of dewatering should not affect
the adjoining structures If well points deep wells or other
methods which will lower the general water table are utilized
it will be necessary to evaluate the possible settlement of
the near by structures The settlements must be evaluated
individually for each proposed dewatering method
Inlet Pipe and Force Main
At the proposed entrance invert elevation of 13 feet for the
inlet sewer and the exit invert of elevation 0 to 2 feet
W 1975
the base of these pipes will be within the loose reef debris
Even though net loadings from these structures will be rela
tively low they will bear upon highly compressible soils
Stabilized Invert
If the pipes can be designed to withstand moderate settlements
of up to three inches the proposed drains can be supported
upon a stabilized invert of compacted gravel The compacted
gravel should be a minimum of three feet in thickness to
stabilize the invert equalize differential settlements and
to provide a dry working base during construction
Sheeting excavations will be required for the inlet and force
main construction Dewatering can be accomplished within the
sheeted excavation utilizing sump pumps in the base gravel
layer The sheeting should be designed for the soil parame
ters given in the above paragraphs and should extend a mini
mum depth of ten feet below the excavation to prevent piping
or heaving of the bottom
It is anticipated that settlements associated with this method
of construction will be from 2 5 to 3 0 inches Generally
the settlements will be relatively uniform except at the con
nection points to existing structures or to the new pump
station Since the new pump station will be essentially
founded upon incompressible foundation materials the possible
W O
2 5 inch differential settlement will be concentrated at the
intersection of the pipes and the new pumping station wall
It will therefore be necessary to design this portion of the
pipes for the high differential settlements This can gen
erally be accomplished by utilizing a reinforced concrete
bedding to distribute the differential settlements over a
longer length of the piping
Pile Foundations
Pile support is recommended for the inlet sewer from the
existing line to the new pump station Since the existing
line and the pump station will have little or no settlements
the differential settlements may be sufficient to damage the
sewer Pile foundation may also be required for the force
main to reduce the differential settlements
The piles should be driven to bear upon the coral formation
encountered between elevations 15 8 feet and 28 4 feet
The following pile capacities can be used for the foundation
designs

Backfill
The backfill material around the sides of the conduits should
consist of select free draining well graded materials com
pacted to approximately 90 per cent of the maximum density
It is anticipated that jetting would be the preferred method
of compaction however mechanical equipment can be utilized
With the exception of the upper crust the remaining on site
soils are generally suitable for this backfilling Compress
ible organic material rubbish debris or other deleterious
materials should not be utilized nor should rocks greater
than 8 inches in diameter be placed adjacent to the struc
tures
If jetting is utilized for the backfill compaction precautions
should be taken to prevent floatation of the pipe due to the
entry of large quantities of water into the excavation
Asphalt Paving
The present surface fill materials will provide good subgrade
support for the proposed pavement areas It is recommended
W O
that the pavements be designed for a CBR value of approxi
mately 20 This corresponds to a pavement section consisting
of approximately 2 inches of asphalt concrete and 6 inches
of base course material The base course should be compacted
to a minimum of 95 per cent of the maximum density
Inspection During Construction
It is recommended that the excavating backfilling and pile
driving operations be inspected on a full time basis to assure
that the recommendations are correctly implemented
The foundation bearing levels should also be inspected to
assure that adequate bearing has been reached

APPENDIX
FIELD EXPLORATIONS
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drill
ing three borings B 1 through B 3 in 1973 and seven borings
T 1 through T 7 in 1975 utilizing truck mounted equipment
The depths of the borings varied from 32 to 60 feet below the
existing ground surface The locations of the borings are
Shown on the enclosed Site Plan Plate 1
The soils were classified by visual and textural examination
in the field by our engineer who continuously supervised the
drilling operations The classifications were verified by
visual inspection and testing in the laboratory All soils
were classified in accordance with the Unified Soil Classi
fication System A graphic presentation of the soils encoun
tered is presented on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through
A 7 and B 1 through B 3
Soil samples were obtained by driving either a 2 4 inch I D
or a standard penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer
free falling a distance of 30 inches The blow counts to
drive the sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the boring
logs at the appropriate sample depth
