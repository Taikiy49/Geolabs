2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano became
extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and it was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank
Kalihi Valley was formed by stream erosion of Tertiary Age Koolau Range basalt
flows This initial period of erosion and valley carving was followed by several periods of
relative rise and fall of sea level which permitted valley filling by alluvium followed by
subsequent periods of erosion During the Quaternary Age Honolulu Volcanic Series lava
flowed down the valley and was subsequently partially covered by Quaternary Age
alluvium The project site is located on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano on the
slope of Kahauiki Ridge and to the northwest side of Kalihi Valley Continued recent
alluvial processes have covered the site with black to dark gray adobe clay

Our field exploration indicated that the in situ soils generally exist in a very moist to
wet condition Therefore it would be very difficult to achieve the normal 90 percent
relative compaction requirement without significant aeration of the in situ soils Since the

requirement will need to be lowered to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction for

expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
After clearing demolition areas at grade or designated to receive fill should be

yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require

at the end of each work day to reduce the potential for ponding and saturation are

The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal
compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations Fill
slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to
obtain a well compacted slope face In addition slope planting should be provided as
soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes

In general we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the parking lot of this project

maintenance vehicles Based on our field exploration and laboratory test results the

The aggregate base course and aggregate subbase should consist of crushed
basalt aggregate compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction CBR and
field density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during
construction to confirm the adequacy of the above section
Based on the above discussions on the in situ high moisture clayey soils we
believe that the pavement subgrade soils should also be recompacted to a minimum of
85 percent relative compaction in lieu of the normal 95 percent relative compaction Prior
to the placement of the aggregate subbase the clayey subgrade soils should be stabilized
and strengthened with the placement of a layer of woven geotextile fabric such as Mirafi
500X or equivalent overlain by 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel A layer of non woven filter
fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be provided between the upper
aggregate subbase and the underlying open graded No 3B Fine gravel layer The
geotextile fabric and the gravel layer will serve as a working platform for the pavement
construction as well as a subsurface drainage layer below the pavement areas

3 6 Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement surface
should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface water off the
pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should not be
allowed on the site during or after construction
To reduce the potential for water infiltration into the pavement structural section we
recommend that a subdrain be installed along the upper boundary of the new parking lot to
intercept subterranean seepage from the upper hillside The subdrain should be setback
away from the pavement edges to reduce the potential for water infiltrating into the
pavement areas In general the subdrain along this area should be on the order of about
3 to 5 feet deep and extend at least 12 inches below the bottom of the No 3B Fine gravel
layer as recommended above The subdrain should consist of a 4 inch diameter PVC
perforated pipe with perforations facing down The perforated pipe should be surrounded
and underlain by at least 6 inches of drainage material such as No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or equivalent The No 3B Fine gravel is also equivalent to
the Size 67 coarse aggregate in Section 703 2 of the HSS Table 703 Il A permeable
separator non woven filter fabric conforming to Section 648 of the Hawaii Standard

drainage material as shown on Plate 3

parking lot to collect the infiltrated water from the No 3B Fine gravel layer below the
pavement structural section The subdrain along this area should be in direct contact with
the No 3B Fine gravel layer underneath the pavement section to collect the infiltrated
water for proper discharge In general the subdrain should consist of 4 inch diameter
PVC perforated pipes with perforations facing down surrounded and underlain by at least

3 8 Drainage
It should be noted that surface and subsurface drainage at the site are especially
critical considering the sloping terrain and wet environment at the site Therefore the
finished grades should be sloped to shed water away from the slabs and foundations and
to reduce the potential for ponding around the structures Excessive landscape watering
near the slabs and foundations should also be avoided
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete
to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface Drainage
swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface
water runoff away from the slabs and foundations

The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling

augers The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field engineer geologist who observed the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by
visual observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general
conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 10
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of

30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third

Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Four one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples of
the near surface clayey soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the subsurface materials
The results of these tests are summarized on Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B 2
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the clayey soils encountered The results of
the direct shear tests are presented on Plates B 3 and B 4
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 5 and B 6
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 7 and B 8
