INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Aha Punana Leo Video Production Facility located in the Keaau area in the
District of Puna on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site
are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings based on the field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the
proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of building
foundations slabs on grade site grading pavements and leach fields only and are subject
to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project site is located within the existing Aha Punana Leo Video
Production Facility located along Keaau Pahoa Road in the District of Puna on the Island of
Hawaii We understand that it is proposed to construct a new one story building within the
above mentioned site The new building will be located to the north of the existing Ohia Hale
Building An asphaltic concrete paved parking area will be constructed just north of the new
building In addition a leach field will be constructed either to the east or the west of the new
building location Based on the topographic survey map the existing ground surface
elevations at the site are at about 496 to 498 feet Mean Sea Level MSL We anticipate
that minimal site grading work on the order of less than 2 feet may be required to achieve the
design finished grades
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our field exploration was to obtain an overview of the surface and
subsurface conditions at the site The subsurface information obtained was utilized to
develop a soil rock data set for the formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations
pertinent to the design of building foundations slabs on grade site grading pavements and
leach fields To accomplish this we conducted an exploration program generally consisting
of the following tasks and work efforts

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These include the following Kohala which has been long extinct Mauna Kea
GEOLABS INC
W O 4325 00 Page 4
which has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801
and Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is located on the northern flank of the Kilauea Shield Volcano which
has been built up by successive layers of basaltic lava flows These flows are of the
Pliocene to Pleistocene Epochs and belong to the Puna Volcanic Series The lava
formation encountered at the project site appears to be of pahoehoe flow which is
characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas These
cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes
are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like
structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow
cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered a surface layer of volcanic ash materials
consisting of medium stiff clayey silt with traces of basalt gravel of about 0 5 to 1 5 feet
thick The volcanic ash materials were underlain by dense silty basalt cobbles and gravel
extending to depths of about 3 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface The silty basalt
cobbles and gravel were underlain by medium hard to hard basalt formation extending to
the maximum depth explored of about 21 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in our borings at the time of our exploration
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site we believe that a
shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the
dense silty basalt cobbles and gravel may be used to support the proposed one story
structure If the volcanic ash or loose materials are encountered at the footing subgrade
level these materials should be over excavated to expose the underlying dense materials
The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with compacted fill material As an
alternative the bottom of footing may be extended through the volcanic ash materials and
bear directly on the dense silty basalt cobbles and gravel
Percolation tests were performed in Boring Nos 3 through 6 to evaluate the
permeability of the subsurface materials for the disposal of wastewater Percolation tests
were performed in Boring Nos 4 and 5 which were drilled at the proposed leach field
location while the percolation tests performed in Boring Nos 3 and 6 were drilled at the
alternate leach field location Based on the results of our percolation tests we believe that
the basalt cobbles and gravel encountered at about 4 to 5 feet below the existing ground
surface at the alternate leach field location Boring Nos 3 and 6 would be the preferred
location for siting of the wastewater disposal system

Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings be used
to support the new one story building An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds
per square foot psf may be used for the design of footings bearing on the on site basalt
cobbles and gravel This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Foundations should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent
finished grade Where foundations are located next to utility ttenches and or easements
they should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench or embedded as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
If volcanic ash or loose materials are encountered at the footing subgrade level the
volcanic ash or loose materials should be over excavated to expose the underlying dense
materials The over excavation should be backfilled with fill materials with a maximum
particle size of 3 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction As an alternative the footing
bottom may be extended deeper through the surface volcanic ash soils to bear directly on
the silty basalt cobbles and gravel
The soils encountered at the footing subgrade level should be moisture conditioned
to above the optinum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with procedures described in ASTM Test
GEOLABS INC
W O 4325 00 Page 8
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate that total settlements of footings bearing on the
recompacted on site soils may be on the order of about 1 inch We estimate that
differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on the recompacted on site
soils to be on the order of about 0 5 inches
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 40 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted basalt cobbles and
gravel Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils
around the footings are well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected It should be
emphasized that surface water should be diverted away from foundations
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of Geolabs
prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing
conditions and the required embedment depths
Slabs On Grade
We envision that concrete slabs on grade will be provided for the new building In
general we recommend that a cushion fill layer consisting of a minimum of 4 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below the slab to provide more
uniform support and to serve as a capillary break A moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic
membrane should be incorporated above the cushion fill layer to reduce the potential for
damage to moisture sensitive floor coverings by moisture infiltration Two inches of moist
sand may be considered above the plastic membrane to provide protection of the membrane
GEOLABS INC
W O 4325 00 Page 9
and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Provision of the 2 inches of fine sand above the
vapor barrier will be left up to the discretion of the project structural engineer
Prior to placement of the cushion fill layer for the slabs the volcanic ash materials
encountered at the slab subgrade level should be removed and replaced with
well compacted fill materials The soils encountered at the slab subgrade level should be
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Exterior concrete slabs such as walkways planned for the proposed project should
be supported on a minimum 4 inch thick layer of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation below the exterior concrete slabs To reduce the potential for extensive slab
cracking due to concrete shrinkage crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal
to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
Site Grading
Based on the existing topography we anticipate that minimal site grading work would
be required to achieve the design finished grades In general areas within the contract
grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed at the on set of earthwork
Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed
of properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should
be over excavated to expose firm material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled
with well compacted fill
Concrete slabs and exterior walkways that are to be demolished should be
completely removed Over excavation resulting from the demolition should be backfilled
with compacted fills Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting
excavation should be properly backfilled with fill material placed in 8 inch loose lifts
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities to be abandoned in place under the proposed
structures should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure

Excavations
Based on our field exploration at the project site the site is underlain by a surface
layer of volcanic ash The volcanic ash materials were underlain by silty basalt cobbles
and gravel The silty basalt cobbles and gravel were underlain by hard basalt formations
It is anticipated that the near surface soils may be excavated with normal heavy excavation
equipment such as a backhoe and ripping with large bulldozers However excavations
into the deeper formations may require the use of hoerams chipping or blasting
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials is based on
our visual observation of the existing rock formation and field data from the borings
performed at the site We recommend that the contractors be encouraged to examine the
site conditions and the boring data to make their own interpretation
Utility Lines
We anticipate that new utility line trenches planned for the proposed project will be
excavated in the dense silty basalt cobbles and gravel and the hard basalt formations In
general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation is recommended below the pipes Free draining granular materials such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should be used for the initial trench
backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes It is critical that free draining granular

materials be used to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the
compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to

a maximum particle size of 6 inches The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above

Drainage
The finished grades outside the building structure should be sloped to shed water
away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also
recommended that gutter systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge
be diverted away from the foundation and slab areas Excessive landscape watering near
the foundations and slabs should be avoided Planters next to foundations should also be
avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into
the subsoils
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab
edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration
In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs

Field Percolation Testing
Four falling head percolation tests were performed in four of the borings Boring
Nos 3 through 6 to evaluate the permeability characteristics of the subsurface materials
encountered at the proposed and alternative leach field locations The percolation tests were
performed in general accordance with the procedures described in the State of Hawaii
and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards
Based on the percolation tests conducted the percolation rate at the proposed leach
field location performed at depths of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface were
about 40 and 60 minutes per inch Boring Nos 4 and 5 The percolation rates at the
alternate leach field location performed at depths of about 5 feet below the existing ground
surface were about 1 minute per inch in Boring No 3 and about 60 minutes per inch in
Boring No 6 The locations of the percolation tests are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Results of the individual percolation tests are presented on Plates C 1 through C 4 of this
report The percolation tests at the proposed leach field location were performed in the
moderately fractured hard basalt formation while the percolation tests at the alternate leach
field location were performed in basalt cobbles and gravel
Based on the results of our percolation tests it appears that the silty basalt cobbles
and gravel encountered at depths of about 4 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface at
the alternate leach field location may be the preferred location for siting of the wastewater
disposal system We believe that a percolation rate of approximately 30 minutes per inch
may be used to size the leach field at the alternate leach field location It should be
emphasized that the percolation rate of the silty basalt cobbles and gravel and the rock
formation at the site may be variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids the
fracturing of the rock formation and the presence of embedded clinker seams Due to the
variability of the subsurface conditions the absorption capacity of the disposal system should
be confirmed by additional percolation tests during construction

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 extending to depths ranging from
approximately 5 to 21 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations
of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with solid stem continuous flight augers and rotary coring
tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6 of Appendix A
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of the basalt rock formations encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery Is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would
be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
Appendix A Continued
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense rock formation with a high
RQD would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of
excavation

APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
Four field percolation tests were performed in Boring Nos 3 through 6 in general
accordance with the procedures described in the State of Hawaii Department of Health s
Administrative Rules Chapter 2 Wastewater Systems and Chapter 10 of the Ten
States Standards To evaluate the permeability of the subsurface materials for disposal of
wastewater the percolation tests were performed by drilling 4 0 inch diameter boreholes
extending to depths of about 5 to 6 feet below the existing ground surface The percolation
tests were performed in the silty basalt cobbles and gravel and the basalt rock formation as
encountered in the borings
Approximately 1 to 2 inches of gravel was then placed at the bottom of the boreholes
to protect the bottom of the holes from scouring and sediments The materials in the
boreholes were soaked overnight prior to commencing with the percolation test During the
test the holes were carefully filled with water to a minimum height of 6 inches above the
gravel pack The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon
which calculation of the percolation rates were based The results of the individual
percolation tests are presented on Plates C 1 through C 4
