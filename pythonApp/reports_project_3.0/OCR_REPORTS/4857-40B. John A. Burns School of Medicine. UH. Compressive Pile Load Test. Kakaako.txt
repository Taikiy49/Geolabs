In accordance with the project specifications one compressive pile load test was
conducted on a selected test pile Pile No R 145 for the University of Hawaii John A
Burns School of Medicine project in the Kaka ako area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
This letter report presents the results of our field observations and the test data collected
during the static pile load test conducted on
The static compressive pile load test was conducted on a 16 5 inch octagonal
precast prestressed concrete pile The test pile was driven on using
a hydraulic hammer delivering a maximum energy of up to about
44 23 kip feet During the pile driving operations on the test pile a log of the blow counts
needed to advance the test pile was maintained by an engineer from our office The test
pile driving record is provided on Plate 1 for ease of reference
The test pile was loaded in general accordance with ASTM D 1143 Quick Load
Test Method and modified in the project specifications The maximum test load on the
test pile was planned at about 400 kips In general the load test was loaded to a test load
of 320 kips 200 percent of the design load of 160 kips in 16 kip load increments Based
on the load settlement characteristics the load test was further advanced to a maximum
test load of 400 kips in 20 kip load increments beyond the 160 kip test load
In general each load increment of the load test was held for at least 5 minutes or
until the rate of settlement was generally stable In addition a 4 hour hold was maintained
at the 320 kip load increment to evaluate the creep effects of the test pile After loading
the test pile to the maximum test load 400 kips the test load was unloaded in
approximately four equal decrements and held for at least 5 minutes at each decrement
The results of the load test are presented on Plates 2 1 through 2 3 and are summarized
as follows

A plot of the compressive pile load test measurements at the top of the load test
pile is provided on Plate 3 Based on the shape of the load test curve it appears that the
total pile head displacement of the 16 5 inch octagonal concrete pile Pile No R 145
exceeded the Davisson failure criteria at the maximum test load of 400 kips which is two

supporting a test load of 320 kips two times the design load and the rate of the pile head
movement within the 4 hour hold time was generally stable without signs of appreciable
creep

of 160 kips pile design capacity and 320 kips two times the design load were observed
estimated for the project Therefore we believe that the 16 5 inch octagonal concrete pile
Pile No R 145 is capable of supporting a design load of 160 kips with an adequate factor
of safety and with reasonable settlement characteristics compatible with the design of the
project

Contract No Z607762 Change Order No 1 Work entered into on we
have performed seismic shear wave velocity testing at the University of Hawaii John A
Burns School of Medicine project in the Kaka ako area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
This letter report summarizes the field exploration performed and presents our
findings and recommendations in support of the seismic design of the new buildings
planned for the project These findings and recommendations are intended for design
input only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report
Project Considerations
We understand that the John A Burns School of Medicine for the University of
Hawaii is planned in the Kaka ako area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
subject property is approximately rectangular in shape and encompasses approximately
10 acres in land area The site is bounded in a clockwise direction by Ilalo Street to the
north Cooke Street to the east Kelikoi Street to the south and developed land to the
west
Because of the poor subsurface soil conditions encountered at the site it was
desired to perform seismic shear wave velocity profiling using Cone Penetrometer Testing
CPT at the site in an effort to more closely analyze the seismic design considerations for
ihe site The CPT method involves inserting a sensitive probe into the soil at a constant
speed Various sensors embedded in the probe continuously measure the tip resistance
side friction and induced pore pressures as the probe is systematically pushed through
the soil

In general subsurface explorations consisting of CPT yields more accurate results
for highly sensitive materials such as the loose granular soils at the site because the data
is obtained directly from the in situ soils Therefore CPT with seismic shear wave velocity
profiling is considered to be the industry standard for higher order analysis such as
detailed seismic analyses especially in these subsurface conditions

extending down to a depth of about 100 feet below the ground level was conducted using
seismic cone penetration testing equipment

Field Exploration
Our additional field exploration at the project site generally encountered stiff and
medium dense surface fills of about 5 to 10 feet thick across the project site The surface
fills were generally underlain by soft loose lagoonal deposits extending to depths of about
45 to 55 feet below the existing ground surface Within and below the highly compressible
lagoonal deposits our field exploration generally encountered coral ledges of variable
thickness and hardness over a large portion of the project site The lagoonal deposits
and or coral ledges were generally underlain by medium dense coralline detritus materials
extending to depths of about 110 to 156 feet below the existing ground surface
Due to the highly sensitive nature of the subsurface soil conditions at the site it was
desired to perform seismic shear wave velocity profiling using Cone Penetrometer Testing
CPT at the site in an effort to more closely analyze the seismic design considerations for
the site Shear wave velocity tests and cone penetration tests were performed extending
to depths of about 101 to 107 feet below the ground surface using seismic piezocone
SCPTU at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 1
The seismic piezocone penetration test consists of hydraulically pushing a steel
cone with an apex angle of 60 degrees and an area of 10 square centimeters connected
to a friction sleeve with an area of 150 square centimeters into the ground The tip and
side friction resistance and pore pressure induced during penetration are measured by
strain gages within the instrument and are recorded versus the depth of penetration The
values of the tip resistance friction ratio sleeve friction over tip resistance and pore
pressure can be correlated to soil strength and classification
The cone carries a uniaxial horizontal accelerator to detect the arrival of a shear
wave propagated from the ground surface The seismic measurements were made when
the SCPTU had stopped A shear wave was generated at the surface by striking a
loaded plank with a switched hammer The propagation time of the wave from the
hammer blow to the cone was measured at each depth interval The difference of these
depths divided by the time difference gave the average shear wave velocity over the
depth Results of the shear wave velocity testing are presented on Plates 2 through 4 of
this letter report
Soil Profile Type
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration the
weighted average shear wave velocity for the top 100 feet of the soil profile ranged from
about 812 to 1 027 feet per second Based on the shear wave velocity test results we
believe that the project site may be classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as being
a Stiff Soil Profile site corresponding to an Sp soil profile type based on the Uniform
Building Code Table No 16 J 1997 Edition
