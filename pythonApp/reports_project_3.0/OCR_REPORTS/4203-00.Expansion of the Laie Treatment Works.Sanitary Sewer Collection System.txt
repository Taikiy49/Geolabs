REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu encompassing approximately 604 square miles of land area may
be divided into four basic geographic provinces The provinces include the
Mountains Mountains 3 Leilehua Plateau and 4 Coastal Plain The Island of
Oahu was formed by the merging of basalt lavas erupted from the two prominent volcanoes
the Waianae and Ko olau Volcanoes Of the two the Waianae Volcano is the older having
breached the ocean surface nearly 4 million years ago The Ko olau Volcano rose above sea
level approximately 2 7 million years ago making it the younger of the two volcanoes
In general the two volcanoes were built by the long term eruption of thinly bedded a a
and pahoehoe lava flows which erupted from elongate and linear trending volcanic vents
associated with the prominent rift zones of each of the volcanoes The Ko olau lavas banked
against the lava flows of the Wai anae Volcano to form the Leilehua Plateau of the central
GEOLABS INC
W O 4203 00 Page 6
interior portion of Oahu The Wai anae Mountains which form the western third of the Island
of Oahu contain the oldest basalt rock formations on the Island of Oahu The Ko olau
Mountains form the eastern two thirds of the island and include the rock layers exposed
along the extending from Waimanalo to Kahuku
During the final stages of eruption and continuing well after the cessation of the
Wai anae and Ko olau eruptions the island mass began to subside thousands of feet under
the tremendous weight of the rock In addition to the subsidence of the island the force of
stream erosion worked in conjunction with the large scale fracture and slumping of portions
of the land mass to form the topography of today Between 850 000 to 70 000 years ago
volcanic activity returned to the Island of Oahu with the eruption of ash cinder and basalt
lavas from vents located throughout the eastern and southeastern portions of the island
These most recent eruptions are collectively known as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
Subsequently erosion by streams and ocean waves have continued to work on the exposed
land surfaces to form the topography of the present day
The Island of Oahu is no longer sinking beneath the sea however the effects of the
early whole island subsidence combined with the added effect of major Pleistocene age sea
level fluctuations worked to create drowned river valley systems and the development of
coral reefs The coral reefs and lithified sand dunes may be found exposed subaerially on
Oahu and also buried in submarine deposits well offshore of the island The coral and sand
dune deposits were formed in shallow offshore seas during periods of higher and lower sea
levels associated with the effects of the advance and retreat of glaciers which controlled the
volume of water contained in the seas Today some of the shallow sea coralline deposits
are visible on the flanks of mountains or buried beneath terrestrial sediments evidence of
the higher and lower stands of the sea in prehistoric time
GEOLOGY OF THE LA IE AREA
The coastal area of La ie lies within the Coastal Plain province of Oahu and is located
to the northeast of the Ko olau Mountain province As a result much of the generally flat land
area is underlain by unconsolidated coastal sediments coralline silts and sands with
GEOLABS INC
W O 4203 00 Page 7
pockets of hard cemented sand dunes sandstone and coral limestone rock formation
Progressing toward the hills of the Ko olau Mountains the subsurface conditions gradually
change to reflect an increase in thickness of terrestrial sediments such as the alluvial soils
derived from the hills and valleys located to the southwest of the coastline The alluvial soils
may overlie the buried coral and sand deposits in the subsurface
The coral and limestone rock encountered buried in the subsurface often contain
locally occurring void and cavity structures originating during the deposition of the calcareous
deposits in shallow marine environments In addition some of the voids develop following
the coral and limestone deposition as a result of the slow percolation of fresh water down
through the geologic strata leaching the calcareous mineralogy of the materials
Some areas of La ie are underlain by soft compressible soils associated with marsh
land and swamp type deposits Some of these soft ground deposits may be buried in the
shallow subsurface beneath firmer fill soils placed to raise the ground elevation during
development of the region The exposed rocky ground comprising La ie Point is believed to
represent fossil reef and calcareous dune deposits which at one time represented beach
deposits situated along an old shoreline

Horizontal Directional Drilling HDD
For the new sewerlines planned along the busy Kamehameha Highway and in areas
not practical to be served by gravity systems deep trench excavations we believe that the
use of trenchless methods horizontal directional drilling in particular may be a viable
alternative for the low pressure sewer force main installation in these areas Horizontal
directional drilling is a trenchless steerable installation method using a multi axis drilling
machine to bore a small diameter pilot hole in the subsurface The pilot hole is bored using
either controlled fluid jetting or fluid assisted mechanical cutting or a combination of both
The pilot hole is reamed as necessary to accommodate the pipe to be installed The pipe
is pulled back into the reamed hole by the drilling machine Most HDD systems use a
slurry to stabilize the walls of the borehole and to reduce the frictional drag on the pipeline
being installed Steering corrections are made in the soils by rotating the slanted cutter
shoe to the desired orientation and then advancing the shoe and string without rotation
In general the HDD hole would be drilled nearly horizontal with entry and exit angles
limited to a maximum of about 14 degrees measured from horizontal The drill unit should
be positioned at an appropriate setback distance such that the required depth starting from
the entry point can be achieved In general the product pipe or utility line should be installed
in one continuous operation preferably without pause and should protrude a sufficient
distance beyond the entry and exit points to allow proper termination or connection at a later
time Shallow working pits may be required at each end of the bore to allow placement of
the drill pipe and allow transition to the conventional pipe trench installation
GEOLABS INC
W O 4203 00 Page 11
The vertical and horizontal alignment of the drill hole is monitored from the surface by
a locator which tracks signals from the transmitter contained inside the drill head housing
The contractor should carefully monitor the drilling operation when approaching existing
underground utilities and structures to assure proper clearance
The feasibility of the HDD technique should be established based on ground
conditions diameter and length of the pipeline and the required achievable degree of
accuracy in both alignment and grade HDD is not currently practical for gravity sewer lines
which require precise line and grade tolerances HDD method is best suited for soils with
some cohesion i e clays However the method has been quite successfully used in sandy
soils by adding bentonite to the drilling fluid to ensure borehole stability HDD method is not
well suited for gravelly soils greater than 50 percent gravel size particles due to difficulties
in drilling the pilot hole and maintaining the stability of the hole It should be noted that widely
heterogeneous formations such as alluvial soils with sands gravel cobbles and boulders
present difficult challenges to penetration and steering control Therefore use of the HDD
method in these ground conditions may be inappropriate
The advantage of HDD installation on this project would be the elimination of the deep
trench excavations needed for the gravity sewer system This would greatly reduce or
eliminate the shoring and dewatering requirements for the project However due to
limitations on the drilling angles at the entry and exit points at the ground surface HDD would
require a much longer installed length of pipe This may require relocation of some of the
lateral lines resulting in longer lengths of lateral pipes Additionally the accuracy of HDD is
less than that of microtunneling therefore care must be taken when crossing above or under
existing utilities and structures to provide the proper clearance between the new and existing
pipelines Nevertheless HDD appears to be a viable and possibly cost effective alternative
for the installation of low pressure sewer force main planned for this project
Geotechnical Conditions
The technical feasibility of the HDD method depends on the characteristics of the
materials at the depth of the pipeline profile as well as the length of the drill path and
diameter of the pipe to be installed American Gas Association 1995 In general
GEOLABS INC
W O 4203 00 Page 12
gravel size material cannot be readily fluidized by the drilling fluid and may not be
stable enough to be cut and removed by the drilling fluid without caving in Therefore
subsoils containing a high percentage of gravel may present a serious limitation on
the utilization of the HDD method In addition it should be noted that obstructions
such as cobbles and boulders within the drill path may halt the advance of the drill bit
reamer or pipeline during pullback
Based on our field exploration coralline sands were generally encountered in the
majority of the borings drilled across the project site However coralline deposits with
a high percentage of gravel were also observed in localized areas throughout the
project site In addition loose coralline deposits and hard coral formation were also
encountered in some of the drilled borings performed for the subject project
In general HDD method should be suitable for pipeline installation in the hard coral
formation provided that appropriate rock drilling tools and experienced drilling crews
are utilized during construction However zones of coralline deposits containing high
gravel content about 50 percent or more and cobble size coral fragments would be
problematic and are expected to cause difficulty in drilling and steering the pilot hole
as well as hole stability problems such as caving in of the pilot and reamed holes
The caved in materials will obstruct or divert reaming tools or the pipe string during
pipe pullback This will substantially increase the pulling stresses on the pipe string
and may impede pipe pulling or damage the pipeline In addition loose sandy soils
as encountered in some of the drilled borings may also cave into the drill hole and
obstruct and divert the pipe string during pullback Deflection of the pilot drill bit may
occur at the interface between the loose sandy soils and hard coral formation due to
an abrupt change in soil conditions This may result in difficulties in steering during
the pilot hole drilling and the potential change in the drill path alignment
The low pressure sewer force main profile should be evaluated with respect to the
anticipated geotechnical considerations to reduce some of the difficulties discussed
above Therefore Geolabs should review the sewer force main profile when the
preliminary alignment and profile has been selected

The most common product pipes used for HDD method are HDPE PVC welded
steel and copper Factors affecting the loads and stresses on the pipe include the
following
Radius of curvature
Modulus of the pipe material
Thickness of the pipe

For the HDD method the most important design consideration for the pipe is the
tensile stresses imposed on the pipe when it is pulled into place through the drill hole
The magnitude of these tensile stresses is a function of the length of the installation
as well as the subsurface conditions Other important pipe design considerations are
the internal and external pressures acting on the pipe during and after installation In
addition the selected pipe material must have adequate corrosion resistance or be
protected to resist internal and external corrosion
HDPE pipes generally have low tensile strength and may be susceptible to failure
during installation This limits the length of installation that can be completed with an
HDPE pipe Therefore it is critical that the pipe be selected with an adequate
allowable pulling tensile capacity Pipe stress analyses should be performed to
evaluate if an HDPE pipe can withstand the pulling stresses during pipe installation
and the external pressures hydrostatic and soil on the installed pipeline
The HDPE pipe should be selected to provide the maximum pipe thickness and
strength for the proposed installation based on the results of the pipe stress analyses
considerations regarding HDD installation procedures probable drill hole sizes and
materials in filling the annulus between the HDD installed HDPE pipeline and the drill
hole Proper ballasting of the pipelines during installation into the HDD drill hole

Construction Considerations
Buried underground hazards including storage tanks electrical cables natural gas
lines water lines piping carrying other liquids telecommunication cables and CATV
cables may be present within the limits of the drill path during the HDD operation
Therefore the directional control of the pilot drill bit is critical to the success of the
proposed pipeline installation by HDD method These underground utilities and
structures should be identified located and avoided during the project construction
It is important that the HDD contractor utilize an in hole steering tool in conjunction
with a surface tracking system to provide reliable horizontal and vertical directional
control during the pipeline installation
The primary selection criteria for the HDD machine are thrust pullback and torque
capabilities However project related requirements such as length of installation and
diameter of the product pipe play an important role in the selection process
Depending on the length and size of the drill hole required for the HDD installation
the size of the drilling equipment and its capacity in rotational power as well as pulling
power should be checked by the contractor for the specific project

Type of Drilling Fluid
Depending on the soil conditions water only or water mixed with bentonite and or
polymer can be used as a drilling fluid in a directional drilling application Water alone
is normally suitable for drilling in silty or clayey soils If a highly plastic or expansive
clay is encountered polymer mixed with water will improve the hole lubrication and
reduce the swelling tendency of clay swelling tends to close off the hole which
increases torque and pullback requirements For sand or other poorly consolidated
formations such as that encountered at the project site a bentonite mixture should
be used to support the borehole The amount of drilling additives bentonite or
polymer depends on the soil conditions and experience of the contractor The
water used in the drilling fluid should not be hard or salty because this will cause poor
mixing or yield with the bentonite or polymer and the flocculated bentonite may tend
to settle to the bottom of the tank Hard water should be treated with soda ash and
salt water should be avoided
Muck and Slurry Disposal
Special muck and slurry handling procedures may be required for the HDD installation
method for the proposed sewer line The properties of the various geologic units
anticipated along the drill path as indicated by the grain size and hydrometer tests
should be taken into account in selecting an appropriate slurry separation plant Due
to the low specific gravity of some of the coralline materials these coralline materials
may not settle out quickly in a cascading tank type of slurry separation system and
a slurry separation plant may be required under such conditions
In order to aid in a clean site and to more readily separate the materials a solids
control or slurry separation plant with shakers screens de sanders de silter cones
and centrifuge may need to be considered This will facilitate a more rapid separation
and disposal process Additives may need to be included to get the fine grained
materials to flocculate so that a clean slurry can be returned to the drill head
Muck resulting from the HDD operations should be removed from the site and should
be disposed of properly at an acceptable facility or designated area Slurry from the

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon
information obtained from field borings Variations of subsoil conditions between and beyond
the field borings may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become
evident until construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will be necessary
to re evaluate the recommendations provided in this report
The locations of the field borings indicated in this report are approximate having been
estimated by taping from the features shown on the Topographic Map provided by Hawaii
Reserves Inc on March 11 1999 Elevations of the field borings were estimated by
interpolation from the contour lines shown on the same plan The physical location and
elevation of the borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the
methods used
The stratification lines shown on graphic representations of the borings depict the
approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote a gradual transition
Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the graphic
representations and or in the text of this report These data have been reviewed and
interpretations made in the formulation of this report However it must be noted that
fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall tides temperature and other factors

client Hawaii Reserves Inc and their subconsultants for specific application to the Laie
Sanitary Sewer Collection System project in accordance with generally accepted

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
14 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 14 extending to depths ranging from about
11 5 to 51 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the
borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted
drill rig equipped with solid stem and hollow stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer and or geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 thru A 14
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition samples were also obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate
sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil sample
to evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentation
of the test results is provided on Plate C 1
Fourteen sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 ASTM D 1140 were performed on
selected samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid
in soil classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on
Plates C 2 through C 4
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct
shear test are presented on Plates C 5

APENDIX D
Field Permeability Testing
APPENDIX D
Field Permeability Testing
The permeability of the in situ soils were evaluated by performing six constant head
and four falling head tests in selected borings Boring 10 11 and 14 at depths
ranging from approximately 4 4 to 19 3 feet below the existing ground surface
The constant head tests were performed by pumping water into the boreholes until
the water surface has stabilized and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time
The water height and the rate of pumping were then measured to provide data upon which
to base calculation of the subsoil permeability
During the falling head testing the boreholes were carefully filled with water to the top
of the boreholes The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data
upon which to base calculation of the permeability values of the subsoils The test results
of the field permeability tests are presented on Plates D 1 through D 8
