PRE-PRODUCTION MICROPILE INSTALLATION AND LOAD TESTING 
EMERGENCY KAUPAKALUA BRIDGE REPLACEMENT 
HAIKU, MAUI, HAWAII 
Dear Mr. Fujioka: 
This letter report summarizes our geotechnical engineering observation services 
performed during the pre-production micropile installation and load testing for the 
Emergency Kaupakalua Bridge Replacement project located in Haiku on the Island of Maui, 
Hawaii. 
This letter report presents our field observations and the results of load testing 
performed for the pre-production micropile from April 12 through April 19, 2022. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located on Peahi Road in the Haiku area on the Island of Maui, 
Hawaii.  Due to recent heavy rains and the Kaupakalua Dam breach above the road, the 
existing wooden bridge structure was destroyed with only one of the abutment structures 
remaining.  We understand that an Acrow type bridge structure will replace the destroyed 
bridge.  The new bridge will be supported by 7.625-inch diameter micropiles on both 
abutments.  The micropiles on the Paia Abutment have a design load capacity of 80 kips, 
and the micropiles on the Hana Abutment have a design load capacity of 64 kips.  Per the 
project specifications, the contractor is required to perform a pre-production load test near 
the Hana Abutment.  The sacrificial pre-production micropile should be loaded to 
100 percent of the nominal axial capacity determined using AASHTO LRFD standards: 
92 kips.  
MICROPILE INSTALLATION 
A pre-production test micropile, designated as TP-1, was installed near the Hana 
Abutment at the project site.  The approximate location of the pre-production micropile is 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 1.  The pre-production micropile was installed by Kelikai, Inc. 
on April 12, 2022, utilizing a TD75 track-mounted drill rig.  In general, the pre-production

micropile TP-1 was installed, extending to a depth of about 48.6 feet below the existing 
ground surface with about 20 feet of 7.625-inch OD steel casing left in-place with about 
1.7 feet of the steel casing above the ground surface.  Installation record of the 
pre-production micropile is provided on Plates 2.1 and 2.2. 
Based on our field observations, the pre-production micropile TP-1 was installed by 
advancing the steel casing to a depth of about 48.6 feet below the ground surface.  The 
pre-production micropile generally extended through the soft to medium stiff fill and alluvial 
soils and into the stiff residual soils.  Upon reaching the tip elevation, the cement grout was 
pumped via a tremie pipe from the bottom to the top of the casing, displacing the 
groundwater inside the steel casing.  After the initial grout application, the #14 Grade 75 
all-threaded rebar with centralizers was installed to the full depth of the borehole with 
centralizers spaced at every 10 feet. 
The steel casing was extracted from the bottom 32.6 feet of the pre-production 
micropile, providing the bond length within the underlying residual soils.  The remaining steel 
casing (about 20 feet) was left in-place at the top of the micropile (unbonded zone) as the 
permanent casing.  During the setting of the permanent casing, the steel casing was raised 
and plunged 3 feet into the residual soil.  Prior to load testing, the steel casing protruding 
above the ground was cut and removed to provide access for the load testing apparatus. 
MICROPILE TESTING 
The pre-production load test was conducted as a tensile load test in general 
accordance with ASTM D3689.  The maximum specified test load for the pre-production 
micropile was 92 kips.  A hydraulic jack system reacting against a reaction beam was used 
to apply the testing load to the load test micropile.  In general, the reaction beam was 
supported on wooden dunnage at both ends of the beam.  The micropile testing was 
performed by Kelikai, Inc. 
A load cell was used to measure the test loads applied to the micropile.  The micropile 
displacements were measured with two dial gauges on the left and right sides of the load 
test micropile and a 1/100 division ruler was set onto an extension from the micropile.  Each 
dial gauge was attached to an independent reference beam with a 1/100-inch division ruler 
to verify reference beam movements that could affect the dial gauge readings.  In addition, 
a 1/100-inch division ruler was set on a remote reference to verify potential ground 
displacement during the load testing that could affect the dial gauge readings.  The 
surveyor's level was used to observe the rulers and the remote reference for movement. 
In general, the pre-production micropile TP-1 was loaded to the maximum test load 
of 92 kips in approximately 8-kip load increments (about 12.5 percent of the 64-kip design 
load capacity).  Each load increment was held for at least 5 minutes.  The ultimate load 
capacity of 92 kips was held for 2 hours to evaluate the effects of creep.  The gross rate of 
displacement at the 92-kip load increment was no greater than 0.01 inches per hour for both 
hours of the hold.  Subsequently, the pre-production micropile was unloaded in 
approximately four equal decrements and held for at least 2 minutes for each decrement.
