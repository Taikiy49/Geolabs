SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, the subsurface conditions at the project site generally consisted of fills 
over medium hard to hard basalt rock formation underlain by beach deposits, coral 
formation, and coralline detritus.  The fills across the site extended to depths of about 1 to 
4 feet below the existing ground surface.  The fills near the proposed Tank TK-1601 could 
be as deep as 11 feet below the existing ground surface.  The basalt rock formation 
extended to depths of about 25 to 41 feet below the existing ground surface.  Small voids 
were encountered in the basalt rock formation in some borings at depths between 3.5 and 
8 feet below the existing ground surface.  Below the basalt rock formation, beach deposits, 
coral formation, and coralline detritus were encountered in the borings extending to the 
maximum depth. We encountered groundwater at depths of about 11.8 to 16.6 feet below 
the existing ground surface in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the competent subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we 
recommend supporting the tank structures on a perimeter ring footing and concrete 
slab-on-grade.  In order to provide uniform bearing for the tank foundations and concrete 
floor, we recommend that a 12-inch thick layer of compacted aggregate subbase be 
provided below the foundations and concrete floor of the tank structures. The 12-inch thick 
layer of aggregate subbase should also extend a minimum of 12 inches beyond the edges 
of the tank structures. The aggregate subbase materials should be moisture-conditioned to 
above optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
We recommend using the following allowable bearing pressures for the design of shallow 
foundations for the tank structures.  
TK-3001 through TK-3004 – 6,000 psf 
TK-1601 – 4,000 psf 
Due to the potential for the presence of cavities as commonly encountered in basaltic 
lava flows, we recommend implementing a program of probing and grouting of the 
foundation locations for the fuel tanks, processing building, and office building during 
construction. The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to 
a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation.  
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
recommendations for the design of the project.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii, the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago, covers an area of 
approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of five shield 
volcanoes, which include the following:  Kohala, which is long extinct; Mauna Kea, which 
has had activity during recent geologic time; Hualalai, which last erupted in 1801; and 
Mauna Loa and Kilauea, both of which are still active. 
The project site is located on the northeastern flank of the Mauna Loa Shield 
Volcano, which had been built up by the successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows 
and pyroclastic materials. The lava formation encountered at the site appears to consist 
of both a'a and pahoehoe type flows, which spread and ponded as they approached the 
ocean. A’a lava is typically characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface 
resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments, including cobbles and boulders. 
Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal 
structure of vesicular (porous) rock. 
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas, especially pahoehoe type 
lava. Cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state, and represent both lava 
tubes (intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes are 
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow, leaving a hollow tube-like structure, 
which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow. Blisters and pockets 
are generally smaller in horizontal extent. 
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite 
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions. In general, water is transmitted 
through the porous rock matrix, along joints, fractures and inter-flow contacts, cavities, 
and along clinker layers. The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high; therefore, 
they serve as the primary water transmission features.

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code, 2018 Edition (IBC 2018), the project site 
may be subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be 
addressed. The following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity and soil 
profile for seismic design at the project site: 
 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
Generally, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island 
of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small 
that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of the 
earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 
90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of 
ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the active volcanoes located 
in the southern portion of the Island of Hawaii.

3.1 
Tank Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we envision 
that shallow foundations consisting of a perimeter ring footing and concrete slab-on-grade 
may be used to support the new tank walls and interior floors. In order to provide uniform 
bearing for the foundations and concrete floor, we recommend that a 12-inch thick layer 
of compacted aggregate subbase be provided below the foundations and concrete floor 
of the tank structures. The 12-inch thick layer of aggregate subbase should also extend 
a minimum of 12 inches beyond the edges of the tank structures. The aggregate subbase 
materials should be moisture-conditioned to above optimum moisture and compacted to 
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same 
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture content is the water 
content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
As mentioned previously, hard to very hard basalt rock formation was encountered 
at relatively shallow depths at the locations of the tanks designated as TK-3001 through 
TK-3004. The tank location designated as TK-1601 may be underlain by medium dense 
to dense gravelly sand fill extending to a depth of about 11 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the tank locations, we 
recommend using the following allowable bearing pressures for the design of shallow 
foundations.

TK-1601 
4,000 psf

In general, the fuel tank foundations should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finished grade. Foundations located next to retaining walls, 
other foundations, utility trenches, or easements should be embedded below a 45-degree 
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench, or the

Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of foundation and tank floor 
excavations should be over-excavated until basalt rock is encountered in the excavations 
at TK-3001 through TK-3004. The over-excavation should be backfilled with concrete.

underlying basalt rock.  Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of 
foundation and tank floor excavations at TK-1601 should be over-excavated to firm/dense 
soils and the resulting over-excavation should be backfilled with compacted structural fills. 
If the foundations for the tank structures are designed and constructed in 
accordance with the recommendations presented herein, the total settlement of 
foundations is estimated to be on the order of 1 inch with differential settlements on the 
order of about 0.5 inches. 
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance 
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth 
pressure developed against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of 
foundations. A coefficient of friction of 0.45 may be used for foundations bearing on the 
compacted aggregate subbase. Resistance to lateral loads due to passive earth pressure 
may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 450 pounds per square foot per 
foot of depth (pcf) for TK-3001 through TK-3004 foundations and 350 pcf for TK-1601 
foundations. These values assume that the soils around the foundations are 
well-compacted. Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the 
upper 12 inches below the finished grade should be neglected.

3.2 
Building Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the processing and office 
building locations, we recommend using shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings 
to support the new buildings planned. As an alternative, thickened-edge slab footings may 
be considered in supporting the new buildings. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of 
footings bearing on the on-site recompacted granular fill material. This bearing value is 
for supporting dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient 
loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
In general, the bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finished grade. Foundations located next to retaining walls, 
other foundations, utility trenches, or easements should be embedded below a 45-degree 
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench (or retaining 
wall). Alternatively, the footings should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of 
the utility lines or the bottom of the retaining wall. 
Volcanic ash soils were encountered in the boring at the office building location.  If 
volcanic ash is encountered at the footing subgrades, the footing subgrades should be 
over-excavated until firm materials are encountered and backfilled with compacted 
aggregate subbase materials (3-inch minus granular materials) wrapped on all sides with 
filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equal). The aggregate subbase material layer should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the foundations to be less than 
1 inch. Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on similar materials 
may be on the order of about 0.5 inches or less.

the floor slab because they can better mask minor slab cracking.

should be provided below the slabs in lieu of the 4-inch thick cushion fill layer. The 
aggregate subbase material layer should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. The 
impervious moisture barrier may be omitted for these slabs.

3.4 
Foundation Probing 
Based on our exploration and experience in the area, voids and/or cavities appear 
to be present in the basaltic lava flows at the project site. To reduce the potential for loss 
of foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities below foundations, we 
recommend implementing a program of cavity probing and grouting of the tank structure 
foundations during construction. Foundation probing and grouting should also be 
implemented for the processing building and office building. 
For estimating purposes, we recommend that probe holes for the tank structures 
be drilled at 10-foot on centers for the perimeter ring foundation. In addition, probe holes 
should be drilled at each isolated spread footing location. Probe holes also should be 
drilled within the tank floor area at about 10-foot on center, starting at the center of the 
tank and radiating out at 10-foot radial increments. We recommend that probe holes for 
the processing and office buildings be drilled at each isolated spread footing location (one 
probe hole for every 50 square feet of footing area) and at 10 feet on-center along wall 
foundations or continuous strip footings. The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in 
diameter and should extend to a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of 
foundation.  
If cavities and/or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation, 
additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacings to help delineate the vertical 
and lateral extent of the cavity and/or void. The probe holes and cavities discovered 
should be backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) with a slump of about 
6 to 9 inches. The CLSM should be injected at low to moderate pressures. As an alternate 
to CLSM, sand-cement grout with a slump of about 6 to 9 inches also may be used for 
the grouting operations.

 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to 
at least 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the 
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other 
types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of rocks, 
boulders, and/or cobbles. Instead, a testing program to evaluate the number of 
passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be 
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a 
Geolabs representative. Based on this testing program, the number of passes then 
may be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction.  
For preliminary estimating purposes, compaction may be accomplished by using 
a large vibratory drum roller having a static weight of at least 10 tons traveling at a 
speed of about 300 feet per minute (about 3½ miles per hour). The roller should 
pass over the fill surface at least eight times for structural fill. 
Select granular fill materials required for the project should be placed in level lifts 
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. The compaction requirement for the last lift of fill (finished subgrade) 
below areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be increased to at least 95 percent 
relative compaction. Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, 
vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water 
tamping, jetting, or ponding should not be allowed to compact the fills.

anticipate most of the trenches for the utility lines will be excavated in the compacted fill 
and/or basalt rock formation encountered in our borings.

(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) should be provided below the pipes for uniform bearing

the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well-graded granular materials 
less than 6 inches in maximum particle size. The backfill should be moisture-conditioned 
to above the optimum moisture, placed in about 8-inch level loose lifts, and mechanically 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Where trenches are located in 
paved areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be 
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction.

Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers and coring equipment.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-13. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration 
sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond 
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow 
bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is 
mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Six Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Eight Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012 Method C) were performed on 
selected rock cores to evaluate their unconfined compressive strength of the rock formation 
encountered.  Results of the unconfined compression tests are presented on Plate B-3. 
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-4 through B-6. 
Three Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557 C) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-7 through B-9.
