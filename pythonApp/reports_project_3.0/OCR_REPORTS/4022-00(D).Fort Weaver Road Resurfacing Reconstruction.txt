INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering services
performed for the proposed resurfacing of Fort Weaver Road from Laulaunui Street to
Hanakahi Street in the Ewa area on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location of
the project area is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings from our site reconnaissance and our review
of available information and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations
derived from our analyses for the proposed project These geotechnical findings and
recommendations are intended for pavement design purposes only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau fava thus forming a
broad plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau or Central Oahu
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating sediments
which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site these alluvial
sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and coral algal reef
formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from zero feet in the area of
H 1 Freeway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and
serves as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of
southern Oahu Deposition of these sediments has continued from earlier geologic time
through the present

Methodology of Pavement Design
The methodology used to determine the required AC overlay thickness for
rehabilitation and thickness of reconstructed pavement sections generally follows that
outlined in the State of Hawaii Department of Transportation Material Testing and
Research Branch s Pavement Design Manual dated May 1995
in the method outlined for AC overlays the strength of the existing AC and base
materials are evaluated based on the type and severity of the distresses observed For
example the existing AC may be reduced to that of ACB untreated base UTB or may
be assumed to have the similar strength characteristics as new AC
Reconstruction of portions of the existing pavements will be required in those
areas where the observed distresses were likely caused by failures in the underlying
base subbase and or subgrade materials New pavement sections were determined
based on the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the design manual which is based
on the Hveem Stabilometer method developed and used by the California Department of
Transportation

the subgrade should be scarified moisture conditioned and recompacted Saturation
and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor
drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted engineered fill
Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the subbase base course and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement
surface should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface water off
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should not be
allowed anywhere during or after construction Development of good shoulder drainage
along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage structures is strongly
recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or premature failure of
the pavements
