2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
younger shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in
age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and it was partially buried below
Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank
Kalihi Valley was formed by stream erosion of Tertiary Age Koolau Range basalt
flows This initial period of erosion and valley carving was followed by several periods
of relative rise and fall of sea level which permitted valley filling by alluvium followed by
subsequent periods of erosion During the Quaternary Age Honolulu Volcanic Series
lava flowed down the valley and was subsequently partially covered by Quaternary Age
alluvium The project site is located on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano on the
slope of Kahauiki Ridge and to the northwest side of Kalihi Valley Continued recent
alluvial processes have covered the site with black to dark gray adobe clay

3 1 Foundations
Based on the relatively light structural loads and anticipated subsurface soil
conditions at the project site we believe that shallow foundations consisting of spread
footings may be used to support the proposed new structures planned for the site and
dwelling improvement project An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per
square foot psf may be used for the foundation design of the proposed new structures
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Due to the wet environment and potential for shallow subterranean seepage at
the project site a 12 inch thick layer of compacted structural fill material should be
provided below the bottom of footings The 12 inch thick structural fill will serve to
provide more uniform bearing support and facilitate the footing construction work during
wet season The over excavation for the compacted structural fill should extend beyond
the edges of the footing a minimum of 12 inches Prior to placement of the structural fill
the bottom of the footing over excavations should be recompacted to not less than
85 percent relative compaction Soft and or loose materials encountered at the bottom

materials The over excavation should be backfilled with structural fill material
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The amount of additional
over excavation and the need for geotextile fabric around the structural backfill material

field conditions during construction

3 2 Slabs On Grade
We understand that new concrete walkways are planned throughout the project
limits for the proposed site and dwelling improvement project Based on our field
exploration and laboratory testing the project site is generally underlain by near surface
adobe clayey soils with high expansion potential when subjected to moisture
fluctuations To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded
slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes
in the moisture content we recommend that the subgrade soils below the concrete
walkways be recompacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction in lieu of the
normal 90 percent relative compaction
The slab subgrades should be kept moist until the final placement of concrete
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks Saturation and subsequent
yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may
require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill
For the design of the concrete walkways we recommend that a minimum 24 inch
thick layer of non expansive select granular fill or aggregate subbase be placed below
the concrete walkways for uniform support The select granular fill or aggregate
subbase should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction In
addition crack control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the
walkways Thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded
at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade It should be noted that
areas adjacent to the slab should be backfilled tightly against the slab edges with the
on site soil and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for
water ponding around the slabs

3 6 Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement
surface should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface water off
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should not be
allowed on the site during or after construction
To reduce the potential for water infiltration into the pavement structural section
we recommend that a subdrain be installed along the lower boundary of the new
parking lot to collect the infiltrated water from the No 3B Fine gravel layer below the
pavement structural section The subdrain along this area should be in direct contact
with the No 3B Fine gravel layer underneath the pavement section to collect the
infiltrated water for proper discharge In general the subdrain should consist of 4 inch
diameter PVC perforated pipes with perforations facing down surrounded and
underlain by at least 6 inches of drainage material as described above A non woven
filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent should wrap around the drainage
material In general subdrain trenches should be at least 12 inches wide and on the
order of about 1 to 2 feet deep as shown on Plate 3
Care should be exercised when placing the subbase base and or construction
of the surface course to avoid damaging the subdrain collector pipe The subdrains
should be daylighted into drainage ditches culverts or other drainage structures for
proper discharge Development of good shoulder drainage along with a program to
prevent obstructions in the drainage structures is strongly recommended to reduce the
potential for pavement deterioration or premature failure of the pavements

3 7 Utility Trenches
New utility lines will be required for the site and dwelling improvement project In
general good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling
of the trenches for the new utilities at the site The method and equipment to be used
for trench excavation should be determined by the contractor subject to practical limits
and safety considerations In addition the excavations should comply with the
applicable federal state and local safety requirements Trench shoring design and
installation should be made the responsibility of the contractor
In general we recommend that granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used for support under the pipes
Our experience from the previous phase of construction indicates that soft soils are
commonly encountered near the pipe invert elevations especially during and after
heavy rains or when the excavated trench is left open for a period of time Where soft
soils are encountered near the pipe invert elevations we recommend that the utility pipe
be supported on an additional 18 to 24 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No
67 gradation wrapped in a non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent The
amount of additional over excavation and the need for filter fabric should be further
evaluated by a representative from Geolabs based on the actual exposed field
conditions during construction
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes It is critical to
use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper
trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select fill materials with a
maximum particle size of 3 inches The backfill should be placed in maximum 8 inch
level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative

3 8 Drainage
It should be noted that surface and subsurface drainage at the site are especially
critical considering the sloping terrain and wet environment at the site Therefore the
finished grades should be sloped to shed water away from the slabs and foundations
and to reduce the potential for ponding around the structures Excessive landscape
watering near the slabs and foundations should also be avoided
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to
drain surface water runoff away from the slabs and foundations

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Three one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed
samples of the near surface clayey soils to evaluate the swelling potential of the
subsurface materials The results of these tests are summarized on Plate C 1
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate C 2
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test ASTM D 1883 was performed on a
bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics
of the soils The test results are presented on Plate C 3
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationship The test results are presented on Plate C 4
