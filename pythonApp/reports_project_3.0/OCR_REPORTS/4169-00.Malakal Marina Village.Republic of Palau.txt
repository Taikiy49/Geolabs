GEOLOGY OF KOROR AND MALAKAL ISLANDS
The Palau Islands form a curved chain of islands of approximately 100 miles in length
extending from north to southwest The Palau Islands are the exposed crest of the Palau
Ridge expressed as a series of long arcuate submarine ridges separating the Philippine Sea
and the Pacific Ocean There are about 350 larger islands that comprise the Palau Island
chain The Palau barrier fringing reef encloses most of the Palau Islands forming a broad
shallow water lagoon occasionally separated by deep water passages Babeldaob Island is
the largest island of the Palau Island chain and is located to the north of Koror Island A
floating bridge structure currently connects the two islands across a deep water passage
In general the Islands of Koror and Malakal in the Republic of Palau are composed of
the accumulation of Tertiary Epoch volcanic materials and the growth of organic limestone
The geomorphology of the islands is controlled by many factors including faulting repeated
historic land submergence and emergence episodes as a result of sea level fluctuation
submarine erosion and subaerial deep tropical weathering and erosion of landforms
The volcanic islands are a result of the accumulation of volcanic deposits along the
crest of the Palau Ridge The limestone islands were once formed in shallow waters and have
experienced a complex history of tectonic movements in addition to repeated submergence
and emergence related to sea level fluctuations affecting the growth and distribution of
limestone deposits
GEOLABS INC
W O 4169 00 Page 7
Malakal island is situated at the mapped transition zone between volcanic and
limestone island geology As a result Malakal Island contains both volcanic and limestone
rock and their derived soils Malakal Island is separated from the volcanic island of
Arakebesang located to the north Koror island is primarily of volcanic origin with a small area
along the east side composed of a limestone rock ridge Causeways constructed on the
shallow water reef platforms between the individual islands connect the islands of Koror
Malakal and Arakebesang
Based on the Geology Map of Babelthuap and Adjacent Islands Palau Islands
prepared by the U S Army and the U S Geological Survey the geologic materials mapped on
Babeldaob Koror and Malakal Islands are mainly volcanic rock deposits However based
on our previous field exploration the volcanic deposits volcanic breccia are locally overlain
by layers of weathered rock materials and younger alluvial estuarine lagoonal and coralline
beach deposits
Volcanic breccia may be defined as a coarse grained volcanic rock composed of
various broken rock fragments called clasts that are welded together by heat and pressure
with a fine grained mineral rock matrix Breccia rock is typically formed by volcanic eruptions
and or volcanic debris flows consisting of super heated rock masses
The emerged limestone islands are generally steeply sloping and densely vegetated
with little surface soil cover The coastal areas often exhibit mangrove swamp underlain by
soft organic soils and lagoonal deposits of reef formation and coralline fragments and sands
It should be noted that the limestone frequently exhibits signs of rock dissolution producing
cave and shoreline overhanging structures
The volcanic islands typically contain some soil cover grading to volcanic rock
formation at depth The soil thickness may vary depending on location The islands are
typically incised by multiple streams and contain steep slopes covered by dense vegetation
often obscuring the ground surface Broad areas of mangrove swamp are more typical of the

volcanic islands where larger volumes of alluvial sediments are generated and deposited along
the coast and at the mouths of streams

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling ten
borings for a total footage of approximately 626 lineal feet of field exploration Five of the
borings Boring Nos 1 through 5 were drilled for the proposed low rise structures within
Malakal Marina Village and extended to depths of about 25 to 60 feet below the existing
grades Three deep borings Boring Nos 6 through 8 were drilled to depths of about 96 to
107 5 feet for the buildings fronting Malakal Harbor sheet pile bulkhead and marina facilities
Two off shore borings Boring Nos 9 and 10 were drilled to depths of about 63 3 and 65 feet
below the mudline for the marina facilities The approximate locations of the borings are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by a 10 to 15 foot
thick layer of undocumented fill The upper 4 to 5 feet of the undocumented fill consisted of
a mixture of medium dense silty sands and stiff silty clays The lower portion of the
undocumented fill layer is generally comprised of loose sands with coral fragments and soft
clays The undocumented fills were placed over loose to medium dense lagoonal deposits
consisting of coralline sands with shell and coral fragments extending to depths of about 50
to 60 feet below the existing ground surface
The lagoonal deposits coralline sands encountered in the borings ranged from loose
to medium dense with most corrected Standard Penetration Test SPT N values falling
between 3 and 30 Occasional coral fragments cobbles and boulders were observed in the
coralline sands and the measured SPT N values were above 30 The coralline sands were
generally underlain by stiff alluvial or saprolitic clay soils overlying rock formations Two types
of rock formation consisting of limestone and volcanic breccia were encountered at depths of
about 60 to 80 feet below the ground surface
Groundwater levels were measured at about 3 to 6 feet below the ground surface

Structural fills required underneath the buildings and sidewalks walkways should
consist of crushed coral volcanic breccia limestone mudrock or basalt The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension In addition the fill material should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a CBR value
of 20 or more and a swell value of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with
ASTM D 1883
Fills or backfilis below the water ievel inciuding the fills behind the new sheet pile
bulkhead should consist of clean granular material such as drain rocks ASTM C 33
No 4 or No 67 gradation up to a minimum of 12 inches above the highest anticipated
water level For backfilling operations below the water level the excavated trenches
or building footprint areas should be lined on all sides with a layer of non woven filter

fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent prior to backfilling This is to reduce the
potential for migration of the finer adjacent soils into the open graded clean granular
fill materials resulting in future ground settlement A layer of filter fabric should also be
provided between the open graded clean granular fill materials and the backfill above
Where imported fill materials will be required the imported fill materials should conform
to the requirements presented above for structural fill and backfill The materials
should be tested and approved prior to delivery to the project site to confirm that the
materials are suitable for the intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
As previously indicated fills or backfills below the water level should consist of
free draining granular materials such as drain rock ASTM C 338 No 4 or No 67
gradation Mechanical compaction of these materials will not be required during
placement However densification by pounding the material with the bucket of a
backhoe or other vibratory action should be performed during placement In general
we recommend that the free draining granular materials be used up to above the
groundwater level or up to about Elevation 4 feet MSL only whichever is lower We
recommend that fill materials consisting of either compacted general fill or structural fill
material be used above the free draining granular materials especially above Elevation
4 feet MSL
General fills and backfills above the water table should be placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Structural fills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
moisture conditioned to above ihe optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum
GEOLABS INC

moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
Utility Trench Excavation
We envision that new utility lines such as sewer drain and water lines will likely be
required for the project It should be noted that groundwater was encountered at depths of
about 3 to 6 feet below the existing ground surface Depending on the tides groundwater may
be encountered near the existing ground surface Therefore some of the utility trench
excavations will likely encounter the groundwater level The deeper excavations greater than
about 5 to 8 feet for installation of the utility lines may need to be adequately shored The
excavated materials should not be stockpiled adjacent to the planned excavation within a
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the excavation to reduce the potential for appreciable
ground movement The excavation support and shoring system used is the responsibility of
the contractor and must comply with all applicable safety requirements
One possible method of shoring would be to use interlocking sheet pile shoring Use
of a sheet pile shoring system may also serve as a cut off wall to aid in the dewatering
operations which is further discussed in the Dewatering section of this report Based on the
borings drilled cobbles and boulders may be present in the near surface undocumented fills
Therefore difficulties may arise during installation of the interlocking sheet pile walls when the
cobbles and boulders are encountered
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the responsibility
of the contractor Therefore it is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be

Utility Trench Backfill
In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of drain rock ASTM C33
No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes If soft loose soils are encountered at the
bottom of the trench excavation an additional 18 to 24 inches of drain rock wrapped in a filter
fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be provided below the bedding layer for more uniform
support Free draining granular materials such as drain rock ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes
or 12 inches above the groundwater level to provide adequate support around the pipes and

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes or
groundwater level to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of general
backfill material less than 6 inches in maximum particle size The backfill should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in maximum 8 inch

Subsoil Permeability
In situ permeability tests were not conducted for this project due to budget limitations
Based on the borings the near surface undocumented fills consist of a mixture of
sands silts gravel volcanic rock fragments and possibly cobbles and boulders The
thickness of the near surface undocumented fill material is variable across the project
site
Due to the heterogeneous subsurface conditions accurate prediction of subsoil
permeability is very difficult The actual subsoil permeability may range broadly and
also vary locally in terms of orders of magnitude It should be noted that the
permeability of the subsoils at the site should be considered at least as permeable as
clean coarse sand based on the materials encountered Therefore the contractor
should pay special attention to the site specific dewatering plan for the proposed
excavations
Dewatering Method
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and he she
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on the conditions encountered The dewatering method
selected should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the
proposed excavation As previously indicated the underlying fill materials at this site
may be highly permeable and capable of transmitting large quantities of water
especially when the bottom of excavation is significantly below the high tide water table
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents should be
written to include ali works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater tabie
and or to exclude the water from the excavations to allow construction of the proposed
structures under safe and dry conditions These works or systems may include but
are not limited to grouting cut off walls tremie concrete plugs or any combination of
the above and or other possible methods Based on previous experience an
GEOLABS INC

interlocking sheet pile shoring system embedded deep into the granular lagoonal
deposits would work successfully in reducing the amount of seepage water
Dewatering Considerations
We suggest that the following four basic criteria be considered in selection of a suitable
method of dewatering
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or damage
to existing structures roads and environment
b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and provide safe
and dry working conditions in the excavation
C The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions
d The dewatering should be carried out such that over pumping to lower
the groundwater level is avoided such that the existing improvements
surrounding the project site would not be adversely impacted
Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering
It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained by the contractor
to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the responsibility
of the contractor His her qualified representative who should be required to be
continuously present on site during dewatering activities will have the best opportunity
to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and to implement as
soon as possible the necessary precautionary or remedial measures including but not
limited to stopping or slowing down the dewatering operations

Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils may be
susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations should be
carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the excavation bottoms
Therefore the project dewatering operations should be performed without pumping out
soil fines pumping clear water only and should be coordinated with shoring installation
such that the excavation stability is not adversely affected Excessive pumping which
removes soil fines may result in a blowing or heaving of the excavation bottom or
sides
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches connected to
excavations If this occurs the granular bedding and or backfill in the utility trenches
could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater drawdown resulting in
settlements and potential damage to utility lines and or other existing adjacent
structures

DESCRIPTIONS OF SITE GRADING TERMS
MALAKAL MARINA VILLAGE
MALAKAL ISLAND REPUBLIC OF PALAU
The following terms have been used in the context of this report A brief description
and or definition of these terms is provided below to aid in the understanding of the earthwork
discussions and recommendations presented in this report
Subgrade
The material in excavation cuts and fills embankments immediately below any subbase
base pavement or other improvement Also as a secondary definition the level below which
work above is referenced
General Fill
General fill refers to the fill materials placed outside the limits of the new building and
improvement areas The material should be free of particles rock fragments larger than
6 inches in maximum dimension The material should also be free of soft wet soils vegetation
and other deleterious materials
Structural Fill
Structural fill refers to the fill materials placed within the limits of the new building and
improvement areas The material should consist of crushed coral volcanic breccia limestone
or basalt The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension The material should contain not more than 30 percent particles
passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a CBR value of 20 or more and a swell
value of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883
Aggregate Subbase
Aggregate subbase refers to the cushion materials placed underneath the walkway slabs The
material should conform to the requirements for structural fill presented above
Backfill
A specified material used in refilling a cut trench or other excavation placed at a specified
degree of compaction Backfill inside utility trench excavations should consist of granular
materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum size Pipe bedding and initial trench backfill
should consist of free draining drain rock sand and or well graded granular materials Backfill
behind retaining walls should consist of granular materials or the on site soils with a maximum
particle size of 3 inches
Backfill Below Water
Backfill below the water level should consist of clean free draining granular materials such
as ASTM C 33 No 4 gradation or ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
Relative Compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91 The moist preparation method wet to dry should be used for the on site soils
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum
dry density

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the Malakal Marina Village site were explored by drilling
and sampling ten borings extending to depths ranging from about 25 to 107 5 feet below the
existing ground surface or mudline Two of the borings Boring Nos 9 and 10 were drilled
from a barge anchored in Malakal Harbor The approximate locations of the borings drilled are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using truck mounted and portable
drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer or geologist who monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 10
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock formations and boulder materials encountered at the site
were obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling
is a rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material
left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring
attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as REC 80
Appendix A Continued
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures
or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the RQD would be
50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical Handbook
of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
In addition specific gravity tests ASTM D 854 were performed on the soil samples to
evaluate the degree of saturation and void ratio of the soil samples The results of these tests
are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Six Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths The test results are summarized on Tables B 1 1 through
B 1 8
Twenty two sieve analysis including hydrometer tests ASTM D 422 were performed
on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
Two consolidation tests ASTM D 2435 were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the soils Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plates B 1 through B 2
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected soil sample to
evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plate B 3
Two Modified Proctor tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on the bulk samples of the
near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content relationships The results
of the tests are presented on Plates B 4 and B 5
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The results of the tests are presented on Plates B 6 and B 7
