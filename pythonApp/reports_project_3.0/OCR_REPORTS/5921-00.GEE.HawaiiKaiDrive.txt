SECTION 1 0 GENERAL i
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Drain Outlet Structure on Hawaii Kai Drive in Hawaii Kai on
the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended to assist in the design of the drainage
outlet structure only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to
the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project involves the construction of a drain outlet structure for an existing
drain line in the vicinity of Hawaii Kai Drive and Kamilonui Place in Hawaii Kai on the
Island of Oahu Hawaii The existing 48 inch drain line carries run off from the
Kealaula Kai subdivision to discharge into the Hawaii Kai marina The existing drain
outlet does not have wing walls or any similar drainage outlet structure The last
reinforced concrete pipe section of the drain line at the discharge end has lost support
due to erosion and has become detached from the existing drain line Based on the
plans provided the new outlet structure will be constructed of grouted rock masonry and
will have a headwall and wing walls
1 3 Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the surface and
subsurface conditions at the site to develop a soil rock data set to formulate
geotechnical engineering recommendations for design of the proposed drainage outlet
structure Our work was performed in general accordance with our fee proposal dated

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of the western
third of the island while the younger Koolau Shield forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below the Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology and
subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea level
during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The
subsurface conditions within the project area are dominated by alluvial and related
deposits laid during varied stands of the sea The base of the stratigraphic section in this
area is Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age many sea level changes occurred as
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level of Oahu During lower stands deep amphitheater headed
valleys were cut into the Koolau massif The bases of some of these valleys extend below
current sea level Alluvial sediments accumulated in the floors of some of the valleys
About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that
rise the valleys in the project area were drowned In the last 10 000 years or so the sea
level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued
to accumulate in low energy estuarine lagoonal or marshy environments resulting in thick
deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in the areas that were formerly valleys
and drainageways Accumulation of littoral sediments at the mouth of the valleys formed a
broad lagoonal area which was modified into a fish pond referred to as Kuapa Pond

2 2 Existing Site Conditions
The project site is in the southeastern portion of the Island of Oahu Hawaii within
Kamilonui Valley in the Hawaii Kai community The existing drain outlet is at the
northern end of Hawaii Kai Drive in the headwaters of Kuapa Pond or the Hawaii Kai
Marina Existing ground elevations at site were not available at the time this report was
prepared however based on visual observations it is estimated to be on the order of
about 10 to 15 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
The site and the surrounding area is currently vacant raw land and is covered by
dry land grasses and shrubs The existing drain outlet is situated on a bare earth slope
approximately 10 to 12 feet in height sloping down into the water of Kuapa Pond or the
Hawaii Kai Marina A section of the 48 inch reinforced concrete pipe which has detached
from the existing drain line rests at the toe of the slope Upstream of the existing outlet is a
lined drainage channel providing drainage for the floodplain of Kamilonui Valley This
channel discharges to the marina upstream of the project site and appears to have
contributed to scour at the base of the slope
2 3 Subsurface Conditions
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
one boring designated as Boring No 1 to a depth of about 41 5 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration generally encountered a thin surface layer of fill about
2 5 feet in thickness underlain by interbedded very stiff colluvial and alluvial soils to a
depth of about 12 5 feet below the existing ground surface Below these colluvial and
alluvial soils our exploration encountered a sequence of relatively soft marsh and
lagoonal soils to a depth of about 19 5 feet below the existing surface These relatively
soft soils were in turn underlain by stiff to very stiff older alluvial soils to the maximum
depth explored of 41 5 feet below the existing ground surface

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious materials and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site or
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should
be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft soils may be
used as fill provided that they meet fill material requirements
Areas to receive fill should be lightly scarified moisture conditioned to about
2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density We
wish to emphasize that moisture conditioning of the fill materials is an integral part
of the fill placement recommendations provided herein Compaction should be
accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable
compaction equipment These initial compaction operations should be performed
in the presence of a Geolabs representative
Keying and Benching
Keying and benching is recommended for fills to be placed at the site in areas
where the existing slope is steeper than five horizontal to one vertical 5H 1V
These keys and benches should be firmly seated in the native soils at the site

Fills and Backfills
Fills will generally be limited to culvert and wall backfills Fill materials should
consist of non expansive materials less than 3 inches in largest dimension with a
minimum CBR value of 20 The fill materials should also be free of vegetation and
deleterious materials Geolabs should observe and or test imported fill materials for
their suitability prior to being transported to the site for the intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum
dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density We wish to emphasize that moisture conditioning of the fill
materials is an integral part of the fill placement recommendations provided herein
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or
other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the subgrade soils are critical
elements of earthwork a Geolabs representative should perform observations and
soil density tests during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the
required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content Where
compaction is less than required additional compactive effort should be applied
with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified
compaction It should be noted that the moisture requirement of the fills and
subgrades about 2 percent above the optimum moisture is an important
requirement for the use of the on site silty ash soils

Excavation
The excavation for the outlet structure and tie in to the existing culvert will penetrate
the near surface very stiff to hard soils It is anticipated that these shallow soils
may be excavated with normal excavation equipment such as excavators
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based
on field data obtained from the field exploration performed at the project site We
recommend encouraging contractors bidding on this project to examine the site
conditions and soil data to make their own interpretation
Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration it appears
that most of the cut slopes would expose the surface residual soils consisting of stiff
to very stiff to hard clayey silts and silty clays In general we believe that the cut
slopes may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter If the
existing ground surface is steeper than 5H 1V fills should be keyed and benched
into the existing slope as discussed above to provide stability of the new fill
against sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue
up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement
recommendations
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope
ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face Track rolling of slopes should not be
accepted In the event that over cutting of a slope occurs keying and benching
requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design
grade with sliver fills Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and
slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for
erosion of the finished slopes

3 2 Outlet Structure
In general we anticipate that a shallow foundation will be utilized for support of
the planned reinforced concrete box culvert structures Our exploration indicates that
the base of the structure may be soft and loose sediments Therefore special
stabilization procedures may be required to support the new outlet structure foundation
if soft soils are encountered
These special procedures would consist of over excavating the foundation
subgrade to a depth of 3 feet below the foundation elevation and installing a
stabilization mat The mat should be constructed by leveling the bottom of the
over excavated area and placing a layer of non woven geotextile such as Mirafi 160N
or similar across the bottom of the excavation allowing sufficient material for the textile
to extend up the sidewalls and to overlap over the top of the excavated area The
over excavated area should then be backfilled with self densifying clean granular
material such as No 3B Fine ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation to an elevation of at least
1 foot over the high water level The granular backfill material should then be
mechanically tamped to further densify the material and for the geotextile to fully overlap
across the top of the area forming a capsule
Based on this procedure we believe that an allowable bearing value of up to
2 000 pounds per cubic foot pcf may be used for the structure

Note that free standing walls are defined as walls that are allowed to rotate about
0 5 percent of the wall height Surcharge and hydrostatic loads are not accounted for in
the above values Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges live loads and point
loads should be considered in the design
For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall a
rectangular distribution with uniform pressure equal to 31 percent of the vertical
surcharge pressure acting on the entire height of the wall which is free to deflect
cantilever may be used in design For walls that are restrained a rectangular
distribution equal to 47 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire
height of the wall may be used for design Additional analyses during design may be
needed to evaluate the surcharge effects of point loads and line loads
Backfill behind retaining walls should consist of on site excavated weathered
rock or imported select granular fill material compacted to between 90 and 95 percent
relative compaction Over compaction of the retaining wall backfill should be avoided
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system consists of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation placed directly
around the perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the wall discharging to an
appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated drainage product
such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used in lieu of the drainage material The
prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated
pipe at the base of the wall Unless covered by concrete the upper 12 inches of backfill
should consist of impervious material such as the on site silty clay material to reduce
water infiltration behind the walls

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling a
single boring designated as Boring No 1 to a depth of about 41 5 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate location shown on the Site Plan Plate 3 The boring
was drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger and coring equipment

examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Log of Boring Plate A 1

ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample
depths
