This technical memorandum presents our geotechnical recommendations for the 
planning and preliminary design of the Shore Protection project at the Naval Support 
Facility, Diego Garcia in the British Indian Ocean Territory. The approximate location of 
Diego Garcia within the Indian Ocean is shown on the Area Map, Plate 1. 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located at the Naval Support Facility, Diego Garcia in the 
British Indian Ocean Territory. The project location and general vicinity are shown on 
the Project Location Map, Plate 2. Based on the information provided, we understand 
that shoreline erosion has been observed adjacent to the Diego Garcia 2 (DG2) Access 
Road. Concern has been expressed regarding the rate of erosion, potential damage 
from storms, and potential impacts to the infrastructure and operational readiness of the 
facility. Therefore, it is desired to construct approximately 1,370 linear meters of 
engineered shoreline revetment adjacent to the Base Operating Services Contractor 
(BOSC) Camp, Seabreeze Village, North Power Plant, and POL (Petroleum, Oil, & 
Lubricants) Farm and Wastewater Treatment Plant. It has been proposed that a sheet 
pile wall will be installed immediately landward of the revetment to protect against 
extreme wave events. 
Within the limits of the project, approximately 280 linear meters of existing 
revetment is present along the shoreline near the POL tank farm, and a separate 
contract has been issued for the repair of the existing revetment.  
Existing Site Conditions 
A team of two engineers and an engineering technician from Geolabs conducted 
a site reconnaissance of the project area from February 27 through March 6, 2015. The 
project site is located seaward of the DG2 Access Road and is bounded roughly by the 
sewer outfall to the south and Manila Way to the north. The site primarily consists of a 
dune, beach, and a submerged reef flat.

Submerged Reef Flat 
Immediately offshore and adjacent to the beach lays a submerged reef flat. 
According to available information, the reef is composed principally of cemented coral 
and coralline debris. This material is found in situ in both its original coral structure, i.e., 
the structure and framework in which it grew, and as a limestone composed of 
re-cemented fragmental material. The competency and degree of cementation of these 
materials are anticipated to be variable. During low tide, the reef remains slightly 
submerged; however, surf rolls across the flat and breaks on the beach at high tide.  
According to records, the reef was extensively mined for aggregate during the 
1970s and early 1980s. In combination with blasting, two operations were used in 
mining the reef. The first was a crane and dragline – Saurman-type system where 
material was scraped shoreward. The second used a front end loader tractor, which 
loaded trucks during low tide. The second method was accomplished two hours a day 
before and after the extreme low tide, or essentially four hours a day. Remnants of the 
mined reef are visually apparent during our reconnaissance. 
With the exception of the mined areas, the natural reef is relatively flat and 
uniform in a regional sense. In some areas, a slight ridge is present on the seaward 
edge of the reef before dropping off to depths of several hundred meters.  
Beach 
In general, the beach slopes upward from the reef flat to the dune. Visible 
beachrock is present on the seaward (lower) portion of the beach; however, the extent, 
depth, width, cementation, and hardness of the beachrock vary.  
Beachrock is formed by the cementation of unconsolidated beach sand, silt, and 
gravel through super-saturation of calcium carbonate (CaCO3) through direct 
evaporation of seawater. Older zones of beachrock are located in the seaward part of 
the formation and younger zones are on the landward side. The thickness and width of 
the exposed beachrock was measured in several areas and ranged from 150 to 
750 mm and 1 to 12 meters, respectively. In a few areas, beachrock was not visible at 
the surface but was buried beneath unconsolidated sand. A cut (about 14 meters 
parallel with the beach) was observed in the beachrock just south of the power plant.  
It also was noted that the beachrock in some areas is being undermined by wave 
action and is sloughing into the sea. In general, the exposed beachrock was relatively 
hard and usually did not fracture when struck by a hammer. It is anticipated that the 
hardness/cementation of the beachrock will decrease when progressing landward.

As mentioned above, an existing revetment is present on the beach adjacent to 
the POL tank farm. The existing revetment is in various states of disrepair with the 
southern section of the revetment in relatively good condition and the northern section 
in relatively poor condition. According to available information, the northern section of 
the existing revetment consists of a multi-layer concrete slab/stone revetment keyed 
into large concrete blocks along the toe. It is unclear whether beachrock is buried 
beneath the sand and/or revetment, or the sand has been removed in its entirety in this 
area. The southern section of the existing revetment consists of large concrete blocks 
and slabs stacked in multiple layers from the beachrock to the berm crest. 
Rehabilitation, reconstruction, and/or modification of the existing revetment are not part 
of the scope for the MCON P-185 project.  
Dune 
A dune is immediately present landward of the beach. In most areas of the 
project site, a vertical scarp between the beach and dune is present resulting in 
measured height differentials of up to 2.5 meters. On the landward side of the dune, the 
dune slopes downward toward the DG2 Access Road. With a few man-made 
exceptions, the dune is heavily vegetated with scaevola shrubs and coconut palm trees. 
Within the scope of the project, the dune width ranges from roughly 10 to 45 meters. 
Record drawings indicate several utilities are present on the landward edge of the dune, 
immediately adjacent and parallel to the DG2 Access Road.  
Anticipated Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by excavating 
eighteen test pits, designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 through 18, extending to depths 
between about 1.2 and 2.7 meters below the existing ground surface. The approximate 
locations of the test pits are shown on the Overall Site Plan (Plate 3) and Enlarged Site 
Plans (Plates 4.1 through 4.7). The majority of the test pits were excavated between the 
exposed beachrock and dune except for Test Pit Nos. 13 and 18, which were excavated 
on top of the dune. In order to evaluate the relative density of the near-surface soils, 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) tests were conducted in general accordance with 
ASTM D6951 at the test pit locations.  
Based on our field exploration, it appears that the project site may be underlain 
by very loose to medium dense near-surface beach and dune deposits consisting of 
relatively clean gravelly sands. These carbonate materials frequently possess some 
degree of cementation, which may be present in layers or lenses of varying thickness 
and continuity. Near the water table, medium dense to very dense silty coralline gravel 
was encountered that was underlain by medium hard to hard coral/algal limestone. 
Based on available information, the depth of the limestone probably varies from less 
than 3 meters to in excess of 6 meters. It is anticipated the limestone contain cavities

Groundwater was encountered near sea level during our field exploration. 
Because of the porous nature of the subsurface materials, the groundwater level is

Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site, a shore

Detailed discussions and preliminary recommendations are presented in the following 
sections herein.

consisting of some excavations and/or embankments will be required for the installation

Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed. Vegetation, debris, and other unsuitable materials 
should be removed and disposed properly off-site to reduce the potential for 
contamination of the excavated materials designated to be reused as fill and/or backfill.  
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed, and the resulting 
excavation should be properly backfilled with granular fill materials placed in 200-mm 
loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.

After clearing, grubbing, and demolition, the exposed subgrade should be 
scarified to a minimum depth of 200 mm, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage 
of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM 
D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Soft and/or loose, weak, or yielding areas (or cavities) disclosed during site 
preparation operations should be over-excavated to expose firm or dense ground, and 
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with fill materials (well graded 75-mm 
minus materials) compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Saturation 
and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrades due to inclement weather and poor 
drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
compacted fill material. 
Excavation 
Based on our field exploration and the anticipated grading, excavation for this 
project generally will consist of excavations for a revetment and/or other infrastructure. 
Some of the grading and infrastructure work may encounter cobbles, layers or lenses of 
strongly cemented material, beachrock, and possibly limestone. It is anticipated that the 
near-surface beach and dune deposits may be excavated with conventional heavy 
excavation equipment. However, deeper excavations may require the use of hoerams 
and/or other rock excavation equipment. We recommend encouraging contractors 
bidding on this project to examine the site conditions and geotechnical data to make 
their own prudent and reasonable interpretation. 
Fills and Backfills 
In general, the excavated on-site granular soils (without deleterious materials 
and/or over-sized materials) may be re-used as a source of fill material. Because of the 
relatively shallow groundwater and the proximity to the shoreline, drying or aeration of 
the excavated materials may be required prior to their use as compacted fills. Therefore, 
a drying area should be designated for the stockpiling of the usable excavated granular 
materials. Due to the porous nature of the materials, the time required for the aeration of 
the excavated materials should be relatively short, on the order of about 1 to 3 days, 
depending on the weather conditions and the thickness of the stockpile.  
In the event that imported fill materials are needed for the project construction, 
imported fill materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger 
than 75 mm (3 inches) in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory

In general, drain rock fill consisting of relatively clean, free-draining granular fill

testing laboratory should test the imported fill materials for conformance with these 
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
As indicated above, fills and/or backfills below the water level should consist of 
free-draining granular materials, such as drain rock fill, as recommended above. 
Mechanical compaction of these materials will not be required during placement. 
However, densification by pounding the material with the bucket of a backhoe, track-
walking or by other acceptable means should be done in a consistent manner during 
placement. The last lift of the material above the water level should be proof-rolled a 
minimum of eight passes with the use of a vibratory plate or similar equipment prior to 
the placement of the non-woven geotextile fabric, such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent, 
and capping with compacted fill to the finished subgrade. 
General fill materials above the water table (minimum 300 mm above water level) 
should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 200 mm in loose thickness, moisture-
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to at least 
90 percent relative compaction.  
Revetment  
We envision a revetment system consisting of armor rock over an underlayer 
rock may be used to reduce the potential for appreciable shore erosion at the project 
site. Detailed discussions and recommendations pertaining to the geotechnical aspects 
of revetment construction are presented below.

Revetment Ground Settlement 
Due to the heavy weight of the armor rocks, the existing ground under a 
revetment could settle under the new loads if soft and/or loose soils are present in the 
shallow subsurface. In order to evaluate the relative density of the near-surface soils, 
we reviewed and processed the field data obtained from the DCP tests conducted along 
the shore protection alignment. 
The procedures used in our data processing generally were derived based on the 
publication entitled “ASTM Special Technical Publication #399 – Dynamic Cone for 
Shallow In-Situ Penetration Testing” prepared by George F. Sowers and 
Charles S. Hedges. In the publication, the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Resistance in 
blows per increment is determined by the number of blows to advance a 45-degree 
cone a distance of 45 mm utilizing a 6.8-kg steel ring weight falling 500 mm. The 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Resistance obtained is then correlated to the Standard 
Penetration Test (SPT) N-Values in blows per 300 mm based on a chart (presented 
herein as Plate 5) developed by the authors. Based on this information, we developed a 
correlation between Dynamic Cone Penetrometer Resistance, SPT N-Values, and 
apparent soil density, which is presented in the table below.

significant portion of the ground settlement should occur as the armor rocks are

To reduce the potential for significant undermining of the revetment due to wave 
action, we recommend toeing the revetment into materials that are resistant to erosion 
such as the beachrock or limestone. Based on the topographic information provided, the 
elevation of the top of the exposed beachrock was measured between Elevation +0.2 to 
+1.5 meters Mean Sea Level (MSL). Based on our field exploration and available 
subsurface information, the top of limestone appears to be at an elevation between +0.3 
to -1.0 meter MSL. 
Porosity of Armor Stone Revetment 
It should be noted that the porosity used in the armor stone revetment is 
essentially an empirical number based on past experience with stone revetment design 
and construction work. In general, the porosity of the armor rock and underlayer rock 
depends to a large extent on the method of installation, i.e. randomly placed versus 
tightly packed, as well as the shape of the stones, etc. Based on our literature research 
and experience with revetment design in Diego Garcia, we believe a porosity between 
about 30 to 40 percent may be used for both the armor rock and underlayer rock. 
Sheet Piling 
A sheet pile wall has been proposed along the top of dune in addition to a 
revetment. Available subsurface information indicates a layer of medium hard to very 
hard limestone underlying the loose beach and dune deposits. As mentioned previously, 
we estimate the top of limestone in the area to be at an elevation of about +0.3 to -1.0 
meter MSL.  
The limestone and underlying coralline materials appear to be sufficiently thick to 
provide embedment for a sheet pile system, if needed; however, it is anticipated there 
will be some variability in the hardness and thickness of the limestone across the project 
site. Due to the hardness of the limestone, some predrilling likely would be necessary to 
install the sheet piles into limestone. Special attention should to be given to corrosion 
protection of the steel sheet piles considering the near-shore marine environment of the 
project.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of excavating and sampling eighteen test 
pits, designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 through 18, extending to depths between about 1.2 
and 2.7 meters below the existing ground surface. The approximate locations of the test 
pits are shown on the Overall Site Plan (Plate 3) and Enlarged Site Plans (Plates 4.1 
through 4.7). We used an Airman AX40 track-mounted excavator to excavate the test 
pits. 
Our engineer observed the excavation operations on a near-continuous basis 
and classified the materials encountered in the test pits by visual and textural 
examination in the field. These classifications were further reviewed by visual 
observation and testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance 
with the Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A-0.1. Deviations made 
to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Test Pits, Plates A-1 through A-18. 
In order to evaluate the consistency or relative density of the near-surface soils, 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) tests were conducted at the test pit locations. The 
procedures used in our DCP tests are in general accordance with ASTM D6951, 
Standard Test Method for Use of the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer in Shallow Pavement 
Applications. The DCP field data is presented in Appendix C and also graphically on the 
Logs of Test Pits (Appendix A).

Laboratory Tests 
Four Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected 
bulk samples taken from the test pits to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the 
near-surface soils and to aid in soil classification. The fines content of the samples 
tested (percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve) are indicated on the Logs of Test 
Pits at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution 
is provided on Plate B-1. 
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils taken from the test pits to evaluate the quality of 
the materials tested for use as fills. The test results are presented on Plates B-2 through 
B-5. 
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils taken from the test pits to evaluate the dry density and 
moisture content relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-6 through 
B-9.
