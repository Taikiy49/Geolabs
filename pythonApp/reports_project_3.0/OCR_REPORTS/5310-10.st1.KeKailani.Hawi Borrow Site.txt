Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area
of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes which include the following Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which
has had activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea both of which are still active
The proposed borrow site is situated on the western flank of Kohala Mountain
which has formed the northern portion of the island The proposed borrow site is underlain
by basaltic lava flows of the Hawi Volcanic Series of Kohala Mountain which were
deposited during the Pleistocene Epoch Stearns and Macdonald 1946 The Hawi
Volcanic Series typically is composed of basaltic lava flows that are overlain by a relatively
thin layer of palagonitized ash locally referred to as Pahala Ash The volcanic ash soil at
the project site differs from Pahala Ash found in other portions of the Island of Hawaii in
that the in situ moisture content is generally lower and the soil exists in a dry friable state
The near surface soils at the proposed borrow site have been subjected to wind erosion
that has resulted in the formation of a desert pavement on the surface of the site This
desert pavement is a surface layer of cobbles and gravel which were left behind as the
finer ash was blown away
The lava formation at the project site appears to be of a a and pahoehoe basalt
type flows which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean A a lava typically is
characterized by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged
accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders Typically a more dense
and layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core Pahoehoe lava is
characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock Cavities are encountered commonly in pahoehoe lavas These
cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are
generally smaller in horizontal extent

Discussions and Recommendations
Based on our site reconnaissance test pit exploration and laboratory test results
we believe that the materials generated from the proposed borrow site in the Hawi area
may be used as a source of fill materials for the proposed Ke Kailani project located within
the Mauna Lani Resort However it should be noted that several geotechnical
considerations may have the potential for adverse impacts on the design and construction
of the proposed project The following sections discuss the geotechnical issues that
should be considered when the materials generated from the proposed borrow site in the
Hawi area are used as fill materials for the Ke Kailani project
Cinder Materials
It appears that cinder materials are present below the volcanic ash materials at the
proposed Hawi borrow site The cinder materials encountered in the test pits excavated
generally consisted of medium dense gravelly sands with occasional larger rock
fragments In general the cinder materials consisting of gravelly sands with occasional
larger rock fragments encountered in the test pits excavated may be used as a source of

Volcanic Ash Materials
Our laboratory test results indicate that the volcanic ash materials may be
compacted into a stiff mass with a laboratory CBR value of over 25 Based on the
laboratory tests results we believe that the volcanic ash materials may be used as a
source of general fill materials provided that the materials are free of vegetation
deleterious materials and rock fragments greater than 6 inches in largest dimension It
should be noted that the volcanic ash materials encountered at the proposed Hawi borrow
site have high in situ moisture contents and the materials are sensitive to moisture
contents Due to the moisture sensitive nature of the volcanic ash materials we do not
recommend use of the volcanic ash materials as structural fills or fill materials within the
upper 2 feet of finished subgrades and or finished grades Volcanic ash materials that will
be used as general fills should be capped with a minimum of 2 feet of structural fills to
reduce the potential for excessive moisture fluctuations in the general fills
It should be noted that several special considerations should be given if use of the
volcanic ash materials as a source of general fills is desired which may pose some
difficulties during construction The following paragraphs summarize the required special
considerations if the volcanic ash materials are utilized as general fill materials
Moisture Sensitivity
The engineering properties of volcanic ash materials generally are very sensitive to
moisture fluctuations Based on the generally arid climate at the Ke Kailani project
site volcanic ash materials are likely to experience relatively high fluctuations in the
moisture content from the in situ moisture content of the materials located in the
Hawi area
Based on our experience with volcanic ash materials achieving compaction
requirements generally are more difficult with volcanic ash materials compared to
rock fills typically used on the Island of Hawaii The contractor generally would
have to take extra care in maintaining the moisture content of the volcanic ash
materials close to the optimum moisture content in order to work the materials as
compacted general fill Addition of water would be required if the volcanic ash
materials are allowed to dry out to below the optimum moisture content for
compaction purposes However addition of too much water will require that the

It should be noted that based on our laboratory test results the in situ moisture

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general well graded rocky materials of less than 12 inches in maximum
dimension 12 inch minus materials are commonly used as general fills on the
Island of Hawaii Due to the size and nature of the materials the 12 inch minus
materials generally are allowed to be placed in maximum loose lifts of up to
24 inches However based on the fine grained nature of the volcanic ash
materials general fills consisting of volcanic ash materials should be placed in
maximum loose lifts of 12 inches or less Due to the moisture sensitive nature of
the volcanic ash materials the maximum loose lift thickness of the volcanic ash
materials may need to be reduced to achieve the specified compaction
requirements In addition compaction tests should be taken on each general fill lift
to evaluate the moisture content and compaction of the materials
Based on our field and laboratory test data it appears that excavation of the
volcanic ash materials from the proposed Hawi borrow site and placed as
compacted general fill at the Ke Kailani project site would result in a volume loss
shrinkage between about 15 and 20 percent To apply this shrinkage to the
excavation volume a factor of 0 80 to 0 85 may be used to obtain the compacted
general fill volume when multiplying the in situ volume at the proposed Hawi borrow
site We wish to emphasize that the shrinkage factors provided herein are best
guess estimates only based on the field and laboratory test data our experience
and site reconnaissance of the proposed borrow site only
As mentioned previously the owner and contractor should be aware of the impact
that placement of fill materials in thinner lifts with adequate compaction effort of
each lift may have on the earthwork construction schedule

Use of Volcanic Ash with Other Rock Fills
Based on our laboratory test results the volcanic ash materials generally are
fine grained soils with limited materials retained on the 4 sieve gravel sized
materials Due to the fine grained nature the volcanic ash materials should not be
used in conjunction with rock fills greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension
This requirement is necessary to reduce the potential for the fine grained soils to
migrate into the openings of rocky materials with the passage of time which may
result in formation of voids in the fill materials We believe that the volcanic ash
materials still may be used in conjunction with well graded structural fill materials
6 inch minus however there may be some restrictions placed on the combination
of materials at specific areas of the project site These potential restrictions will
need to be determined depending on the various types and gradation of materials
proposed for use as fill materials are known
Therefore proper management and coordination of fill materials to be used on the
Ke Kailani project site will be required if volcanic ash materials are to be used for
the project
Limitations
It should be noted that it is not possible to observe all the site conditions based on a
limited test pit exploration of the proposed Hawi borrow site Variations of subsurface
conditions from those encountered in our limited test pits excavated across the proposed
Hawi borrow site may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not
become evident until further excavations are underway at the proposed borrow site
