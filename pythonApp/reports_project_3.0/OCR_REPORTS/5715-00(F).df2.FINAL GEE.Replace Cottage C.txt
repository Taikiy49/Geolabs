PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project involves renovation and replacement of some cottages in the
University of Hawaii at Manoa UHM Lyon Arboretum in Manoa Valley on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The project site is located in the rear of the valley above Paradise Park
as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The existing Cottage C is a two story wood framed structure that is currently
being used as the laboratory facilities It is proposed to demolish the existing cottage and
replace it with a new cottage laboratory The details of the replacement structure were still
under development at the time this report was prepared However it is anticipated that the
new cottage will be a one or two story lightly loaded wood framed structure Preliminary
plans indicate that the new cottage will sit predominantly in cut

Our field exploration program for the Replace Cottage C project consisted of
drilling and sampling two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths
of approximately 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered we believe that shallow
spread and continuous footings bearing on recompacted native soils or properly
compacted structural fill may be used to support the building planned provided that the
recommendations presented herein are followed
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections
Foundations
Based on the anticipated subsurface soil conditions we believe that shallow spread
and continuous footing foundations may be used to support the proposed building An
allowable bearing pressure of 2 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for the
foundations bearing on recompacted native soils or properly compacted structural fill The
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
In general the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches
below the lowest adjacent finished grades Foundations located next to utility trenches
should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane
extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
If pumping and yielding conditions occur at the bottom of the excavation we
recommend over excavating a minimum of 18 inches and backfilling with No 3B Fine
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped with non woven filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N
or equivalent
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed between
the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure acting
against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 4 may
be used for footings bearing on the recompacted native soils or compacted structural fills
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 250 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils

Slabs On Grade
For the interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend
placing a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below the slabs to serve as a capillary break and to
provide more uniform support of the slabs To reduce moisture infiltration and subsequent
damage to floor coverings we recommend placing an impervious moisture barrier on top
of the No 3B Fine gravel Flexible floor coverings such as the carpet or sheet vinyl
should be considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project Control joints
should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at
right angle intersections
The thickened edges of the slab adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at
least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab
should be backfilled tightly against the slab with on site soil and graded to divert water
away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the foundations
Site Grading
At the commencement of grading work areas within the grading limits should be
cleared grubbed and scarified to a minimum depth of 6 inches moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content and recompacted to no less than 90 percent relative
compaction ASTM D 1557 Boulders should be removed to a minimum depth of
12 inches below the structural fill subgrade Trees should be removed and roots greater
than 3 inches in diameter should be over excavated and then backfilled with fill placed in
12 inch maximum loose lifts moisture conditioned to at least the optimum moisture
content and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
Soft and yielding areas encountered should be over excavated to expose firm
natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with fill placed in 12 inch
maximum loose lifts moisture conditioned to at least the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction The excavated soils should be
disposed properly off site and or used in landscape areas where appropriate Boulders
produced from the site grading operations should be segregated and disposed at an
appropriate off site location
Cut areas should be brought to finished subgrade level scarified to a depth of
about 6 inches moisture conditioned to within about 2 percent of the optimum moisture

Existing Seepage Pit
The project site is situated in an area that is not served by a public wastewater
system therefore on site treatment and disposal of wastewater is required At present
wastewater generated in the cottage is treated and disposed through a cesspool It is
proposed to install a septic tank system and to re utilize the existing cesspool or seepage
pit as the disposal component of the system
Personnel from the University maintenance department exposed the top of the
cesspool for testing On Geolabs Inc conducted an infiltration test of
the cesspool Upon opening the plug in the lid it was observed that there was no liquid in
the pit at the time of testing There was a thin layer of sludge in the bottom
During the first run of infiltration testing about 710 gallons of water were
emplaced into the pit at a rate of about 95 gallons per minute A maximum head of
1 4 feet was attained in the bottom of the pit This water dissipated within seven
minutes with all of the water draining out
During the second run an additional 1 750 gallons was injected at a rate of about
87 gallons per minute This test achieved a maximum head build up of 2 feet in the
hole All of the water that was emplaced for this run dissipated in about 11 minutes
Our infiltration testing of the existing cesspool indicates that the cesspool is
draining rapidly and is capable of accepting a relatively large amount of waste fluid
During our testing over 2 450 gallons of water were pumped into the cesspool and all of
the water dissipated rapidly

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to a depth of about 21 5 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate boring locations are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually
and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with
ASTM D 2487 Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths The
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows
required for the specific sampler type used The blow counts may need to be factored to
obtain the Standard Penetration Test SPT blow counts
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
