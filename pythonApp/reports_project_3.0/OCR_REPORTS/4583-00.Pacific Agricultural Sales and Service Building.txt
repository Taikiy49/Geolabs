INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the Pacific Agricultural Sales Service Inc building located on the mauka side of the
intersection of Olai Street and Kalaeloa Boulevard at the Campbell Industrial Park in Kapolei
on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the design of foundations site preparation and
pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is a rectangular shaped parcel of land along Olai Street at the ewa
and mauka sides of the intersection with Kalaeloa Boulevard within Campbell Industrial
Park in Kaoplei on the Island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the information provided the
project calls for the design and construction of a single story combined warehouse and
office building approximately 17 500 square feet in floor area The proposed warehouse
will have a partial mezzanine floor and will be constructed with prefabricated steel
structural members and low CMU walls Estimated maximum column loads will be in the
order of approximately 23 kips The development will also include a parking area at the
northern end of the building and a loading dock at the trucking area at the southern end
of the building
Our experience in the area suggested that the project site may be underlain by
surficial fill soils over dense coral formation Therefore special attention was given to the
quality of the near surface fill soils and the potential for presence of near surface
cavities voids in the underlying coral deposits which may collapse under structural and
vehicular loads

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the
oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of accumulated
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials
caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials Land development and reclamation projects within the last
century have brought the project site to its present form

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration data indicates that the project site may be underlain by medium
dense fill materials at shallow depths overlying dense coral formation Therefore we believe
that the primary geotechnical considerations for the proposed construction will include
preparation of footing subgrades probing and grouting for potential cavities beneath the
building foundations proof rolling of future slab and pavement subgrades and pavement
design for parking areas
Based on the subsurface soil conditions encountered at the new building site we
believe that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings
may be used to support the proposed warehouse structure planned at the site An allowable
bearing pressure of 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for design of footings
bearing on recompacted on site soils or imported fill needed to achieve the design finished
grades To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the
cavities below foundations we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting at
the foundation locations be implemented during construction
An in filled cavity was encountered at a shallow depth in one of the drilled borings In
addition several shallow cavities were observed from the surface during our field exploration
Therefore we recommend that the excavated subgrades underneath future slabs and
pavements be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller or similar heavy
equipment a minimum of 6 passes in an attempt to detect near surface cavities or loose
zones Special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation requirements
presented in the Site Preparation section of this report to reduce the potential for future
distress on the floor slabs and pavements Detailed discussions and recommendations
pertaining to these items are further presented in the following sections of this report

In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grades Footings located adjacent to planned retaining walls should be
embedded deep enough to avoid surcharging the retaining wall foundations If foundations
are located next to utility trenches or easements the foundations should be embedded
below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 4 may be used for footings bearing on the compacted fills Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds
per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils around the footings are
well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper
12 inches of soil should be neglected
We estimate that footing settlements under the anticipated design loads for footings

Foundation Probing
Our experience and current field exploration at the project site indicate that cavities
and or voids are commonly encountered in coralline deposits To reduce the potential for
loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities below foundations we
recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting at the foundation locations be
implemented during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probe holes be drilled at each spread
footing location one probe hole for every 50 square feet of footing area and at 10 feet on
center along wall foundations The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and
should extend to at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation elevation If cavities
and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probe
holes should be drilled at closer spacing to aid in delineating the vertical and lateral extent
of the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with
sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures We recommend that a low
strength sand cement grout with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the grouting
operations In lieu of the sand cement grout fluid lean concrete such as controlled low
strength material flowable fill may also be used for the grouting operation
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during bidding
In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting operation
is complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer lag time between
probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in the construction
schedule
Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration results we anticipate that new concrete slabs on grade
for the proposed development will likely bear on the existing compacted fill materials and
therefore we believe that conventional slab on grade design may be utilized However if
clayey soils or loose soils are encountered at the subgrade level these soils should be

over excavated and replaced with select granular fill materials compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction
We recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer consisting of free draining
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be provided below the
slab above the capping fill to provide for uniform support of the slab To reduce the
potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the cushion layer Two inches of
moist fine sand may be considered above the moisture barrier to provide protection of the
moisture barrier and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Provision of the 2 inches of moist
fine sand above the moisture barrier will be left up to the discretion of the project structural
engineer
For slabs subjected to vehicular traffic and or vibrations due to machinery we
recommend that the cushion fill consist of 6 inches of compacted select borrow subbase
materials in lieu of the 4 inch gravel cushion layer 2 inch moist fine sand layer and the
moisture barrier The select borrow subbase material should be compacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction For the design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade
reaction of 250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the
compacted subbase material Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the
slab joints which will be subjected to vehicular traffic The thickened edges of slabs adjacent
to unpaved area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
For exterior concrete walkways we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion
layer consisting of select borrow subbase material or No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation be provided below the concrete walkway slab Construction joints should be
provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle
intersections

Site Preparation
As previously mentioned our field exploration disclosed that the project site may be
underlain by medium dense subsoils and cavities near the existing ground surface
Therefore special attention should be given to the subgrade preparation for the project
development
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation organic debris construction debris rubbish
old pavements and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly
off site or placed in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials with the cleared and grubbed spoils Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing should be over excavated to expose firm natural
material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill
Existing slabs and foundations of demolished structures should be completely
removed Over excavation resulting from the demolition should be backfilled with compacted
granular fill material Abandoned utilities should also be removed and the resulting
excavation should be properly backfilled with select granular fill material
moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture content placed in 6 to 8 inch level loose
lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities to be
abandoned in place under the proposed structure should be backfilled by pumping lean
concrete under low pressure
The excavated subgrades underneath future slabs and pavements should be
proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory drum roller or similar heavy equipment a
minimum of 6 passes in an attempt to detect the presence of near surface cavities or loose
zones If the proof rolling operations detect a near surface cavity or loose zone the area
GEOLABS INC
W O 4583 00 Page 11
should be ripped open cleaned of debris and then backfilled with compacted fill material
or lean concrete
In general the excavated on site granular soils may be re used as a source of fill
material provided that the largest rock fragment is less than 3 inches in maximum dimension
and that the materials are free of vegetation and deleterious materials Select granular fill
should consist of non expansive granular material such as crushed coral and basalt The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should
have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883 The on site coralline materials should be suitable for use as select
granular fill material provided that they are processed to meet the above requirements
Imported fill material should be tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to
delivery to the project site for its intended use
Select borrow subbase and base course materials should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate and should conform to Sections 703 17 and 703 06 of the Hawaii Standard
Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994
Fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Select borrow subbase and base course
materials should be moisture conditioned to near the optimum moisture placed in level lifts
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

Pavement Design
In general we anticipate that the vehicle loading for the new parking area will consist
primarily of passenger vehicles and light pick up trucks However vehicle loading for the new
access road may consist of heavy delivery trucks and maintenance vehicles Based on the
near surface soil conditions encountered in our field exploration we believe that the following
flexible pavement sections may be used for preliminary design purposes
Pavements Subjected to Private Automobiles and Light Pick Up Trucks
2 0 Inch Asphalt Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
8 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness over Compacted Moist Subgrade
Pavements Subjected to Heavy Delivery and Maintenance Trucks
3 0 Inch Asphalt Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
9 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness over Compacted Moist Subgrade
The pavement subgrades should be proof rolled with a minimum 10 ton vibratory
drum roller or similar heavy equipment a minimum of 6 passes to detect the presence of
potential near surface cavities or loose zones The aggregate base course should consist
of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
CBR and field density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered
during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above sections
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry
the surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site
during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or
adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs be extended a minimum of

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility connections may be required for the proposed project In
general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of
6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the pipes
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus
reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material
to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in
settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of select fill material The backfill should
be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in maximum 8 inch level
loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to
reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement
areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems
be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation
and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should
GEOLABS INC
W O 4583 00 Page 14
also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms
and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils Drainage swales
should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface run off away from
the slabs and foundations
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface clayey soils The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings to depths ranging from about 11 5 to 21 5 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were
drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2116 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One sieve analysis test ASTM C 136 was performed on a selected soil sample to
evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification Graphic
presentation of the test result is provided on Plate B 1
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio Test ASTM D 1883 was performed on the
near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils The test
result is presented on Plate B 2
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on the typical
near surface soils encountered at the site The test result is presented on Plate B 3
