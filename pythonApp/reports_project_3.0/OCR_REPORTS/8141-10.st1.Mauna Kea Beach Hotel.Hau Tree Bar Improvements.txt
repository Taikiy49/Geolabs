performed for the Hau Tree Bar Improvements project planned within Mauna Kea Beach 
Hotel in the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii. The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. This report summarizes 
the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing and presents our 
geotechnical recommendations derived from our engineering analyses for the design of 
the proposed project. 
The recommendations provided herein are intended for the design of foundations, 
slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, site grading, and underground utility lines only. The 
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at 
the end of this report.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers 
an area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during the 
recent geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea 
(both still active). 
The project site is situated on the western flank of Mauna Kea Mountain, which 
makes up the north-central portion of the island. Based on available geologic information 
and geomorphological interpretation, the project site appears to be underlain by basaltic 
lava flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea Volcano, which were deposited 
during the Pleistocene Epoch (Stearns and Macdonald, 1946). 
The lava formation underlying the site generally consists of a’a and pahoehoe 
basalt type flows, which spread and ponded as they flowed toward the ocean. A'a lava 
typically is characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface resembling a 
jagged accumulation of rock fragments, including cobbles and boulders. Typically, a 
denser and more layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core. 
Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth, rope-like, or billowy surface and an internal 
structure of vesicular (porous) rock. Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe 
lavas. Cavities form when lavas are still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes 
(intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form when 
molten lava drains from the cooling flow, leaving a hollow tube-like structure, which may 
extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets (interflow 
cavities) are generally smaller in horizontal extent.

Earthquakes and Seismicity 
Generally, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in the 
tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground movement 
of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii 
experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small that they can

are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island of

3.2 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We anticipate concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the ground floors of the 
new structures. For the interior slabs (not subjected to vehicular traffic), we recommend 
providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill below the slabs for uniform support. 
The cushion fill should consist of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) to 
serve as a capillary moisture break. 
To reduce the potential for excess moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to 
floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel 
cushion fill layer. Where provided, flexible floor coverings should be considered above 
the floor slab because they can better mask minor slab cracking. 
Where slabs are subjected to vehicular traffic or sustained vibrations, a minimum 
of 6 inches of compacted select borrow subbase materials (3-inch minus granular 
materials) should be provided below the slabs in lieu of the 4-inch thick cushion fill layer. 
The select borrow subbase material layer should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. The impervious moisture barrier may be omitted for these slabs. 
In addition, exterior concrete walkways and exterior flatwork may be required for 
the project. Generally, we recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick cushion fill layer 
of open-graded 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) or select borrow subbase 
below exterior concrete slabs that will not be subjected to vehicular traffic. Select borrow 
subbase, where utilized, should be compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.

3.3 
Retaining Structures 
Retaining structures of up to about 10 to 12 feet in height may be required for the 
project. The following general guidelines may be used in the design of retaining structures 
planned at the project site. 
3.3.1 Retaining Structure Foundations 
In general, we believe retaining structure foundations may be designed in 
accordance with the recommendations and parameters presented in the 
"Foundations" section herein. The retaining structure footings should have a 
minimum width of 18 inches. In addition, the bottom of wall foundations on relatively 
flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest 
adjacent finished grade. A Geolabs representative should observe the footing 
excavations during construction to evaluate the exposed conditions. 
For sloping ground conditions, the bottom of the footing should extend deeper to 
obtain a minimum 6-foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside 
edge of the footing to the slope face. Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction 
of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings.

3.3.4 Drainage 
In general, retaining structures should be well-drained to reduce the potential for 
build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 
12-inch wide zone of permeable material, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, 
No. 67 gradation), placed directly around a perforated pipe (perforations facing down) 
at the base of the retaining wall and along the length of the wall. The perforated pipe 
should discharge to an appropriate outlet or weepholes.

consist of relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for significant water 
infiltration into the subsurface. In addition, the upper 12 inches of the retaining wall 
backfill should consist of relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for 
significant water infiltration behind the retaining structure unless covered by 
concrete slabs at the surface.

3.4.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. 
Loose and/or soft soils, where encountered, should be over-excavated to expose 
firm and/or dense materials, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted fills. The excavated loose and/or soft soils should be disposed of 
properly off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate. 
After clearing and grubbing, the cut subgrades and areas to be filled should be 
scarified to a depth of about 8 inches (where possible), moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. The compaction requirement should be increased to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction under areas subjected to vehicular 
traffic. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as 
a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to 
the maximum dry density. 
Where scarification is not practicable, such as in areas exposing dense basalt 
formation, subgrade materials should be proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton 
vibratory drum roller for a minimum of eight passes. The vibratory drum roller also 
should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. 
3.4.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, we recommend fill materials required to raise grades for the project 
consist of structural fill materials. Structural fill materials should consist of 
well-graded granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension 
with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass. In 
addition, structural fill materials should contain less than 30 percent of particles 
passing the No. 200 sieve by weight.

In general, the excavated on-site materials may be reused as a source of structural 
fill materials, provided they are processed to meet the maximum particle size and 
gradation requirements presented above. We envision that excavated materials 
consisting of boulders and native basalt formation will need to be processed prior 
to their use as structural fill materials. 
The maximum particle size of the fills and backfills should be limited to a maximum 
of 3 inches for fills and backfills to be placed in confined locations. Confined locations 
are defined as areas where the compaction equipment is limited in size to less than 
2 tons in static weight. In addition, if the contractor elects to use smaller-sized 
compaction equipment (less than 2 tons in static weight), the maximum particle size 
of the fill and backfill materials should be limited to 3 inches in dimension. 
Select granular fill should be non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coral 
or basalt. The select granular fill should be well-graded from coarse to fine with 
particles no larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension. The material should have 
a California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 
percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. The material should 
also contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve.  
Aggregate base and subbase course materials required for the project should 
consist of crushed basaltic aggregates and should conform to Sections 31 and 30 
of the County of Hawaii Standard Specifications (September 1986). 
Imported fill materials should be free of organics and deleterious materials and 
should be suitable for the intended use. In addition, imported fill materials should 
be observed and/or tested by Geolabs prior to being transported to the site for the 
intended use. 
3.4.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to 
at least 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the

same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other 
types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of rocks, 
boulders, and/or cobbles. Instead, a testing program to evaluate the number of 
passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be 
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a

may be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction.  
For preliminary estimating purposes, compaction may be accomplished by using 
a large vibratory drum roller having a static weight of at least 10 tons and traveling 
at a speed of about 300 feet per minute (about 3½ miles per hour). The roller 
should pass over the fill surface at least eight times for structural fill. 
Select granular fill materials required for the project should be placed in level lifts 
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. The compaction requirement for the last lift of fill (finished subgrade) 
below areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be increased to at least 95 percent 
relative compaction. Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, 
vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water 
tamping, jetting, or ponding should not be allowed to compact the fills.

We envision that new underground utility lines will be required for the project 
development. We anticipate most of the trenches for the utility lines will be excavated in 
the beach sands, compacted fill encountered in our boring or basalt formation. 
In general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open-graded gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) should be provided below the pipes for uniform bearing 
support. Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33,

formation and result in a potential loss of bearing strength for footings in its vicinity. 
3.6 
Drainage 
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away 
from the foundations and slabs and reduce the potential for ponding. In addition, it is 
advised to install roof gutter systems around the buildings and divert the discharge away 
from the foundation and slab areas. Excessive landscape watering near the foundations 
and slabs also should be avoided. Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have 
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration into 
the subsurface.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling one boring, 
designated as Boring No. 101, extending to a depth of about 21.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The boring was drilled with a portable drill rig equipped with continuous flight 
augers and coring tools. The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
