INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Shioda Residence in the Maili area in the District of Waianae
on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings based on the field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed residence These recommendations are intended for the
design of site grading house foundations retaining walls underground utilities and
pavements for the subject project only The findings and recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located at 87 587 and Highway in the Maili
area in the District of Waianae on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The subject property is
rectangular in shape with a total area of approximately 58 439 square feet and is
presently vacant with little vegetation Based on the information provided the project
involves the construction of a main house with a garage a swimming pool and a
separate guesthouse We anticipate that major site grading will not be required to
achieve the design grades and that the proposed structures will be slab on grade with
spread footings to support column loadings

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
GEOLABS INC
W O 4319 00 Page 4
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau
referred to as the Schofield Plateau Both Waianae and Koolau Ranges have lost most
of their original shield outlines and are now long narrow ridges shaped largely by erosion
The Waianae Range is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and pyroclastic
material which are grouped and classified as the Waianae Volcanic Series The
Waianae Volcanic Series are divided into lower middle and upper volcanic members
The lower member comprises lava flows and associated pyroclastic rocks that built the
main mass of the Waianae Shield Volcano The middle member consists of rock that
accumulated and gradually filled the vast volcanic caldera The upper member is a
relatively thin capping layer which covered the entire top of the shield volcano late in its
history of evolution The northern side of Makaha Valley comprises the northern wall of
the vast caldera which extends southeastward to Lualualei Valley The lava rock layers
forming the caldera walls are nearly horizontal in deposition as visible in the ridgeline
behind the subject site
Once the Waianae Shield Volcano formed a long period of deep erosion
sedimentation and subsidence of Oahu occurred which produced the large valleys on
the western side of the shield volcano These erosional valleys were gradually filled with
enormous accumulations of alluvium and colluvium deposited as a combined result of
stream erosion base level rise sea level rise and subsidence of the island mass Over
time and as a result of sea level fluctuation some of the older alluvial and colluvial
deposits were buried and consolidated by cementation to form relatively indurated
deposits

The project site is located at 87 587 and Highway in the Maili

designated as Boring Nos 1 through 7 extending to depths of about 6 5 to 21 feet below

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions at the project site
generally consist of silt and sand with gravel extending from the surface to a depth of
approximately 21 feet below the existing ground surface the maximum depth drilled The
sand soil was generally loose to medium dense near the surface to a depth of about
10 feet below the existing ground and graded to very dense from 10 feet to the depths
drilled
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site slab on grade
with spread and or wall footings may be used to support the houses An allowable
bearing pressure of up to may be used for the design of house foundations
Foundations should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the outside finished
grade in order to reduce the potential for erosion of the sand under the footings
Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Previous house foundations vegetation
debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed
of properly off site
The ground surface should then be moisture conditioned proof rolled and
compacted with a heavy vibratory roller in an attempt to densify the near surface
sand soils prior to house construction In areas to receive fill the subgrade should
be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacted to at least
95 percent relative compaction The excavated materials may be reused as a
source of fill materials provided that the materials meet the gradation requirements
presented in the Fills and Backfills subsection of this report Loose and or soft
spots encountered at the excavated subgrade levels should be removed and
replaced with granular fill materials compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
Fills and Backfills
In general we believe that the excavated on site soils may be reused as a source
of general fill material at the site provided that the materials are free of vegetation
deleterious materials clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in
maximum dimension In addition the fill material should be moisture conditioned
and compacted as recommended in the following subsection entitled Fill
Placement and Compaction Requirements
Imported material should be well graded granular material with no particles greater
than 3 inches in largest dimension with less than 25 percent passing the No 200
GEOLABS INC

suitability prior to being transported to the site
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches
in loose thickness moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Where fills are placed under pavements the upper 3 feet of fill below the
pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
House Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we
recommend that shallow spread or wall foundations be used to support the new
residence An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for the design
of footings bearing on the soils This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces Foundations should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the outside
finished grade in order to reduce the potential for erosion of the sand under the footings
Where foundations are located next to retaining structures utility trenches and or
easements they should be embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the retaining structure
foundations utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to reduce the potential of surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement

If soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing
excavations they should be over excavated to expose firm materials The
over excavation should either be backfilled with granular material compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom should be extended
down to underlying competent material Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with procedures described in ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91

of inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 4 may be used for footings bearing on soils Resistance due to
passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soil around
footings is well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive

diverted away from foundations

Swimming Pool
A new swimming pool is planned at the project site In general we believe that the
swimming pool walls and foundations may be designed in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Retaining Structures section of this report Since the
swimming pool will be a water retention structure we envision that the foundations for the
pool will consist of a mat foundation Based on our field exploration the bottom of the
swimming pool will encounter the sand deposits The sandy subgrade soils should be
thoroughly moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the buildings and the
pool deck across project site In general we recommend that a cushion fill layer
consisting of 4 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be provided
below the slab for more uniform support The open graded gravel cushion fill would also
serve as a Capillary break A moisture vapor membrane should be incorporated above
the cushion fill to reduce the potential for damage to moisture sensitive floor coverings by
moisture infiltration
The subgrade soils beneath the slab areas should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction prior to placement of the cushion fill material Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
Exterior concrete flatwork and walkways that are planned for the proposed
project may be supported directly on the compacted subgrade or on cushion fill material

Drainage
Retaining structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 12 inch wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations down at the base of the
retaining structures discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an
alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain
may be used instead of the drainage material The prefabricated drainage
GEOLABS INC
W O 4319 00 Page 14
product should also be hydraulically connected to a perforated pipe at the base
of the retaining structure
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of well graded
granular fill material less than 3 inches in maximum dimension Unless covered by
concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively
impervious material to reduce the potential for water infiltration behind the retaining
structure
Considering the relatively permeable nature of the sand and silt we believe that
the build up of hydrostatic pressures behind the swimming pool walls should be
negligible without a drainage system Therefore the typical drainage system
behind retaining structures may be omitted for the swimming pool walls only

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
seven borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 7 to depths of approximately 6 5 to
21 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 7 of
Appendix A
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 tests and unit weight determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Two sieve analyses were performed to provide information on the particle size
distribution of the soils encountered First a representative soil sample was oven dried
and then wet sieved through a No 200 sieve with running water to determine fine
percentage of the sample After oven drying the sample was sieved through a set of
sieves to evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification The tests
were performed in accordance with ASTM Test Designations C 117 and C 136 Graphic
presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B 1
Two California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on two
bulk samples of near surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement
subgrade support Results of the CBR test are presented on Plates B 2 and B 3
Four one inch ring swell tests were performed on one relatively undisturbed
sample and three remoided samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils under
surcharge loads The test on the relatively undisturbed sample was run without air drying
while the tests on the remolded samples were run on air dried samples Results of these
swell tests are presented on Plate B 4
