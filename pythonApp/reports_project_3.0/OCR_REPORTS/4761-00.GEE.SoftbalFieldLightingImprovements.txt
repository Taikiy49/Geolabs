2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano became
extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and it was partially buried below Koolau lavas
banking against its eastern flank
The project site is located on the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano adjacent to
Kalihi Stream The subsurface conditions at the project site are composed of terrigenous
alluvial soils from Kalihi Stream deposited by continuous erosion of the volcanic dome
The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt Land developments in
this century and placement of man made fills have brought the project site to its present
form
2 2 Site Description
The project site is located within the existing Kalihi Valley District Park off
Kamehameha IV Road in the Kalihi area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The
park is bordered by Kaewai Elementary School along the north Kalihi Stream along the
east Dole Intermediate School along the south and Kamehameha IV Road along the
west
Based on our field observations and the preliminary plan provided the majority of
the park site is relatively level with grassed baseball and softball playing fields The
existing ground elevations at the park generally range from about 153 feet Mean Sea
Level MSL near the backstop area at the north to about 144 feet MSL at the south
The boundary along the southeast side of the park site is sloping down toward
Kalihni Stream at a slope gradient ranging from about 1H 1V one horizontal to one

3 1 Drilled Shaft Foundations
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances the proposed
new light pole structures may be supported by a foundation system consisting of
cast in place concrete drilled shafts Based on the base plate dimensions for the new light
pole structures we believe that a minimum diameter of 48 inches should be used for the
proposed drilled shaft foundations Based on the anticipated structural loads and the
subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we recommend that drilled shaft
foundations with an embedment length of not less than 13 feet below the design finished
grades be used for the new light pole structures located on level ground conditions Some
of the drilled shafts at the site will likely encounter basalt rock and or cobbles and boulders
within the depths of the drilled shafts Where rock conditions are encountered in the drilled
shaft foundations the lengths of the drilled shafts may be reduced slightly to account for
the better subsurface conditions As a minimum the drilled shaft foundations should be
embedded at least 5 feet into the basalt formation Determination of the reduced length of
the drilled shaft lengths where appropriate should be made by a representative from
Geolabs in the field during construction
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally
from skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable compressive
load capacity of up to 20 kips per shaft may be used for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces
Lateral loads imposed on the light pole foundations generally may be resisted by
resistance of the shaft soil system to deflect Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is
a function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable
deflection at the top of the shaft and induced moment in the shaft Based on the

3 2 Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the light pole foundations because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations
However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the
following conditions would have an impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft
foundations
Dense Boulders and Hard Rock Formation
Based on our field exploration the proposed new light pole locations are generally
underlain by saprolitic materials consisting of stiff to very stiff clayey soils
throughout the majority of the depths explored However dense boulders and hard
basalt rock formation were encountered at greater depths across the site in some of
the borings drilled Therefore coring into potentially dense boulders and hard rock
formation should be anticipated during the drilled shaft construction Appropriate
drilling equipment and coring tools should be utilized by the drilled shaft contractor
to install the drilled shafts to the depths and dimensions recommended herein
Workmanship
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper

3 3 Fence Line Options
Based on the information provided we understand that two versions of the fence
line details are proposed along the top bank of Kalihi Steam for the above referenced
project The two versions include the following 1 an integral curb gutter and fence
footing and 2 a gutter with a CRM wall
Our field observations of the site and the available information suggest that the
existing slope has exhibited signs of creep movement of the underlying adobe clay soils
Therefore we believe that new improvements constructed along the top bank of the
existing slope will be subjected to the effects of the creeping soils unless special measures
are implemented during the design and construction of the fence line improvements
If the gutter and CRM wall version is selected for the project it is our opinion that
the foundation for the CRM wall should extend through the adobe clay soils and embeded
into the underlying basalt rock formation in order to reduce the potential for appreciable
future movement and distress to the wall structure Based on the available soil boring
data excavation on the order of about 8 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface will
be required for the CRM wall construction in order to extend the CRM wall through the
adobe clay soils Due to the significant depth of the excavation required we anticipate
that it would not be practical or cost effective to embed the CRM wall through the adobe

clay soils Therefore we envision that the CRM wall foundation will likely bear on the
near surface adobe clay soils instead of being socketed into the underlying basalt rock
formation As a result it should be anticipated that the wall structure bearing on the

creeping movement of the near surface adobe soils
Based on the subsurface conditions at the site it is our opinion that similar

significantly reduced by supporting the fence posts on drilled shafts extending into the
underlying basalt rock formation
3 4 Utility Trench
We envision that new electrical duct lines will be required to service the proposed
new lighting system In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or
S4C is recommended under the electrical duct lines Free draining granular materials
such as clean sand or S4C should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about
6 inches above the duct lines to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce
the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It
is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper backfill material and placement procedures could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the duct lines
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site soils or imported select
granular fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 extending to depths of about 15 to
21 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools The
approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials

Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock formation and or boulder materials encountered at the
site were obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination
and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would
be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its

high RQD value would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous
methods of excavation
