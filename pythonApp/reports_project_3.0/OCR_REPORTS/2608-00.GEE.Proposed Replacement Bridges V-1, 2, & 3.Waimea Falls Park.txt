INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the three 3 proposed replacement bridges across a stream in Waimea
Falls Park in Waimea on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general overall location of the
project site is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from analyses of the data obtained
for the subject project These recommendations are intended for design input and are
not intended for construction bidding or estimation purposes
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface
conditions at the three 3 replacement bridge sites in order to develop a generalized
soil rock data set for the formulation of our geotechnical engineering recommendations
pertinent to the site grading and foundation design of the proposed bridges

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to build three 3 new concrete bridges Nos V 1 V 2 and V 3 at
higher elevations to replace three 3 existing bridges which were flooded and damaged
during a recent heavy rain storm in the park
Bridge No V 1 will be relocated approximately 20 feet downstream from the
existing bridge Bridge No V 2 will be built at the same location as the existing bridge
Bridge No V 3 will be relocated approximately 30 feet upstream from the existing bridge
to accommodate grade and road alignment considerations
The abutments of the new bridges will be set further back from the stream than the
existing ones and raised above the existing ground by embankment fill
Based on the current information Bridge Nos V 1 and V 2 will have a span of
approximately 40 feet and Bridge No V 3 will have a span of approximately 70 feet The
typical bridge section will consist of concrete decks over precast prestressed concrete
girders The bridge decks will be approximately 19 feet wide
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2608 00 Page 5
Passenger vehicle traffic as well as truck loads are anticipated for the proposed
bridges It is our understanding that the bridge decks and traffic may generate a dead
and live design loading of up to 20 kips per foot of width of the deck
GENERAL SITE CONDITIONS
Waimea Falls Park is a major tourist attraction located in a valley on the southern
side of Kamehameha Highway opposite Waimea Beach Park in Waimea at the north
shore of Oahu Hawaii During a recent heavy rain storm the park was flooded and
suffered extensive damage
The park is divided by a stream originating from Waimea Falls and flowing through
the valley into the ocean The three 3 existing bridges Bridge Nos V 1 through V 3
are an essential part of the road system in the park and span over the stream at various
locations Bridges V 1 and V 2 are located downstream from Bridge V 3 which is located
more deeply in the valley
It is Our understanding that the existing bridges were submerged several feet below
the high flood level during the heavy rain storm As observed during our field exploration
the bridge pavements or deck surfaces were rather muddy and appeared to be damaged
Abundant boulders were observed along the stream and at the bridge sites The
existing ground at the proposed replacement bridge abutment locations is generally level
Based on the available topographic information the existing Bridge No V 1 is at
approximately 36 feet above Mean Sea Level MSL The existing Bridge No V 2 is at
approximately 88 feet MSL and Bridge No V 3 is at approximately 96 feet MSL

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsoil conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling a total of
six 6 borings for the three 8 replacement bridge sites to depths of 28 0 to 52 5 feet
below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans
Plates 2 through 4
Subsoil Conditions
The borings encountered a generally dense crust of cobbles gravel and boulders
embedded in a soil matrix of silty sand clayey silt or clay at the bridge sites The
boulders encountered generally are unweathered to slightly weathered and ranged from
small sizes to as large as 4 5 feet in diameter
A weathered basalt formation was encountered at approximately 15 5 to 29 5 feet
below the existing ground surface to the maximum drilled depths in the borings The
degree of weathering varies with depth and between the bridge sites
At the sites of Bridge Nos V 1 and V 2 the basalt formation encountered was
generally highly to extremely weathered mostly in a form resembling saprolite and may
be classified as soft rock Softer seams of completely weathered zones were also
encountered in the soft rock formation The basalt formation encountered was slightly
to highly weathered at the site of the proposed replacement Bridge No V 3 which is
inside the valley
Groundwater
Groundwater was encountered at shallow depths in three 3 of the six 6 borings
drilled next to the stream During our field exploration groundwater level was measured
at approximately 1 5 to 7 3 feet below the existing ground surface at the bridge sites

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
It is proposed to build the replacement bridges above flood level by raising the
bridge approaches with new embankment fill It is our understanding that the highest
flood level noted during the recent heavy rainstorm reached several feet above the
existing bridge decks
It is required that the bridge foundations be embedded adequately below the
potential scour depth caused by the stream during flood flows Due to the limited
available data regarding the characteristics of the stream the potential maximum scour
depth cannot be accurately determined at this time However based on the very limited
scouring of the stream noted after the recent storm it appears that the potential scour
depth may be relatively shallow
Based on these design considerations it is our opinion that spread footing
foundations may be used to support the new bridges provided it can be assumed that
the maximum scour depth is less than a few feet so that the footings can be embedded
adequately below the existing ground surface
An alternate foundation system utilizing piles or piers bearing on the underlying
hard basalt rock was also considered to minimize the potential effect of scouring on the
bridge foundations during flood flows However serious construction difficulties and
excessive costs are anticipated if deep foundations were to be used to support the new
bridges This is due to the upper bouldery subsoils which the piles and the piers would
have to penetrate

Footing Foundations
It is our understanding that the new bridges will have a span of about 40 feet for
Bridge Nos V 1 and V 2 and 70 feet for Bridge No V 3 A design loading up to about
20 kips per foot width of the bridge decks was assumed
Based on the design loading we believe that the new bridges may be supported
by spread footing foundations bearing on the in underneath the
new embankment fill We recommend that the new bridge footings be set back a
minimum distance of 10 feet from the stream and embedded a minimum of three 8 feet
below the existing ground surface
it is also recommended that the footing subgrade in bouldery soils be over
excavated by a minimum of two 2 feet and backfilled with compacted select granular
material such as select borrow to provide a more uniform subgrade support The select
borrow should be compacted to a minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density as
determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Provided the footing subgrade is properly compacted as recommended above we
recommend that an allowable bearing capacity of 5000 p s f be used for the footing
design This bearing value is for total dead and live loads only and may be increased by
up to one third 1 3 for consideration of short term loadings including seismic and wind
loads
It is estimated that footing settlements under the design loading will be on the order
Resistance to lateral loads acting on the new piers and spread footings may be
provided by passive earth pressure and friction developed against the structures We
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 2608 00 Page 9
recommend that an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 p s f be used to determine the
passive earth pressure developed against the vertical faces of the embedded piers and
spread footings A coefficient of friction of 0 4 may be used to calculate the sliding friction
developed between the concrete footings and the compacted footing subgrade
Deep Foundations
Various deep foundation systems were considered to support the new bridges
each with its own cost and construction difficulties The deep foundation systems
evaluated include driving precast prestressed concrete piles or cast in place concrete
piers or caissons in predrilled holes
Serious construction difficulties and excessive costs are expected in predrilling and
keeping the holes open in the bouldery subsoils down to the bearing stratum of hard
basalt rock formation It appears that conventional caisson drilling equipment could be
used to remove the cobbles and smaller boulders at a slow advancement rate However
very large boulders up to two thirds of the shaft diameter of the predrilled hole may
require removal with special tools such as boulder bits and possibly some manual effort
The water table as encountered in our field exploration would also hinder the
progress of predrilling and require tremie concrete placement for cast in place piers or
caissons Due to this high groundwater table the use of steel casing to prevent a
predrilled hole from caving in also seems inevitable
Therefore if a deep foundation system is selected the owner is urged to retain a
specialty contractor who should work together with the project geotechnical engineer to
devise a feasible and economical solution

Site Grading
Besides the embankment fill required at the new bridge abutments minimal cut and
fill are anticipated for the project site grading
Ground Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the project grading limits should be cleared
and grubbed of all vegetation rubbish debris and existing structures which should
be properly disposed of off site
After clearing and grubbing the ground to receive fill should be scarified to a
minimum of six 6 inches below the cleared level and recompacted to a minimum
of 90 of its maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557
78
Any local soft or loose spots and or unsuitable materials encountered at the
scarified level should be removed and replaced with properly compacted select
granular fill such as select borrow The backfill should be placed in maximum
eight 8 inch loose lifts and compacted to obtain a minimum of 90 of its
maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
lf large boulders are encountered protruding above the scarified level they should
be removed and also replaced with select borrow placed and compacted in the
same manner as recommended in the preceding paragraph
Any sloping ground steeper than five horizontal to one vertical 5H 1V to receive
fill should be properly keyed and benched prior to filling as shown on Plate 5
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2608 00 Page 11
Embankment Fill
An abundant supply of granular subsoils of different sizes ranging from gravel
cobbles and boulders is available in the park However these subsoils are not
recommended for use in the new embankment fill unless crushed down to six 6
inches or smaller in size and properly mixed to provide a uniformly graded product
To provide a uniform support to the new pavements behind the new abutment
walls we recommend that granular material such as select borrow be used as the
new embankment fill The embankment fill should be placed in loose horizontal
lifts not thicker than 12 inches and compacted to a minimum of 90 of its
maximum dry density determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Embankment and cut slopes should not be steeper than two horizontal to one
vertical 2H 1V
Abutment Walls
The bridge abutment walls should be designed as retaining walls to support the
granular embankment fill behind Assuming level backfill and restrained wall top
conditions we recommend that a lateral earth pressure of 50 p c f equivalent fluid
pressure be used to design the new abutment walls
Additional loads due to traffic seismic and or other loading considerations should
be included as additional lateral earth pressures on the abutment walls for design where
appropriate
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2608 00 Page 12
A subsurface drainage system should be used to prevent the build up of
hydrostatic pressure behind the retaining walls Subdrains should be properly
constructed to drain water away from the walls
Design Review
Plans and specifications for the proposed construction should be forwarded to the
project geotechnical engineer for review and written comments prior to construction This
review is needed to determine adherence to the recommendations given herein If this
review is not made the geotechnical engineer cannot assume responsibility for
misinterpretation of the recommendations
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the project geotechnical engineer be retained to provide
geotechnical engineering services during construction of the foundation phases of the
work This is to observe compliance with the design concepts specifications or
recommendations and to allow design changes in the event that subsurface conditions
differ from that anticipated prior to start of construction The recommendations given in
this report are contingent upon such observations
If actual exposed rock soil conditions encountered during construction are different
from those assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made
