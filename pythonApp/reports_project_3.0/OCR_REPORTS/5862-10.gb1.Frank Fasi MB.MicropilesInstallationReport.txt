Dear Mr. Lee: 
This letter report summarizes our geotechnical engineering services performed 
during the micropile installation for the Frank F. Fasi Municipal Building, Emergency 
Generator System Upgrade project in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
In general, this letter report presents our observations performed on the micropiles 
for the project from March 15 to March 18, 2010. Ralph S. Inouye Co., Ltd. was the 
general contractor for the project, and Pacific Ground Systems, Inc. was the specialty 
subcontractor responsible for the micropile installation. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The site is at the southeast corner of the Frank F. Fasi Municipal Building in the 
Honolulu area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The Frank F. Fasi Municipal Building is at 
the intersection of South King Street and Alapai Street.  We understand that a new 
one-story generator building is proposed at the site. 
We understand that portions of the generator building will be supported on 
nine 40-kip capacity micropiles.  In addition, one generator pad will be supported on four 
15-kip capacity micropiles.   
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering services during construction of the 
project was to observe the installation and testing of the micropiles for general 
conformance with the project plans and specifications.

MICROPILE INSTALLATION AND TESTING 
Personnel from Pacific Ground Systems, Inc. installed the 13 micropiles for the 
project. The approximate locations of the 13 micropiles are shown on the Micropile 
Identification Plan, Plate 2. Installation of the micropiles for the project progressed from 
March 15 to March 18, 2010. 
Micropile Installation 
Geolabs representatives were present to observe the micropile installation.  The 
micropiles installed at the site consisted of Titan 40/16 micropiles. The Titan micropiles 
system consists of hollow, steel rods that are continuously advanced into the ground.  
These drill rods are abandoned in the hole and act as the steel reinforcing for the 
micropile.  The micropiles were drilled using a track excavator-mounted rotary percussion 
drill rig.  Drilling fluid consisting of a cement grout mixture (1 cement bag per 8 gallons of 
water) flushes soils cuttings from the hole and creates a grout bulb.  After reaching the 
micropile tip elevation, a thicker cement grout mixture (1 bag per 5 gallons of water) is 
pumped into the drilled hole. The drilling of the hole, installation of the reinforcing member, 
and grouting of the micropiles occurs in a single operation. 
In general, the 15-kip capacity micropiles were drilled to depths of about 20.5 to 
22.8 feet below the existing ground surface.  The 40-kip capacity micropiles were drilled to 
depths of about 37.4 to 37.9 feet below the existing ground surface. The bond lengths of 
the 15-kip and 40-kip capacity micropiles ranged from about 15.5 to 17.8 feet and about 
32.7 to 35 feet, respectively. A summary of the micropile lengths is presented on Plate 3. 
It should be noted that the plan location of MP-6 was over an existing sewer line.  
MP-6 was eliminated and replaced with two micropiles, MP-6A and MP-6B.  These 
micropiles were located on each side of the existing sewer line.  
Based on the observations of our field representatives and the confirmation proof 
tests, we believe the 13 micropiles have been installed in general conformance with the 
project plans and specifications and within the limits of generally accepted construction 
practices. 
Micropile Testing 
In general, the production micropiles that were proof tested met the acceptance 
criteria specified in the project specifications. Based on our observations of the micropile 
proof tests performed by the subcontractor, it is our professional opinion that the 
micropiles have been installed to provide the design load with tolerable movements. 
LIMITATIONS 
It should be noted that it is not possible to continuously observe all activities 
occurring simultaneously on a construction site; therefore, some of the work for the 
micropile installation and testing may have occurred without our direct observation.
