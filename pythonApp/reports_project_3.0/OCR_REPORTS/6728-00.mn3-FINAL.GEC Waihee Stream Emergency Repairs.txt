SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our site observations and field exploration, the project site is generally 
underlain by medium dense to dense, friable alluvial deposits comprising of sandy 
gravel with some silt and cobbles. The abrupt 90-degree bend of the stream makes the 
bank along the turn particularly vulnerable to erosion especially during high intensity 
rainfall events when the stream swells and becomes turbulent. Therefore, special 
channel protection is necessary to limit scouring and erosion of the stream bank that is 
in direct contact with the stream flow at the project site.  
A 40-foot section of the existing CRM-lined slope along the north bank of the 
stream next to the spillway was undermined by erosion resulting in its collapse. The 
upper bank slipped by an estimated 3 to 4 feet from the existing fence line. The CRM 
covering the slope within the slide zone was practically reduced into rubble due to loss 
of support. On the opposite side of the channel, the erosive force of the cascading 
stream undermined and demolished the downstream portion of the existing CRM wing 
wall and consequently washed away the unprotected fill behind it.  
Remedial work should consist of re-establishing foundation support of the GRP 
slope protection below the projected stream scour. However, determination of the future 
scour level is very difficult to predict. Therefore, we recommend stabilizing the stream 
bottom with GRP to reduce future scour. Once the stream bottom is stabilized, CRM 
walls or tieback anchors may be used to restore the slope. 
Detailed discussion of these items and our engineering design recommendations 
are presented in the following sections of this report.

Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Waihee Stream Emergency Repairs in Kahaluu on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations derived

The recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of emergency 
erosion control measures for the project only. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located east of the crossing of Waihee Stream under 
Ahilama Road, approximately 980 feet south of Waihee Road. After crossing under 
Ahilama Road, the stream makes a 90 degree turn to the south adjacent to an existing 
residence. The existing residence is about 13 feet above the stream level. The stream 
bank adjacent to this residence is lined with grouted rubble paving (GRP) also referred 
to as CRM in the plans. The abrupt turn in the stream concentrates stream flow against 
the outer bank along the bend contributing to the continued erosion at the toe of the 
GRP protected slope.  
Based on our site observations, previous attempts have been made to protect 
the toe of the slope with the installation of concrete cutoff walls. However the stream 
continues to scour and undermine this cutoff wall causing destabilization of the 
GRP-covered slope. Current conditions indicate a 40-foot section of the CRM protected 
slope next to the spillway had collapsed apparently due to loss of support after the 
cutoff wall along its toe was undermined. 
We understand that the purpose of this project is to design stabilization 
measures to resist scour and restore support to the GRP covered slope. It is equally 
important to control the erosive potential of the cascading stream at the spillway.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct 
while Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau 
lavas banking against its eastern flank.  These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad 
plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosion processes are dominated by 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls, which are then transported 
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once these 
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become 
dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. In general, 
stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the stream flows transmit 
large volumes of water for very short durations.  Because of this, transport of sediments 
is intermittent, and the bulk of the stream's hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted 
mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines.  When the erosion base levels 
change, these sediment loads are left as deposits. 
When deposits are left in-place for long periods of time, chemical processes 
begin to alter the materials, simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the 
material. Chemical processes also cause induration, or cementation, of the 
coarse-grained portion of the sediment into a poorly-consolidated sedimentary rock, or 
conglomerate. Simultaneously, erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors 
and upstream in headwaters.  This continued erosion generates material which is 
transported downslope covering the older alluvial deposits.  Depending on the local 
base level and rate of transport, these new sediments are generally transient in terms of 
geologic time. In addition, their consistency and density are generally less than those of 
older, partially consolidated deposits.

The project site is situated near the base of the Koolau Pali, a high cliff that 
extends along most of the windward side of the Koolau Mountain Range on the Island of 
Oahu.  The climate along the Pali is typified by a high to very high average annual 
rainfall.  Following the main shield building stage of the Koolau volcano in the late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene Epoch, stream erosion, landslide and deep weathering of 
the basalt rock became the dominant agents affecting the morphology of the area. 
The project site is generally composed of alluvial soil derived from large-scale 
erosion and landslide along the base of the Koolau forming colluvial aprons that were 
subsequently transported by runoff to create the alluvial fans generally forming the 
present day topography of the region.

Existing Site Conditions 
The project site encompasses about a 120-foot section of the Waihee Stream 
channel between Ahilama Road and the residential property described under 
TMK: 4-7-12: 3 (47-551 Ahilama Road). Just after crossing under Ahilama Road, the

existing residence is about 13 feet above the stream and is about 5 feet from the crest

A 40-foot section of the GRP covered slope next to the spillway collapsed

dislocated wire fence that now sits about 4 feet below its original elevation and about

Our field exploration generally encountered surface fill materials of sandy gravel

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Undermining of the soil under the slope lining by stream scour has resulted in the 
failure of about 40 feet of the slope protection and is jeopardizing the remaining lining 
adjacent to the existing residence. The stream scour is due to the turbulence at the 
spillway at the outlet of the culvert under Ahilama Road and the 90 degree turn that the 
stream makes in front of the existing residence. The sloping bank in front of the 
residence was lined with GRP to help resist the erosive forces of the stream. However, 
over time the stream scour undermined the slope protection and required remedial work 
to resist the scour. The remedial work consisted of the installation of precast concrete 
panels that extended below the GRP lining. Continued scour by the stream resulted in 
the undermining of the concrete panels, and the resulting instability. 
Generally, remedial work should consist of re-establishing foundation support of 
the GRP slope protection below the projected stream scour level. However, 
determination of the future scour level is very difficult to predict. Additionally, excavation 
below the existing GRP slope protection to install new foundation supports could 
de-stabilize the slope and cause damage to the existing residence. Therefore, methods 
to stabilize the existing stream bottom to resist further scour may be more effective for 
this project. Once the stream bottom is stabilized, more conventional repair options 
such as CRM retaining walls and GRP may be used to restore the slope protection. 
Additionally, tieback anchors may be used to stabilize the existing concrete cutoff walls. 
3.1 
Stream Bed Scour Protection 
We believe that protecting the stream bed against scour is very important to 
provide long term protection for the slopes from being undermined. Erosion at the 
project site is due to the force of the water that comes into contact with the unprotected 
stream bed. As a means to reduce erosion at the stream bed adjacent to the cutoff walls 
in contact with the stream flow, we recommend placing GRP along the stream bed to 
form a continuous armor layer. The stream stabilization should extend from the toe of 
the slope to a minimum of 20 feet across the stream bed. (See Plate 3.)

The 40-foot section of collapsed GRP should be reconstructed and tied to the 
new stream bed GRP protection layer. The damaged concrete cutoff walls in front of the 
existing residence should be re-stabilized by installing tieback anchors into the slope. 
This system is advantageous because it greatly reduces the risk of destabilizing the 
existing slope and residence since further excavation is not required. However, the 
tieback anchors extend into private property and require an easement. 
In general, the permanent tieback anchor system is constructed by drilling a 
borehole, installing a steel tendon or reinforcing bar into the borehole, and grouting the 
borehole.  The basic components of a grouted permanent tieback anchor consist of the 
anchorage and the bonded and unbonded lengths of the steel tendon or reinforcing bar.  
In general, the unbonded length of the tieback anchor is the portion of the steel tendon 
(or reinforcing bar) that does not have direct contact with the grout, allowing the tendon 
(or reinforcing bar) to elongate elastically and transfer the resisting force from the 
structure to the bonded length.  The bonded length of the tieback anchor is the portion 
of the steel tendon (or reinforcing bar) that is directly bonded to the grout allowing it to 
transfer the applied load into the ground.  
We envision that the permanent tieback anchors would derive support principally 
from the bond between the grout and the alluvium anticipated at the project site.  The 
bond stress, which is the pullout resistance per unit area of the grout/soil interface 
contact, is variable depending on the type of soil and grout, the overburden stress, and 
the construction procedures used in installing the permanent tieback anchors.  An 
average bond stress of 1,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the 
alluvium. The bond stress used in the design will need to be confirmed during 
construction by load testing.  The following guidelines may be used in designing the 
permanent tieback system for this project.

installation of the tieback anchors during construction.  Construction of the tieback 
anchors should be performed by a specialty contractor experienced in the 
construction of permanent tieback anchors.  In our opinion, the following may have 
a significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the tieback anchors. 
Based on the subsurface conditions in the area, drilling of the tieback anchor holes

of the tieback anchors.  A neat-cement grout or sand-cement grout mix should be

of the hole.  The grout should be placed through the tremie pipe by pressure 
methods.  The grouting operations should be conducted promptly (within 8 hours) 
after drilling to reduce the potential for significant caving-in of the sides of the drilled 
holes. 
As the alluvium may consist of gravel, cobbles and boulders, it is not uncommon 
that grout loss from the drilled hole exceeds a few times the volume of the annular 
space between the drilled hole and tieback.  The contractor should anticipate the 
potential grout loss and incorporate the necessary costs into the unit prices during 
the bidding process. 
3.1.2 Tieback Anchor Testing 
In general, we recommend that a creep test be performed on the first tieback 
anchor to be installed before construction of the remaining tieback anchors.  The 
creep tests should consist by subjecting the tieback anchor to at least 150 percent 
of the design loads, and the load should be held for at least 8 hours. 
Pullout tests (proof tests) should be performed on all the remaining tieback anchors 
during construction to confirm the bond stresses used in the design.  We 
recommend that all of the tieback anchors constructed be tested for pullout.  The

The values provided above assume that the on-site soils (particles less than

Backfill material shall only be placed against a masonry retaining wall after the grout 
has either reached design strength or has cured for a minimum of 28 days. Compaction 
of backfill material by jetting or ponding with water should not be used. Each layer of 
backfill must be moisture conditioned and thoroughly tamped, rolled or otherwise 
compacted until the relative compaction is not less than 90 percent. Over-compaction of 
the retaining wall backfill should be avoided.  
In general, an active condition may be used for gravity walls and walls that are 
free to deflect by as much as 0.5 percent of the wall height.  If the tops of the walls are 
not free to deflect beyond this degree, or are restrained, the walls should be designed 
for the at-rest condition.  These lateral earth pressures do not include hydrostatic 
pressures that might be caused by groundwater trapped behind the walls.  Therefore, it 
is imperative to incorporate proper drainage in the construction details of retaining walls. 
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges, line loads, and point loads within a 
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be considered in the design. 
For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall, a rectangular 
distribution with uniform pressure equal to 33 percent of the vertical surcharge pressure 
acting on the entire height of the wall, which is free to deflect (cantilever), may be used 
in design.  For walls that are restrained, a rectangular distribution equal to 50 percent of 
the vertical surcharge pressure acting over the entire height of the wall may be used for 
design.  Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the surcharge 
effects of point loads and line loads.

New retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build-up of 
hydrostatic pressures.  A typical drainage system would consist of a 
12-inch wide zone of permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), directly around a perforated pipe 
(perforations facing down) at the base of the wall discharging to an 
appropriate outlet or weep holes. Typically these weep holes consist of

3.3 
Spillway Scour Mitigation 
Based on our reconnaissance, we believe that the failed 40-foot section of 
CRM-protected slope below the spillway was undermined initiating its collapse. It 
appears that scouring brought about by the significant head difference between the 
spillway and the stream bed (on the order of up to 9 feet) generates significant erosive 
kinetic energy particularly during torrential rain events. The sustained flow developed a 
6-foot deep erosion cavity within the impact area which migrated laterally under the toe 
of the adjacent slope. The lateral migration of this erosion cavity also undermined the 
existing CRM apron of the outfall structure and demolished the lower one-half portion of 
the existing CRM wing wall. 
To inhibit the lateral spread of the scour hole under the adjacent structures, we 
recommend backfilling the hole with grouted rocks and providing a stepped grade 
downstream transition to lessen the erosive potential of the water.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling one boring, designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of 36.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface at the upper bank of the stream north of the project 
site.  The approximate boring location is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  The boring 
was drilled using a truck-mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight 
augers. 
Data from the above boring including soil observations of the exposed stream 
channel surface was used for the geotechnical analysis. 
The materials encountered in the boring were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  These classifications were further reviewed visually and by 
testing in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified 
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A.  Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Log of Boring, Plate A. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled boring in 
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving 
a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance 
with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Log of Boring at the appropriate 
sample depths.
