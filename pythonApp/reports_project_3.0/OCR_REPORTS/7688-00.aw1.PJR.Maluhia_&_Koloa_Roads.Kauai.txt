SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our analyses performed 
for the design of the new pavement for the widening of Maluhia Road and Koloa Road 
located in Koloa on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The project location and general site 
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes our findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report.  
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project involves widening Maluhia Road and Koloa Road about 4 to 6 feet on 
each side of the roadways for a paved shoulder. We understand it is desired that the 
new paved shoulders be designed for future traffic anticipated on the travel lanes. Other 
improvements for the project include new bridge railings, guardrails and end treatments, 
and a roundabout and underground box culvert at the intersection of Koloa Road and 
Ala Kalanikaumaka Street. The Maluhia and Koloa Roads are under the jurisdiction of 
the County of Kauai, Department of Public Works. 
This pavement justification report has been prepared in support of the design of 
the new pavement for the widening of Koloa and Maluhia Roads. It should be noted that 
we prepared a separate geotechnical engineering report in support of the design of 
other elements of the project.  Detailed discussion and geotechnical recommendations 
for design of the other elements of the project are presented in that Geotechnical 
Engineering Report.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano 
built by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series beginning about 
5 to 6 million years ago. The eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series ended about 
2½ million years ago and was followed by a long period of erosion. Following the 
cessation of this main volcano shield-building phase, about 1½ million years ago, there 
was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the post-erosional 
Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the thick alluvial sediments of 
the Palikea Formation. 
Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as 
thick lava flows composed of dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in 
north-south trends at various locales. Associated with the Koloa Volcanic Series lava 
flows are some deposits of pyroclastic materials (volcanic ash and cinders), which 
usually form cinder cones surrounding the vent. Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic 
Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of Kauai, including the project site. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, many sea level changes occurred as a result of 
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great continental 
glaciers accumulated, the level of the ocean fell since less water was available to fill the 
oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global sea levels rose 
because more water was available. The landmass of Kauai remained essentially stable 
during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea, which were both higher and 
lower relative to the present sea level of the Island of Kauai. 
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by extrusion of the 
lavas of the Koloa Volcanic Series. The basalt rock formation observed in the Koloa 
area appear to be representative of the pahoehoe lava flow type, which spread and 
ponded as it approached the ocean from inland areas. Pahoehoe lavas are typically 
characterized by a smoother, billowy surface and internal structure of vesicular (porous) 
rock with limited clinker materials.

In some places, in-situ chemical weathering of the Koloa lava flows has formed a 
mantle of residual and saprolitic soils. In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty 
material while residual soils are more clayey. Both residual and saprolitic soils are 
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The residual and saprolitic soils 
grade to basaltic rock formation with increased depth. Grading operations from the 
previous development brought the site to its present form.  
2.2 
Site Description 
In general, Maluhia Road and Koloa Road are two-lane roadways with grassed 
shoulders. Both roadways slope downward from their intersections with Kaumualii 
Highway (Hwy 50) toward Koloa town. The elevations along Maluhia Road range from 
about +206 to +631 feet and elevations along Koloa Road range from about +206 to 
+496 feet. All elevations in this report are referenced to Mean Sea Level (MSL) unless 
otherwise stated. The project site is shown on the Overall Site Plan (Plate 2) and 
individual Site Plans (Plates 3.1 through 3.17). 
2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling 37 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 118 (Maluhia Road) and Nos. 201 through 219 
(Koloa Road), extending to depths of about 2.6 to 6.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. In addition, 17 bulk samples of the near-surface soils were collected for 
laboratory testing to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the materials. The 
approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 
through 3.17. 
In general, our field exploration encountered 3 to 9 inches of asphaltic concrete 
pavement overlying fill and a combination of alluvium, residual soil, and saprolite. The 
fill generally consisted of 6 to 15 inches of granular material (SM & GP); however, no 
granular fill material was encountered in Boring Nos. 103 or 105. The fill was underlain 
by alluvium/residual soil/saprolite consisting primarily of soft to hard fine-grained 
materials (ML, CL, MH, & CH).  
Groundwater was encountered in Boring No. 101 at a depth of 2.5 feet below the 
pavement surface; however, we did not encounter groundwater in the other shallow

3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
Since the project consists of widening the existing flexible pavements, only 
flexible pavements were considered in the pavement analyses except for the truck 
apron for the roundabout at the intersection of Koloa Road and Ala Kalanikaumaka 
Street that will be constructed with a rigid pavement for visibility and aesthetic purposes.  
The flexible pavement sections presented herein were generally determined based on 
the methodology described in Chapter 3 of the revised Pavement Design Manual dated 
March 2002, prepared by the State of Hawaii, Department of Transportation, Highways 
Division, Material Testing and Research Branch. The pavement design methodology is 
based on the Hveem Stabilometer method developed and used by the California 
Department of Transportation (Caltrans). 
The design procedures for the rigid pavement sections are generally based on 
the design procedures described in the Portland Cement Association “Thickness Design 
for Concrete Highway and Street Pavements”.

3.4 
Rehabilitation of Existing Pavements 
In general, the type and severity of the distress exhibited in the pavement areas 
would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing pavements 
should be performed.  Existing pavements with minor distresses are generally 
rehabilitated with an AC overlay with cold-planing.  Based on our evaluation, it is our 
opinion that pavement areas with minor distresses may be rehabilitated with an AC 
overlay consisting of cold-planing 2.5 inches of the existing pavements and replacing 
with 2.5 inches of new AC overlay. 
In general, the contractor should vacuum-sweep the pavement areas 
immediately after cold-planing to remove gravel-sized materials and dust not picked up 
by the cold-planing operation.  It should be noted that the vacuum-sweep should not 
preclude the dust control operation during cold-planing. In general, the contractor 
should begin paving no later than three working days after cold-planing begins.  Finally, 
vacuum sweeping of cold-planed surfaces should also be performed immediately prior 
to paving. 
3.5 
Design Pavement Sections 
As discussed in the previous sections herein, some of the observed distress 
along the roadways are likely caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, 
and/or subgrade materials. In general, these areas should be reconstructed with a new 
pavement section in lieu of an AC overlay section. In addition, these distressed areas 
should be repaired prior to the AC overlay work for the entire roadway under 
consideration. 
Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicate that some portion of the 
roadway alignments under consideration for this project is generally underlain by 
subgrade soils which exhibit relatively high natural water content. Within the tree tunnel 
area on Maluhia Road, the natural water content of the in-situ soils is very high. 
Therefore, the installation of a stabilization layer below the pavement section is 
recommended within the tree tunnel area on Maluhia Road.

3.7 
Subgrade Preparation Below Pavement Section 
We anticipate that the majority of the new roadway pavements will be 
constructed at or near the existing grades or in a cut condition. In general, the area 
within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed at the 
on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other unsuitable 
materials should be removed and properly disposed of off-site or disposed of in a 
designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials. 
After removal of the existing AC and base layers to accommodate the new 
pavement structural sections, the exposed subgrade soils should be proof-rolled with a 
minimum 10-ton (static weight) drum roller for a minimum of eight passes to compact 
the exposed subgrade soils. The drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 
300 feet per minute.

in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage 
by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. Some drying of the subgrade 
soils should be anticipated for the project.

3.8 
Fill Materials 
In general, the excavated on-site materials with particles no larger than 3 inches 
may be reused as a source of general fill material.  In general, the on-site materials 
exhibit high natural water content, and drying should be anticipated prior to their use.  
Imported fill materials, if required, should consist of non-expansive select granular 
material, such as crushed coralline or basaltic materials. The materials should be well-
graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension 
and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve.  
The materials should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or 
more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less.  Imported fill materials 
should be tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the 
project site for the intended use and conformance to the State of Hawaii, Standard 
Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction (2005) and subsequent amendments. 
3.9 
Compaction Requirements 
In general, fill and backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  Due to the 
high natural water content of the subgrade soils, the required compaction for the 
subgrade was reduced from 95 percent relative compaction.  The aggregate base and 
subbase materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, placed in 8-inch level loose lifts, and compacted to not less than 95 percent 
relative compaction. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D2041. Asphalt concrete (AC)

3.10 Pavement Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. It should be noted that the 
clayey subgrade soils are susceptible to softening and loss of strength upon saturation. 
Therefore, special attention should be given to the surface drainage of the pavements.  
It is essential to slope the pavement surface adequately and to maintain positive 
drainage gradients at the pavement surface such that surface water may be carried off 
the pavement quickly for proper discharge. Surface water ponding should not be 
allowed on-site during or after construction.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
37 exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 118 (Maluhia Road) and 
Nos. 201 through 219 (Koloa Road), extending to depths of about 2.6 to 6.5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface. The borings were drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped 
with 4-inch OD solid-stem augers. The borings were drilled at the approximate locations 
shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.17. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-37. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance 
with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD modified 
California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Please note that the efficiencies of the safety hammers used for this project were 
measured in May 2015 and the resulting energy transfer ratios are shown on the Logs of 
Borings. The soil consistencies and densities presented on the logs of borings have been 
adjusted based on the energy transfer ratio, as appropriate.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Twenty-two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The 
test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, 
and graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plates B-1 through B-3. 
Eleven Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. The fines content of the samples tested (percent particles passing the 
No. 200 sieve) are indicated on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plates B-4 through B-6. 
Three Unconfined Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D2166) were performed 
on selected soil samples to evaluate the uniaxial compressive strength of the underlying 
soils.  The test results are presented on Plates B-7 through B-9. 
Seven Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test 
results are presented on Plates B-10 through B-16. 
Six Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557, Method A) were performed 
on the bulk soil samples to evaluate the relationship between the moisture content and 
the dry density of the near-surface soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-17 
through B-22. 
Ten California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on bulk soil 
samples to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-23 through B-32.  
Twenty laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D2844) were performed on 
selected bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-33 through B-52.
