INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Paheehee Ridge Subdivision project located in the Lualualei
area on the leeward side of the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity
of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed improvements to the subdivision project These
recommendations are intended for design input only and are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The project site is situated in Lualualei Valley along the southern base of
Paheehee Ridge a high ridge extending from Puu Paheehee on the makai side to Mauna
Kuwale on the mauka side Lualualei Valley was formed by the lower and middle
members of the Waianae Volcanic Series during the Tertiary Period Erosion has since
removed most of the western slope of the Waianae shield and exposed the internal
structure of the volcano This erosion formed the large broad Lualualei Valley which is
filled with substantial accumulations of alluvium and colluvium The older consolidated
alluvium of the western portion of the Waianae Range appears to be graded to various
elevations reflecting higher stands of the sea during the Pleistocene Epoch These
alluvial deposits are largely a result of stream deposits and to a lesser extent mud flows
Toward the mouth of the valley the non marine alluvium grades to marine deposits of
stratified clay with lenticular beds of sand and gravel
Eustatic fluctuations of sea level in the Hawaiian Islands have caused changes in
the shoreline in the past The alluvial deposits in the valley and abutting the Waianae
Range grade in part to former high sea levels and are being slowly removed by present
day stream erosion Weathering of the upper portions of the ridges has led to the
accumulation of colluvium found at their bases

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located along the existing Paheehee Road at the southern base
of Paheehee Ridge in the Lualualei area of the District of Waianae on the island of Oahu
Hawaii Based on field observations the northeastern portion from about Lot Nos 7
through 13 of the existing Paheehee Road has been recently improved with an asphalt
concrete AC overlay The remaining portion from about Lot Nos 2 through 6 of the
roadway is generally unimproved
The project site is flanked on the south by the lower slopes of Paheehee Ridge
with relatively flat terrain and surface slopes flatter than ten horizontal to one vertical
10H 1V sloping down toward the south The existing Paheehee Road is bordered to the
south by single family homes and vacant lots with sparse vegetation The northern
portion of the existing Paheehee Road is gently sloping upward at a relatively flat gradient
before rising steeply up toward the ridge The majority of the area is vacant with relatively
thick vegetation and abandoned vehicles scattered over the place The existing ground
elevations along the Paheehee Road range from about 75 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
at the southwestern terminus near Lot No 2 to about 90 feet MSL at the northeastern
end near Lot No 13

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration along a majority of the existing Paheehee Road for the
proposed improvement project generally encountered very stiff to hard clayey soils
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 21 feet below the existing
ground surface Near surface dense sandy subsoils were encountered along a short
segment of the roadway extending from Hakalina Road to the northeastern terminus of
the alignment at Lot No 13
Based on our field exploration and laboratory tests the near surface clayey soils
encountered over a majority of the roadway alignment exhibit poor pavement support
characteristics with a California Bearing Ratio CBR of about 2 6 and a swell of
8 4 percent Therefore we recommend that a pavement structural section consisting of

clayey subsoils indicated a relatively low CBR value of 2 6 and a swell of up to
8 4 percent Test borings near Lot Nos 12 and 13 encountered dense sandy soils near
the existing ground surface with a CBR value of 29 and a swell of 0 35 percent

Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the anticipated design finish grades for the
proposed roadway improvements we anticipate that minimal site grading work will be
required for the project construction In general the area within the contract grading limits
should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed at the on set of earthwork All vegetation
debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of
properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing below any areas
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm
or stiff material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted
engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of
off site
Existing structures and pavements that are to be demolished should be completely
compacted granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed
and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with granular fill material
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch level loose
lifts and compacted to a minimum of 95 relative compaction Utilities that are to be
abandoned in place under the proposed structures should be backfilled by pumping lean
concrete under low pressure
After clearing grubbing and demolition the future pavement areas should be
excavated where necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the base
course or subbase layer The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of
about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density

If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the pumping
subgrade may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for pumping
subgrade conditions As a guide one sack of cement may be used for approximately
25 square feet of subgrade area
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soils be scarified and re prepared as recommended above at no
additional cost to the Owner Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed
subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the
soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill at no additional cost to
the Owner Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for
over excavation and engineered fill placement to account for variations in the
over excavation quantities
Select borrow and base course materials required for the new pavements should
conform to the requirements stipulated in the Standard Specifications for Road Bridge
and Public Works Construction 1994 The materials should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Imported fill materials below the subbase
and base course layers if required should consist of non expansive granular material
such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well
graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and
should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum
swell of 1 percent or less Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base course subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement
surface should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all surface water off
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should not be
allowed anywhere on site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to
isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs
course layers to reduce migration of landscape water into the pavement section
Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect excessive water from
landscaping irrigation For long term performance we recommend that a subdrain
system be constructed along the sides of the roadway

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling five
borings extending to depths between about 20 and 21 2 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous
flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
California sampler with a 440 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of
borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the cohesive soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

the test are presented on Plate B 1

bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
