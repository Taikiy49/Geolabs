LANAKILA SENIOR CENTER 
STRUCTURAL STUDY 
HONOLULU, OAHU, HAWAII 
Dear Mr. Hayashida: 
This letter report presents our preliminary findings from our field exploration 
program and initial floor level survey performed for the Lanakila Senior Center Structural 
Study project in the Honolulu area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The Lanakila Senior Center is located adjacent to Lanakila Avenue in the 
Honolulu District on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The Lanakila Senior Center is located 
north of Lanakila Elementary School and west of the Lanakila Health Center. The 
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
The Lanakila Senior Center is a single-story structure supported on spread 
footings with concrete slabs-on-grade. Settlements of up to about 1.5 inches were 
observed at the building concrete slabs-on-grade. The most pronounced settlements 
were observed in the Arts and Crafts Room. We understand that the concrete floor 
slabs in the Lounge and Library Rooms have been previously repaired. 
REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield 
volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau.  The older volcano, Waianae, is estimated to be middle 
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
shield volcano, Koolau, is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age 
(Ice Age) and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Waianae 
volcano became extinct while the Koolau volcano was still active, and its eastern flank was 
partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank.

The project area is located on the southern flank of elongated Koolau Volcanic 
Range, which forms the southern portion of the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The range was 
formed from basalt lava flows originated from a rift zone roughly paralleling the existing 
mountain trends.  During the late Tertiary and Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), deep valleys 
including the Nuuanu Valley were eroded by streams.  During higher stands of the sea 
level in the Pleistocene Epoch, the valleys were infilled with alluvial sediments to the high 
sea level stands. 
Underlying these alluvial sediments are the older volcanic lava rock formation.  
In-situ chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million 
years.  The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic soils.  In 
general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty material while residual soils are more clayey.  
Both residual and saprolite soils are typical of the in-situ weathering of volcanic rocks.  The 
residual and saprolitic soils grade to basaltic rock formation with increased depth. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling two (2) borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 21.5 and 22.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface, respectively. The approximate boring locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, the subsurface conditions at the project site consisted of about 4 to 
4.5 feet of fill underlain by alluvial materials extending to a depth of about 9 to 11 feet 
below the existing ground surface. Below the alluvium, saprolite was encountered 
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 22.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The fill and alluvial materials generally consisted of stiff to very stiff clay 
with varying amounts of gravel and boulder. The underlying saprolitic material generally 
consisted of very stiff clayey silt with some sand.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field 
exploration. However, groundwater levels may change due to seasonal precipitation, 
surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of 
Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2, Appendix A. Results of the laboratory tests performed on 
selected soil samples retrieved from our field exploration are presented in Appendix B, 
Plates B-1 through B-4.  
OBSERVED DISTRESSES 
In general, building distresses were observed throughout most of the Lanakila 
Senior Center structure and appear to be more severe around the perimeter of the 
property. The observed distresses included cracks through the slabs-on-grade, cracks on 
the floor tiles, separation between the slabs-on-grade from the wall base moldings, uneven 
finished floor surfaces, and cracks in the outside walls.

In the courtyard area, we understand that the rain water ponds adjacent to the side

FLOOR LEVEL SURVEY 
The initial floor level survey was performed on August 18, 2017 for the Lanakila 
Senior Center property including the main facility building, outdoor concrete slab, 
multi-purpose room, and kitchen areas. The floor level survey equipment consists of a 
pressurized hydrostatic altimeter that utilizes a pressurized system that measures changes 
in elevation between a determined datum (zero location) and a point of interest.  The zero 
datum location was selected at the north corner of the Inner Court near the center of the 
property. 
The initial floor level survey readings ranged from -9.4 to +1.0 inches. Note that the 
-9.4-inch reading was taken at the Outdoor Court Area on a drain inlet structure. The 
readings ranged from -2.2 to -8.1 inches along the outside northern office wall.  The large 
difference in elevation from the datum was partly due to the grade drop from the main 
building and the courtyard area. The lowest point from the datum within the Lanakila 
Senior Center structure was along the west wall of the Lounge Room with a reading of -
4.6 inches. We understand that the Lounge Room was previously an outdoor concrete 
patio that was enclosed and re-constructed. The highest point from the datum (+1.0-inch 
reading) was measured within the Covered Activity Lanai near the eastern wall of the 
Multi-Purpose Room.  It appears that the roots of the existing banyan tree in the courtyard 
may be the cause of the high point reading.  The results of the initial floor level survey are 
presented on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
PRELIMINARY FINDINGS 
Based on the results of our borings and laboratory testing, the underlying clay soils 
have a high to very high shrink/swell potential when subjected to variations in moisture 
content. Due to poor drainage conditions at the site, it appears that the underlying clay 
soils have experienced swelling and shrinkage causing the movement and distress 
observed.    
The results of the initial floor level survey performed generally indicate that there 
are differential movements throughout the building complex.  The most severe areas 
appear to be at the west and southwest portions of the Lanakila Senior Center. These 
areas include the existing Lounge Room, Library, offices, and Arts and Crafts Room.  
An additional floor level survey will be performed at a later time to determine if there 
are any changes in floor level elevations. If the results of the additional floor level survey 
indicate that the soil movement has stabilized, repairs to the existing building structure 
may be considered. However, if the soil movement has not stabilized, re-construction of 
the Lanakila Senior Center building may be recommended.

ROCK FRACTURE CHARACTERISTICS
The following terms describe general fracture spacing of a rock:
Massive: Greater than 24 inches apart
Slightly Fractured: 12 to 24 inches apart
Moderately Fractured: 6 to 12 inches apart
Closely Fractured: 3 to 6 inches apart
Severely Fractured: Less than 3 inches apart
DEGREE OF WEATHERING
The following terms describe the chemical weathering of a rock:
Unweathered: Rock shows no sign of discoloration or loss of strength.
Slightly Weathered: Slight discoloration inwards from open fractures.
Moderately Weathered: Discoloration throughout and noticeably weakened though not able to break by hand.
Highly Weathered: Most minerals decomposed with some corestones present in residual soil mass. Can be broken by hand.
Extremely Weathered: Saprolite. Mineral residue completely decomposed to soil but fabric and structure preserved.
HARDNESS
The following terms describe the resistance of a rock to indentation or scratching:
Very Hard: Specimen breaks with difficulty after several "pinging" hammer blows.
Example: Dense, fine grain volcanic rock
Hard: Specimen breaks with some difficulty after several hammer blows.
Example: Vesicular, vugular, coarse-grained rock
Medium Hard: Specimen can be broked by one hammer blow. Cannot be scraped by knife. SPT may penetrate by
~25 blows per inch with bounce.
Example: Porous rock such as clinker, cinder, and coral reef
