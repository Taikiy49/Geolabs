SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The proposed waterline alignments traverse a large area. Therefore, our field 
exploration encountered variable subsurface conditions along the water system 
improvement project. Pavement sections generally consisted of AC pavement, PCC 
pavement, or PCC overlain by AC pavement. The presence and thickness of the base 
course layer and/or fill layer varied greatly throughout the project alignment. Beneath the 
pavement section, fill, cinder, volcanic ash, residual soil, alluvium, colluvium, and 
weathered clinker were encountered extending to the maximum depth explored of about 
16.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. Hard to very hard basalt formation was 
encountered near the bottom of two of our borings at depths of about 6 to 7 feet below 
the existing pavement surface. Cobbles and boulders were frequently encountered within 
the alluvial and colluvial deposits during our field exploration at various locations.  
We encountered groundwater in the drilled borings at depths of about 6 to 11.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface, corresponding to elevations of about +152.5 to 
+318.5 feet MSL. It should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to 
rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, perched groundwater, and 
other factors. 
We anticipate that the new water main inverts will be about 4 to 5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface. However, deeper excavations may be required where the new 
water main traverses under existing utilities. Installation of the proposed water mains will 
involve excavation into the underlying fills, cinder, volcanic ash, residual soil, alluvium, 
colluvium, weathered clinker, hard to very hard basalt formation, and cobbles and 
boulders. In general, we anticipate that the in-situ soils along the proposed water main 
alignments may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. However, trench 
excavations into the underlying basalt rock formation and/or cobbles and boulders may 
require chipping and/or the use of hoerams to remove the materials and to achieve the 
design invert elevations. 
Where deep excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary 
shoring or sloping and benching should be implemented for the trench excavations. 
Based on our site observations, we believe that temporary cut slopes for open cut 
excavations are not feasible. Therefore, trench walls would have to be cut near vertical, 
necessitating the use of temporary shoring during construction.  
In general, the excavated on-site soils encountered during our field exploration may 
be used in the upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the pavement subgrade, provided that the materials are free of vegetation, deleterious

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Manoa Water System Improvements – Area 2 project in the Manoa 
area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses for the water system improvements project. These recommendations are 
intended for the design of trench excavation, backfilling, pavement restoration, thrust 
block/vault design, and corrosion potential only. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The Manoa Water System Improvements – Area 2 project is located at the eastern 
portion of the Metro 705’ system and the Metro 405’ system in the Manoa area of Honolulu 
on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project consists of replacing approximately 
16,365 linear feet of 8 and 12-inch diameter waterline along Ventura Street, Ferdinand 
Avenue, Awapuhi Street, Manoa Road, Oahu Avenue, Rainbow Drive, Huelani Drive, 
Kahawai Street, Lowrey Avenue, Pawaina Street, Pakanu Street, Old East Manoa Road, 
East Manoa Road, Alani Drive, Woodlawn Drive, Woodlawn Terrace Place, and Loulu 
Street. The scope of work requires that the system be designed in accordance with the 
Honolulu Board of Water Supply standards, including corrosion control. 
Based on the water system standards, corrosion testing should be performed at 
every 300-foot interval along the waterline alignment. However, we were directed by the 
Board of Water Supply to test at every 500-foot interval for this project. The invert 
elevations of the 8 and 12-inch diameter waterlines were not available at the time this 
report was prepared. However, we anticipate that the new waterlines may have an invert 
elevation of about 4 to 5 feet below the existing pavement surface.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield 
volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle 
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae became extinct while 
Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas 
banking against its eastern flank. These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau 
referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, there was a renewal of volcanic activity with the 
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series.  Along the crest of the ridge on the 
westerly side of Manoa Valley, there are the Tantalus, Roundtop, and Sugarloaf vents. 
When these vents were active, one or more lava flows from these vents spilled down the 
westerly wall of the valley and moved across the valley floor. The lava flows ponded and 
filled the lower portions of the valley. The ponding allowed the lava to densify as it cooled, 
forming dense, massive rock. 
During this epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of continental 
glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic 
basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When the glaciers 
melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose 
to above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosion processes are dominated by 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported downslope

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located at the eastern portion of the Metro 705’ system and the 
Metro 405’ system. Within the project limits, most of the roadways are paved with 
asphaltic concrete (AC). Ventura Street was the only roadway observed to be paved with 
Portland Cement Concrete (PCC) pavement, likely due to the steep grade of the road.  
In general, the topography of the project site slopes downward from the head of 
the valley towards the ocean (northeast to southwest) with roadway inclinations ranging 
from nearly level to about 20 percent. Based on the topographic survey map provided, 
the existing top-of-pavement elevations range from approximately +161 feet 
Mean Sea Level (MSL) along Lowrey Avenue to about +549 feet MSL near the top of 
Woodlawn Terrace Place.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The proposed waterline alignments traverse a large area. Therefore, our field 
exploration encountered variable subsurface conditions along the water system 
improvement project. Pavement sections generally consisted of AC pavement, 
PCC pavement, or PCC overlain by AC pavement. The presence and thickness of the 
base course layer and/or fill layer varied greatly throughout the project alignment. 
Beneath the pavement section, fill, cinder, volcanic ash, residual soil, alluvium, colluvium, 
and weathered clinker were encountered extending to the maximum depth explored of 
about 16.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. Basalt formation was encountered 
in two of our borings. We encountered groundwater in the drilled borings at depths of 
about 6 to 12 feet below the existing ground surface, corresponding to elevations of about

We understand that the construction of the water mains will generally consist of 
trench excavation, pipe placement, trench backfill, and pavement restoration. Detailed 
discussions for the design of the water system improvements project, including trench 
excavation, trench excavation support, backfilling, pavement restoration, thrust

and boulders were frequently encountered within the alluvial and colluvial deposits during

3.2 
Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate that excavations up to about 4 to 5 feet below the pavement surface 
will be required for the installation of the new water mains. Deeper excavations may be 
required where the water mains traverse existing underground utilities. Where deeper 
excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary shoring or sloping and 
benching should be implemented for the trench excavations. Based on our site 
observations, we believe that temporary cut slopes for open cut excavations are not 
feasible. Therefore, trench walls would have to be cut near vertical, necessitating the use 
of temporary shoring during construction. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and 
safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative should be on-site at all 
times during excavation and construction work for the opportunity to promptly observe 
changing or unforeseen conditions, such as high groundwater, inappropriate construction 
sequence or techniques, etc., which may affect the shoring stability. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for 
excessive ground movement.

It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the 
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction. The properly braced shoring is 
essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent ground 
into the excavation, which may result in potential settlement or distress to adjacent 
structures or other improvements. It should be noted that relatively thick layers of very 
loose to medium dense cinder sand were encountered in several of our drilled borings.  
The cinder sand may tend to slough and “run” during excavation, causing loss of support 
to the surrounding pavement.  Special attention should be paid to properly brace the 
trench excavations into the underlying cinder deposits. 
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the 
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. Due 
to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the magnitude 
of movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship, such as how 
quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed, the subsurface 
conditions, the size of the excavation, and the rate of excavation. 
Therefore, it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed 
properly and as early as practical. The adjacent ground should be continuously monitored 
for cracks, dips, and/or other indications of movements with instruments until the 
subsurface water system pipes are finally backfilled. 
3.3 
Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the underlying fills, volcanic ash, cinder, residual soil, 
alluvium, colluvium, weathered clinker, or basalt rock formation, a minimum of 6 inches 
of granular cushion material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) 
should be provided below the pipe. Where soft and/or loose soils are encountered at or 
near the invert of the pipes, a stabilization layer consisting of an additional 18 inches of 
open-graded gravel, such as No. 3B Fine gravel, wrapped in a non-woven filter fabric 
(Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below the cushion layer for uniform 
support.

3.4 
Trench Backfill 
In general, the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the 
Water System Standards, State of Hawaii 2002. The first zone of backfill extending from 
the cushion material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes (or 12 inches above 
the groundwater level, whichever is greater) should consist of select free-draining 
granular material (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) to reduce the compaction effort required 
and resulting stresses on the pipe. In addition, it is critical to use this free-draining material 
to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide 
adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill 
settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the pavement subgrade should consist of the excavated on-site soils or imported fill 
materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious materials, organic material, and clay 
lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension. It should be 
noted that the fill requirements presented herein are intended as guidelines only and may 
be modified based on Geolabs additional laboratory testing and field observations on the 
available fill materials. 
Imported fill materials used for trench backfill should be well graded from coarse 
to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension. The material should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material 
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell 
potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Geolabs 
should test the imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations.

3.5 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfill materials consisting of the on-site soils should be moisture-conditioned to 
at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches 
in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The 
upper 3 feet below the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. 
Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course and subbase 
materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed 
in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of 
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established 
in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by 
dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, or ponding 
should not be allowed.

We envision that concrete valve structures may be required for the proposed 
waterlines. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend 
an allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,500 pounds per square foot (psf) for foundations 
bearing on the underlying fills, volcanic ash, cinder, residual soil, alluvium, colluvium, 
weathered clinker, or basalt rock formation. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads 
and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by 
seismic forces.

3.8 
Thrust Blocks 
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide 
resistance to the thrust forces due to the pressurized pipes. The thrust force acting on the 
pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of the thrust 
block. We anticipate that most of the planned inverts will be about 4 to 5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface. However, some of the thrust blocks may need to be located 
at deeper depths in areas where the new water main traverses existing utilities. 
Based on our field exploration, variable materials including very loose to very 
dense sand, soft to hard clayey/sandy silt and silty clay, and basalt rock formation were 
encountered within the depths explored. Therefore, we recommend using a lateral 
bearing pressure of 500 pounds per square foot (psf) for the design of thrust blocks 
bearing against the in-situ materials underlying the site. 
3.9 
Settlements 
Primary settlement of the water mains is normally caused by the difference in the 
unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the 
underlying subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our engineering analyses, 
settlement on the order of less than ½ inch is anticipated for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could occur if 
improper trench support is used.

content, and sulfate content, were performed on selected samples obtained during our 
field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the near-surface soils at the project site. It

of the Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) tests performed by

Design of metallic substructures, such as metallic piping, should consider the 
effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. Resistivity is generally 
recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics regarding the corrosivity of 
the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the resistivity, the greater the 
potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. Conversely, the higher the 
resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion of metallic objects. Results 
of the resistivity testing indicate that the on-site soils have resistivity values ranging from 
830 to 12,000 ohm-cm with pH values varying from 5.34 to 8.24. Therefore, the on-site 
near-surface soils may be considered mildly to extremely corrosive based on the 
Board of Water Supply, City and County of Honolulu Water System External Corrosion 
Control Standards dated 1991. 
In addition, chloride content and sulfate content were performed by TestAmerica 
Laboratories, Inc. to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site soils encountered. Based on 
the chloride and sulfate content tests performed on the on-site soils, the test values are 
generally relatively low. It may be appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion 
engineer to review the test results and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion 
protection.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling 42 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 119, 201 through 204, 301 through 317, 401 and 
402, extending to depths of about 5.5 to 16.5 feet below the existing pavement surface at 
the approximate locations shown on the Overall Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2, and Site 
Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.12. We used a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers to advance the borings. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock samples were described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate 
A-0.3. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs 
of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-42. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration 
sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Twenty-seven Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plates B-1 through B-3. 
Ten Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plates B-4 and B-5. 
Forty sets of pH (ASTM G51) and Minimum Resistivity (ASTM G57) tests were 
performed by our office, and Chloride (EPA 300) and Sulfate Content (EPA 300) tests 
were performed by TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on selected soil samples obtained 
from our field exploration. The test results are presented on Plates B-6 and B-7. 
Six laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-8 through B-13.
