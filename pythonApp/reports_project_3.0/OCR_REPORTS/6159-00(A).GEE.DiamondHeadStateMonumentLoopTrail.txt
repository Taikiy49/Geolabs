SECTION 1. GENERAL
1.1. Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Diamond Head State Monument, Loop Trail project in the Honolulu
District on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are
shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and
engineering analyses. These recommendations are intended for the design of
foundations, slabs-on-grade, and site preparation only. The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report.
1.2. Project Considerations
The Diamond Head State Monument, Loop Trail project site is within Diamond
Head Crater on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The new Loop Trail will be located near the
observation area.
The Loop Trail work consists of the following:
1. Installation of new Main Trail with minimum width of 4 feet and hardening
of surface from the lookout down to the lower area. The trail will consist of
stairs and landings.
2. Installation of new Main Trail with minimum width of 4 feet and hardening
of surface along the lower area and connecting to the existing rest area
located at the end of the existing tunnel. Guardrails will be installed on the
down slope side of the trail.
3. Installation of a Secondary Trail extending from the Main Trail to a new
Lookout Area.
4. Installation of a new Lookout Area at the end of the Secondary Trail. The
Lookout Area will have a hardened surface and guardrails.

SECTION 2. SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of
the eastern two-thirds of the islands. It is believed that Waianae became extinct while
Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in
central Oahu.
Volcanic activity from Koolau Volcano ceased for nearly 2 million years. Volcanic
activity once again resumed from mid-Pleistocene Epoch and has lasted through recent
geologic time. These volcanic events, known as the Honolulu Volcanic Series, have
formed cinder, spatter, tuff and ash cones throughout the southern portion of the Island
of Oahu.
One of the most prominent vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series is Diamond
Head. Diamond Head is a tuff cone and was generally formed by hydromagmatic
explosions. Materials within the tuff cone consist of magmatic debris, blocks of Koolau
basalt, and fragments of coral limestone torn from the underlying reef. The project site is
located on the western slope within Diamond Head Crater.
2.2 Site Description
The project site is located within the western portion of Diamond Head Crater on
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.
The majority of the project site is overgrown with low to tall brush. A lone palm
tree is at the eastern end of the project.
In general, the site slopes down towards the eastern portion of the project site at
a slope inclination ranging from about one and one half horizontal to one vertical
(1.5H:1V) to 3H:1V. The ground surface elevations range from about +730 feet Mean

2.3. Shallow Subsurface Conditions
We explored the shallow subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and
sampling five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1, 1A, 2 through 4 to depths ranging
from about 0.6 to 0.7 feet below the existing ground surface. A 3-inch diameter hand
auger was used for the field exploration. In addition, a hand pick was used to extend the
boring into the underlying volcanic tuff deposit. The approximate boring locations are
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
In general, our field exploration encountered a surface layer consisting of
weathered volcanic tuff about 0.3 to 0.6 feet thick. The weathered tuff was comprised of
loose to medium dense silty sand with some gravel. The surface weathered tuff layer
was underlain by a soft to hard volcanic tuff formation extending to about 0.7 feet, the
maximum depth explored.
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field
exploration. Groundwater levels may vary with seasonal precipitation, surface run-off,
and other factors.

Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by a
relatively thin layer of loose to medium dense silty sand with some gravel underlain by
soft to hard volcanic tuff formation extending to the maximum depth explored of about
0.7 feet below the existing ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in the
borings at the time of the field exploration.
Fill placement is not desired for this project because of the relatively steep
ground conditions. Therefore, the majority of the site grading will involve cut slopes and
excavation.
A rigid pavement will be used for the new trail path. Since the new trail path will
be constructed on a sloping ground condition, we recommend utilizing a thickened-edge
concrete slab for the path.
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site, we believe that
shallow foundations consisting of spread and/or continuous footings bearing on the
volcanic tuff formation may be used to support the new metal stair structure. Detailed
discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering recommendations are
presented in the following sections.

3.1.1 Site Preparation
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site or
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials.
Soft and yielding areas encountered during the clearing and grubbing should be
over-excavated to expose firm natural material, and the resulting excavation should
be backfilled with lean concrete.
3.1.2 Fills and Backfills
We understand that fill materials will be required behind low retaining walls planned
at the lookout areas. The on-site silty sand and volcanic tuff materials less than
3 inches in largest dimension may be used as fill materials. The fill material should
be free of vegetation and deleterious materials.
Select granular fill and imported fill should consist of non-expansive granular
material, such as crushed coral, basalt or cinder sand. The material should be
well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and
should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance
with ASTM D 1883. Geolabs should test imported fill materials for conformance with
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use.
3.1.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fill placement is not desired for this project due to the steep existing ground
conditions. Therefore, the majority of the site grading will involve cut slopes and
excavation. Fill placement will only be required behind the low retaining walls at the
lookout areas.

3.4.3 Drainage
Retaining walls should be well-drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic
pressures. A typical drainage system consists of a 1 to 2-foot wide zone of
permeable material, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation),
placed directly around the perforated pipe (perforations down) at the base of the
wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes. As an alternative, a
prefabricated drainage product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used
in lieu of the drainage material. The prefabricated drainage product should also be

required embedment depths.

The finished grades outside the foundations and slabs should be sloped to shed
water away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding. In addition,
concentration of surface run-off water near the foundations and slabs should be
avoided. These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the

Field Exploration

five borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1, 1A, 2, 3 and 4 extending to depths ranging from

textural examination in the field and observed the drilling operations on a near-continuous
basis. These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and testing in the
laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representation of the materials encountered is
presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5.
