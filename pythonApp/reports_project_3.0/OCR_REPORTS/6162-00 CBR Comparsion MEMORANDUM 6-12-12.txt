SUBJECT: Rehabilitation of City Streets 
Oahu, Hawaii 
This technical memorandum presents the results of our evaluation of California 
Bearing Ratio (CBR) values derived from various field and laboratory testing methods 
utilized during exploration of the street rehabilitation project in Oahu, Hawaii.  Specifically, 
three (3) methodologies were used to estimate the subgrade CBR values for the 
pavement rehabilitation design: 1) laboratory CBR tests, 2) Dynamic Cone Penetrometer 
(DCP); and 3) Falling Weight Deflectometer (FWD).  The following five (5) pavement areas 
(units) were selected for this evaluation because at least 2 of the 3 tests were performed in 
each unit, which provided us an opportunity to compare the estimated CBR values from 
various approaches.

California Bearing Ratio (CBR) 
Exploratory borings were drilled in the street pavement areas listed above.  Bulk 
soil samples were collected in the borings for laboratory CBR tests.  The CBR tests were 
performed in accordance with test method ASTM D1883.  The results of the laboratory 
CBR tests along with the soil boring logs were presented in the following Geolabs 
reports/technical memoranda: 
 “Preliminary Pavement Recommendations, Rehabilitation of Streets, Unit 48, 
(W.O. 6167-00),” dated January 11, 2011. 
 “Preliminary Pavement Recommendations, Rehabilitation of Streets, Unit 51, 
(W.O. 6165-00),” dated January 6, 2010. 
 “Preliminary Pavement Recommendations, Rehabilitation of Streets, Unit 55, 
Pearl City, Oahu, Hawaii, (W.O. 6166-00),” dated April 4, 2010. 
 “Preliminary Pavement Recommendations, Rehabilitation of Streets, Unit 57, 
Honolulu, Oahu, Hawaii, (W.O. 6162-00),” dated September 27, 2010. 
 “Preliminary Pavement Recommendations, Rehabilitation of Streets, Unit 6, 
Honolulu, Oahu, Hawaii, (W.O. 3968-10),” dated December 7, 2010. 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) 
DCP tests were conducted at selected site locations in Units 51 and 57 in 
accordance with test method ASTM D6951.  An 8-kg (17.6-pound) hammer was used for 
the tests.  The DCP tests were usually conducted within 5 feet from the nearby borings in 
order to correlate with the soil layering.  In general, the DCP locations were predrilled 
through the pavement and the base rock to a depth of about 12 to 18 inches prior to 
performing the DCP test.  Subsequently, the hammer blows required for every inch of 
penetration for the DCP test were recorded. 
The DCP blow count data were input into the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, 
PCASE Version 2.09.01 computer program to estimate the CBR values.  The soil profiles 
from the adjacent borings were used to establish the soil layer breaks.  We also combined 
some of the soil layers based on description of the laboratory CBR samples.  The PCASE 
program generated average CBR values for the soil layers.

Falling Weight Deflectometer (FWD) 
Non-Destructive Testing was performed at selected roadway alignments for the 
project by All About Pavements Inc. (API).  In general, API performed field tests using a 
Falling Weight Deflectometer (FWD).  The FWD simulates a moving wheel load of a 
vehicle on the roadway pavements.  API also performed analyses using the FWD 
deflection measurements to back-calculate the resilient modulus of the pavement 
components including the asphalt and PCC concrete surfacing, the aggregate base 
course, and the subgrade.  The subgrade soil CBR values were then estimated based on 
the resilient modulus of the subgrade.  The results of the FWD testing along with the 
estimated subgrade CBR values were presented in API’s report entitled “Pavement 
Evaluation and Rehabilitation Design for City of Honolulu Streets, Honolulu, Hawaii (API 
Project No. 09C005N)”, dated February 2011. 
The FWD method utilized the mechanistic (elastic layered analysis) approach by 
back-calculating the resilient modulus of the pavement materials (asphalt concrete, base 
rock and subgrade soil) using the measured pavement deflections.  Resilient modulus was 
assigned to each pavement material, and the predicted pavement deflections were 
estimated.  The analyses terminated when the predicted pavement deflection basin closely 
matched the field measurements.  Then the subgrade CBR would be calculated using the 
following E and CBR relationships, where E is the subgrade modulus in psi:  
 E = 1500 x CBR  
This relationship is considered reasonable when applied to a modulus (E) 
back-calculated from FWD measurements, at which the CBR values are between 
3 and 20.

The FWD deflections were recorded along the roadway alignment at an interval of 
about 100 feet apart.  The pavement deflection data were then grouped and analyzed.  
The back-calculated subgrade CBRs were average values along both vertical (depth) and 
horizontal (distance) directions, and would be less sensitive to isolated gravel 
layers/pockets.   
DCP blow counts were recorded continuously below the pre-drilled depth, and the 
PCASE analyses provided an average CBR value of each soil layer over the depth of 
measurement at the test location.  The DCP blow counts are more sensitive to the 
presence of gravel pockets, and could vary significantly between test locations.  In the 
PCASE analyses, soil correlation for “All Soils” option was used instead of selecting 
“Heavy Clay” or “Lean Clay”.  We found that the “All Soils” option generally resulted in 
better correlations with the laboratory CBR test results. 
Laboratory CBR tests were performed on bulk soil samples collected from the 
borings.  The test method is primarily intended for cohesive materials having maximum 
particle sizes of ¾-inch or less. Subgrade materials with larger gravel size could be 
problematic even though there are modified test procedures available to address this 
situation.  In addition, proper sampling procedures are critical to ensure that base rock and 
other surface gravel materials are not inadvertently sampled along with the subgrade soils.

Comparisons of the CBR values derived from DCP and laboratory tests are 
presented on Plate 1.  Comparisons of the CBR values estimated from FWD and 
laboratory tests are presented on Plate 2.  Comparisons of the CBR values from DCP and 
FWD are presented on Plate 3.   
In general, the CBR values from laboratory tests were higher than those obtained 
from DCP and FWD (see Plates 1 and 2), and the CBR values from DCP were higher than 
those derived from FWD (see Plate 3).  The following paragraphs present brief 
discussions of our findings: 
DCP vs. Laboratory CBR Tests - As shown on Plate 1, the CBR values derived from DCP 
were approximately 1.5 times higher than the laboratory tests for CBR of 20 or less.  
Between 20 and 50, the laboratory tests produced CBR values of about one to 2 times 
higher than those from DCP.  For laboratory CBR over 50, there were not sufficient data to 
make correlations.  Also, only limited data were available for silt and granular (sand and 
gravel) soils, and no clear correlations can be established at this time. 
FWD vs. Laboratory CBR Tests - As shown on Plate 2, the CBR values derived from FWD 
approximately correlate with the laboratory tests for CBR between 10 and 20.  Between 
20 and 35, the laboratory tests produced CBR values of about 2 times larger than those 
from FWD.  Between 35 and 70, the laboratory tests produced CBR values of about 
4 times higher than those from FWD.  For laboratory CBR over 70, there were insufficient 
data to make any correlations, and no clear correlations could be established at this time. 
FWD vs. DCP - As shown on Plate 3, the CBR values estimated from FWD correlate 
relatively well with those obtained from DCP for CBR of 20 or less.  Between 20 and 60, 
the DCP produced CBR values of about 2 to 4 times higher than those from FWD.  For 
CBR over 60, there was insufficient data to make any correlations.   
Conclusions 
In pavement design projects, the design engineer should review all available field 
and laboratory testing data, and make appropriate adjustments, if necessary, based on 
historical pavement performance records at the project sites and on similar project 
experience.  In general, laboratory CBR tests are more applicable for new pavement 
construction or reconstruction where the subgrade soil can be compacted to match 
laboratory conditions.  However, DCP and FWD are more representative of the actual

subgrade conditions, which are especially important for pavement rehabilitation projects 
(overlay instead of reconstruction). 
In the mechanistic-empirical (elastic layered) pavement design, the subgrade 
modulus instead of subgrade CBR value is the design parameter used for the subgrade. In 
clay soils with CBR value less than 20, the subgrade modulus may be estimated using the 
relationships discussed previously where CBR values could be obtained from laboratory 
tests or DCP.  For clay soils with CBR value greater than 20, it may be more reliable to 
obtain the subgrade modulus back-calculated directly from FWD.  Otherwise, the 
laboratory CBR value should be reduced by ½ to ¼ when estimating the subgrade 
modulus for the elastic layered pavement design.   
Additional FWD and DCP data will be gathered in future projects to increase the 
database so that more specific correlations could be established, especially in granular soil 
sites.  In our opinion, FWD pavement deflection measurements should be considered for 
pavement rehabilitation projects as well as in granular soil sites.  In clay soils sites with 
CBR less than 20, laboratory CBR tests and field DCP could be performed in lieu of FWD. 
Limitations 
The estimated CBR values from FWD were taken directly from the API’s 
February 2011 report.  We had not performed independent checks on the back-calculation 
analyses, and cannot guarantee the accuracy of the CBR values estimated by API.
