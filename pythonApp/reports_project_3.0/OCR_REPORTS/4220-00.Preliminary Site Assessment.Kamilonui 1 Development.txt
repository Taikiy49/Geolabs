REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu encompasses an area of about 604 square miles and
generally consists of four major geomorphic provinces 1 Koolau Range 2 Waianae
Range 3 Schofield Plateau 4 Coastal Plain Of the four provinces Kamilonui
Valley is located within the Koolau Range province The Koolau Range was formed by
the Koolau shield volcano which comprises the majority of the eastern and
northeastern portion of the island of Oahu The elongate shape of Koolau Range is
approximately 37 miles in length and has been deeply eroded by stream activity over
the past several million years The Koolau Range generally consists of massive
accumulations of thinly bedded lava flows which began to build after the older Waianae
Range of western Oahu was established
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 4220 00 Page 6
The Koolau Range erupted in Tertiary time approximately 2 to 5 million years
ago Following the formation of the Koolau Range the island of Oahu began to
submerge under the weight of the volcanic mass This slow submergence combined
with long term sea level fluctuations as a result of the worldwide continental glacial
advance and retreat worked to erode the island of Oahu to what it is now today For
example during periods of higher than normal today s sea level the raised sea level
served to promote the deposition of shallow water marine sediment and coral deposits
on ground which today may be exposed at or above the present day sea level In
contrast during periods of low sea level terrestrial streams were forced to cut deeper
into the exposed ground to carry the terrestrial sediment further out into what once was
a marine or shallow lagoonal environment Thus a complex series of marine and
terrestrial deposits were formed around the coastal and relatively low elevation portions
of the island of Oahu The varying types of deposits can be found interlayered making
reconstruction of the history of the development of the coastal areas of the island of
Oahu very complex
Kamilonui Valley is described as a relatively mature stage U Shaped erosional
valley formed as the streams draining the Koolau summit cut down through the rock
layers to incise and form the present day valley walls and floor The walls of the valley
provide exposure of the bedded lava flows of the Koolau shield volcano The valley
floor is generally composed of alluvial and colluvial deposits of volcanic material that
had been weathered eroded and transported downslope for deposition The valley
floor typically contains soil and rock materials that have been transported by stream
activity and buried over long periods of time forming thick deposits of mixtures of clay
and boulders The buried clayey cobbles and boulders have since undergone
additional chemicai weathering in the depositional environment As a result of in situ
weathering and burial the older deeper deposits have become somewhat
consolidated and tightly packed to form a sedimentary conglomerate
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 4220 00 Page 7
More recent Quaternary time volcanic activity occurred in the vicinity of eastern
Oahu after the main Koolau volcano activity ceased The most recent volcanic activity is
part of the Honolulu volcanic series and is represented by the eruption of pyroclastic
material ash and cinder and some lava flows The cinder and ash cones located
nearest the Kamilonui project site comprise the Koko Head fissure which forms the
extreme southeastern portion of the island of Oahu Although the cone and crater
deposits are concentrated to the southeast of the project site some of the pyroclastic
materials are believed to have been locally deposited in the vicinity of the site These
localized deposits may be buried beneath some of the more recent alluvial and shallow
marine deposits of the area as evidenced by the stratigraphy exposed in Test Pits Nos 3
and 4 excavated at the project site

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration and geologic mapping the proposed development
site is generally underlain by adobe clay soils with cobbles and boulders extending to
depths of over 18 feet below the existing ground surface These clayey soils which are
classified as CH according to the Unified Soil Classification System exhibit high
shrink swell potential and will lose considerable shear strength when saturated Under
extended period of wet weather the adobe clay soils will eventually get saturated and
lose considerable shear strength thus initialing downhill creep movement in areas
where the terrain is steeper than about 4H 1V When the adobe clay soils experience
significant movement shear slip surfaces will then develop which will further reduce
the shear strength along the slip surfaces to a very low residual shear value and may
result in an accelerated downhill movement or slope failure In general these downhill
creep movements may be attributed to a combination of the following factors
1 Poor subsoil conditions such as the highly plastic adobe clay soils
commonly encountered in the area
2 Steep hillside terrain especially in areas where the terrain is steeper than
4H 1V
3 Excessive water infiltration particularly for the subject site which is located
in a relatively high moisture environment
Based on our preliminary engineering evaluation and analyses we believe that
the southern narrow portion of the project site could be at a state of equilibrium with
respect to downhill creep movement Therefore it is our opinion that planned
development at the subject site is feasible from a geotechnical engineering
point of view provided that special site grading and slope stabilization measures are
implemented during the design and construction phases of the subject project to reduce
the contributing factors

Mechanism of Downhill Creep Movement
Based on our field exploration and geologic mapping the project site is generally
underlain by adobe clay soils with a high concentration of cobbles and boulders
extending to depths of over 18 feet below the existing ground surface Due to the limited
penetration of the excavator the actual thickness of the adobe deposits could not be
determined from the current field exploration Our past experience in the area suggests
that the thickness of the adobe deposits could be over 40 feet and possibly thicker along
the valley floor
The adobe clay soils encountered at the project site which are classified as CH
according to the Unified Soil Classification System exhibit high shrink swell potential
when subjected to moisture fluctuation In addition the adobe clay soils are very weak
soils which are significantly affected by an increase in moisture content i e it will lose
considerable shear strength when saturated As a result they are subject to slope creep
or landslide movement in a sloping terrain during extended wet periods
Due to the relatively low permeability of the clay soils run off from heavy rainfall
may flow overland and concentrate in ditches and gullies where it would flow turbulently
downslope accelerating erosion of the slopes Due to the frequent rainfall in the valley
increasing in frequency and intensity to the north soils were observed to be relatively
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4220 00 Page 14
wet most of the time Dry weather periods were observed to cause the surficial soils to
desiccate and form shrinkage cracks as the soil contracted Subsequent rainfall would
cause the cracks to fill with water and cause swelling of the clay minerals The
repeated shrinking and swelling process often causes the soils to overturn and creep
slowly downslope creating potential slope stability problems
Due to the clayey nature of the adobe soils and the relatively steep terrain at
portions of the project site it is likely that the near surface soils were slowly creeping
downhill as evidenced by the slickensided zones and wet surfaces observed in some of
the excavated test pits indicating possible shear creep movement of the deposit in the
past Generally slope creep movements are initially very slow and may go undetected
for many years However with time the clay soils may eventually get saturated due to
continual infiltration of rainfall seepage etc and lose a portion of their initial soil shear
strength thereby increasing or accelerating downslope ground movements Water
infiltration then increases due to subsequent surface cracks developed in the ground
caused by the previous movement Due to the continued movement the soil strength
continues to decrease to its lowest value residual strength At this point the movements
could accelerate to rates that are very noticeable and measurable particularly if there is a
build up of water pressure or increased seepage and may result in large scale landslides
and slope failures
Evaluation of Slope Stability
The high clay content and slickensided zones in the adobe deposits create weak
planes which substantially reduce the shear strength of the soil mass Therefore
residual shear strength values which are significantly lower than the normally used
peak shear strength values must be utilized to evaluate the long term stability of these
clay soils The slickensided surfaces also reduce the effective cohesion which can be
developed by the soil particles Laboratory direct shear tests performed for the in situ
adobe clay soils indicate a residual effective friction angle of about 17 degrees and a
cohesion value of about 500 pounds per square foot psf Based on our past experience

with similar soils we recommend that a residual effective friction angle of about
11 degrees and a cohesion value of about 100 psf be used for the engineering analyses

Slope Stabilization Measures
As discussed above the adobe clay soils encountered at the project site possess
a tendency to creep downhill and result in potential slope failures under certain
conditions We believe that the major causes of these downhill slope movements are
1 poor subsoil conditions 2 steep hillside terrain and 3 excessive water infiltration
Therefore site grading and slope stabilization measures should be directed to these three
areas in order to reduce the potential for downhill creep movements and slope failures
Detailed discussions of these site grading and slope stabilization measures are presented
in following subsections of this report
Removal and Replacement of Adobe Clay Soils
The weak adobe clay soils encountered at the project site could be excavated
and replaced with select on site and or off site borrow materials placed in
controlled lifts Conventional grading methods could then be utilized for the
development of the subject site This method would be the least risky since it
requires the removal of the poor adobe clay soils and replacement with
competent materials However due to the areal and vertical extents of the
adobe clay soils removal required and the associated costs for hauling the
adobe clay soils away and importing fill materials this alternative could be very
costly and may not be a practical solution for the entire project site Therefore we
believe that this approach may be considered in conjunction with the second site
grading measure Flattening of Existing Site Grades as discussed below
Flattening of Existing Site Grades
Based on our preliminary slope stability study and previous experience with the
adobe clay soils a slope gradient of about 7H 1V or flatter would be desirable to
reduce the long term downhill creep movement of these soils In general a flatter
slope grade may be obtained by selective excavation and embankment fills in the
upper and lower slope areas respectively Additional evaluation and analyses of
this approach should be performed upon finalizing the site grading concept for the
W O 4220 00 Page 17
future development and conducting an updated topographic survey of the project
site
Construction of Earth Retaining Systems
In order to increase the resisting force against creep movements and slope
failures it is our opinion that earth retaining systems may be constructed to retain
the adobe clay soils from creeping downhill These retaining systems may
include but not be limited to the construction of buttress fills caisson walls and
reinforced earth walls etc
Buttress fills may be constructed near the toe of the existing slope to retain the
adobe clay soils In order to provide a stable and effective retaining structure the
adobe clay soils below the buttress should be completely removed to expose the
underlying competent materials or rock formation The bottom of the buttress fill
may consist of on site boulders to create a solid base and working platform
Select granular material such as the on site excavated rocky material generally
less than 12 inches in size or select borrow may be used to construct the
remaining portion of the buttress fill A chimney drain about 2 feet wide should be
incorporated in the rear portion of the buttress to intercept subsurface seepage
The chimney drain should be connected to a subdrain system and daylighted for
proper drainage disposal
Due to the very deep and extensive excavations required it should be noted that
the use of buttress fills to prevent downhill creep movement may not be feasible if
the adobe deposits are too thick Therefore the use of caisson walls may need
to be considered as an alternative under such conditions It is our opinion that the
caisson walls should penetrate through the upper unstable adobe clay soils and
should be embedded in the underlying competent materials The walls should be
designed to withstand external soil and groundwater pressures by taking the
lateral pressures either by cantilever action or by the installation of post tensioned
earth or rock anchors

Installation of Drainage Structures
Water infiltration into the subsurface adobe soils will not only decrease the shear
strength of the soils but also increase its unit weight This will affect the stability of
the existing slopes by reducing the resisting force and inducing additional driving
force Therefore measures should be taken and implemented to intercept surface
run off and subterranean seepage in order to reduce the potential for saturation of
the underlying adobe clay soils These may include the construction of 1 large
diameter drainage wells 2 trench drains and 3 concrete interceptor ditches
In areas where the unstable masses are deep and cover large areas the use of
large diameter deep drainage wells for slope stabilization should be considered
The technique involves drilling a series of large diameter shafts up to 5 feet in
diameter and about 15 to 50 feet on centers through the upper unstable adobe
clay soils Each shaft is connected to its neighbor by a horizontal drill hole located
just above the shaft base Subsurface seepage water is intercepted by these large
drainage columns and is transmitted by gravity to a convenient draw off point The
array geometry can be readily modified and adapted to the geology structure and
permeability of the unstable mass revealed during construction
Alternately a trench drain system extending through the adobe clay layer may be
installed at selected locations across the project site for subterranean seepage
interception The trench drain should be daylighted to a suitable outlet for
drainage disposal Due to the anticipated depth of excavation the trench drain
would probably have to be constructed in small sections to reduce the potential for
instability due to excavation
Concrete interceptor ditches should be planned along the upper boundaries of the
project site for surface run off interception The ditch should be designed to collect
concentrated flow where gullies or drainageways are crossed This is important in
order to prevent surface run off from jumping over the ditch and onto the slope
below Due to the high shrink swell potential of the adobe clay soils heaving and
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 4220 00 Page 19
cracking of the interceptor ditch would occur if it were placed directly on the
adobe clay soil Therefore a non expansive cushion layer is recommended
around and below the concrete ditch to reduce heaving of the underlying
expansive soils Because of the anticipated deformation due to continuous
creeping action of the adobe clay mass a flexible lining should be provided in the
ditch construction
The drainage measures recommended would probably help to reduce the lateral
ground movements by reducing additional driving force from the water However
it should be noted that implementation of these drainage measures alone may not
completely arrest the downhill creep movement Other site grading and slope
stabilization measures mentioned might need to be incorporated and implemented
in the design and construction of the planned development
Existing Fill Slope
The conditions of the existing fill slope bordering the Kealaula Kai subdivision
were explored by drilling and sampling a total of three borings designated as Boring
Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about 18 to 21 feet below the existing ground
surface at the locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the drilled borings it appears
that the existing fill slope was constructed with compacted fill materials consisting of stiff
clayey soils overlying dense coral detritus material at greater depths We believe that the
underlying coral detritus fill material may be obtained from the dredging of the nearby
Kuapa Pond The fill slope was then capped off with clayey soils obtained from the valley
floor to the west of the future Hawaii Kai Drive Extension
Based on our visual observation field exploration results and our engineering
analyses we believe that the existing fill slope should be stable against potential slope
failures provided that the existing fill slope will not be subjected to substantial fill and or
structural loads Considerations should be given to flatten the existing slope grade to
W O 4220 00 Page 20
increase the factor of safety against potential slope failures Upon finalizing the site
grading concept for the future planned development a detailed slope stability analyses
should be conducted for the existing fill slope during the design level of the project
development
Falling Boulder Hazard
Our site reconnaissance indicated that boulders up to about 8 feet in diameter
were observed scattered randomly over the ground surface where they have landed as
they rolled down from the upslope areas The falling boulders are general phenomena
occurring in all ridges on the island of Oahu Various methods of boulder barriers or
catchments can be used to reduce the hazard of falling boulders and the degree of safety
is a function of economics For this site construction of a boulder catchment is
recommended A rockfall catchment ditch should be placed at the toe of the slope to
collect falling rocks and debris Guidelines from FHWA indicate that the ditch should be
about 25 feet wide for a slope height over 100 feet
