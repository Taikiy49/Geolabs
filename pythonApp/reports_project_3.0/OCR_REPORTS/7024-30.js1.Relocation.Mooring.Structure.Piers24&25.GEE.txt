PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS

Oahu, Hawaii. The project involves the construction of a large drydock at Piers 24 and 
25, with dimensions of about 444 feet long and 141 feet wide. Based on the current 
design concepts, the new drydock structure will be secured to mooring structures 
consisting of 36-inch diameter steel pipe piles with a wall thickness of 1 inch, installed at 
two locations along Pier 24. The new mooring structures will be mounted on the existing 
pier bulkhead with steel brackets. The project location and general vicinity are shown on

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age. The rocks of the Waianae Volcanic Series are divided into Lower, 
Middle and Upper Members.  The lavas of the Lower Member consist largely of thinly 
bedded pahoehoe flows whereas the Middle Member consists of interbedded pahoehoe 
and a`a flows with a higher occurrence of the a`a form.  The Upper Member consists 
primarily of massive a`a lava flows. 
Erosion deeply dissected the Waianae Shield removing much of the western 
slope of the dome resulting in broad amphitheater headed valleys. During the 
Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of continental 
glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill the 
oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.  When the 
glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of 
the calcareous reef materials. 
The project site generally is underlain by deposits of calcareous sediments and 
alluvium. The deposition of soils at the site is likely associated with the nearby Nuuanu 
Stream, lagoonal deposits from the Honolulu Harbor, and coralline formation. A surface

FIELD EXPLORATION 
The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous 
flight augers and/or rotary coring tools. Our geologist classified the materials 
encountered in the borings by visual and textural examination in the field in general 
accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for Description and Identification of 
Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These 
classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were 
classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification 
of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the 
Soil Log Legend, Plate 3.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with 
ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate 3.2. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered in the drilled borings are presented on the 
Logs of Borings, Plates 4.1 through 5.3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550/D3550M, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate 3.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” 
by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material 
is recovered from a 5-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be 
shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe 
the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of 
Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

The excavation characteristic of a rock mass is a function of the relative 
hardness of the rock, its relative quality, brittleness, and fissile characteristics. A dense 
rock formation with a high RQD value would be very difficult to excavate and probably 
would require more arduous methods of excavation.

Laboratory testing was performed on selected soil samples obtained during the 
field exploration as an aid in classifying the materials and evaluating their engineering 
properties. The tests performed are described below.

samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
The project site is located at Piers 24 and 25 of the Honolulu Harbor on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The proposed large drydock and mooring structures will be 
mounted on the existing Pier 24 bulkhead. At the time of our field exploration, the area 
of the Honolulu Harbor within the vicinity of the proposed drydock structure was vacant. 
The existing Piers 24 and 25 ground surface adjacent to the proposed drydock was 
generally level and paved with concrete and/or asphaltic concrete overlay. 
Our field exploration program for the project consisted of drilling and sampling a 
total of two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 201 and 202, to depths of about 95 and 
96 feet below the existing ground surface.  The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our borings generally encountered the existing pavement sections consisting of 
4 inches of asphaltic concrete in Boring No. 201 and 6 inches of concrete pavement in 
Boring No. 202. The pavement sections were generally underlain by surface fills and 
harbor/lagoonal deposits over coralline reef formations. 
The surface fills were comprised of very loose to medium dense clayey sands 
and gravels generally extending to depths of about 4 to 6.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. Very loose to medium dense clayey and silty sand lagoonal deposits 
were encountered below the surface fills, extending to about 6 to 12.5 feet below the 
ground surface.  The lagoonal deposits were underlain by medium hard coral formation 
and medium dense coralline detritus, extending to about 88.5 to 95 feet below the 
ground surface. Below the coralline deposits, stiff silty clay and older alluvium were 
encountered, extending to the maximum depth explored of about 96 feet below the 
existing ground surface. 
We encountered groundwater in the borings at depths between about 5 and 
5.3 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. It should

MOORING STRUCTURE FOUNDATION 
We understand it is proposed to install a mooring pile foundation system consisting 
of 36-inch diameter steel pipe piles with a wall thickness of 1 inch at two locations along 
Pier 24 to support the new drydock structure. In general, the steel pipe pile system will 
absorb the energy imparted by the drydock structure. Therefore, the lateral load capacity 
of the steel pipe pile is the primary design consideration for the mooring foundation 
system. 
Based on the loading information provided, we understand the maximum lateral 
load from the drydock structure imposed on a single 36-inch outside diameter (OD) steel 
pipe pile under drydock submerge condition is approximately 43 kips, acting between 
elevations +1.2 and -18.5 feet MSL. In addition, lateral loads under the extreme event 
conditions, such as heavy weather and earthquake, are 639 and 1,380 kips, 
respectively, acting between elevations +14.7 and +1.2 feet MSL. 
Based on the mooring foundation design diagram provided, we understand that the 
steel pipe piles will be mounted on the existing pier bulkhead at about elevations +8 to 
+2 feet MSL with the top elevation at +17.5 feet MSL. Mudline was measured at about 
elevation -29.5 feet MSL. We envision that majority of the lateral loads induced under the 
extreme event conditions will be absorbed by the existing pier bulkhead. Design 
consideration was given to the steel pipe pile resisting the operational lateral loads under 
the submerge drydock condition.  
Based on the results of our subsurface investigation and lateral load analysis, we 
believe that the 36-inch steel pipe pile should be installed to a tip elevation of 
approximately -70.5 feet MSL with a total length of 88 feet as summarized in the table 
below, to provide sufficient lateral resistance supporting the new drydock structure.

Lateral Load Analyses 
Based on the information provided, the mooring piles will be mounted on the 
existing Pier 24 bulkhead with a steel bracket roughly between elevations +8 and 
+2 feet MSL. Therefore, we envision that lateral loads induced under the extreme event 
conditions (acting at elevations between +14.7 and +1.2 feet MSL) will be primarily 
absorbed by the existing Pier 24 bulkhead at each pipe pile location. Based on the 
pinned boundary condition at the steel bracket, the mooring pipe pile foundation design 
was determined to resist the operational lateral loads under the submerge drydock 
condition, where loading will be applied between elevations of +1.2 to -18.5 feet MSL. 
Our analyses are based on a 35-inch diameter steel pipe pile, discounting the 36-inch 
OD steel pipe pile with 0.5-inch wall thickness due to corrosion. 
The lateral load resistance of driven piles is a function of the stiffness of the 
surrounding soil, the stiffness of the pile, allowable deflection at the top of the pile, and 
the induced moment in the pile.  The lateral load analyses were performed using the 
program LPILE-plus for Windows, which is a microcomputer adaptation of a finite 
difference, laterally loaded pile program.  The program solves for a deflection and 
bending moment along a pile under lateral loads as a function of the depth.  The 
analysis was carried out with the use of non-linear “p-y” curves to represent soil moduli. 
Lateral load analyses were performed for the drydock operation submerge 
condition on the mooring pipe piles with a pinned pile head boundary condition. To ease 
pile driving operation, we recommend utilizing an open-ended pipe pile. We also 
recommend concrete in-fill for the upper portion of the pipe pile where there is no soil 
plug, to increase the mooring pile stiffness effectively. In our analysis, we have 
assumed the pile will be filled with concrete [minimum compressive strength of 
5,000 pounds per square inch (psi) @ 28 days] from 10 feet below the mudline 
(-39.5 feet MSL) up to the top of the pile (+17.5 feet MSL). Lateral analysis results are

Axial Compressive Load Capacity 
Static pile compression load analyses were conducted to calculate the axial 
compressive load capacity at each mooring pile location and to confirm the selected pile 
length to resist the maximum axial reaction. 
Based on the results of the static pile compression load analyses, we believe that 
the selected 36-inch OD open-ended steel pipe piles with 1 inch wall thickness and 
partial concrete in-fill installed to the recommended tip elevations should provide 
sufficient capacity to support the maximum axial reaction force of about 200 kips with a 
factor of safety of 2.4 under heavy weather conditions. 
Steel Pipe Pile Corrosion Analysis 
Although the above-mentioned analyses are primarily based on a 36-inch 
diameter pipe pile with 1-inch thick steel by discounting the 0.5-inch thick steel pipe 
(due to corrosion), it is desired to evaluate the corrosion rate of the steel pipe pile 
considering one of the most severe (highly aggressive) corrosion environments. 
In general, the rate of corrosion is greater in the first few years of burial and then 
levels off to a steady but significantly lower rate (refer to the “Durability/Corrosion of Soil 
Reinforced Structures,” Publication No. FHWA-RD-89-186, December 1990). The 
categories of corrosion environments are generally rated based on the site 
characteristics, such as soil pH, resistivity, chloride contents, and sulfate contents, etc. 
One set of laboratory corrosion tests, including Chloride Content and Sulfate 
Content, was performed by TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. to evaluate the corrosivity of 
the on-site soils encountered.  Levels of the chloride content and sulfate content of the 
on-site soils encountered near the existing mudline are moderately high on the order of 
1,700 and 310 parts per million (ppm), respectively. Results of the soil analyses 
conducted on the selected soil sample is summarized on Plate B-12. 
Based on our experience, we believe that the project site may be classified as a 
highly aggressive corrosion environment. To be conservative, we based our corrosion 
analysis on bare carbon steel that would be in direct contact with a highly corrosive
