INTRODUCTION
In general accordance with our revised fee proposal dated June 29th we have
provided geotechnical engineering support for the evaluation of the structural distress
Magazine NAVMAG Lualualei site in the West Loch area on the island of Oahu
Hawaii This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering findings derived from our evaluation
of the subject project
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located between Main Street and H Avenue in the U S
Naval Magazine Lualualei site in the West Loch area on the island of Oahu Hawaii
The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location
Map Plate 1
The development included four one story buildings MK 50 Torpedo Maintenance
Facility Inert Storehouse Facility Ready Service Magazine and Electrical
Substation Generator Building Other improvements included installation of a septic
tank and leaching field asphalt concrete parking area and concrete pavements and

the information provided extensive cracking has been observed in the concrete slab

cracking from a geotechnical engineering point of view

FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS
Based on our field observations and testing performed for the project it is our
Opinion that the cracked slabs observed at the site may be the result of one or more
of the following
1 Potential swelling of the underlying moderately expansive subgrade soils
It should be noted that according to the recommendations presented in
the soils report prepared by Harding Lawson Associates the subgrade
soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 6 inches moisture
conditioned and compacted with a vibratory roller to 90 percent relative
compaction Itis our opinion that the intent of the moisture conditioning
recommended in the soils report is not properly reflected in the project
specifications Based on the in place density test results performed by
Pacific Testing Laboratories Inc presented on Plate 3 the subgrade soils
were not properly moisture conditioned with moisture contents significantly
dry of the optimum moisture content of 22 5 percent about 1 5 to 18 3
percent dry of optimum The subgrade soils were subsequently wetted
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3123 00 D Page 9
with existing moisture contents ranging between 18 to 26 percent These
large fluctuations of moisture contents would result in swelling of the
subgrade soils and subsequent cracking of the slab
2 Inadequate compaction of the base course layer as well as the majority
of the underlying controlled fill material as indicated by the field density
test results performed during our field exploration This was also visually
observed and confirmed with the relatively easy excavation of the base
course and controlled fill materials during our test pit excavations
3 Improper placement of the wire mesh for the 7 inch thick concrete slab
resulting in shrinkage cracking of the slab Wire mesh was found to be
placed at the bottom one third of the slab instead of the top one third of
the slab as indicated on the plans This will need to be further evaluated
by a Structural Engineer
Detailed discussions of these probable cause s are presented in the following
subsections of this report
Expansive Subgrade Soils
Laboratory swell tests were performed on the subgrade soils encountered below
the base course and controlled fill layer to evaluate the swelling characteristics and
potential impact on the performance of the concrete slabs
Four laboratory swell tests were performed on bulk samples of the subgrade
soils encountered in the excavated test pits to evaluate the potential swelling
characteristics of the subgrade soils The four samples were compacted to about
90 percent relative compaction based on the proctor test results performed on the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3123 00 D Page 10
subgrade soils at about the existing moisture contents Three of the samples were air
dried overnight and then saturated for 24 hours under a surcharge load of 55 pounds
per square foot p s f The fourth sample was tested at the existing moisture content
under a surcharge load of 110 p s f Results of our laboratory swell tests are presented
on Plate B 1 of Appendix B
Based on the laboratory swell test results presented on Plate B 1 the subgrade
soils generally have a low shrink swell potential when subjected to the moisture
fluctuations indicated with an initial molding moisture content of about 22 to 23 percent
near the optimum moisture content of 22 5 percent However our review of the
compaction tests performed during construction provided to us after completion of our
laboratory testing program indicated that the moisture contents of the subgrade soils
during subgrade preparation are generally between 7 and 13 percent 9 5 to 15 5
percent below optimum moisture content We were not able to perform additional
swell tests to further evaluate the swelling potential of the subgrade soils due to lack
of soil samples However based on the laboratory CBR tests performed by Pacific
Geotechnical Engineers Inc it appears that the subgrade soils may be moderately
expansive when placed at moisture contents between about 8 and 10 percent and
allowed to saturate and swell Therefore it is our opinion that swelling of the
underlying moderately expansive subgrade soils may have contributed to the observed
slab distress at the subject site
Considering the moderately expansive nature of the subgrade soils moisture
conditioning of the subgrade soils to above the optimum moisture content during
construction should reduce the swelling potential of the subgrade soils Based on the
compaction tests performed during construction moisture conditioning of the subgrade
soils to above the optimum moisture was not done However the project specifications
did not require the contractor to moisture condition the subgrade soils to above the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3123 00 D Page 11
optimum moisture The requirements for moisture conditioning in the project
specification Section 02221 Subsection Subgrade Preparation are as follows
Moisten or aerate material as necessary to provide the moisture content
that will readily facilitate obtaining the specified compaction with the
equipment used
Based on our review of the project soils report by HLA and the project
specifications the intent of the moisture conditioning of the subgrade soils
recommended by HLA was not properly reflected in the project specifications In
addition it appears that there was a modification to the moisture conditioning
requirement by HLA between their original report dated page 4
of Consultation Letter by PGE and their revised report dated The
original report dated by HLA was not available for our review
Based on our field exploration the existing moisture contents of the subgrade
soils were tested between 18 and 26 percent Therefore we believe that the majority
of the swelling of the subgrade soils has already occurred under the existing moisture
contents which are near or wet of the optimum moisture content Therefore the
potential for future cracking of the slab caused by additional swelling of the subgrade
soils should be low based on our swell test results However there may be areas
where the subgrade moisture contents are still significantly dry of the optimum moisture
content Therefore additional swelling of the subgrade soils and cracking of the slab
may occur in the future when wetted to moisture contents near or wet of optimum
moisture
Inadequate Compaction
Samples of the base course controlled fill and subgrade soils were obtained for
laboratory testing to determine their maximum dry densities and optimum moisture

W O 3123 00 D Page 12
contents The results of these tests are summarized on Plate B 2 Graphic

In situ density tests using both the sand cone method and nuclear density gauge
equipment were performed on each exposed layer to evaluate the degree of
compaction The in place dry density and moisture contents performed included Test
Nos 1 through 16 performed on the base course layer Test Nos 17 through 37 on the
controlled fill and Test Nos 38 through 67 on the subgrade soils The test results are
presented on the Summary of Field Density Test Results Plates C 1 through C 7 of
Appendix C
Our in situ density tests performed on the subgrade soils indicated that the
degree of compaction range from about 80 to 100 percent relative compaction with a
majority of the test results around 90 percent relative compaction Based on the test
results it is our opinion that the subgrade soils were generally compacted to the degree
of compaction recommended in the project soils report by HLA
For the base course material our field density test results indicated that the
degree of compaction varies from about 76 to 92 percent with a majority of the in place
field density test results in the high 70 s and low 80 s The degree of compaction for
the controlled fill encountered in Test Pit Nos 2 and 3 also varies from 72 to 82 percent
with a majority of the test results in the high 70 s and low 80 s The controlled fill
appeared to be properly compacted in Test Pit No 1
A review of the project soils report selected plans and portions of the
specifications indicates that the compaction requirement for the controlled fill are in
conflict with one another with the minimum degree of compaction varying between 95
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3123 00 D Page 13
and 100 percent Based on a less stringent compaction requirement of 95 percent our
field density test results indicate that the base course and controlled fill materials are
still significantly lower than the required degree of compaction and could be considered
poorly compacted However based on the test results by the Contractor Quality
Control CQC representative Pacific Testing Laboratories Inc the base course and
controlled fill materials were compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
The proctor values used by Pacific Testing Laboratories Inc to determine the
degree of compaction of the base course and controlled fill materials during the project
construction are significantly lower than those obtained by our office and by Pacific
Geotechnical Engineers Inc Use of these proctor curves with lower maximum dry
density values have resulted in test results with higher degree of compaction ranging
between 95 and 107 percent relative compaction thus appearing to have met the
minimum compaction requirements for the project construction A summary of the in
place density tests performed by Pacific Testing Laboratories Inc on the subgrade
soils is presented on Plate 3 However based on the results of the field exploration
conducted by our office and by PGE the base course and controlled fill materials have
not been compacted to the minimum requirements of the project specifications
In general we believe that the contribution of inadequate compaction of the base
course and the underlying controlled fill to the cracked slab should be minor or
secondary This belief is based on our assumption that the base course and controlled
fill are subjected to only minor static loads weight of slab and vehicular traffic
dymanic loads However we believe that additional slab cracking in the future would
occur due to slab settlement caused by densification of the underlying base course and
controlled fill when the slab is subjected to vehicular traffic such as from the use of
forklifts and other heavy equipment We estimate that the potential slab settlement
under such dynamic loading could be on the order of inch from its current elevation

Construction of New Concrete Slab
Due to the potential for further slab cracking when the slab on grade is subjected
to dynamic loading from forklifts or heavy equipment usage we recommend that the
existing slab be replaced with a properly designed and constructed concrete slab
Particular attention should be given to the design and construction of the new slab with
respect to the observations and findings presented above
Based on the field density test results it is our opinion that the base course and
controlled fill below the existing concrete slab have not been adequately compacted
and would not perform as intended when subjected to vehicular traffic such as forklifts
We recommend that the existing base course and controlled fill materials within the
proposed new slab area be completely removed to expose the underlying subgrade
soils After removal of the overlying base course and controlled fill materials the
existing subgrade should be scarified to a minimum depth of 6 inches properly
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
recompacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density as determined by ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
We believe that the excavated base course and controlled fill materials if not
contaminated with the underlying silty clayey sand soils may be reused as a source
of controlled fill material underneath the new base course layer The controlled fill
should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted
to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
We recommend that new base course material be imported for the new
replacement concrete slab The base course supporting the concrete slab should
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3123 00 D Page 15
consist of crushed basalt aggregates compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
Care should be exercised by the Contractor during placement of the steel
reinforcement to ensure proper clearance from the slab bottom as indicated on the
plans

variations may not become evident until reconstruction is underway If variations then
appear evident it will be necessary to reevaluate the recommendations provided in this
report

been estimated by taping from features shown on the Grading Plan prepared by Upham

plan The physical locations and elevations of the field test pits should be considered
accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used
Water level data from the test pits were measured at the times shown on the
graphic representations and or in the text of this report These data have been
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report However it must
be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in rainfall tides temperature and
other factors

APPENDIX C
Summary of Field Density Test Results
In place density tests were performed on the base course controlled fill and
subgrade soils in each of the three test pits to evaluate the degrees of compaction of
the existing materials The tests were performed in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1556 Sand Cone Method and D 2922 Nuclear Method The test
results are summarized on Plates C 1 through C 7
In general a minimum of four density tests were taken at each excavated level
in the test pits Density tests were taken at the corners of the test pit using nuclear
density gauge equipment One density test was taken at the center of the test pit using
the Sand Cone Method
