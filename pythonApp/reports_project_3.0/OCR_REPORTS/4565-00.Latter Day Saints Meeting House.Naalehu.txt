PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
A seismic evaluation is to be conducted for the existing Latter Day Saints meeting
house located at 92 8357 Marlin Road adjacent to Mamalahoa Highway in the Town of
Naalehu in the District of Ka u on the Island of Hawaii The general location of the project site

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes including the following Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea some activity during
recent geologic times Hualalai last erupted in 1801 and is considered dormant and
Mauna Loa and Kilauea both still active The project site is situated on the southeast flank
of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano

The volume of Mauna Loa extending from the ocean floor is estimated to be on the
order of about 10 000 cubic miles making it possibly the largest volcanic mountain on earth
Furthermore it is estimated from current lava production that Mauna Loa may have been
forming for the past 1 to 2 million years Mauna Loa is actually composed of two separate
shield volcanoes Mauna Loa and Ninole The latter having been covered by Mauna Loa
lavas left only a few localized surface expressions visible at present The Mauna Loa
shield has been built by volcanic eruptions along two principal rift zones that extend
southwestward and east northeastward from the summit caldera
The majority of the surface rock exposures at Mauna Loa consist of lavas and
volcanic deposits belonging to the Ka u Volcanic Series Pleistocene and Holocene age
and the older Kahuku Volcanic Series Pleistocene age In addition a regional ash
deposit identified as Pahala Ash Pleistocene age forms a soil mantle overlying and
interbedded within the rocks of the Ka u Volcanic Series The ash deposits typically occur
as kipukas isolated deposits with some significant accumulations ranging up to about
30 feet in thickness
Pahala Ash is a low plasticity silt derived from the weathering of ash cinder and
Pele s Hair It generally has low shear strength and when the moisture content is high
enough it becomes thixotrophic i e it loses strength when remolded Therefore the
typical Pahala Ash may be potentially liquefiable during seismic events However it should
be noted that the volcanic ash soils encountered at the project site appear to be different
from the typical Pahala Ash in that the moisture content of the silty clays and clayey silts
encountered at the site possess lower moisture contents less than about 80 percent
The project site is located on land that has been mapped by others as Pahala Ash
covering most of the ground surface as shown on the Geologic Map Plate 3 Beneath
the surface ash layer rocks of the Ka u Volcanic Series may be anticipated within about
100 feet below the existing ground surface The Ka u Volcanic Series essentially consists
of tholeiitic basalt Based on a review of previous geotechnical data in the vicinity of the
project site the basalt rock underlying the project site generally consists of pahoehoe type
lava with a high incidence of embedded lava tubes
Pahoehoe basalt rock is generally the result of thinner more fluid lava flows The
feeding river lobes of a pahoehoe flow crust over to develop a continuous roof leaving the
interior lava stream to flow through a tunnel of its own making These remnant flow tunnels
or voids are known as lava tubes The lava tubes feed the advancing lava flow front and
margins As a result the lava tube may branch or converge generally in a downhill
direction to maintain the lava flow In the waning stages of an eruption the lava may drain
from the main tube system leaving open tunnels ranging from several feet to over 40 feet
in diameter The open tubes resemble tunnels with arched roofs and nearly flat floors The
main lava tubes may extend downslope for several miles occasionally reaching all the way
to the coastline

Another type of void space that is typically encountered in pahoehoe rock is a pocket
blister These blisters are typically isolated closed void structures formed by trapped
volcanic gases within the flow interior Some blisters may extend a short distance downhill
before pinching out and terminating
The surface of pahoehoe flows are generally smoother than their rough and irregular
counterpart the a a flow A a flows form from generally more viscous slower moving lavas
and are expressed by a solid rock core sandwiched between an upper and lower less
dense clinker layer The pahoehoe flow surface may be expressed by broad
dome shaped hillocks created by the heaving and buckling of the flow crust as it
approaches obstructions
FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface conditions at the meeting house site were explored by drilling and
sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about
16 5 to 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings
drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted
drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate 4 Graphic representations of the materials encountered in the
borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates 5 through 7
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by

LIQUEFACTION POTENTIAL
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore water
pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In this process
GEOLABS INC
The Church of Jesus Christ Page 6
of Latter Day Saints
W O 4565 00
when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such as during an
earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an increase in pore
water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build up of pore
water pressure the effective stress internal strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained
vibration the pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially
reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid During
liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical
movements and if not confined will result in significant deformations
Soils that are most susceptible to liquefaction consist of loose uniformly graded
fine grained sands and loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that
liquefaction will not occur if the deposit is greater than 50 feet below the ground surface In
deeper deposits the greater overburden pressure is generally sufficient to prevent liquefaction
from occurring The major factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are
as follows
1 Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more susceptible
to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative density
3 Magnitude _and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake
Based on our field exploration the project site is underlain by very stiff to hard cohesive
soils with lower moisture contents as compared to the typical Pahala Ash These near
surface soils are underlain by volcanic rock at greater depths Therefore we believe that the
potential for liquefaction at this project site is low based on the types of subsurface materials
encountered

GEOLOGIC HAZARD EVALUATION
The project site is located on the lower southeastern flank of the Mauna Loa Volcano
in the Town of Naalehu on the Island of is considered to be an active
shield volcano that has erupted sporadically with lava flows in recent and historic time Some
historic lava flows dated 1868 erupted from Mauna Loa and are located approximately
8 miles to the southwest with respect to the project site Other lava flows that erupted in the
early 1900s are located further upslope and northwesterly of the project site Mauna Loa is
anticipated to erupt again in the near future although the location and magnitude of the
potential activity cannot be predicted
The project site is located approximately 10 miles westerly of the southwesterly
trending principal rift zone which extends from the summit of Mauna Loa The rift zone is an
elongated region of volcanic activity from which eruptive activity is more commonly
concentrated
Based on a review of available geologic information pertaining to the Town of Naalehu
and vicinity the project site is underlain by weathered volcanic ash deposits that are believed
to be part of the Pahala Ash Pleistocene Epoch regional ash deposits The volcanic ash is
exposed at the ground surface where younger lava flows have not completely buried the once
extensive surface ash deposits The volcanic ash deposited in the vicinity of the Town of
Naalehu is believed to be older than about 31 000 years in age based on age dating
conducted on an overlying lava flow The volcanic ash is believed to be on the order of about
20 to 30 feet thick and overlies the older basalt rock of the Kahuku Volcanic Series
Geologic maps of the Naalehu area indicate the presence of multiple normal faults that
have been identified by previous research and geologic mapping efforts performed by others
The Waiohinu Fault is located approximately 1 5 miles westerly of the project site in the vicinity
of Waiohinu as shown on the Geologic Map Plate 3 The regional Honuapo Kaoiki Fault
System is suspected to be located approximately 5 miles toward the east and southeast of the
project site This regional fault system consists of a series of echelon tangential faults along
which the seaward side has moved relatively downward The step faulting has produced a
series of terraces and scarps that extend for significant horizontal distances
In the vicinity of the project site other studies have mapped many smaller unnamed
faults that have been approximately located at the surface The presence of potential fault
systems may sometimes be identified at the ground surface where abrupt changes in
topographic character are observed An unnamed surface fault appears to be located within
Close proximity of the project site as shown on the Geologic Map Plate 3 Based on our

Bearing Capacity of Shallow Footings
We envision that the foundations of the existing meeting house are bearing on the
near surface soils encountered in the borings drilled for the project Based on the stiff to very
stiff soil conditions encountered at the project site we believe that an allowable bearing
pressure of 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for evaluation of the shallow
spread footings bearing on the in situ stiff clayey soils This bearing pressure is for evaluating
sustained loads and may be increased by up to 100 percent for transient loads such as those
caused by seismic forces or other temporary loading conditions
Lateral Load Resistance of Shallow Footings
Lateral loads acting on the building structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of
friction of 0 30 may be used for footings bearing on the stiff clayey soils encountered in the
borings Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soils
around the footings are well compacted or relatively undisturbed Unless covered by
pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
Based on a footing embedment depth of about 10 feet or less below the existing ground
surface the frictional and passive pressure components of the lateral load resistance of the
footings may be used in combination It should be noted that the passive earth pressure for
foundations located on slopes should be reduced
