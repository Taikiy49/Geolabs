DRAINAGE STRUCTURES 
Based on the information provided, we understand drainage structures are 
planned along the new roadways for the project. Based on the subsurface conditions 
encountered, the following general guidelines may be used for design of the drainage 
structures at the project site. 
Drainage Structure Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, an allowable bearing pressure of 
up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be utilized for design of the drainage 
structures bearing on the stiff residual soils at the project site. To provide uniform 
bearing support for the drainage structure, we recommend providing a 6-inch thick layer of 
No. 3B Fine Gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the bottom of the structure.

Backfilling of Drainage Structure Excavations 
The drainage structure excavations will need to be properly backfilled. The 
excavated on-site soils or imported fill materials that are free of vegetation, deleterious 
materials, and clay lumps and rock fragments greater than 3 inches in maximum 
dimension may be used as backfill above the cushion layer up to the finish grades.  
Backfills using on-site soils should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Where drainage 
structures are located below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the drain box backfill 
below the pavement grade should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum 
dry density.  
THRUST BLOCKS 
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide 
resistance to the thrust forces due to the pressurized water line pipes. The thrust force 
acting on the pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of 
the thrust block.  
Based on the available information, we anticipate the inverts of the planned water 
lines and thrust blocks will be about 5 to 8 feet below the existing ground surface. We 
anticipate most of the trench excavations will likely encounter surficial fill consisting of 
medium dense gravelly sand and/or very stiff to hard silty clay/clayey silt and residual soils 
consisting of very stiff to hard silty clay. We recommend using lateral bearing pressures of 
up to 1,500 pounds per square foot (psf) for the design of thrust blocks bearing on the 
stiff/medium dense fill materials and/or the stiff residual soils underlying the site.

Vertical Load Resistance 
Based on the structural load demands provided and the subsoil conditions 
encountered at the project site, we recommend that the 30-inch diameter drilled shafts for 
the new street light pole structures have a minimum embedment length of 5 feet below the 
design finished grade. In addition, we recommend that the 36 and 42-inch diameter drilled 
shaft foundations for the new traffic signal pole structures have minimum embedment 
lengths of 8 and 12 feet, respectively, below the design finished grade. 
The cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support primarily from 
skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils. Net allowable compressive load 
capacities of up to 21 kips per shaft may be used for the 30-inch diameter drilled shafts 
supporting the street light pole structures and up to 41 and 72 kips per shaft may be used 
for the 36 and 42-inch diameter drilled shafts, respectively, supporting the traffic signal 
pole structures.

surrounding soil, the stiffness of the drilled shaft, allowable deflection at the top of the 
drilled shaft, and the induced moment in the drilled shaft. The lateral load analyses were 
performed using the program LPILE for Windows, which is a microcomputer adaptation of 
a finite difference, laterally loaded deep foundation program originally developed at the

Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations 
In general, the performance of drilled shafts depends significantly upon the 
contractor's method of installation and construction procedures. The following conditions 
would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft 
foundations. 
The axial load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend, to a significant extent, on 
the friction between the shaft and the surrounding soils. Therefore, proper construction 
techniques especially during the drilling operations are important. The contractor should 
exercise care in drilling the shaft hole and in placing concrete into the drilled hole. 
It should be noted that cobbles and boulders may be encountered within the depth 
of the drilled shaft, although they were not encountered in the borings drilled. Therefore, 
some difficult drilling conditions may be expected. The drilled shaft subcontractor should 
have the appropriate equipment and tools to drill through these types of natural 
obstructions, where encountered. The drilled shaft subcontractor should demonstrate that 
the proposed drilling equipment will be capable of installing the drilled shaft to the 
recommended depth and dimension. 
Based on the estimated length of the drilled shafts and the boring logs, 
groundwater is generally not anticipated in the drilled holes during shaft installation. 
However, concrete placement by tremie methods is recommended during construction of 
the drilled shafts in lieu of free fall methods. This is to reduce the potential for concrete 
from striking the steel reinforcement cage or shaft sidewalls during concrete placement.  
A low-shrink concrete mix with high slump (7 to 9-inch slump range) should be used 
to provide close contact between the drilled shaft and the surrounding soils. The concrete 
should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for segregation of the 
aggregates from the concrete mix. In addition, the concrete should be placed promptly 
after drilling (within 24 hours after drilling of the holes) to reduce the potential for softening 
of the sides of the drilled hole. 
It is imperative for a Geolabs representative to be present at the project site to 
observe the drilling and installation of drilled shaft during construction. Although the drilled 
shaft is designed based primarily on skin friction, the bottom of the drilled hole should be 
relatively free of loose materials prior to placement of concrete.

pavement analyses for the subgrade conditions along the proposed roadway alignments.

Site Preparation Below Pavement Areas 
We anticipate that the majority of the new roadway pavements will be 
constructed at or near the existing grades or in a cut condition. In general, the area 
within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed at the 
on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other unsuitable 
materials should be removed and properly disposed of off-site or disposed of in a 
designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials.  
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over-excavated to expose 
stiff and/or dense materials. The resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
aggregate base course materials. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be 
properly disposed of off-site.  
After clearing and grubbing, the pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a 
depth of at least 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture content, and compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction.

Fill Materials Below Pavement Areas 
In general, the excavated on-site materials with particles no larger than 3 inches 
may be re-used as a source of general fill material. Imported fill materials, if required, 
should consist of non-expansive select granular material, such as crushed coralline or 
basaltic materials. The materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles 
no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The materials should have a laboratory 
CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less. 
Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance with these recommendations 
prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
Aggregate base course should meet the material requirements for Aggregate 
Base Course as specified in Section 31 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works 
Construction, City & County of Honolulu, September 1986. Geolabs should test 
imported fill materials for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to 
the project site for the intended use. 
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fill and backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 
8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. The aggregate base course should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basaltic 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and

Pavement Drainage
The recommended pavement sections assume that adequate drainage will be
provided for the paved areas which should be sloped and drainage gradients
maintained to carry surface water off the pavements to appropriate drainage structures.
Surface water ponding should not be allowed on-site during or after construction. When
concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas, we
recommend extending the curbs a minimum of 2inches into the soils below the
aggregate base layer to reduce the potential for migration of excessive landscape water
into the pavement section. Alternatively, a subdrain system could be constructed to
collect the excess water from landscaping irrigation. For long-term performance, we
recommend constructing a subdrain system adjacent to the paved/landscaped areas.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Ka Uka Boulevard Widening project located in Waipio, 
Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general site vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the proposed project. These recommendations are intended for the design of 
pavements, earthwork, and utilities only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.  
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project involves widening and making improvements to the existing Ka Uka 
Boulevard between Moaniani Street and the central part of the Gentry Waipio Industrial 
area in the Waipio area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The improvements will include 
installation of turn out areas along the medial strip and moving the existing overhead 
utility lines to underground ducts. The project area extends an estimated 2,200 linear 
feet along Ka Uka Boulevard.  
Traffic information was provided for Ka Uka Boulevard from a study conducted 
between Ukee Street and Waipio Uka Street in the Year 2014. This data was used in 
our analyses and in forming our pavement design recommendations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau shield is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased, lava flows from Koolau 
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, known as Schofield 
Plateau.  The project site is located on the Schofield Plateau. 
The project area is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lava of the 
Koolau Volcanic Series.  These rocks are generally characterized by flows of jointed 
dense vesicular basalt rock formation with inter-bedded thin clinker layers.  In-situ 
chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million 
years.  The weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils, which grade to 
saprolite with depth.  In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is 
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks.  The saprolite grades to basaltic rock 
formation with increasing depth.  
2.1 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project is located along the existing Ka Uka Boulevard between Moaniani 
Street and the central part of the Gentry Waipio Industrial area in the Waipio area on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. Ka Uka Boulevard is a four-lane divided roadway with a grassed 
median. The roadway connects Interstate Route H-2 (east of project site) with 
Kamehameha Highway (west of project site). The existing Ka Uka Boulevard slopes 
downward, toward Kamehameha Highway, with existing ground surface elevations of 
about +450 to +483 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).

Methodology of Pavement Design 
For rigid pavement construction, roadway closures will be required to allow time 
for the concrete pavement to cure and harden. Therefore, we recommend using a 
flexible pavement section for the new pavements for the widening project to reduce the

Research Branch. The pavement design methodology is based on the Hveem 
Stabilometer method developed and used by the California Department of

3.2 
Subgrade Preparation Below Pavement Section 
We anticipate that the majority of the new roadway pavements will be 
constructed at or near the existing grades or in a cut condition. In general, the area 
within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed at the 
on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and other unsuitable 
materials should be removed and properly disposed of off-site or disposed of in a 
designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over-excavated to expose 
stiff and/or dense materials. The resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
aggregate subbase materials. The excavated soft and/or organic soils should be 
properly disposed off-site.

After clearing and grubbing, the future pavement areas should be excavated, 
where necessary, to the pavement subgrade level (i.e., bottom of the aggregate 
subbase course layer).  The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of 
at least 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction.  Relative compaction refers 
to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO T 180 (ASTM D1557).  
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade, we 
recommend scarifying and re-preparing the soils as recommended above. Saturation 
and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor 
drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with 
well-compacted fill. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit 
prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the 
over-excavation quantities. 
3.3 
Fill Materials 
In general, the excavated on-site materials with particles no larger than 3 inches 
may be re-used as a source of general fill material.  Imported fill materials, if required, 
should consist of non-expansive select granular material, such as crushed coralline or 
basaltic materials. The materials should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles 
no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 
30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve.  The materials should have a laboratory 
CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less.  
Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance with these recommendations 
prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use.

optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate

3.5 
Pavement Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, special attention 
should be given to the surface drainage of the pavements.  
As mentioned previously, an existing drainage system nor a drainage layer is 
present below portion of the existing pavements at the site. Additional infrastructure 
SECTION 3. DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS  
W.O. 7377-00

work (such as new drain lines and inlets) would be necessary in order to incorporate a 
drainage layer into the new pavements. We believe this would not be feasible nor 
practical and that it would result in a build-up of subsurface water within the drainage 
layer if proper drainage outlets are not provided. The subsurface water likely would 
cause saturated soil conditions, which would cause the pavement subgrade soils to 
soften and result in premature pavement problems.  
Therefore, it is essential to slope the pavement surface adequately and to 
maintain positive drainage gradients at the pavement surface such that surface water 
may be carried off the pavement quickly for proper discharge. Surface water ponding 
should not be allowed on-site during or after construction. 
3.6 
Utility Trenches 
Based on our understanding of the project, new utility lines will be installed. In 
general, good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling 
of the trenches for the proposed utilities. The contractor should determine the method 
and equipment to be used for excavation, subject to practical limits and safety 
considerations. The excavation should comply with the applicable local, state, and 
federal safety requirements. The contractor should be responsible for trench shoring 
design and installation. Trench shoring and bracing also should conform to the 
appropriate health and safety requirements. 
Granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, 
No. 67 gradation) is recommended under the pipes. Free-draining granular materials, 
such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation), should also be used for the 
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support 
around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort on the backfill, thus reducing the 
potential for pipe damage. It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the 
potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate 
support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill material and placement could 
result in backfill settlement and pipe damage.  
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on-site soils (maximum

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
six exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending to a depth of 
about 10 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch O.D. solid stem augers. The borings were 
drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Overall Site Plan, Plate 2 and the Site 
Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.3. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
O.D. standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance 
with ASTM D3550-01 (2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch O.D. 
modified California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to 
drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Please note that the efficiencies of the safety hammers used for this project were 
measured in May 2015 and the resulting energy transfer ratios are shown on the Logs of 
Borings. The soil consistencies and densities presented in the boring logs have been 
adjusted based on the energy transfer ratio, as appropriate.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Three one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to 
evaluate the swelling potential of the soils under surcharge pressures. The tests were 
run on relatively undisturbed and remolded samples. The test results are presented on 
Plate B-1.  
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and 
graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B-2. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results 
are presented on Plate B-3. 
Three Unconfined Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D2166) were performed 
on selected soil samples to evaluate the uniaxial compressive strength of the underlying 
soils.  The test results are presented on Plates B-4 through B-6. 
Three laboratory soaked California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were 
performed on bulk soil samples to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the 
soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-7 through B-9.
