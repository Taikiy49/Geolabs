INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Mauka Makai Walkway Connection project within the existing Salt Lake
District Park in the Salt Lake area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input only and are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As the volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the
Schofield Plateau
The project site is located near the southeastern flank of the Aliamanu vent and on
the northeastern flank of the Salt Lake vent complex of the Honolulu Volcanic Series As
the molten magma ascended through these vents it reacted with the groundwater
generating steam This steam caused explosive eruptions which deposited low cones of
ash material that have consolidated to form tuff locally referred to as mudrock
The Aliamanu tuff is an older water laid ash deposit that was laid down when the
sea level was higher than the present level The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its
material over alluvium originating from Halawa Stream
After this phase of volcanic activity ceased sedimentary deposits accumulated over
the Aliamanu tuff before the next phase of volcanic activity began Then the Salt Lake
eruptions began when the sea level was at about 40 to 60 feet below its present level The
Salt Lake eruptive event deposited its material over earlier tuffs from the older Aliamanu
events

The water that accumulated in the crater was believed to originate from
depressional springs and rainwater During periods of little rainfall the shallow ponded
water was reduced by evaporation and over the years the mineral salt content of the
ponded water increased resulting in the name Salt Lake Recently man has developed
the crater floor by filling and grading the area for residential and golf course purposes

GEOPHYSICAL SURVEY METHODOLOGY
A seismic refraction survey is a geophysical subsurface exploration method which
involves the application of energy to the ground surface at a source location and the
subsequent measurement of the rate of energy transmission in the form of elastic
compression waves passing through the subsurface soil and rock materials In the case of
relatively shallow depth seismic refraction surveys such as those performed for this project
the energy source is provided by the impact of a 12 pound sledgehammer striking the ground
surface A series of geophones are spread in a linear array at varying distances on the
ground surface located with respect to the energy wave source called the shot point The
geophones are essentially sensitive listening devices that detect the first arrival time of the
propagating energy wave as it is refracted through the subsurface materials encountered
A record of the energy wave propagation as detected by the geophone array is recorded
by the seismograph machine This recorded data is used to determine an average wave
velocity for different layers in the subsurface to form a general profile of the subsurface
conditions
In general the elastic energy waves travel more slowly through relatively soft
low density materials such as soil and highly weathered rock The elastic waves travel more
rapidly through relatively high density material such as rock formation The boundaries
between horizontal layers of substantially differing energy wave velocities may be estimated
by the analysis of the energy wave travel times to the geophone array
Using seismic refraction methods successive layers of materials with increasing wave
velocity corresponding to increasing ground density may be measured Typically subsurface
materials show a general increase in seismic velocity with increasing depth due to normal
weathering processes However a potential disadvantage to this exploration method is that
lower velocity layers such as a highly weathered rock seam or buried soil layer overlain by
higher velocity layers such as dense rock may be masked and will not be detected by this
method
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4129 00 Page 9
As ageneral rule seismic refraction methods measure the velocities of the subsurface
materials to a depth equal to about one third of the length of the survey line Survey lines
for this study were about 65 feet long therefore seismic velocities of the subsurface
materials were measured to depths of about 20 feet below ground level
The complexity of local volcanic formations often creates difficulty in the accurate
interpretation of seismic refraction data In some cases volcanic formations may become
horizontally discontinuous or layers of dense rock may overlie layers of less dense material
such as clinker or strongly vesicular rock thus producing a complex data set of seismic wave
recordings Therefore a significant amount of judgment must be used to interpret the type
of rock material that a given seismic velocity layer may represent The calculated velocities
may be empirically related to the excavation characteristics of the materials as discussed in
the Excavation section of this report
For this exploration a 12 pound sledgehammer was used as the energy source The
hammer was used to apply energy to the ground at shot points located at about 5 feet from
both ends of the geophone array The geophone array consisted of 12 geophones with a
spacing of 5 feet between geophones Energy arrivals at the geophones were measured
and recorded with an EG G Model 1225 12 channel signal enhancing seismograph Both
forward and reverse shots were utilized to provide for better seismic data control and to
assist in the data interpretation
The seismic refraction data collected in the field were transferred to a computer and
analyzed using the Refract Version 3 software program The software program assists
the geophysicist by plotting charts showing elastic wave arrival times at the geophones
versus distance from the energy source The program also facilitates the calculations and
interpretation of the data as a layered geologic model

Light Pole Foundations
Based on the information provided we understand that a lighting system consisting
of 15 foot high light poles is also planned to service the new walkway improvements In
order to develop the required bearing and lateral load capacities the proposed new light
pole structures may be supported by a foundation system consisting of cast in place
concrete drilled shafts Based on the anticipated structural loads and the subsoil
conditions encountered at the project site we recommend that drilled shafts with a
diameter of about 18 to 24 inches be used to support the proposed light pole structures
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site drilled shaft
embedment depths of about 5 feet below the design finished grades may be used for the
light pole foundations
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally from
skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable compressive load
capacity of up to 10 kips per shaft may be used for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces The
end bearing component of the drilled shafts should be discounted due to the potential for
caving in of the surficial soils in the upper 3 feet that would preclude obtaining a clean bottom
during drilled shaft installation
Lateral loads imposed on the foundations may be resisted by a combination of the
following 1 resistance of the shaft soil system to deflect and 2 passive resistance on the
vertical faces of shaft caps where applicable Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is
a function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable

Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the light pole foundations because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations
However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the following conditions
would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations
Loose Subsoils Hard Rock Formation
Based on our field exploration the proposed new light poie locations may be
underlain by surficial loose soils in the upper 3 feet of subsoils To reduce the
potential for caving in of the drilled holes temporary casing may be required during
the foundation construction In addition our field exploration suggests that hard
volcanic tuff formation may be anticipated at shallow depths along the entire
walkway alignment Therefore coring into the dense volcanic tuff formation will be
required Appropriate drilling equipment and coring tools should be utilized by the
drilled shaft contractor to install the drilled shafts to the depths and dimensions
recommended herein
Workmanship
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the contact
between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper construction
techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft
holes and in placing concrete into the holes
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch range should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soil The

installation of drilled shafts during construction

Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the design finished grades of the proposed
_ improvements we envision that the majority of the grading work will consist of excavation
into the existing hillside for the construction of the new walkway improvements At the on set
of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and
grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and
disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and
grubbing below areas designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm
natural material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill
The excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
After clearing and grubbing the area within the new walkway limits should be
over excavated where necessary to provide the minimum 6 inch thick select borrow
subbase layer below the concrete paving The over excavated subgrades or areas
designated to receive fills should then be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches if possible
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soil subgrade be scarified and prepared again as recommended above at no
additional cost to the owner Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade
due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas
and replacement with well compacted fill at no additional cost to the owner
The weathered tuff rock generated from the cut slope excavations may be re used as
a source of general fill and or backfill materials provided that the materials are properly
processed and reduced to not more than 3 inches in largest dimension and are free of

Water Lines
We envision that new water lines will be required for this project In general for
support of the water lines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
1 inch minus granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
be used under the pipes The initial backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should
consist of 1 inch minus granular materials or free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine
gravel to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is
critical that a free draining granular material be used to reduce the potential for formation of
voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the
pipes The use of clayey soils as backfill immediately around water line pipes is not
recommended
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the on site soils less than 3 inches in

maximum dimension or select granular material The backfill should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Where trenches are

Since volcanic tuff formation was encountered at shallow depths during our field

Drainage
The finished grades outside the improvements should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that the
discharge be diverted away from the pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near
the foundations should also be avoided
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be properly
backfilled against the walls or footings immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the
potential for water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and
should be maintained to drain surface water run off away from the foundations
