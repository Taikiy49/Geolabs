INTRODUCTION
The purpose of this exploration and testing was to provide subsurface
soil permeability data for use in the design of the proposed injection
wells for disposal of sewage effluent for the future treatment plant A
separate geotechnical engineering report will be issued for the wastewater
treatment plant
Two 2 exploratory borings were drilled to depths of about 89 to 100
feet utilizing NQ 4 inch diameter equipment Preliminary testing
indicated suitable permeability for disposal sites therefore 4 inch Pvc
casing was installed in a larger reamed out hole Slotted casing was used
in the lower portion of the well where porous zones were encountered
Subsequent to the construction of the test wells permeability
testing was performed to evaluate the potential for effluent disposal
As per our meeting of three additional exploratory
borings were drilled to a depth of about 50 feet to delineate the lateral
extent of the pervious coral formation
The results of our field exploration and tests which were used in
the analysis that formulated our conclusions and recommendations are
presented in the appendices of this report
The injection well site location is shown on the Project Location and
Geologic Map Plate 1 The approximate boring locations at the injection
well site are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located on the north shore of the island of Oahu
in the Mokuleia area in an abandoned sugarcane field designated as Mill
5 by the Waialua Sugar Company The site is approximately 1900 by 700
feet in plan dimensions and is bordered to the north by Crozier Drive
The Waialua Sugar mill is about 1 to miles southeast of the site
The site slopes up very gently towards the north with surface
elevations varying from about 10 to 18 feet above Mean Sea Level
PROPOSED CONSTRUCTION
A new WWIP facility is planned in the area A series of injection
wells is planned to dispose of the treated effluent The proposed
injection well site covers an area of about 30 acres 1900 by 700 feet in
plan
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The project site is located on the north flank of the Waianae Shield
Volcano After the cessation of volcanic activity in the late Pliocene
early Pleistocene erosional dissection of the original dome has created a
thick alluvial apron along the seaward margins of the Waianae Range thus
providing a salient for the growth of coral reefs
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1814 00 Page 3
Dk No 31
During the Pleistocene sea level fluctuated widely as the result of
glacio eustatic changes When the sea stood at higher levels than the
present the coral reefs grew to elevations above present sea level At
lower stands of the sea erosion of the adjacent land mass resulted in the
accumulation of alluvial deposits The formations encountered at the
project site are product of these Pleistocene depositional processes and
are composed of interbedded sediments of both marine and terrigenous
origin
Soil Conditions
In general our explorations encountered a thin surface layer of
stiff brown clayey silt extending to a maximm depth of 7 5 feet below the
existing ground surface Below this the drilling encountered a 4 0 to
15 0 foot thick layer of beach sand underlain by pervious coralline
reefrock and reef detritus to a depth of about 45 0 feet Below the
coralline formations were interbeds of stiff alluvial deposits and marine
sediments to about 100 feet the maximum depth drilled
Groundwater was encountered in the borings at depths of 6 8 to 10 4
feet below the existing ground surface
Detailed boring logs of the soils and rocks encountered during
drilling of the borings are presented in appendix A
Permeability
In general permeability is a function of the grain size of the
soil Coarse grained soils such as sands and gravels readily transmit
water Fine grained soils such as silts and clays generally have low
permeability
The interbeds of granular marine sediments and coralline reef rock
encountered at the site have relatively good permeability and appear to be
practical for use in underground disposal of treated effluent

Well Drilling and Testing
The two test wells were drilled utilizing truck mounted rotary
drilling equipment Drive samples were taken in the unconsolidated
deposits and core samples were obtained in the reef deposits for material
classification
Boring No 2 was drilled to a depth of 100 feet and Boring No 3 to
89 3 feet A 4 inch I D perforated pipe casing was installed at each
well
At Boring No 3 an additional 2 shallow observation holes were
drilled to monitor the drawdown or drop in water level during testing
After the drilling of the test wells they were developed by
air lifting using a compressor As the water being developed cleared the
supply of compressed air was closed off and then opened to create a
surging effect which produced additional flow of sand This process was
continued for approximately 1 5 to 2 hours per well at which time only
minimal amount of sand were developed with each surge
Following the development the wells were tested using a nearby fire
hydrant to inject water for Boring No 2 and a 2 inch centrifugal pump to
pump out water in Boring No 3 Flow rates were measured by the use of an
in line paddle wheel flow sensor The pump test and injection data are
shown on Plates C 1 through C 5
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The exploratory borings and test wells which were drilled at the
project site generally encountered coralline reef formation In general
the coralline materials are quite permeable and can readily transmit clear
water for injection wells

Injection Well Permeability
The field data for the gravity injection at Boring No 2 showed a
head build up of 6 2 feet for an injection rate of 310 GPM This leads to
a calculated coefficient of permeability k of 0 19 feet per minute For
Boring No 3 the drawdown was 0 5 feet for a pump out rate of 100 GPM and
a calculated k value of 0 41 feet per minute
For a schematic diagram of the injection testing refer to Plates C 3
and C 4
It should be noted that these values were obtained from testing open
wells using clear water and no air entrainment Lower values are
anticipated under the proposed use
Injection Well Design
By extrapolation of the field test data our calculations indicated
that disposal wells in this area with a diameter of 0 7 feet and a depth
of 40 feet could theoretically accommodate a gravity injection of 0 2
approximately 80 feet would increase the injection capacity the potential
for clogging and formation caving in the lower alluvial and granular
marine sediments is relatively high Therefore it is our opinion that
gravity injection should be limited to a depth of 40 feet or the bottom of
the pervious coral formation
The calculated relationship between discharge and head build up for
different sizes and depths of wells is presented on Plate C 5
Typically water injected into disposal wells contains suspended
solids micro organisms and other matter which may reduce the potential
permeability Here in Hawaii there have been numerous failures of
disposal wells injecting into coral algal formations These failures have
been attributed to numerous causes e g lack of maintenance
accumulation of solids and formation caving
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1814 00 Page 6
Dk No 31
In view of this past history of failures we recommend that the
injection system be overdesigned to allow for reduction in capacity or
well failure In addition provision should be made in the facility
design for a maintenance program to extend the useful life of the wells
It is our opinion that underground disposal of treated effluent could
be accomplished at the site by utilizing a series of production wells to
minimize the build up of accumulated solids and to maintain effluent
percolation in the event of reduced injection capacity or peak flows
during maintenance or construction of other wells
Provision should be incorporated in the development scheme for
additional production wells to be installed as the original wells reach
the end of their effective service life
