SUMMARY OF RECOMMENDATIONS
The subject landslide occurred on an old cut slope following
the heavy rains of hurricane Iniki The landslide appears to
have occurred due to a number of factors including the
steepness of the slope weakening of the slope over a long
period of time and saturation of the soil on the slope
Construction of a reinforced concrete retaining wall appears
to be most viable remedial approach for the subject landslide
area considering the slope and access constraints We
envision that the reinforced concrete wall would be supported
by a drilled pier foundation system An additional boring
exploration is recommended to develop detailed retaining wall
foundation design recommendations
The test of this report should be referred to for detailed
discussions of findings and preliminary geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertinent to the subject project
INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering
exploration of the landslide at 4073 Round Top Drive in Honolulu Oahu Hawaii The
general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1 This report summarizes our efforts on the project and presents our findings and
preliminary recommendations pertinent to the subject landslide

SITE DESCRIPTION
The subject landslide is located on the southeast side of Round Top Drive in
Honolulu Oahu Hawaii A Site Plan of the project area is included on Plate 2 showing
the location of the test boring A photograph of the landslide is included on Plate 4
We understand that the landslide occurred soon after hurricane Iniki in
September 1992 The landslide occurred on an old road cut for Round Top Drive Prior
to the occurrence of the landslide the old cut slope appears to have been about 25 feet
high with a slope gradient of about three fourths horizontal to one vertical 4H 1V
Portions of the old cut slope were covered with vegetation including a stand of bamboo
The overall relief in the landslide area is about 30 feet from the toe of the slide on
Round Top Drive to the crown of the slide on the adjacent Tyson property The landslide
scarp is up to about 16 feet high and comes within 10 feet of the house on the Tyson
property The landslide is about 50 feet wide and about 25 feet long from the crown to
the toe
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Most of the landslide scarp is covered with loose disturbed soil and heavy
vegetation debris In situ cinder deposits were observed along the northern margin of the
slide The cinder deposits were observed to be predominantly sand sized slightly
cemented and thinly bedded Bedding planes were measured striking N45W dipping
18SW which are roughly in the same direction as the slope face Topography and
geomorphic features in the landslide suggest that the slide mass is probably on the order
of five feet 5 thick in the central portion of the slide
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3108 00 Page 4
Subsurface conditions immediately upslope of the slide were explored by drilling
and sampling one 1 boring to a depth of 41 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Th drilling and sampling operations were observed by a Field Geologist from our firm and
a log of the materials encountered was maintained on a near continuous basis A
detailed description of the field exploration methodology along with the Boring Log is
presented in Appendix A of this report
The boring encountered residual soil about 1 foot thick overlying in situ cinder
deposits The residual soils encountered consisted of brown clayey silts From about 1
foot to 20 feet deep the cinder deposits consisted of relatively loose sands Frorn 20 feet
to 41 5 feet the cinder deposits were medium dense to dense sands and gravelly sands
Groundwater was not encountered in the boring during our field exploration
Evidence of previous water flow in the drainage gully along the toe of the slide was visible
however it was not clear if this previous water flow had resulted from seepage from the
slide or simply from area surface runoff
DISCUSSION OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our observations and exploratory drilling possible factors that appear
to have contributed to the instability of the subject slope include
1 the relatively steep and high initial slope condition
2 weakening of the materials on the face of the sloe from weathering and
bioturbation
3 saturation of the slope from rainfall associated with hurricane Iniki and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3108 00 Page 5
4 bedding structure within the cinder deposit may tend to direct groundwater
toward the face of the slope As previously indicated it appears that the
landslide mass is relatively thin on the order of 5 feet thick in the central
portion of the slide We do not believe that the slide mass is a deep
seated landslide In our opinion the likelihood of continued movement of
the existing landslide debris is high Furthermore the oversteepened slope
configuration in the landslide area does not appear to have adequate long
term stability
Remedial Alternatives
An approach involving construction of a retaining wall at the top of the slope was
considered Since the distance from the crown of the slide to the existing house is only
about 10 feet construction access would be a concern if a drilled pier foundation were
to be used for the retaining wall If a T footing were used appropriate foundation
setbacks from the face of the slope and from the existing house could not be maintained
The stability of the high steep slope that would remain below a retaining wall located at
the top of the slope would continue to be a concern and the visual impact of the high
steep slope in such close proximity to the existing house may not be acceptable
A range of remedial alternatives involving a retaining wall located at the toe of the
landslide were considered including gabion wall reinforced earth fill crib wall and
reinforced concrete wall Gravity walls or gravity fill alternatives all involve difficulties with
high steep temporary construction back slopes in close proximity to the existing home
Use of a reinforced concrete wall RCW appears to reduce the problems noted
above Th RCW also has the advantage that a drilled pier foundation could be used
which would reduce the amount of excavation into the slope and reduce the potential for
disruption of the traffic lane on Round Top Drive Closure of one lane would be required
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3108 00 Page 6
during construction hours however it should be possible to have two open lanes at
night
Reinforced Concrete Retaining Wall
Following discussion with the City and County staff a reinforced concrete retaining
wall appears to be a viable remedial approach A cross section showing the conceptual
reinforced concrete retaining wall approach is presented on the attached Plate 3 Based
on the existing slope configuration the RCW would need to be about 11 feet high if a
steeper fill slope ratio of 1 H to 1V were constructed from the top of the wall to the
crown of the slide For planning purposes the RCW approach should include the
following features
1 Drilled Pier Foundation Additional boring exploration should be performed
at the toe of the slide area to evaluate the properties of the foundation
supporting materials at the wall location Bearing and lateral earth pressure
Capacities of the foundation soils for use in the wall s pier foundation design
should also be evaluated Previous experience with this type of wall
indicated that the foundation will likely involve piers about 2 feet in diameter
located at about 6 to 8 feet on center with pier depths of about 15 to 20
feet
2 Subdrainage We recommend that subdrainage be provided along the back
side of the wall and at the interface between the new slope fill and native
material A conceptual subdrain layout is also shown on Plate 3
3 Fill Slope If a fill slope gradient of 1 to 1 is used at the top of the wall
select non expansive granular fill material should be used for fill Even
using select material erosion of the slope will still be a concern until
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3108 00 Page 7
vegetation becomes established Consideration should be given to the use
of hydroseed and jute mat for erosion protection Runoff from the area
upslope of the slide should be directed away from the slope using a V
shaped ditch or other appropriate means
Additional Exploration
As noted above additional boring exploration will be needed at the toe of the slide
to evaluate the properties of the underlying competent soil materials at the proposed wall
location Based on the results of this additional exploration design level geotechnical
recommendations should be prepared regarding the retaining wall design slope
reconstruction and specifications for materials We will be pleased to discuss the details
and scope of work for this subsequent geotechnical engineering exploration
Design Review
Subsequent preliminary drawings and specifications developed for the proposed
construction should be forwarded to Geolabs Hawaii for review and written comments
prior to preparation of final plans and specifications This review is necessary to evaluate
conformance with the intent of the preliminary and subsequent additional geotechnical
engineering recommendations provided herein If this review is not made Geolabs Hawaii
cannot be responsible for misinterpretation of our recommendations

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
a oe a ee co ne nt a
Laboratory Testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in evaluating pertinent engineering properties of the soil and or rock materials
sampled
Classification Tests
Moisture content and dry density determinations are used as an aid to soil
classification and to aid in evaluating certain properties such as relative saturation The
moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight of the tested sample and
dry density are shown at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs
Sieve analyses are performed to determine the grain size distribution of the soil
The grain size distribution is used for classification in the Unified Soil Classification System
and to provide indices on other engineering properties The results of these tests are
summarized on Plates B 1 and B 2
Physical Property Tests
Direct Shear tests ASTM Test Method D 3080 72 are used to determine the angle
of internal friction and the cohesion in a soil sample These data are used in various
engineering calculations The result of the direct shear tests are presented on Plates B 3
through B 3
