2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Lanai was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from a
single shield volcano The volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
in age As the volcanic activity ceased the central portion subsided forming a low
basin now known as the Palawai Basin Block faulting on the southern flank of the
island formed a broad graben leading from the Palawai Basin to the south to
Manele Bay The project site is located within the Manele Graben
Our exploration indicates that the site is underlain by a surface mantle of poorly
developed residual soil which grades with depth to competent basalt lava rock formation at
relatively shallow depths
2 2 Existing Site Conditions
The project site is located at the southern portion of the Island of Lanai Hawaii
The proposed debris basins and drainage culverts are located north and northwest of
the Manele Bay Resort Hotel Based on the topographic plan in the Manele Drainage
Master Plan the existing ground elevations at the debris basin and culvert sites range
from about 140 to 500 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
The site and the surrounding area is currently vacant raw land and is covered by
dry land grasses and shrubs Occasional boulders occur on the surface and lava rock
outcrops are visible in areas Locally drainage consists of sheet flow leading to poorly
defined drainageways
2 3 Subsurface Conditions

These factors are discussed in the following paragraphs
a Flyrock
Flyrock is the blaster s term for flying rock or other debris which is
launched into the air during open blasting by the force of the detonating
explosives These projectiles often have tremendous kinetic energy and
can crush or destroy objects in their flight path or at their point of impact
The occurrence of flyrock may be reduced and possibly eliminated by
increasing the confinement or the blast depth of the explosive charges
and or by using safety mats
6 Ground Vibration
With respect to the project ground vibration is probably the most critical
potential hazardous factor to be anticipated during blasting operations
When an explosive detonates below ground it generates intense stress
waves in the surrounding material These waves crush the rock
immediately adjacent to the explosion and permanently distorts and
cracks the rock for some distance
Beyond the area in which the intensity of the stress waves attenuate to
such a degree that there is no longer any permanent deformation of the
rock the stress is further propagated through the rock as elastic waves
These elastic waves create ground vibration
The potential of the ground vibration to damage a structure is a function of
the intensity of the vibration and the natural frequency of the structure
According to the DuPont Blaster s Handbook a formula has been
developed to relate the intensity of ground vibration at a given point in
terms of particle displacement velocity and acceleration to the weight of

Vibration would be a consideration in the vicinity of the developed areas

c Air Blast
The air blast from an explosion is a compression wave in the air Noise is
the portion of the air blast with frequencies between 20 and
concussion is the portion with frequencies below 20 Hz The DuPont
Blaster s Handbook also sets forth a scaled distance relationship for air
blast
The effects of the air blast which depends on atmospheric and or other
considerations can be a consideration for much greater distances than for
vibration
If blasting is to be used for rock excavation we recommend that the
scaled distances of the explosive charges be limited to those that will

Cutand Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration it appears
that most of the cut slopes would expose the surface residual soils consisting of stiff
but friable low plasticity silts and weathered basalt rock In general we believe that
the cut slopes planned at the basin sites may be designed with a slope inclination
of one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V or flatter However we anticipate that
clinker zones and or voids may be encountered at the cut slope face In the event
that clinker zones are exposed at the cut slope face the cut slope may need to be
protected with GRP Large cavities that may be exposed on the cut slope face
should be backfilled and or grouted if necessary We believe that the permanent
cut slopes in basalt formation do not need benches for cut slopes up to 30 feet in
vertical height
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter If
necessary slope benches are recommended at maximum 20 foot vertical height
intervals If the existing ground surface is steeper than 5H 1V fills should be keyed
and benched into the existing slope as discussed above to provide stability of the
new fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest point and
continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above fill
placement recommendations
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope
ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face Track rolling of slopes should not be
accepted In the event that over cutting of a slope occurs keying and benching
requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design
grade with sliver fills Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and
slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the potential for
erosion of the finished slopes
It should be noted that in their natural state the on site residual soils are relatively
dry and friable As such they are highly susceptible to wind and rain erosion
Therefore it is recommended that finished slopes that do not receive slope

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
13 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 13 to depths of about 28 0 to 56 3 feet
below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans
Plates 2 and 3 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
auger and coring equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 13
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
