PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is at Box Culvert 5 along Waikupanaha Street The project
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Based on the information provided and our field observations it appears that a
portion of the existing slope at the box culvert area has failed resulting in the collapse of
a section of the existing concrete flume In addition the top of the distressed area
appears to encroach within several feet from the edge of the existing roadway We
observed tension cracks along the edge of the pavement surface alongside the
distressed area indicating potential movement of the roadway towards the slope
Therefore it is desired to construct a concrete retaining wall to reduce the potential for
undermining the existing roadway and the potential for further pavement movement

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by stiff to
very stiff clayey soils extending to the maximum depth explored of about 36 5 feet below
the existing pavement surface Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the
site we believe that shallow foundations consisting of continuous footings bearing on
the recompacted stiff on site soils may be used to support the proposed new concrete
retaining wall planned To reduce the potential for further undermining of the existing
roadway during the retaining wall construction the new retaining wall should be set back
away from the roadway and aligned with the front face of the existing box culvert The
backfill behind the new retaining walls and existing box culvert may consist of a
one and one half horizontal to one vertical sloping backfill lined with grouted
rubble paving GRP to protect the reconstructed slope from future erosion
In addition to the concrete retaining wall we also have reviewed and evaluated the
use of a sheet pile retaining wall system Based on the very stiff clayey soil conditions
encountered and the potential for presence of boulders at the stream channel we believe
that the use of a steel sheet pile shoring system will likely encounter significant difficulties
due to the potential for penetration refusal during the sheet pile installation operations
We have also considered the potential for reconstructing the failed slope and
protecting it with a grouted rubble paving However this option does not appear feasible
due to the substantial slope height and limited setback distance of the existing roadway to
the failed slope Detailed discussion of our geotechnical engineering recommendations for
the retaining wall design and the site preparation are presented in the following sections
herein

Retaining Wall Foundations
In general we anticipate that a shallow foundation system will be utilized to support
the planned retaining wall along the box culvert structure Based on the subsurface
conditions encountered in our field exploration we envision that the new retaining
structure may be supported on shallow foundations such as continuous footings
An allowable bearing pressure up to about 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may
be used for design of shallow foundations bearing on the stiff to very stiff on site
clayey soils as encountered in the borings drilled This bearing value is for
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
For retaining wall footings bearing on the on site materials the bottom of the footing
excavations should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to
provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of
reinforcing steel or concrete Soft and or loose materials that may be encountered
at the footing subgrade level should be over excavated to expose the underlying
firm or dense materials The over excavation should be backfilled with select
granular fill material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or
the footings may extend down to bear on the firm or dense materials
Retaining structure footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches Due to the
steep slope about 1H 1V in front of the originally planned retaining wall alignment
we believe that the planned retaining wall may be subjected to movement and
instability unless the footing foundation is embedded below the stream floor
elevation Therefore the new retaining wall should be set back away from the
roadway and aligned with the front face of the existing box culvert In addition the
bottom of the wall footing should extend down to a minimum of 2 feet below the
stream floor elevation to reduce the potential for appreciable wall movement and
instability
Lateral loads acting on the retaining structure may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the stiff on site
soils Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf
assuming that soil around footings is well compacted The passive pressure should
be reduced for foundations on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected

Drainage
A typical drainage system may consist of a sack of permeable material placed at
each weephole location The sack of permeable material should consist of about
1 to 2 cubic feet of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation material
wrapped with non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent The weepholes
should consist of a minimum 4 inch diameter PVC pipe spaced no more than 6 feet
apart
Backfill directly behind permeable drainage materials should consist of select
granular fill material with a maximum particle size of 3 inches Unless covered by
concrete slabs or GRP the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of relatively
impervious material to reduce the potential for significant water infiltration behind
the walls
Reconstructed Slope with Grouted Rubble Paving
As previously mentioned we recommend aligning the new retaining walls with the
front face of the existing box culvert to reduce the potential for further undermining of the
existing roadway during the retaining wall construction In addition we recommend
extending the new retaining walls up to about the same height as the existing box culvert
structure The area behind the new retaining walls and existing box culvert may then be
reconstructed with a sloping backfill with a minimum 18 inch thick grouted rubble
paving GRP to protect the reconstructed slope from future erosion In addition a
minimum 2 foot wide and 3 foot deep keyway should be provided along the top of the
reconstructed slope Weepholes should be provided in a grid pattern over the slope face
to relieve the hydrostatic pressures that may build up behind the reconstructed slope
It should be noted that the west side of the stream channel also exhibits substantial
slope failure near the collapsed concrete spillway Consideration should be given to
extending the new retaining wall alignment along this area to remedy the existing situation
and to reduce the potential for further slope erosion and failure at this area
Site Preparation
In general the area within the contract grading limits should be cleared and
grubbed thoroughly at the on set of earthwork Vegetation debris deleterious material
and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site Soft
and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or
future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm and or stiff material and
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fill The excavated
soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed off site
After clearing and grubbing the existing ground should be over excavated
where necessary to the bottom of the retaining wall footing The over excavated

subgrade should be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Fill materials required for the project should consist of non expansive select
granular material such as crushed coral or basalt The material should be well graded
from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension In
addition the material should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and
should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with
ASTM D 1883 The materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Geolabs should test imported fill materials for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use
Geolabs should monitor site grading operations to observe the site preparation to
evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and
scarification process and to evaluate whether the exposed soil rock conditions are
similar to those encountered in our field exploration

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling two borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to depths of about 36 3 and 36 5 feet
below the existing pavement surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted
drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on the Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch outside diameter OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer
falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled boring in
general accordance with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
