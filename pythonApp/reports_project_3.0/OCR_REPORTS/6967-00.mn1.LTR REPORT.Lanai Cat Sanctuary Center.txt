geotechnical engineering evaluation in support of design for the Lanai Cat Sanctuary 
Center project, on the Island of Lanai, Hawaii.  The project location and general vicinity

This letter report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering

the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
It was proposed to construct a 5,000-gallon water tank and a structural slab at the 
existing cat sanctuary center on the Island of Lanai, Hawaii.  The existing cat sanctuary 
center is to the west of the Lanai Airport near the intersection of Kaumalapau Highway and 
Kaupili Road. 
Based on the preliminary information provided, we understand that a 5,000-gallon 
water tank was proposed in an open area of the existing cat sanctuary center for water 
storage.  A new concrete slab was also planned for a mobile veterinary clinic.  Structural 
details of the clinic facility were not available at the time this report was prepared. 
The existing cat sanctuary center is located on generally level ground surface.  We 
envision cut and fill of less than 1 to 2 feet for the site grading.  We anticipate the 
subsurface conditions to consist of residual soils overlying basalt rock formation.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Lanai is a shield volcano built by eruptions at the summit and along 
three rift zones more than 1.20 to 1.46 million years ago. The principal rift zone trends 
northwestward as a broad ridge and is responsible for the conspicuous elongation of the 
island in that direction. A less conspicuous bulge on the southern side of the island is the 
result of volcanic extrusion on the southwest rift zone. The rift zones intersect at the island 
summit. The summit of the shield volcano collapsed to form a caldera from which a

shallow graben, bordered by an echelon of step faults, extends south-southeastward 
toward Manele Bay. Numerous dikes exposed in the sea cliff indicate that this Manele 
Graben lies along another rift zone. 
The caldera was largely, but not completely, filled by lava flows and the present 
Palawai Basin is a remnant of the caldera. Just to the west of it, Miki Basin (with an 
average diameter of about 0.9 miles) is a nearly filled pit crater. The top of the ridge 
between these basins is about 140 feet above the floor of the Palawai Basin. On the 
south, the floor of Palawai Basin merges with the floor of the Manele Graben, where the 
most recent lava flows in the caldera overflowed onto the outer slope of the volcano. 
The Island of Lanai was built by the extrusion of thin-bedded a’a and pahoehoe 
tholeiitic basaltic flows that are generally inclined at about 6 to 15 degrees from horizontal 
(where not disturbed by faulting). Volcanic rocks on the Island of Lanai are grouped as the 
Lanai Volcanic Series. The near-surface soils generally consist of well-drained, 
fine-textured and moderately fine-textured soils derived from volcanic ash and the in-situ 
weathering of the igneous rocks. The formation of the Island of Lanai was also the result of 
large changes in sea-level that caused submergence and re-emergence of the land mass 
through geologic time. Faulting and stream erosion in conjunction with changes in 
sea-level are also responsible for the high sea cliffs along the southern and western 
coastlines. 
The project site is southerly from the Miki Basin and is near the western crust of 
the island caldera and transitions over the edge into a ridge and gully formation towards 
the island western shoreline.  Two adjacent gulches (Kaumalapau and Kalamaiki) are 
located on the south and the north of the site traversing in an east-west direction.  
Recent agricultural development created the gentle sloping terrain within the plateau 
encompassing the Palawai Basin and Miki Basin.  Most of the soils in the project area 
are alluvium and residual/saprolitic soils, derived from the gulch/stream depositions and 
in-situ weathering of volcanic ash and igneous rock.  In general, the residual and 
saprolitic soils grade to basaltic rock formation with depth. 
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is situated on the northwestern fringe of Palawai Basin plateau 
approximately 1.8 miles east of Kaumalapau Harbor and about half a mile west of Lanai 
Airport.  The site is slightly sloping downward to the western shoreline.  The explored area 
is within the existing Lanai Cat Sanctuary facility and an unpaved parking lot.  The site is 
surrounded by sparse vegetation consisting of native grass and shrubs and is accessible 
via an unpaved road about 600 feet south from Kaumalapau Highway. 
Ground elevations in the vicinity of the project site range on the order of about 
+1,068 to about +1,086 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).

SUBSURFACE CONDITION 
The subsurface conditions were explored by excavating two test pits, designated as 
Test Pit Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 5 to 6 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  Soil samples were obtained from each of the test pits for laboratory testing of 
index property and relevant engineering characteristics.  The approximate test pit locations 
are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our field exploration generally encountered a thin (less than 1 foot) layer of top soil 
overlying residual soils consisting of very stiff to hard clayey silt extending to the maximum 
depth explored of 6 feet below the existing ground surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the test pits excavated at the time of our field 
exploration.  However, groundwater levels may change due to seasonal precipitation, 
surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of 
Test Pits, Plates A-1 and A-2 of Appendix A.  Results of the laboratory tests performed on 
selected soil samples are presented in Appendix B. 
SEISMIC DESIGN CONSIDERATION 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition), the project site may be 
subject to seismic activity that can impact the planned improvements, and seismic design 
considerations may need to be addressed. 
The geotechnical data disclosed by our limited field exploration and review of the 
geologic data of the project site were used to classify the subsurface conditions for seismic 
design considerations.  Generally, the test pit exploration encountered very stiff to hard 
residual soils.  We anticipate basalt formation at greater depths, underlying the stiff 
residual soil.  Because the depth of the basalt formation is unknown, we assume the upper 
100 feet of the soil profile consists of stiff residual soil.  Based on these subsurface site 
conditions, we classified the site under “Stiff Soil Profile” corresponding to a Site Class “D” 
in accordance with the International Building Code (Table No. 1613.5.2), 2006 Edition. 
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we envision using 
shallow continuous footings, such as a perimeter ring footing, to support the new water 
tank planned at the site.  We recommend using shallow spread and/or continuous footings 
to support the proposed clinic building. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 
pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design of shallow foundation system 
bearing on recompacted stiff residual soils. 
Detailed discussions and recommendations for foundation design and site grading 
are presented in the following sections.

Shallow Foundations 
We envision that the proposed water tank and clinic building foundation 
subgrades will consist of stiff clayey residual soils.  Therefore, we recommend using 
shallow spread and/or continuous footings to support the proposed structures at the 
project site.  As an alternative, the foundation for support of the building may also 
consist of thickened-edge slab footing. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of 
shallow foundations.  This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be 
increased by one-third for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces.  For design of the water tank floor, a modulus of subgrade reaction of 
150 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection (pci) may be used for the compacted 
subbase. 
In general, the foundation should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below 
the lowest adjacent finished grade.  Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on 
sloping ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal 
setback distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of 
the slope. 
Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of footing excavations 
should be over-excavated down to stiff/dense materials or to a maximum of 2 feet deep 
in the footing excavation.  The over-excavation should be backfilled with non-expansive 
select granular fill materials moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Based on the subsurface conditions at the project site, we envision that shallow 
continuous footings, such as a perimeter ring footing, may be used to support the new 
5,000-gallon water tank planned at the site.  In addition, we envision that the new water 
tank will have a concrete floor in the interior of the tank.  In order to provide a uniform 
bearing surface for the concrete floor, we recommend providing a 12-inch thick layer of 
compacted subbase below the concrete floor of the tank.  The 12-inch thick layer of 
compacted subbase should extend beyond the edges of the tank structure by at least 
2 feet. 
The structural fill materials should consist of well-graded granular materials 
generally less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the 
occurrence of voids in the compacted mass.  The subbase materials recommended 
below the water tanks should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates (3-inch minus 
granular materials).  The structural fill and subbase materials should be compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction in accordance with ASTM D 1557.  Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557.  
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density.

If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with the 
recommendations presented herein, total settlement of foundations is estimated to be 
on the order of less than 1 inch with differential settlements on the order of about 
0.5 inches. 
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance 
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth 
pressure developed against the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of 
foundations.  A coefficient of friction of 0.3 may be used for foundations bearing on the 
compacted subbase.  Resistance to lateral loads due to passive earth pressure may be 
calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of 
depth (pcf) for foundations embedded in the compacted subbase or structural fill 
materials.  These values assume that the soils around the foundations are well 
compacted.  Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 
12 inches below the finished grade should be neglected. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor footing excavations prior to the 
placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions 
and the required embedment depths, and observe backfill placement to evaluate the 
compaction quality of the fill material. 
Site Grading 
We anticipate that site grading will involve less than 1 to 2 feet of cuts and fills, to 
achieve the finished grades.  In general, a Geolabs representative should monitor the site 
preparation to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions during excavation are 
similar to those anticipated and/or encountered in our field exploration.  The following 
recommendations are intended to provide guidelines for the design of site preparation 
work. 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
thoroughly cleared and grubbed.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site to reduce the 
potential for contamination of the excavated materials to be used as embankment fill 
materials. 
In general, the on-site materials generated from the excavations may be re-used as 
a source of general fill and backfill materials, provided that the materials are screened of 
over-sized materials (greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension) and are free of 
deleterious materials.  Where used as fill and backfill materials, the on-site soils should be 
properly moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts 
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction. 
Areas to receive fill should be scarified to a depth of 8 inches, moisture conditioned 
to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to at least 90 percent

Drainage 
The finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from 
the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding.  It is also advised to install gutter 
systems around the building and that the discharge is diverted away from the foundation 
areas.  Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be 
avoided considering the slight to moderate shrink/swell potential of the on-site soils.  
Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to 
reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above 
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation 
and subsequent loss of strength and/or heaving of the near-surface soils.  The foundation 
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of 
the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration.  In addition, drainage swales 
should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface water runoff away 
from the foundations and slabs. 
Utility Trenches 
In general, good construction practices should be utilized for the installation and 
backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities.  The excavations should comply with the 
applicable federal, state, and local safety requirements. The contractor should be 
responsible for trench shoring design and installation. 
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) for support under the pipes. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 
gradation), should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above 
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes. It is critical to use this 
free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of 
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill 
could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level of 12 inches above the pipes to 
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on-site soils (with a maximum 
particle size of 6 inches) or select granular fill material. The backfill should be placed in 
maximum 8-inch loose level lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent 
relative compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence.

necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein.
