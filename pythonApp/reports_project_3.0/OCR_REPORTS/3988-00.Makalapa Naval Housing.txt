Geotechnical Engineering Exploration
Proposed Exit Stairway at Building 250
Makalapa Naval Housing
In accordance with our fee proposal dated March 25 we have performed a
geotechnical engineering exploration to evaluate the approximate depth to the top of the
retaining wall footing and depth of the underlying mudrock for the above referenced
project This letter report serves to summarize our work and present our findings for the
project
Project Considerations
The proposed project will include the construction of an exit stairway near the
southern side of Building 250 in the Makalapa area of Pearl Harbor on the island of Oahu
Hawaii The proposed stairway will extend from the sewer ejection station at the
basement level of Building 250 up toward Makalapa Drive The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on Plate 1
The new stairway will be offset approximately 28 feet to the south of the lower
levels of Building 250 which corresponds to about 9 feet south from an existing concrete
retaining wall adjacent to the structure s walkway Based on the plans provided
excavations to a depth of about 30 feet will be required to construct the exit stairway
We understand that it is desired to evaluate the depth to the top of the retaining
wall footing and the depth to mudrock for further evaluation in support of the stairway
excavation

Purpose and Scope
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering services was to evaluate the
subsurface conditions with respect to the proposed exit stairway excavation To
accomplish this we performed the following tasks and work efforts
1 Research and review of available in house soil and boring data pertinent to

Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age and forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lava banking
against its eastern flank

After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep
valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows
followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu
Volcanic Series Due to the interaction of groundwater with the rising magma many of
the eruptions resulted in steam explosions that expelled large quantities of pyroclastic
material consisting predominantly of ash and cinder Some of these eruptions produced
ash that cemented together rapidly to form a firm rock known as tuff locally known as
mudrock This type of rock underlies the project site at depths of about 8 3 to 10 feet
below the ground surface Man made fills cover the mudrock to the existing ground
elevation
Existing Site Conditions
The project site is located to the south of Building 250 in the Makalapa area of
Pearl Harbor on the island of Oahu Hawaii Building 250 is a multi story structure of
reinforced concrete and masonry construction Based on the plans provided the
structure also has a basement and a lower level A concrete walkway extends around the
lower level of Building 250 Single story wood framed structures offices and a concrete
walkway extend from Building 250 at the Makalapa Drive level These structures span
the lower level walkway in the vicinity of the proposed stainway The proposed stairway
site is relatively level and covered with asphalt concrete
A retaining wall situated on a slope protected with a Cement Rubble Masonry
CRM facing is used for grade separation between the lower level walkway and the wood
framed offices A 4 foot high CRM wall is located south of the proposed stairway The
concrete retaining wall is located approximately 5 feet to the north of the proposed
stairway excavation and the CRM wall is located at a distance of about 3 feet to the
south
Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four exploratory
borings extending to depths ranging from about 8 to 40 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered a fill layer consisting of grayish brown
medium dense to dense sandy gravel to depths ranging from about 8 3 to 10 feet below
the ground The sandy gravel was covered by approximately 1 5 inches of asphalt
concrete in the four borings
In Boring and 4 the sandy gravel was underlain by moderately to slightly
weathered brownish gray tuff which is locally known as mudrock The tuff was

Discussion
Based on the plans provided and the subsurface conditions encountered the
depth to mudrock may be slightly deeper than that shown on the plans As a result the
existing concrete retaining wall foundation may not be bearing on the hard mudrock
However the retaining wall foundation may also have been extended to the depth of the
mudrock since concrete retaining wall footing was encountered in our boring at a slightly
deeper depth than that shown on the plans Due to the disparities between the
conditions encountered in our borings and the plans it is our opinion that the final design
for excavation bracing will need to be confirmed during construction
Limitations
The discussions submitted in this letter report are based in part upon information
obtained from field borings Variations of subsoil conditions between the borings may
occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until
construction is underway The stratification lines shown on graphic representations of the
borings depict the approximate boundaries between soil types and as such may denote
a gradual transition If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to reevaluate
the findings provided in this report

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 at the approximate locations shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of our borings ranged from about 8 to 40 feet below
the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Field Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
