REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be of the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of the late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during
which erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with
a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles
of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available
to fill the ocean basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea
When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacioeustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 7
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are transported
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the
stream flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this
transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load
consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines
When the erosional base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes
begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the
material Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the coarse
grained portion of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or
conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors
and upstream in headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is
transported downslope covering the older alluvial deposits
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer sediments
are generally transient in terms of geological time In addition their consistency and
density are generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits

proposed highway widening project traverses variable soil conditions ranging from soft
compressible soil deposits to stiff to hard residual soil deposits near the ground surface

the residual soils The principal geotechnical engineering considerations for the
proposed highway widening project include the following
stability of the existing embankment fills with respect to the existing steep
slopes after construction of the proposed new walls and fills on the east
and west sides of the highway
settlements of the underlying potentially compressible soils due to the new
fill loads on the west side of the highway
effects of settlements on the existing culvert structures located within
Valley and 3
foundation support of the proposed concrete retaining walls and the
proposed Mechanically Stabilized Earth Retaining Walls MSERW and
potential construction difficulties due to the limited space sloping terrain
of the project area and the high groundwater levels in the valleys

Foundations
As previously indicated improvement of the foundation soils at Valley No 1 will
be required to facilitate construction of the planned MSERW at this location The
selected method for improving the foundation soils in Valley No 1 is to remove the soft
compressible deposits and replace with free draining granular fill materials For Valley
Nos 2 and 3 the existing foundation soils are generally stiff to very stiff with potentially
compressible deposits located at greater depths The MSERW may be supported on
2 feet of compacted granular fill material such as aggregate base or subbase Section
703 06 or 703 17 of the Standard Specifications bearing on the existing foundation
soils in Valley Nos 2 and 3 Further details on the foundation requirements for the
MSERW and new fills are discussed below
Valley No 1
The existing foundation soils at Valley No 1 consist of very soft to soft silty clays
and clayey silts Basalt rock was encountered directly below the very soft to soft
soil deposits in Boring Nos 33 and 53 In Boring and 54 the soft
soils are underlain by stiff residual soils and basalt rock The removal and
replacement design concept entails the complete removal of the soft
compressible soils to expose competent materials such as stiff soils and or
basalt rock and replacement with free draining granular fill materials such as
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3087 00 Page 18
No 2 Rock ASTM C 33 No 4 gradation The No 2 rock is also equivalent to
the size 4 coarse aggregate in Section 703 05 of the Standard Specifications
Table of Geolabs Hawaii should be present to confirm
that the soft soils have been removed
This method of foundation soil improvement will require excavations on the order
of 15 to 20 feet or more to remove the soft compressible soils and to expose
competent material prior to backfilling the excavation with free draining granular
fill materials Use of a free draining type of granular fill material such as No 2
Rock ASTM C 33 No 4 gradation is recommended to facilitate placement of
fills in a wet environment within the excavation Because of the potentially large
voids between the free draining granular fill material we recommend that the
temporary shoring needed to support the excavation be abandoned in place to
reduce the potential for migration of fines from the adjacent fine grained soils
into the voids of the free draining granular fill foundation material
In addition a geotextile fabric Section 716 02 of the Special Provisions should
be placed above the free draining granular fill material to reduce the potential for
migration of fines from fills above the free draining granular materials A
minimum 2 foot layer of compacted granular materials such as aggregate base
or subbase materials Section 703 06 or 703 17 of the Standard Specifications
should be placed above the geotextile fabric prior to construction of the MSERW
The granular fill material should be compacted to not less than 95 percent
relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with AASHTO T 180 Test Methods

Several existing culverts are present in the three valleys We understand that
the existing culverts will generally remain in place with culvert extensions and new head
walls A new culvert may be constructed in one of the valleys It should be noted that
the new culvert should be installed before the MSERW is to be constructed in this area
Construction of the culvert prior to the MSERW construction would allow the
construction of the MSERW around the culvert However if the MSERW were to be
constructed prior to installation of the culvert excavation through the facing panel and
reinforced soil zone will be necessary and would be very difficult We should also point
out that the effects of the estimated settlements on the order of 1 foot on the existing
and new culverts should be evaluated by the Civil Engineer depending on the
installation sequence of the culvert and MSERW

Underground Utilities
For support of utility pipes we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of
6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the pipes
in stiff ground conditions Granular materials should also be used for trench backfill in
accordance with Section 703 21 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications The backfill up
to about 1 foot above the pipes should be lightly tamped to reduce the potential for
damaging the pipes The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot
above the pipes to the finish grade should be placed in 8 inch maximum horizontal
loose lifts and mechanically compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
since we anticipate the utility trenches to be below the roadway pavement

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 24
borings designated as Boring Nos 31 through 54 at the approximate locations shown
on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3 Boring Nos 1 through 30 were drilled for the project
by others and are not included in this report The depths of our borings range between
about 21 to 92 feet below the existing ground surface Seven of the borings were
drilled with a Diedrich D 25 skid mounted drill rig Another two borings were drilled
using portable drill rigs such as the MinuteMan and Concore drilling units The
remaining 15 borings were drilled using truck mounted drill rigs equipped with
continuous flight augers and advancing rotary coring tools as appropriate
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A of Appendix A Graphic presentation
of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 1
through A 24 2
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
nia sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to
drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Relatively undisturbed samples of the soft to medium stiff deposits were re
trieved using a Shelby tube sampler The Shelby tube sampler using a 2 inch diam
eter and 30 inch long tube was hydraulically pushed into the soil formation by the drill
rig The hydraulic pressures needed to advance the sampler 1 foot to obtain the
Shelby tube samples are recorded as the Penetration Resistance in pounds per square
inch psi per foot of penetration on the Logs of Borings
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left
in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of
GEOLABS HAWAII
Appendix A Continued Page 2
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock
its relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt with a high
RQD would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods
of excavation

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Six sieve analyses were performed on selected samples of the soils to evaluate
the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification The test results
are summarized on Plates B 1 through B 3 Graphic presentation of the grain size
distribution is presented on Plates B 5 through B 9 and Plate B 25
Ten Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of the
tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is shown on Plate B 4
Seven consolidation tests with time rates were performed on the potentially
compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the materials encountered
Results of the consolidation tests are presented on Plates B 10 through B 16
Twenty unconfined compression tests were performed on selected samples of
cohesive soils to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the material Results
of the unconfined compression tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Two unconsolidated undrained UU triaxial compression tests were performed
on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the soils The test
results are presented on Plates B 17 through B 18
One consolidated undrained CU triaxial compression test with pore pressure
measurements was performed ona selected soil sample to evaluate the undrained and
drained strength parameters The test results are presented on Plate B 19
Four direct shear tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the
shear strength of the material Results of the direct shear tests are presented on
Plates B 20 through B 23
One compaction test was performed on a bulk sample of the embankment fill
materials to evaluate the maximum dry density and optimum moisture content
relationship of the embankment fill The test results are presented on Plate B 24

Eight tests for pH and minimum resistivity were performed on the embankment
fill soils and foundation soils to evaluate the potential for corrosivity of the soils
Results of the pH and minimum resistivity values are presented on Plates B 1 through
