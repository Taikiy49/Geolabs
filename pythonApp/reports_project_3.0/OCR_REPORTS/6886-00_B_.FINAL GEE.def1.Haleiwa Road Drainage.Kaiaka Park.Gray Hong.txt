SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, our field exploration indicated that the project site is underlain by a 
relatively thin surface layer of fill consisting of medium dense sands over medium dense 
beach and estuarine deposits.  Below the beach and estuarine deposits, our exploration 
encountered lagoonal deposits consisting of loose to very loose silty sands.  Coralline 
reef formation was encountered in the deeper portions of Boring Nos. 3 and 4.  It is 
believed to be below the excavation limits for the new culvert in the area of 
Boring No. 3; however, it may be an excavation consideration in the area of 
Boring No. 4. 
We encountered groundwater in all of the borings; and, it is anticipated that 
dewatering will be required for the culvert construction.  It should be noted that the 
Haleiwa-Waialua area is a discharge area for a major artesian basin; and, that the 
groundwater levels may be elevated above normal base elevations.  The elevated 
groundwater levels, along with the sandy nature of the subsoils at the site, may make 
dewatering more difficult during construction.  
It is anticipated that the new culvert would have invert depths on the order of 
about 6 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings generally indicate very 
loose to loose silty sand soils within this zone. Based on the subsurface soil conditions 
encountered, we believe that conventional excavation methods may be used for 
installation of the culvert; however, shoring or trench wall sloping will most likely be 
required to stabilize the trench walls during construction. 
Due to the poor soil conditions encountered at the anticipated culvert invert 
depth, special trench bottom stabilization and bedding will be required to provide 
uniform support for the culvert structure.  
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
recommendations for design of the new drainage project.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration and 
engineering analyses performed for the Haleiwa Road Drainage project in Haleiwa on 
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations based 
on our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. The 
recommendations presented herein are intended for the design of trench excavation 
and backfilling only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to 
the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
Based on information from City and County of Honolulu, flooding occurs during 
heavy/prolonged rainfall and often results in road closures between Haleiwa Elementary 
School and Waialua Fire Station along the Kaiaka Bay Beach Park frontage.  The City 
and County of Honolulu Department of Design and Construction (DDC) is proposing the 
construction of drainage improvements to alleviate localized flooding along Haleiwa 
Road in the vicinity of Kaiaka Bay Beach Park.   
At present, storm water collects in a depressed area near the entrance of Kaiaka 
Bay Beach Park.  This low area does not have a drainage system to convey runoff away 
from it; and, the ponded water backs up on to Haleiwa Road and the surrounding 
properties.   
Under this project, it is proposed to convey storm water from the depression to 
Kaiaka Bay through an underground drainage system along the back of Haleiwa 
Elementary School crossing the entry of Kaiaka Bay Beach Park.  The underground 
drainage system will consist of a 1,200-foot long box culvert with an invert depth of 
6 to 11 feet below the ground surface.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be 
late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age.  The project site is situated on the northern 
coast of the Island of Oahu; and, is in the general area of the contact between the 
Waianae and Koolau shields. 
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of 
depositional and erosional events due to glacio-eustatic sea level changes and 
post-erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch.  The Koolau Volcanic 
Shield was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows. 
Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano, alluvial soil materials 
and marine sediments were deposited around the coastal margin of Oahu. The sea 
level fluctuated several times, receding perhaps 300 feet below and rising about 
100 feet above the present sea level. 
During the Recent Epoch, as the sea level rose to its current stand, the area was 
covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of soft and loose lagoonal marine 
deposits and intermixed beach and estuarine deposits.  Park development projects in 
the past 40 to 60 years have added man-made fills, bringing the site to its present 
configuration. 
2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is located in Kaiaka Bay Beach Park to the southwest of the town 
of Haleiwa on the North Shore of the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The park is situated next 
to, and behind, Haleiwa Elementary School; and generally consists of open lawn area 
with some mature trees.  Access from Haleiwa Road is by way of a single paved 
roadway, Fresh Air Camp Road, which forms a loop in the park.

3.1 
Culvert Bedding 
Our exploration generally encountered loose to very loose sandy soils at the 
anticipated invert depths.  In order to provide uniform support for the box culvert 
structure, we recommend over-excavating the trench a minimum of 2 feet below the 
invert elevation; and, providing a minimum of 18 inches of No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) below the culvert.  This material should be fully 
encapsulated in a non-woven geotextile, such as Mirafi 160N™, to provide a working 
platform.  A minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular bedding material, such as 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) should be provided above the 
encapsulated granular material and below the box.  
The granular bedding should extend beyond the sides a minimum width of 
one-fourth (1/4) the box width.  A Geolabs representative should observe the excavation 
work and installation of the bedding to confirm the materials exposed at the bottom of 
the trench or whether the bedding should be extended deeper.

the adjacent soils and surcharge effects.  Based on the anticipated subsoil conditions, we 
recommend designing the new structures to resist the following lateral earth pressures

Trench Excavation 
We anticipate that the new culvert inverts would be on the order of about 6 feet 
below the existing ground surface with depths on the order of 11 feet as the culvert 
passes under the berm on the western end of the alignment.  Installation of the 
proposed culvert will involve excavation through the surface fills and into the underlying 
lagoonal deposits.  It is anticipated that the fills and lagoonal deposits may be 
excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment. 
The above discussion regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials is 
based solely on field data obtained from the borings drilled at the project site. 
3.4 
Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate that excavation depths of up to about 8 to 9 feet below the ground 
surface with depths on the order of 13 feet as the culvert passes under the berm on the 
western end of the alignment will be required for the installation of the new box culvert 
and stabilization layer.  Where excavations greater than 4 feet in depth are planned or 
when ground water is encountered, temporary shoring or sloping and benching should 
be used.  
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and 
safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative (i.e., competent

Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal

It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the 
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. 
Due to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the 
magnitude of movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship, such 
as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed, the subsurface 
conditions, the size of the excavation, and the rate of excavation. 
Therefore, it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed 
properly and as early as practical. The adjacent ground should be continuously 
monitored for cracks, dips and/or other indications of movements until construction of 
the culvert, including backfilling, is completed.

Mean Sea Level (MSL).

Based on the groundwater levels and the permeable nature of the sandy soils 
anticipated within the excavation, dewatering will be required for the project 
construction.  Therefore, dewatering provisions will need to be included in the contract 
documents for the proposed construction.  Because the excavation will involve 
discharge of groundwater, a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) 
permit will be required for this discharge.  The contractor should consult their 
independent consultant for the latest regulations and information pertaining to the permit 
application. 
Because of the granular nature of the subsurface soils, it is anticipated that the 
soils encountered are relatively permeable.  Therefore, dewatering of the excavation 
may involve large volumes of water.  Dewatering by means of a well point system along 
the outside of the excavations is generally not recommended.  The resultant areal 
depression of the natural groundwater table could induce consolidation of the 
compressible subsurface soils resulting in potential ground settlements, which could 
affect nearby existing structures and roadways.  The potential impact of the dewatering 
system selected on depressing the natural groundwater table must be carefully 
evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering. 
It is our opinion that a cut-off wall system, such as interlocking sheet piles, should 
be considered to aid in dewatering the excavation.  However, sumps will be needed to 
collect water that percolates up into the base of the excavation or infiltrates through the 
sheet piles.  The sheet piles should be driven to a sufficient depth to reduce the 
potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the amount of dewatering within the 
excavations. 
The installation of the sheet pile shoring to the desired depths at the site may 
require predrilling of the sheet pile locations where hard coral is encountered.  The 
presence of these predrilled holes directly adjacent to the sheet pile shoring may serve 
as conduits for groundwater to infiltrate the sheet pile shored excavation.  This condition 
could result in large volumes of seepage water to infiltrate the shored excavation 
requiring additional dewatering efforts and disposal of the dewatered effluent.

groundwater level surrounding the proposed excavation.  The dewatering operations 
should be coordinated with the shoring support such that the stability of the excavations 
is not jeopardized.  The operations should be carried-out without softening the bottom of 
the excavations. 
It is our opinion that the definition of "Dewatering" in the contract documents 
should be written to include works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater 
table and/or to exclude water from the excavations to allow construction of the proposed 
structures under safe and dry conditions.  These works or systems may include, but are 
not limited to, pumping groundwater. 
3.5.1 Subsurface Soil Permeability 
Based on percolation tests performed as part of our exploration, we estimate that 
the subsurface permeability of the on-site soils is on the order of about 0.04 to 
0.1 centimeters per second, equivalent to 0.08 to 0.2 feet per minute. 
It is our opinion that the above estimated permeability values should be used with 
caution due to the normally heterogeneous nature of the coralline subsurface soils.  
The actual subsurface soil permeability may range broadly and also vary locally in 
terms of orders of magnitude.  Therefore, special attention should be given to the 
site-specific dewatering plan for the project.

The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural 
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering.  
It is recommended that the contractor retain a qualified geotechnical engineer to

The contractor should be solely responsible for the impact and safety of the 
dewatering operations.  His/her qualified representative, who should be 
continuously present on-site during dewatering activities, will have the best 
opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and to 
implement, as soon as possible, necessary precautionary or remedial measures 
including, but not limited to, slowing down or stopping the dewatering operations. 
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations, permeable granular subsurface 
soils may be susceptible to piping and “quick” conditions.  The dewatering 
operations should be carried-out without creating a “quick” condition or softening at 
the bottom of the excavations.  Therefore, the project dewatering operations should 
be performed without pumping out soil fines (pumping clear water only) and should 
be coordinated with the shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not 
adversely affected.  Excessive pumping, which removes soil fines, may result in 
“blowing” or heaving of the excavation bottom or sides. 
Groundwater drawdown outside the excavation will cause additional settlement 
resulting from consolidation of the loose and/or soft compressible soils.  Therefore, 
the use of a deep well system outside the excavation to draw down the 
groundwater level should not be allowed.

3.6 
Trench Backfill 
Based on our field exploration, the existing subsurface soils at the site are of 
suitable quality for use as trench backfill above water table.  The excavated material 
should be moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above optimum moisture content and 
processed to remove particles greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension.  Backfill 
under water table should consist of 3B Fine gravel.  
For the road crossing, the trench backfill above the box to 18 inches below the 
pavement subgrade may consist of excavated on-site material less than 3 inches in 
size. Backfill within 18 inches of the pavement subgrade should consist of imported 
non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coral or basalt. The material should 
be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest 
dimension. The material should also contain less than 25 percent particles passing the 
No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 25 or more and 
should have a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less. Imported materials should be 
tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site 
for the intended use. 
3.7 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfills should be moisture-conditioned to 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted 
to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  The upper 2 feet below the pavement grade 
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557.  
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density.  Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, 
vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during backfilling to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper 
moisture content.  Where compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort

3.8 
Culvert Headwall 
Based on our exploration, laboratory testing and analyses, an allowable bearing 
pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of the outlet and headwall 
structures when bearing on undisturbed cut soils.  If the footings are designed in 
accordance with our recommendations, total settlements of the structures are estimated 
to be on the order of less than 1 inch. 
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction between the base 
of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure developed against 
the near-vertical faces of the embedded portion of the foundation. A coefficient of 
friction of 0.40 may be used for design purposes based on bearing on the dense in-situ 
material. Resistance to lateral loads due to passive earth pressure may be calculated 
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). 
These values assume that the concrete for footings is cast neat against the surrounding 
soils or that the surrounding soils will be well compacted. Unless covered by pavements 
or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below the finished grade should 
be neglected. 
It is important that a Geolabs representative monitor the footing excavations to 
evaluate the competency of the bearing materials. If unsuitable materials are 
encountered in the footing excavations, these materials should be over-excavated to 
expose the underlying firm materials and replaced with compacted aggregate subbase 
materials.

3.10 Settlements 
Primary settlement of the culvert is normally caused by the difference in the unit 
weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipes.  The net increase in loading will cause settlement of the 
underlying subsoils below the trench invert.  Based on our calculations, settlement on 
the order of less than 0.5-inch is anticipated for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction.  Additional settlement could occur if 
improper trench support is used.
