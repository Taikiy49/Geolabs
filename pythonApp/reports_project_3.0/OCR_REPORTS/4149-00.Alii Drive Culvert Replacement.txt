INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Alii Drive Culvert Replacement project in the District of North Kona on the
Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations retaining
structures and site grading only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located along Alii Drive south of the intersection with Lunapule
Road in the District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii Based on the information
provided it is proposed to replace the existing culvert with a new bridge measuring
approximately 10 feet high 34 feet wide and 80 feet long The new bridge structure will be
a multi span structure with four equal spans of about 20 feet long and will be situated
generally to the northwest of the existing culvert as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on the information provided by the project structural engineer bent loads of up
to about 11 kips per lineal foot may be anticipated for the new bridge structure Therefore
we envision that the new bridge structure will be supported by continuous strip footings at the
pier and abutment locations
Based on our experience in the vicinity of the project site basalt rock formation may
be anticipated at the foundation subgrade level of the new replacement bridge Therefore
we envision that a shallow foundation system bearing on the basalt rock formation may be
used to support the new bridge structure In addition special attention was given to the
depth and quality of the rock formation encountered for both foundation support
considerations as well as for stream scour evaluations

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the southern flank of Mauna Loa which has formed the
west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the site appears to be
pahoehoe flow which spread and ponded as it approached the ocean Pahoehoe is
characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular
porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava These cavities are
formed when the lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow
cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes intra flow cavities
are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are
generally smaller in horizontal extent

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration at the bridge replacement site generally encountered medium
hard to hard competent basalt rock formation at the anticipated foundation subgrade level
of the new bridge structure Based on the competent subsurface conditions encountered in
our field exploration we believe that a shallow foundation system bearing on the medium
hard to hard basalt rock formation may be used to support the proposed new abutment and
pier structures
Due to the relatively high quality of the basalt formation encountered at the site we
believe that counter measures to reduce the potential for scour of the basalt rock formation
at the new footing elevations would not be required However counter measures to reduce
the effects of scour may be required for areas with soil backfill or for areas exposing the
on site fill soils To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse
of cavities below a footing we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be

implemented at the foundations for the abutments and pier structures during the project
construction

Seismic Design Considerations
Based on the design criteria provided by the County of Hawaii and State of
Hawaii Department of Transportation the Alii Drive Culvert Replacement project will need
to be designed based on a seismic acceleration coefficient of 0 35g Based on the
subsurface materials encountered in the borings drilled the project site may be classified
from a seismic analysis standpoint as a rock site corresponding to a Type Soil Profile A
seismic site coefficient of 1 0 may be used for the project site for seismic analysis of the
proposed bridge structure
Scour Potential
Scour is the result of erosive action of flowing water excavating and carrying away
material from the bed and banks of the stream Total scour over a period of time generally
consists of three components 1 Aggregation and Degradation 2 Contraction Scour and
3 Local Scour The rates of scour depend on a number of factors such as the shape and
dimensions of a pier or abutment depth of flow velocity of approach flow size and
gradation of stream bed material and bed configuration
One of the factors affecting the scour depth is the grain size characteristics of the
stream bed material The median diameter of the stream bed material D in conjunction
with the depth of flow and flow velocity is used to calculate the fall velocity of streambed
materials in scour depth analysis Based on the materials encountered in the borings
drilled for the project it is anticipated that high quality basalt rock formation will be
encountered at the proposed bottom of footing elevations of the new bridge about 12 feet
below the pavement grade The basalt rock formation at this depth may be considered
W O 4149 00 Page 8
a non erodible surface since its rock classification has a Rock Quality Designation RQD
of more than 50 percent and therefore should not be affected by scour
As a result of the high quality rock formation encountered at the site
counter measures to reduce the potential for scour will not be required for the new bridge
foundations provided that the bridge foundations bear directly on the high quality basalt
rock formation encountered in the drilled borings A representative of Geolabs Hawaii
should confirm the quality of the basalt rock formation at the bottom of footing elevations
during construction
Bridge Foundations
Based on the design criteria provided by the County of Hawaii the new bridge
foundations will be designed based on the Load and Resistance Factor Design LRFD
method Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we recommend that a shallow
foundation system bearing on the medium hard to hard slightly to moderately weathered
slightly fractured basalt rock formation encountered at a depth of about 12 feet below the
pavement grade be used for support of the planned bridge abutment and pier structures
Based on LRFD methods an ultimate bearing pressure of up to 32 000 pounds per
square foot psf may be used to evaluate the extreme event limit state of the footings
bearing on the slightly to moderately weathered medium hard to hard basalt formation To
evaluate the strength limit state of the foundations a bearing pressure of may be
used
In general footings should be embedded deep enough to bear directly on the
medium hard to hard slightly fractured basalt rock formation f soft and or loose materials

Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the area voids and or cavities may exist in the basalt rock
formation encountered at the project site To reduce the potential for loss of foundation
support resulting from the collapse of cavities below a footing we recommend that a program
of cavity probing and grouting be implemented during construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that a probe hole be drilled for every
100 square feet of foundation area or at 10 feet on center along the abutment and wing wall
foundations The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend at
least 10 feet below the planned bottom of footing elevation If cavities and or voids are
encountered or suspected during the probing operation additional probes should be drilled
at closer spacing to aid in delineating the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void
The probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout
injected at low to moderate pressures We recommend that a low strength sand cement
grout mixture with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that the probe drill be available on site until the probing and grouting operations
are complete It should be noted that a longer lag time between probing grouting operations
and foundation construction might be required in the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of
Cavities and or voids and to allow for additional recommendations to be made if excess grout
take and or changed conditions are observed

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths ranging from
about 15 5 to 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the
borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using portable drilling
equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with
a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would
be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical
Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
