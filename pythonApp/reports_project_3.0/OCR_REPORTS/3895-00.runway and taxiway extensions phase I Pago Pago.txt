INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Runway and Taxiway Extensions Phase 1 project within the Pago Pago
International Airport on the Island of Tutuila American Samoa The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location and Vicinity Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed runway extension project These geotechnical findings and recommendations
are intended for the design of pavements site grading and drainage structures only
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the
end of this report

The Pago Pago International Airport is located in the eastern part of the Island of

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
American Samoa is a group of seven islands including Tutuila Aunu u Ofu Olosega
Ta u Rose and Swains It has a total area of 76 square miles and is located approximately
2 276 miles south southwest of the State of Hawaii The Island of Tutuila was built during the
Pliocene and early Pleistocene age by the extrusion of basaltic lava and pyroclastic materials
from five volcanoes Following the cessation of the main shield building activity there was a
long period of erosion accompanied by the growth of fringing coral reefs During the late
Quaternary Age volcanism was renewed by eruptions of lava and pyroclastic materials at
several vents on the south side of the island forming the Tafuna Leone Plain
The main mountain mass of Tutuila was divided into seven formations The rocks are
thought to be of Pliocene Epoch consisting of thinly bedded to massive flows of basalt
andesitic basalt and andesite interbedded with cinder cones and tuff beds with thrachytic
dikes plugs and crater fills The rock units were erupted from as many as five eruptive
centers but the principal volcano was near the middle of the island Pago Pago Harbor is
believed to occupy part of the collapsed caldera of that principal volcano Erosion along a
major fault along the caldera wall formed the near vertical cliff on the northeast side of the
harbor
Prior to the eruption of the Leone Volcano beach and lagoonal deposits accumulated
on and behind a submerged barrier reef at the present site of the Leone Plain These deposits
were later buried under several hundred feet of volcanic materials These older beach and
lagoonal deposits consist partially of sand and coralline gravel
Three tuff cones and a thick sequence of lava flow form the plain between Tafuna and
Leone The rock is thought to be of Holocene Age possibly only a few thousand years old as
GEOLABS INC

evidenced by geologic relationships in the area The lava flows are mainly thinly bedded
basalt with interbeds of tuff and breccia
Clays silts sands and gravel have been deposited on the numerous valley floors
located close to the ocean by alluvial processes In some valleys the alluvial soil deposits
may be on the order of more than 100 feet thick
GENERAL SITE CONDITIONS
The Pago Pago International Airport is located in the eastern part of the Island of
Tutuila American Samoa The airport is surrounded by the gently sloping Tafuna Plain with
land uses such as industrial park schools warehouses and some residential neighborhoods
The existing Pago Pago International Airport is comprised of approximately 700 acres of land
with two paved runways support taxiways aprons and terminal facilities Runway 5 23 is the
primary runway with asphaltic concrete paved surface measuring about 9 000 feet long and
150 feet wide Runway 8 26 is a visual runway with asphaltic concrete paved surface
measuring about 3 800 feet long and 75 feet wide
The project site is located at the western end of the existing primary Runway 5 23 at
the southwestern edge of the Pago Pago International Airport on the Island of Tutuila
American Samoa The proposed runway extension project involves extending the
southwestern end of the existing Runway 5 by approximately 1 400 feet which includes an
additional 400 foot long blast pad In addition an asphaltic concrete paved turnaround area
will be provided at the western end of the extended primary Runway 5 near the new blast pad
area
The limits of the airport are generally bounded by chain link fence with some dirt
roads for vehicular access The shoreline along the southern and eastern part of the
airport is comprised of basalt rock outcrops indicating that basalt rock formation may lie
in this area within shallow depth The majority of the existing ground surface is relatively
flat and consists of sandy gravel and cobbles with silt matrix The proposed runway
extension alignment is generally covered by grass and shrubs and is generally accessible
GEOLABS INC
W O 3895 00 Page 7
to two wheel drive vehicles Ponding of rainwater or the presence of soft soil areas was
not observed at the time of our field exploration In addition we did not observe substantial
cracks settlements or potholes on the existing runway taxiway pavements
In general the existing lagoon consists of coral sand with shell and coral fragments
and some basalt cobbles and boulders in some areas Existing concrete riprap was observed
along the northeastern and southeastern edges of the lagoon Based on the current scope
of work for the Phase 1 development plan we understand that the project will involve widening
of the Runway 23 safety area including 3 165 lineal feet of shoreline protection along the
lagoon bordering the northern edge of Runway 23 A partially completed or segmented circle
was observed adjacent to Runway 23
Based on the grading plans provided existing ground surface elevations vary from
approximately 22 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the bottom of the lagoon to 50 feet MSL
in the vicinity of the new blast pad area

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the design guidelines presented in the Advisory Circular No
Airport Pavement Design and Evaluation prepared by the Federal Aviation Administration
FAA we understand that a stabilized pavement section would be required to accommodate
heavy aircraft weighing 100 000 pounds or more Based on the subsurface conditions
encountered and the forecast traffic mix provided we recommend that a stabilized pavement
section consisting of 5 inches of hot mix asphalt surface over 8 inches of cement treated base
GEOLABS INC
W O 3895 00 Page 9
course on 13 inches of aggregate subbase course be used for the proposed primary runway
extension planned at the Pago Pago International Airport
Alternatively an unstabilized pavement section consisting of 5 inches of hot mix asphalt
surface over 11 inches of crushed aggregate base course on 16 inches of subbase course
may be considered for the subject project However this would require the use of superior
materials with minimum California Bearing Ratio CBR values of 100 and 35 for the base and
subbase respectively Our experience in American Samoa suggests that such materials may
not be readily available in Pago Pago and it would be very difficult to produce such materials
in a consistent manner from the available quarries Unless it can be confirmed that the
superior materials are readily available in Pago Pago the unstabilized pavement section
should not be considered in the design for the subject project The base and subbase courses
for the stabilized and unstabilized pavement sections should be compacted to 100 percent
relative compaction Cement treated base course should be compacted to at least 98 percent
relative compaction based on ASTM D 558
Our field exploration along the proposed primary runway extension alignment generally
encountered dense cinder sands and basalt gravel overlying cobbles and rock formation at
greater depths In general we believe that the excavated materials may be re used as a
source of fill materials for embankment construction up to the bottom of the subbase course
provided that the materials are properly processed and placed in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Site Grading section of this report

primary functions of the base and subbase courses consist of the following
1 To provide uniform support for the pavement surface course throughout the
design life
2 To reduce potential pumping by providing drainage through the use of a material
with little or no fines
3 To provide a construction platform to expedite construction
The base and subbase materials required should conform to the project specification
requirements The on site excavated granular soils such as the in situ sandy coral gravel
may be re used as a source of subbase material provided that they meet the project
specification requirements The cement treated base and aggregate subbase courses should
be moisture conditioned to near and above the optimum moisture content respectively placed
in thin lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to not less than 98 and
100 percent relative compaction respectively Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 558 96 and 1557 91 test procedures for the cement
treated base and crushed aggregate subbase respectively Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

Cut materials generated from excavations into the underlying basalt formation may be

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the proposed primary runway extension alignment
were explored by drilling and sampling thirteen borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through
13 extending to depths ranging from about 15 to 17 feet below the existing pavement or
ground surface The subsurface conditions in the vicinity of the lagoon along the shoreline
protection areas were explored by drilling two probeholes designated as Probe Hole
Nos 1 and 2 to depths of about 34 and 35 feet below the existing ground surface The profile
of the basalt rock formation at the Runway 5 safety area was explored by excavating two test
pits designated as Test Pit Nos 1 and 2 at the existing ground surface The approximate
locations of the drilled borings probe holes and test pits are shown on the Site Plans
Plates 2 1 and 2 2 The borings and probeholes were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist and engineer who observed the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 13 Results of the
probe hole penetration are presented on the Logs of Probe Holes Plates A 14 and A 15
Results of the test pit excavation are presented on the Log of Test Pits Plate A 16
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the dense basalt rock formations encountered at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring
attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 4 feet of material is recovered from
a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the RQD
would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally
the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical
Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense rock formation with a high RQD
would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of excavation

Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations were
performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths

samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are presented on Plates B 1
through B 3
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils
Dynamic compaction of the sample was performed by dropping a 10 pound hammer from a
height of 18 inches The samples were compacted in five equal layers with each layer
receiving 56 blows of the hammer The samples were soaked for one to three days prior to
the penetration test Results of the tests are presented on Plates B 4 through B 7
