2 1 Regional Geology
Saipan and the other Mariana Islands occur along an eastwardly convex oceanic
ridge for approximately 400 miles north to south Parallel to this ridge approximately
60 to 100 miles further east is a deep marine trench The trench coincides with a zone
of earthquake foci occurring at increasing depth westward from it indicating the
probable presence of a tectonically active subduction zone beneath the island chain
In general the Island of Saipan consists of a volcanic interior surrounded by
younger limestones and recent sedimentary deposits Major northeast trending faults
have displaced and locally folded the volcanic rocks and limestone deposits on the
island It is believed that movements along some faults have been recurrent at intervals
from probably Pre Miocene to recent time
The project site is located along the northeastern flank of Mount Tagpochau
Tagpochau limestone is dominated by a massive compact inequigranular pinkish
limestone but also includes rubbly marly tuffaceous and transitional carbonate facies
deposits

3 2 Foundation Probing
Based on our experience in the project vicinity cavities and or voids may be
present in the coral limestone To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support
resulting from collapse of the cavities below foundations we recommend that a program
of cavity probing and grouting at the foundation locations be conducted during
construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each interior
foundation location and at 10 foot on center for the perimeter wall foundation The probe
holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below
the planned bottom of foundation elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or
suspected during the probing operation additional probes should be drilled at closer
spacing to help delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The
probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected
at low to moderate pressures A typical sand cement grout should have a slump of about
8 inches
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and
grouting operation is complete The contractor should be made aware that a longer lag
time between probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be required
in the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
Geolabs representative This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of cavities and
to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or changed
conditions are observed

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general structural fills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture and compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The base course layer below the
reservoir should also be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density

Because the project site is located on the seismically active area Seismic Zone 4
forces due to dynamic lateral earth pressures may need to be considered in the
design of retaining walls The force due to dynamic lateral earth pressures
may be estimated using 16H pounds per foot of wall length for level
backfill conditions where H is the height of the wall in feet For a maximum 3H 1V
sloping backfill condition dynamic lateral earth forces due to seismic loading may
be estimated using 28H pounds per lineal foot of wall
It should be noted that the forces due to dynamic lateral earth pressures provided
assume that the wall will be allowed to move laterally by up to about 2 to 4 inches in
the event of an earthquake If this amount of lateral movement is not acceptable
the retaining wall should be designed with higher dynamic lateral forces for a
semi restrained wall condition For a semi restrained wall condition the forces due
to dynamic lateral earth pressures may be estimated using 28H pounds per lineal
foot of wall for level backfill conditions

Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important
design consideration For pipes bearing on the stiff to very stiff silty clays and
medium dense to dense silty and clayey gravel and sands a minimum of 6 inches
of compacted granular bedding material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation should be provided below the pipe
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of
one fourth 1 4 the outside pipe diameter and should extend half way up the sides
of the pipe Before placing the bedding material the excavated trench bottom
should be observed by a Geolabs representative to confirm that firm materials are
exposed at the bottom of the trench or if the bedding should be extended deeper as
recommended above
Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Saipan
CNMI s Commonwealth Utilities Corporation CUC Water System Standards The
first zone of backfill extending from the bedding material to at least 12 inches above
the top of the pipes should consist of select free draining well graded granular
material to reduce the compaction effort required and resulting stresses on the
pipe The granular material should be well graded less than 1 inch in maximum
size and should have less than 10 percent passing the No 200 sieve Compaction
by water ponding or jetting may be allowed provided the granular material is
self draining such as No 3B Fine gravel
The trench backfill from 12 inches above the top of the pipes to the finished
subgrade may consist of the excavated on site clayey gravelly and sandy soils
provided that they are free of deleterious materials vegetation and are screened of
particles greater than 6 inches in largest dimension The backfill should be placed
in thin lifts normally 8 inches in loose lift thickness with each lift mechanically
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction

Thrust Blocks and Earth Pressure on Pipes
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide
resistance to the thrust forces due to the pressurized pipes The thrust force acting
on the pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of
the thrust block Based on the available information the planned inverts are about
5 feet below the existing ground surface
Based on our field exploration medium dense to hard coral limestone was
encountered at shallow depths We recommend a lateral bearing pressure of
2 500 pounds per square foot psf be used for the design of thrust blocks
Settlements
Primary settlement of the new water main construction is normally caused by the
difference in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier
compacted backfill material placed over the pipes The net increase in loading will
cause settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert Based on our
calculations primary settlement on the order of less than 1 inch is anticipated for
the project

3 6 Soil Corrosion Study
The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and
structures are dependent on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in the
soil A soil corrosion study was performed to obtain an overview of the corrosion
characteristics of the subsurface soils along the proposed 8 inch and 10 inch diameter
transmission main planned at the project site The pH and resistivity of the soil was
determined in general conformance with Test Methods for Evaluating Solid Waste
Physical chemical Methods Edition Methods for Chemical Analysis of
Water and Wastes Detailed findings and discussions are presented in the following
subsections of this report Results of the soil analyses conducted on selected soil
samples are summarized on Plate C 1 of this report
In general our test results indicate that the subsurface materials along most of
the 8 inch and 10 inch diameter transmission main alignment may be classified as
corrosive
3 7 Drainage
The finished grades outside the new structures should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive landscape
watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to
foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the
potential for water infiltration into the subsoils Drainage swales should be provided as
soon as possible and maintained to drain surface run off away from the slabs and
foundations

The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring extending to depths of about 50 feet
below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Enlarged
Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 8 The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling
equipment equipped with continuous flight augers In addition 10 test pits were
excavated along the proposed water transmission line using a Case 580 backhoe to
depths extending from 2 to 8 feet below the existing ground surface The locations of
the test pits are shown on the Enlarged Site Plans Plates 8
The materials encountered in the borings and test pits were classified by visual
and textural examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling and test
pit operations on a continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by
visual observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general
conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representation of the materials encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings and
Logs of Test Pits Plates A 1 through A 12
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
