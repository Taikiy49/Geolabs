SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings encountered a surface layer of volcanic ash over dense basalt 
formation extending down to the maximum depth explored of about 21 feet below the 
ground surface.  The volcanic ash generally consisted of medium stiff silty soils with very 
high moisture content and generally extended to depths of 1 to 2 feet below the ground 
surface.  Thicker ash material may be present in localized spots at the site, as 
encountered in Boring No. 3, extending to about 4.5 feet below the ground surface.  The 
basalt formation encountered was generally hard and slightly weathered. 
In general, cavities and/or voids are commonly present in the basaltic lava flows of 
the vicinity.  Loose clinker materials are also commonly interbedded in the basaltic lava 
flows.  Therefore, the presence of clinker, cavities and/or voids should be expected during 
construction.  We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our 
field exploration, except seepage water was encountered in Boring No. 2 at a depth of 
about 2.5 feet below the ground surface. 
We recommend removal of the surface ash materials to expose the underlying 
basalt rock formation within the footprints of the building structures, driveways and parking 
lots. The over-excavation should be backfilled with structural fills and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
We recommend using shallow spread and/or continuous footings to support the 
new building structures planned. As an alternative, foundations for support of the structural 
loads of the new structures planned also may consist of thickened-edge slab footings. An 
allowable bearing pressure of up to 4,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for 
the design of footings bearing on dense basalt formation or compacted fill. 
Cavities and/or voids are commonly encountered in the pahoehoe lava flows that 
characterize the project site.  We recommend implementing a program of cavity probing 
and grouting of the new structure foundations. We recommend drilling probe holes at 
10-foot on centers for the continuous strip footings and/or thickened-edge wall 
foundations.  In addition, probe holes should be drilled at each isolated spread footing 
(or column) location (one probe per 50 square feet).  The probe holes should be at least 
3 inches in diameter and should extend to a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned 
bottom of foundation. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
design recommendations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an 
area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct); Mauna Kea (activity during recent 
geologic time); Hualalai (erupted in 1801 - 1803) and Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both still 
active). 
The project site is on the northeastern flank of the Mauna Loa Shield Volcano, 
which had been built up by the successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and 
pyroclastic materials. The lava formation encountered at the site appears to consist of 
both a'a and pahoehoe type flows, which spread and ponded as they approached the 
ocean. A’a lava is typically characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface 
resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and boulders. 
Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal 
structure of vesicular (porous) rock. 
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas, especially pahoehoe type 
lava. Cavities form when the lava is still in a molten state, and they represent both lava 
tubes (intra-flow cavities) and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form 
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow, leaving a hollow tube-like structure, 
which may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets 
are generally smaller in horizontal extent.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling seven borings, designated 
as Boring Nos. 1 through 7, extending to depths of about 5 to 21 feet below the existing 
ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on our borings, the project site is underlain by a surface layer of volcanic 
ash over dense basalt formation extending down to the maximum depth explored of 
about 21 feet below the ground surface.  The volcanic ash generally consisted of 
medium stiff silty soils with very high moisture content and generally extended to depths 
of 1 to 2 feet below the ground surface.  Thicker ash material may be present in 
localized spots at the site, as encountered in Boring No. 3, extending to about 4.5 feet 
below the ground surface.  The basalt formation encountered was generally hard and 
slightly weathered. 
In general, cavities and/or voids are commonly present in the basaltic lava flows 
of the vicinity.  Loose clinker materials are also commonly interbedded in the basaltic 
lava flows.  Therefore, the presence of clinker, cavities and/or voids should be expected 
during construction. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration, except seepage water encountered in Boring No. 2 at a depth of about 
2.5 feet below the ground surface. However, groundwater levels likely will change in 
response to seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the field exploration methodology and graphic

Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in 
the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to 
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or

Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small that 
they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of the earthquakes 
are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate damage.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings encountered a surface layer of volcanic ash over dense basalt 
formation extending down to the maximum depth explored of about 21 feet below the 
ground surface.  The volcanic ash generally consists of medium stiff silty soils with very 
high moisture contents and generally extends to depths of 1 to 2 feet below the ground 
surface. Thicker ash material should be anticipated in localized spots at the site, as 
encountered in Boring No. 3, extending to about 4.5 feet below the ground surface.  The 
basalt formation encountered was generally hard and slightly weathered.  
It should be noted that cavities and/or voids are commonly present in the basaltic 
lava flows of the vicinity.  Loose clinker materials are also commonly interbedded in the 
basaltic lava flows.  Therefore, the presence of clinker, cavities and/or voids should be 
expected during construction.  We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings 
at the time of our field exploration, except seepage water encountered in Boring No. 2 at 
a depth of about 2.5 feet below the ground surface. 
We recommend removal of surface ash materials to expose the underlying basalt 
rock formation within the footprints of the building structures, driveways and parking

at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of foundation.

3.1 
Site Grading 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site, we 
recommend removal of the surface ash materials to expose the underlying basalt rock 
formation within the footprints of the building structures, driveways and parking lots.  
The over-excavation limits should be extended beyond the footprints of the building and 
driveway/parking for at least 5 feet in horizontal distance.  The over-excavation should 
be backfilled with structural fills and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction.  The contractor should anticipate variable thickness of the ash material at 
the site, as evident from Boring No. 3 where the ash extends to about 4.5 feet below the 
ground surface. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the site grading operations to observe 
whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and scarification 
process, and to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar to those 
encountered in our field exploration. 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, old 
foundations, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly 
off-site to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials to be used 
as fill materials. 
Finished subgrades in cut areas and areas to receive fills should be proof-rolled 
with a minimum 10-ton (static weight) vibratory drum roller for a minimum of 
eight passes to help detect and collapse near-surface cavities and/or voids.  The 
vibratory drum roller should also be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. 
The proof-rolling operations should be performed in the presence of a Geolabs 
representative.  Yielding areas, loose areas, or cavities disclosed during clearing and 
proof-rolling operations should be over-excavated and backfilled with compacted fill

This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3)

In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the 
lowest adjacent exterior grade.  Where loose clinker materials are exposed at the 
footing subgrades, the clinker materials should be recompacted to provide firm and

clinkers and basalt rock formation are exposed at the foundation subgrade level, we

with compacted structural fills for uniform bearing support. 
Foundations next to retaining walls, other foundations, utility trenches, or 
easements should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward 
from the bottom edge of the structure or utility trench. Alternatively, footings should be 
extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines or bottom of the retaining 
walls. This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade 
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable 
foundation settlement. 
Based on our experience in the project vicinity, cavities and/or voids are 
commonly encountered in the pahoehoe lava flows that characterize the project site. To 
reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from the collapse of cavities 
below foundations, we recommend implementing a program of cavity probing and 
grouting of the new structure foundations.  Foundation probing and grouting 
requirements are further discussed in the following "Foundation Probing and Grouting" 
section. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of foundations to be less than 
1 inch. Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on similar materials 
may be on the order of about 0.5 inches.

location (one probe per 50 square feet).  The probe holes should be at least 3 inches in 
diameter and should extend to a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned bottom of 
foundation.

3.4 
Slabs-on-Grade 
For interior slabs that will not be subjected to vehicular traffic or sustained 
vibration, we recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill below 
the slabs to provide uniform support.  The cushion fill material should consist of open-
graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation).  In addition, the cushion fill layer also 
would serve as a capillary moisture break.  To reduce the potential for excessive future 
moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings, an impervious moisture 
barrier is recommended on top of the cushion fill layer.  Flexible floor coverings should 
be considered on top of the slab because they can better mask minor slab cracking. 
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic or sustained vibration, we 
recommend providing a 6-inch layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs in lieu of the

3.5 
Leach Field 
We understand that wastewater effluent generated from the new facility will be 
disposed of on-site.  Therefore, we envision that the disposal of wastewater effluent will 
be by the leach field method.  Field percolation tests were conducted during our field 
exploration to obtain data pertaining to the preliminary design of the wastewater 
disposal system.  Two field percolation tests were performed at the locations of 
Boring Nos. 6 and 7. 
The field percolation tests were performed in general accordance with the 
provisions of the State of Hawaii, Department of Health’s Administrative Rules, Chapter 
11-62-31.2, “Wastewater Systems” and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards to 
evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the subsurface soils for wastewater disposal.  
The tests were performed by drilling 4-inch diameter boreholes extending to a depth of 
about 5 feet below the existing ground surface.  Approximately 6 inches of gravel was 
then placed at the bottom of the boreholes to protect the bottom of the holes from 
scouring.  The test holes were presoaked for at least 4 hours. 
During the testing, the holes were carefully filled with water to a depth of about 
12 inches above the gravel layer.  The time intervals and water drops were then 
recorded to provide data upon which the calculation of the percolation rate was based.

Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, 
No. 67 gradation), should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches 
above the pipes.  It is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for 
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the 
sides of the pipes.  Improper backfill material around the pipes and improper placement 
of the backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage.

12 inches above the top of the pipes) may consist of 1-inch minus, well-graded granular 
materials (aggregate base materials). Where the 1-inch minus, well-graded granular 
materials are used, the granular bedding and the initial trench backfill should be 
moisture-conditioned and compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction.

to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well-graded granular 
materials less than 6 inches in maximum particle size.  The backfill should be

3.9 
Drainage 
The finished grades outside the buildings and other structures should be sloped 
to shed water away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for 
ponding.  In addition, it is advised to install roof gutter systems around the buildings and 
to divert the discharge away from the foundation and slab areas.  Excessive landscape 
watering near the foundations and slabs also should be avoided.  Planters next to 
foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the 
potential for excessive water infiltration into the subsurface, especially subsurface 
conditions consisting of fill materials. 
The foundation excavations should be backfilled properly against the walls or 
slab edges immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce the potential for 
appreciable water infiltration into the subsurface.  In addition, drainage swales should 
be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water runoff 
away from the foundations and slabs.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
seven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 7, extending to depths of about 5 to 
21 feet below the existing ground surface.  The borings were drilled with a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools.  
The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  Soils, where encountered, were classified in general 
conformance with the Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on the Soil Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.1.  Symbols and abbreviations used in the boring logs are also 
described on the Soil Log Legend.  Deviations made to the soil classification in 
accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil Classification Log Key, 
Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the 
Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-7. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In addition, we obtained 
some samples from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow counts needed to 
drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. 
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound 
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any
