INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed The Bluffs Subdivision in
Princeville on the island of Kauai Hawaii The general location of the
project site is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our exploration and our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses based
on the data obtained during the exploration These recommendations are
intended for design input and are not intended for construction estimation
or bidding purposes

W O Page 2
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to subdivide the project site into a residential
development with both townhouse and detached single family lots No
site grading plans are currently available however we anticipate that
a major portion of the site grading will be fill placement to raise the
grade of parts of the site as much as 15 feet or more
It is our understanding that the westernmost portion of the site
along the golf course will be used as a disposal site for rubble and
other material generated from the demolition of an existing hotel in
Princeville This rubble material will generally consist of concrete
slabs and fragments and will be placed in the deeper portions of the
fill

project

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated in the Princeville area on the
northern coast of the island of Kauai Hawaii The project site is
located to the east of the existing Princeville development area in the
Princeville Phase II Master Plan area on top of the bluffs overlooking
Anini Beach The general location of the site and its vicinity is shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
Formerly the site was used for the cultivation of pineapple and
then as a pasture for the grazing of cattle and horses Currently the
site is being used as a staging area for the on going construction of
the Phase II golf course and or being maintained as a well trinmed open
field
Based on our field reconnaissance the available topographic map
and site plan it appears that the site slopes gently towards the north
and the existing ground elevation ranges from about 220 to 300 feet
above Mean Sea Level MSL At the northernmost end of the site beyond
the project limits the ground surface slopes down to the north forming
the bluffs overlooking the Anini Beach area These slopes grade from
relatively gentle at the top to near vertical at the base
Along portions of the eastern boundary of the site there are steep
Slopes overlooking Anini Valley These slopes are at ratios of about
one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The project site is situated on a broad gently sloping plateau
along the north shore of the island of Kauai This plateau was built
during the late Pliocene and Pleistocene by the extrusion of basaltic
GEOLABS HAWAII
W 10 Page 5
lavas and related pyroclastics from the Pooku vent of the Koloa Volcanic
Series
Most of the soils in the project area are residual soils derived
from the in situ weathering of these basic igneous rocks The residual
soils grade with depth to saprolite a form of residual soil which
retains the relict structures vesicles and joints of the parent rock
In former pasture areas the upper foot or so of soil has been
reworked as a result of the past agricultural activities and generally
has a high organic content
Soil Conditions
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling
six 6 shallow borings to depths of 11 5 to 36 5 feet and one
145 3 foot deep boring below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the soil conditions encountered throughout the project
site appear relatively uniform Most of our drilled borings encountered
a surface layer between 1 and 4 feet thick of medium stiff to stiff
brown clayey silt topsoil
Below the topsoil layer the borings generally encountered stiff to
very stiff reddish brown to brown brown clayey silts varying in
thickness between 4 0 and 13 0 feet These are residual soils that
derived from the in situ weathering of Koloa Basalts and are generally
structureless
Underlying the residual soils our borings encountered generally
medium stiff to stiff saprolite soils consisting of clayey silts and
silts having predominantly brown color mottled with black spots or
streaks to the maximum drilled depth of the borings Occasional softer
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O Page 6
pockets and or seams were encountered in the saprolite layers at depths
generally less than 15 feet below the ground surface The saprolite
soils appear to extend to depths of about 75 feet below the surface
Below the saprolite our deep boring encountered extremely
weathered basalt which at a depth of about 142 feet graded to weathered
basalt
During our field exploration groundwater was encountered in Boring
109 and Boring A at depths of 26 1 and 91 0 feet respectively below
the existing ground surface This groundwater is probably in a perched
condition and is generally the result of low permeability ash beds or
lenses within the Koloa Volcanic Series Therefore groundwater may be
encountered in deep excavations within the project site
DISCUSSTON AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The field exploration encountered generally competent subsoils
within the project site However modified grading techniques are
required for the earthwork portion of the proposed site improvements due
to the relatively high in situ moisture content of the subsoils and the
relatively high rainfall occurring in the area
We recommend that shallow spread footing foundations be used to
support the proposed residential structures
Site Grading
The proposed development is in the conceptual stage and detailed
grading plans are not available However fills of up to about 10 to 15
feet or more in thickness are anticipated in the western portion of the
site

Fill Compaction
Since the project site is located in a high rainfall area most of
the subsoils within the top 15 feet had in situ moisture contents
of about 40 to 55 per cent at the time of our exploration These
moisture contents are considerably higher than the anticipated
optimum moisture contents which range from about 32 to 36 percent
for similar soils in the Princeville area Therefore considerable
drying of the excavated materials would normally be required prior
to their use as fill materials
However in view of the relatively frequent rainfall that generally
occurs in the area throughout the year complete drying of large
quantities of soils might not be readily achieved during mass
grading operations To facilitate construction it is our opinion
that the normal 90 ASTM D 1557 78 compaction standard for general
site fills as used in drier areas may be reduced to 85 for this
project Our previous experience in Princeville and other similar
areas indicates that 85 compaction is generally adequate for site
fills under roadway and light building loads
Fills within 12 inches below roadway or footing subgrade should be
compacted to a minimum of 90 of its ASTM D dary
density

Fill Placement
Based on the field exploration and laboratory test results it is
our opinion that the on site soils have low expansion potential and
except for the high moisture content they may be used as a source
of fill and backfill materials If additional off site borrow is
required the material should be tested and approved by the
geotechnical engineer prior to hauling to the site to see that it
is suitable for the proposed construction The material should
generally be non expansive and should not contain large percentages
of boulders organic or deleterious matters

have dry densities in the range of about 65 to 88 p c f When they

Rubble Fill
It is our understanding that it is desired to use the deep fill
portions of the site for the disposal of concrete rubble material
from the demolition of a hotel in another part of Princeville In
order to dispose of this rubble the following recommendations
should be followed
1 The rubble fill should be placed deep enough not to interfere
with future building foundations and utility installations We
recommend a minimum separation of five 5 feet of fine grained
soil between the rubble fill and finished grade Care should
also be exercised to avoid placement of rubble fill below
proposed sewerage lift stations or other deep excavations

2
The rubble material should be reduced to relatively well graded
particles generally less than six 6 inches in diameter and
compacted so that a stable mass is formed to support the upper
fill Reinforcing steel should be removed or cut off flush
with the surface of the concrete rubble
Compactive effort should be applied by either a heavy vibratory
drum compactor such as a Raygo 410 or a large bulldozer such
as a Caterpillar D 8 A testing program should be implemented
to determine the actual lift thickness and number of passes
required to achieve adequate compaction
If it is necessary to place large slabs of concrete the slabs
should be lain flat at the base of the fill with sufficient
space between the slabs to allow compaction equipment to work
between the slabs A minimum of two 2 feet of fine grained
rubble or soil should be placed between lifts of slabs
Unless the rubble can be reduced to a well graded material and
all voids filled during placement a choking layer of
progressively smaller granular material should be placed on the
top of the rubble fill to act as a filter to minimize the loss
of fines from the upper fill This choking layer should be a
minimum of eighteen 18 to twenty four 24 inches in
thickness Alternately a layer of geotextile filter fabric
such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent could be placed over the
rubble fill in lieu of the choking layer
The placement of the rubble fill should be under the full time
observation of the project geotechnical engineer or his
representative so that procedures may be modified if
necessary

Fill Settlement
It is our understanding that the proposed site grading will include
the placement of fills up to about 20 feet deep at the project
site Due to the reduced compaction level which will be used in
the earthwork for this project we anticipate that fills will
continue to consolidate or settle after the completion of the
earthwork
To reduce the potential of settlement induced distresses to future
structures we recommend that settlement gauges be placed at the
surface of all new fills greater than five 5 feet deep These
gauges should be surveyed periodically for about three 3 months
for vertical movements and the readings should be transmitted to
the project geotechnical engineer for review and analyses
Construction of structures and or utilities should be delayed for a
minimum of several months until fill settlements have reached
tolerable rates We believe the future development scheduling
should incorporate this fill settlement waiting period The
results of the final settlement gauge readings should be used to
verify when structural construction can be initiated
Steep Existing Slopes
The easternmost portion of the project site encroaches on the steep
existing slopes overlooking Anini Valley These slopes grade from
gentle at the top to about one horizontal to one vertical 1V 1H in
their mid points Our exploration indicates that the upper portions of
these slopes are composed of soil materials i e residual soils and
saprolite to depths of about 75 feet below the existing ground surface
Over the years there have been several slides on these steep
existing slopes The majority of these slides have been shallow seated
failures or skin tears involving only the slope surface to a depth of a
few feet However there appears also to have been more deeply seated
slides

Based on limited engineering analyses it is our opinion that a
minimum structural set back of forty 40 feet away from the tops of
these slopes is necessary to maintain an adequate factor of safety for
future residential structures located in the single family increment
Lot Nos 22 through 26 inclusive
Subdrains
Due to the wet climate conditions and based upon previous
experience of encountering seepage in drainageways in the Princeville
area we recommend that subdrains be installed along the base axes of
drainageways and side slopes of fill embankment to relieve potential
seepage which may occur or accumulate along the interface between the
existing natural ground and the new fill See Plates 4 and 5 Such
accumulation of water could create a plane of weakness between the fill
and the existing subsoils potentially causing slippage or creep of the
fill The final location and quantity of subdrains should be determined
in the field by the project geotechnical engineer
Foundations
Based on the soil conditions we believe that shallow spread and
continuous footing foundations bearing on the recompacted in situ
subsoils may be used to support the proposed residential structures
An allowable bearing value of up to 2 500 p s f with a minimum
footing embedment of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent grade is
recommended The bearing value is for total dead plus live load only
and may be increased by one third 1 3 for short duration wind or
seismic loading
For buildings to be constructed on or adjacent to slopes the
building should be set back or the depth of the footing embedment
extended so that there is a minimm of ten 10 feet except as
discussed above between the toe of the footing and the outside slope
face

If a footing foundation is located next to a utility trench the
footing should be extended to the bottom of the utility trench or be
designed to span over the trench to reduce footing settlement due to
settlement of the trench backfill
Based on the boring data estimated total footing settlements under
the design loads are anticipated to be on the order of less than one 1
inch total and one half 1 2 of an inch differential
Resistance to lateral loads on the proposed structure may be
provided by passive soil pressure and frictional resistance A
frictional coefficient of 0 4 may be used to compute the frictional
resistance between the bottoms of the footings and the recompacted
in situ soils In addition an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 p s f
per foot of depth may be used against the buried vertical faces of the
footings to determine the passive soil pressure
Slabs On Grade
Slab on grade construction with 4 inch concrete slabs placed over a
4 inch layer of 3B Fine gravel may be used for the proposed residential
structures The thickened edges of the on grade slabs should extend a
minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent ground surface For
slabs located adjacent to slopes the building should be set back or the
depth of embedment extended so that there is a minimum of ten 10 feet
except as discussed above between the toe of the thickened edge and the
outside slope face
To protect against moisture damage of the floor covering material
a moisture barrier should be considered for use below the slabs
Our laboratory test results indicate that the on site soils in
general have low swelling potential at their natural moisture contents
and exhibit a tendency to shrink when allowed to dry Therefore the
slab subgrade should be kept moist and not allowed to dry out using

Retaining Walls
If sewerage pump stations or other buried structures are to be
installed as part of the site improvements the walls should be designed
as retaining walls under a restrained condition
An allowable bearing value of 2 500 p s f is recommended for the
design of wall foundations bearing on recompacted natural ground
Granular materials such as base course rock 3B Fine gravel sand
or similar should be used as backfill material directly behind the
walls This would reduce the compactive effort required for the
backfill and reduce the build up of additional lateral pressures on the
walls Impervious on site silty material should be used for the top 2
feet of the backfill
We recommend that the walls be designed to resist a lateral earth
pressure of 55 p c f equivalent fluid pressure under restrained
condition

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based
in part upon information obtained from field data points such as
borings Variations of conditions between the field data points may
occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become
evident until construction If variations then appear evident it will
be necessary to re evaluate the recommendations given in this report
The locations of the field data points were determined by taping
from reference points shown on The Bluffs Preliminary Plan by Belt
Collins Associates dated Elevations of the data
points were determined by interpolation from contours and elevation
points on the same plan The physical location and elevation of the
field data points should be considered accurate only to the degree
implied by the method used
The stratification lines shown in graphic representations of the
field data points depict the approximate boundaries between soil types
and as such may denote a gradual transition The strata lines shown
on profiles or cross section are based upon interpolation between field
data points and may not represent actual subsurface conditions
Water level data from the field data points were collected at the
times given on the graphic representations and or in the text of this
report These data have been reviewed and interpretations made in the
formulation of this report However it must be noted that fluctuation
in water level may occur due to variations in rainfall tides
temperature and other factors

Placement and Compaction of Fill
A
The fill shall be placed in horizontal layers which will not
exceed ten inches 10 in loose thickness Each layer shall be
spread uniformly and blade mixed to attain uniformity of
material and water content within each layer
Additional fill material shall not be placed on any fill layer
which has not been properly compacted Subsequent removal of
any unacceptable layer shall be done at the Contractor s
expense
Rock fragments or boulders less than twelve inches 12 in
diameter may be utilized provided they are not concentrated in
any one area and that there is sufficient fine grained material
to fill the voids between the rocks
Rocks greater than twelve inches 12 in diameter shall be
removed from the fill unless specifically approved for placement
in the fill by the Geotechnical Engineer
All fill shall be compacted to a minimum of 85 percent of the
maximum dry density established by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
unless otherwise specified in the Geotechnical Engineer s
Report
Boulders encountered in excavations may be used in the deeper
fills provided that the following are followed
1 Boulders shall be spread out and must not be nested
together They should be placed so that compaction
equipment will be able to compact around then
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O Plate C 8
Appendix C Continued
2 No part of the boulder should encroach any closer to
finished subgrade than the boulder s largest dimension and
in no case be closer than 3 feet to finished subgrade
3 No boulder larger than 5 feet in its largest dimension
should be placed in the fill It may be necessary to reduce
the size of larger boulders or dispose of them off site
4 Care must be exercised to avoid placement of boulders in the
proposed utility alignments to prevent difficulty in the
later excavation of the utility trenches
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in
rubble fill due to the large particle size Instead a testing
program consisting of visual observations and test strips to
determine the lift thickness and number of passes of a compactor
required to achieve the desired compaction should be conducted
at the start of the project The number of passes can then be
used as the criteria for compaction
The compaction equipment may be sheepsfoot vibratory or
pneumatic tire rollers suited to the soil type and capable of
producing the specified results in an expedient and efficient
manner The equipment shall be in good working condition fully
ballasted and self cleaning
The compaction shall be accomplished in a systematic and uniform
manner over the entire fill area to produce a uniformly
compacted fill
The Contractor shall be required to obtain the above specified
levels of compaction out to the finish slope of fill slopes
This may be achieved by either over building the slope and
