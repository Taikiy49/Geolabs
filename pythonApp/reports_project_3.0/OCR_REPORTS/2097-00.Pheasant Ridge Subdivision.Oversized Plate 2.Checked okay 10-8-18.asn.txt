PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the
general subsurface soils and rock conditions at the project site in order
to obtain data for the formulation of engineering recommendations for the
design of the project site and road grading infrastructure installation
and foundations of the proposed residential structures

GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located in Waikoloa Village District of South
Kohala on the island of Hawaii The site is situated at the northern end
of Kimo Nui Street and is bordered to the north and west by raw
undeveloped land to the east by Paniolo Avenue and to the south by
existing single family residences
The terrain is generally rolling with some steep slopes locally
approaching an estimated ratio of 1 1 H V The terrain typically
reflects the original surface of thick basaltic lava flows in the A a
form The ground surface elevations range from about 764 to approximately
887 feet above sea level with drainage trending towards the west
The site is vegetated with a thin cover of dry grasses with scrub
kiawe algaroba trees Most of the ground surface is covered with
basaltic cobbles and gravel mantling an ashy soil There are localized
exposures of basalt clinker and rock formation
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to develop the site into a single family residential
subdivision of 44 lots Preliminary road grading plans by Imata and
Associates indicate cuts and fills of up to about 7 feet Lot grading
plans were not available at the time of our exploration however it is
anticipated that lot grading will generally be minimal Infrastructure
installation will consist of a waterline to service the subdivision
Permeability testing for drainage purposes was not included in the scope
of this report
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The project site is underlain by basaltic lava flows of the Hamakua
Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea which were deposited during the Pleistocene
Stearns and Macdonald 1946
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2097 00 Page 4
ak 65
The Hamakua series is typically composed of basaltic lava flows which
are mantled by a relatively thin layer of palagonitized ash locally
referred to as Pahala Ash The ash soil at the project site differs from
Pahala Ash found in other portions of the island of Hawaii in that the
in situ moisture content is generally lower and the soil exists ina dry
friable state
The soils at the site have been subjected to wind erosion which has
resulted in the formation of a desert pavement on the surface of the
site This desert pavement is a surficial layer of cobbles and gravel
which were left behind as the finer ash was blown away
The flows are in the A a form which is characterized by a rough
spiny or rubbly surface of fragmental clinker underlain by a dense core of
massive basalt which frequently has a thinner less continuous layer of
clinker below the massive core The clinker surface of the lavas at the
site have been mantled by the Pahala Ash as discussed above
Subsurface Conditions
Underlying the surficial desert pavement is a thin layer of yellowish
brown to reddish brown soil Pahala Ash This layer is predominately a
dry low plasticity silt which contains occasional gravel and cobbles
The silt has very little cohesion and once exposed is subject to wind and
water erosion The thickness of the silt layer is generally less than one
1 foot
Based on visual examination of road cuts and previous experience in
the area the ash soil usually grades into a layer of volcanic clinker _
which generally ranges in thickness from about 7 feet to more than the
maximum depth drilled This clinker material consists predominantly of
gravel and cobble sized scoraceous basalt fragments and represents the
original surface of A a flows The clinker also appears to contain many
accretionary lava balls boulders which may be up to 5 feet in diameter
Some of the clinker is weakly cemented

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our exploration and field reconnaissance of the area our
preliminary findings and recommendations are presented below
General
Our exploration indicates that the site is suitable for the proposed
development from a geotechnical engineering viewpoint
The in situ volcanic clinker formation encountered at the project
site is generally competent and should provide adequate bearing for the
proposed development With the exception of the ash soil which should be
stockpiled for use as topsoil excavated volcanic materials in general
should provide a suitable source of fill material
Site Grading
In general it is our opinion that conventional grading methods may
be used for the site and roadway grading of the proposed development It
should be understood that customary grading practices on the western side
of the island of Hawaii are usually modified somewhat due to the rocky
nature of the material
Prior to commencement of grading surface vegetation and organic
matter should be cleared and grubbed The resulting spoils should be
disposed of properly off site or stockpiled for use in landscaping
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 6
ak 65
Ash soils generally have relatively low strength and if the in situ
moisture contents are high enough they are thixotropic i e they lose
strength when remolded Therefore it is our recommendation that the ash
soil be removed from fill roadway and structural areas The ash soil may
be either stockpiled for use as topsoil or thoroughly blended into
rockfill material as discussed below
Due to the dry and sometimes windy site conditions moisture
conditioning of fill material will be necessary It is also advisable to
institute a dust control program during grading operations
Soft or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing
should be overexcavated to firm natural material and the resulting
depression backfilled with well compacted fill
In general the excavated soil and rock materials less than 12
inches in size should be suitable as fill material The excavated rock
material may not contain sufficient fines to produce a well graded
material therefore the surficial ash soil should be mixed into the rocky
excavated material to increase the percentage of fines and to create a
better graded fill material
Generally fills should be placed in level lifts and compacted to 90
maximum dry density as established by ASTM D 1557 78 Conventional
compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
excavated volcanic material Instead a testing program to determine the
optimum fill thickness and the number of passes of a compactor needed to
achieve the desired level of compaction should be conducted at the start
of the grading phase of the project On the basis of this testing
program the number of passes on each fill lift may then be used as the
field criterion for adequate compaction

All fills to be placed on slopes having ratios steeper than 5 1
horizontal to vertical should be keyed and benched into the underlying
consolidated clinker or basalt formation to lessen potential instability
of the new fill see Plate 3 The keys and benches should not be based
in the upper ash soil or in loose clinker
Imported fill material if required should conform to the
requirements stipulated in Section 30 of the Standard Specifications for
Public Works Construction Select Borrow for Subbase Course dated
September 1986
Boulder Fill
Boulders Le rock fragments having any dimension in excess of 12
inches may be used in deep fills provided that the following
recommendations are followed
1 Boulders must not be nested together They should be placed so
that compaction equipment will be able to compact around them
2 No part of the boulder should encroach any closer to finish
subgrade than the boulder s largest dimension and in no case be
closer than 3 feet to finish subgrade
3 No boulder larger than five 5 feet in its largest dimension
should be placed in the fill It may be necessary to reduce the
size of larger boulders or to dispose of them off site
4 Care must be exercised to avoid placement of boulders in proposed
utility alignments

Excavation
It is anticipated that most of the ash and clinker materials may be
conventionally excavated Some hard excavation and or ripping may be
encountered in localized well cemented areas or when large accretionary
lava balis boulders are encountered
The underlying basalt formation will most likely require some hard
excavation such as heavy ripping hoerams or possibly blasting if
encountered in excavations
Slopes
Provided the granular materials 12 inch minus are utilized for
recommended If fine grained soils are used we recommend slopes of 2 1
H V
For cut slopes the following ratios are recommended
a Ash Soil 3 1 H V
c Cemented Clinker H V
d Dense Basalt H V
In general any cuts or fills greater than 20 feet in height should
have a drainage bench at the midpoint of the slope
Foundation Support a a
We recommend that residential structure foundations bear on in situ
basalt clinker or compacted structural fill placed in accordance with
the recommendations presented herein For the design of foundations
bearing on in situ basalt or clinker an allowable bearing capacity of
4000 p s f may be utilized For foundations bearing on compacted fill
an allowable bearing capacity of 3000 p s f may be utilized

These values may be increased by one third when temporary wind or
seismic loads are considered
For spread footing foundations on level ground they should be
embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent outside grade
For footings on or near sloping surfaces further embedment would be
required to obtain a minimum horizontal set back distance of 6 feet
Settlements
Minimal settlements are anticipated for properly constructed
foundations bearing on the in situ weathered basalt formation or on
properly compacted structural fill
Slab On Grade
The floors of the proposed residential structures may be concrete
Slabs on grade The slabs and their cushions may be supported by the
basalt clinker or compacted fill A 4 inch layer of 3B Fine rock or
compacted base course should be provided below the slabs as cushion fill
The slab subgrade should be kept moist prior to concrete placement
In order to prevent future moisture infiltration and subsequent
damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture membrane is recommended
below the slabs
Design Review
We recommend that the plans and specifications for the proposed
construction should be forwarded to the geotechnical engineer for review
and written comment prior to construction This review is needed to
determine adherence to the earthwork and foundation recommendations given
herein If this review is not made the geotechnical engineer cannot
assume responsibility for misinterpretation of the recommendations

Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the geotechnical engineer be retained to
provide geotechnical engineering services during construction of the
excavation and foundation phases of the work This is to observe
compliance with the design concepts specifications or recommendations and
to allow design changes in the event that subsurface conditions differ
from that anticipated prior to start of construction The recommendations
given in this report are contingent upon such observations
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and
approved by the geotechnical engineer prior to hauling to the site
If actual exposed rock soil conditions encountered during
construction are different from those assumed or considered in this
report then appropriate modifications to the design should be made

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling ten
10 borings to depths ranging from 14 0 to 15 5 feet below the existing
ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate
2 A truck mounted drill rig was used to drill the borings
The soils encountered in the borings were classified by visual and
textural examination in the field by our geologist who continuously
monitored the drilling operations The classifications were further
reviewed by visual inspection and testing in the laboratory All soils
were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A summary of the materials encountered are
presented in the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 10
Soil samples were obtained form the borings by driving a 2 inch
standard split spoon sampler with a 140 pound hammer free falling 30
inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12 inches are shown
on the Boring Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Rock core samples were obtained by using an HQ wireline core barrel
advancing diamond core bits to drill and retrieve 2 5 inch diameter core
samples The percent recovery REC and rock quality determination RQD
of each core run are shown at the appropriate depths on the Boring Logs
