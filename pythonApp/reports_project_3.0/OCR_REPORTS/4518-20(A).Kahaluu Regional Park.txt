REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island After a long period of volcanic
inactivity during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic
activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations
These more recent volcanic eruptions are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the worldwide glaciers grew and advanced less water
was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded a larger volume of water became
available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level The larger
glacio eustatic sea level fluctuations ranged from about 250 feet above the present sea
level to about 400 feet below the present sea level Therefore the island shorelines have
transgressed and regressed in response to the sea level fluctuations
The processes of terrestrial erosion and the associated depositional environments
were affected by these glacio eustatic sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low
the erosional base level was correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths
below the present sea level As a result terrestrial sediments were carried farther seaward
and deposited in what had been marine environments When the sea level was high the
erosional base level was raised such that sediments accumulated at higher elevations In
addition the higher sea level allowed for the deposition of marine deposits such as coral
and limestone in environments that had been covered with terrestrial soils and rock
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock mass from the slopes and movement of the material
downslope toward the axis of the valley by gravity to form colluvial soil deposits Once the
colluvium reaches streams in the valley alluvial processes become dominant and the

The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the volcanic dome of the
Koolau Shield The headward erosion of these valleys reached the easily eroded materials
in the caldera complex and carved broad amphitheater headed valleys The heads of

The project site is situated in the lower portions of Waihee Valley When this area

the soft marshy sediments to reclaim the land In many cases the fill materials used to fill
the area especially the dredged soil stockpile were derived from the nearby marshy
areas Therefore the composition of the fill materials appears to be the same as that of the
marshy sediments of the area

FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions underlying the project site
consist of stiff fill and soft dredged material overlying weathered basalt formation
Groundwater was encountered at about Elevations 8 to 10 feet MSL
Percolation test results indicated that the subsoil at the planned leach field locations
are moderately permeable and may be suitable for the sewage disposal system
Based on relative competent soil conditions we recommend that the proposed
drainage headwall retaining structures may be supported on shallow foundation system
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used
for the design of the foundations bearing on the recompacted on site soils or compacted fill
material
Due to poor infiltration characteristics of on site soils and the presence of seepage
water surface and subsurface drainage consisting of cut off trench drain should be
provided to reduce the potential for excessive water ponding
Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of leach field retaining

The field percolation tests were performed in general accordance with the State of

foundation systems An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 000 pounds per

In general the bottom of foundation excavations should be compacted to a firm and
unyielding condition prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete If soft
and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the foundation
excavations the soft and or loose materials should be over excavated to expose
the underlying firm materials The over excavation to remove the soft and or loose
materials should be backfilled with select granular fill materials compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the foundation may extend deeper
to bear directly on the underlying competent material
It should be noted that the groundwater encountered in our borings was about
11 feet below the existing ground surface We envision that relatively moist to wet
soil conditions may be encountered near the bottom of the excavation If pumping
and yielding conditions occur at the bottom of the excavation we recommend a
minimum 18 inches over excavation be conducted and backfilled with No 3B Fine
ASTM C33 No 67 Gradation wrapped with non woven filter fabric such as
Mirafi 180N or equivalent
If foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate total settlements of the foundation to be less than
1 inch Differential settlements of foundations supported on similar materials would
be on the order of about 0 5 inch
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted
on site soils Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated by using
an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf
This passive earth resistance should be reduced to 150 pcf under water This
assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted Unless covered by
pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be
neglected
It is important to compact the bottom of foundation excavations to a firm and
unyielding condition prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete We
recommend that a Geolabs representative observe foundation excavations prior to
placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing
conditions and the required over excavation depths

Surface and Subsurface Drainages
Based on our experience in the vicinity seepage water is present at the project site
We understand that the subdrain system was installed along the western side of the
Kahaluu Regional Park to intercept the higher seepage water heading down gradient
towards the park The site improvements will require relocating portion of the existing
subdrain system that may interfere with the planned leaching field
In general the shallow subsurface trench drains would be at least 18 inches wide
and 2 feet deep consisting of 4 inch or 6 inch diameter perforated pipes with perforations
facing down The perforated pipes should be surrounded and underlain by at least
6 inches of drainage material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
or equivalent A non woven filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent should wrap
around the drainage material The subdrain trenches should be capped with granular fill
and should daylight into appropriate drainage structures for proper discharge
The infiltration characteristics of the on site soils appear to be poor and ponding
water would cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength
of the soils Therefore we wish to emphasize the importance of surface drainage at the
site Due to the terraced ground conditions we recommend incorporating a surface water
runoff collection system into the project construction to provide for proper surface
drainage

In general the over excavated subgrades and areas designated to receive fills
exposing soils should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction
Site grading operations should be observed by qualified technical personnel It is

scarification process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those
encountered in our exploration
Imported material used as select granular fill should be non expansive granular
material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The select granular fill
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest
dimension The material should also contain less than 15 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 25 or more and should
have a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less Imported fill materials should be tested
for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use
In general fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Fills and backfills within 3 feet of the
pavement grade elevation should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction should be accomplished by
sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment
Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed to compact the fills
It should be noted that some of the on site soils generally exist in a moist to wet
condition Therefore significant moisture reduction may be required to achieve the
minimum 90 percent compaction criteria especially for materials primarily consisting of
silts and clays Aeration to lower the soil moisture and more compaction effort to achieve
the specified compaction requirements would generally reduce the rate of fill placement for
this project In addition the higher rainfall of the areas would hinder aeration efforts to
properly compact the soil materials Contractors proposing to work on this project should
be encouraged to examine the site conditions and limitations

We understand that a driveway will be constructed between the Waihee Road and
the residence adjacent to the existing leach field We envision that asphaltic concrete
pavements will be designed for the driveway In general we anticipate that the vehicle
loading will primarily consist of passenger vehicles and light pick up trucks Considering
the generally light traffic conditions anticipated and our experience at the project site we
recommend using the following pavement design for preliminary budgeting purposes
Flexible Pavement Section
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
4 0 Inch Asphalt Concrete Base 91 Percent Relative Specific Gravity
6 0 Inch Aggregate Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
12 0 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Moist Subgrade
The subgrade soils below the new pavement areas should be scarified to a depth
of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to
not less than 90 percent relative compaction It should be noted that we have reduced the
compaction requirement of the pavement subgrade soils considering the relative wet
conditions at the project site
The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basalt aggregates compacted
to not less than 95 percent relative compaction CBR and field density tests should be
performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the
adequacy of the above section

Underground Utility Trenches
New utility lines and utility connections will likely be needed in support of the
proposed project In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should
also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes or
12 inches above the groundwater level to provide adequate support around the pipes
It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of
voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the
pipes Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe damage
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
or groundwater level to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the
excavated on site soils The backfill materials should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content placed in about 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are located below pavement
areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 The approximate boring locations are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered during drilling are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1
and A 2
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD split barrel sampler with a 140 pound
hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and
third 6 inches a total of 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings
at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
