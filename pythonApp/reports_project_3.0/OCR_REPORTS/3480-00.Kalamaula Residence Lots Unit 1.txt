INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Kalamaula Residence Lots Unit 1 project in the Kalamaula
area on the island of Molokai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design
input only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed project site is situated easterly of the town of Kaunakakai on the
island of Molokai Hawaii It is proposed to develop the site for residential use by the
Department of Hawaiian Home Lands DHHL The proposed project will involve the
construction of about 123 single family residential lots ranging in size from about to
1 acre with associated infrastructure such as dry sewer water drainage and roadway
designed for light to medium traffic The lots will be turned over on an as is basis with
the individual leasees being responsible for individual lot grading and installation of
individual wastewater systems We understand that on site individual sewage treatment
and disposal facilities will probably be utilized on an interim basis until a regional
sewage system can be developed Therefore field percolation tests were conducted
to obtain data for preliminary design of the sewage disposal system

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Molokai was built during the early to middle Pleistocene Epoch by
basaltic lava flows from two shield volcanoes East Molokai Mountain and West
Molokai Mountain The project site is located on the Hoolehua Plain between the East
and West Molokai Domes The Hoolehua Plain or isthmus of Molokai is composed of
lava flows from the East Molokai Volcano banked against the older West Molokai
Volcano
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted
global sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Molokai
remained essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic
in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were
both higher and lower relative to the present sea level on Molokai
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of upper areas of the

volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy
conditions The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans
of terrigenous sediments at correspondingly higher elevations in areas protected by
fringing reefs and beach strands During periods of no significant sea level changes
continued stream action extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward
About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that
rapid rise the shallow stream valleys were submerged In the last 10 000 years or so
the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous alluvial sediments have
continued to accumulate in the area in what were formerly valleys and drainageways
Based on our current field exploration it appears that the alluvial soils form a relatively
thin mantle over the in situ basaltic rock formation and its weathered products across
the project site

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered a relatively thin mantle of stiff alluvial
soils over competent basalt rock formation across the project site The alluvial soils
generally consist of clayey and sandy silts in a relatively dry and friable state In
general we believe that the on site silty soils and basalt rocks generated from cut
areas may be reused as a source of fill material provided that they are properly
processed and placed on prepared subgrades as recommended in the Site Grading
section of this report Due to the relatively dry and windy conditions at the project site
proper moisture conditioning of the fill materials and implementation of a dust control
program will be of critical considerations during the grading operations of the project
construction

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to
reduce potential contamination of the excavated materials The coral boulders
noted in Area No 5 of the Site Plan Plate 2 should be removed to expose firm
ground and the boulders may be disposed of in deep fill areas as recommended
in the Boulder Disposal subsection of this report The near surface silty soils
encountered in cut areas may be stockpiled and reused as a source of fill
material provided that the recommendations presented in the Fill Placement and
Compaction Requirements subsection are properly followed
Soft and wet soils may be encountered at the ground surface in the vicinity of
the drainage features observed at the subject site We recommend that these
soft and wet soils be removed where encountered to expose the underlying firm
natural soils prior to the placement of fill materials
After clearing and grubbing areas designated to receive fills should be scarified
to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 If the
subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the subgrade should
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3480 00 Page 12
be proof rolled and a filter fabric such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent should be
placed on the subgrade prior to placement of fill material Where shrinkage
cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soil
be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks or scarified moisture conditioned
and recompacted Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade
due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the
soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill
In areas where the cut subgrades expose basalt rock formation the subgrades
should be proof rolled with a minimum 20 ton vibratory roller or a Caterpillar D 9
bulldozer or similar heavy equipment for a minimum of 6 passes to help detect
and collapse near surface cavities Any loose areas or cavities disclosed during
clearing and proof rolling operations should be backfilled with well compacted
engineered fill materials The proof rolling operation should also be performed
at the finish subgrades of cut areas in basalt rock formation
Due to the potential presence of soft and or wet soils and cavities contract
documents should include unit prices for over excavation and engineered fill
placement to account for variations in the over excavation and backfill quantities
Fills and Backfills
The fill and backfill materials to be used as general fill material within the upper
4 feet from the finish subgrades should consist of well graded materials less than
6 inches in largest dimension In general the near surface silty soils
encountered during our field exploration should be suitable for use as general
fill materials provided that they are moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction The on site
cut materials generated from excavations into the underlying basalt formation
may be used as general fill or backfill materials within the upper 4 feet from the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3480 00 Page 13
finish subgrades provided that they are screened of the over sized materials
greater than 6 inches in largest dimension and processed to meet the above
gradation requirements This requirement is to facilitate future construction of
building foundations and utility trench excavations Over sized rock fragments
may be used in deep fills or broken down to smaller sized materials and incorpo
rated into the fill material If the excavated materials do not contain sufficient
fines to produce a well graded material off site borrow or on site rock crushing
of large sized rock fragments or boulders should be considered to provide the
required gradation and particle sizes to develop a well graded material
On site materials consisting of boulders up to 4 feet in diameter may be used as
rock fill provided that the top of the rock fill layer is placed at least 4 feet below
the finish subgrades In general placement of boulders in fills should be
performed in accordance with the procedures recommended in the Boulder
Disposal subsection
If imported material is required the material should be non expansive granular
material such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches
in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles
passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value
of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less
Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance with
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its intended use

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Backfills behind retaining
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3480 00 Page 15
walls should be compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction
Over compaction of the retaining wall backfill would exert additional lateral earth
pressures on the walls and should be avoided Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content corresponding to the
maximum dry density Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot
rollers vibratory rollers multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of
acceptable compaction equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
boulders Placement should be by haul trucks traversing previously placed lifts
and dumping at the edge of the currently placed lift Spreading of the rock fill
should be by a bulldozer to facilitate seating of the rock The rock fill should
be watered heavily during placement by water trucks traversing in front of the
current rock lift face and spraying water continuously during placement
Compaction equipment such as a 20 ton compactor or other similar compaction
equipment providing suitable energy to achieve the required compaction should
be utilized

of this testing program the number of passes may then be used as the field

Excavation
Our field exploration disclosed that the site is underlain by fractured volcanic
rock ranging from medium hard to very hard within 1 to 8 feet below the existing
ground surface It is anticipated that the volcanic rock may be excavated with
normal heavy excavation equipment such as ripping with large bulldozers
where the rock is near the existing ground surface However excavations into
the deeper formations may require the use of hoerams chipping or blasting
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are
based on field data obtained from the borings and test pits performed at the
subject site We recommend that contractors be encouraged to examine the site
conditions and soil data to make their own interpretation
Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings and test pits it
appears that most of the cut slopes would expose the stiff silty soils and
relatively dense basalt rock with frequent fractures In general we believe that
the cut slopes planned at the site should be designed with a slope inclination of
two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter Where the cut slopes expose
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 3480 00 Page 17
dense basalt formation at the slope face the cut slope may be steepened to a
slope inclination of 1H 1V Cavities that may be exposed on the cut slope face
should be backfilled and or grouted if necessary
Permanent fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or
flatter For cut and fill slopes steeper than 4H 1V with vertical heights greater
than 20 feet we recommend that a minimum 8 foot wide bench be provided at
the mid point of the slope to reduce the potential for erosion due to surface run
off water We also recommend that V ditches or earth berms be provided at
the top and bottom of slopes In addition we also recommend placement of
geotextile fabrics on the slope to reduce the potential for erosion
Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling
operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal
compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design
slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face In the event that over cutting
of a slope occurs keying and benching requirements should be implemented
instead of backfilling the slope to the design grade with sliver fills Alternatively
cut slopes may be buttressed with fill slopes constructed of non expansive fills
Water should be diverted away from the top of slopes and slope planting should
be provided as soon as possible to reduce potential erosion of the finished
slopes A sketch of typical keying and benching requirements is shown on
Plate 3

The subsurface conditions at the site were also explored by the performance of
nine seismic refraction surveys at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The seismic refraction surveys were conducted using a 12 channel signal

Refraction seismography is based on the transmission of elastic energy
compressional waves through geologic materials Generally the denser or more
consolidated a material is the faster the transmission of the compressional waves
These waves are refracted at interfaces between materials
The refracted waves are received by geophones on the surface and recorded by
the operator The recorded data is input into a desktop computer for processing which
involve the interpretive selection of the arrival times of the refracted waves

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings and Logs of Test Pits at the
appropriate sample depths
Three 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the subsoils The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test was performed on a selected sample of the soils to
evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test results are
presented on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentation
of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One sieve analyses was performed on a selected sample to determine the
gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification Graphic
presentation of the grain size distribution is presented on Plate B 3
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM Test Method D 1883 87
were performed on bulk samples of the near surface clayey silt soils to evaluate the
pavement support characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plates
B 4 and B 5
Two Compaction tests ASTM D 1557 91 were performed on bulk samples of
the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content relationships
The test results are summarized on Plates B 6 and B 7

APPENDIX C
Field Percolation Testing
The field percolation rates of the on site soils were estimated by performing four
falling head percolation tests in Boring Nos 1 through 4 Field percolation testing were
performed in general accordance with the State of Hawaii Department of Health s
Administrative Rules Chapter 2 Wastewater Systems and Chapter 10 of
the Ten States Standards to evaluate the permeability of the subsurface soils for
wastewater disposal The test results are presented on Plates C 1 through C 4
The percolation tests were performed by drilling 4 inch diameter boreholes to
depths ranging from approximately 4 to 4 feet below the existing ground surface Six
inches to 1 foot of gravels were then placed at the bottom of the boreholes to protect
the bottom of the holes from scouring and sediments The boreholes were then
presoaked for at least 4 hours prior to testing
During testing the holes were carefully filled with water to a minimum depth of
12 inches over the gravel pack The time intervals and water drops were then recorded
to provide data upon which to base calculation of the percolation rates
