Project Considerations
The Ward Village Auahi Street Shops project site is located in the Kaka ako area of
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The proposed Ward Village Auahi Street Shops
will be located on the mauka side of Auahi Street between Kamake e Street and Queen
Lane The project generally will consist of two 2 story structures
Based on the information provided the typical compressive column loads for the
new building structures will be about 100 to 250 kips dead plus live loads with a
maximum compressive load of up to about 360 kips per column
Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were evaluated based on borings
drilled for the original Ward Village Shops project The field exploration for that project
generally consisted of drilling and sampling eight borings extending down to depths of up
to about 165 5 feet below the existing ground surface In addition seismic shear wave
velocity profiling was performed in two borings extending down to about 120 feet below
the ground surface

Based on the field exploration conducted at the project site the subsurface
conditions generally consist of fill materials at the ground surface overlying lagoonal
deposits and coralline deposits at greater depths The fill materials generally consisted of
medium dense sands and gravel and stiff clayey silts extending down to depths of about
4 to 7 feet below the existing ground surface In some of the borings drilled loose and or
soft fill materials of up to about 2 feet in thickness were encountered near the existing
ground surface
The lagoonal deposits underlying the fill materials generally consist of very loose to
loose silty coralline sands with pockets of soft clayey silts extending down to depths of
about 16 to 45 feet below the existing ground surface Based on Boring No 6 lagoonal
deposits at the Ward Village Auahi Street Shops area generally extended down to about
31 feet below the ground surface The lagoonal deposits were underlain by medium hard
coral formation inter bedded with layers of loose to dense coralline detritus and medium
stiff to stiff silty clay alluvium extending down to the maximum depth explored of
approximately 165 5 feet below the ground surface In general the subsurface conditions
encountered at the project site may be considered highly variable due to its coralline
nature
Groundwater was encountered in the borings drilled at depths of about 3 9 to

Micropile Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site and other
cultural considerations we recommend using a cased micropile foundation system to
support the new building structures for the Ward Village Auahi Street Shops In general
the micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter usually 12 inches or less
drilled and grouted pile with steel reinforcing The micropile foundation typically is
constructed by drilling a borehole placing reinforcing steel in the hole and grouting the
borehole Micropiles are desirable because they can be readily installed in access
restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we recommend
using a cased micropile system with an outside casing diameter of 7 inches to support the
new buildings The micropiles should be drilled and grouted with a temporary casing
extending down for the full depth of the micropile to the tip elevation In addition the
unbonded zone of the micropiles should have a permanent casing with an outside
diameter OD of at least 7 inches The permanent casing unbonded zone should extend
at least 3 feet below the top of the coralline layer The coralline layer generally was
encountered at an elevation of about 25 feet MSL
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site we recommend
utilizing a micropile with an allowable compressive load capacity of up to 80 kips to support
the building structures The allowable compressive load capacities of the micropiles are for
loads such as wind or seismic forces
The micropiles would derive its vertical support primarily in the bonded zone from
skin friction between the grout and the surrounding coralline materials To provide the
allowable compressive load capacity of 80 kips the bonded zone of the micropiles should
be embedded a minimum of 12 feet into the top of the coralline layer A factor of safety of
2 0 was used in arriving at the allowable compressive load capacity For resistance to uplift
loads an ultimate uplift load capacity of up to 80 kips per micropile may be used in the
design The project structural engineer should check the structural capacity of the
micropile member in tension

Downdrag Loads
In the event of seismically induced liquefaction some settlement of the loose
lagoonal deposits will occur at the project site When these loose deposits move
downward relative to the micropile the downward movement will result in negative skin
friction forces generally known as downdrag forces acting on the micropile The
magnitude of the downdrag force depends on the thickness of the liquefiable layer relative
density of the potentially liquefiable lagoonal deposits and the pile perimeter
Due to the potential for liquefaction of the loose lagoonal deposits at the site
settlement of the loose deposits will result in downdrag loading of the micropile
foundations extending through the potentially liquefiable materials Based on our
foundation analyses we estimate that the downdrag loads imposed on the micropiles due
to liquefaction of the loose deposits may be on the order of about 15 kips per micropile It
should be noted that the recommended allowable compressive load capacity of the
micropiles has been reduced by the expected downdrag loads in the micropile foundation
analyses

Micropile Foundation Settlements
Settlements of the micropile foundations will result primarily from elastic
compression of the micropile member and subgrade response We estimate the total
settlement of the micropile supported foundations to be 0 5 inches or less with differential
settlements between columns supported on micropiles not exceeding about one half of the
total settlement We believe that these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur
as the loads are applied
Micropile Load Test Program
It should be noted that the load capacity of the micropiles is dependent on the
drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the contractor to install the
micropile Therefore the compressive load capacity of the micropile may vary
considerably between different contractors and micropile foundation systems In order to
determine whether the contractor s methods of micropile installation are adequate and to
determine the ultimate compressive load capacity we recommend performing at least
one sacrificial pre production compressive load test on a sacrificial micropile In general
the purpose of the sacrificial pre production load test on a micropile is to fulfill the following
objectives
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed by the
contractor to install the micropiles to the depths required
To assess the contractor s method of drilling and grout placement
To confirm the production micropile length and to evaluate the degree of
long term settlements of the micropile
In general the pre production load test should be performed in accordance with
ASTM D 1143 Standard Loading Procedure Based on experience we believe the load
test should be conducted no earlier than 7 days after completion of the micropile
installation to allow the grout adequate time to cure Two additional sacrificial micropiles
may be used for reaction during the compressive load testing of the pre production load
test micropile The reaction micropiles may be installed to depths as deep as the load test
micropile to provide adequate reaction in uplift to be determined by the contractor

The load test micropile should be loaded gradually to at least 200 percent of the
allowable design load in compression In addition we recommend holding the micropile for
a minimum of 4 hours at 200 percent of the allowable design load depending on the
recorded movements of the load test micropile Please note that the pre production load
test is an integral part of the design of the micropile foundation system Therefore we
recommend a Geolabs representative observe the pre production load tests
In addition to the pre production load test we also recommend performing pullout
tests proof tests on selected production micropiles during construction to confirm the load
carrying capacity of the installed micropiles We recommend testing a minimum 10 percent
of the total number of production micropiles or minimum of six micropiles for pullout The
pullout tests should consist of subjecting the micropile to at least 133 percent of the design
loads and the maximum test load should be held for at least 10 minutes Pullout tests on
the micropiles also are integral parts of the design of the micropile foundation system
Therefore we recommend a Geolabs representative observe the pullout tests
Construction Considerations
We recommend a specialty subcontractor experienced in the construction of a
cased micropile foundation system perform the micropile installation It should be noted
that hard drilling such as cobbles and concrete may be encountered locally within the fill
materials at the site In addition localized zones of cementation may be encountered in
the lagoonal deposits Furthermore the micropiles are designed to extend into the medium
hard to hard coral formation below the lagoonal deposits Therefore the contractor should
anticipate hard drilling conditions in order to achieve the minimum tip elevations
recommended herein
Due to the specialized nature of the micropile foundation construction observation
of the micropile foundation installation system and testing of the micropiles should be
designated a Special Inspection item Therefore observation of the micropile installation
operations by Geolabs Special Inspector is necessary to confirm our design assumptions
and should be designated a Special Inspection item in accordance with Section 1704 of
IBC 2003
