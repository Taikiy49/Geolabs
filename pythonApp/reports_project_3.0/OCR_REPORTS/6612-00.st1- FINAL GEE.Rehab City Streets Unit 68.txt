SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration along Meheula Parkway and Anania Drive generally 
encountered a pavement structure that consisted of 2 to 5 inches of asphaltic concrete 
underlain by 5 to 9 inches of base/subbase material.  It should be noted that in some 
areas, asphaltic concrete of up to about 8 inches was encountered in our borings.  In 
addition, base course thickness as little as 1 to 3 inches were also encountered in some 
areas.  The base/subbase materials encountered consist of sandy gravel with some silt 
and are generally very dense in relative density.  The subgrade materials generally 
consist of stiff to very stiff clays and clayey silts. 
Our field observations indicate that the streets generally exhibit medium to high 
severity pavement distress. Meheula Parkway and Anania Drive have concrete curbs 
and gutters on both sides of the roadway.  We anticipate new finished pavement 
surface would need to match the existing curbs and gutters. Therefore, the street 
rehabilitation should consist of milling (cold-planing) and should be replaced with new 
asphaltic concrete pavements for Meheula Parkway and Anania Drive. 
The new pavement section for Meheula Parkway should consist of 3 inches of 
AC on 6 inches of ACB.  For Anania Drive, the pavement section should consist of 
4 inches of AC over 6 inches of base course for portion of the roadway from Meheula 
Parkway to Kaholo Street. For Anania Drive from Kaholo Street to the end of the 
roadway, the pavement section should consist of 2.5 inches of AC over 6 inches of base 
course. The new pavement sections for Anania Drive consisting of asphaltic concrete 
only may be considered by taking into account of the contribution of the remaining 
existing base course materials, in lieu of complete pavement reconstruction.  If these 
thinner pavement sections are adopted, the City should be made aware that there exists 
the potential for localized areas to experience premature pavement distress and/or 
failure depending on the actual thickness and quality of the remaining existing base 
course materials. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
pavement design recommendations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct 
while Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below the Koolau 
lavas banking against its eastern flank.  These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad 
plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
The Schofield Plateau was formed when lava flows from the Koolau Volcano 
ponded against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late 
Pleistocene Epoch.  The dips of the lava beds are near horizontal (between 3 and 
5 degrees from horizontal).  The lava flows on the plateau have undergone in-situ 
weathering to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red soil. 
This in-situ weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils that 
grade to saprolite with depth.  In general, saprolite is mainly composed of silty material 
and is typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks.  Spheroidally weathered 
boulders are typical in the Schofield Plateau.  The saprolite grades to basaltic rock 
formation with depth.  The project site is on the Schofield Plateau and is underlain by fill, 
residual soils, and saprolite.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling 10 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 10, at selected locations along the roadway 
alignment. The borings generally extended to depths of about 8 to 11.5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface. In addition, the boring exploration was supplemented by 
coring the existing pavement section to depths of about 1 to 1.2 feet below the existing 
pavement surface at 20 selected locations, identified as Boring Nos. 101 through 120. 
The approximate boring and coring locations for this project are shown on the 
Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.27. 
In addition, Dynamic Cone Penetrometer (DCP) testing was conducted in 
Boring Nos. 1 through 10 to estimate the CBR value of the subgrade soils.  This 
instrument is typically used to assess material properties down to a depth of about 
39 inches below the surface.  The 17.6-pound DCP can be used to estimate the 
strength characteristics of fine and coarse-grained soils, granular construction materials 
and weak stabilized or modified materials. The 17.6-pound DCP generally cannot be 
used in highly stabilized or cemented materials or for granular materials containing a 
large percentage of aggregates greater than 2 inches.

After excavation to the bottom of the new pavement structural sections, the 
exposed base materials or asphaltic concrete remnants should be proof-rolled with a 
minimum 10-ton (static weight) drum roller for a minimum of eight passes to compact 
the exposed base materials. The drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 
300 feet per minute. If broken up asphaltic concrete is exposed after the milling process, 
consideration should be given to complete removal of the broken up asphaltic concrete 
to expose the underlying intact asphaltic concrete or aggregate base materials before 
proof-rolling the base materials. 
If soft soils are exposed after the milling process, we recommend providing a 
stabilization layer in lieu of proof-rolling the soft subgrade, which may induce pumping 
condition.  The stabilization layer should consist of 8 inches of aggregate base course 
over a layer of geogrid (such as Tensar Triax TX 160 or equivalent) underlain by a 
woven geotextile fabric (such as Mirafi HP370 or equivalent) as shown on Plate 5. The 
aggregate base course should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T-180 
(ASTM D 1557). Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and 
scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to 
those encountered in our field exploration. 
The aggregate base course should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  Asphalt concrete base 
(ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt aggregate, placed in a layer not

3.5 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway rehabilitation by drilling 
and sampling ten borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 10, extending to depths 
ranging from about 8 to 11.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. In addition, the 
boring exploration was supplemented by coring the existing pavement section to depths of 
about 1 to 1.2 feet below the existing pavement surface at 20 selected locations, identified 
as Boring Nos. 101 through 120.  The approximate boring and coring locations for this 
project are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.27. The borings were drilled 
using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D 2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-30. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) 
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and 
evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths. 
Five Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D 1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-2 through B-5. 
Four laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D 2844) were performed on 
selected bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-6 through B-9.
