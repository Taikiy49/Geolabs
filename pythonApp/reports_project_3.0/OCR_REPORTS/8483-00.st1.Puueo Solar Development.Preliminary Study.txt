REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an 
area of approximately 4,000 square miles. The island was formed by the activity of the 
following five shield volcanoes: Kohala (long extinct), Mauna Kea (activity during the recent 
geologic time), Hualalai (last erupted in 1801 – 1803), and Mauna Loa and Kilauea (both 
still active). 
The project site is situated on the eastern flank of Mauna Kea Mountain, which makes 
up the north-central portion of the island.  Based on available geologic information and 
geomorphological interpretation, the project site appears to be underlain by basaltic lava 
flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea Volcano, which were deposited during 
the Pleistocene Epoch (Stearns and Macdonald, 1946). 
Following the cessation of volcanic activity in Kohala and Mauna Kea, activity from 
the other shields resulted in widespread aerial fall of ash over much of the island. This ash 
has weathered rapidly into a fine silty soil, which is typified by low in-situ densities and high 
field moisture contents. In some locales, when the field moisture content is sufficiently high, 
the ash soil exhibits thixotropic properties (i.e., the soil temporarily loses strength when 
remolded and regains its strength after internal pore pressures dissipate).  In more arid 
areas, the ash soil exists in a dry friable state even though field moisture contents can 
approach 100 percent of the dry unit weight.  Our exploration indicates that the project site 
is underlain by this wetter volcanic ash soil, which should transition to residual soils, saprolite 
and basalt formation at deeper depths. 
The lava formation underlying the site generally consists of a’a and pahoehoe basalt 
type flows, which spread and ponded as they flowed toward the ocean. A'a lava is typically 
characterized by a porous, rough, and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged 
accumulation of rock fragments, including cobbles and boulders. Typically, a denser and 
more layered lava rock material is contained within the lava flow core. Pahoehoe lava is 
characterized by a smooth, rope-like or billowy surface and an internal structure of the 
vesicular (porous) rock. Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas. Cavities 
form when lavas are still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes (intra-flow cavities) 
and blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities). Lava tubes form when molten lava drains from 
the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube-like structure, which may extend for a large 
longitudinal distance along the flow. Blisters and pockets (inter-flow cavities) are generally 
smaller in horizontal extent.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our preliminary geotechnical engineering exploration consisted of excavating a 
total of 36 test pits, designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 through 36, extending to depths of about 
8.5 to 13 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate test pit locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on our test pits excavated, the subsurface conditions at the site generally 
consist of silty and clayey soils.  Near-surface soils generally consisted of soft to medium 
stiff clayey silts with a little sand extending down to about 1 to 2 feet below the ground 
surface.  The clayey silt soils were generally underlain by soft silty clays extending down 
to the maximum depth explored of about 13 feet below the ground surface. 
In some borings, the silty clay soils were underlain by saprolite and basalt 
formation.  Saprolite generally consisted of medium dense sands, gravel and cobbles.  
The basalt formation encountered was generally highly weathered and medium hard to 
hard.  Based on the limited field exploration conducted at the site, it appears that the 
depth of the basalt formation is generally greater than 12 feet at the majority of the project 
site, with some exceptions. 
We encountered groundwater in two of the 36 test pits excavated at the time of our 
field exploration. In addition, we encountered some wet zones in the clayey soils in some of 
the borings.  Based on our field exploration, we believe that perched groundwater conditions 
are likely present throughout the project site.   
We did not encounter groundwater in the majority of the test pits at the time of 
excavation.  However, groundwater levels likely will change in response to seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered during our field exploration are 
presented on the Logs of Test Pits in Appendix A, Plates A-1 through A-36. Photographs of 
Test Pit Excavation are presented in Appendix B.

the site, we believe the proposed solar farm development project generally is feasible

design and construction of the proposed project. Based on our experience and a review 
of the available information for this site, the geotechnical considerations include, but are

Mass Grading 
The project site generally consists of a former macadamia nut orchard.  Therefore, 
we envision that removal of macadamia nut trees would be required for project 
development.  In order not to interfere with the installation of solar array foundations, we 
envision that tree stumps and roots greater than about 3 inches in diameter would need 
to be removed. 
In general, the project site slopes down from west to east.  In addition, several 
prominent drainage gullies traverse portions of the project site.  In order to utilize the 
majority of the project site for solar array installation, we envision that some mass grading 
may be required after clearing to provide a relatively level or a constant sloping subgrade.  
Due to the large areal extent of the project site, a substantial quantity of cuts and fills may 
be required for the project. 
Solar Array Foundation System Selection 
Based on the limited field exploration conducted at the site, it appears that the 
subsurface conditions generally consist of soft to medium stiff clays and silts.  The basalt 
formation is generally anticipated at a depth greater than 12 feet.  Therefore, we envision 
that driven H- or C-piles typically used for solar array foundation systems would be 
appropriate for the project site. 
However, the majority of the silty and clayey soils encountered at the project site 
were soft to medium stiff.  In addition, the subsurface soils generally have high in-situ 
moisture contents.  Therefore, the performance of a design-level field exploration with a 
laboratory testing program will be required for the project development.  In addition, we 
recommend performing pile load testing on the potential solar array foundation system to 
evaluate its feasibility and applicability to the site conditions. 
Silty and Clayey Soils 
In general, the subsurface materials at the project site consist of silty and clayey 
soils. We envision that site grading required at the site will involve the placement of fills 
consisting of silty and clayey materials.  In addition, silty and clayey soils will likely be 
present at the subgrades of new structures and pavements required for the development 
of the project. 
As mentioned above, the on-site soils generally exist in a relatively moist to wet 
condition.  Therefore, some moisture reduction may be required to achieve the minimum 
compaction criteria.  Aeration to lower the soil moisture and more compaction effort to 
achieve the specified compaction may be required. However, since the project site is 
generally located in a relatively wet climate area, aeration of silty and clay soils may be

Other Foundation Considerations 
We envision that some single-story buildings may be required for the project to house 
solar equipment and other appurtenances. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered 
at the project site, we believe shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings may be used 
to support the new buildings planned for the project. As an alternative, foundations for 
support of the new structures also may consist of thickened-edge slab footings. 
We envision that the building foundations may be supported by in-situ silty and clayey 
soils.  However, proper preparation and compaction of the foundation subgrades will be 
critical during construction.  Where soft or pumping ground conditions are encountered at 
the foundation subgrades, over-excavation and replacement with suitable fills may be 
required.  
This due diligence study did not include the performance of field or laboratory 
resistivity testing.  Due to the nature of the development being considered at the project site, 
field and/or laboratory resistivity testing should be conducted during design development to 
evaluate the subsurface resistivity on the proposed electrical systems.

upon information obtained from the excavated test pits. Variations of the subsurface 
conditions between and beyond the field data points may occur, and the nature and extent 
of these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then 
appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. A 
design-level geotechnical engineering exploration should be conducted to confirm and/or 
modify the preliminary geotechnical recommendations provided herein.

by Goodfellow Bros., Inc.  The physical locations of the test pits and elevations should be 
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used.
