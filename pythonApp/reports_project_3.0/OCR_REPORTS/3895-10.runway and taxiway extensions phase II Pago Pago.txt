INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Runway and Taxiway Extensions Phase 2 project within the Pago Pago
International Airport on the Island of Tutuila American Samoa The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location and Vicinity Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the proposed taxiway extension project These geotechnical findings and recommendations
are intended for the design of pavements and site grading only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
American Samoa is a group of seven islands including Tutuila Aunu u Ofu Olosega
Ta u Rose and Swains It has a total area of about 76 square miles and is located
approximately 2 276 miles south southwest of the State of Hawaii The Island of Tutuila was
built during the Pliocene and early Pleistocene age by the extrusion of basaltic lava and
pyroclastic materials from five volcanoes Following the cessation of the main shield building
activity there was a long period of erosion accompanied by the growth of fringing coral reefs
During the late Quaternary Age volcanism was renewed by eruptions of lava and pyroclastic
materials at several vents on the south side of the island forming the Tafuna Leone Plain
GEOLABS INC
W O 3895 10 Page 5
The main mountain mass of Tutuila was divided into seven formations The rocks are
believed to be of Pliocene Epoch consisting of thinly bedded to massive flows of basalt
andesitic basalt and andesite These massive rock formations are interbedded with cinder
cones and tuff beds with thrachytic dikes plugs and crater fills The rock units were erupted
from as many as five eruptive centers but the principal volcano was near the middle of the
island Pago Pago Harbor is believed to occupy part of the collapsed caldera of that principal
volcano Erosion along a major fault along the caldera wall formed the near vertical cliff on
the northeast side of the harbor
Prior to the eruption of the Leone Volcano beach and lagoonal deposits accumulated
on and behind a submerged barrier reef at the present site of the Leone Plain These
deposits were later buried under several hundred feet of volcanic materials These older
beach and lagoonal deposits consist partially of sand and coralline gravel
Three tuff cones and a thick sequence of lava flow form the plain between Tafuna and
Leone The rock is believed to be of Holocene Age possibly only a few thousand years old
as evidenced by geologic relationships in the area The lava flows are mainly thinly bedded
basalt with interbeds of tuff and breccia
Clays silts sands and gravel have been deposited on the numerous valley floors
located close to the ocean by alluvial processes In some valleys the alluvial soil deposits
may be on the order of more than 100 feet thick

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the design guidelines presented in the Advisory Circular No
Airport Pavement Design and Evaluation prepared by the Federal Aviation Administration
FAA we understand that a stabilized pavement section would be required to accommodate
heavy aircraft weighing 100 000 pounds or more Based on the subsurface conditions
encountered and the forecast traffic mix provided we recommend that a stabilized pavement
section consisting of 5 inches of hot mix asphalt surface over 8 inches of cement treated
base course on 13 inches of aggregate subbase course be used for the proposed taxiway
extension planned at the Pago Pago International Airport
Alternatively an unstabilized pavement section consisting of 5 inches of hot mix
asphalt surface over 11 inches of crushed aggregate base course on 16 inches of subbase
course may be considered for the subject project However this would require the use of
superior materials with minimum California Bearing Ratio CBR values of 100 and 35 for the
base and subbase respectively Our experience in American Samoa suggests that such
materials may not be readily available in Pago Pago and it would be very difficult to produce
such materials in a consistent manner from the available quarries Unless it can be
confirmed that the superior materials are readily available in Pago Pago the unstabilized
pavement section should not be considered in the design for the subject project The base
and subbase courses for the stabilized and unstabilized pavement sections should be
compacted to 100 percent relative compaction Cement treated base course should be
compacted to at least 98 percent relative compaction based on ASTM D 558
Our field exploration along the proposed taxiway extension alignment generally
encountered dense cinder sands and basalt gravel overlying cobbles and rock formation at
greater depths In general we believe that the excavated materials may be re used as a

Base and Subbase Preparation
The new flexible pavement sections recommended for this project will consist of hot
mix asphalt surface overlying cement treated base course on aggregate subbase course
The primary functions of the base and subbase courses consist of the following
1 To provide uniform support for the pavement surface course throughout the
design life
2 To reduce potential pumping by providing drainage through the use of a
material with little or no fines
3 To provide a construction platform to expedite construction
The base and subbase materials required should conform to the project specification
requirements The excavated on site granular soils such as the on site sandy coral gravel
may be re used as a source of subbase material provided that they meet the project
specification requirements The cement treated base and aggregate subbase courses should
be moisture conditioned to near and above the optimum moisture content respectively
placed in thin lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to not less than
98 and 100 percent relative compaction respectively Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 558 96 and 1557 91 test procedures for
the cement treated base and crushed aggregate subbase respectively Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the proposed Taxiway A western extension
alignment were explored by drilling and sampling 19 borings designated as Boring Nos 1
through 19 extending to depths ranging from about 5 to 17 3 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the drilled borings are shown on the Site Plans
Plates 3 1 and 3 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 19
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as
the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the dense basalt rock formations encountered at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would be shown on
the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50

APPENDIX B
Test Pit Exploration
The subsurface conditions in the vicinity of the sanitary landfill areas were explored
by excavating sixteen test pits designated as Test Pit Nos 1 through 16 extending to depths
of up to about 8 feet below the existing ground surface In addition three test pits
designated as Test Pit Nos 17 through 19 were excavated at the eastern end of the
Taxiway A extension The approximate locations of the excavated test pits are shown on
the Site Plans Plates 3 1 and 3 2 The test pits were excavated using a rubber tired
excavator
The materials encountered in the test pits were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the excavation operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A of Appendix A Logs of the materials
encountered in the test pits are presented on the Logs of Test Pits Plates B 1 through B 6
