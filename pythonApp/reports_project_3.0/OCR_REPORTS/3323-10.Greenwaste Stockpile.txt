SUMMARY REPORT
GEOTECHNICAL ENGINEERING EVALUATION
GREENWASTE STOCKPILE
In accordance with your request we have conducted a geotechnical engineering
evaluation of the shredded greenwaste stockpiled at the above referenced site This
report presents the results of our laboratory testing and the statistical analyses
performed for the subject project Our work was performed in general accordance with
the scope of services outlined in our fee proposal dated
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The greenwaste stockpile site is located on about 40 acres of cleared land along
Koolau Road in the Moloaa area on the island of Kauai Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The
greenwaste materials exists as stockpile in multiple windrows of varying lengths each
of approximately 6 to 8 feet high and about 12 feet wide
We understand that the greenwaste material was hauled to the Moloaa site after
being processed and shredded in Anahola by a contractor The stockpile was derived
from greenwaste generated from hurricane debris and collected from various areas of
Kauai after Hurricane It is believed that some amount of mixing with soils
occurred during the processing of the greenwaste material Therefore it is desired to
evaluate the approximate inorganic soil content in the greenwaste stockpile located

At the time of our August 1994 site visit the greenwaste stockpile was observed
to be stockpiled in about six separate areas The greenwaste stockpile was observed
to have been placed in windrows of approximately 6 to 8 feet in height at each of the
areas The variability of the material appeared high with some areas of the stockpile
containing significant amounts of oversized tree branches and trunks whereas other
areas contained more uniform shredded materials
The materials observed generally consisted of a light brown mixture of shredded
greenwaste with some fine material The fine fraction consisted of organic materials
derived from the breakdown of larger wood material along with some inorganic material
of what appeared to be soil Localized portions of the stockpile were observed to
contain some beach sand and reddish brown clayey soil The relative amount of the
inorganic soil and organic materials was difficult to establish based purely on visual
observation In general the greenwaste stockpile observed did not appear to contain
an excessive amount of inorganic soil material with the exception of localized portions
of some windrows
A second site visit was made on April 24 and 25 1995 to conduct a sampling
effort to collect representative samples of the greenwaste stockpile for laboratory testing
and engineering analyses The excavation and sample collection efforts were assisted
by a backhoe and operator provided by the County of Kauai Mr John Murphy was
present for the initiation of the sampling effort and Mr Mark Freeman of Eco Logic Inc
was present during the majority of the sampling work For ease of reference the entire
site was arbitrarily divided in the field into individual lots identified as Lots A through
J as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
At the time of the April site visit it was observed that some of the greenwaste
stockpile had been removed from the site such as Lots A and G and a portion of Lot
B to the north of Sample No 15 as evidenced by vacant areas which were previously
noted to contain greenwaste stockpile It was also observed that the stockpiles placed
in Lots A B and C had been mechanically reworked by the contractor to facilitate the
composting process These observations were confirmed by personal communication
with Mr Freeman in the field In addition it was stated by both Mr Murphy and Mr
Freeman that additional material had been hauled to the site and mixed with the
existing materials since our August 1994 site visit Based on our visual observation in

were labelled sealed and shipped in bulk to Honolulu for laboratory

Testing Procedures
Each of the bulk samples were analyzed utilizing the following procedures to
determine the percentage by weight of the organic and inorganic contents within each
sample
Approximately one third of the bulk sample by weight was split and placed
in an oven overnight at about 110 degrees Fahrenheit in order to calculate the
natural moisture content of the sample per ASTM Test Designation D2974 87
The dried sample was then segregated using a No 4 sieve which is the sieve
size usually used to segregate fine soil from gravel sized material No 4 sieve
openings are in diameter We believe that the material passing
through the No 4 sieve should contain the majority of the inorganic or soil
material that is present in each sample A representative portion of these
segregated fines organic inorganic mixture was burned in a furnace to
determine the percentage by weight of the organic and inorganic materials
passing through the No 4 sieve in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D2974 87
Because a significant amount of soil remained attached to the larger wood
fragments which were retained on the No 4 sieve it was necessary to closely
examine and segregate the inorganic soil fraction attached to these wood
fragments The organic wood material retained on the No 4 sieve was manually
broken into smaller fragments and a representative portion was burned in a
furnace to determine the percentage by weight of the organic and inorganic
materials retained on the No 4 sieve
The procedures outlined above were utilized to determine the percentage by
weight of the organic and inorganic contents of the greenwaste stockpile In order to
estimate the percentage by volume of the organic and inorganic materials in the
greenwaste stockpile it was necessary to obtain the specific gravity of the inorganic soil

LIMITATIONS
The laboratory test results and statistical analyses submitted in this report are
based in part upon information obtained from field data points such as stockpile
sampling locations and visual observations made in the field Variations of stockpile
conditions between the field data points may occur and the nature and extent of these
variations may not become evident until a later point in time In addition a long time
has elapsed between the time the materials were placed and the time our field work
was conducted Therefore variations in the material volume and composition should
be expected If significant variations appear evident it will be necessary to reevaluate
the findings presented in this report
The locations of the field data points were approximately determined by
reference from existing features in the field such as lot corners and boundaries The
physical location of the field data points should be considered accurate only to the
degree implied by the method used
