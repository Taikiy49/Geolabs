SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, the site is underlain by undocumented fills consisting of reddish-brown 
silty clay with varying amounts of boulders, cobbles, and gravel overlying older alluvial 
deposits and weathered coral formation.  The undocumented fills range from about 3 to 
11 feet thick.  The consistency of the undocumented fills ranges from stiff to hard.  We 
observed traces of man-made debris within the undocumented fills.  However, we did not 
observe significant amounts or layers of organic materials within the undocumented fills. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings and test pits at the time of our field 
exploration.   
Based on our field exploration and laboratory testing, we believe that the 
undocumented fills may be left in place due to the stiff to hard consistency of the material 
encountered in the test pits and borings.  The undocumented fills may also be used as 
general fill material to fill the low areas at the site.   
However, the laboratory tests indicate that the undocumented fills consisting of the 
reddish-brown silty clay soils have a moderate swelling potential when subjected to 
moisture fluctuations. Therefore, we recommend placing a capping layer consisting of non-
expansive, select granular fill material at the site.  Since the site will be developed by 
others, it is not known as to what finished floor elevations or type of foundation and slab 
system will be used for the future building structures.  The thickness of the capping fill 
placed at the site should ensure that at least 24 inches of select granular fill would be 
present below future slabs-on-grades and concrete sidewalks, which are generally 
susceptible to structural distress due to shrinking and swelling of the undocumented fills.  
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific design 
recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Lot 16537, TMK: 9-1-88: 20 project in the District of Ewa, on 
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the design of site grading only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are 
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
Lot 16537 (TMK: 9-1-88: 20) is located in the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. It is bounded in a clockwise direction by Haumea Street, Alohikea Street, Ala 
Kahawai Street and Uluohia Street. We understand that it is planned to grade the lot to 
match the existing sidewalk elevations surrounding the lot.  The existing ground surface 
elevations at the site range between +58 and +69 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  The 
existing sidewalk elevations surrounding the site range between +64 and +65 feet MSL. 
After grading the site, building structures and pavements may be constructed in the 
future by others. We have assumed that a design-level geotechnical engineering 
exploration will be conducted by the future lot owner for their planned development. 
Based on the existing topography of the site and the information provided, we 
understand that the site was used to stockpile soil material. There is no documentation 
of how the soils were placed at the site; therefore, we have assumed that proper 
placement, moisture-conditioning, and compaction of these soil materials may not have 
been conducted.   
To determine the thickness, the swell characteristics, and the suitability of the 
stockpiled materials for building foundation and pavement support, we conducted a field 
exploration program consisting of both borings and test pits.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was mainly built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western portion of the island.  
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene 
(Ice Age) in age and forms the bulk of the northern and eastern portions of the island.  
As the older Waianae volcanic activity ceased, younger lava flows from Koolau 
continued to bank against Waianae’s east facing slopes forming a broad upland, known 
as the Schofield Plateau. The Koolau Volcano reached the end of its main 
shield-building phase approximately 2 million years ago.   
Chemical weathering and physical erosion of the volcanic uplands generated 
copious terrestrial sediments, which were transported down slope toward the coastline. 
The terrestrial and embedded marine sediments accumulated to comprise the low 
topographic gradient coastal plain known as the Ewa Plain.  In the project vicinity 
(Ko Olina) and further towards the east and northeast (East Kapolei), the terrestrial 
sediments accumulated and became interbedded with other marine sediments and 
hardened coral-algal and coral limestone reef formations to form a thick sedimentary 
wedge of material fronting the mountain foothills.  The thickness of the collective 
sedimentary wedge ranges from nearly absent in the area underlain by the Interstate 
Route H-1 Highway to over 1,000 feet at the shoreline near Ewa Beach.  This wedge of 
sedimentary and calcareous reef materials comprising the Ewa Plain serves as the 
confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu.  
Deposition of recent or Holocene age sediments has continued from earlier geologic 
time through the present.   
During the Pleistocene Epoch, the global sea level fluctuated in response to 
cycles of continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of 
the sea.  When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available 
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level.

The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of 
the calcareous reef materials.  These changes in sea level, over periods of geologic 
time, was a major driving force in both the deposition and erosion of the terrestrial 
alluvial/colluvial deposits and coralline reef materials underlying the project site.   
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
Lot 16537 (TMK: 9-1-88: 20) is located in the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. It is bounded in a clockwise direction by Haumea Street, Alohikea Street, Ala 
Kahawai Street and Uluohia Street.  
At the time of our field exploration, the site was moderately vegetated with wild 
grass up to about 1 foot high and small to medium size shrubs.  The existing ground 
surface elevations at the site range between +58 and +69 feet MSL. The site was 
generally higher at the eastern and southern corners.   
We observed low areas (ground surface elevations below the sidewalk 
elevations) along the perimeter of the site.  Some surface and embedded boulders were 
observed in these low areas.  The embedded boulders indicate that there is a high 
potential for encountering boulders within the undocumented fills.  A small stockpile of 
concrete debris was observed at the eastern corner of the site.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, the site is underlain by undocumented fills consisting of reddish-
brown silty clay with varying amounts of boulders, cobbles, and gravel overlying alluvial 
deposits and weathered coral formation.  The undocumented fills range from about 3 to 
11 feet thick.  The consistency of the undocumented fills ranges from stiff to hard.  We 
observed traces of man-made debris within the undocumented fills.  However, we did 
not observe significant amounts or layers of organic materials within the undocumented 
fills. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings and test pits at the time of our 
field exploration.   
The laboratory tests indicate that the undocumented fills consisting of the 
reddish-brown silty clay soils have a moderate swelling potential when subjected to 
moisture fluctuations. Since the undocumented fills encountered at the site have a 
moderate swell potential, we recommend placing a capping layer consisting of non-
expansive, select granular fill material at the site.  Since the site will be developed by 
others, it is not known as to what finished floor elevations or type of foundation and slab 
system will be used for the future building structures.  The thickness of the capping fill 
placed at the site should ensure that at least 24 inches of select granular fill would be 
present below future slabs-on-grades and concrete sidewalks, which are generally 
susceptible to structural distress due to shrinking and swelling of the undocumented 
fills.

Based on our field exploration, the undocumented fills consist of reddish-brown 
silty clay with varying amounts of boulders, cobbles, and gravel.  The penetration 
resistance (number of blows needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches 
of an 18-inch drive with a 140-lb hammer falling 30 inches) generally ranged between 
13 and 56.  Pocket penetrometer tests, which provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the in-situ soils, were performed.  The unconfined compressive 
strength of these soils ranged between 1 to 4.5 tons per square foot (tsf).  The blow 
counts and unconfined compressive strength values indicate that the undocumented fills 
have consistencies ranging between stiff to hard. The test pit excavated at the 
southcentral area of the site encountered some boulders and cobbles within the silty 
clay.  This mixture of silty clay and boulders and cobbles appeared to be in a relatively 
dry and loose state.  It should be noted that the potential for encountering boulders in 
the undocumented fills is relatively high.   
We observed traces of man-made debris within the undocumented fills.  However, 
we did not observe significant amounts or layers of organic materials within the 
undocumented fills.

ranged between about 15 and 20 percent, which is generally dry of the optimum moisture 
content.  Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding 
to the maximum dry density.  In addition, the laboratory ring swell and California Bearing 
Ratio (CBR) tests indicate that the undocumented fills have a moderate potential for 
shrinking and swelling due to moisture fluctuations.  The ring swell generally ranged 
between 0.6 and 6.8 percent, and the CBR swell ranged between 0.2 and 1.7 percent.

undocumented fills may be left in place due to the stiff to hard consistency of the material 
encountered in the test pits and borings.  The undocumented fills may also be used as 
general fill material to fill the low areas at the site.

3.2.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site.  
Soft and/or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill should be over-excavated to expose firm materials, and 
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fills. The 
excavated soft and/or organic soils should be disposed of properly off-site and/or 
used in landscape areas, where appropriate.  Contract documents should include

additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement 
to account for variations in the over-excavation quantities. 
After clearing and grubbing, areas to receive fills and finished subgrades in cut 
areas should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry 
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the 
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry

Where shrinkage cracks are observed after the subgrade compaction, we 
recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as recommended above. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavating the soft areas and 
replacing these areas with select granular fill compacted to at least 90 percent 
relative compaction. A Geolabs representative in the field should evaluate the 
need for over-excavation due to soft subgrade soil conditions. 
3.2.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, we believe that the excavated undocumented fills may be used as a 
source of general fill materials provided that the materials are properly processed 
and placed in accordance with the “Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements” 
subsection herein.  The excavated undocumented fills to be used as general fill 
material should be free of vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock fragments 
greater than 6 inches in maximum dimension.  The potential for encountering 
boulders within the excavated undocumented fills is high.  If boulders are 
encountered in the excavated soils, the boulders should not be reused as fill 
material. 
Imported general fill materials needed for the site filling should consist of materials

3.2.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
General fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above 
the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  
Select granular fill should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, 
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to at 
least 90 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, 
multiple-wheel pneumatic-tired rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction 
equipment. 
Because moisture-conditioning and compaction of the on-site soils are critical 
elements of earthwork, Geolabs should conduct observations and perform soil

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from the borings and test pits. Variations of subsoil conditions 
between and beyond the borings and test pits may occur, and the nature and extent of 
these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations 
then appear evident, Geolabs should be contacted to re-evaluate the recommendations 
presented herein. 
The boring and test pit locations indicated herein are approximate, using a 
hand-held global positioning system with coordinates obtained from Google EarthTM. 
Elevations of the borings and test pits were interpolated from the spot elevations and 
contour lines shown on the site topographic survey map prepared by AECOM dated 
February 5, 2014. The physical locations and elevations of the borings and test pits

Field Exploration (Borings) 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling five 
borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5. The borings extended to a depth of about 
21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped 
with continuous flight augers. 
Our engineer and geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by 
visual and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, 
Standard Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling 
operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further 
reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general 
accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for 
Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with 
ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Field Exploration (Test Pits) 
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by excavating and hand 
sampling seven test pits, designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 through 7, each extending to a 
depth of about 10 feet below the existing ground surface.  The test pits were excavated 
with a rubber-tired backhoe at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the test pits by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the excavation 
operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further 
reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general 
accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for 
Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log 
Legend, Plate B-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with 
ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate B-0.2. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Test Pits, 
Plates B-1 through B-7. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths.
