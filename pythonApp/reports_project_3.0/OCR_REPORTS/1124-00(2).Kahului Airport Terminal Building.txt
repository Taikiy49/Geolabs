INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our soils engineering investigation
conducted for the Terminal Building portion of the proposed Kahului
Airport Terminal Complex Expansion project
State Project No M 1055 The general location of the site and its
vicinity is shown on Plate 1 Project Location Map
The entire air terminal complex expansion project includes the access
roadways and automobile parking lots identified as the Landside
improvement the Air Cargo Building improvement a new aircraft
parking apron and the adjoining Hemaloa Street improvements identified
as the Airside improvement and the Terminal Building improvement
itself Soil engineering investigations for the Landside Air
Cargo Building and Airside improvements have been conducted under
Separate contracts Reports for those portions were submitted under
separate covers

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The new terminal building improvement project will include the main
terminal building two passenger concourses and pier holdrooms and
related enplaning and deplaning loading bridges A proposed layout is
presented on the Site Plan Plate 2 It is our understanding that
this layout has been modified slightly According to the sets of
drawings for the project dated May 21 and the new main
terminal building will be about 220 by 930 feet in plan dimension with
consideration for a future second story addition The concourses will
extend southeastward some 460 feet from the new terminal building into
the apron area where there will be passenger holdrooms for enplaning
and deplaning at second level above the apron grade Provisions are
to be made to extend the concourses and construct additional holdrooms
for future additional enplaning and deplaning capabilities
According to the structural engineer for the terminal building design
the anticipated foundation loads for the project will range from 100
to 350 kips per column Some uplift loadings are anticipated at the
concourse and loading bridges The majority of columns will be spaced
at 20 feet spans in the direction of the longer axes of the terminal
building concourses and pier holdrooms while column spacing may vary
up to 40 feet in the direction of the shorter axes Concrete frame
structures are being considered for the terminal structures The con
courses and pier holdrooms will be elevated

pier holdrooms will be used by aircraft service vehicles or as office

structure will be phased to maintain a continuous operation of the

SITE CONDITIONS
Geology
The project site is located on the north shore of the island of Maui
on the northwest flank of Haleakala Volcano
The bulk of the Haleakala shield was built during the late Pliocene
and early Pleistocene by thin bedded basaltic lava flows of the Hono
manu Volcanic Series During the Pleistocene the characteristics of
the lavas changed to thickly bedded flows of andesitic composition
These lavas have been grouped as the Kula Volcanic Series and are
generally much less vesicular hence more dense and harder than the
more vesicular thinnly bedded basalt of Honomanu Volcanic series
The Kula lava flows buried most of the Honomanu rocks throughout East
Maui and in some areas those flows buried alluvial deposits derived
from both Honomanu and early Kula rocks At the project site most of
the area is underlain by what appears to be a Single thick flow of
Kula lava that overlies the older alluvial soils
Also encountered within the project site were varying amounts of
man made fill which may have resulted from grading operations during
the construction of the existing airport facilities

Surface Conditions
Most of the project site had been used previously as a small aircraft
apron The southwestern portion of the site is occupied by several
quonset buildings and other small structures The northeastern side
of the site is occupied by a weather Station the main approach
roadway to the existing terminal and an automobile parking lot that
is landscaped with many trees and shrubs The existing site slopes
gently from about elevation 40 feet at the northeastern side to about
elevation 28 at the southwestern end of the proposed main terminal
building site
Subsurface Conditions
The borings in the main Passenger terminal area penetrated several
different pavement sections ranging from asphaltic concrete pavements
that varied from 2 to 12 inches thick and a 15 inch thick Portland
cement concrete pavement These were Supported on 6 to 12 inches of
base course Below these existing pavement sections most of the
borings encountered residual soils consisting predominantly of stiff
reddish brown and brown clayey silt at depths ranging from 0 8 to 13 2
feet In Borings 16 and 18 about 7 to 8 feet of fill consisting of
stiff brown silt with gravel was found below the pavement section and
over the residual stiff reddish brown silt
GEOLABS HAWAII

Underlying the residual soils the borings encountered hard gray
massive andesitic basalt rock locally referred to as blue rock at
depths between 0 7 and 13 2 feet below the existing ground In Boring
3 this blue hard andesitic basalt rock was found immediately below
the pavement section at 0 7 foot depth without intervening fill or
residual soil This andesitic basalt rock extended to the maximum
depths drilled in all borings
Based on borings drilled in the Airside improvement portion of the

elevation 3 0 feet

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
It is our understanding that finished floor grades in the southwestern
quarter of the new terminal building site will be at elevation 40
feet the central portion at Elevation 44 feet and the remainder at
elevation 42 feet The existing grades are about Elevation 30 in the
southwestern end to 40 feet at the northeastern end Thus the entire
airport terminal project will require a substantial amount of fill to
raise the site grade The thickness of the fill required will vary
from 2 feet on the northeastern end to about 10 feet on the
southwestern end of the building Other portions of the terminal
complex expansion improvements will require considerably more fill
It is our understanding that the mass grading work at the new terminal
building site will be done under the Landside improvement not as a
separate work item under the Terminal Building portion of the
project Mass grading for the pier holdroom sites will be included in
the Airside improvements
Details of our recommendations regarding site clearing and grading
are presented in our reports for the Airside and Landside
improvement portions of the project Reference 3 and 5 It is anti
cipated that the fill will be of Stable non expansive soil material
placed and compacted according to engineered fi11 requirements to
permit on grade slab construction
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 9
Due to large quantities of fill required on this improvement project
it is possible that a combination of fill materials may be used
However it is strongly recommended that the borrow source or sources
of fill material be identified as soon as possible and material
qualities be evaluated by a soils engineer for their acceptibility as
project fill materials We will be pleased to render such a service
Because the fill source or sources are yet to be determined the
final quality of the fill may vary depending on the availability of
materials To establish a framework for the terminal building
foundation design and analysis we have based our recommendations on
three different types of material that are readily available It is
recognized that these design recommendations may need to be modified
in the future according to the actual materials and procedures used
We have also reviewed the suitability of several foundation types for
the support of the terminal building structures These include spread
footing mat foundation pile foundation and caisson foundation It
is felt that a mat or pile foundation would not be as cost effective
as spread footings or drilled caissons for the project Detailed
recommendations for spread footings and drilled caissons are presented
in the following subsections

Spread Footings
Spread footings are recommended for foundation Support of the
terminal buildings either in the engineering fills or in the natural
soil
For fill consideration we have reviewed briefly three types of
potential fill materials These include Select Borrow from Waikapu
quarry calcareous dune sand from the vicinity of Kahului Wailuku
and a non organic reddish brown clayey silt similar to the on site
residual soils in the project area Other types of material may also
be used if found acceptable However they are not discussed for the
purpose of this report
1 Waikapu Select Borrow This product from Waikapu
Quarry consists of well graded silty sand with some
gravel From our past experience with this material it
is known that this material will form a dense stable soil
mass suitable for subbase

Estimated Settlements for Spread Footings
The recommendations presented above have considered probable fill
settlement under the foundation loadings We believe that for the
design maximum column loading of 350 kips colum with the residual
soil type of fill materials discussed the settlements may be
anticipated to range up to one inch with a maximum differential
settlement up to one half of an inch over a distance of 20 feet the
typical column spacing A more pronounced differential settlement may
develop between footings on rock and on soil However it is felt
that where a minimum distance of 20 feet is provided between the
columns founded on rock and on soil the maximm differential
settlement will be about one half of an inch
Drilled Caissons
Because competent rock is found at relatively shallow depths ranging
from 5 to 15 feet below finish grades it may also be practical to use
caisson foundations drilled into rock Only straight shaft caissons
are discussed herein
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 14
Belled caissons resting on soil would be more difficult to construct
because of shallow rock surface and the occasional cobbles and
weathered clinker zone that occur just below the residual soil over
the rock If the cost of belled caissons is competitive using a soil
bearing pressure of 5000 p s f detailed recommendations may be
developed if it is so desired Otherwise this report will address
the straight sided caissons drilled into the underlying basalt rock
The cost effectiveness of caissons will be greatly influenced if
cohesionless soil such as boulders crushed rock beach sand or
cinder is used for fill These material will tend to cave into
drilled holes and cause construction difficulties However if the
silty sand of Waikapu Select Borrow or the clayey silt residual soil
are utilized as fill material it is anticipated that only minor
problems will be caused for caisson drilling Any underground
obstacles such as buried foundations cesspool manholes etc if
abandoned in place will hinder the drilling operation and may
substantially increase the cost of construction
Two alternate designs for caisson Support may be considered for the
project The first method would rely on the shaft side friction in
soil and rock ignoring the end bearing at the bottom while the
second would consider mainly the end bearing on the competent massive
andesitic basalt rock

End Bearing Caissons
Alternatively caissons may be founded directly on the massive
andesitic basalt rock For direct end bearing caissons the
minimum size should be 3 feet in diameter so that the bottom
can be handcleaned by lowering a worker into the hole if
deemed necessary Temporary protective casing will be
required in such a case
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 17
The bottom of a caisson should be an average of 6 inches into
the massive hard dense andesitic basalt rock and should not
be terminated in weathered basaltic clinker layer occasionally
overlying the hard massive rock The maximum bottom elevation
difference should not be more than 12 inches between the
highest and lowest points within a given caisson Jack
hammering and hand cleaning may have to be used to achieve
this if deemed necessary
The caisson bottom prepared to such a condition may be
designed to an allowable bearing pressure of 25 000 p s f
with one third increase allowed for temporary wind or seismic
loading
Shaft resistance in soil may also be included in the design
However the portion of the shaft friction within 2 diameters
above the bottom of the caisson should be ignored For
example for a 3 foot diameter shaft with a length of 10 feet
below the top of foundation only the top 4 foot portion of
shaft should be considered for soil friction For Waikapu
Select Borrow or residual soils the allowable skin friction
should be limited to 300 p s f resulting in a shaft
resistance of about 11 kips

Lateral Resistance
To resist lateral forces passive earth pressures may be used
For compacted fills using the type of soils discussed in a
previous section under Spread Footings an allowable passive
lateral earth pressure of 250 p s f per foot of depth may be
used Passive lateral resistance for caissons in rock can be
increased to 12 000 p s f These pressures apply to the
vertically projected areas of footings or caissons in the
direction of the horizontal force
For caisson foundations we believe that tie beams may not be
required Caisson foundations generally offer good resistance to
overturning moments We will be pleased to provide design
criteria when such a requirement is identified
Uplift Resistance
To resist uplift forces the dead weight of foundation as well as
resistance from soil and rock may be considered It is recom
mended that the allowable uplift Capacity be estimated from the
lower value of the following two procedures
1 The ultimate uplift capacity may be estimated from the dead
weight of rock and soil in an inverted cone starting at the
tip of the caisson with the center of caisson as that of
the cone
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 19
For caissons embedded 4 feet or more into rock the shape
of the cone may be estimated to spread 30 degrees from
vertical in the rock and continuing through the soil at 15
degrees from the vertical at soil rock interface see Plate
4 The rock weight may be assumed to be 160 pounds per
cubic foot and soil at 115 pounds per cubic foot The
factor of safety should be at least 2 5 for allowable uplift
Capacity
2 Allowable upward shearing resistances along the concrete
caisson shaft are 600 p s f in the rock and 160 p sef in
the soil
In addition the dead weight of foundation itself may be added
with a factor of safety close to 1 0 or as appropriate
If drilled caissons are used to resist uplift force a minimum
of 4 feet of embedment into massive hard basalt is recommended
The above uplift resistance is limited to caissons acting as
single caissons rather than grouped caissons Caissons grouped
together to resist uplift forces need to be analyzed separately
depending on the magnitude of load and the caisson spacings

On Grade Floor Slabs
Slabs supported on grade may be used if the fill material is non
expansive The expansion potential of a fill material should be
evaluated if it is deemed necessary by a soil engineer prior to
the fill construction The range of expansion potential of the
reddish brown residual soil fill would be low Usually the
damage potential from this residual soil expansion may be reduced
by placing a 6 inch thick cushion layer of S4C type crushed rock
pre moistening the soils and providing thickened edges as cut off
walls to reduce soil moisture fluctuation However specific
details should be developed after an evaluation of the fill source
is made
A minimum of 4 inches of rock cushion should generally be provided
under the slab supported on grade over the other two types of fill
materials mentioned earlier Vapor barriers are recommended under
the floor slabs in office or storage areas

Moisture content unit weight and Atterberg Limit determina
tions were performed on selected samples to aid in the classi
fication and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the log of borings at the appro
priate sample depths The Atterberg Limits are also summarized
on Plate B 1 and plotted on Plate B 2
Six one inch ring swell tests were performed on remolded and
undisturbed samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the
soils in both natural and compacted conditions The test
results are summarized on Plate B 3
Six direct shear tests were performed on undisturbed samples to
evaluate the strength characteristics of the natural soil The
test results are presented on Plates B 4 through B 9
Three consolidation tests were performed on undisturbed and
recompacted samples to evaluate settlement characteristics
The test results are presented on Plates B 10 through B 12
