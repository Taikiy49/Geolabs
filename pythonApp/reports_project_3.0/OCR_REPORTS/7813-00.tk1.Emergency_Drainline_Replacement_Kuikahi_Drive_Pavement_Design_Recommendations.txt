Dear Mr. Ishikawa: 
The project is located at Wailuku Heights, Unit I Subdivision on Kuikahi Drive, 
between the intersections of Kualau Street and South Alu Road.  We understand that it 
is proposed to replace the existing corroded drainline with a new equivalent sized 
high-density polyethylene (HDPE) drainline. As part of the project construction, it is also 
desired to reconstruct the existing Portland Cement Concrete (PCC) pavements, 
measuring approximately 420 by 36 feet, that exhibit signs of distresses, such as 
cracking and depression. This letter report summarizes our work on the project and 
presents our engineering analyses and pavement design recommendations for the 
concrete pavement reconstruction project at Kuikahi Drive

pertinent information contained in the above-referenced report.

Design Subgrade Conditions 
Based on the result of field exploration conducted for the Kuikahi Drive Pavement 
Rehabilitation project in the aforementioned report, we anticipate that the pavement 
subgrade soils below the existing concrete pavements will likely consist of very stiff to 
hard clayey silts. Laboratory Resistance (R) Value test performed on the near-surface 
soils in the proximity of the existing concrete pavements indicated that the materials 
exhibited an R-value of approximately 23. Therefore, a design R-value of 20 was adopted 
in our pavement analyses for the subgrade materials. If site grading exposes soils other 
than those assumed, additional tests should be performed to confirm and/or revise the 
recommended pavement section for actual field conditions. 
Based on a design R-value of 20 adopted for the pavement subgrade soils as 
described above, an Unadjusted Modulus of Subgrade Reaction (k-value) of 271 pounds 
per square inch per inch of deflection (pci) was obtained based on a subbase thickness of 
6 inches. The k-value was reduced to 30 pci due to loss of support in general accordance 
with AASHTO design guidelines and is adopted for our rigid pavement analyses. The 
PCC should have a minimum flexural strength of 650 pounds per square inch (psi) at 
28 days when tested in accordance with ASTM C78.

Design Pavement Section 
We understand that distresses such as cracking and depression of the existing 
concrete pavements were observed, especially in the areas above the existing drainline, 
suggesting that there is a potential for pavement subgrade failure. Therefore, we 
recommend that the pavement subgrade be stabilized by incorporating a layer of triaxial 
geogrid between the upper aggregate subbase and underlying clayey silt subgrade 
soils. To maximize the benefits of the triaxial geogrid, we understand that aggregate 
base course with a nominal maximum size of 1.5 inches should be used in lieu of the 
aggregate subbase. In general, the triaxial geogrid will interlock with the aggregate base 
course, resulting in two benefits during initial construction and for the life of the 
project: 1) lateral confinement – increasing the modulus of the aggregate base course, 
and 2) subgrade bearing capacity enhancement. 
Based on our pavement analyses, we recommend that the following pavement 
structural section be used for design and cost estimating purposes. Detailed 
calculations of the pavement design section are presented on Plate 2.

The exposed pavement subgrade materials should be proof-rolled with a large 
vibratory drum roller (minimum 10 tons static weight) a minimum of eight passes. The 
vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and 
scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to 
those assumed in this report. 
Aggregate base course materials required for the project should consist of crushed 
basaltic aggregates with a 1.5-inch maximum nominal size and should conform to 
Subsection 703.06 of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge 
Construction (2005). Aggregate base material should be moisture-conditioned to above 
the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
AASHTO T-180 or ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by 
dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

Pavement Joints 
In general, an adequate amount and pattern of joints should be provided for the 
concrete pavement reconstruction project. In general, joints should be provided in rigid 
pavements for the following reasons: 
1. 
To reduce potential curling and warping stresses in the pavement caused by 
temperature and moisture gradients across the pavement slabs. 
2. 
To reduce and control cracking due to volume changes in the concrete. 
3. 
To reduce damage to immovable structures. 
4. 
To facilitate construction. 
Paragraph 3.3.6 “Pavement Joints” in Chapter 3 of the “Pavement Design 
Manual,” prepared by the State of Hawaii – Department of Transportation, should be 
referred to for joint details and construction requirements. 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface 
should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface 
water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. Surface water ponding should 
not be allowed during or after construction. Development of good shoulder drainage along 
with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage structures is strongly 
recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or premature failure of 
the pavements.
