SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a thin mantle of residual and/or 
saprolitic soils underlain by weathered clinker and basalt formation extending to the 
maximum depth drilled of approximately 16.5 feet below the ground surface.  We did not 
encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field exploration. 
However, it should be noted that water levels may vary with seasonal rainfall, time of 
year, and other environmental factors. 
Since weathered clinker and basalt formation were encountered at shallow depths, 
driven pile foundation for the solar panel array structures is unlikely feasible.  To provide 
sufficient uplift and compressive/lateral support, the solar panel array structures may be 
supported on a deep foundation system consisting of micropiles. Based on the subsurface 
conditions encountered, we recommend using an ultimate skin friction of 0.85 ksf for the 
residual and saprolitic soils encountered at the site to determine the ultimate compressive 
and uplift load capacities. Due to the potential for tension crack developing between the 
pile and the surrounding soil at the ground surface, we recommend ignoring the top 2 feet 
of soil resistance when determining the ultimate compressive and uplift load capacities. 
Based on the anticipated light structural loads and the generally stiff subsurface 
conditions, we recommend using shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to 
support the proposed solar substation building.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 
3,000 psf may be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the 
recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills needed to achieve the design 
finished grades. For footings bearing on the recompacted on-site soils and/or new 
compacted fills, the bottom of the footing excavations should be scarified at least 
8 inches and recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a 
relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement of reinforcing steel 
and/or concrete. 
Based on our laboratory tests, the on-site clayey soils exhibit slight to moderate 
swelling and shrinkage potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. To reduce the 
potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking 
and swelling of the clayey soils, we recommend capping the slab subgrades with at 
least 6 inches of non-expansive select granular fill materials compacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and 
recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
conducted for the proposed West Oahu Solar Farm Development project in the Kapolei 
area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project location and general vicinity are shown 
on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of 
foundations, slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, site grading, and pavements only. The 
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at 
the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located off the Interstate Route H-1 Freeway to the east of 
Makakilo Ridge in the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The general vicinity of 
the West Oahu Solar Farm Development is presented on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1.  We understand it is desired to develop a 12.5 MW of solar photovoltaic power 
plus  50-MWh battery energy storage plant located in Kapolei.  The plant will be constructed 
on approximately 80 acres of open land and will consist of solar panels installed on 
ground-mounted systems, various pads for electrical equipment, such as switchgear, 
transformers and inverters, and overhead or underground electrical circuits. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that detailed foundation 
support for the solar array will be designed by the vendor. Detailed information 
pertaining to the project layouts were not available at the time this report was prepared. 
However, we understand that the solar panels will be mounted on a fixed tilt system with 
no moving parts and no equipment higher than 10 feet tall.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau Shield Volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct 
while Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau 
lavas banking against its eastern flank.  These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad 
plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau. 
During the later stage of its activity, the composition of the lavas from Waianae 
shifted towards andesitic or trachytic basalts.  A series of events along the southerly rift 
zone formed several composite cinder and lava cones, including Puu Makakilo and 
Puu Kapuai.  Lavas in these cones are typically very dense with a platy structure 
resulting from horizontal jointing.  The project site is situated on the southern facing 
slope of Puu Kapuai.  Previous exploration in the vicinity of the project site indicates that 
subsurface conditions generally consist of a relatively thin mantle of residual soil 
overlying basaltic rock formation. 
2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is located on the northern side of the H-1 Freeway and Kualakai 
Parkway in the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  A general site plan showing 
the project layout is provided as Plate 2.   
The project site generally slopes downward from north to south.  Based on 
Google Earth Pro, the existing ground surface elevations range from about +600 to 
+300 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  The ground surface consisted of reddish brown silty 
clay and was covered with grasses and shrubs.  An old irrigation ditch system, 
apparently abandoned, traverses approximately through the middle of the project site 
was observed.  Currently, the project site is being used as ranch lands.

Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling 14 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 14, extending to depths of approximately 14 to 
16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, four bulk samples of the 
near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-4, were collected for laboratory 
California Bearing Ratio (CBR), R-value, Redox Potential and Thermal Resistivity 
testing to evaluate the pavement support characteristics, corrosivity and heat transfer 
characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate locations of borings drilled 
and bulk samples collected are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The project site is generally underlain by residual soils over saprolite and 
weathered basalt formation.  The residual soils, consisting of stiff to hard silty and 
clayey soils, were encountered with variable thicknesses primarily between about 1 and 
8.5 feet, however, in localized areas extended to the maximum depth drilled of about 
16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Saprolite soils (completed weathered basalt 
rock), consisting of stiff to hard sandy and silty soils or dense sandy and gravelly soils, 
were encountered primarily from about 6.5 to 9.5 feet, however, in localized areas 
extended to the maximum depth drilled of approximately 16.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface. 
Weathered clinker and basalt formation were encountered at relatively shallow 
depths of about 1 to 4 feet below the existing ground surface in three borings (Boring 
Nos. 3 through 5).  In other areas (Boring Nos. 8, 10, 11, 12 and 14), the weathered 
basalt formation including clinker was encountered at depths of about 6.5 to 9.5 feet, 
extending to the maximum depth drilled of about 16.5 feet below the ground surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted groundwater levels are subject to change due 
to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.

3.1 
Solar Array Foundations 
We understand tracker systems supported on driven wide flange beams are 
usually planned for the support of the solar arrays for the project.  However, based on 
the subsoil conditions encountered, driving the wide flange beams will be very difficult to 
penetrate the very stiff residual/saprolite soils and weathered rock formation. An 
alternate foundation system, such as micropiles, may be considered.  Based on 
experience with other similar projects, we understand the structural loads of the 
ground-mounted tracker systems are relatively light with dead weight and wind forces 
governing the foundation design. To develop the required bearing, uplift, and lateral 
load resistances, we envision that the solar panel array structures may be supported by 
a micropile system consisting of a small diameter (typically 5.5 or 7 inches in diameter 
as local availability) drilled and grouted pile with center bar reinforcing. 
The micropile would derive vertical support principally from skin friction between 
the micropile and the surrounding soil/rock. Based on the subsurface conditions 
encountered, we recommend using an ultimate skin friction of 0.85 kips per square foot 
(ksf) for the residual and saprolitic soils encountered at the site to determine the

It should be noted that the clayey and silty materials encountered at the site may 
be susceptible to tension cracking near the ground surface due to soil moisture 
fluctuations and lateral movements of the solar array micropile foundation system. The 
tension crack would result in the development of a gap between the micropile and the 
surrounding soil extending from the ground surface down into the subsurface. 
Therefore, we recommend that the upper 2 feet of soil resistance be ignored when 
calculating the compressive and upload load capacities of the solar array micropile 
system.

3.1.2 Micropile Construction Considerations 
It should be noted that the bond stress between the grout bulb and the soil is highly 
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the 
contractor to install the micropile.  Therefore, the bond stress between the grout

3.1.3 Micropile Load Test Program 
It should be noted that the micropile load capacity is highly dependent on the 
drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the contractor to install 
the micropile.  Therefore, the micropile load capacity may vary considerably 
between different contractors and micropile foundation systems.  In order to 
determine whether the contractor’s methods of micropile installation are adequate 
and to determine the ultimate load capacity, we recommend performing 
one pre-production load test.  In general, the purpose of the pre-production load 
test on a sacrificial micropile is to fulfill the following objectives: 
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed 
by the contractor to install the micropiles to the depths required 
To assess the contractor’s method of drilling and grouting 
To develop criteria for determination of the bonded zone and evaluate 
the ultimate bonded stress 
In general, the pre-production load test should be performed in accordance with 
ASTM Test Designation D3689 (Procedure A - Quick Test).  Based on experience, 
we believe that the load test should be conducted at least 7 days after completion 
of the micropile installation to allow the grout adequate time to cure.  The loading 
apparatus should consider the reaction mechanism to support the tensile testing 
load.  
The load test micropile should be loaded gradually to a maximum testing load of at 
least 200 percent of the allowable design load in tension and reaching a failure load 
or a rebar yielding load, whichever is smaller.  To facilitate greater testing loads, we 
recommend that the pre-production load test micropile be reinforced by a rebar with 
the design size and grade determined based on the maximum testing load 
conforming to ASTM A615. 
In addition to the pre-production load test, we also recommend performing proof 
test on selected micropiles during construction to confirm the load carrying capacity 
of the installed micropiles.  Based on the micropile drilling and grouting records, 
Geolabs should select test micropiles after completion of the production micropile

3.2 
Building Foundations 
We understand that a new solar power generating facility is planned. Details of the 
PV facility layout plans and structural loading information were not available at the time 
this report was prepared.  
Based on the anticipated structural loads and the subsurface conditions 
encountered, we believe that the new buildings may be supported on a shallow foundation 
system, consisting of spread and strip footings. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 
3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design of the footings bearing on 
the recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills needed to achieve the design 
finished grades. The bearing value recommended is for dead-plus-live loads and may be 
increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces. 
Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of footing excavations should 
be over-excavated until stiff/dense materials or to a maximum of 2 feet deep in the footing 
excavation. The over-excavation may be backfilled with select granular materials 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction in accordance with ASTM D1557. Relative 
compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the 
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557.

resistance between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive

3.9 
Corrosion Potential 
Design of metallic substructures, such as electrical conduits or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of 
metallic objects. 
A total of five field resistivity surveys were conducted at a maximum of about 
20-foot intervals at five locations, designated as R-1 to R-5, using the in-situ Wenner 
4-Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured by Nilsson 
Electrical Laboratory, Inc. Locations of the resistivity tests are presented in the Site 
Plan, Plate 2. The method utilizes four surface probes or pins (electrodes) to evaluate 
the average resistivity (an indication of corrosivity) of the underlying soil mass. The field 
resistivity survey was used to evaluate the average resistivity values of the soil mass at 
the approximate survey locations. Results of the field resistivity surveys are presented 
in Appendix D. It should be noted that the field resistivity survey may be influenced by 
temperature, weather conditions, soil moisture, existing underground utilities and other 
factors. 
Based on the results of our field resistivity survey, the subsurface soils at the 
project site have resistivity values ranging from about 6,800 to 12,800 ohm-cm, 
corresponding to corrosion ratings of 4 to 5, respectively. It should be noted that the 
resistivity values of the subsurface soils varied substantially with depth and location

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
17 boreholes, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 14 and I-1 through I-3, extending to 
depths of approximately 5 to 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a 
track-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Rock formations were described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3.  Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the 
Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-17. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on 
Plate B-1. 
Five Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-2. 
Eight one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and/or 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils.  The test 
results are summarized on Plate B-3. 
Five 
Unconsolidated 
Undrained 
Triaxial 
Compression 
(TXUU) 
tests 
(ASTM D2850) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained 
shear strength of the clayey and silty soils encountered.  The approximate in-situ 
effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the 
relatively “undisturbed” soil sample.  The test results and the stress-strain curves are 
presented on Plates B-4 through B-8. 
Three sets of Corrosivity tests, including pH Value (ASTM G51), Minimum 
Resistivity (ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0) and Sulfate Content 
(EPA 300.0), were performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field 
exploration. Two Oxidation Reduction (Redox) potential tests (SM 2580B) were 
performed on bulk samples of the near-surface soil samples obtained from our field 
exploration.  The test results are summarized on Plate B-9. 
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the relationship between the moisture 
content and the dry density of the near-surface soils as fill materials.  The test results 
are presented on Plates B-10 through B-13. 
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils.  The test results are presented on Plates B-14 through B-17.

Field Infiltration Tests 
Three field infiltration tests were conducted at the site to evaluate the infiltration 
characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered. The infiltration tests were 
performed at locations designated as I-1 through I-3 as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
These tests were performed in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D 
of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, 
Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with other state’s 
procedures and may generally be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by drilling boreholes utilizing 4-inch 
solid-stem auger to a depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Upon 
reaching the test depth, a solid PVC casing was set to the bottom of the drilled hole to 
allow infiltration only through the soil exposed on the bottom of the boring. Falling head 
infiltration tests were performed to determine the infiltration rate of the underlying 
subsurface materials. Each test consisted of four trials of filling the casing with 
24 inches of water and taking periodic readings over a one-hour trial period. The test 
results are presented on Plates C-1 through C-3.

Field Resistivity Surveys 
Geophysical survey equipment (i.e. resistance meter) was mobilized to the project 
site on July 17, 2019 for the field resistivity survey.  Five soil resistivity surveys were 
conducted at locations designated as R-1 through R-5, using the in-situ Wenner 4-Pin 
Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured by Nilsson Electrical 
Laboratory, Inc. The method utilizes four surface probes or pins (electrodes) to evaluate 
the average resistivity of the underlying soil mass. The average resistivity values of the soil 
mass, extending from the existing ground surface to the test depth, were assessed by 
using this method at the approximate survey locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The soil resistivity surveys were performed along the existing ground surface at the 
approximate survey locations shown on the site plan. The average soil resistance along 
the proposed alignment was evaluated at each survey location with the electrodes aligned 
at equal spacing of generally 2.5, 5, 10, and 20 feet. The depth of penetration of a 
resistivity sounding is a function of the electrode spacing. 
The resistance meter (Nilsson Model 400) provides a voltage between the outer 
electrodes, causing an electrical current to flow between them. The voltage drop between 
the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive, built-in voltmeter. As a result, the soil 
resistance (in ohms) is obtained from the voltmeter dial reading. The soil resistivity 
(in ohm-cm), which is directly related to both resistance and spacing, can then be 
computed. The evaluated soil resistances and computed resistivity values are presented 
on Plate D-1. In addition, the computed resistivity values with pin spacing at the test 
locations are presented in the graphs on Plate D-2. At the time of resistivity surveying, the 
temperature was about 85º to 90º F. The existing ground surface was damp to moist. It 
should be noted that the soil resistivity may be influenced by temperature, weather 
conditions, existing underground utilities and other factors.
