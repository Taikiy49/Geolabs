SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
General
The project site is located on the island of Oahu the third largest of the eight major
islands of the Hawaiian Archipelago Oahu was formed by the volcanic activity of two
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 20 Page 6
large shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau North Halawa Valley is an erosional feature
which has been deeply incised into the Koolau dome
Koolau was built during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene by the accumulation
of thinly bedded basaltic lava flows extruded from vents along a fissure line over thirty
miles in length which roughly paralleled the trend of what is now the Koolau Range The
main shield building activity ended in the early Pleistocene approximately 2 5 million years
ago
After the end of the main shield building activity the forces of erosion began to cut
deep valleys into the volcanic dome During the middle to late Pleistocene the Ice Age
sea levels fluctuated ecstatically in response to the cycles of continental glaciation As
the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the ocean basins so sea
levels fell to stands below present sea level When the glaciers melted and retreated an
excess of water became available so sea levels rose to elevations above present sea
level
The processes of erosion and deposition were effected by these glacioectatic sea
level fluctuations When sea level was low the erosional base level was correspondingly
lower and valleys were carved to depths below present sea level When sea level was
high the base level was raised so that sediments accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are dominated by
mass transfer i e masses of soil and or rock become detached from the steep valley
walls and are transported downslope toward the axis of the valley primarily by gravity as
colluvium Once this material reaches the stream in the central portion of the valley
alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited
as alluvium
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 20 Page 7
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy i e the flows transmit
large volumes of water for very short periods Because of this transport of sediments is
intermittent and the bulk of a stream s hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture
of boulders cobbles gravel sand and fines When base leveis change these sediment
loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place long enough chemical processes begin to alter the
materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the material and causing
an induration or cementation of the coarse portion of the sediment into a poorly
consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in
the areas above the valley floors and upstream in the headwaters This continued erosion
generates material which is transported downslope or downstream covering the older
alluvial deposits
Depending upon the local base level and the rate of transport these newer
sediments are generally transient in terms of geological time In addition their
consistency and density is generally less than that of the older partially consolidated
deposits
In general our exploration encountered several distinct types of geological units
The following are descriptions of the material types which are approximately delineated
on the Geology Map of Project Area on Plate 4
Koolau Basalt
Koolau Basalts consist of thinly bedded basaltic lava flows with an average flow
unit thickness of 10 feet or less The flows occur both in the a a and pahoehoe
forms with transitions between the two forms The lavas are generally vesicular
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 20 Page 8
with jointing resulting from contraction during cooling and solidification of the
molten rock
The Koolau Basalts grade with depth from extremely weathered saprolites to
relatively unweathered The basalts generally have good strength characteristics
and may be considered excellent bearing formations
Residual Materials
i
Residual materials are actually a subset of Koolau Basalts since they are soil
materials derived from the in situ chemical weathering of the parent basaltic rocks
These soils range from structureless residual clayey silts or silty clays to saprolites
extremely weathered basalt which are soils that retain the relict structure of the
parent rock e g joints vesicles and or flow structures
These soils generally grade through the range with depth and continue to grade
to the underlying basalt The residual materials generally exhibit good strength
characteristics and are good bearing materials for lightly to moderately loaded
structures
Older Sediments
These materials were laid down during the later Pleistocene and are poorly sorted
bouldery alluvium and colluvium In situ weathering and induration have altered
the materials into a highly weathered poorly consolidated conglomerate Core
samples of these older sediments are typified as highly weathered cobbles and
gravel in a matrix of cemented sandy silt
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 20 Page 9
These materials generally have good to excellent strength characteristics and
provide an excellent bearing layer for foundation loads
Recent Sediments
These materials are differentiated into two units Alluvium and Colluvium The
alluvium is composed of terrestrial sediments deposited by the movement of water
and is mostly silts and clays with gravel cobbles and boulders The deposits are
poorly sorted highly variable in consistency density and are encountered in and
adjacent to active stream channels
Colluvium is detrital rock and soil which has been transported from its original
position by gravity This material is generally bouldery Adobe clays and typically
have poor stability and foundation support characteristics
Man Made Fill
This unit consists of miscellaneous materials placed for site grading in the
industrialized lower portion of the project site and part of the construction of the
pioneer and access roads for the H 3 Highway project

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on the subsoil conditions it appears that the upper alluvial deposits are
competent to support the high embankment fills below the abutments of the proposed
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 20 Page 12
bridges However the generally stiff soils are not suitable for supporting the high
foundation loads from the piers and abutments of the bridges Estimated settlements of
the piers and abutments bearing on the stiff soils are on the order of one inch
Additionally the stiff soils are susceptible to scour which could undermine the footings
Due to scour depth considerations deep foundation embedments are required for
all the bridge piers Except for the eastern pier at Bridge No 5 the remaining piers
should be supported on spread footings bearing on the dense conglomerate layer
Spread footing support may also be considered at the western abutment of Bridge No
5 where the foundation subgrade may be in dense conglomerate
The other bridge abutments and the eastern pier of Bridge No 5 may be
supported on piles end bearing on the underlying highly weathered basalt rock
Predrilling should be used prior to pile driving to penetrate the cobbles and boulders in
the upper alluvial deposits and conglomerate Slow and difficult predrilling should be
anticipated through the cobbles and boulders
Deep excavations and dewatering are anticipated to be required for the foundation
construction The excavated on site materials generally have high moisture contents and
significant percentage of boulders which require additional handling and processing prior
to being re used Due to these limitations we believe that it may be more expedient to
import granular material for the new embankment fills
Site Grading
Besides deep foundation excavations the project construction may involve up to
about 10 feet of cuts and up to about 35 feet of embankment fills at the bridge
abutments
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 20 Page 13
Ground Preparation
The site should be cleared of vegetation debris and existing structures
Any local soft spots and or unsuitable soft materials encountered on the clear
ground within the construction area should be removed and replaced with properly
compacted select fill material
After clearing and grubbing areas designated to receive fill should be scarified to
a depth of six 6 inches and recompacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum
dry density as determined by AASHTO Test Method T 180 The moisture content
of the subgrade soil should be within 3 of the optimum moisture content during
recompaction
Fill Materials
Potential embankment fill material may be generated from cuts at the bridge sites
The in situ materials have a significant percentage of boulders and generally have
moisture contents in excess of 40 which is about 10 above their optimum
moisture contents In addition some of the cut materials from near hillside cuts
may have significant clay proportions with moderate to high swelling potential
Therefore although use of the natural on site materials would result in a significant
savings on material costs there are definite limitations in the use of these materials
as described below
1 The excavated soil would need to be dried out to near its optimum moisture
content prior to placement in the new fill In light of the relatively high
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 20 Page 14
rainfall normally experienced in North Halawa Valley the drying of the soil
would not be feasible during a significant portion of the year thereby
slowing construction of the bridge embankments
2 The boulders would need to be screened and separated from the finer
portions of the fill to exclude them from the embankments This additional
processing of the soil would slow the embankment operations and increase
costs
3 Due to the expansive potential of the clayey soil the on site material should
not be used within the top three 3 feet of the embankment fill
In light of the above limitations consideration should be given to the use of
imported granular material for the embankment fills to minimize the above
problems Since the cost of off site material is high the project manager must
weigh the difficulties of using the on site material versus the high cost of import
Imported borrow should consist of non expansive granular material generally less
than three inches in size with less than 20 percent passing a No 200 sieve The
imported borrow material should be tested and approved by the geotechnical
engineer prior to being transported to the site
Generally the excavated basalt rock material may be used for the embankment fills
if the particles larger than 6 inches in size are first screened out or reduced to less
than 6 inches in size
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 20 Page 15
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
After site preparation the new fill should be moisture conditioned to near its
optinum moisture content then placed in maximum 10 inch loose lifts and
compacted to obtain a minimum 90 of its maximum dry density established by
the above AASHTO test designation
The upper three feet of fill should be compacted to a minimum of 95 of its
maximum dry density as determined by AASHTO Test Method T 180
Where the existing ground is steeper than five horizontal to one vertical 5H 1V
adequately prepared benching and keying of the ground are required to properly
bond the new fill to the existing slope surface The embankment fill operations
should start at the lowest point and continue up in level compacted layers in
accordance with the above fill requirements The typical benching and keying
construction are shown on Plate 7
Fill Settlements
Ground settlements on the order of less than one 1 inch are anticipated for the
embankment fills placed over the alluvium Due to the granular contents of the
alluvium the anticipated settlements should occur relatively quickly following the
embankment construction

Bridge Piers
The bottoms of the three 3 bridge pier foundations will be placed at elevations
of 288 to 292 feet MSL due to scour depth considerations It appears that
weathered conglomerate which may be considered as a soft rock and is generally
competent for bearing support will be encountered at these foundation subgrade
levels Therefore spread footings may be used to support the piers
However the heterogeneous conglomerate contains soil particles of broadly
variable sizes including gravel cobbles and boulders slightly cemented in a soil
matrix To provide uniform subgrade support below the pier footings we
recommend that the foundation subgrade be over excavated by a minimum depth
of 2 feet and any loose pockets in the underlying conglomerate subgrade be also
removed The over excavations should be backfilled with select borrow material
compacted to a minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density as determined by
AASHTO Test Method T 180
We recommend that an allowable bearing capacity of 4 000 p s f be used to
design the spread footings bearing on the compacted select borrow backfill
Settlements of the pier footings are difficult to estimate due to the heterogeneous
nature of the conglomerate subsoil It appears that the pier footing settlements
under the design load of 200 kips per pier are on the order of less than one 1
inch total and one half 4 of an inch differential

Resistance to lateral loads acting on the proposed bridge may be obtained by
passive earth pressure and frictional resistance developed against the piers and
their footings We recommend that an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 p s f per

Bridge Abutments
The bridge abutments are outside the lateral design scour limits of the stream and
therefore may be placed at higher foundation elevations than the piers However
it appears that the upper alluvial deposits are not competent to support the high
abutment loads of 20 kips per lineal foot
Therefore pile foundations extending down to the highly weathered basalt
formation should be used to support the two 2 end abutments Sixteen and one
half inch 16 octagonal precast and prestressed concrete piles are
recommended Twenty inch 20 octagonal piles were also evaluated and were
found to be less economical than 16 inch octagonal due to the relatively small
increase in lateral capacity as compared to the decrease in capacity due to
reduction for group action
Pile Capacity
The piles would develop their load capacity primarily from end bearing on the
dense highly weathered basalt formation It is estimated that the piles would need
to be driven to a minimum depth of about 40 to 45 feet below the design pile cap
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 20 Page 18
bottom at 319 feet elevation at the western abutment and about 40 to 50 feet
below the design pile cap bottom at 327 feet elevation at the eastern abutment
We recommend that an allowable vertical capacity of 100 tons per pile be used for
the abutment foundation design Pile settlements on the order of less than
one half of an inch are anticipated under the design vertical compression loads
An allowable uplift capacity of 1 0 kip per foot of embedded pile length below the
design scour level may also be used
Based on our lateral pile analyses we also estimate that the vertical piles may
provide an allowable lateral pile capacity of 17 5 kips per pile with a corresponding
pile top deflection of less than one quarter 4 of an inch Where more than one
row of piles is used in the direction of the lateral loadings the above lateral
capacity for a single pile may be reduced approximately by one half for group
action and or seismic loading considerations For example the first row of piles
against the direction of loading may be designed for the full lateral capacity
however the lateral capacity of the piles in the remaining rows should be reduced
to 10 kips per pile for design
We also recommend that the abutment pile caps be set back a minimum distance
of 10 feet from the embankment slope faces in order to develop the design lateral
capacity of the piles
The use of batter piles to resist lateral loads is not recommended for the bridge
site since predrilling and driving piles through the upper river deposits at an angle
would be extremely difficult due to a high potential of collapsing of the predrilled
angle holes prior to pile driving and or pile placement inside the holes

Predrilling
Due to the cobble and boulder contents in the upper subsoils above the
end bearing layer predrilling at the abutment pile locations would be required
Slow and very difficult predrilling is anticipated since the boulders and cobbles in
the soil matrix would obstruct and slow down drilling The predrilled holes should
be a minimum of 20 inches in diameter and we estimate that a minimum predrilling
depth of 40 to 50 feet per pile is needed
The annular space between the pile and the predrilled hole should be backfilled
with sand to provide good contact and allow development of the lateral capacity
of the pile
Cobbles and smaller boulders particularly when they are in a clayey silt and or
silty sand matrix can generally be removed with conventional caisson drilling
equipment though drilling progress would be slow However very large boulders
may require removal by special tools like boulder bits or buckets and in some
cases may also require some manual effort The water table as encountered in
our field exploration may also hinder the progress of predrilling
Pile Driving
We recommend the use of a pile driving hammer which can deliver a minimum of
32 000 foot pounds of energy during the final driving of the piles The piles should
be driven to a minimum resistance of about 100 blows per foot for the last foot of
penetration or 10 blows per inch for the last 3 inches
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2254 20 Page 20
The driving process should be continuous without interruption for each pile A pile
may be rejected or its design capacity may be modified if the driving process is
interrupted for more than four 4 hours
During easy driving when the pile tip is within loose soils such as predrilling
cuttings the hammer stroke or energy should be carefully controlled so that
excessive tensile stresses are not developed in the piles
The pile driving should be observed by a qualified geotechnical engineer to see
that the piles are driven to a sufficient depth and into to a competent bearing
layer material

fulfill the following objectives in order to construct safe and economical pile
foundations for the proposed bridge
1 To more accurately determine the predrilling depth s and the production
pile lerigth s
2 To help evaluate and modify if necessary the pile driving criteria
We recommend that approximately ten 10 percent of the total number of piles be
driven as test piles at the bridge site These test piles may later be incorporated
into the actual foundation system of the proposed bridge

design capacity
Splice cans should be provided at the site during test pile driving to expedite

4 of an inch thick and should be at least five 5 feet long Splices should not
be utilized for piles which are required to resist uplift

Excavation
Due to the required embedments deep excavations are anticipated for construction
of the bridge foundations Since the subsoils appear to be generally stiff the foundation
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 20 Page 25
excavations may be sloped back on all sides to two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V
slopes Alternately if vertical cuts are used the excavations should be properly
constructed and shored as recommended below
Excavation Method
Based on the design foundation elevations excavation depths in excess of 20 feet
and up to approximately 40 feet are anticipated
Based on the subsoil conditions we believe that excavation operations may be
moderately difficult due to the amount of boulders anticipated However
excavation into the highly weathered basalt formation may occasionally require the
use of hard ripping equipment such as a hoeram It is also anticipated that the
excavations would recover a significant quantity of cobbles boulders and rock
fragments which may need to be disposed off the project site
In general the method and equipment to be used for excavation should be left to
the choice of the contractor within practical limits and safety considerations
Excavation Support
If vertical excavations are utilized we recommend that the deep excavations be
properly supported to comply with applicable safety regulations
The adequacy and safety of the design and installation of the shoring and bracing
system used should be made the sole responsibility of the contractor His qualified
representative who should be required to be continuously present on site will
have the best opportunity to promptly observe any changing conditions during
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 2254 20 Page 26
construction such as unforseen subsoils unexpected high groundwater table
unsuitable construction sequence or techniques etc which might affect the
excavation shoring stability
Even with good construction procedure some movement of the shoring system
might still occur because of changes in earth stresses which will normally occur
during excavation The magnitude of movement will depend greatly on
workmanship for example how quickly and tightly the excavation support is
installed It also depends on the types of subsoils the depth and width of
excavation and also the progress rate of excavation
In view of the complexity of the problem it is difficult to accurately predict
movement of a shoring system and its adjacent ground Therefore it is important
to realize that excavation support should be installed properly as early as
practicable and that the adjacent ground be continuously monitored for cracks
dips and or other indications of movements such as excavation face sloughing
Dewatering
Dewatering of a relatively large quantity of water may be required in the deep
foundation excavations which will extend well below the stream bed and the stream water
level In addition the excavation bottoms may terminate in highly permeable cobble and
boulder subsoils
Dewatering Method
The method and equipment to be used for the project dewatering should be left
to the choice of the contractor within practical limits and safety considerations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 20 Page 27
However it is our opinion that Dewatering shall mean any works or systems
required to lower the natural groundwater table and to exclude it from the
excavations to permit construction of the proposed bridge foundations under safe
and dry conditions These works or systems should therefore include but are not
limited to pumping only
We suggest that the following two 2 basic criteria be considered in selecting a
suitable method of dewatering
a The method should maintain adequate excavation stability and
provide safe and dry working conditions in the excavations
b The method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modification to
accommodate various ground conditions
In general it appears that sump pumping inside the excavations or dewatering by
means of a well point system along the outside of excavation may be used
Construction Precaution
The potential impact of the selected dewatering system on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to operation
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His qualified representative who should be
required to be continuously present on site will have the best opportunity to
promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and to implement
as soon as possible necessary precautionary or remedial measures including but
not limited to stopping or slowing down the dewatering operations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 20 Page 28
Where encountered at the excavation bottom permeable and granular subsoils
may be susceptible to piping and quick conditions Therefore the project
dewatering operations should be done without pumping out soil fines pumping
clear water only and should be coordinated with shoring installation so that the
excavations are not jeopardized The dewatering operations should be carried out
without creating a quick condition or softening of the excavation bottoms
