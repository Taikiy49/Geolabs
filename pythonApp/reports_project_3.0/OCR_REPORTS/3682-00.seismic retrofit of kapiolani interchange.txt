PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
A seismic retrofit study was conducted for the existing Kapiolani Interchange in
Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The subject project included the portion of the
Interstate Route H 1 Highway bridge structure between approximately Kapiolani
Boulevard and ist Avenue This seismic retrofit study also included the westbound
off ramp across the Manoa Palolo Drainage Canal The general location of the project
is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
As part of the seismic retrofit study the structural capacities and stiffness of the
existing foundations were critical to the modeling of the bridge structure foundations
Our geotechnical engineering services were directed at evaluating the subsurface soil
conditions from the available subsurface information and providing the foundation
capacities and parameters for stiffness modeling of the existing and proposed retrofitted
foundations Retrofitting of the foundations involved thickening of the spread footings
increasing the number of piles and therefore also increasing the size of the pile cap
and installation of precast concrete blocks at the abutments to engage the abutment
backfill for lateral load resistance during a seismic event

EXISTING CONDITIONS
Based on our review of the construction plans the existing bridge structure
consists of multiple spans supported by a combination of spread footings and driven pile
foundations The abutment structures are also supported on a combination of spread
footings and driven pile foundations The driven pile foundations generally consist of
16 5 inch diameter octagonal shaped concrete piles The existing foundation systems
described in this report were based on the construction plans from the following
projects
The main bridge structure consists of 44 columns numbered from M1 through
M44 which are all supported by spread footings of various sizes and depths Columns
for the off ramp structure are numbered 1 through 9 and R1 through R21 for a total of
30 columns Columns 4 through 9 and R1 through R5 are supported on pile
foundations while the remaining columns Columns 1 through 3 and R6 through R21
are supported on spread footings
In addition Abutment A1 Retaining Wall 2 and a portion of Abutment A2 are
supported on pile foundations The other abutments and retaining walls are supported
on spread footings The approximate locations of the columns and abutments and the
foundation types are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2

LIQUEFACTION POTENTIAL
Liquefaction occurs when a build up of excess pore water pressure is induced in
saturated cohesionless soils resulting in a substantial loss of strength The most
common cause of this phenomenon is the cyclic loading due to earthquakes This
process may permit the soil to make both vertical and horizontal movements and if not
confined would result in significant deformations Loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion are most susceptible to liquefaction Generally
liquefaction is difficult to occur if the loose deposit is deeper than 50 feet below the
ground surface The greater overburden pressure would be sufficient to prevent
liquefaction in the deeper soil deposits
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3682 00 Page 5
As mentioned earlier the loose river deposits under Columns R1 through R5 may
be considered potentially liquefiable Based on the descriptions in the old borings and
past experience with the general area we estimate the Standard Penetration Test SPT
blow counts to be approximately 2 to 4 blows per foot for the loose river deposits
Given a design earthquake magnitude of 6 5 and a peak ground acceleration of 0 18g
we estimate that SPT blow counts of about 16 blows per foot would be required to resist
the potential for liquefaction Furthermore using an estimated SPT blow count of 2 to
4 blows per foot a peak ground acceleration of 0 03g or greater resulting from a
magnitude 6 5 seismic event will likely cause liquefaction of the loose river deposit
Should liquefaction of the loose river deposits occur the potential exists for
significant vertical and lateral displacement of the loose river deposits and distress and
loss of integrity of the off ramp structure foundations The vertical displacement would
impose downdrag forces on the pile foundations and may cause bearing failure of the
pile foundations The horizontal displacement caused by liquefaction may result in
shearing of the pile foundations and loss of lateral support of the upper soil material
It is our understanding that the potential for liquefaction and mitigation measures
for liquefaction at this site will be addressed separately in a subsequent project after the
existing foundations are retrofitted Please note that the field exploration methods for
evaluating the potential for liquefaction and mitigating methods will require specialized
techniques specifically for liquefaction evaluation

Lateral Load Resistance of Abutments
The abutment structure may be considered a large footing bearing on the
abutment fill soils for resistance of lateral loads during a seismic event in the longitudinal
direction For lateral loads imposed on the abutments the stiffness of the abutment fill
soils will generally be mobilized prior to the lateral resistance of its foundations either
spread footings or pile foundations Provided that intimate contact exists between the
backfill soil and the abutments a soil stiffness spring equal to approximately 330 kips
per inch of deflection per lineal foot of wall may be used in resisting lateral loads To
reduce the potential for failing the abutment fill soils the lateral deflection should be
limited to 1 inch or less Therefore the maximum lateral load resistance of the
abutment structure should be limited to 330 kips per lineal foot of abutment wall The
structural engineer should check the structure displacement required in order to engage
the abutment fill soils for lateral load resistance
Please note that the stiffness of the abutment fill soils only applies for forces that
cause the retaining wall to move into the backfill soil longitudinal direction Therefore
only the lateral load resistance of the abutment foundation and wing walls may be
utilized to resist the lateral loads in the transverse direction

Axial Compression and Uplift Capacities of Pile Foundations
For the portion of the structure supported on pile foundations the capacities of
the piles in compression and uplift are provided on Plate A 3 1 The uplift capacity of
the pile is based on the full length of the pile without splicing The uplift capacity of the
pile is also based on the assumption that a mechanical connection capable of
transmitting tension is provided at the pile cap If a non mechanical splice was used for
the pile or if a mechanical connection was not provided at the pile cap the uplift
capacity of the pile will need to be reduced or neglected as appropriate
Based on the construction drawings it appears that non mechanical splices were
used and that a mechanical connection capable of transmitting tension was not made at
the pile cap Therefore the uplift capacity of the existing pile foundations should be
neglected in our analyses due to the uncertainty in the ability of the connections to
transmit tension forces If mechanical connections are provided between the pile cap
and the pile head the uplift capacities estimated in Plate A 3 1 may be used
The uplift capacities for Columns 4 and 9 were calculated using reduced soil
adhesion parameters because these piles were pre drilled to a depth of at least 2 feet
into the basalt rock formation prior to installation
The capacities of the proposed retrofit piles in compression and uplift are
provided on Plate A 5 The uplift capacity of the pile is based on the full length of the
pile with mechanical splices and a mechanical connection at the pile cap

required to expose the existing footings Due to the limited space available we believe
that shoring of the excavations deeper than about 5 feet below the existing ground

The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with all

PROPOSED RETROFIT PILE CONSTRUCTION
Based on the seismic retrofit study retrofit piles are proposed for Columns 5 6
and 8 and Columns R1 R3 and R5 The proposed retrofit pile configurations are
the foundation retrofit we believe that steel pipe piles would be an effective retrofit
alternative for this project It should be noted that various retrofit pile configurations
were investigated in our study to optimize the foundation retrofit scheme We also
investigated the effect of using a fixed head connection for the retrofit piles to the pile
cap to provide the additional stiffness among others
Because Pier 20 Column R2 was located at the median of the Interstate Route
H 1 Highway Pier 21 Column R1 was chosen to be retrofitted in lieu of Pier 20
Column R2 However Pier 21 Column R1 is located within the Manoa Palolo
Drainage Canal and therefore will require the acquisition of a permit to perform the
retrofit work
The proposed deep foundation retrofit construction will generally consist of the
addition of steel pipe piles around the perimeter of the retrofitted footing or pile cap In
order to resemble the existing pile foundations and to approximately match the stiffness
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3682 00 Page 12
of the existing 16 5 inch octagonal concrete piles we recommend that 16 inch diameter
steel pipe piles be used as the retrofit piles Due to the significant structural load
demands at Column R4 we recommend the use of 24 inch diameter steel pipe piles be
used for foundation retrofit Otherwise two rows of 16 inch diameter steel pipe piles
would be needed to retrofit the foundation for Column R4 Therefore the steel pipe
piles recommended for this seismic retrofit project will have outside diameters of
16 inches or 24 inches a steel thickness of 0 5 inches and a conical driving tip to
facilitate pile driving The interior section of the pipe piles should be in filled with
concrete after the driving is complete Due to the potentially corrosive environment of
the subsurface the design of the steel pipe piles has taken into consideration that up to
0 0625 inches of the steel pipe pile thickness may be lost to corrosion
Due to limited headroom and to facilitate the pile installation process the upper
portion of the steel pipe piles may be installed using a vibratory hammer However the
last 5 feet of the steel pipe pile should be installed using an impact pile driving hammer
Use of steel pipe piles for this project will allow splicing of the piles by welding the upper
pile section to the lower driven pile section The use of an outside sleeve for pile
splicing is not recommended as it may lead to less intimate contact of the pile with the
adjacent soil and reduce the lateral and uplift load capacities of the pile
In general the steel pipe piles should have a minimum center to center pile
spacing of 2 5 times the pile diameter to avoid significant reduction in compressive
load capacity due to group action Due to the relatively tight spacing of the footings
we believe that the center to center spacing of 3 feet for the 16 inch diameter steel
pipe piles will be acceptable The foundation analyses conducted have taken into
consideration the slightly reduced center to center spacing of some of the 16 inch
diameter steel pipe piles

recommended depths and driving resistance

DYNAMIC LOAD TESTING
Dynamic load testing involves attaching two strain transducers and two
accelerometers to the pile at approximately 2 to 3 pile diameters below the pile head
during impact driving or at a convenient location during re tap testing A cable connects
the gauges near the pile head with the pile driving analyses system located at ground
level and 50 to 100 feet laterally from the pile location
The dynamic load testing should be performed using a GC GCPC or PAK
Model Pile Driving Analyzer PDA or acceptable equivalent All equipment necessary
for the dynamic load testing such as gauges cables etc should be furnished by the
Contractor The equipment should conform to the requirements of ASTM D 4945 89
Standard Test Method for High Strain Dynamic Testing of Piles
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3682 00 Page 16
The equipment should be operated and stored in a sheltered area with a
temperature between 45 and 90 degrees Fahrenheit and outside of direct sunlight The
equipment should be provided with a regular power source line power or portable
generator providing at least 1 800 watts of 115 volt AC power with a frequency of
60 Hz Direct current welders or non constant power sources should not be allowed
Prior to impact driving holes should be drilled in the pile and the gauges should
then be installed The stresses in the pile should be monitored to evaluate if addition of
pile cushions or reduction in driving energy should be performed The pile should also
be monitored for non axial driving in which case realignment will be necessary
The driving should temporarily cease when the gauges are within 1 foot from the
ground surface to avoid damage to the gauges The gauges should then be reattached
to the top of the next pile segment prior to resuming driving
The re tapping should commence after at least 24 hours following installation with
a hammer that has been warmed up with at least 20 continuous blows on a suitable
object other than the structure The termination criteria for the re driven test piles
should be a maximum length of penetration of 6 inches or a maximum total blow count
of 100 blows whichever occurs first
The CAPWAP Case Pile Wave Analysis Program analyses and other refined
wave equation analysis will be based upon the subsurface conditions and the results of
the dynamic load tests Both the driving and the re tapping data may be used from the
dynamic load tests to perform the CAPWAP analyses
