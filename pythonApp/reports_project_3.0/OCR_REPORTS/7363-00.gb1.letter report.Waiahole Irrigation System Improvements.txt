PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project involves improvements to the Department of Agriculture’s Waiahole 
Irrigation System on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The improvements include installing 
Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition (SCADA) towers at four locations along 
Waiahole Irrigation Ditch.  The SCADA towers will be situated at the following locations. 
SCADA #1 – North side of Mililani Memorial Park and Mortuary 
SCADA #2 – Inlet of Siphon C on the east side of Kipapa Gulch 
SCADA #3 – Inlet of Siphon D in the vicinity of the west end of Ahiku Street  
SCADA #4 – In the vicinity of Kunia Road crossing

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and 
Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early 
Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion 
incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series of lava 
flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred to as the 
Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against 
the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch. The dips 
of the lava beds generally range from 3 to 5 degrees. The lava flows on the plateau have 
weathered to depths of about 50 to 100 feet and are characterized by the red colors of the 
soil. The deep narrow canyons, such as Waikakalaua, Kipapa, and Kaukonahua, are the 
result of this weathering. The brown and reddish brown soils generally consist of ancient 
alluvial deposits overlying the weathered rock. 
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The planned improvements along the existing Waiahole Irrigation Ditch are located 
at the north side of Mililani Memorial Park and Mortuary, inlet of Siphon C located on the 
east side of Kipapa Gulch, inlet of Siphon D located in the vicinity of the west end of 
Ahiku Street, and in the vicinity of Kunia Road crossing.  These areas are shown on the 
Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.4. 
In general, the locations are relatively open and the existing ground surfaces are 
flat.  The ground surfaces generally consist of clayey soils that were generally in a very 
stiff to hard consistency.  The irrigation ditch was generally lined with concrete.  Each site 
can be accessed via dirt roads. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling one boring at each proposed SCADA 
tower location.  Each boring extended to a depth of about 16.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface.  The borings are designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4 and were drilled 
at SCADA #1 through SCADA #4, respectively.   
Based on our field exploration, the subsurface conditions at each proposed SCADA 
tower location generally consisted of fill about 1 to 4 feet thick.  The fill consisted of stiff to 
very stiff clayey silt.  Below the fill, residual soils consisting of stiff to hard silty clay and 
clayey silt were generally encountered to the maximum depth explored at SCADA # 1

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, the SCADA towers may be 
supported on either cast-in-place concrete drilled shaft or shallow spread footing 
foundations.  However, we believe that cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts should be 
used since concrete drilled shafts provide bearing and lateral load resistances using a 
smaller area within the site and can be placed relatively closer to the irrigation ditch, if 
needed.  Detailed discussions and recommendations for foundation design are presented 
in the following sections. 
SCADA TOWER DRILLED SHAFT FOUNDATIONS 
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances for the new 
SCADA tower structures, cast-in-place concrete drilled shaft foundations may be used to 
support the SCADA towers.  Based on the anticipated structural loads and the subsoil 
conditions encountered at the project sites, we recommend using a single cast-in-place 
concrete drilled shaft with a minimum diameter of 20 inches and an embedment length of 
not less than 6 feet below the design finished grade to support each SCADA tower.   
The cast-in-place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally 
from skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils.  An allowable 
compressive load capacity of up to 5 kips per shaft may be used for dead-plus-live 
loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as wind or 
seismic forces. 
Lateral loads imposed on the SCADA tower foundations may generally be 
resisted by the shaft-soil system to deflect.  Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is 
a function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil, the stiffness of the shaft, allowable 
deflection at the top of the shaft, and induced moment in the shaft.  The lateral load 
analyses were performed using the program LPILE-plus for Windows, which is a 
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference, laterally loaded deep foundation program 
originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin. The program solves for deflection 
and bending moment along a deep foundation under lateral loads as a function of depth. 
The analysis was carried out with the use of non-linear “p-y” curves to represent soil 
moduli. The lateral deflection was then computed using the appropriate soil moduli at 
various depths.

Based on the information provided, each drilled shaft (20-inch diameter) for the 
SCADA tower will be subjected to a lateral load of 826 pounds and an overturning 
moment of about 10,000 pound-feet at the ground surface.  Based on our foundation 
analyses, the maximum moment induced in the drilled shaft increases slightly to about 
10.3 kip-feet at a depth of about 0.8 feet below the top of the shaft.  The maximum 
shear induced in the drilled shaft also increases to about 3.7 kips at a depth of about 
4.8 feet below the top of the shaft.  Our lateral load analyses indicated that the 
estimated lateral deflection at the top of the drilled shaft should be less than ¼-inch. 
The anticipated settlements of the SCADA tower foundations are estimated to be 
on the order of less than 0.5 inch.  We believe that a significant portion of the settlements 
is elastic and should occur as the loads are applied. 
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend, to a large extent, on the 
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils.  Therefore, proper 
construction techniques are important.  The contractor should exercise care in drilling the 
shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes. 
A low-shrink concrete mix with high slump (6 to 9-inch range) should be used for 
the concreting operation.  The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce 
the potential for segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix.  If pre-cast concrete 
pole bases are used, the gap between the pole base and the surrounding soils should be 
backfilled with concrete or lean concrete. 
In general, there is a potential for encountering hard basalt rock (in the form of 
relatively unweathered cobbles and boulders) within the residual soil at SCADA #1 through 
SCADA #3 locations.  Therefore, difficult drilling conditions may be encountered at the site 
and should be expected.  The drilled shaft contractor will need to have the appropriate 
equipment and tools to drill through the hard cobbles and boulders, if encountered. 
SCADA TOWER SHALLOW FOUNDATIONS 
As an alternative foundation system, shallow spread footing foundations may be 
used to support the SCADA towers.  An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds 
per square foot (psf) may be used for design of the foundations bearing on the in-situ soils.  
This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for 
transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
The soils encountered at the bottom of footing excavations should be re-compacted 
to a firm and unyielding base. Soft and/or loose soils encountered at the bottom of the 
footing excavations should be removed to expose the underlying firm materials. The 
resulting over-excavation should be backfilled with engineered fill or the footing may 
extend down to bear on the underlying firm material.

In general, the bottom of footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches 
below the lowest adjacent finished grades.  Footings constructed near tops of slopes or on 
sloping ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal 
setback distance of 6 feet measured from the outside bottom edge of the footings to the 
slope face.  
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below a 
one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom 
edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines.  This requirement is 
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with additional structural 
loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement. 
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our 
recommendations, we estimate that total footing settlements may be on the order of 1 inch 
or less.   
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by friction developed 
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure 
acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation.  A coefficient of friction of 0.35 may 
be used for footings bearing on in-situ soils.  Resistance due to passive earth pressure 
may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per 
foot of depth (pcf).  This assumes that the soils around the footings are well compacted.  
Unless covered by pavements or slabs, the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of 
soil should be neglected.   
SITE PREPARATION 
We believe that only minor site grading work will be required to achieve the finished 
grades at the site.  In general, areas within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly 
cleared at the on-set of earthwork.  Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and other 
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site.  Soft, loose, and 
yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or future 
improvements should be over-excavated to expose firm or dense material, and the 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill.  The excavated soft, 
loose, and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site.  In addition, contract 
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation and 
compacted fill placement to account for variation in the over-excavation quantities. 
After clearing, the areas to receive fill and finished subgrades in cut areas should 
be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above 
the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction.  The compaction requirement for the finished subgrades in areas subjected to 
vehicular traffic should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of 
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557.

Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density.   
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft and/or loose areas and 
replacement with well-compacted fill.  A Geolabs representative should evaluate the need 
for over-excavation in the field. 
In general, the excavated on-site soils may be re-used as a source of general fill or 
backfill materials, provided that they are free of deleterious materials (vegetation) and are 
screened of particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension.  Imported fill materials 
needed for the site filling should consist of non-expansive granular materials, such as 
crushed coral, basalt, or cinder sand.  The materials should be well graded from coarse to 
fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve.  The materials should have a 
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less 
when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.   
Fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, 
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  In general, compaction 
should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other similar types of 
acceptable compaction equipment. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the earthwork operations to observe 
whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation process, and to 
confirm whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to those encountered in our 
exploration. 
UTILITY TRENCH 
New electrical duct lines will be required to service the proposed SCADA towers. In 
general, granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) is recommended under the duct lines.   
Free-draining granular materials, such as clean sand or No. 3B Fine gravel 
(ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 
6 inches above the duct lines to provide adequate support around the pipes, and to reduce 
the compaction effort of the backfill, thus reducing the possibility of pipe damage. It is 
critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below 
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. 
Improper backfill material and placement procedures could result in backfill settlement and 
pipe damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the duct lines 
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on-site soils or imported select

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, each extending to a depth of about 16.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
