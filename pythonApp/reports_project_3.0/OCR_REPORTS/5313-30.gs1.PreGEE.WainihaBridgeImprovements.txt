SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our preliminary exploratory borings at the proposed replacement bridge sites
crossing Wainiha Stream generally encountered a thin surface fill layer underlain by an
alluvial deposit to the maximum depth explored of 150 feet below the existing ground
surface Two of the borings encountered residual and saprolitic soil below the alluvium
We encountered very soft to very stiff swamp deposit about 4 to 18 feet thick within the
alluvium We encountered groundwater in the borings at depths ranging from about 4 9 to
9 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration
Based on the results of our exploration the preliminary foundation loads provided
and the preliminary scour depths we recommend using deep foundations consisting of
drilled shafts for foundation support of the new bridge structures The drilled shafts will
extend through the loose to very soft alluvial and swamp deposits and into the underlying
medium stiff to hard and medium dense to very dense alluvial deposits and stiff to hard
residual and saprolite soil The drilled shafts will derive support principally from adhesion
between the drilled shaft and the medium stiff to hard and medium dense to very dense
alluvial soils and stiff to hard residual and saprolitic soils encountered in our borings
Based on the preliminary structural loads imposed on the foundations we
recommend using a shaft diameter of 3 feet with embedment depths of about 75 to
125 feet below the planned bottom of footing elevations for the drilled shaft foundations
The use of larger diameter shafts may be considered It should be noted that potentially
difficult drilling conditions may be encountered during the installation of the drilled shafts
due to the presence of cobbles and boulders within the surface fill layer and alluvial
deposits encountered in the borings drilled at the replacement bridge sites
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific
design recommendations for the replacement bridge project

Introduction
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed Wainiha Bridge Improvements Kuhio Highway
project in Wainiha on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The project location and general
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and presents our preliminary geotechnical
engineering recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing
and engineering analyses These recommendations are intended for the preliminary
design of bridge foundations only A more detailed geotechnical engineering exploration
should be performed after the bridge replacement scheme is selected The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late
Pliocene Epoch more than million years ago Following the cessation of this main
shield building phase renewed volcanic activity occurred with the extrusion of basaltic
lavas of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of
alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation
The majority of the Island of Kauai is covered by lavas of the Waimea Canyon
Volcanic Series These lavas consist of four distinct formations Napali Olokele
Haupu and Makaweli These formations are comprised of thin bedded a a and
pahoehoe flows to massive basalt flows that ponded in calderas and graben
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of
Kauai These rocks are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded
from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various locales Associated with
the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at the vent
During the Pliestocene Epoch Ice Age many sea level changes occurred as a
result of widespread continental glaciation As the great continental glaciers
accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill the
oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels rose
because more water was available The land mass of Kauai remained essentially stable
during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to
present sea level of Kauai
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenious sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs

Existing Site Conditions
The bridge improvement project sites are along Kuhio Highway Route 560
crossing three branches of the Wainiha River in the Hanalei District on the Island of

The existing bridge is approximately 42 feet long and about 11 feet wide
supporting the lane of traffic The roadway approaches to the bridge is covered with
asphaltic concrete The upstream area of the bridge was heavily vegetated with trees
and brush Based on available plans the existing bridge elevation is about 10 feet
Mean Sea Level MSL
Wainiha Bridge No 2 is a single span steel Acrow bridge structure that was
recently constructed Alaeke Road is located on the northern side of the bridge The
bridge structure is approximately 78 feet long and supports one lane of traffic The
upstream and downstream banks are heavily vegetated with grass and brush The
existing ground surface elevations range from about 9 to 10 feet MSL at the existing
bridge structure
Wainiha Bridge No 3 is a three span steel bridge structure with wooden
decking The bridge structure is approximately 146 feet long and 11 feet wide A steel

Drilled Shaft Foundations
Based on our field exploration performed for the project loose to very soft alluvial
and swamp deposits were encountered below the bridge foundations Because of
the excessive settlements that may occur for footings bearing on the loose to very
soft alluvial and swamp deposits and preliminary scour depths we recommend
using drilled shaft foundations with a nominal diameter of 3 feet to support the
abutment and pier structures of the replacement bridge structures at
Wainiha Stream The use of larger diameter shafts may be considered The drilled
shaft foundations would derive support principally from adhesion between the sides
of the drilled shaft and the medium stiff to hard and medium dense to very dense
alluvial deposits and stiff to hard residual and saprolitic soils encountered in our
borings The contribution from end bearing of the drilled shafts was discounted in
our analyses
To facilitate construction of the abutment structures and to avoid construction of a
footing below the streambed level we recommend placing the abutment footings as
high as possible above the groundwater level

Construction Considerations
The performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the following
may have a significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft
foundations
Based on our field exploration the proposed bridge locations are underlain by loose
and very soft alluvial and swamp deposits below the existing ground surface Due
to the loose and very soft consistency of these materials there is a strong potential
for caving in of the materials during the drilling operations To reduce the potential
for significant caving in of the drilled holes temporary casing of the drilled holes will
be required during drilled shaft installation Care should be exercised during
removal of the temporary casing to reduce the potential for necking of the drilled
shaft concrete
Cobbles and boulders were encountered within the fill and alluvial deposits in the
borings drilled at the replacement bridge foundation locations during our preliminary
field exploration Therefore the drilled shaft contractor will need to have the
appropriate equipment and drilling tools to drill through these obstructions where
encountered

Site Grading
We anticipate that the grading work for the project will generally consist of cuts
and fills on the order of about 5 feet thick or less relative to the existing ground surface
for construction of the abutment structures In general grading work should conform to
Section 200 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction
2005 and the preliminary site specific recommendations contained herein
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious materials and other

unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site or stockpiled in
a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted engineered fill In general the excavated soft and wet
soils may not be re used as a source of fill and backfill materials
In general the on site silty clay and clayey silt soils encountered during our field
exploration should be suitable for use as general fill materials provided that the
maximum particle size is less than 3 inches in largest dimension The excavated on site
materials generated from excavations into the near surface materials may be used as
general fill or backfill materials provided that they are screened of the over sized
materials and or processed to meet the gradation requirements less than 3 inches in
largest dimension In addition fill materials should be free of vegetation and deleterious
materials However the excavated soft and wet soils and swamp soils may not be
re used as a source of fill and backfill materials
Imported materials to be used as select granular fill should consist of
non expansive granular material such as crushed coral basalt or cinder sand The
select granular fill should be well graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than
3 inches in largest dimension The material should also contain less than 15 percent
particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value
of 25 or more and should have a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less
Preliminary cut and fill slopes at an inclination of two horizontal to one vertical
two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V or flatter may be used

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the proposed bridge improvement sites
by drilling and sampling five borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 extending
to depths ranging from about 101 to 150 feet below the existing ground surface We
drilled the borings using truck mounted and skid mounted drill rigs equipped with
continuous flight augers and coring tools The approximate boring locations are shown
on the Site Plan Plates 2 1 and 2 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer or geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by laboratory testing Soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A 0 1 Graphic representations of
the materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings
We obtained relatively undisturbed soil samples in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition we obtained
some samples from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586 Penetration
Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration
sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the
bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description Rock cores were
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown on the
Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Six Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 136 including three Hydrometer tests
ASTM D 422 were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation
characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification Graphic presentation of the test
results is provided on Plates B 1 and B 2
A One Dimensional Consolidation test ASTM D 2435 was performed on a
selected soil sample to evaluate the consolidation characteristics of the soils Graphic
presentation of the test result is provided on Plate B 3
Two Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plates B 4
and B 5
Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests ASTM D 2850 were
performed on selected in situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strengths of
the soils The approximate in situ effective overburden pressures were used as the
applied confining pressures for the relatively undisturbed soil samples The test results
and the stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 6 and B 7
One Unconfined Compression test ASTM D 2166 was performed on a selected
in situ cohesive soil sample to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the
soils The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depth and graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 8
