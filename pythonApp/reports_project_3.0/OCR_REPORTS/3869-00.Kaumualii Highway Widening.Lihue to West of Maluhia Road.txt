SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our literature research site reconnaissance and preliminary
field exploration performed for the proposed Kaumualii Highway
Widening project we anticipate that the proposed highway alignments
will traverse weathered volcanic rock and alluvial soil deposits The
majority of the project site is underlain by medium stiff to very stiff
saprolitic soils weathered volcanic deposits Basalt formations were
also encountered at various depths in several borings along the
proposed alignments The basalt formations generally range from
highly weathered to relatively unweathered and from soft to hard In
addition the proposed project will encounter some soft alluvial soil
deposits due to the numerous streams and drainageways through
which the existing highway traverses
Several geotechnical considerations that may have adverse impacts
during the project construction were identified in the field during our site
reconnaissance and preliminary exploration program These
geotechnical considerations include slope raveling settlement due to
soft soil deposits seepage of groundwater and flooding associated
with high rainfall storms Special attention should be given to the
recommendations for cut slope design soft ground stabilization
subgrade stabilization drainage design and erosion control measures
during the design and construction of the highway project
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated along the proposed
alignments we believe that preliminary slope inclinations of 2H 1V may
be used for the design of the planned cut and fill slopes In addition we
believe that a cut slope inclination as steep as may be feasible
in selected areas where very stiff saprolites are encountered Further
study should be conducted to provide site specific recommendations
pertaining to cut and fill slopes with slope inclinations steeper than the
recommended preliminary slope inclinations

In general we believe that a large portion of the excavated materials
may be re used as a source of fill materials for embankment
construction However the soft and or loose materials generated from
excavations into the recent alluvial deposits should not be used as fill
materials and should be properly disposed of off site Since the project
site is located in an area with high rainfall throughout the year we
believe that some aeration of the high moisture in situ soils will be
necessary in order to achieve the minimum 90 percent relative
compaction during earthwork construction
We anticipate that the proposed highway alignment will traverse soft
loose and or unstable ground such as swamps gullies and stream
crossings underlain by recent alluvial soils In general the soft and or
loose soil deposits encountered should be removed where practical
and replaced with compacted fills Otherwise we recommend that a
working platform or a drainage blanket consisting of a woven geotextile
fabric and a gravel layer be established at these soft ground areas
prior to fill placement Where relatively thick soft soil deposits are
encountered soil stabilization methods such as vibro replacement or
jet grouting will likely be necessary especially if large amounts of new
fills and or new structures are planned
Depending on the actual soft ground thickness and the planned fill
heights substantial ground settlements exceeding 3 feet in magnitude
may occur as a result of consolidation of the soft soil deposits induced
by the new fill loads Due to the soft and yielding nature of the subsoils
new fills should be placed in thin lifts and at slow rates to reduce the
potential for shear failure in the soft material A settlement waiting
period will be required for embankment construction over the soft
ground areas In addition a surcharge program with settlement
monitoring may be required to reduce the settlement waiting period
In areas where significant fill heights are planned over the soft soils it
will be necessary to implement staged construction in order to
construct the entire fill embankment Even with staged construction
there will be some long term settlements of the permanent drainage
structure Therefore consideration should be given to over sizing some
of the drainage structures to account for the loss in capacity due to
long term settlement of the drainage structures due to the underlying
soft ground
It should be noted that the preliminary recommendations provided in
this report are intended for planning and preliminary design purposes
only The text of this report should be referred to for detailed
discussion of our findings and preliminary recommendations

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lava of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lava of the
post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial sediments
of the Palikea Formation
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental glaciers
accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill the oceanic
basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels rose because more
water was available The land mass of Kauai remained essentially stable during these
changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations
resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative to the present sea level
of Kauai
GEOLABS INC
W O 3869 00 Page 10
Most of the soils in the project area are composed of residual and saprolitic soils
derived from the in situ weathering of the Koloa Volcanic Series igneous rocks In general
residual and saprolitic soils grade to weathered basalt formations with increasing depth
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt
extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various locales Associated
with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at the vent
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of Kauai
including the majority of the proposed alignment
The Napali Formation of the older Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series is found near the
area known as the Knudsen Gap along the project alignment The rocks of the Napali
Formation are typically composed of olivine basalt Due to the age of these formations the
Napali Formations generally have upper mantles of residual and saprolitic soils grading to
weathered basalt formation with increasing depth
The highway alignment traverses severai streams and many smaller drainageways
and gullies Alluvial soil deposits of the Quaternary Period in age underlie some portions
of the project alignment The areas near Weoweopilau Huleia and Nawiliwili Streams
contain extensive deposits of alluvial sediments These sediments are generally
unconsolidated to moderately consolidated non calcareous soils deposited by erosion of the
upper Koloa Volcanic Series Smaller drainageways and gullies have also deposited alluvial
sediments in localized areas Agricultural and commercial developments within the last
century have brought the project site to its present conditions

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general fills and backfills up to 3 feet of the finished subgrade elevation should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to at least 90 percent relative
compaction Fills and backfills within 3 feet of the pavement subgrade elevation
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO T 180
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Due to the high moisture content of the in situ soils some moisture reduction may be
required to achieve the minimum 90 percent compaction criteria especially for
materials generated from the deep excavations Aeration to lower the soil moisture
and more compaction effort to achieve the specified compaction would generally
reduce the rate of fill placement for this project Compaction should be accomplished
by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed to compact the
on site clayey soils

that it would not have sufficient strength to support the weight of heavy construction
equipment Therefore a working platform or drainage blanket should be established
prior to filling of the area The working platform may consist of a combination of a
woven geotextile fabric and a gravel layer The woven geotextile fabric would reduce
the need for strict gradation requirements of the drainage blanket since it would act
as a filter medium to reduce the potential for gravel loss into the soft ground In
addition the woven geotextile would also prevent the mud and fine soil particles from
contaminating the granular blanket material It also provides tensile reinforcement to
the gravel layer and permits earlier use of heavy equipment on a minimum thickness
of gravel
The drainage blanket should consist of granular crushed rock material such as No 2
rock Size 4 Coarse Aggregate of Section 703 05 of HSS and should be at least
30 inches thick A layer of non woven filter fabric or permeable separator
Section 648 of HSS should be provided between the crushed rock material and the
upper fill material This drainage blanket should extend a minimum of 8 feet beyond
the toe of the fill slope as shown on Plate 7

W O 3869 00 Page 33
Due to the soft and yielding nature of the subsoils new fills should be placed in thin
level lifts at slow filling rates to reduce the potential for shear failure in the soft
materials The controlled rate of filling should be maintained to allow the consolidating
soils to gain sufficient strength to support the next lift The actual filling rate in the soft
areas should be established in the field based on settlement gauge readings and
adjusted to meet field conditions
The stability of the new fill slopes should be evaluated for potential local and global
failure In areas where slope stability becomes a concern due to extensive deposits
of soft soils and or excessive fill heights alternative methods of soft soil stabilization
may be required We envision that blanket stabilization may be applicable where the
thickness of the soft soil deposits is less than about 10 feet vertically and or the new
fill heights is less than about 15 feet vertically
Vibro Replacement Stone Columns
Vibro replacement methods may be preferred to mitigate soft soil problems when
relatively thick soft soil deposits are encountered and extend deep into the
subsurface Vibro replacement methods consist of inserting a vibrating probe into the
soft soils placing crushed rock in the void created by the probe and compacting the
crushed rock to form a stone column Stone columns typically have diameters of
about 2 5 to 3 feet and are placed at about 4 to 10 feet on center Closer spacings
are generally used for the more heavily loaded cases
The stone columns act to reinforce the soft soils and provide strength and stability to
the surrounding mass of soil In addition the stone columns will provide a means to
dissipate pore pressures generated when new fills are placed thus increasing the rate
of consolidation of the soft soil deposits
We envision that soil stabilization by vibro replacement would be more cost effective
at localized areas containing extensive soft soil deposits For preliminary cost
estimation purposes vibro replacement methods should be considered where the
GEOLABS iNC
W O 3869 00 Page 34
thickness of the soft soil deposits exceed about 10 feet vertically and or the new fill
heights exceed about 15 feet vertically
Jet Grouting
Jet grouting is a ground modification method in which thin high pressure jets of
cement grout are discharged sideways into the borehole wall to simultaneously
excavate and then mix with the soil The result is a column of modified soil with low
permeability and improved strength This technique is effective across the widest
range of soil types including granular soils such as sands and gravel and cohesive
soils such as silts and clays
Since jet grouting will provide greater strength to the improved soil than
vibro replacement jet grouting may be applicable in heavily loaded areas where new
structures are involved We envision that jet grouting may be more prudent than
vibro replacement in areas where new structures are planned with fill heights greater
than about 25 feet over soft soil deposits
It should be noted that the above soft ground stabilization methods may mitigate most
of the stability and settlement problems associated with soft soils However some areas
may not be economically and or practically feasible to stabilize the soft ground using only the
above methods In those areas a combination of soft ground stabilization methods and
settlement waiting period and or staged construction may need to be implemented Further
discussions on settlement waiting period and staged construction are contained in the
Ground Settlement and the Box Culvert subsections of the New Structures section of this
report

Implementation of an adequate erosion control program would be crucial to the

failure or raveling In general erosion of the existing slopes planned cut slopes or new fill
embankments are caused by surface water runoff General guidelines such as

soils establishment of vegetation at the slope face may be difficult and may require a long

Temporary Erosion Control
The flexibility weight web structure and porosity features of the flexible geosynthetic
matting makes it particularly effective in temporary erosion control It conforms easily
to the ground surface and remains firmly in place as a stable ground armor The mat
shields soil surface from the erosive forces of wind and rain and prevents soil seed
and fertilizer from washing away Finally it provides a rough surface to retain
sediment deposits during water runoff
Mulching
The high porosity web structure of the flexible geosynthetic matting acts as a
non deteriorating muich It holds soil seed and fertilizer in place and retains
sediment runoff as a medium for root growth It also retains moisture and heat
necessary for germination and allows uninhibited growth of grass and other
vegetation
Permanent Erosion Control
Once vegetation growth is established the flexible geosynthetic matting continues to
provide permanent erosion control as it becomes embedded in the new stem and root
system It protects new vegetation from washout and reinforces and anchors the
vegetation in place as a cohesive unit
Reduced Surface Water Runoff Velocities
Revegetated slopes and ditches reinforced with the matting reduce surface water
runoff flow velocities which is an important advantage that cannot be achieved with
GEOLABS iNC

concrete lining It reduces runoff flow volume and concentrations and reduces the
need for energy dissipation at the ditch outlet or slope base It also promotes water
percolation and groundwater recharge

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the project alignment were explored by drilling and
sampling 29 borings extending to depths of approximately 19 5 to 102 5 feet below the
existing ground surface The borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment
equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools as appropriate The approximate
locations of the borings drilled are shown on the General Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2 and
the Detailed Site Plans Plates 3 1 through 3 15
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer or geologist who monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches Some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample
depths Relatively undisturbed samples of the soft soil formation were obtained by pushing
a 3 inch OD thin walled stainless steel Shelby tube into the soil formation
Pocket penetrometer and torvane shear tests were performed on selected cohesive
soil samples retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample The torvane shear test provides a quick
estimate of the undrained shear strength of the soil sample The results of the pocket
penetrometer and torvane shear tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses
a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit
is mechanically recovered for examination and description

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would
be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical
Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD

Excellent 90 100
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD values
would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of
excavation

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Samples recovered from our preliminary field exploration program were visually
examined and classified in our laboratory in Honolulu In addition strength characteristics
of the cohesive soil samples recovered were further evaluated using pocket penetrometer
and Torvane shear apparatus Selected samples were tested to evaluate the physical
characteristics and engineering properties of the soils encountered The laboratory testing
program was formulated with the emphasis on evaluating the moisture density plasticity
gradation compressibility shear strength and compaction characteristics of the soils
encountered
The laboratory tests performed include determination of natural moisture content and
dry density Atterberg Limits sieve analysis hydrometer direct shear triaxial compression
consolidation Standard Proctor compaction and California Bearing Ratio CBR tests
Results of the tests performed are summarized in Tables B 1 through B 4 of Appendix B
Detailed descriptions of the various tests performed for this preliminary exploration are
presented below
Moisture and Density
Moisture content and dry density unit weight tests were performed on soil samples
to aid in defining the interface between various soil layers encountered and correlating the
layers between test borings The moisture content tests were performed in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 2216 and the unit weight tests were performed
in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 2937 The results of these tests are presented
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Atterberg Limits
Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the liquid
limit LL and plastic limit PL as an aid in classifying the soils The Atterberg Limits tests
were performed in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 4318 The test results are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic
presentations of the test results are provided on Plates B 1 thru B 3
Sieve Analysis and Hydrometer
To provide information on the particle size distribution of the soils encountered three
sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 and D 1140 and six hydrometer tests were performed to
evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification The hydrometer tests
were performed in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 422 Graphic presentations
of the test results are provided on Plates B 4 through B 7

Direct Shear
To evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the on site soils strained controlled
consolidated drained direct shear tests were performed on the in situ and remolded soil
samples in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3080 The test results are presented
on Plates B 8 through B 13
Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression TXUU
To evaluate the undrained shear strength of the clayey soils eight unconsolidated
undrained triaxial compression tests were performed on selected in situ and remolded
samples in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 2850 The approximate in situ
effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the relatively
undisturbed soil samples For the remolded samples the confining pressure was varied
to reflect the different fill heights up to about 55 feet thick The test results and the
stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 14 through B 21
Multi Stage Consolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression Test TXCU
To evaluate the long term shear strengths of the clayey soils when used as fill
embankment one consolidated undrained triaxial compression test was performed on an
in situ soil sample and one was performed on a remolded soil sample The two tests were
performed in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 4767 The test results and
Consolidation
Four consolidation tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the
compressibility characteristics of the on site soils The tests were performed in accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 2435 and the test results are plotted on Plates B 24 through
B 27
Standard Proctor Test
Ten laboratory compaction tests were performed on the bulk samples obtained to
evaluate the relationship between the moisture content and the dry density of the
near surface soils The Standard Proctor tests were performed in accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 698 The test results are presented on Plates B 28 through B 37
California Bearing Ratio CBR
Ten CBR tests were performed on the near surface soils to evaluate the strength of
the in situ cohesive materials for pavement support The tests were performed in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 The test results are presented on
Plates B 38 through B 47
