The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 AASHTO T 180
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
The Portland Cement Concrete should have a minimum flexural strength of
650 pounds per square inch psi when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
C 78 The aggregate base course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The asphalt concrete base material
should consist of asphalt treated basalt aggregates compacted to a density of not less
than 91 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity determined in accordance
with ASTM D 2041 or AASHTO T 209 The maximum compacted thickness of each layer
should not exceed 6 inches We recommend that CBR and field density tests be
performed on the actual pavement subgrade materials encountered during construction to
confirm the adequacy of the recommended pavement sections
Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the aggregate base course and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement
surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface
water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should
not be allowed on the site during or after construction Where landscaping is planned
adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that a subdrain system be constructed
to collect excessive water from landscaping irrigation and to reduce the potential for
migration of landscape water into the pavement section The recommended pavement
sections assume that good drainage will be provided for the paved areas
Pavement Joints
Due to the rigidity of the Portland cement concrete pavements significant stresses
may develop in the concrete from variations in temperature and moisture content In

order to relieve the high level of stress and to reduce the potential for cracking adequate
joints should be provided in the Portland cement concrete pavement alternative The
following describes the various types of joints and general guidelines that should be
implemented in the design of concrete pavements
Crack Control Joints Contraction Joints
In general decreases in temperature or moisture content such as that during
concrete curing will cause the concrete to contract and possibly crack Crack
control joints are used to provide controlled cracking due to volume changes and
relieve the stresses caused by curling or warping Crack control joints such as
saw cut joints or formed grooves are provided to a depth of generally
one fourth 7 the slab thickness These saw cut joints or formed grooves crack
control joints provide a weakened plane which will likely crack through the full
depth as the concrete shrinks during the curing process
In general the spacing of crack control joints should be limited to a maximum of
20 feet Smaller joint spacing should be used if the concrete pavement will be
subjected to extreme temperature gradients during the placement and or life span
of the pavement The crack control joints should be provided in both longitudinal
and transverse directions The transverse joint spacing should not vary from the
longitudinal joint spacing by more than 25 percent
Expansion Joints Isolation Joints
Expansion joints are typically used to relieve compressive stresses developed at
critical locations due to fluctuations in temperature Expansion joints are cut to the
full depth of the concrete slab and compressible filler materials are placed in the
cut These joints allow for isolated lateral movement of the slabs and reduce the
potential for damage to the adjoining concrete spalling of the edges of the
concrete slabs
Expansion joints should be used at pavement intersections and at intersections of
pavements with fixed structures Because expansion joints generally do not
provide for load transfer across the joint placement of dowels with lubrication on
one end of the dowel should be considered for load transfer across the expansion
joint where required
Construction Joints
Construction joints are generally required when two abutting slabs are placed at
different times Therefore construction joints usually form the edges of each day s
work For the relatively thick concrete slab 16 inches planned for this project the
construction joints should consist of a keyed or butted joint In general
construction joints should be aligned with the crack control or expansion joints to
reduce the number of pavement joints required

In accordance with our fee proposal dated March 30 1998 we recently completed
the field exploration work and laboratory testing pertaining to the Pier 1C Extension
project at Kahului Harbor on the island of Maui Hawaii This letter summarizes our work
performed thus far on the project and presents our preliminary geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our analyses for the subject project
Project Considerations
The proposed project includes the construction of an approximately 300 foot
extension on the western side of the existing Pier 1C at Kahului Harbor on the island of
Maui Hawaii The extension will include a pile supported concrete pier approximately
48 feet wide and a sheet pile bulkhead supporting asphalt concrete pavement for the
container storage yard The new pier system will be designed to sustain a uniform live
load of Caterpillar V925 loading impact from docking vessels as well as
loads from mooring wind and surge forces from berthed vessels New utility lines
including water lighting molasses and fuel are also planned to serve the new pier
extension

Unbalanced Hydraulic Pressures
For waterfront structures such as sheet pile supported piers the maximum
lateral pressures may be expected when the tide is at its lowest level A
receding tide may cause higher water levels behind the sheet pile than in front of
it depending on the type of soils behind the sheet piles The high water level
behind the sheet pile wall will introduce additional seepage pressures due to
hydrostatic load For soils encountered at the project site a hydrostatic pressure
estimated based on the maximum tide difference may be used for the sheet pile
design
Tie Rods and Deadman Anchors
Tie rods and deadman anchors will be required to provide the necessary lateral
restraint for the sheet pile bulkhead We understand that the tie rods and
deadman anchors are currently planned near the sea level elevation To avoid
the need for deep excavations and dewatering it is advisable to place the
tie rods and anchors above the groundwater level or at about elevation
0 feet MLW We expect that the deadman anchors will be located in the fill layer
Based on the soil conditions compacted granular backfill material we
recommend the following design parameters be used for the deadman anchors
The ultimate resistance of the deadman anchors may be estimated based on a
coefficient of friction of 0 4 between the base and top of the deadman anchors
and the surrounding soil In addition passive earth pressure equivalent to a fluid
weighing 350 pcf and an active pressure equivalent to a fluid pressure of 35 pcf
may be used to evaluate the deadman anchors In order for the anchorage
system to develop full capacity the anchors must be located a sufficient distance
behind the bulkhead wall such that the resisting anchor passive wedge does not
intersect the active soil wedge behind the bulkhead An effective friction angle of
35 degrees may be used to estimate the active and passive zones for the design
of deadman anchors
Construction Considerations
Our field exploration indicated that varying amounts of boulders may be present
along the sheet pile bulkhead alignments The potential for boulders may pose
some difficulties to the Contractor during sheet pile installation Therefore the
Contractor should be made aware of this site condition To facilitate installation
of the sheet pile it may be necessary to first excavate a narrow trench through
the surface fill and slightly into the soft harbor sediment layer The sheet pile
may then be installed to the desired depth The voids between the sheet pile
and soil on the land side should be filled using lean concrete or compacted soil
The Contractor should exercise care when backfilling behind the sheet pile wall
using compacted fill because stresses from the compaction effort may push the

be allowed for the deadman anchor construction The sides of the excavations
