INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of the soil and foundation investigation
conducted for the proposed air cargo building for the proposed Kahului
Airport Terminal Complex Expansion project State
Project No M 1055 The general location of the site is shown on Plate l
Project Location Map

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our investigation was to evaluate the subsurface conditions at
the site to provide soil data for the design of the foundations for the
proposed building
The scope of the investigation included a field reconnaissance the drilling
of 4 exploratory borings laboratory testing engineering analysis and report
preparation
The work was performed in accordance with our proposal dated March 15 1982
except that the boring depths were shortened due to the very competent rock
formation encountered at shallow depths
The study was based on preliminary building plans by John M Okita
Associates Inc dated and tentative finished grades by Park
Engineering Inc received on
Based on the boring data and laboratory test results engineering analysis
was performed to evaluate the settlement and strength characteristics of the
subsoils due to site filling and building loading Analysis was also per
formed to develop recommendations for the design of foundation retaining
wall slab on grade pavement and site grading

W O Page 3
PROPOSED CONSTRUCTION
The proposed building will consist of a one story steel framed warehouse
structure with metal siding approximately 65 by 216 feet in plan dimension
Maximum column loads of 22 5 kips are anticipated
Concrete slab on grade will be utilized for the cargo building where forklift
traffic is anticipated
A 3 to 4 feet high loading dock area is planned along the northwestern one
fourth of the building
Fills from 1 0 southern portion to 3 5 feet northern portion are
required to raise the building site to the proposed finished grade A
retaining wall about 3 5 feet high is planned along the northern portion
of the building to retain the new fill
Refrigeration and freezer units are also planned in the warehouse structure
Asphaltic concrete pavement is planned for the driveway area
SITE DESCRIPTION
The site is located on the southern side and near the eastern end of Keolani
Place in the existing Kahului Airport area on the Island of Maui Hawaii
The site is bounded to the north by Keolani Place to the west by the airport
employee s parking lot and to the south and east by the general aviation
apron
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 4
The area slopes gently to the north and is covered by moderately deteriorated
asphaltic concrete pavement About half the site is situated on the existing
general aviation apron and the remainder is in the existing airport
employee s parking lot
The ground surface elevation varies from about 33 to 37 feet above sea level
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The project site is located on the northwest flank of Haleakala Volcano
and is underlain by lava flows of the Kula Volcanic Series Stearns and
Macdonald 1942 The lava flows of this series which consist primarily of
basaltic andesites andesitic basalts and picrite basalt are generally thick
bedded

13 0 feet below the existing ground surface

Soil Conditions
In general after penetrating the surfical pavement section the borings
encountered very stiff moist reddish brown silt and gravel This material
appears to have been derived from the in situ weathering of volcanic
clinker Laboratory tests indicate that this material has low expansion
potential
Below this material at depths between 1 7 and 3 4 feet the borings encoun
tered very hard dense gray basaltic rock This formation is generally mas
sive to slightly jointed and extended to 13 0 feet the maximum depth drilled
No groundwater was encountered in the borings at the time of our field
exploration
DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of our field explorations and our engineering analysis
the site is suitable for the proposed structure
Spread or continuous footing foundations bearing on the underlying rock
formation are recommended for the air cargo building
Foundations
The field exploration disclosed that the project site is underlain by stiff
residual brown silty gravel over a competent basaltic rock formation encoun
tered at shallow depths of about 1 5 feet southern portion to 3 5 feet
northern portion
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 6
The footings could bear on either the stiff residual soil or rock however
in order to minimize differential settlement of footings due to variable
thicknesses of residual soil over rock the spread or continuous footings
should extend through the upper soils to bear on the rock formation at about
1 5 to 3 5 foot depth
For the proposed light 1 story structure an allowable bearing value of 8 000
p s f is recommended for footings bearing on the rock formation This bear
ing capacity value is for total dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third when temporary wind and seismic loads are considered
Surface water should be diverted away from the building foundations
We believe minimal settlements will occur for the above designed foundations
bearing on the rock formation
Loading Dock Wall Retaining Wall
A short retaining wall generally less than 5 feet high is planned along the
northern side of the building to provide the necessary grade separation A
portion of this wall will be used as a loading dock
An allowable bearing value of 8000 p s f is recommended for the wall
footings bearing on the dense rock formation
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 7
Well graded granular material such as base course rock sand or equivalent
should be used as backfill material directly behind the walls This would
minimize the compactive effort required for the backfill and reduce the
build up of additional lateral pressures on the walls
We recommend that the walls should be designed to resist lateral earth
pressures as follows
Unrestrained at top equivalent
active condition fluid pressure
Restrained at top equivalent
at rest condition fluid pressure
Additional loads due to surcharge or traffic
should be included where applicable
A friction factor of 0 5 between concrete and rock can be utilized to deter
mine the sliding resistance of the wall
Additionally a passive soil resistance of 300 p c f can be used
A subsurface drainage system should be used to prevent the build up of hydro
static pressure behind the walls
Site Grading
The existing ground surface slopes down slightly toward the north There
fore about 1 foot southern portion to 3 5 feet northern portion of fill
is required to reach the proposed finished floor elevation of the building
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 8
It is our understanding that no cut is planned for this development there
fore imported fill will be required
Imported fill should be non expansive material less than 3 inches in size
and capable of producing a minimum laboratory CBR value of 8 Fill and
backfill should be placed in 9 inch maximum loose lifts and compacted to a
minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density as established by ASTM D 1557
Prior to placement of fill the existing asphalt pavement within 2 feet of
finished grade should be removed The broken up asphalt concrete less than 3
inches in size can be reused in the fill The existing base course material
should be moistened proofrolled and recompacted to a minimum of 90 of its
maximum dry density
Slab On Grade
Concrete slab on grade bearing on compacted fill is planned for the
structure
It is anticipated that the floor slabs will support moderate amount of
pneumatic tired forklift traffic Discussion with the interisland airlines
indicated that generally 4 kip capacity forklifts would be used
The thickness of the concrete slab required will depend on the subgrade
modulus or CBR value of the fill material used below the slab We recommend
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 9
that the fill material should be capable of providing a minimum CBR value of
8 The corresponding thickness of the concrete slab for this material is 5 5
inches The slab thickness can be reduced to 5 inches if granular material
capable of providing a CBR value of 25 or more is used for the fill below the
slab In both cases the edges of the slab should be thickened to 8 inches for
load transfer capability
The concrete slabs should be placed over a 6 inch thick layer of base course
compacted to a minimum of 95 of its maximum dry density The base material
should meet the requirements set forth in Section 31 Aggregate Base Course
in the Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction 1976 Hawaii
Highway Divsion Department of Transportation
The fills beneath the slabs should be compacted in 9 inch maximum loose lifts
to at least 95 of the maximum density determined in accordance with the ASTM
D 1557 test procedure
Cold Storage Unit
It is our understanding that cold storage refrigeration and freezer units are
planned for the building The freezing temperatures developed by the cold
storage unit could initiate frost action in the subsoils
Frost action generally occurs when there is a combination of freezing
temperature high water level or available water source and subgrade soils
which have high capillary rise potential When the surface of the soil is
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 10
below freezing temperature upward moisture migration from the high perched
water level or condensation can occur due to capillary action The water
will then collect and freeze to form lenses of ice in the upper part of the
ground which will displace the soil and cause heaving The formation of the
ice lenses can continue indefinitely as long as suitable conditions exist
The on site soils consist of silts and gravels which generally have some
potential for frost action In addition the dense rock formation could act
as a vertical water barrier which could provide a source of perched water
and therefore the subgrade soils may be susceptible to freezing and subse
quent heaving below the cold storage unit
In order to reduce the potential of frost action below the new cold storage
unit a capillary break layer is recommended below the unit to prevent upward
migration of water and to act as a drain for accumulated condensation from
the freezer unit
A clean gravel such as 3 Fine gravel is recommended for use as the capil
lary break material The thickness of the capillary break layer would depend
on the effectiveness of the insulation and depth of frost penetration Gen
erally a minimum thickness of 2 feet of 3 Fine is required
Pavement
Asphaltic concrete pavements are planned around the building
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O Page 11
The proposed pavement finished grades are generally less than 12 inches above
the existing pavement surface therefore the new pavement section will be
affected by the existing thin base course layer and natural soils The
existing asphaltic concrete could act as a drainage barrier and cause
softening of the base course therefore we recommend that the existing
pavement be removed Granular material such as sand select borrow or broken
up asphaltic concrete less than 3 inches in size should be used to fill up to
the pavement section
Assuming moderate truck traffic and a well drained subgrade the recommended
pavement section is as follows
2 5 inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 inch Base Course
8 5 inch Total Pavement Thickness
Additional CBR tests and field observations should be performed on the actual
subgrade during construction to verify the design section

CONSTRUCTION MONITORING
It is recommended that the soils engineer be present during earthwork con
struction to monitor the contractor s grading operations
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and approved
by the soils engineer prior to hauling to the site
It is also recommended that the footing excavations be observed by a soils
engineer during construction to see that adequate soil bearing strata have
been obtained and that the subsurface conditions are consistent with the
findings of our subsurface investigation
The recommendations given in this report are contingent upon the above
inspections

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by
drilling 0 to 13 0 feet below the existing ground
surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 Truck mounted drilling equipment was used to drill
the borings
The soils rock encountered in the borings were classi
fied by visual and textural examination in the field by our
geologist who continously monitored the drilling operations
The classifications were further reviewed by visual inspection
and testing in the laboratory The soils were classified in
general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System A graphic presentation of the materials encountered is
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Soil samples were obtained from the boring by driving
either a standard penetration sampler or a 2 4 inch I D split
barrel sampler with a 140 pound hammer free falling at a height
of 30 inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the last 12
inches are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Rock core samples were obtained by using a rotary wash
NWD4 series core barrel The core recovery and rock quality
designation RQD are presented on the logs of borings
