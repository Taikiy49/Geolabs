SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The subsurface conditions across the project site were determined from a review 
of geologic maps, available subsurface information from past projects in the vicinity, and 
our field exploration program consisting of seven DCP tests performed extending to 
depths ranging from about 0.5 to 5 feet below the exposed pavement subgrade surface. 
Based on our field exploration performed, the existing pavement sections generally 
consisted of about 3 to 5 inches of asphaltic concrete on top of 3 to 8 inches of aggregate 
base course materials. The pavement sections were generally underlain by alluvium 
consisting of medium dense to dense silty/clayey gravel and silty/clayey sand with some 
cobbles extended to the maximum depth explored of about 5 feet below the pavement 
subgrade surface.  
Based on the results of our field exploration, the pavement subgrades along the 
proposed pavement reconstruction alignment are generally underlain by silty and clayey 
soils. A design CBR value of 5.6 was adopted in our pavement analyses for the 
recommended pavement reconstruction based on the laboratory CBR test results. 
Based on the traffic design parameters provided and the design subgrade CBRs 
adopted for the pavement subgrade conditions, we recommend using the following new 
pavement structural sections for preliminary design purposes: 
Imi Kala Street and West End of Eha Street 
  3.0 Inches Asphaltic Concrete 
  4.0 Inches Asphalt Concrete Base (92 Percent Relative Specific Gravity) 
  6.0 Inches Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction)

Wili Pa Loop 
  2.0 Inches Asphaltic Concrete 
  3.0 Inches Asphalt Concrete Base (92 Percent Relative Specific Gravity) 
  6.0 Inches Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
11.0 Inches Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade

geotechnical recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Millyard Subdivision Pavement Reconstruction project in Wailuku on 
the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of 
pavement reconstruction only. The findings and recommendations presented herein are 
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located within the Millyard Subdivision in Wailuku on the Island of 
Maui, Hawaii. We understand that pavement reconstruction is planned for Imi Kala Street, 
Wili Pa Loop, and the western end of Eha Street. We understand that Imi Kala Street is 
classified as a “major collector road”, and Wili Pa Loop and Eha Street are classified as 
“minor collector roads” in accordance with the County of Maui’s Street Design Manual, dated 
December 2018.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui (Tertiary 
Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene Epoch).  
The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located between these two 
volcanoes.  The project site is situated on the isthmus connecting the two volcanic domes. 
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the underlying basalts 
of the two volcanoes.  During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fell to stands lower than 
present sea level exposing large areas of coralline sand on what was the ocean bottom. 
Wind action transported these coralline sands inland forming extensive areas of sand 
dunes.  In some areas, these dunes have been mantled by man-made fill which was 
apparently placed during the period that the area was used for the cultivation of sugar 
cane. 
2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is located within Millyard Subdivision in Wailuku on the Island of 
Maui, Hawaii. The proposed pavement reconstruction is planned for Imi Kala Street, Wili 
Pa Loop, and the western end of Eha Street. In general, the site is occupied with 
commercial buildings. Moderate to high severity alligator cracks were observed on 
majority of the pavements within the project limits. Alligator cracks are pavement 
structural distresses likely caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or 
subgrade materials. 
Based on the topographic information provided, the project site generally slopes 
down from southwest to northeast with the existing ground surface elevations ranging 
from about +220 to +172 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL), respectively.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The subsurface conditions across the project site were determined from a review

3.1 
Methodology of Pavement Design 
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement areas 
would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing pavements should 
be performed. Existing pavements with minor distresses are generally rehabilitated with 
an asphaltic concrete overlay with cold-planing. Existing pavements with extensive 
structural distresses, such as severe alligator cracking, would require total reconstruction 
with a new pavement section. The procedures used in determining the new pavement 
section were generally based on the flexible pavement design procedures presented in 
the “Guide for Design of Pavement Structures” (1993) developed by the American 
Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials (AASHTO).

3.4 
Design Pavement Sections 
As discussed above, majority of the observed distresses along the project 
roadways are likely caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade 
materials. In general, these areas should be reconstructed with a new pavement section 
in lieu of an AC overlay section.

Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicate that the roadway alignment 
under consideration for this project is generally underlain by silty/clayey soils.  We 
understand that placement of asphalt concrete base (ACB) directly on the silty/clayey 
subgrade is not recommended.  Based on our evaluation and these construction 
considerations, we recommend providing a layer of aggregate base course between the 
upper ACB layer and the underlying subgrade soils.  The aggregate base course will 
serve to provide a compacted granular surface for the placement of the ACB. 
Based on the assumptions and the construction considerations described above, 
we have determined the following flexible pavement design sections for the proposed 
pavement reconstruction project.  Detailed calculations and analyses of the pavement 
design are presented on Plates C-3.1 and 3.2 in Appendix C. 
Imi Kala Street and West End of Eha Street 
  3.0 Inches Asphaltic Concrete 
  4.0 Inches Asphalt Concrete Base (92 Percent Relative Specific Gravity) 
  6.0 Inches Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
13.0 Inches Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade 
Wili Pa Loop 
  2.0 Inches Asphaltic Concrete 
  3.0 Inches Asphalt Concrete Base (92 Percent Relative Specific Gravity) 
  6.0 Inches Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
11.0 Inches Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade 
The exposed pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 
8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and compacted 
to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same 
soil established in accordance with AASHTO T-180 or ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture 
is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry 
density.

3.5 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be maintained 
to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. Surface water 
ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development of good 
shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage structures 
is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or premature 
failure of the pavements.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from our field DCP tests and bulk samples. Variations of subsurface 
conditions between and beyond the field data points may occur, and the nature and extent 
of these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations 
then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations presented 
herein. 
The field DCP test and bulk sample locations indicated herein are approximate, 
having been determined using a hand-held Global Positioning System (GPS) device at 
the project site. The physical locations of the DCP tests and bulk samples should be 
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the method used. 
This report has been prepared for the exclusive use of Sato & Associates, Inc. and 
their project consultants for specific application to the Millyard Subdivision Pavement 
Reconstruction project in Wailuku on the Island of Maui as described herein in

No warranty is expressed or implied. 
This report has been prepared solely for the purpose of assisting the design 
engineers in the design of the proposed project. Therefore, this report may not contain 
sufficient data, or the proper information to serve as a basis for detailed construction cost 
estimates.  
The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated soil conditions are commonly 
encountered. Unforeseen subsurface conditions such as perched groundwater, soft

probing or corrections in the field (which may result in construction delays) to attain a 
properly constructed project. Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund is recommended to 
accommodate these possible extra costs.

Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests 
Our field exploration program consisted of performing Dynamic Cone Penetration 
(DCP) tests at seven locations, designated as DCP-1 through DCP-7, extending to depths 
of about 0.5 to 5 feet below the exposed pavement subgrade surface. The approximate 
locations where DCP tests were performed are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
At each DCP test location, the existing asphaltic concrete pavement was saw cut to 
approximately 18 inches by 24 inches in plan dimensions prior to excavation.  A Case 580 
Super N backhoe loader was used to excavate the existing asphaltic concrete pavement 
and aggregate base course material to expose the pavement subgrade for DCP tests. 
The procedures used in our DCP tests are in general accordance with ASTM D6951, 
Standard Test Method for Use of the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer in Shallow Pavement 
Applications. The DCP apparatus consists of a 5/8-inch diameter steel rod with a 60-degree 
conical tip. The rod is topped with an anvil that is connected to a second steel rod. This rod 
is used as a guide to allow a 17.6-lb hammer to be repeatedly raised and dropped from a 
height of 22.6 inches. The connection between the two rods consists of an anvil to allow for 
quick connections between the rods and for efficient energy transfer from the falling weight 
to the penetrating rod. 
Once the test apparatus is assembled, the DCP equipment is placed at the test 
location and the initial penetration of the rod is recorded to provide a zeroing scale. While 
holding the rod vertically, the weight is raised to the top of the rod (22.6 inches above the 
anvil) and dropped. The penetration of the rod is measured after each drop. 
In the DCP tests conducted by Geolabs, the same equipment described above was 
used to record the depth of penetration per hammer drop. Field logs of the Dynamic Cone 
Penetration tests are presented on Plates A-1.0 through A-7.1.
