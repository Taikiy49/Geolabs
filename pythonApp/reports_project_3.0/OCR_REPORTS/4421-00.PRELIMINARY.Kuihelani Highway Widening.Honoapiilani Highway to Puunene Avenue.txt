INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Kuihelani Highway Widening project located along Highway Route 380 in the
Wailuku District on the Island of Maui Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for site grading design of foundations
retaining structures and drainage structures only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes the older West Maui Tertiary
Epoch and the more recent East Maui also known as Haleakala Pleistocene Epoch
Between these two volcanoes a narrow gently sloping plain called the Isthmus of Maui is
located The Isthmus of Maui was formed when lavas of Haleakala banked against the older
West Maui volcano
W O 4421 00 PRELIMINARY REPORT Page 6
Stream erosion of the West Maui volcano produced broad alluvial fans on the eastern
and southwestern sides of the mountain The large accumulation of alluvial material is partly
due to the loss of water from the streams to the permeable lavas of Haleakala that built the
Maui Isthmus These alluvial fans may be more than 900 feet in thickness
During the Pleistocene Epoch the sea stood about 350 feet lower than the present sea
level During this period sand was blown inland and extensive lithified calcareous dunes were
formed on the Isthmus over the alluvial fans Near the northern coast some of the dunes are
as much as 180 feet high
The project site is located near the central portion of the island within the Isthmus of
Maui

The highway widening project site is located along Kuihelani Highway Route 380 of the
District of Wailuku on the Island of Maui Hawaii Based on the information provided it is

Based on our field observations the existing highway is currently paved with asphalt

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling fourteen borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 14 along the proposed highway widening alignment
The borings were extended to depths ranging from about 5 5 to 76 5 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the General Site Plan
Plate 2 and the Site Pians Plates 3 1 to 3 5
In general the borings encountered an older alluvial deposit consisting of stiff to
hard clayey silts and silts with sand gravel and cobbles and dense silty sand with gravel
extending to the maximum depth explored of approximately 76 5 feet below the existing
ground surface In the central and northern areas of the alignment boring nos 8 through
14 generally encountered consolidated dune deposits overlying the alluvial deposit The
consolidated dunes deposit generally consisted of medium dense fine calcareous sand
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field exploration
Considering that the alignment crosses several streams and drainage structures the
groundwater levels will likely vary in response to the water level in the streams and
drainageways Water levels at the project site may also be influenced by seasonal
precipitation and other factors
Detailed descriptions of the field exploration methodology are presented in Appendix A
of this report Descriptions and graphic representations of the materials encountered in the
drilled borings are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 14 Laboratory tests
were performed on selected soil samples and the results are presented in Appendix B

The project site is generally underlain by stiff to hard older alluvial deposits and
medium dense consolidated dune deposits Groundwater was not encountered in the
borings during our field exploration

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly
cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials
should be removed and disposed of properly off site or stockpiled in a designated area
to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft soils may be used
as fill provided that it meets the requirements for fill material
Prior to filling the existing ground should be scarified to a depth of 10 inches and
moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction For pavement subgrades the
minimum relative compaction should be 95 percent Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Fills and Backfills
The fill material should consist of low expansive materials less than 3 inches in
largest dimension with a CBR swell less than 1 percent The fill material should
also be free of vegetation and deleterious materials The fill material below 5 feet
from finished subgrade may consist of larger sized material 12 inches in largest
dimension provided that the material is well graded In general the on site clayey
silt and sandy soils should be suitable for fill material
W O 4421 00 PRELIMINARY REPORT Page 10
Base course and select borrow subbase material should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate and should meet the requirements of Section 703 06 and 703 17
respectively of the State of Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and
Public Works Construction 1994
Select granular fill and imported fill should consist of non expansive granular material
such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well
graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension
and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve
The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883 Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs Inc for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
Base course and select borrow subbase materials should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage
by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
W O 4421 00 PRELIMINARY REPORT Page 11
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils are critical
elements of earthwork observations and soil density tests should be performed by
Geolabs Inc during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required
degree of compaction and the proper moisture content Where compaction is less than
required additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture
content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction It should be noted that the
moisture requirement of the fills and subgrades about 2 percent above the optimum
moisture is an important requirement for the use of the on site clayey soils

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings it appears that most
of the cut slopes would expose soils consisting of medium dense fine sand and stiff
to hard clayey silt To reduce the potential for long term raveling of the fine sand
slopes we believe that cut slopes into the soil material may be cut at an inclination
of three horizontal to one vertical 3H 1V or flatter
Fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 3H 1V or flatter Slope
benches are recommended at maximum 20 foot vertical height intervals Fills
placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing
slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling operations should
W O 4421 00 PRELIMINARY REPORT Page 12
start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in
accordance with the above fill placement recommendations
Fill slopes in excess of 5 feet in height should be constructed by overfilling and cutting
back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well compacted slope face Track rolling of
slopes in excess of 5 feet in height should not be accepted In the event that
over cutting of a slope occurs keying and benching requirements should be
implemented instead of backfilling the slope to the design grade with sliver fills Water
should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided
as soon as possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes

Seismic Design Considerations
Based on the design criteria provided by the State of Hawaii Department of
Transportation the new stream bridges will need to be designed based on a horizontal
seismic acceleration coefficient of 0 26g Based on the subsurface materials
encountered in the borings drilled the project site may be classified from a seismic
analysis standpoint as a stiff soil site corresponding to a Soil Profile Type II A seismic
site coefficient S of 1 2 may be used to include the effects of site conditions on the
elastic seismic response spectra for the proposed bridge structures
Stream Channel Material for Scour Analysis
One of the most common causes of bridge failure stems from scouring of bridge
foundations from flood or other water flow damage Therefore the foundation
design of the bridge abutments will need to take into consideration the potential for
stream channel scour
W O 4421 00 PRELIMINARY REPORT Page 15
Scour is the result of erosive action of flowing water excavating and carrying
material away from the bed and banks of streams channels Total scour over a
period of time generally consists of three components 1 Aggregation and
Degradation 2 Contraction Scour and 3 Local Scour The rates of scour depend
on a number of factors such as the shape and dimensions of a pier or abutment
depth of flow velocity of approach flow size and gradation of stream channel bed
material and bed configuration
One of the factors affecting the scour depth is the grain size characteristics of the
stream bed material The median diameter of the stream bed material D50 in
conjunction with the depth of flow and flow velocity is used to calculate fall velocity
of streambed materials in scour depth analysis In order to evaluate the size and
gradation of the streambed material for scour depth analysis at the bridge abutment
locations soil samples from bulk samples of the stream bed materials were
retrieved for our laboratory tests
Streambed materials were obtained just upstream of the proposed abutment sites
for the bridge structures at Paleaahu and Waikapu Streams The stream bed
materials generally consist of sandy gravel and cobbles Because of the amount
and size of the cobbles in the streambed materials the percentage of cobbles were
estimated about 50 percent by weight and only the stream bed materials smaller
than the cobbles were obtained from the sites for laboratory testing Sieve analyses
and hydrometer testing were performed on the recovered samples to provide grain
size distribution of the materials Based on the laboratory test results the median
diameter of the stream bed material D50 at the planned bridge structures was
determined and presented in the following table In addition the gradation curves
were adjusted to take into account the estimated amount of cobbies in the

Settlement of the drilled shaft foundations will result primarily from elastic
compression of the drilled shaft and the subgrade response We estimate that the

Construction Considerations
The performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures As a result of these potential
variations we recommend that a representative from Geolabs be present to observe
the installation of drilled shafts during construction In our opinion the following may
have a significant impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft
foundations
Based on our field exploration the proposed Waikapu Stream abutment locations
are underlain by layers of silty sands and gravel extending to a depth of
approximately 44 feet below the existing ground surface Due to the granular nature
of these materials there is a potential for caving in of the materials during the drilling
operations To reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes temporary
casing of the drilled holes may be required during drilled shaft installation Care
should be exercised during removal of the temporary casing to reduce the potential
for necking of the drilled shaft
Some dense cobbles and gravels were encountered in the borings drilled at the
proposed bridge foundation locations during our field exploration Therefore the drilled
shaft contractor will need to have the appropriate equipment and tools to drill through
these obstructions where encountered
Workmanship
The load carrying capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
W O 4421 00 PRELIMINARY REPORT Page 21
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes
Since high capacities are recommended for these drilled shafts and because proper
drilled shaft installation is critical in obtaining the required capacities recommended
for the shafts a representative from Geolabs should be present to observe the
installation of drilled shafts during construction
Trial Shaft Program
A trial shaft program is normally required and highly recommended for bridge
projects Considering the diameter and structural load capacities of the drilled
shafts we recommend that a trial shaft program including the performance of an
instrumented load test at each of the bridge sites Paleaahu and Waikapu Stream
Bridges be undertaken to fulfill the following objectives
e To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed by the
contractor to install the drilled shafts into the stiff to hard alluvial deposits
e To confirm or modify the estimated tip elevations of the drilled shafts
To assess the contractor s method of placing and extracting the temporary
casing if required for the drilled shaft
To assess the contractor s method of concrete placement
To achieve these objectives we recommend that the trial shaft program consist of
drilling a 5 foot diameter trial shaft extending to a depth of about 55 feet below the
ground surface at each bridge location The location of the trial shaft should be
near but outside of the abutment foundations After drilling of the trial shaft the
trial shaft should be structurally reinforced and instrumented with embedment strain
gauges for load testing purposes The embedment strain gauges should be placed
starting from the bottom at an elevation of about 5 feet above the tip of the trial shaft
and subsequently at 10 foot intervals as shown on the Drilled Shaft Load Test
Detail Plate 4

Due to the high capacities recommended for the drilled shafts a conventional load
test wouid not be practical and would be costly to conduct Therefore after
installation of the trial shaft a bi directional axial load test should be conducted on
the reinforced trial shaft using an expandable base load cell Osterberg Load Cell
The expandable base load cell will need to be attached to the bottom of the trial
shaft reinforcing cage prior to lowering the cage in piace The drilled shaft load test
should be performed in general accordance with the Quick Load Test Method of
ASTM Test Designation D 1143 The trial shaft should be loaded to failure to
evaluate the ultimate side shear resistance of the trial shaft Installation of the
expandable base load cell and embedment strain gauges performance of the
bi directional axial load test and analyses of the load test data should be performed
by a qualified professional experienced in these types of load testing procedures
Considering the specialized nature of the trial shaft program we recommend that

developed from our analysis using the field exploration data Therefore observation
of the drilled shaft installation operations by Geolabs is a vital part of the foundation
design to confirm the design assumptions
Structure Approach Slabs
As previously indicated cuts of up to about 17 feet deep and fills of up to about 10 feet
thick will be required at the abutment areas of the new bridge structures at Paleaahu and
Waikapu Streams To reduce the potential for abrupt differential settlements between the
drilled shaft supported bridge structure and the in situ soils and or compacted backfills we
recommend that structure approach slabs be provided at the abutment locations
W O 4421 00 PRELIMINARY REPORT Page 23
The structure approach slabs should be supported on a minimum of 8 inches of
aggregate subbase course piaced on a prepared subgrade The subgrade should be scarified
to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction The
aggregate subbase course should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T 180 or ASTM D 1557 91 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density

minimum resistivity were performed on selected soil samples obtained during our field

APPENDIX B

PRELIMINARY REPORT
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Four Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil sample
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentation of the test results is
provided on Plate B 1
Five sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 including four hydrometer tests ASTM D 422
were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils
and to aid in soil classification Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plates
B 2 through B 4
Two direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected soil sample to
evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results are presented on Plates B 5 and
B 6
Four unconsolidated undrained triaxial compression tests ASTM D 2850 were
performed on selected in situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strengths of the
soils The approximate in situ effective overburden pressures were used as the applied
confining pressures for the relatively undisturbed soil samples The test results and the
stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 7 through B 10
One Standard Modified Proctor test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk sample
of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content relationships The
results of the test are presented on Plate B 11
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The samples were remolded to near the optimum moisture content of the soils
Compaction of the samples was performed by dropping a 10 pound hammer from a height of
18 inches The samples were compacted in five equal layers with each layer receiving
56 blows of energy The samples were soaked in water prior to the penetration test The
results of the tests are presented on Plates B 12 through B 74
Two sets of and minimum resistivity ASTM G57 were performed on
selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration The results of the tests are
presented on Plate B 15
