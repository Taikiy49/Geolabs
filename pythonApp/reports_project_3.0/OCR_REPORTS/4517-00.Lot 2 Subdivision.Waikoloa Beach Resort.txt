INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Lot 2 Subdivision project within the Waikoloa Beach Resort The Waikoloa
Beach Resort is located in the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations based on
our review and analyses of the site conditions the grading plan and the subsurface
information obtained for the project These recommendations are intended for the design
of site grading building foundations retaining structures and roadway pavements for the
project only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the site
appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe flows which spread and ponded as they
approached the ocean Aja lava is typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular
GEOLABS INC
W O 4517 00 Page 5
flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and
boulders Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an
internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basalt lavas especially pahoehoe lava These
cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may
_ extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and pockets are generally
smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted through
the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as
the major water transmission features

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration a large portion of the project site is underlain by
crunched pahoehoe lavas and granular fills extending to depths of about 1 5 to 5 feet below
the existing ground surface The crunched lavas and granular fills are underlain by medium
hard basalt formation The western portion of the project site near the shoreline is underlain
by coralline sands and gravel which extend to depths of about 4 to 8 5 feet below the
existing ground surface The coralline sands and gravel are underlain by medium hard basalt
formation The northern portion of the project site Lots 10 and 11 and the areas near the
existing ponds consists of pahoehoe lavas with numerous cavities collapsed lava tube
features and loosely scattered cobbles and boulders at the ground surface Groundwater
was encountered at depths of about 2 5 to 6 8 feet below the ground surface at the time
of our field exploration
Due to the varying surface conditions at the project site we recommend that special
attention be given to the site preparation recommendations prior to commencement of fill
placement at the site We anticipate that the relative density of the crunched lavas and the
granular fills will likely vary across the site Therefore we recommend that the subgrades
of fill areas be ripped to a depth of at least 3 feet below the existing ground surface to
disclose potential soft and or loose spots within the crunched lavas and the granular fills

surface the ground should be proof rolled with a Caterpillar D 10 bulldozer or similar size

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to
at least 95 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills should be placed
in level lifts not exceeding 18 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other
types of acceptable compaction equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
majority of the rocks or 3 feet whichever is less Placement should be by haul trucks
traversing previously placed lifts and placing at the edge of the currently placed lift
Spreading of the rock fill should be done with a large bulldozer to facilitate seating
of the rocks The rock fill should be watered heavily during placement by water trucks
traversing the front of the current rock lift face and spraying water continuously during
placement Compaction equipment such as a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar
heavy compaction equipment providing suitable energy to achieve the required
compaction should be utilized The number of passes usually six to eight passes
to be made to achieve proper compaction should be determined at the on set of site
grading by the performance of a trial compaction test or by plate bearing tests
Subsequent layers of rock fill above the previously placed rock fill should provide
a choking layer of smaller sized boulder materials to fill in the voids between the
boulders of the preceding rock fill layer
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rocks boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number
of passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should
GEOLABS INC
W O 4517 00 Page 15
be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation
of a representative from Geolabs Based on this testing program the number of
passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction For
preliminary estimating purposes compaction may be accomplished by using a large
vibratory drum roller having a static weight of at least 15 tons traveling at a speed
of about 300 feet per minute The roller should pass over the fill surface at least

Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar size equipment may be used in lieu of the

Percolation Tests
Four percolation tests were performed in Boring 3 and 5 to evaluate the
subsurface permeability conditions at the site The constant head method was used for the
percolation tests After the boring was drilled water was injected into the boring The flow
rate was recorded by timing and calculating the volume of water injected as a relatively
constant flow rate was achieved The head levels within the test borings were monitored
by an electric water level sounder during the injection of water Results of the constant
head percolation tests are presented in Appendix C The test indicated that the basalt
formation have coefficient of permeability k in the range of about 0 025 to 0 079 feet per
second
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the basalt rock formation
at the site is highly variable due to the presence or absence of significant voids in the lava
or the presence of embedded clinker seams which greatly affect the disposal capacity of
a dry well or seepage pit In general the greater the number of clinker seams and or voids
encountered by the dry well or seepage pit excavation the higher the overall permeability
and disposal capacity

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings extending to depths of approximately 15 to 30 5 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight
augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6 of Appendix A
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses
a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit
is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would
be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical
Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX D
Percolation Testing
APPENDIX D
Percolation Testing
Percolation tests were performed by using the constant head method in four of the
drilled borings Boring 3 and 5 The drilled borings have a diameter of
3 5 inches and depths of about 20 to 30 feet at the time of testing A water truck was used
to pump water into the test holes The flow rate was recorded by timing and calculating the
volume of water injected as a relatively constant flow rate was achieved The head levels
within the test borings were monitored by an electric water level sounder during the
injection of water The results of the percolation tests are presented on Plates D 1 and
D 2
