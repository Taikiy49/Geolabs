Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age
and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae became extinct
while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau
lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad
plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosion processes are dominated by
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls which are then transported
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley alluvial processes become
dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium In general
stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such that the stream flows transmit
large volumes of water for very short durations Because of this transport of sediments is
intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture
of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosion base levels change
these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes
begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the
material Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the
coarse grained portion of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or
conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors
and upstream in headwaters This continued erosion generates material which is
transported down slope covering the older alluvial deposits Depending on the local
base level and rate of transport these new sediments are generally transient in terms of
geologic time In addition their consistency and density are generally less than those of
older partially consolidated deposits

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic
activity therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so

Liquefaction Potential
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils near the ground
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess pore
water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes In
this process when loose saturated sand deposits are subjected to vibration
such as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume
causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly
enough to dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective stress
internal strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the pore water
pressure build up could equal the overburden pressure essentially reducing the
soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid During
liquefaction the soil acquires mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and
vertical movements and if not confined will result in significant deformations Soils
most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained sands and

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicated that the project site is generally underlain by
mottled stiff clayey silts with traces of extremely to completely weathered gravel from
coalesced colluvium and alluvial sediments Based on our exploration we believe that
the in situ soils at the proposed water tank pad can provide adequate support for the
proposed water tank instrument house and appurtenant structures if designed
within the guidelines presented in this report
Our laboratory testing on the in situ materials indicated relatively high moisture
content which is generally associated with poor workability and less favorable support
characteristics Therefore proper site preparation and earthwork procedures are
necessary to provide adequate support for the planned water tank and
appurtenant structures at the project site
Based on the relatively stiff soil conditions encountered at the project site we
recommend a shallow foundation system to support the water tank We recommend the
removing 12 inches of the in situ subgrade under the floor slab and footings of the water
tank extending up to at least 36 inches beyond the perimeter of the tank and replacing
with 12 inches of compacted base course or select borrow subbase compacted to at
least 95 percent relative compaction for a more uniform support The subgrade under
the slab and footings should be kept moist and free of shrinkage cracks prior to
placement of structural fill and slab concrete
We anticipate that high moisture soils will be encountered in trench excavations
and subsequent backfilling operations at the project site Significant aeration of the high
moisture soils prior to placement and compaction in trench backfills will be required in
order to achieve the required relative moisture and density requirements

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious material and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce
the potential for contamination of the previously cleared and or filled areas
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below finished
subgrades and areas designated to receive fill should be overexcavated to expose
firm natural material The resulting excavation should be backfilled with compacted
select borrow fills The excavated soft and or organic soils should be disposed of
off site
Over excavated subgrades finished subgrades and areas designated to receive
fills at the tank pad and roadway areas should be proof rolled to a relative
compaction of at least 85 percent Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same
soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close cracks or moisture conditioning
and recompacting the subgrade Saturation and subsequent yielding of the
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted structural
fill

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Due to the relatively high moisture content of the in situ soils substantial aeration of
the high moisture soils is essential to achieve the required relative compaction
General fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction Fill
should be compacted to form a dense stable mass Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum
moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density
The contractor working on this project should be made aware that aeration to lower
the soil moisture and additional effort of thinner fill lifts to achieve the specified
compaction will reduce the rate of fill placement for this project Compaction should
be accomplished by sheep foot rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment Water tamping jetting or ponding should not be allowed to compact the
on site silty soils Due to the high rainfall environment at the project site we believe
that good construction practices such as sealing and grading of the ground surface
to provide positive drainage at the end of each work day to reduce the potential for
ponding and saturation are essential to the earthwork operations for the proposed
project

Rockfall Hazards
Rockfall is a type of natural mass wasting process that is in part responsible for the
evolution of mountain slopes Rockfall is a common natural hazard due to the steep
volcanic terrain comprising the mountains and valleys of the Islands of Hawaii
Rockfall activity involves the detachment of a rock mass from the slope face and
the fall of rock materials under the force of gravity Rockfall may involve a single
unstable block of rock outcrop or it may involve a rockslide which encompasses
multiple rock components
Falling rock behavior can be complicated by the varying character of the ground
surface and topography encountered along the rock fall line Rocks may either roll
bounce or have combined movement vectors Falling rock may also hang up
temporarily or permanently on the slope without reaching the bottom under the
effects of terrain roughness topography vegetation and the presence of natural
features such as berms and terraces formed by other rock outcroppings
Based on observed general site conditions the project lies along the crest of a
ridge that is generally covered with vegetation It was also observed that the uphill
portion of the ridge is devoid of rock outcrops that could potentially roll into the site
and therefore rockfall hazard is not a consideration in the design of the proposed
project and no rockfall hazard mitigation is necessary
Hazards of Debris Flow Into Project Site
From a geographic perspective the project site generally exists above the toe of a
ridgeline transitioning into a fan composed of alluvial and colluvial deposits as it
merges into the upper valley floor Except for a few trail marks and some signs of
previous clearing and grading work the natural vegetation cover of the uphill
environment is largely preserved The ridge on the uphill side of the project
generally drains to the north and south relative to the project site into tributaries of
Waiahole Stream Because of the vegetation cover and terrain configuration
surrounding the project the hazards for a mudslide or debris flowing into the
project site is unlikely

Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight determinations ASTM D 2937
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation
of soil properties The test results are presented on the logs of borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Seven Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected
samples to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B 1
Two Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the representative materials tested The
results of the tests are presented on Plates B 2 and B 3
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the in situ soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the
soils along the access road and water tank area Results of the CBR tests were also
used to evaluate the quality of the materials for use as fills The test results are
presented on Plates B 4 through B 7
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on
selected bulk samples of the in situ soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture
content relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 8 and B 9
One Consolidation test with time rates ASTM D 2435 was performed on a
sample of the on site soils within the tank pad area to evaluate the compressibility
characteristics of a representative sample of the clayey silt soil commonly encountered
at the project site The Consolidation test data is presented on Plate B 10
