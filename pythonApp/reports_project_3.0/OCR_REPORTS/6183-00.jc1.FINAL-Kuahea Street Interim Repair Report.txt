BACKGROUND
The Kuahea Street area subdivision was constructed during the years of 1951 and
1952 Beginning in 1952 problems associated with some earth movements and slight
property damage were first observed In 1954 following a large rainstorm significant
damage related to large scale earth movements on the order of several feet was
experienced The first remedial repairs were made in 1957 consisting of the construction
of a concrete curtain wall on Kuahea Street below the quarry site in addition to some
roadway repairs and water seepage control along Kuahea Street
Dr Ralph Peck a world renowned landslide consultant studied the landslide from
1958 through 1969 utilizing tiltmeter and piezometer instrumentation to monitor the
subsurface movements and hydrologic conditions over time During this period Dr Peck
focused on arresting the landslide through the use of subsurface drainage such as
vertical and horizontal drainage structures His intensive studies confirmed that the earth
movements were directly related to rainfall events as surface storm water penetrated and
accumulated in the poorly drained landslide mass In addition the seat or bottom of the
landslide mass was estimated to be located at a maximum depth of about 60 feet below
the ground surface at that time near the center of the landslide based on the
interpretation of the tiltmeter data
As a result remedial measures such as surface drainage improvements including
the sealing of potential avenues of water percolation into the landslide were the first to be
implemented on the project site Early on it was believed that the old quarry site was
contributing to the groundwater accumulation within the landslide therefore drainage of
the quarry site was improved as part of the initial remedial measures In addition some
underground utility pipes were abandoned and relocated onto more stable ground to
reduce the risk of adding subsurface water to the landslide mass during pipeline
breakages or leaks
By the end of 1958 three vertical drains 10 inch and 12 inch diameter had been
installed through the landslide mass along Waiomao Road The vertical drains were
terminated in the underlying more permeable basalt rock ground condition The effects of
the vertical drains were nominal in that the static groundwater levels in the drains
decreased some but not on a regional level as originally anticipated Again the
construction of horizontal drainage structures was suggested as a possible alternative for
draining and arresting the landslide
Through the early 1960s the landslide did not appear to broaden but earth
movement rates remained essentially unchanged imparting significant distortion and
damage to public thoroughfares and private property An example of the severe
disturbance was the nearly 10 foot vertical drop of Kuahea Street along the head of the
landslide and the near closure of Kipona Place at the toe of the landslide from
encroaching earth and building materials

In 1967 Dr Peck recommended the installation of a single row of 4 to 6 foot
diameter pumped vertical drains along Waiomao Road extending across the entire
landslide An alternative measure was proposed to deepen the large diameter drains by a
smaller diameter boring at the bottom of the vertical drain which would allow for gravity
drainage into the more permeable ground located below the landslide plane In addition a
horizontal drainage tunnel with radial drains was suggested based on recent innovative
construction developments at that time in the field of such structures
In the late 1960s through the early 1970s the City and County of Honolulu began
to purchase the lots that had been severely damaged by the landslide and may be
considered uninhabitable Approximately 20 lots within the landslide footprint were
acquired Following the acquisition of the lots the work performed included the demolition
of structures and re grading of land area in an effort to promote enough stability to
continue with restoration of necessary public facilities By doing so the plan was to
reduce the potential for landslide growth by loss of support at the head of the landslide
and minimize additional damage at the toe of the landslide The method selected to
stabilize the landslide was general mass grading of the land surface to reduce the earth
pressure driving force of the landslide mass It was believed that once the movements
became small enough measures would be taken to restore the roads and utilities within
the site
In the 1970s observations began to indicate that the Waiomao Landslide might be
expanding at the western boundary particularly in the area south and west of
Waiomao Road where previous movements had not been experienced By July 1977
the Waiomao Landslide Phase I remedial work consisting of grading vacant lots and
restoration of Waiomao Road and Kipona Place was completed In conjunction with the
restoration of Kipona Place earthwork was performed that included the removal of soil
material from the top of the slope near Waiomao Road and the construction of a buttress
of select fill materials at the toe of the slope at Kipona Place
The buttress fill extended across the entire toe of the slope with the exception of a
short section of about 100 feet in length next to the intersection of Kipona Place and
Lamaku Place In addition temporary repairs to Kuahea Street consisting of roadway
maintenance and utility line relocation were completed The ground disturbance to
Kuahea Street was observed as continuing though at a slower rate than previously
experienced As part of the road restoration work trench drains and subsurface drains
subdrains were constructed along Kuahea Street and Waiomao Road that daylighted in
the open slopes below the respective roadways
Subsequently a localized slump movement developed within the landslide area
following the grading work The location of the slump was just downslope of the
intersection of Lamaku Place and Waiomao Road Two other localized slumps also
developed in the graded land area located between Waiomao Road and Kipona Place

In the early months of 1979 observations of Kuahea Street began to indicate
renewed movement of the roadway as evidenced by increased sinking and deformation
of the pavement As a result of the renewed movements Walter Lum Associates WLA
began a geotechnical investigation of the site as part of the Waiomao Landslide Phase II
remedial work Based on the findings of the WLA Phase II study stabilization
recommendations were proposed and consisted of the construction of a reinforced earth
wall at Kuahea Street and buttress fills along Lamaku Place and Kipona Place In
addition the construction of horizontal subdrains was again recommended for use
throughout the site
In 1980 the City and County of Honolulu prepared a summary of alternative
solutions and estimated costs based on the WLA 1979 engineering report The following
summarizes the recommended remedial measures that would best serve the community
and provide stability to the landslide mass
The remedial work proposed for Kuahea Street should include the
construction of a retaining structure at the right of way line to maintain the
existing traffic lanes and sidewalk in the hope that removing additional
weight from the top of the landslide would aid in overall landslide
stabilization
The remedial work proposed for the slump next to Lamaku Place should
include the construction of a 200 foot long retaining structure along Lamaku
Place to maintain the road width and provide stability to the landslide
The remedial work proposed for the overall landslide area should include
the installation of a series of horizontal subdrains designed to lower the
groundwater table and aid in stabilizing the landslide
No other engineering studies or remedial work were conducted for the project
between 1981 and 1995
OTHER KUAHEA STREET AREA EARTH MOVEMENTS
In 1995 there was renewed concern that the landslide may be expanding or a new
landslide area may be developing in the area of Kuahea Street near Kuahea Place The
concern emerged as a result of reports of repeated underground utility line leakage and
ground surface disturbance to the roadway
As a result of the leakage concern the City and County of Honolulu Board of
Water Supply BWS and the former City and County of Honolulu Department of
Wastewater Management DWWM conducted a series of inspections and video
surveillance of their respective underground utility lines in the area It was determined that
many of the lines contained cracks offset joints sunken manholes and obstructions that
may be related to earth movements Furthermore although the movements of the

As a result of the reported expansion of utility line and infrastructure damage in the
vicinity of the Waiomao Landslide it was desired to initiate a comprehensive study of the
Kuahea Street area to evaluate the existing site conditions with respect to earth
movement It was desired to identify areas of suspected earth movement quantify the
movement and develop conceptual remedial measures to stabilize the movements
In 1996 the City and County of Honolulu commissioned Geolabs Inc to perform a
geotechnical engineering study of the existing and potential earth movements occurring
within the Kuahea Street area and vicinity The study area limits were defined and based
on our site reconnaissance and field mapping efforts at the time three areas of potential
earth movement in the Kuahea Street area and vicinity were identified for long term
monitoring by inclinometer instrumentation The three potential earth movement areas in
addition to the documented Waiomao Landslide were identified as Areas A B and C
as shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2 The three areas of potential movement were
identified based on our observation of existing fixed structure distress and ground surface
deformation
Initial subsurface monitoring instrumentation consisting of eight inclinometers
2000 for the Kuahea Street Area Movement project Between 2000 and 2009 monitoring
of the earth movements by inclinometer and piezometer instrumentation continued
Additional inclinometers were installed periodically to replace and supplement older
inclinometers that were decommissioned due to the effects of compounded casing
deformation From 2000 through the present time inclinometer and piezometer readings
were recorded in the field at about two month intervals Summary plots of the measured
cumulative ground movements from the Waiomao Landslide inclinometers between
October 1999 and May 2011 are presented on Plates 4 1 and 4 2 The two plots separate
the Waiomao Landslide into the upper section and the lower section The upper
section is defined by the landslide above Waiomao Road near Kuahea Street and the
lower section is defined by the portion of the landslide at and below Waiomao Road
Based on our monitoring of the Waiomao Landslide between 2000 and 2011 the

surface settlement and pavement deformation ground rupture In addition the continuous
slow earth movement recorded in the subsurface at the landslide head resulted in the
need to replace multiple inclinometers that became inoperable as a result of compounded

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the ongoing earth movement monitoring and our analysis of the existing
landslide conditions the head of the Waiomao Landslide extends across five lots fronting
Kuahea Street The approximate landslide footprint is shown on the General Site Plan
Plate 2 In general the anticipated landslide boundary at the head of Waiomao Landslide
extended across 2388 and 2392 Kuahea Street five lots owned by the
City and County of Honolulu
As mentioned previously the drilled shaft retaining structure was proposed for a
permanent repair alternative for this portion of the landslide Details of the alternative
design are presented in the report entitled Preliminary Engineering Report Kuahea Street
Earth Movement Mitigation dated August 16 2010
As an alternative consideration may be given to the following interim repairs to
accommodate the continuous earth movement and to maintain the new infrastructure
system roadway and underground utilities in a serviceable level for the next 10 to
15 years Generally the interim mitigation consists of the following items
Removing and replacing the existing asphalt concrete pavement
Extending the excavation about 4 5 feet below the pavement surface and
backfilling with geogrid reinforced soil mat
Relocating the existing water line and sewer line further away from the earth
moving wedge with new man holes and or thrust blocks as needed
Installing 6 inch diameter subdrains along the makai edge of the excavation
to intercept potential seepage water and daylight to adjacent catchment
basin
It should be noted that the existing sewer line within the project limits is partially
located under the 28 foot wide travelway from about Station 7 25 to Station 8 40 From
Station 8 40 to Station 10 10 the sewer line is under the mauka grass sidewalk A typical
cross section of the Typical Interim Mitigation Details is presented on Plate 5

In addition geofoam material may be used as backfill material to reduce the
potential landslide driving force for the excavation beyond the 28 foot travelway The top of
the geofoam material should be embedded at least 2 feet below the finished ground
surface We do not recommend using geofoam to backfill the excavation within the
travelway due to the limited depth It is our opinion that the geogrid reinforced soil mat
provides better inter lock strength to keep the soil mat as a unit and perform better in the
moving ground conditions
Provisions should be given to protect the existing CRM walls overhead power line
and the power poles from damage during the proposed trench excavation and
compaction The contractor should be advised to provide sufficient shoring and or bracing
as temporary protection during construction

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling eight borings
maximum depths of about 11 5 to 102 feet below the existing ground surface The borings
were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and
coring tools as appropriate The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 3
Our engineers and or geologists classified the materials encountered in the borings
by visual and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488
Standard Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling

Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Standard Practice for Thick Wall Ring Lined
Spilt Barrel Drive Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD modified California sampler
with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from
the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D 1586 Standard Test Method for
Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques consisting of a PQ wireline coring system with
double tube core barrel core size of 3 35 inches in general accordance with
ASTM D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description Rock
cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown
on the Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2 The Rock Description System is based on the
publication Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock
Masses by International Society for Rock Mechanics March 1977
