REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are referred 
to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is on the coastal plain of Southern Oahu. The coastal plain was 
built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano, which forms the eastern two-thirds of the 
Island of Oahu. The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of alluvium derived 
from erosion of the volcano, interbedded with coral reefs and associated deposits. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. Most of the coastal plains were developed during the 
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly. As the glaciers grew and 
advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below

the present stands of the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water 
became available such that the sea levels rose to above its present level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea 
level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
The project site generally is underlain by deposits consisting of calcareous 
sediments. A surface layer of man-made fill was placed over these deposits for 
development of the Kaka’ako area within the last century. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions alongside the bike lane alignment between 
Queen Street and Kapiolani Boulevard by drilling and sampling four borings, designated 
as Boring Nos. 5 through 8, extending to depths of about 10.25 to 11.5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface.  Due to the presence of underground utilities within the bike 
lane corridor, our borings had to be relocated to the adjacent travel lane.  In addition, four 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-4, were 
collected at the boring locations to evaluate their pavement support characteristics. The 
approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.2.  
Our field exploration suggested that the section of bike lane alignment between 
Queen Street and Kapiolani Boulevard is likely underlain by asphalt concrete pavements 
overlying fill materials placed over coralline detritus at shallow depths.  In general, our 
drilled borings encountered 8 to 9 inches of asphaltic concrete at the travel lane.  Fill 
materials consisting of medium dense silty sands with some gravel were generally 
encountered to depths of about 5 to 8 feet below the existing pavement surface.  Below 
the fill materials, coralline detritus consisting of very loose to medium dense silty sands 
and gravel were encountered to the maximum depth explored of about 11.5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface. 
It should be noted that Boring No. 5 had to be moved around multiple times due to 
the encountering of obstruction at a depth of about 2 feet below the existing pavement 
surface.  It is speculated that the obstruction might be the old 7’x3’ box drain that was 
abandoned in place. 
We encountered groundwater at depths ranging from about 4.7 to 5.2 feet below 
the existing pavement surface. The groundwater levels encountered generally correspond 
to elevations of about +0.8 to +1.8 feet MSL at the time of our field exploration. Due to the 
proximity of the project site to the Pacific Ocean, water levels are expected to vary with 
tidal fluctuations and storm events. It should be noted that water levels may also vary with 
seasonal rainfall, time of the year, and other factors.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered asphaltic concrete and fill materials placed over 
coralline detritus extending to the maximum depth explored of about 11.5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface. The groundwater levels encountered generally correspond to 
elevations of about +0.8 to +1.8 feet MSL at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on the results of our field exploration, we anticipate the pavement subgrades 
along the existing bike lane alignment is likely underlain by medium dense silty sands with 
some gravel. Based on the laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) test results, a design 
CBR value of 40 may be adopted for the anticipated subgrade conditions along the bike 
lane alignment.  Detailed discussions and recommendations for the permeable pavement 
design are presented in the following section: 
Pavement Design 
Based on the information provided, the following four types of permeable 
pavements are planned at the existing bike lane along South Street between Pohukaina 
Street and Kapiolani Boulevard: 
Pohukaina Street to Halekauwila Street 
Porous AC Pavement 
Halekauwila Street to Queen Street 
Pervious Concrete Pavement 
Queen Street to Kawaiahao Street 
Permeable Paver Type 1 - PaveDrain Interlocking Concrete Pavers 
Kawaiahao Street to Kapiolani Boulevard 
Permeable Paver Type 2 - HydroFlo Concrete Pavers 
We understand that all the permeable pavement sections will be within the 
two-way bicycle track with some occasional vehicles driving over for maintenance 
purposes and also at the driveways.  In addition, the permeable pavement sections will 
only function for filtering purposes and will not allow water infiltration into the underlying 
subgrade soils. Therefore, an impervious thermoplastic liner will be installed at the 
bottom of the permeable pavement section.  Subdrains will be installed and daylighted 
to the nearest catch basin to dispose the collected water.  Based on these assumptions, 
it is our opinion that the following permeable pavement design may be used for the 
project:

Prior to placing the permeable pavement section, we recommend properly 
scarifying the subgrade to a depth of at least 10 inches, moisture-conditioning to above the 
optimum moisture content, and recompacting to a minimum of 95 percent relative 
compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density.  Saturation and subsequent yielding of the 
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require 
over-excavation of the soft/loose areas and replacement with well-compacted fill. 
A Geolabs representative or a representative under direct supervision of a qualified 
geotechnical engineer licensed in the State of Hawaii with at least 10 years of earthwork 
experience should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to observe whether 
undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation, observe the scarification 
process, and confirm whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to those encountered 
in our field exploration.  The CBR tests and/or field observations should be performed on 
the actual subgrade soils during construction to confirm that the above pavement design 
sections are adequate.

LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the borings drilled and bulk samples collected. Variations of 
subsoil conditions beyond the field data points may occur, and the nature and extent of 
these variations may not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then 
appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein. 
The boring locations indicated in this report are approximate, having been obtained 
using a hand-held Global Positioning System (GPS) device. Elevations noted on the 
borings were interpolated from spot elevations shown on the Site Plan 2.2, prepared by 
the Department of Facility Maintenance (DFM), City and County of Honolulu, Storm Water 
Quality (SWQ) Division.  The physical locations and elevations of the borings should be 
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote 
a gradual transition. Water level data from the borings was measured at the time shown on 
the graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. These data have 
been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. It should be noted 
that groundwater fluctuations may occur due to variation in tidal fluctuations, rainfall, 
runoff, seepage, temperature, and other factors.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface condition alongside the bicycle lane alignment was explored by 
drilling and sampling four exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 5 through 8, 
extending to depths of about 10.25 to 11.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.2. The borings were 
drilled with a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 4-inch OD solid-stem augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. The soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.
