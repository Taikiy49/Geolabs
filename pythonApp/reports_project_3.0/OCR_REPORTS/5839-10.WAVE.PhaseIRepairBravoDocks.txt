PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS

Station on the Island of Oahu Hawaii We understand it is planned to demolish and
remove the existing timber fender pile system and replace it with a concrete fender pile
system The project also includes concrete rehabilitation of the existing curbs beams
slabs and piles and other incidental related work

square precast prestressed concrete piles with pneumatic rubber marine fenders Based

Hammer and Driving System
A single acting diesel hammer was proposed for the pile driving
operations by Hawaiian Dredging Construction Company HDCC Based on the pile
driving equipment data forms provided the hammer has a ram weight of
6 615 pounds and a maximum stroke of 10 57 feet The maximum rated energy of the
is 69 990 foot pounds at the maximum pump setting
Based on the Contract Documents a pile cushion consisting of at least 12 inches of
plywood cushion block is required for the pile driving operations The plywood cushion
block generally has a cross section area of approximately 568 square inches Schematic
details of the driving system are presented on the Pile and Driving Equipment Data Forms
in the Appendix Pages 1 1 and 1 2
Pile Details
The specified fender piles will consist of 18 inch and 24 inch square precast
prestressed concrete piles The length of the 18 inch square piles will be 87 8 feet and the
length of the 24 inch square piles will range between about 72 8 and 87 8 feet
We understand that the piles will be cast from concrete with a minimum 28 day
unconfined compressive strength of 7 500 and 7 000 pounds per square inch psi for the
18 inch square and 24 inch square concrete fender piles respectively We also
understand that the effective prestress of the 18 inch and 24 inch square piles are
approximately 1 917 and 1 262 psi respectively Based on this information the maximum
allowable compressive stress in the 18 inch and 24 inch square precast prestressed
concrete piles during pile driving is about 2 583 and 2 928 psi respectively The maximum

proposed by Hawaiian Dredging Construction Company and the subsurface conditions
presented in the referenced geotechnical engineering report The wave equation analyses
were performed using the computer program GRLWEAP Wave Equation Analysis of Pile
Foundations which is an extension of the WEAP87 program developed under the
sponsorship of the Federal Highway Administration
The wave equation analyses were performed on five subsurface conditions

five soil profile types anticipated at the project site are summarized in the following table It
should be noted the 18 inch and 24 inch piles were analyzed in Soil Profile Type 5
Therefore six cases of wave equation analyses were performed

Driving Stresses
A graph showing the computed maximum compression and tensile stresses during
driving is provided on Plates 2 1 through 2 6 for the six cases analyzed Please note that
the graph was prepared based on kips per square inch ksi which is equivalent to
1 000 psi
Based on our analyses the compressive stresses during the pile driving for Soil
Profile Types 1 through 5 are less than the allowable compressive stress of 2 583 psi for
the 18 inch square piles and 2 928 psi for the 24 inch square piles The compressive
stresses developed during pile driving did not exceed the allowable compressive stresses
for the full range of driving resistance at the maximum pumping setting
The tensile stresses developed during the pile driving estimated for Soil Profile
Types 1 through 5 also are less than the allowable tensile stress of 1 917 psi for the
18 inch square piles and 1 262 psi for the 24 inch square piles for the full range of driving
resistance at Pump Settings 1 through 4
Based on the above results it is our professional opinion that the proposed pile
driving equipment is able to install the fender piles to the specified tip elevations without
over stressing the concrete fender piles

ABOUT THE WAVE EQUATION ANALYSIS RESULTS
The GRLWEAP program simulates the behavior of a preformed pile driven by
either an impact hammer or a vibratory hammer The program is based on
mathematical models which describe motion and forces of hammer driving
system pile and soil under the hammer action Under certain conditions
the models only crudely approximate often complex dynamic situations
A wave equation analysis generally relies on input data which represents
normal situations In particular the hammer data file supplied with the
program assumes that the hammer is in good working order All of the input
data selected by the user may be the best available information at the time
when the analysis is performed However input data and therefore results
may significantly differ from actual field conditions
Therefore the program authors recommend prudent use of the GRLWEAP results
Soil response and hammer performance should be verified by static and or
dynamic testing and measurements Estimates of bending or other local
non axial stresses and prestress effects must also be accounted for by the
user
The calculated capacity blow count relationship i e the bearing graph
should be used in conjunction with observed blow counts for the capacity
assessment of a driven pile Soil setup occurring after pile installation
may produce bearing capacity values that differ substantially from those
expected from a wave equation analysis due to soil setup or relaxation This
is particularly true for pile driven with vibratory hammers The GRLWEAP user
must estimate such effects and should also use proper care when applying blow
counts from restrike because of the variability of hammer energy soil
resistance and blow count during early restriking
Finally the GRLWEAP capacities are ultimate values They MUST be reduced by
means of an appropriate factor of safety to yield a design or working load
The selection of a factor of safety should consider the quality of the
construction control the variability of the site conditions uncertainties
in the loads the importance of building and other factors
