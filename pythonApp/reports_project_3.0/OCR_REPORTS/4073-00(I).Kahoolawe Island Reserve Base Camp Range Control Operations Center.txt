REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Kaho olawe is located about 11 kilometers to the south of the Island of
Maui and about 27 kilometers to the southeast of the Island of Lana i as shown on the Project
Location Map Plate is approximately 117 square kilometers in land area with
a maximum elevation of about 450 meters above sea level at the Lua Makika summit on the
eastern portion of the island
Kaho olawe is believed to be composed of a single shield volcano formed by the
extrusion of successive layered thinly bedded pahoehoe and a a basalt lava flows that
emanated from the rift zones and caldera at Lua Makika The primary eruptive rift zones of
the former volcano extend westerly from the summit and to a lesser degree to the north and
east from the summit Two cinder cone remnants can be seen located at Puu Moiwi and
Puu Kamama The area at Lua Kealialalo is believed to represent the weathered remains of
a collapsed crater at the crest of the broad lava shield Some of the more recent aged rocks
composing the upper caldera filling layers of the island have been dated at about 1 million
years old
The existing Base Camp facility is located adjacent to the southwest facing shoreline
and is situated on thin alluvial and colluvial soil deposits overlying weathered basalt rock
formation at a relatively shallow depth

As previously mentioned the proposed Range Control Operations Center site is
presently occupied by a fuel storage area surrounded by earth berms The earth berms
appear to be comprised of sandy clay soils with embedded cobbles and boulders of about
0 5 meters in diameter The berms are estimated to range from about 1 5 to 3 meters in
height above the surrounding generally flat terrain The area contained within the berms is
also generally flat with some slight grade down toward the westerly direction
Based on our observation of the site the existing ground surface is composed of
compact dark brown sandy clay soils with some cobbles and boulders that sporadically
protrude approximately 100 millimeters mm above the soil ground surface The ground
surface is slightly irregular due to the rock protrusions and other stiff soil humps Based on the
very compact character of the ground within the fuel storage berms it is believed that the site
has been subjected to repeated vehicular traffic in conjunction with the present use of the site
Portions of the site exhibit more sandy surface soils due to the presence of imported beach
sands that have been mixed with the native clayey soils
Based on our observation of the topography surrounding the earth berms the project
site may have received some thin fills at the western half of the site to provide for the flat
ground surface contained within the fuel storage area The fill material appears to consist of
mainly clayey and sandy soils with some cobbles and small boulders Some surface ground
cracks were observed that are likely related to the expansive character of the dark brown
colored clayey soils present at the site Based on our observation some of the clayey soils
used as fill may potentially contain some small quantities of root and grass fiber Probing of
the ground surface at the site revealed that the soils are very firm and tightly compacted
Six shallow test pits were excavated by pick and shovel to sample the near surface
soils The test pits could not be excavated below about 150 to 200 mm depths due to the
hardness of the ground and pick refusal Soil samples were recovered from each of the six
test pits and selected samples were tested in our laboratory for the purpose of soil
classification The locations of the six test pits are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
NEAR SURFACE SOIL CONDITIONS
The soils observed at the project site appear to be relatively uniform in appearance and
may be classified as sandy and clayey soils containing some gravel cobbles and small
boulders that represent alluvial colluvial soils derived from the weathering of basalt rock
Based on our observation of the site the brown and dark brown colored sandy clay soils
appear to be very stiff though friable due to the compact soil character and the arid
environment The soils appear to have a generally high shrink swell potential

Based on our field reconnaissance and laboratory test results we believe that the
principal geotechnical engineering considerations for construction of the proposed Base Camp
Range Conirol Operations Center PMU trailers include the following
The near surface brown colored clayey soils at the site possess a high swell

Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering recommendations
for design of the PMU trailer foundations and grading are presented in the following sections
of this letter report
Building Foundations
We understand that it is desired to utilize post and beam type construction for support
of the proposed PMU trailer buildings Therefore we envision that the building posts will be
supported on a concrete pad foundation bearing on the near surface soils at the site Based
on the near surface soil conditions anticipated at the project site we believe that the
recompacted near surface soils should provide adequate foundation support for the proposed
modular buildings For foundations bearing on the recompacted clayey soils or compacted
fill an allowable bearing pressure on the order of about may be used
for design of foundations The bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased
by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
Since the existing near surface clayey soils may become friable when exposed to the
windy environment and or lose substantial bearing strength or heave upon saturation we
recommend that the concrete foundations be embedded below the zone of seasonal moisture
fluctuations As a minimum we recommend that the concrete foundations be embedded at
least 500 mm below the lowest adjacent finished grades In addition special attention should
be given to the recommendations presented in the Drainage section of this report for
post and beam type foundations

Where footings are located adjacent to slopes or on slopes the footings should be
embedded to a sufficient depth to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of at least
2 meters measured from the outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope If foundations
are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be embedded below a 45 degree
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the
inverts of the utility lines
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with the
recommendations presented herein we estimate that total footing settlements under the
anticipated light design loads to be less than 25 millimeters mm Differential settlements of
footings bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or compacted fill as recommended herein
should be on the order of about 15 mm
Lateral loads acting on the building structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth pressure
developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings A coefficient
of friction of 0 30 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted in situ soils or
compacted fill Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated using an equivalent
fluid pressure of 50 kilonewton per cubic meter kKN m for footings embedded in the
recompacted in situ clayey soils and compacted fill These values assume that the soils
around the footings are relatively undisturbed or well compacted Unless covered by
pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 300 mm below the finished grade
should be neglected
Uplift loads acting on the foundations may be resisted by the gravity load of the footings
and by the side shear along the vertical faces of the footings A side shear value of 50 kPa
may be used for estimating resistance to uplift forces This value is intended for transient
loads and should not be increased by one third 1 3 Additional uplift resistance may be
obtained from the gravity loads of grade beams that are structurally connected to the
foundations

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly
cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials
should be removed and disposed of properly off site or stockpiled in a designated area
to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should bé
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted general fill or controlled fill The excavated soft soils
should be properly disposed of off site and or stockpiled in a designated area where
appropriate
Finished subgrades and areas designated to receive fills should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 200 mm moisture conditioned to between 10 percent below the
optimum moisture and the optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 698 Optimum moisture is
the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soil be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or that the soil subgrade be
prepared again as recommended above Saturation and subsequent yielding of the
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted general fill or
controlled fill

Fills and Backfills
In general the excavated on site soils may be re used as a source of general fill or
controlled fill materials provided that they are free of vegetation and deleterious
materials and the maximum particle size should be less than 150 mm in maximum
dimension It should be noted that the on site soils appear to be relatively dry between
10 and 20 percent below the optimum moisture content Therefore an ample water
supply will be necessary during the grading operations to provide adequate water to
achieve the specified moisture and compaction requirements
Based on the remote location of the project site from available sources of imported fill
material we do not envision the need for imported general fill material for this project
Where additional general fill material is necessary we envision that the fill material
would be obtained from nearby projects on the island Therefore borrow materials
should conform to the requirements of general fill as described above
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill materials and or controlled fill should be moisture conditioned to between
10 percent below the optimum moisture and the optimum moisture content placed in
level lifts not exceeding 200 mm in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 698 test procedures Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where required aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials should be
moisture conditioned placed in level lifts not exceeding 200 mm in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction based on ASTM Test
Designation D 1557
Drainage
The finished grades adjacent to and beyond the buildings should be sloped to shed
water away from the pad and finished surfaces to reduce the potential for ponding Surface
water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction These drainage
requirements are essential for the proper performance of the building foundations since
ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of
strength In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the buildings
