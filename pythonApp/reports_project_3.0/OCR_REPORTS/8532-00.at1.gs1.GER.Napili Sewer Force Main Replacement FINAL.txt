SECTION 1.  
PROJECT CONSIDERATION 
This geotechnical engineering report summarizes our findings and geotechnical 
recommendations for the Pump Station 1, Napili Sewer Force Main project based on our 
engineering analyses utilizing the information obtained from our literature research, past 
experience in the project vicinity, and the Geotechnical Data Report prepared for the Pump 
Station 1, Napili Sewer Force Main Replacement project.  The project location and general vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. The boring drilled for this effort is shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. The findings and recommendations provided in this report are intended to 
assist the project team in the design of the Pump Station 1, Napili Sewer Force Main Replacement 
project. 
The project site is located along Lower Honoapiilani Road and Honoapiilani Highway in 
Kaanapali on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. We understand that this project consists of replacing or 
rehabilitating the existing 3,000 linear feet of 20-inch sewer force main connecting the existing 
Napili 1 Sewage Pump Station No. 9 to the Lahaina Wastewater Reclamation Facility (WWRF). Based 
on the preliminary drawings provided, a new wet well structure will be constructed with a bottom 
slab invert elevation of  -15.67 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). Therefore, the new wet well will extend 
about 26 feet below the existing ground surface.

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes: the older West Maui (Tertiary 
Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene Epoch). The Isthmus 
of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain between these two volcanoes. 
The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows from Haleakala ponding on West Maui. It 
is comprised of alluvium washed from the slopes of West Maui and East Maui (Haleakala). The 
erosional processes are dominated by the detachment of soil and rock masses from the mountain 
walls, and the soil materials are transported downslope toward the isthmus primarily by gravity 
as colluvium. Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial 
processes become dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium.  
The project site is located on the western flank of the West Maui Volcano, which forms 
the west side of the Island of Maui. The West Maui Mountains have been deeply incised by 
erosion depositing large quantities of recent unconsolidated sediments along the coastal regions. 
In general, the project site is underlain by fill, lagoonal deposits (calcareous sediments), alluvium, 
and basalt formation.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
Based on the Geological Map by Sherrod, etc., one main stratigraphic unit covers the 
project site and is presented on the Geology Map, Plate 3. This stratigraphic unit is Recent 
Alluvium. 
These soils generally are the result of sedimentation in a very low-energy environment 
such as an estuary or bay. Frequently, the soils assigned to this unit accumulated very rapidly 
underwater and might not have been exposed to air. Therefore, there is a tendency to encounter 
under-consolidated sediments, which settle under the weight of minimal overburden structural 
loading. Recent alluvium generally is found within streams and within broad valley floors and 
basin features surrounded by hills. These soils also may be found in proximity to swamp and flat 
tidal environments. 
Recent Alluvium generally consists of brown to gray silty clays, clayey silts, and sandy silts 
with varying amounts of sands and infrequent pockets of gravel and cobbles. Recent Alluvium 
also contains clayey/silty sands and sandy gravels. The consistency is generally in the range of 
very soft to stiff and very loose to medium dense. 
Our boring generally encountered a surface AC layer about 2 inches thick. The AC layer 
was underlain by about 6 feet of fill material. Below the fill layer, lagoonal deposits and a thin 
alluvial layer were encountered to a depth of about 43 feet below the existing ground surface. 
The alluvium was underlain by basalt formation with clinker to the maximum depth explored of 
about 61.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
The fill layer consisted of dense sandy gravel and medium stiff to very soft silt. The 
lagoonal deposits generally consisted of loose to very loose sand and silty sand, and very soft silty 
clay. The lagoonal deposits were underlain by a 3-foot thick alluvium layer consisting of stiff silty 
clay. The basalt formation was closely to slightly fractured, highly to slightly weathered, and very 
soft to very hard.

3.1 
Seismic Design Parameters 
Based on the International Building Code, 2012 Edition (IBC 2012), the project site may 
be subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed. The 
following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity, soil profile type for seismic design, 
and potential for liquefaction at the project site. 
 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic plates. In 
contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic activity; therefore, 
earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during volcanic eruptions. In 
addition, earthquakes may result from the underground movement of magma that comes 
close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of 
earthquakes each year, but most are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive 
instruments. However, some earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause 
minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the Island of 
Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of magma are 
concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island of Hawaii. 
Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) are related to volcanic 
activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased 
distance from the Island of Hawaii.

 Liquefaction Potential 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the ground 
surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess pore water 
pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes. In this process, 
when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration (such as during an 
earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume, causing an increase in pore 
water pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of 
pore water pressure, the effective stress (internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under 
sustained vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could equal the overburden pressure, 
essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. 
During liquefaction, the soil acquires sufficient mobility to permit both horizontal and 
vertical movements, and if not confined, it will result in significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained sands and 
loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics of 
a soil deposit are as follows:

Wet Well Structure 
We understand that the wet well structure will be about 26 feet below the existing ground 
surface and below the groundwater level at the project site. Due to potentially liquefiable soils 
beneath the wet well structure, we recommend that soil stabilization by jet grouting be 
performed below the wet well structure. Detail discussion on jet grouting is provided in the

An allowable bearing pressure of up to 5,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for 
the design of the wet well structure bearing on the jet-grouted soil. The bearing value recommended 
is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those 
caused by wind or seismic forces. If the wet well structure is designed and constructed in accordance

recommend placing a minimum 8-inch layer of cushion fill material (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) or 
concrete mud slab below the bottom of the structure to provide uniform support over the 
jet-grouted columns.

3.3 
Soil Stabilization by Jet Grouting 
In order to stabilize the loose to very loose lagoonal deposits from potential liquefaction 
and reduce the potential for significant settlement of the wet well structure, we recommend the 
loose to very loose lagoonal deposits below the planned wet well structure be stabilized using 
the jet grouting method.  
In general, jet grouting is a technique utilizing a special drill bit and injection monitor with 
radial horizontal nozzles to produce stabilized soil-cement columns. The jet-grouting technique 
is a process that produces soil-cement columns by pumping neat cement grout slurry through 
horizontal jets injected at high pressures. The horizontal jets of cement grout slurry cuts and 
mixes the surrounding in-situ materials with the neat cement slurry grout as the drill bit is slowly 
rotated and withdrawn to form a soil-cement column. 
In order to provide support for the new wet well structure, we recommend installing 
jet-grouted columns under the structure. The jet-grouted columns would derive vertical support 
primarily from bearing on the underlying stiff alluvium or basalt formation. Based on our 
evaluation of the subsurface conditions and the load supporting capacity of the jet-grouted 
columns, we recommend soil stabilization consisting of 36-inch diameter jet-grouted columns.

 Jet-Grouted Columns 
Based on experience, the jet-grouted columns should have a minimum average diameter 
of 36 inches. In addition, the grout mix should have a specific gravity of at least 1.5 and 
should be able to produce jet-grouted columns with a 7-day unconfined compressive 
strength of at least 200 pounds per square inch (psi) and a 28-day unconfined compressive 
strength of at least 500 psi. 
In general, the jet-grouted columns should be embedded a minimum of 2 feet into the 
underlying stiff alluvium or basalt formation at depths starting from 42 to 43 feet below 
the existing ground surface. For estimating purposes, the tip of the jet-grouted columns 
may be assumed at a depth of about 43 feet below the existing ground surface. An existing 
ground elevation of +8 feet MSL was used for the estimated tip elevation.  
Since the jet-grouted columns will be used for foundation support and groundwater 
control, the jet-grouted columns should be overlapped a minimum of 12 inches. The 
coefficient of permeability of the jet grout columns should not exceed 1x10-6 cm/sec. 
It should be noted that local variations in the subsurface profile should be anticipated. 
Because the depths of each column foundation will vary depending on the subsurface 
conditions and depth to the invert at each column location and due to the specialized 
nature of the jet grouting work, a Geolabs representative should be present to observe 
the jet grouting operations.

for the proposed construction. Since the excavation may involve the discharge of groundwater, 
a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permit may be required for this 
discharge. The contractor should consult their independent consultant for the latest regulations 
and information pertaining to the permit application. 
Because of the granular nature of some of the subsurface soils and the fractured nature 
of the encountered basalt rock formation, we anticipate that the subsurface materials 
encountered are relatively permeable. Therefore, dewatering of the excavation may involve 
moderate volumes of water. Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of 
the excavations is generally not recommended. The resultant areal depression of the natural 
groundwater table could induce consolidation of the existing in-situ soils resulting in a potential 
ground settlement that could affect nearby structures. The potential impact of the dewatering

It is our opinion that a cut-off wall system, such as interlocking continuous steel sheet

The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural 
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering. It is 
recommended that the contractor retain a qualified geotechnical engineer to design and

The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole responsibility 
of the contractor. Its qualified representative, who should be continuously present on-site 
during dewatering activities, will have the best opportunity to promptly observe the effects 
of dewatering during construction and to implement, as soon as possible, necessary 
precautionary or remedial measures including, but not limited to, slowing down or stopping 
the dewatering operations. 
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations, permeable granular subsoils may be 
susceptible to piping and "quick" conditions. The dewatering operations should be carried 
out without creating a "quick" condition or softening at the excavation bottoms. Therefore, 
the project dewatering operations should be performed without pumping out soil fines

3.6 
Backfilling of Wet Well Excavation 
In general, free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), should be used for the initial backfill up to about 12 inches above the groundwater 
level in the excavation. The excavated on-site granular soils may be used as backfill above the 
free-draining gravel up to the finished subgrade. 
The soil backfill above the No. 3B Fine gravel should be placed in minimum 8 inches in 
loose lift thickness, with each lift mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction. Within the roadway areas, the top 3 feet below the finished pavement grade should 
be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to 
the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the 
same soil determined in accordance with ASTM D1557.

In general, the subgrades in cut areas and areas designated to receive fills should be scarified 
to a depth of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of

 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the on-site soils encountered during our field exploration should be suitable for 
use as general fill materials provided that the maximum particle size is less than 3 inches in 
largest dimension and free of deleterious material, vegetation, clay lumps, and rocks. 
Imported materials should consist of non-expansive select granular material, such as 
crushed coral or basalt. The select granular fill should be well-graded from coarse to fine 
with particles no larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension. The material should have a 
California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less 
when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Imported fill materials should be tested for 
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the 
intended use. 
Where groundwater is encountered (within the excavations), backfill materials should 
consist of free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), wrapped on all sides with a non-woven filter fabric. The free-draining granular 
materials should be used up to a level of about 12 inches above the groundwater level to 
facilitate the compaction of the fill materials. The free-draining granular fill materials should 
be used in a confined condition. Alternatively, lean concrete may be used as backfill material.

least 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction requirement for the upper 3 feet of

Corrosion Potential 
Design of metallic substructures, such as waterline pipes or other metallic piping, should 
consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. A set of laboratory 
corrosion tests, including pH and minimum resistivity, were performed by our office to evaluate 
the corrosivity of the on-site soils encountered. The test results are provided in the Geotechnical 
Data Report. 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics with 
regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the resistivity, 
the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure will have. Conversely, the 
higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion of metallic objects. 
Results of the resistivity testing indicate that the on-site soils have a resistivity of about 720 ohm-
cm with pH value of 7.8. Therefore, the on-site near-surface soils may be considered “Extremely 
Corrosive” based on the Board of Water Supply, City and County of Honolulu Water System 
External Corrosion Control Standards dated 1991. 
In addition, chloride content and sulfate content were performed by Eurofins to evaluate 
the corrosivity of the on-site soils encountered. Based on the chloride and sulfate content tests
