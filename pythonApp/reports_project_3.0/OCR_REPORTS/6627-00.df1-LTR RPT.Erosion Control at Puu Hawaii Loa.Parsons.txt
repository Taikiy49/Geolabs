PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is situated on the lower slopes of Puu Hawaii Loa within the Marine 
Corps Base Hawaii in Kaneohe on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project location and 
general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
The family housing at the site was developed previously by others.  The upper row of 
homes is separated from the upper slopes of Puu Hawaii Loa by a Keystone™ retaining wall.  
At several locations behind about 10 buildings, water from the upper slopes has overtopped 
the wall, carrying sediment into the yards below.  There is no interceptor ditch behind the 
wall. 
There are some locations above the wall where exposed silty soils have eroded and 
are continuing to erode.  These eroded slopes are in need of repair and installation of erosion 
control measures. 
It was desired to conduct a reconnaissance of the slope area and to provide design 
recommendations pertaining to erosion control.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our services were generally limited to reconnaissance, meeting and consultation, 
and preparation of this letter report.  This letter report summarizes our findings resulting 
from our reconnaissance and engineering analyses. The findings presented herein are 
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
Reconnaissance of the project site indicates that the native soils consist of lean silt 
with some fine sand derived from the in-situ weathering of volcanic cinder. 
Interceptor Ditches 
It is understood that interceptor ditches will be installed behind and above the 
existing Keystone™ retaining walls.  It is further understood that it is planned to use 
SmartDitch™ HDPE liners for the interceptor ditches.  The liners should be installed in 
accordance with the manufacturer’s requirements.  Caution should be exercised in the 
installation of the liners to ensure that the liners are installed tightly to the soil subgrade 
to reduce the potential for accumulation of water behind the liners.  Caution is also 
needed to ensure that excavation for installation of the ditch does not disturb the 
existing grids or tendons for the Keystone wall.  Measures should be taken to ensure 
that surface water does not flow behind the upper lip of the liners so that water does not 
enter and cause erosion or uplift.

Eroded Slope Restoration 
Portions of the slopes above the existing retaining wall have become denuded of 
vegetation and the native silty soils have eroded.  It is our recommendation to restore 
these slopes and to implement erosion control measures. 
The slope restoration should generally consist of regrading the eroded areas to 
match the surrounding slopes.  In deeply eroded areas, the slope may be restored by 
backfilling with native material.  Compaction may be achieved by wheel rolling with 
relatively heavy equipment.  The material should be placed moist of optimum moisture 
content. 
Erosion control measures should include but not be limited to the installation of a 
biodegradable erosion control blanket, such as the North American Green C125BN™ or 
similar.  These measures should also be implemented where the ground has been 
cleared for the installation of the ditch.  The lower edge of the blanket should overlap 
the upper lip of the ditch liner to serve as a wick and to reduce the potential of water 
infiltrating under the liner. 
Consideration should be given to installing interceptor ditches above the restored 
slope areas. 
Site Grading 
Based on the existing terrain at the project site, we anticipate that site grading 
work will generally consist of shallow cuts and fills.  
Site Preparation 
In general, the areas within the contract grading limits should be cleared and 
grubbed thoroughly at the on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious 
material, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly 
off-site or disposed in a designated area to reduce the potential for contaminating 
the excavated materials. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during the clearing and grubbing should be 
over-excavated to expose firm natural material, and the resulting excavation should 
be backfilled with well-compacted engineered fill. In general, the excavated soft and 
wet soils may not be re-used as a source of fill and backfill materials. 
After clearing and grubbing, the existing ground surface in areas designated to 
receive fill or to remain at grade should be scarified to a depth of 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum 
dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557.

Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to

Where shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we 
recommend scarifying the soils and preparing again as recommended above. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and 
replacement with well-compacted fill. 
Fill and Backfill Materials 
In general, the excavated on-site materials may be used as a source of general fill 
materials provided that they are free of deleterious materials.  
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.  Compaction should be 
accomplished using sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other types of 
acceptable compaction equipment. 
Cut and Fill Slopes 
In general, cut slopes and permanent fill slopes constructed of the on-site soils may 
be designed with a slope inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. Fills placed on slopes 
steeper than 5H:1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide 
stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling operations should start at the 
lowest point and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance 
with the above fill placement recommendations. 
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope 
ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. In the event that over-cutting of a slope 
occurs, keying and benching requirements should be implemented instead of 
backfilling the slope to the design grade with sliver fills. Water should be diverted 
away from the tops of slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as 
possible to reduce the potential for erosion of the finished slopes.

Excavation 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by stiff to very 
stiff silty soils.  We anticipate that the near-surface materials may be excavated with 
normal heavy excavation equipment. 
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the materials are based on our 
visual observation of the existing site conditions.  
LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from field reconnaissance.  Variations of soil conditions may occur, 
and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is 
underway. If variations then appear evident, Geolabs should be contacted to re-evaluate 
the findings presented herein.

principles and practices. No warranty is expressed or implied. 
The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated soil conditions are commonly 
encountered. Unforeseen soil conditions, such as perched groundwater, soft deposits, 
hard layers or cavities, may occur in localized areas and may require additional probing or 
corrections in the field (which may result in construction delays) to attain a properly 
constructed project. Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund is recommended to 
accommodate these possible extra costs. 
This geotechnical engineering reconnaissance conducted at the project site was
