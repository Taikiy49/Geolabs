1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Kapa’a Bypass Road project in the Kapa’a area on the Island of 
Kaua’i, Hawai’i. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses. These recommendations are intended for the design of remedial measures 
for the roadway distresses. The findings and recommendations presented herein are 
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located approximately 300 feet north of the roundabout at 
Olohena Road along the one-way portion of Kapa’a Bypass Road. The segment of the 
roadway for the project consists of a single-lane asphaltic concrete paved road with the 
direction of traffic traveling from north to south. The existing roadway is elevated above 
the natural grade by approximately 5 to 7 feet, with slopes adjacent to the shoulder on 
both sides. 
Based on our field observations, numerous distresses were noted on the surface 
of the existing roadway pavement.  The observed distresses consisted of fatigue and 
block cracking, potholes, heavy asphalt patches, rutting, and pumping of the subgrade. 
Additionally, standing water was noted along the base of the elevated embankment 
during heavy rain events. 
We understand that it is desired to determine the potential cause of pavement 
distress and develop remedial measures.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kaua’i is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano 
built by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series beginning about 
5 to 6 million years ago. The eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series ended about 
2½ million years ago and was followed by a long period of erosion. Following the 
cessation of this main volcano shield-building phase about 1½ million years ago, there 
was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the post-erosional 
Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the thick alluvial sediments of 
the Palikea Formation. 
Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as 
thick lava flows composed of dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in 
north-south trends at various locales. Associated with the Koloa Volcanic Series lava 
flows are some deposits of pyroclastic materials (volcanic ash and cinders), which 
usually form cinder cones surrounding the vent. Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic 
Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of Kaua’i, including the project site. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, many sea level changes occurred as a result of 
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great continental 
glaciers accumulated, the level of the ocean fell since less water was available to fill the 
oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global sea levels rose 
because more water was available. The landmass of Kaua’i remained essentially stable 
during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea, which were both higher and 
lower relative to the present sea level of the Island of Kaua’i. 
The project site appears to be in an old amphitheater-headed valley eroded into 
the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series that was subsequently filled with Koloa rocks and 
Palikea sediments.

Design Pavement Section 
Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicate that the roadway alignment 
under consideration for this project is generally underlain by clayey silt with high in-situ 
moisture content. As a result, the subgrade may not be able to compact into a dense 
mass to receive placement and compaction of the aggregate base course layer. In

pavement structural section connected to a subdrain system.

consisting of 24 inches of No. 2 Coarse aggregate (ASTM C33, No. 4 Gradation) 
wrapped in a permeable separator (Mirafi 180N or equivalent).

Due to the high in-situ moisture content of the existing on-site soils, we believe 
that the common compaction requirement of 95 percent relative compaction for the 
subgrade soils may be impractical and should be reduced. Based on our laboratory test 
results and engineering analyses, the subgrade soils under the pavement areas should 
be proof-rolled without the use of vibration to provide a subgrade with a minimum of 
85 percent relative compaction. Use of vibration during proof-rolling will induce a 
pumping condition of the subgrade soils with high in-situ moisture. Based on the 
available compaction tests performed, we believe that the 85 percent relative 
compaction may be achieved with some difficulty. Relative compaction refers to the in-
place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the 
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. Some 
drying of the subgrade soils should be anticipated for the project. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation process 
and to confirm whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar to those 
encountered in our field exploration.

Drainage and Stabilization Layer 
Due to the high in-situ moisture content of the existing on-site soils and the 
proximity of the water table, we believe a roadway stabilization layer consisting of a 
drainage layer should be installed below the new pavement structural section connected 
to a subdrain system. 
For the design pavement section, the roadway stabilization layer should consist 
of 24 inches of No. 2 Coarse aggregate (ASTM C33, No. 4 Gradation) wrapped in a 
permeable separator (Mirafi 180N or equivalent). The coarse aggregate should be 
placed in lifts no greater than 12 inches in thickness and proof-rolled with a large 
smooth drum roller (minimum 10-ton static weight) for a minimum of four passes without 
vibratory action.

For the alternate flexible pavement design section, the roadway stabilization

protect it from damage and reduction in drainage performance. 
Because of the reduced subgrade compaction requirement and the high in-situ 
moisture content, we believe that it would be difficult to obtain a compaction 
requirement of typically 95 percent for the first 9-inch layer of the aggregate base 
course placed over the pavement subgrades called for in the alternate flexible 
pavement design section. Therefore, we recommend compacting the first layer of 
aggregate base course placed above the pavement subgrades to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction.

Compaction Requirements 
In general, fill and backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  Due to the 
high natural water content of the subgrade soils, the required compaction for the 
roadway subgrade was reduced to 85 percent relative compaction. 
Aggregate base course materials required for the project should consist of 
crushed basaltic aggregates with a 1.5-inch maximum nominal size and should conform 
to Subsection 703.06 of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and 
Bridge Construction (2005). Aggregate base course should be moisture-conditioned to

Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a

3.10 Subdrain System 
A subdrain system should be installed for the roadway drainage system to collect 
the subsurface seepage water and to allow for subsurface drainage of the pavement 
section. We recommend using a 6-inch diameter perforated PVC pipe for the subdrain 
system surrounded by No. 3B Fine graded aggregate (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation). 
The 6-inch diameter PVC pipes should be installed with a minimum slope of 
0.5 percent.  
We envision that the stabilization drainage layer would be contained by the 
existing embankment. We do not recommend daylighting the stabilization drainage layer 
to the slope face as the stabilization drainage layer material would not be stable over 
time due to the material being poorly graded. 
The geocomposite drainage product can be daylighted at the embankment slope 
face.  Reconstruction of the embankment beyond the pavement section will be required 
to restore the sides of the embankment after the geocomposite drainage product 
installation.  Alternately, the geocomposite drainage product may be terminated and 
drained into a subdrain system. 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface 
should be sloped to drain, and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to a depth of about 21.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. In addition, one bulk sample of the near-surface 
soils, designated as Bulk-1, was obtained to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig with continuous flight augers.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550 Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test results is 
provided on Plate B-1. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Two One-Inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-3. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. Results of the direct 
shear test are presented on Plate B-4. 
One Triaxial Unconsolidated Undrained Compression test (ASTM D2850) was 
performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the 
in-situ soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used as the 
applied confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test results 
and the stress-strain curve are presented on Plate B-5. 
One Unconfined Compression test (ASTM D2166) was performed on a selected 
soil sample to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the soil.  The test results 
are presented on Plate B-6. 
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a 
bulk sample of the near-surface soils at the in-situ moisture content to evaluate the 
pavement support characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-7. 
One laboratory Resistance (R) Value test (ASTM D2844) was performed on a 
selected bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-8.
