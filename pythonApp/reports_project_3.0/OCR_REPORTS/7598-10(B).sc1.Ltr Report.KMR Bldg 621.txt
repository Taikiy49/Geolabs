Dear Ms. Miyasato: 
This letter report presents the results of our permeability testing conducted for 
the Hawaii Department of Defense Keaukaha Military Reservation Building 621, 
Underground Injection Control (UIC) project in the Hilo airport area on the Island of 
Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location 
Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes our field observations and presents the results of our 
permeability testing and engineering analyses for the project.  These findings are 
intended to support the preparation of a UIC Injection Well Status Report for Injection 
Well Nos. 1 and 2 at the project site. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
Based on the information provided, the Hawaii Department of Defense, Building 
621 facility at the Keaukaha Military Reservation (KMR) operates two injection wells, 
identified as Well Nos. 1 and 2, for the on-site disposal of septic effluent. The KMR 
Building 621 facility is identified by UIC Facility I.D. No. 8-4203.19. Injection Well Nos. 1 
and 2 at the KMR Building 621 facility is regulated by UIC Permit No. UH-2509.  
We understand that observation and permeability testing services are desired in 
support of the preparation of a UIC Injection Well Status Report for Well Nos. 1 and 2 at 
the Hawaii Army National Guard Battery Building 621 facility at the Keaukaha Military 
Reservation in Hilo, Hawaii.

PURPOSE AND SCOPE 
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering services was to obtain an overview 
of the existing condition of the injection wells by visual observation and short-term 
permeability injection testing in support of a UIC Injection Well Status Report prepared 
by others for the project. Generally, the scope of work included the following tasks: 
1. 
Review of available documentations pertaining to the UIC permit 
application. 
2. 
Mobilization of a water truck with one operator to the project site. 
3. 
Performance of short-term duration permeability tests to evaluate the 
disposal capacity of the existing wells. 
4. 
Analysis of the field data by our engineer. 
5. 
Preparation of this letter report summarizing our work on the project and 
presenting our findings and recommendations. 
6. 
Coordination of our overall work on the project by our geologist. 
7. 
Quality assurance of our work and client/design team consultation by our 
principal engineer. 
8. 
Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting, word processing and clerical 
support. 
EXISTING WELL AND SITE CONDITIONS 
The location of Well Nos. 1 and 2 is within the Keaukaha Military Reservation 
adjacent to Building 621, as approximately indicated on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1. Well No. 1 is on the southerly side of Building 621 and serves the women’s 
latrine and septic tank system. Well No. 2 is on the northerly side of Building 621 and 
serves the men’s latrine and septic tank system.  
Our senior engineer conducted the inspection and testing of Well Nos. 1 and 2 
on November 7, 2018. The well covers had been removed for us prior to our site visit. 
Photograph Nos. 1 and 2 show the existing conditions at the Well No. 1 well head and 
vicinity. Photograph Nos. 5 and 6 show the existing conditions at the Well No. 2 well 
head and vicinity. 
Both well heads are sited on generally level ground with lawn grass surrounding 
the well heads and vicinity. We observed that the lawn surface surrounding the well 
heads was treated with weed killer and the ground surface contained no ponded water 
accumulation. The surface completions of both wells consist of a steel manhole cover 
set flush with the ground surface. Below the manhole cover, access to the well is via a

Well No. 1 
Well No. 1 is on the southerly side of Building 621 in an open lawn area adjacent 
to the building. The well head is on generally firm level ground as shown in Photograph 
Nos. 1 and 2. 
The well head is generally constructed flush with the existing lawn surface and 
consists of a steel manhole cover at the ground surface with a recessed access port 
consisting of a concrete plug cover measuring about 16 inches square. We observed 
that the concrete plug cover seats firmly into the concrete well head surface with 
minimal gap space. Photograph No. 3 shows the existing condition of the concrete plug 
cover for Well No. 1. 
We observed steel manhole covers upstream from the Well No. 1 well head that 
house access ports associated with the septic tank. No other existing above-ground 
appurtenances were observed associated with the well operation. 
With the well cover open, we sensed a mild to moderate sewage odor emanating 
from the well. The existing fluid level in the well was measured at 20.0 feet. We 
measured the existing depth of the well at about 22.1 feet below the existing ground 
surface. Therefore, there was an approximate 2.1-foot accumulation of fluid in the well 
prior to our testing. The accumulated fluid contained in the well appeared relatively 
clear. The rough and irregular floor of the well appeared to contain some limited 
accumulation of a dark colored silty sand material. Some limited organic matter and 
debris was observed on the well floor. 
We observed a 6-inch diameter PVC effluent discharge pipe entering the well 
sidewall at about a 4-foot depth below the ground surface. No discharge fluid was 
observed emanating from the pipe during our inspection and testing. 
We observed that the circular-lined well diameter ranges from about 8.8 to 
9.4 feet in the upper 11 feet of the well depth. Below about 11 feet in well depth, the well 
diameter appeared to taper to about 8 feet in diameter extending toward the well 
bottom. 
We observed that the well interior is lined to a depth of about 7 feet below the 
ground surface with grouted rock rubble generally consisting of hard basaltic cobbles 
and boulders containing some interstitial gaps between the rocks comprising the lining. 
Below about 7 feet in depth and extending toward the well floor, the well interior 
appears to be unlined and constructed in native hard basaltic rock condition. 
Photograph No. 4 shows the general condition of the Well No. 1 interior.

PERMEABILITY TESTING 
Our evaluation of the well permeability characteristics was conducted on 
November 7, 2018 between the hours of about 0900 and 1430 under generally dry 
weather and ground surface conditions. 
Permeability testing for Well Nos. 1 and 2 generally involved the short-term 
gravity injection of clear water from a water tanker truck with measurement of the 
increasing head, followed by measurement of the falling head in the well until the well 
drained to the pre-test condition or to the maximum 3 hours of measurements. The 
permeability testing was conducted in an effort to evaluate the drainage (infiltration) 
functionality of the existing wells. 
Prior to the start of each of the two permeability tests, no septic effluent was 
observed discharging into the well. The tests were conducted by discharging 
approximately 2,000 and 1,800 gallons of water from a water tank under gravity feed 
through a 2-inch diameter hose placed in the lower portion of the well at Well Nos. 1 
and 2, respectively.  
It should be noted that Well No. 1 contained a static head of about 26 inches in 
depth of fluid prior to the start of the test. In contrast, Well No. 2 contained a small 
amount of fluid (about 2 inches) prior to the start of the test. No discharge effluent from 
the septic system was received at the wells during the testing of both wells. 
Well No. 1 
Based on our measurements, approximately 2,000 gallons of clear water was 
discharged to the well from the water tanker over a period of about 40 minutes, resulting 
in an average discharge flow rate of about 50 gallons per minute (gpm). The total 
accumulated head was measured at 4.8 feet above the bottom of the well at the end of 
the short-term injection portion of the test.

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the results of our visual observations and the short-term permeability 
testing of Well Nos. 1 and 2 at the KMR Building 621 facility, the wells appear to be 
operating satisfactorily with no overt indication of potential failure. However, based on 
the testing, Well No. 1 appears to have a notable lower disposal capacity than Well 
No. 2.  
R.M. Towill Corporation 
Page 10 
W.O. 7598-10(B) 
December 3, 2018 
GEOLABS, INC. 
Hawaii • California 
Based on the falling head permeability testing conducted, Well No. 1 has an 
effluent capacity of about 9 gpm, equivalent to about 12,960 gallons per day (gpd), 
which exceeds the estimated design flow capacity of 3,000 gpd. A graph showing the 
head versus time relation is presented on Plate A-1 in Appendix A. 
Based on the falling head permeability testing conducted, the Well No. 2 has an 
effluent capacity of at least 250 gpm, equivalent to about 360,000 gpd, which exceeds 
the estimated design flow capacity of 3,000 gpd. A graph showing the head versus time 
relation is presented on Plate A-2 in Appendix A.  
Based on our observation of the existing well site and vicinity conditions, we 
recommend the following to maintain the performance of the wells. 
Perform routine inspection of the fluid levels contained in the septic tank to 
gauge when servicing may be needed and schedule servicing to include 
the pumping and cleaning of the septic tank system on a regular basis. 
Periodically exercise any upstream flow control valves to ensure operation 
and functionality. 
Continue to conduct annual injection well inspections as per the UIC 
permit conditions. 
Review and abide by the best practice guidelines for effluent discharge 
that was provided in the Environmental Notice dated December 2016 and 
provided to the facility operator by the State of Hawaii Department of 
Health, Safe Drinking Water Branch, Underground Injection Control (UIC) 
Program.
