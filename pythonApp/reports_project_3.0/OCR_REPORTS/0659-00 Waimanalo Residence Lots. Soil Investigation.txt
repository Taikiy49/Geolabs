INTRODUCTION
This report presents the findings of our soil investigation
at the subject site and provides recommendations for the
design and construction of the proposed single family housing
development

W es
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located in the upper Waimanalo area of
southeast Oahu Hawaii
The south Koolau Mountain Range runs along the southern
boundary of the site with the Forest Reserve Conservation
Boundary Line coinciding with this southern boundary To
the north and below the project site are the existing units
of the Hawaiian Home Lands Waimanalo Residence Lots Fifth
Series Additional undeveloped Hawaiian Home Lands border
the site on the west and the east The adjacent land west
of the site is currently cultivated for vegetable farming
The site slopes down to the north from about 30 to 14 percent
at the site area above elevation 110 portion Flatter slopes
of about 10 percent exist in the lower portion of the site
below elevation 110 Site topography varies from about ele
vation 70 along the northern boundary up to elevation 150 at
the upper southern boundary
A concrete flume which is about 3 feet deep and 2 feet wide
runs along the mid slope of the site at about elevation 110
and acts as a demarcation between the flatter lower area
and the steeper upper area North of the concrete ditch
the surface vegetation is moderately dense The new growth
of Koa trees in the flatter Slope area indicated the site
has been cleared for cane cultivation Only a few tall trees
and moderate Koa growth are noted in this lower area
GEOLABS HAWAII
W 3 o
Above the ditch the site is covered with dense vegetation
with trees up to 50 feet high and thicker Koa and bush
growth
Gullies typical of the windward side of the island traverse
the site Two major gullies are located in the project vici
nity
The largest gully is located along the western border and it
varies from 10 to 20 feet deep Steep banks are typical for
this gully
The other major gully is located in the upper east portion
of the site It is 15 to 20 feet deep at the upper area
close to the boundary It disappears at around elevation 150
Minor gullies from 2 to 10 feet deep are situated throughout
the other parts of the project area mainly in the upper regions
above the concrete flume
Boulders found at or near the surface were more abundant in
the eastern half of the site mainly in the higher elevations
The largest concentration was found around test pits

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
During the Late Tertiary Period and early Pleistocene Epoch
the Koolau basaltic lava erupted from the eastern portion
of the island of Oahu These flows injected many dense
upward dike complexes
At the Waimanalo stand of the ocean a large coral and marine
deposits were laid on top of these lava flows
Subsequent erosion and weathering reworked large portion of
the Koolau Range forming the Pali Cliffs The flanks of the
ridges were filled with talus debris Continued weathering
resulted in a younger less consolidated alluvium on the
present slope surface
The project site geology is essentially of the ridge edge
talus condition with a thin layer 3 to 5 feet of younger
Adobe clay alluvium on top of a thicker and consolidated
older alluvium From elevation 200 and upward weathered
basalt and basalt rock formation could be encountered with
little or no talus Below this the thickness of the older
GEOLABS HAWAII
W a5 ie
alluvium increases greatly as the slope flattens out toward
the sea At the lower elevation of the project site the
older alluvium may well be over one hundred feet thick
Subsurface Exploration
The subsurface conditions of the project site were explored
by 25 test pits and 3 borings The test pits were excavated
with a Caterpillar 225 track mounted hydraulic backhoe The
borings were drilled with a truck mounted drill rig The
field work was performed during the month of February 1977
The test pit and boring locations are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 1 A detailed description of the field exploration
method and accompanying laboratory testing is given in the
appendix along with the logs of borings and test pits
Soil Conditions
The subsurface exploration disclosed a younger alluvium of
dark brown silty clay to Adobe clay on the site slope
generally from 1 to 4 feet thick below the slope surface
elevations This younger alluvial layer increases to 6 to
8 feet thick around test pits 17 and 19 In the locale of
test pits 22 and 23 about 6 to 10 feet thick of grayish
sandy and clayey silt was noted in the surface layer
Generally in the surface layer gravel cobble and boulder
content consisted of 10 to 40 percent by volume of the total
the percentage of the boulder content increases to about 50
to 60

Two feet of tan coral sand fill were found at the surface
in test pit 25 above the Adobe clay layer Loose cobbles
and boulders were found in the younger alluvial formation in
tests pits and 23
Beneath the surface layer is an older alluvium consisting
of lighter mottled gray tan and brown silty clay soils
which extends down to the depth of the test pit Gravel
cobble and boulder content in the underlying alluvium soil
range from 20 to 80 percent being more rocky in the upper
eastern and central portions of the site
In test pits 16 and 17 highly weathered basalt
layer was found beneath the alluvium layer
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
It is our opinion that the site is suitable for the proposed
single family residences as currently planned and as shown
on the attached site plan Plate 1 provided the project
design and construction incorporate the recommendations
presented in this report
The results of our stability analyses indicate that the exis
ting slopes within the proposed development area have a factor
of safety greater than 1 5 against sliding
GEOLABS HAWAII
W e7
In general the site is blanketed with younger alluvium clay
soils about 1 to 4 feet thick The surface clayey soil is
highly expansive and has very low shearing strength when
saturated Therefore cesspool construction is not recom
mended under these types of topography and soil conditions
In addition the Adobe clayey soils will have poor per
colation rate
Close monitoring and inspection during site development and
house construction are essential for future project perfor
mance because of the potential of surface soil creep
Slope Stability
The currently planned development will mainly occupy the
area below elevation 130 feet
The steepest site slope of 30 percent is located west of
TP 12 at the eastern end of the project site Based on
the results of our field study and laboratory testing
the slope stability analyses for the 30 percent slope area
indicate a factor of safety greater than 1 5 against sliding
This factor of safety in our opinion is an acceptable one
for the proposed development
Steeper slopes exist mauka and outside of the development site
The result of our stability analysis indicates that the factor
of safety for this area ranges from 1 1 to 1 5 However no
future development is anticipated in this area and we recom
mend that this upper area should remain at its present undisturbed
condition
GEOLABS HAWAII
W 8
No evidence of existing slope instability was noted at
the proposed project site during this investigation
Site Grading
Due to the low shear strength soils at the surface layer
cuts and fills should be minimized
Before the grading operation the site should be cleared of
all deleterious and organic matter and removed from the site
In areas to receive fill the ground should be scarified a
minimum depth of 12 inches In areas where desiccation
cracks extend to further depths the scarification will have
to extend to the depth of the desiccation cracks The scari
fied surface shall then be brought to near optimum water con
tent and compacted to a minimum of 90 of the laboratory
maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D1557
Fill shall then be placed in horizontal 9 inch maximum loose
lifts and also compacted to a minimum of 90 of maximum den
sity Where the natural ground slope is 5 horizontal to 1
vertical 20 percent or steeper the fill material should
be keyed and benched into the firm soil as approved by the
soils engineer
Due to the expansive nature of the on site clay soils a
relative compaction of 85 of maximum density as determined
by ASTM Designation D1557 may be desirable if they are used
for fill material The moisture content of the fill soils
GEOLABS HAWAII
W 9
should be at least 2 above the optimum moisture content
during compaction All other materials should be compacted
to a minimum of 90 All imported fill should be non expan
sive granular soils and approved by the soils engineer prior
to their use in fill areas
Over the existing gullies loose boulders of 6 inches and
greater in diameter present within 5 feet below the proposed
rough grade should be removed before new fill operation In
the gully areas where existing surface boulders are over 5
feet below final fill grade granular material should be used
to fill the voids between boulders The initial boulder fill
should be thoroughly watered and tamped before continuing con
struction of other portions of the overlying fill
To minimize underground erosion and piping aceon on new fill
over the gully areas a subdrain system consisting of perforated
pipe and granular filter material around the pipe should be pro
vided at major gully stream bed location where the fill is more
than 5 feet in height
We recommend that all grading operations including rock filling
operations should be monitored and performed under the obser
vation of the soils engineer

Foundation
For the highly expansive surface clay soils post and
beam construction is recommended Spread and wall footing
foundation may be utilized if they are bearing on the
underlying older alluvium or on properly compacted fill
consisting of the on site older alluvium soil or low to
non expansive import soil
For the light structural load anticipated on this project
an allowable bearing pressure of 2 000 p s f may be used
for the footings bearing on the on site soil or upon the
compacted fill The bearing value may be increased by
one third when considering wind and seismic loads
All footings should be founded a minimum of 24 inches below
the final adjacent grade Continuous footings should be
reinforced with a minimum of two No 4 reinforcing bars
placed at the top and the bottom of the footings
GEOLABS HAWAII
W
Continuous footings should have a minimum width of 12 inches
A minimum set back distance of 5 feet should be maintained
from the outer edge of the footing measured horizontally
to the slope face
The backfill immediately against the retaining wall should be
an open graded compacted granular material Weepholes or
a subsurface drainage system should be provided to prevent
the build up of excessive pore pressures behind the wall
Lateral Pressures
It is our opinion that retaining walls parallel to contours
should be avoided wherever possible However if they are
absolutely required it is recommended that an equivalent
fluid pressure of 100 pounds per square foot be used for
the walls in the upper slope area For the area below
elevation 110 the equivalent fluid density may be reduced
to 65 pounds per cubic foot
Retaining walls acting perpendicular to the existing contours
may be designed to resist lateral loads with an equivalent
fluid having a density of 55 pounds per cubic foot Passive
earth pressures may be designed as an equivalent fluid having
a density of 250 pounds per cubic foot
An allowable coefficient of friction between soil and concrete
of 0 35 may be used When combining passive earth pressure
and friction for lateral resistance the passive component
should be reduced by one third

Slab On Grade
Slab on grade construction should be avoided on the Adobe
surface clay layer
If it is necessary to use slab on grade they should be located
along the makai boundary area where site slopes are level One
of the following two alternatives may be incorporated One al
ternative is to construct 2 feet of non expansive capping material
beneath the slab The other is to utilize deeper perimeter footings
designed for high expansive soils For construction details in
these conditions refer to Plates 6 and 7 The on site gray
clayey surface soil is considered as high expansion potential
soil The underlying older alluvium soil is considered as
moderate to high expansion potential soil
Garage Area
Flexible A C pavement is highly recommended for the garage pave
ment where subgrade soil is expansive
If concrete garage pavement is used it shall be separated from
the building foundation or the slab on grade Six inches of
compacted base course material should be provided on the mois
tened clayey subgrade soil Slabs should be reinforced with
wire mesh to minimize cracking

Drainage
The on site surface soil is highly expansive Water should
not be allowed to pond adjacent to foundations and on the
top of the slopes The site should be graded to provide
positive drainage away from all structures and prevent any
runoff over the slopes
Utility Line
Due to the moisture sensitivity of the on site clay soils
it is recommended that flexible joints be used on all utility
pipes Long reaches of lines should be if possible perpen
dicular to the contours
Granular material should be used to backfill around the pipe
in utility trench excavation Positive drainage outlets should
be provided in the utility trenches to bleed out potential ex
cessive amount of water
Rock Disposal
If necessary and if approved by the soils engineer during con
struction boulders and rock larger than 8 inches in diameter
can be windrowed in the following manner and as illustrated on
Plate 8
The windrows should not be over 5 feet in width and not closer
than 10 feet between windrows The height of the windrow should
not exceed 4 feet The maximum size of the rock acceptable within
the windrows should not exceed 4 feet in the greatest dimension
Rock over this size should be broken down or disposed of in
areas other than the fill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W 16
The resultant rock configurations in the windrows will offer
considerable void space between the rocks Granular coral
material should be used for fill along and over the rock
windrows thoroughly watered and consolidated to insure
that all void spaces between the rocks are filled with
coral sand soil
Successive lifts of windrows may be made if the fill is
sufficiently deep However the windrows should be stag
gered horizontally and have a vertical distance of at least
2 feet between the top of the lower windrow and the bottom
of the next windrow Rock should not be placed within 10
feet of the finished pad grades or above the lowest utility
lines in the roadway All boulder disposal should be done
under the constant observation of the soils engineer to
assure that these recommendations are followed
Upper Interceptor Ditch
It is planned to construct a series of interceptor ditches
along the upper boundaries The ditch can be utilized partly
as a boulder catchment for the lower ee The existing dense
vegetation and trees on the slope surface provide additional
source of boulder catchment and that their clearing and damage
should be minimized
Provision to minimize water and moisture infiltration to
saturate the underlying soils should be considered in the
selection of the type of ditch lining construction
GEOLABS HAWAII
W 17
Due to the potential of down slope movement and the highly
expansive on site surface soil flexible lining such as
butyl rubber sheeting should be provided in the ditch
construction
The interceptor ditch could also provide an additional
boulder disposal area Large sized boulders could be
placed on the banks of the upper ditch Positive drain
age should be provided to divert water from this area
Design Review
The plans for foundations lot grading and utility con
struction should be forwarded to the soils engineer for
review and comments prior to construction to assure that
the intent of our recommendations is included in the pro
ject plans and specifications
Additional slope stability analyses would be required for
major cut and fill areas after the overall site grading
has been formulated to determine if special constructional
methods are required
Construction Observation
To a degree the performance of any structure is dependent
upon construction procedures and quality Hence fill placement
inspection of cut slopes and foundation excavations should
be carried out by the soils engineer to permit correlation

The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by digging 25 test
pits with a Caterpillar 225 backhoe and by drilling 3 borings utilizing truck
mounted drilling equipment The depths of the borings were terminated at
14 5 to 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface and the test pits were
terminated at 7 5 to 20 0 feet below the existing ground surface The loca
tions of the test pits and borings are shown on the enclosed Site Plan Plate 1
The soils were classified by visual and textural examination in the field
by our engineer who continuously supervised the test pit excavation and drilling
operations The classification were verified by visual inspection and testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in accordance with the Unified Soil
Classification System A graphic presentation of the soils is presented on
the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 3 A summary of the soils encountered in
the test pits and laboratory test data is presented on the Test Pit Logs
In the borings samples were obtained by driving either a standard pene
tration or a 2 4 inch I D split barrel sampler with a 140 pound hammer free
falling a distance of 30 inches The blow counts to drive the sampler the last
12 inches are shown on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depth
Disturbed samples were taken from the test pits at different depth during exca

and 1A and also shown graphically on Plate B 1
Two swell tests of one inch ring samples were conducted on on site soil
samples for measuring the swell potential The swell test results are sum
marized on Plate B 2

tests were performed on bulk samples of the on site soil to evaluate the suita
bility of the soil as fill and pavement support The results are given on Table 1
and 1A
Seven direct shear tests were performed on selected undisturbed and remolded
ring samples to evaluate the strength characteristics of the soils The soils
in the remolded ring samples were compacted to 90 of its maximum density before
being tested Direct shear tests were run at strain rates of less than 0 05 inch
per minute and the samples were submerged in water during the test The test
results are shown on Plates B 3 through B 9
