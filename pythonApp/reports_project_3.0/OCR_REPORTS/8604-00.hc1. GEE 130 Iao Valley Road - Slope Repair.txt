SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the drilled borings encountered about 8 inches of 
asphaltic concrete underlain by alluvium extending to the maximum depth explored of 
about 26.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The alluvium encountered generally 
consisted of medium stiff to hard sandy clay, silty clay, clayey silt and sandy silt, and loose 
to very dense silty sand and silty gravel with cobbles and boulders. Groundwater was not 
encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. It should be noted 
that groundwater levels encountered in the drilled borings are subject to change due to 
rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
We understand that it is desired to replace the existing failing stacked rock retaining 
wall with a soil nailed retaining wall system to reduce the amount of road closure for the 
proposed project. In general, a soil nailed retaining wall system consists of a series of 
individual reinforcing bars grouted into drilled holes to stabilize an excavation or slope. A 
shotcrete facing is generally applied to the face of the wall or slope, providing the 
appearance of a conventional concrete retaining wall.  
The soil nails should be installed by drilling a minimum 5.5-inch diameter hole with 
an inclination of approximately 15 degrees from horizontal. The inclination of the nails may 
be increased to avoid existing underground structures. The soil nail bar should consist of 
an ASTM A615 Grade 75 threaded bar with a minimum bar diameter of 1 inch.  
Based on our soil nail analysis, we recommend using design nail lengths of 10 feet 
extending into the stiff alluvial soil encountered in our field exploration. The soil nails 
should be spaced 3 feet on-center vertically and 5 feet on-center horizontally. The first nail 
should be installed about 2 feet below the top of the slope. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and 
recommendations.

1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the “130 Iao Valley Road, Guardrail and Wall Replacement, Wailuku, 
Maui, Hawaii” project. The project locations and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses. These recommendations are only intended for the design of a wall 
replacement. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located at 130 Iao Valley Road about 1,300 feet west of the 
intersection of Main Street and Iao Valley Road in Wailuku on the Island of Maui, 
Hawaii. We understand that the existing stacked rock retaining wall supporting the 
roadway is failing, and the existing guardrail is damaged with tilted guardrail posts. 
Concrete Jersey barriers are placed in front of the damaged guardrail system. 
Replacement of the guardrail system and stacked rock retaining wall is desired. 
Based on the preliminary topographic survey map provided, the existing wall is up to 
about 6 feet in height and about 160 feet long. 
We understand that utilizing a soil nail and shotcrete wall system to replace the 
existing stacked rock retaining wall is desired to reduce the amount of road closure for 
the proposed project.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui is the second largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago. It 
was formed by the activity of two volcanoes, East Maui or Haleakala, and West Maui. 
The broad, gently sloping plain connecting the two volcanoes, known as the Maui 
Isthmus, was formed when lavas of the Haleakala Volcano banked against the already 
existing West Maui Volcano. 
The East Maui Volcano, better known as the Haleakala Mountain, makes up the 
eastern portion of the Island of Maui. Haleakala Mountain is considered to be dormant 
with the last eruption taking place on the southerly side of the mountain around 1790. 
Haleakala Mountain consists largely of the Honomanu Volcanic Series that built the 
mountain from three rift zones, probably during the Pliocene and early Pleistocene 
Epochs. 
The project site is located on the east flank of the West Maui Volcano. The 
volcanic rocks of the West Maui Volcano have been divided into three volcanic series. 
The oldest, Wailuku Volcanic Series, is comprised of basaltic lava flows and associated 
pyroclastic and intrusive rocks, which built the major shield volcano. The Wailuku 
Volcanic Series was covered by a thin, discontinuous “frosting” of andesitic and 
trachytic flows, domes, and pyroclastic deposits known as the Honolua Volcanic Series. 
After a long period of inactivity, where erosion incised deep valleys into the volcanic 
flows, volcanic activity returned with eruptions that produced the flows and cones of the 
Lahaina Volcanic Series. 
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, erosion processes are dominated by the 
detachment of soil and rock materials from steep valley walls. The detached materials 
fall or are otherwise transported down the slope toward the valley axis, primarily by 
gravity as colluvium. Once these materials reach the stream in the valley, stream flow 
processes become dominant, and the materials are further transported and may be 
deposited as alluvium.

In general, stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy (i.e., the streams 
transmit large volumes of water for very short durations).  Because of this, the transport 
of sediment is intermittent, and the bulk of the stream's hydraulic load consists of a 
poorly sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fine soil. When the 
erosion base level changes with the passage of time, the stream sediment loads are left 
as deposits. 
When alluvial and colluvial deposits are buried in-place for long periods of time, 
chemical processes begin to alter the materials, simultaneously causing a breakdown 
and weathering of the materials. Chemical processes may also cause induration, or 
cementation, of the sedimentary products into poorly to moderately consolidated 
sedimentary rock termed conglomerate. Simultaneously, ground erosion processes 
continue in the higher terrain above the valley floors. This continued upland erosion 
generates new unconsolidated alluvial and colluvial earth materials, which are then 
transported downslope and cover the older indurated deposits. Depending on the local 
erosion base level and rate of transport, these newer sediments may be generally 
transient in terms of geologic time.  In addition, their consistency and density are 
generally less than those of the older, partially consolidated deposits. 
2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is located at 130 Iao Valley Road about 1,300 feet west of the 
intersection of Main Street and Iao Valley Road in Wailuku on the Island of Maui, 
Hawaii. Based on the preliminary topographic survey map provided, the existing 
stacked rock wall is up to about 6 feet in height and about 160 feet long. The stability of 
the existing stacked rock retaining wall supporting the roadway of Iao Valley Road had 
been compromised. The existing guardrail along the northern side of Iao Valley Road, 
located near the top of the retaining wall, is damaged with tilted guardrail posts. 
Concrete Jersey barriers are placed in front of the damaged guardrail system.  
Based on the preliminary topographic survey map provided, Iao Valley Road 
within the project limits slopes downwards westerly with ground elevations from about 
+450 to about +444 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL). There are limited shoulders along both 
sides of Iao Valley Road and are covered with dense vegetation of bushes and trees.

2.3 
Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to a depth of 
about 26.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. In addition, two bulk samples, 
designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, were obtained near the existing ground surface to 
evaluate the engineering and pavement support characteristics of the near-surface 
soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2. 
In general, the borings encountered about 8 inches of asphaltic concrete 
underlain by alluvium extending to the maximum depth explored of about 26.5 feet 
below the existing pavement surface. The alluvium encountered generally consisted of 
medium stiff to hard sandy clay, silty clay, clayey silt and sandy silt, and loose to very 
dense silty sand and silty gravel with cobbles and boulders.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that water levels may vary with seasonal 
rainfall, time of the year, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered from our field exploration are 
presented on the Logs of Borings in Appendix A. Results of the laboratory tests 
performed on selected soil and rock samples are presented in Appendix B. Photographs 
of core samples are presented in Appendix C.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
We understand that it is desired to replace the existing near-vertical dry stacked 
rock retaining wall adjacent to 130 Iao Valley Road with a soil nailed retaining wall 
system to reduce the amount of road closure that will be required for the construction of 
a conventional cantilever concrete retaining wall. 
Based on our field exploration and soil nail analyses, items pertaining to the soil 
nailed walls design and installation are addressed in the following subsections: 
1. Wall Preparation 
2. Soil Nailed Wall 
3. Soil Nails 
4. Soil Nail Installation 
5. Soil Nail Testing 
6. Shotcrete Facing 
7. Drainage 
3.1 
Wall Preparation 
At the on-set of construction, surface vegetation, existing dry stacked boulders, 
loose material, and deleterious material should be carefully removed from the existing 
retaining wall. The existing dry stacked rock retaining wall should be removed in phases 
with small section less than 25 feet long at each phase to avoid causing slope 
instability. Care should be taken in removing material so that the stability of the existing 
slope and Iao Valley Road is not compromised. A flash coat of shotcrete should be 
applied to the exposed slope surface to prevent raveling and to prepare for the 
installation of the soil nailed wall. We understand that shotcrete backfill, up to about 
2 feet thick, may be required to bring the exposed slope surface to the alignment of the 
back face of the proposed soil nailed wall. 
3.2 
Soil Nailed Wall 
A soil nailed retaining wall system consists of a series of individual reinforcing 
bars grouted into drilled holes to stabilize an excavation. A shotcrete facing is generally 
applied to the face of the reinforced wall, providing the appearance of a conventional 
concrete retaining wall. To provide a natural rock appearance, the shotcrete facing may 
be sculpted and stained to match the appearance of the surrounding environment if

The reinforced soil mass behaves in a manner similar to a gravity retaining wall but 
lacks a footing. A typical section of the soil nailed wall is shown on Plate 3. 
Construction of the permanent soil nailed wall system should be performed by a

3.3 
Soil Nails 
The soil nails should be installed by drilling a minimum 5.5-inch diameter hole 
with an inclination of approximately 15 degrees from horizontal. The inclination of the 
nails may be increased to avoid existing underground structures. The soil nail bar 
should consist of an ASTM A615 Grade 75 threaded bar with a minimum bar diameter 
of 1 inch. We recommend using a double corrosion protection system for the nails 
consisting of epoxy-coated bars surrounded by neat cement grout or sand-cement 
mixture with a minimum 28-day compressive strength of 4,000 pounds per square inch 
(psi). 
Based on our soil nail analysis, we recommend using design nail lengths of 
10 feet extending into the stiff alluvial soil encountered in our field exploration. The soil 
nails should be spaced 3 feet on-center vertically and 5 feet on-center horizontally. The 
first nail should be installed about 2 feet below the top of the slope. 
Based on the above design parameters, the soil nailed retaining wall system 
should have a factor of safety of 1.5 or greater for the combined internal and external 
stability for the static loading condition considering a surcharge load of 250 pounds per 
square foot (psf) on the roadway travel lanes.  
3.4 
Soil Nail Installation 
To facilitate the installation of the soil nails, a shotcrete flash coat should first be 
applied to the face of the exposed slope. Soil nail installation activities should avoid 
causing instability to the existing slope and roadway above.

3.5 
Soil Nail Testing 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated behind the soil nailed retaining 
wall, we recommend performing a minimum of one pre-production verification test on a 
sacrificial soil nail installed near the top of the slope on the eastern end of the existing 
near-vertical dry stacked rock retaining wall. In addition, proof tests should be 
performed on at least 10 percent of the production soil nails during construction to 
confirm the bond stresses used in the design. 
The verification test should be loaded gradually to 150 percent of the design load 
for the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of about 200 percent of 
the design load. The design load is 7 kips per nail. In addition, the load at every loading 
increment should be held for at least 10 minutes, and creep tests should be held for at 
least 60 minutes. The proof tests should consist of subjecting the soil nail to at least 
150 percent of the design loads and the load should be held for at least 10 minutes 
(until stable). The proof test nails may be incorporated into the permanent soil nail 
retaining wall, provided they satisfy the test criteria. Pullout tests and creep tests on the 
soil nails are integral components of the design for the soil nail retaining wall system.  
Therefore, we recommend conducting pullout tests and creep tests in the presence of a 
Geolabs representative. 
3.6 
Shotcrete Facing 
Shotcrete placement should be performed by an experienced nozzleman. Prior to 
production shotcreting, it is recommended that unreinforced test panels of shotcrete be 
constructed for inspection.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field borings. Variations of conditions between and 
beyond the field borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may 
not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it 
will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations presented herein. 
The field boring locations are approximate, having been estimated by using a 
hand-held Global Positioning System (GPS) device and shown on the Overall Plan 
transmitted by Wilson Okamoto Corporation on May 8, 2023. Elevations of the borings 
were estimated based on spot elevations and contours shown on the Topographic 
Survey Map prepared by Controlpoint Surveying, Inc., dated March 6, 2023. The 
physical locations and elevations of the borings should be considered accurate only to 
the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. These data 
have been reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, 
it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variations in seasonal rainfall, surface 
runoff and other factors. These data have been reviewed and interpretations made in 
the formulation of this report.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to a depth of about 
26.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. The approximate boring locations are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools. 
 Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock cores were described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested 
Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the 
International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-3. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch O.D. Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch O.D. standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting 
any drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used 
to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical 
Properties of Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the in-situ soils. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-6 and B-7.

bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
