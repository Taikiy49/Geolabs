REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes the older West Maui Tertiary
Epoch and the more recent East Maui also known as Haleakala Pleistocene Epoch The
Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain located between these two volcanoes
The bulk of the Haleakala shield was built during the late Pliocene and early
Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows of the Honomanu Volcanic Series
During the Pleistocene Epoch the characteristics of the lavas changed to very hard thickly
bedded flows of andesitic composition These lavas have been grouped as the Kula
Volcanic Series Typically the basalt consists of thinly to thickly bedded a a and pahoehoe
type flows
These lava flows have undergone in situ weathering over time This in situ chemical
weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils that grade to saprolite with
depth In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical
weathering of voicanic rocks The project site is located on the western slopes of the
Haleakala shield

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site or stockpiled in a
designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated
materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to
expose firm material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft soils may be used as fill
provided that it meets the requirements for fill material
Prior to filling the existing ground should be scarified to a depth of 10 inches and
moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted
to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction For pavement subgrades the
minimum relative compaction should be 95 percent Where shrinkage cracks are
noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soil be thoroughly
moistened to close all cracks or the subgrade be scarified moisture conditioned and
re compacted Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas
and replacement with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft soils may
be used as fill provided that it meets the requirements for fill material Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the

Fills and Backfills
The fill material should consist of low expansive materials less than 3 inches in largest
dimension with a CBR swell less than 1 percent The fill material should also be free
of vegetation and deleterious materials In general the on site silty clay and clayey
silt material should be suitable for fill material
Base course and select borrow subbase material should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate and should conform to the County of Maui Department of Public Works
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction dated September 1986
Imported fill should consist of non expansive granular material such as crushed
coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded from
coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883 Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
All fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
Base course and select borrow subbase materials should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in
loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
multi wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment

Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by near surface
clayey fill and residual soils with low expansion potential To reduce the potential for future
distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the
clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content we recommend that the subgrade soils
be kept within about 2 percent above the optimum moisture content prior to final placement
of concrete
Concrete slabs on grade will be constructed for the garage We anticipate that the
vehicle loading for the new garage will consist primarily of large maintenance vehicles with
the largest vehicle being a D8 Dozer We have made our preliminary pavement design
assuming that the pavement subgrade soil will consist of recompacted or engineered fills
Based on the above assumptions we recommend that the slab thickness be 8 inches thick
For slabs subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that the slabs be supported on a
6 inch layer of aggregate subbase to support the slabs subjected to the dynamic loads due
to the vehicles The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basalt aggregate
compacted to a minimum 95 percent relative compaction For the design of structural floor
slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection
pci may be used for the compacted aggregate subbase Provisions should be made for
proper load transfer across the slab joints that will be subjected to vehicular traffic

4 inches thick To provide more uniform support we recommend that the concrete

Field Percolation Testing
Field percolation tests were performed in the borings in general accordance with the
Wastewater Systems and Chapter 10 of the Ten States Standards To evaluate the subsoil
profile and permeability of the subsurface soils for wastewater disposal the percolation tests
were performed by drilling 4 0 inch diameter boreholes extending to depths ranging from
about 4 to 15 feet below the existing ground surface A total of four percolation tests were
performed at the locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 Two percolation tests were
performed within the surface fill layer and two tests were performed within boreholes that
extended into the underlying clinker layer
GEOLABS INC
W O Page 15
Prior to the testing the test holes were presoaked for at least 4 hours During the test
the holes were carefully filled with water to a minimum height of 6 inches above the bottom
of the hole The time intervals and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon
which calculation of the percolation rates were based Based on the test results percolation
rates of about 80 and 240 minutes per inch were measured within the surface fill layer For
the percolation tests performed within the underlying clinker layer percolation rates of about
8 minutes per inch were measured The results of the individual percolation tests are
presented on Plates A 10 through A 13 of this report
Based on the results of the field percolation tests performed within the surface fill
layer a shallow leaching field system appears to be unsuitable for the project site Based
on the test results performed within the underlying clinker layer we believe that the
northwestern portion of the project site may be suitable for siting the new septic system
Seepage pits extending into the underlying clinker layer may be considered for the new
septic system

Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems
be installed around the new structures and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface
run off away from the slabs and foundations
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface clayey soils The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration
