2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene
to early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded
against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene
Epoch The dips of the lava beds generally range from 3 to 5 degrees near horizontal
The lava flows on the plateau have weathered to depths of 50 to 100 feet and are
characterized by the red colors of the soil The deep narrow canyons such as
Waikakalaua Kipapa and Kaukonahua are a result of this weathering The brown and
reddish brown soils generally consist of ancient alluvial deposits and residual soils
overlying the weathered rock

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling eleven borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 through 11 extending to depths of about 26 5 feet below
the existing ground surface

boulders and cobbles within the alluvial deposits
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field
exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change
due to seasonal precipitation storm surge conditions and other factors

Earthquake and Seismicity
In general earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic
plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic
activity therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during
volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are
so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments However some
of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to
moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement
of magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa
Volcanoes on the Island of Hawaii Because majority of the earthquakes in
Hawaii over 90 percent are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic
activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from
the Island of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous
earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 M5 however earthquakes are not
confined only to the Island of Hawaii
To a lesser degree the Island of Maui has experienced several earthquakes
greater than Magnitude 5 Therefore moderate to strong earthquakes have
occurred in the County of Maui The effects of earthquakes occurring on the
Islands of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu For example small
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of
Some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly damaged as a
result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 M7

Based on the competent subsurface conditions encountered at the site we
recommend supporting the proposed buildings on a shallow foundation system
consisting of spread and or continuous footings The footings should be embedded a
minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades The soils encountered
at the bottom of footing excavations should be compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction
The on site soils have a low expansion potential when subjected to moisture
fluctuations However we recommend placing a minimum 6 inch thick layer of
non expansive select granular fill material below the slab to reduce moisture changes in
the slab subgrade soils Placement of the non expansive select granular fill layer would
further reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade
resulting from shrinking and swelling of the on site soils due to changes in the moisture
content

the subgrade soils to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioning the subgrade
soils to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacting to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The underlying subgrade soils should be
wetted and kept moist until the final placement of slab concrete or pavement Where
shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
preparing the soils again as recommended

3 2 Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by silty
clay clayey silt fill materials Our laboratory tests indicate that the on site soils have a
low expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations To provide a protective
layer to reduce the potential for changes in the moisture content of the slab subgrade
soils we recommend capping the slab subgrade with a 6 inch thick layer of
non expansive select granular fill The non expansive select granular fill should be
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Prior to placing the non expansive select granular fill we recommend scarifying
the subgrade soils to a depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioning the soils to at
least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacting to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction The underlying subgrade soils and select granular fill
should be wetted and kept moist until the final placement of slab concrete Where
shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
preparing the soils again as recommended Saturation and subsequent yielding of the
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require
over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with engineered fill
For interior building slabs we recommend placing a minimum 4 inch thick layer of
cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below the
slabs to serve as a capillary break and to provide more uniform support of the slabs To
reduce moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious
moisture barrier is recommended on top of the No 3B Fine gravel Flexible floor coverings
such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask minor slab
cracking
We anticipate exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project
We recommend supporting concrete walkways on 4 inches of non expansive select
granular fill The select granular fill should be compacted to at least 90 percent relative
compaction Control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the
walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections

3 4 Excavation
It is anticipated that the soils may be excavated with normal heavy excavation
equipment However there is a high potential for encountering relatively hard and
unweathered cobbles and large boulders within the alluvial deposits Therefore the
contractor for the project should be cautioned that large boulders and cobbles may be
encountered in the excavations Excavations encountering large boulders and cobbles
may require chipping and or the use of hoerams to excavate the materials and to
achieve the required grades

APPENDIX A
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling eleven borings extending to depths of about 26 5 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings were drilled using a track mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight
augers
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 11
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer test results are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Tests
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Five one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
to evaluate the swelling potential of the on site clayey soils The test results are
summarized on Plate B 1
Two Expansion Index of Soil tests ASTM D 4829 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test is a
standardized test used to determine the expansive characteristics of the soil The test
results are presented on Plate B 2
Five Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 3
One Direct Shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected sample to
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested The test results are
presented on Plate B 4
Three Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2166 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength characteristics The test results
along with the stress strain curves are presented on Plates B 5 through B 7
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on a bulk
sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plate B 8
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed
on the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils The
test results are presented on Plate B 9
