SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
New pavements will be required for the new Kealakaha Stream Replacement Bridge
approaches planned at the site Due to the generally high rainfall conditions at the site and
the subgrade soils anticipated at the pavement subgrades we believe that a permeable
drainage layer should be incorporated into the pavement design for the replacement bridge
approaches
Based on our field exploration we anticipate that the majority of pavement subgrade
soils will generally consist of medium stiff to stiff clayey silts with sands and gravel for the
proposed replacement bridge approaches Based on the traffic data provided the relatively
low CBR value Kentucky method of the subgrade soils and the economic analysis
performed we recommend that a flexible pavement structural section be used for the
replacement bridge approaches The recommended flexible pavement section of 3 5 inches
be used for design of pavements for the new Kealakaha Stream Replacement Bridge
approaches
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement surface
should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off the
pavement to appropriate drainage structures To collect infiltrated water into the pavement
structural section we recommend that a pavement edge drain system hydraulically
connected to the untreated permeable base be provided along the edges of the new
pavements
The text of this pavement justification report should be referred to for detailed
discussion and specific recommendations for design of pavements

SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This pavement justification report presents the results of our analyses for the design
of new pavements for the Kealakaha Stream Replacement Bridge project designated as
Project No 26 located in the Kealakaha area in the District of Hamakua on the
Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for the design of
pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is located along Mamalahoa Highway Route 19 Hawaii Belt Road
in the Kealakaha area in the District of Hamakua on the Island of Hawaii The existing bridge
structure is a six span concrete bridge located across Kealakaha Stream It is proposed to
construct a replacement bridge on the makai north side of the existing bridge structure The
new bridge structure will be designed for the relatively high earthquake loads of the area and
will also reduce the horizontal curve in the alignment of the existing bridge
Based on the current design concept the proposed new bridge will be about 720 feet
long and 50 feet wide We understand that the new bridge will consist of three spans
supported by two intermediate piers and the two abutments The center span will consist of
a cast in place concrete segmental span of about 360 feet and the two outer spans will be
about 180 feet resulting in a balanced cantilever In order to reduce the stiffness of the
intermediate piers supporting relatively long bridge spans the pier foundations will be
embedded an average of approximately 40 feet below the existing grades In addition
substantial embedment of the intermediate pier foundations would be needed to provide
adequate horizontal setback distance from the steeply sloping banks of the existing gulch

SECTION 2 0 REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area
of approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the eruptive activity of five
major shield volcanoes Kohala which has long been extinct Mauna Kea which has had
some activity during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna
Loa and Kilauea which are both considered to be active
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Mauna Kea Shield
Volcano which had been built up by the successive accumulation of basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials The most recent lava flows that comprise the ground surface and
shallow subsurface at the project site are believed to be Pliocene to Pleistocene in age
approximately 12 million years to 2 million years before present time These surface lava
flows belong to the upper member of the Hamakua Volcanic Series
The lava formation at the site generally appears to be of pahoehoe type flow which
is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of
vesicular porous rock The basalt formations of the project area are typically layered with
thin flows of dense basalts and scoria basalts Clinker layers are also commonly found
interbedded between the pahoehoe flows In situ weathering of the basalt formation has
yielded layers of soft highly weathered basalts interbedded with more competent hard
basalts
Cavities or voids are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava These cavities were
formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow
cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten
lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for
a large distance longitudinally along the flow Blisters and pockets inter flow cavities are
generally smaller in horizontal extent

3 1 Methodology of Pavement Design
Two types of pavement structural sections flexible and rigid pavements were
considered in the pavement analyses for this project The flexible pavement sections
presented in this report were generally determined based on the methodology described in
Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual dated May 1995 The Pavement Design Manual
was prepared by the State of Hawaii Department of Transportation Highways Division
Material Testing and Research Branch The pavement design methodology is based on the
Hveem Stabilometer method developed and used by the California Department of
Transportation Caltrans
The design procedures for the rigid pavement sections are generally based on the
design procedures described in the Portland Cement Association Thickness Design for
Concrete Highway and Street Pavements

Preparation Below Pavement Section
Based on the grading plans we understand that the majority of the replacement
bridge approaches will be in a cut condition Cuts of up to about 18 feet deep and minor fills
of about 1 foot will be needed for the proposed bridge approach construction In general the
area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed at the
on set of earthwork Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be
removed and disposed of properly off site or disposed of in a designated area to reduce the
potential for contamination of the excavated materials Soft and yielding areas encountered
during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill or future improvements
should be over excavated to expose dense materials The resulting excavation should be
backfilled with aggregate subbase materials The excavated soft and or organic soils should
be properly disposed of off site

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
After clearing and grubbing the future pavement areas should be excavated where
necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the aggregate subbase course
layer The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined
in accordance with AASHTO Test Method T 180 ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
In areas where the pavement subgrades expose dense clinker materials or basalt rock
formation the subgrades should be proof rolled with a minimum 15 ton vibratory roller for a
minimum of eight passes in lieu of scarification to help detect and collapse near surface
cavities Loose areas or cavities disclosed during clearing and proof rolling operations
should be backfilled with aggregate subbase materials The proof rolling operation should
also be performed at the finished subgrades in cut areas The vibratory drum roller should
travel at a speed of about 200 to 300 feet per minute Due to the potential for presence of
cavities lava tubes blisters pockets etc and soft and or wet soils contract documents
should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and aggregate subbase
placement to account for variations in the over excavation and backfill quantities
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the pumping subgrade
may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for pumping subgrade
conditions As a guide stabilization by cement treatment may consist of one sack of cement
for approximately 25 square feet of subgrade area Geolabs should be contacted to evaluate
the pumping subgrade conditions and to evaluate whether pavement sections need to be
revised and or modified based on the exposed subgrade conditions

3 6 Fill Materials
Fill materials to be used as structural fills and backfills should consist of well graded
granular materials less than 6 inches in largest dimension The excavated on site materials
may be re used as structural fill or backfill materials provided that the materials are
processed and reduced to a relatively well graded select granular material with a maximum
particle size of about 6 inches
The maximum particle size of the fills and backfills should be limited to a maximum
of 3 inches for fills and backfills to be placed in confined locations Confined locations are
defined as areas where the compaction equipment is limited in size to less than 10 tons in
static weight In addition if the contractor elects to use smaller sized compaction equipment
compaction equipment less than 10 tons in static weight the maximum particle size of the
fill and backfill materials should be limited to 3 inches in dimension
Imported fill material where required should consist of non expansive granular
material such as crushed basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded from
coarse to fine with no particles greater than 6 inches in largest dimension The material
should have a CBR value of 25 or more and should have a maximum swell value of
1 percent or less In addition the material should have a minimum R value of 60 or more
Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance with these recommendations prior
to delivery to the project site for the intended use
3 7 Compaction Requirements
Structural fill and backfill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The compaction requirement
for fills placed within the pavement areas should be increased to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction

Where fills are composed of rocks boulders and or cobbles conventional compaction
testing is generally not practical Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of
passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Based on this testing program the number of passes may
then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction For preliminary estimating
purposes compaction may be accomplished by using a large vibratory drum roller having a
static weight of at least 15 tons traveling at a speed of about 200 to 300 feet per minute For
95 percent relative compaction the roller should pass over the structural fill material surface
at least eight times
Aggregate subbase materials required for the new pavements should conform to the
requirements stipulated in the State of Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge
and Public Works Construction 1994 The aggregate subbase should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch level loose
lifts and compacted to not less than 95 percent of the maximum density according to Section
106 09 Special Test Methods of the HSS
The glassphalt concrete base GCB material should be mixed and constructed in
accordance with Section 312 Plant Mix Glassphalt Concrete Base Course of the HSS and
subsequent amendments Asphalt concrete base ACB material should consist of
asphalt treated basalt aggregate placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted
thickness and compacted to at least 91 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity
determined in accordance with AASHTO T 209 ASTM D 2041 test procedures
Asphaltic concrete material should be constructed in accordance with Section 401
Asphalt Concrete Pavement of the State of Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road
Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 and subsequent amendments
