2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau Volcano forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that the Waianae Volcano
became extinct while the Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank is
partially buried below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano and its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio eustatic
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the
Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated
by fossil Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high
stands of the sea which are intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the
stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level
changes as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher
and lower relative to the present sea level on Oahu About 15 000 years ago a
relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area
were drowned In the last 10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present
stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy
estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting in thick deposits of loose and or soft
sediments along the coast in the areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways
In the early part of this century the Waikiki area consisted of low marshy areas
As the City of Honolulu grew and the Waikiki area was urbanized man made fills were
placed to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons The fill was placed upon lagoonal
deposits consisting of silty sands with coral fragments The lower clayey material was

2 4 Seismic Design Considerations
Based on the 1997 Uniform Building Code 1997 UBC current code adopted by
the City and County of Honolulu the Island of Oahu is located within Seismic Zone 2A
with an associated peak ground acceleration of 0 15g The project site may be
subjected to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to be
addressed for the project The following sections provide discussions on the seismicity
of the Island of Oahu the soil profile for seismic design at the site and the potential for
liquefaction at the site
Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts
in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most of the earthquakes are so small that they may only be detected by
instruments However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and
a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the
Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of

3 1 Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we
recommend that a deep foundation system be used to support the proposed second
floor addition We envision that the column loads for the proposed steel structure is
relatively low Considering the effects of noise and vibrations generated from a driven
pile foundation during construction on the adjacent existing businesses we recommend
that micropiles be used to support the new structure
In general the micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter usually
less than 12 inches pile drilled and grouted with steel reinforcing The micropile
foundation is typically constructed by drilling a borehole with casing placing reinforcing
steel in the hole and grouting the borehole Micropiles are desirable because they can
be readily installed in access restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and
W O INC Page 8
Hawaii California

ground conditions In addition installation of the micropiles generally causes minimal
disturbance to adjacent structures the adjacent soils and the environment
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the project site we estimate
that a micropile system with a grout bulb diameter of 7 inches or larger may be used to
support the new structures Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated at the
project site we recommend that each micropile be designed based on an allowable
compressive load capacity of 10 tons 20 kips The allowable compressive load
capacity of the micropile is for supporting dead plus live loads and may be increased by
up to 50 percent for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces
To provide an allowable compressive load capacity of 10 tons 20 kips we
recommend that the micropile extend through the loose and or soft lagoonal deposits
and embed a minimum of 10 feet into the bearing layer consisting of medium dense stiff
alluvial deposits and medium dense to dense coralline detritus Based on the boring
information we estimate that the bearing layer start at a depth of approximately 25 feet
below existing ground surface therefore the bottom depth of the micropile would
extend to approximately 35 feet below the existing ground surface The micropiles will
derive its vertical support primarily from skin friction between the grout and the
surrounding alluvial deposits and coralline detritus coral ledge Higher load capacity
may be obtained by increasing the pile embedment depth
To reduce the potential for caving in or necking of the loose and or soft lagoonal
deposits which would result in less grout cover around the steel reinforcement
potential corrosion issue we recommend that the micropiles be cased permanent
casing The outside diameter OD of the permanent casing should also be 7 inches or
greater same as the grout bulb size
In the event of seismically induced liquefaction settlement of the loose lagoonal
deposits will occur at the site When the loose lagoonal deposits move downward
relative to the micropile the downward movement will result in negative skin friction
forces generally known as downdrag forces acting on the micropile The magnitude of

Micropile Load Test Program
It should be noted that the compressive load capacity of the micropile is highly
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the
contractor to install the micropile Therefore the compressive load capacity of the
micropile may vary considerably between different contractors and micropile
foundation systems In order to determine whether the contractor s methods of
micropile installation are adequate and to determine the ultimate compressive load
capacity we recommend that at least one pre production compressive load test be
performed on a sacrificial micropile In general the purpose of the pre production
load test on a sacrificial micropile is to fulfill the following objectives
e To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed by the
contractor to install the micropiles to the depths required
e Toassess the contractor s method of drilling and grout injection

In general the pre production load test should be performed in accordance with
ASTM D 1143 Quick Loading Procedure Based on our experience we believe
that the load test should be conducted no earlier than 7 days after completion of the
micropile installation to allow the grout adequate time to cure Two additional
micropiles may be used for reaction during the compressive load testing of the pre
production load test micropile The reaction micropiles may be installed to depths
as deep as the load test micropile to provide adequate reaction in uplift to be
determined by the contractor
The load test micropile should be loaded gradually to at least 200 percent of the
allowable design load in compression We recommend that the maximum test load
200 percent of the design load be held for a minimum of 4 to 8 hours depending
on the recorded movements of the load test micropile The pre production load test
is an integral part of the micropile foundation system design
In addition to the pre production load test we also recommend that pullout tests
proof tests be performed on selected micropiles during construction to confirm the
load carrying capacity of the installed micropiles We recommend that a minimum
5 percent of the total number of micropiles or minimum of three micropiles be
tested for pullout The pullout tests should consist of subjecting the micropile to at
least 133 percent of the design loads and the maximum test load should be held
for at least 15 minutes Pullout tests on the micropiles also are integral parts of the
design of the micropile foundation system

It should be noted that the micropile foundation system provides low lateral load
resistance due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile Therefore the
lateral load resistance contributed by the micropiles should be neglected Lateral
loads imposed on the building structure may be resisted primarily by passive
pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and grade beams and
by shear resistance acting along the sides of the pile caps and grade beams

Settlements of the micropile foundations will result primarily from elastic
compression of the pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total
settlement of the pile supported foundations to be less than 0 5 inches with
differential settlements between columns supported on micropiles not exceeding
about one half of the total settlement We believe that these settlements are
essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied
Construction Considerations
In general corrosion protection is one of the concerns for the micropile foundation
system Depending on the micropile foundation systems used there is a potential
for having inconsistent grout cover around the reinforcing steel of the micropile We
believe that the potential for inconsistent grout cover could be significantly reduced
if good installation practices and good quality control measures are implemented
We believe that a permanent steel casing for the micropile may provide sufficient
corrosion protection The minimum 7 inch diameter permanent steel casing should
be installed for at least the top 25 feet of the micropile In addition a grout with
water cement ratio of about 0 40 to 0 45 should be used
As mentioned previously the performance of the micropile foundations is highly
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the
contractor Good quality control measures will assure better quality of the micropile
product We believe that a metering device to measure and record the flow rate of
the grout pumped into the micropile should be provided and working at all times

3 3 Utility Trenches
We envision that some new utility lines and utility connections may be required
for the project We envision that most of the new utility lines and utility connections will
be relatively shallow within 4 feet of the ground surface In general granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is
recommended under the pipes for uniform support Free draining granular materials
such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes or 12 inches above the
groundwater level to provide adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use
this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper
trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils
with a maximum particle size of 3 inches The backfill should be moisture conditioned
to above the optimum moisture content placed in about 8 inch level loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are below
pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade
should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction

8 inches moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The aggregate base course and subbase
materials should consist of crushed basaltic aggregate compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction CBR and field density tests should be performed on the
actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to evaluate the adequacy of the
above pavement sections
In addition paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry the surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be
allowed on the site during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to
isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend that the curbs
extend a minimum of 2 inches into the soils below the aggregate base course layer to
reduce the potential for migration of excessive landscape water into the pavement
section Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect excess water
from landscaping irrigation For long term performance we recommend that a subdrain
system be constructed adjacent to paved landscaped areas
3 5 Drainage
Finished grades outside the building should be sloped to shed water away from
the foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for excessive water infiltration
into the subsurface

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings extending to depths of about 31 5 and 41 5 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight
solid stem and hollow stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified
California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are
shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer shear tests were performed on selected cohesive soil
samples retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of
the unconfined compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer shear tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
