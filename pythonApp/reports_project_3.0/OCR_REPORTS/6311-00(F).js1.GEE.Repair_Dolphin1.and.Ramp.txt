Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers
an area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of
the following five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both still active
The project site is situated on the southwestern flank of Kohala Mountain which is
the oldest of the five shield volcanoes forming the Island of Hawaii Kohala Mountain was
built during the Pleistocene Epoch by basaltic lava flows from the Polou and Hawi Volcanic
Series Typically the lava flows of the Polou Volcanic Series are composed of tholeiitic
olivine basaltic lava flows grading to oceanite In general such flows are characterized as
thinly bedded with a high degree of porosity
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted
through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and
along clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they
serve as the major water transmission features
Lagoonal sediments and coralline deposits typically overlie the basalt formations at
the project site A surface layer of man made fill was placed over these deposits for the
development of Kawaihae Harbor within the last century The land reclamation activities
have brought the area around Kawaihae Harbor to its present form

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in
the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to
volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before
or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not
erupt The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but
most are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments
However some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause
minor to moderate damage

Liquefaction Potential
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near
the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of
excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by
earthquakes In this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected
to vibration such as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and
decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is
unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build up of pore water pressure
the effective stress internal strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained
vibrations the pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden
pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to
behave as a viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil acquires sufficient mobility
to permit both horizontal and vertical movements and if not confined will result
in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion The major factors affecting the
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows

Uplift Load Resistance
Uplift loads imposed on the pile foundation may be resisted by a combination of the
weight of the driven pile and by shear resistance acting along the pile surface and
the adjacent soils Based on our estimate an ultimate uplift load capacity of 90 kips
per pile may be used for the 16 5 inch octagonal piles driven to the tip elevation of

It should be noted that the above estimated uplift load capacities are based on
shear resistance distributed on the entire pile length Therefore mechanical
splicing should be used if the pile is spliced to achieve the design tip elevation
The project structural engineer should check the tensile capacity of the pile
member
Foundation Settlement
Settlement of pile foundations will result primarily from elastic compression of the
pile member and the subgrade response We estimate that the total settlement
of the pile supported foundation to be less than 0 5 inches Differential
settlements between adjacent piles should not exceed about one half of the total
foundation settlement We believe that these settlements are essentially elastic
and should occur as the loads are applied

Pile Driving Criteria
For preliminary design of the 16 5 inch octagonal prestressed precast concrete
piles pile driving may be terminated when aggregate blow counts are at least
40 blows per foot for the last foot or 4 blows per inch for the last 6 inches The
above preliminary driving criteria should be confirmed and or modified after
selection of the pile driving equipment and performance of wave equation analyses
Each pile should be driven continuously without interruption A pile may be rejected
or its design capacity may be reduced when the pile driving is interrupted for more
than 4 hours and the pile cannot be driven to the required depth During soft
driving such as when the pile tip is within soft soils the hammer stroke or energy
should be carefully controlled such that significant tensile stresses do not develop
within the pile during the pile driving operation

Test Pile Program
We recommend conducting a test pile program which includes installing test piles
at selected locations and performing load tests to confirm the design capacity and
the driveability It is difficult and costly to perform conventional static pile load test
for the battered offshore concrete pile Therefore we recommend performing
dynamic pile load tests only on four selected battered off shore test piles to fulfill the
following objectives
1
To examine the driveability of the piles with the use of the selected
hammer
2
To estimate the production pile lengths
3
To assess the test pile driving criteria that may be modified for use
as the production pile driving criteria
The test piles may be incorporated into the actual foundation system upon
evaluation of the dynamic load test results When the final foundation plan is
available Geolabs should be consulted for selection of the test pile locations Test
piles should be cast at least 10 feet longer than the preliminary pile lengths
recommended above to account for potential variations Re striking of all the test
piles may be required as part of the test pile program The dynamic pile load test
should be performed in general accordance with ASTM D4945
A Geolabs representative should be present during pile driving operations to
observe the actual driving behavior and to further evaluate the field performance It
should be noted that the pile design and driving criteria recommended were
developed from our pile analysis using the available boring information Therefore

Ship Loading Ramp Design
Based on the information provided the existing ship loading concrete ramp is
badly eroded and required to be repaired replaced A concrete bulk head structure was
installed along the edge of the ship loading ramp Based on the as built drawing
provided we understand that the bulk head structure is supported by concrete
sheetpiles about 23 feet deep and tie rod dead man anchors about 40 feet long The
following traffic information was provided for design of the concrete pavement
Vehicle Type 80 ton M1A1 Abrams Tank
Traffic Frequency 365 vehicles year
Design Life 50 years
Based on our field exploration the existing concrete pavement thickness is about
11 inches The ship loading ramp site is underlain by sandy granular fills extending to
depths of about 11 to 13 feet below the existing ground surface The sandy granular
materials are underlain by coralline detritus consisting of sandy and gravelly soils
extending to a depth of about 22 5 feet below the ground surface Groundwater was
encountered at shallow depths of about 2 to 2 5 feet below the ground surface
Based on results of the laboratory testing we envision the concrete pavement
subgrade soils have a CBR value of about 20 or greater with a modulus of subgrade
reaction k value of about 250 pounds per square inch of deflection pci for pavement
design
The concrete pavement analyses for the new concrete ramp were performed in
general accordance with the U S Army Corps of Engineer s pavement design software
Pavement Transportation Computer Assisted Structural Engineering PCASE Based
on the above traffic assumption and anticipated subgrade condition we recommend the

performed on a bulk sample of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soil The test results are presented on Plate B 3
