EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
The Kuahea Street Area Movement study encompasses the Waiomao
Landslide and surrounding area located in a residential subdivision in Palolo
Valley on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The effects of the Waiomao Landslide
became noticeable after the construction of the subdivision was completed in
the early 1950s Since then the Waiomao Landslide has been studied
periodically in efforts to arrest the slide and stabilize the land contained therein
In the late 1960 s through the 1970 s measures were taken by the City to
acquire the affected land area demolish the existing structures and construct
some improvements including subdrainage and earth grading in an effort to
reduce the earth movements at the project site The earth movements were
significantly slowed following these activities
The Waiomao Landslide has shown some signs of continued movement at the
head of the landslide and at portions of the western landslide boundary In
addition some signs of possible new earth movements have been observed
at the western portion of Kuahea Street the area adjacent to and northwest
of the Waiomao Landslide and the area located near the intersection of
Lamaku Place and Kipona Place The signs of potential earth movements
include the observation of ground surface deformation the seepage of water
at the ground surface and reports of damaged or leaking underground utility
lines in the vicinity
Based on our field reconnaissance we have identified some possible areas
of potential earth movements within the limits of the study In order to evaluate
the subsurface groundwater and soil conditions at these locations we have
planned a subsurface exploration program consisting of the drilling and
installation of groundwater and ground movement instrumentation at selected
locations The instrumentation and monitoring program generally consists of
the installation of seven inclinometers and five sets of nested piezometers in
borings The instruments will be monitored for a minimum period of about
12 months to collect data which will be used to evaluate the subsurface
conditions with respect to the potential earth movements
During the interim period we recommend that the City expand the street
surface survey monitoring and document all underground utility damage
including leakage In addition periodic maintenance of the pavements to
patch surface cracks should be conducted

WAIOMAO LANDSLIDE BACKGROUND
The Waiomao Landslide encompasses the Kuahea Street subdivision and vicinity
The Kuahea Street area subdivision was constructed during the years of 1951 and 1952
Beginning in 1952 problems with some earth movements and slight property damage were
first observed In 1954 following a large rainstorm significant damages related to
large scale earth movements on the order of several feet were experienced The first
remedial repairs were made in 1957 consisting of the construction of a concrete curtain wall
on Kuahea Street below the quarry site in addition to some roadway repairs and water
seepage control along Kuahea Street
Dr Ralph Peck a world renowned landslide consultant studied the landslide from
1958 through 1969 utilizing tiltmeter and piezometer instrumentation to monitor the
subsurface movements and hydrologic conditions over time During this period Dr Peck
focused on arresting the slide through the use of subsurface drainage such as vertical and
horizontal drain structures His intensive studies confirmed that the earth movements were
directly related to rainfall events as storm water penetrated and accumulated in the poorly
drained slide mass In addition the seat or bottom of the slide was estimated to be located
at a maximum depth of about 60 feet below the ground surface at that time near the center
of the slide based on the interpretation of the tiltmeter data
As a result remedial measures such as surface drainage improvements including the
sealing of potential avenues of water percolation into the slide were the first to be
implemented on the project site Early on it was believed that the old quarry site was
contributing to the groundwater accumulation within the slide therefore drainage of the
quarry site was improved as part of the initial remedial measures In addition some
underground utility pipes were abandoned and relocated onto more stable ground to reduce
the risk of adding subsurface water to the slide mass during pipeline breakages or leaks
By the end of 1958 three 10 inch and 12 inch diameter vertical drains had been
installed through the slide mass along Waiomao Road The vertical drains were terminated
in the underlying more permeable basalt rock ground condition The effects of the vertical
W O 3917 00 Page 4
drains were nominal in that the static water levels in the drains decreased some but not on
a regional level as originally anticipated Again the construction of horizontal drain structures
was suggested as a possible alternative for draining and arresting the slide
Through the early 1960s the slide did not appear to broaden but earth movement
rates remained essentially unchanged imparting significant distortion and damage to private
property and public thoroughfares An example of the severe disturbance was the nearly
10 foot vertical drop of Kuahea Street along the head of the slide and the near closure of
Kipona Place at the toe of the slide from encroaching earth and building material
In 1967 Dr Peck recommended the installation of a single row of 4 to 6 foot diameter
pumped vertical drains along Waiomao Road extending across the entire landslide An
alternative measure was proposed to deepen the large diameter drains by a smaller diameter
boring at the bottom of the vertical drain which would allow for gravity drainage into the more
permeable ground located below the slide plane In addition a horizontal drainage tunnel
with radial drains was suggested based on recent innovative construction developments in
the field of such structures
In the late 1960s through the early 1970s the City and County of Honolulu began to
purchase the lots that had been severely damaged and deemed uninhabitable by the slide
Approximately 20 lots located within the landslide footprint were acquired Following the
acquisition of the lots the work performed included the demolition of the structures and
re grading of the land area in an effort to promote enough stability to continue with restoration
of necessary public facilities By doing so the plan was to reduce the possibility of landslide
growth by loss of support at the head of the slide and minimize additional damage at the toe
of the slide The method selected to stabilize the slide was a general mass grading of the
land surface to reduce the earth pressure driving force of the slide mass It was believed that
once the movements became small enough measures would be taken to restore the roads
and utilities within the site

SITE CHARACTERIZATION
The majority of the island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt lavas from two
primary shield volcanoes known as Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is
estimated to be middle to late Pliocene in geochronologic age and forms the bulk of the
western third of the island The younger volcano Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3917 00 Page 9
to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the
island Waianae became extinct while Koolau remained active thus the westward flowing
lavas from Koolau continued to bank against the Waianae Volcano to create the elevated
area known as the Schofield Plateau between the two volcanoes Therefore the island of
Oahu was formed by the coalescence of the two volcanoes
Following the eruption of the bulk of the lavas the island of Oahu was partially
submerged and emerged cyclically due to large scale fluctuations of the sea level in
response to periods of worldwide glaciation As a result of the sea level fluctuations the
erosional baseline likewise fluctuated and major streams were drowned and buried and
later emerged to renew their erosional activities This cyclic activity during the Pleistocene
age generally accounts for the present topography of today including the sedimentary coastal
plains such as Ewa and the area surrounding Honolulu and Waikiki
Today both the Waianae and Koolau Mountains have lost most of their original shield
volcano outlines and exist as mountain ranges shaped largely by continuing erosional
processes The Koolau Range with its highest peak of about 3 105 feet above sea level is
approximately 37 miles in elongated length and is deeply incised and eroded by active
stream cutting In contrast the Waianae Range with its highest peak of about 4 025 feet
above sea level is about 22 miles in length and has been carved into huge valleys separated
by steep ridgelines by a long history of stream erosion and island wide submergence and
re emergence
Geology of the Site
The project area is located along the lower colluvial slope of a spur valley Waiomao
Valley located adjacent to Palolo Valley that trends roughly northeast to southwest from the
Koolau mountain range toward Kaimuki Palolo Valley is essentially a deep erosional valley
carved into the Koolau Shield Volcano by stream processes and mass wasting of the
adjacent slopes As a result the project site is generally underlain by colluvial and alluvial
deposits derived from the weathering and transport of eroded materials downslope toward
the valley axis In addition some fills were placed at portions of the site as a result of the

operation and closure of the old Waiomao Quarry located adjacent to and upslope of
Kuahea Street at the site The fill materials are believed to resemble the native colluvial and

Soil Survey of the Site
Based on the United States Soil Conservation Service SCS data the project site is
generally underlain by seven soil series as shown on the Soil Survey Map Plate 4 The soil
series are generally composed of alluvial and colluvial silty clay and clayey silt with varying
amounts of rocky material The Rock Land series is essentially in situ basalt rock exposure
with a thin surficial soil covering
The seven soil series are all components of a larger class of soils known as vertisols
Vertisol soils may be defined as dark colored clay rich alluvial soils of high shrink swell
capacity generally found as alluvial or colluvial deposits The occurrence of vertisol soils has
been linked with the distribution of many slow moving landslides on the southeastern portion
of Oahu Additionally it has been documented that the geographic occurrence of landslide
prone vertisol soils containing the clay minerals smectite and montmorillonite corresponds
with certain rainfall regimes The particular amount of rainfall is believed to aid in the
chemical weathering of parent materials and the subsequent formation of vertisol soils with
certain clay mineral signatures The soil series found at the project site as shown on

Climate
The project site is situated approximately in the middle portion of the valley between
the Koolau Range and the valley mouth at Kaimuki As a result the climate is typified by
frequent passing trade wind rain showers with the heavier amounts occurring further north
in the valley with amounts gradually decreasing to the south of the valley The rainfall
distribution is generally the result of orographic rainfall and drying of the descending air mass
on the leeward side of the Koolau Range As a result the distribution of rainfall is generally
higher toward the backbone ridge of the Koolau Range to the north and decreases toward
the southern shoreline of Oahu
Based on rainfall data obtained from City and County of Honolulu records the
average annual rainfall at the project site is on the order of about 50 inches per year
Temperatures in the valley range from the mid 50s to lower 90s degrees Fahrenheit The
overall climate may be characterized as maritime humid tropical The National Weather
Service does not maintain a weather recording station near the project site

Limitations of Field Observations
The use of field observations of surface features as a sole means for defining lateral
boundaries of subsurface earth movements should be limited Ground surface
observations should be used in conjunction with other means of site evaluation
because of the subjectivity and judgement involved with visual observation Other
methods of site evaluation should accompany the visual observations to develop an
accurate model of the landslide system For this study some of the methods to be
utilized for definition of the earth movements include the following 1 review of
underground utility damage and repair records 2 review of resident comments
pertaining to significant damage occurring within private properties 3 observation of
water seepage at the ground surface 4 drilling of borings and instrumentation
monitoring and 5 long term ground surface monitoring by optical survey methods

Regional Soil Creep
Based on our past experience and observations of residential development on
colluvial hill slopes it is apparent that soil creep processes are widespread in Palolo
Valley Soil creep is essentially a slow downslope movement of shallow soil material
under the influence of gravity Soil creep is a common erosional process acting on
hill slope environments Soil creep is often enhanced by the presence of high
shrink swell adobe type clays such as vertisol soils The cyclic shrinking and
swelling of open soil cracks in response to moisture fluctuation causes the shallow soil
mass to slowly overturn itself in a vertical motion Thus if the soil mass exists on
sloping ground under the influence of gravity the horizontal downslope movement is
often accelerated by the additional vertical overturning motion
Some physical effects of soil creep may be expressed as leaning or cracking retaining
walls distressed pavement or sidewalks showing cracks upheaval or depressions
tree growth deflections and offset of other ground surface features Many of these
features may be observed throughout the residential subdivisions located on the lower
slopes of Palolo Valley Therefore it is often difficult to differentiate solely on the
basis of ground surface observations between possible shallow soil creep and
deep seated landslide type earth movements
Surface Discharge of Seepage Water
The widespread occurrence of seepage of water at the ground surface was observed
throughout the site and outside of the study area The seepage may be described as
a slow continual discharge of clear water at localized areas such as the base of
walls utility vaults and pavement cracks or joints Long term seepage may be
identified by the presence of algae growth and pavement staining The water
seepage is believed to be caused by a number of factors including surface drainage
at topographic low spots groundwater or spring discharge and various utility line
leakage points
W O 3917 00 Page 15
It was observed that as the relative rainfall amount increased during the month of
June as opposed to the months of April and May the number of seepage points and
the discharge volumes increased throughout the study area By mapping the location
of seepage discharge it is possible to identify areas that should be monitored for
utility problems and potential earth movements However because of the varied
cause for seepage discharge the source of the seepage should be carefully
evaluated prior to concluding that a utility break and thus an earth movement may be
the cause The widespread seepage of water during periods of dry weather
combined with visible distortion of the ground surface may indicate that leaks in
underground utilities may be occurring
Earth Movement Definition
In an attempt to differentiate between shallow soil creep and deeper more significant
earth movements a distress rating system was developed to classify the level of
distress observed in the field as slight moderate or severe distress Slight distress
was generally associated with soil creep movements while moderate to severe
distress was associated with increasingly more significant signs of distress related to
possible deeper earth movement
To evaluate the generalized location of significant earth movement the distress
mapping was combined with available survey data underground utility break repair

SITE DESCRIPTION
The following site descriptions proceed from Kuahea Street to Waiomao Road to
Kipona Place and include description of the Waiomao Landslide and other areas that may

Kuahea Street
Kuahea Street forms a half circle loop connected at both ends with Waiomao Road
The relatively gently sloping central portion of Kuahea Street traverses the head scarp of the
old landslide The head scarp boundary of the Waiomao Landslide is visible on the asphalt
pavement surface as a distinct curved and irregularly shaped fracture line with some vertical
offset in the pavement The existing condition of the scarp appears active with some open
cracks in the asphalt patch of the scarp Visual observation indicates that some movement
along the scarp boundary continues to occur
Easterly or mauka of the slide head scarp directly across from the central portion of
the landslide head the pavement ground surface and rock retaining walls appear to be
affected by some ground settlement In general the homes located on the eastern and
southeastern sides of Kuahea Street across from the landslide appear to be unaffected by
significant earth movement perhaps due to their proximal location on generally more stable
quarry fill or in situ rock formation
The western side of Kuahea Street encompassing the landslide is comprised of
vacant sloping land that was purchased by the City graded and vegetated in the late 1970s
W O 3917 00 Page 17
to protect the ground surface and aid in stabilizing the landslide The vacant lots show no
overt signs of renewed earth movements such as ground scarps soil fractures or deformed
vegetative growth However some irregularly shaped features of unknown age representing

southeast of Kipona Place The approximate locations of the two slump features are shown
on the Structural Distress Plan Plate 5 1 It should be noted that a deposit of rocky debris
fill was observed covering the ground surface along the western road shoulder of Kuahea
Street fronting the landslide and vacant lots The debris appears to be rubble fill placed in
an effort to raise the grade of the road shoulder for safety reasons
Progressing in a northerly direction from the fault scarp the road pavement curbs
and private property appear relatively unaffected by earth movement other than some visible
signs of slight ground creep evidenced by some cracked or leaning retaining walls The

movements based on observation and communication with the resident
Progressing in a southerly direction from the slide area on the generally flat to gently
sloping road surface the asphalt pavement and curbs appear to be in fair but worn condition
with little visible sign of cracking Gradual warping of the pavement surface may indicate
some ground settlement Multiple asphalt patches related to utility work were observed The
homes bordering the southern perimeter of the landslide appear unaffected by earth
movement based on site observation and communication with the residents

Potential Earth Movement Areas
The three areas identified as Areas A B and C encompass possible zones of
earth movement as evidenced by our field observation of generally slight to moderate
ground disturbance The boundaries could not be defined with a great degree of certainty
as surface expressions are currently weakly developed and or irregular in occurrence
allowing only general conclusions to be drawn Furthermore the direction of movement
is also not clear based on field observations of widely varied direction vectors It should
be noted that the disturbed areas may ultimately be only a result of pronounced shallow
ground creep
Area A encompassing western Kuahea Street and the vicinity exhibits the most
significant new area of damage and indication of movement and therefore possesses the
greatest concern for potential movement within the study area The reporting of broken
and leaking underground utilities and the surface damage imparted to structures and
pavements indicate that the potential is high for this area to develop into a problem
landslide The boundaries of this potential earth movement Area A may extend in the
northerly direction to join with the western boundary of the existing Waiomao Landslide
The possibility of a connection between the two areas is mentioned because our field
reconnaissance revealed that generally slight to moderate distress to walls and fixed
structures was observed at the residences and sidewalks fronting the southern side of
Waiomao Road between Area A and the Waiomao Landslide The signs of distress
appear to be older with some repairs having been made making us believe that recent
ground movement has not been experienced In addition the street pavement and curbing
do not show significant signs of disturbance from ground movement Therefore our
preliminary site evaluation does not currently support the possibility of a connection
between the two areas of ground movement
