In accordance with our proposal letter dated December 1
1981 this report presents the results of our preliminary
geologic reconnaissance at the above referenced site
The purpose of this reconnaissance was to examine the
general surface soil conditions in the area in order to
evaluate the proposed access road construction from a soils
engineering point of view
The reconnaissance consisted of a review of available
soils and geologic maps and literature concerning the area
b a general geologic reconnaissance of the project area
including the inspection of the local road cuts and conver
sations with local residents and c a walk through geologic
reconnaissance of the proposed road alignments

PLANNED DEVELOPMENT
It is proposed to construct a golf course subdivision con
sisting of 33 residential lots of approximately half an acre
Size These lots will be serviced by approximately 3750
lineal feet of access roads It is our understanding that
only the roads will be graded and the lots sold on an as
is basis
It is anticipated that the road grading will involve cuts up
to 20 feet deep and fills up to 20 feet thick
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located in the Ouli section of the Dis
trict of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii It is bor
dered to the north by Waiulaula Gulch to the south by the
Mauna Kea Beach Hotel access road to the east by the Queen
Kaahumanu Highway and to the west by the existing Mauna Kea
Beach Hotel Golf Course
The terrain is generally rolling with some steep slopes
locally approaching a ratio of 1 1 H V and typically
reflects the original surface of thick basaltic lava flows
in the Aa form The ground surface elevations vary from 120
to 310 feet with the drainage tending towards the west

The site is thinly covered with dry grasses and dead kiawe
trees which were devastated in a recent fire
The ground surface is covered with basaltic cobbles and
gravel with some localized exposures of basalt clinker and
bedrock
CLIMATIC CONDITIONS
The average annual rainfall at the project site is between 5
to 10 inches and the average temperature varies between 75
and 85 degrees F The area is subjected to strong trade
winds forced through the wind gap between the Kohala Moun
tains and Mauna Kea At the time of our site visit strong
easterly winds were blowing from 15 to 25 knots with occa
Sional gusts to 35 knots
REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The project site is underlain by basaltic lava flows of the
Hamakua Volcanic Series which were deposited during the
Pleistocene Stearns and Macdonald 1946 These flows are
younger than the Pololu Volcanic Series of the Kohala Moun
tains which have been radioactively dated at 0 8 million
years but are older than the Laupahoehoe Series of Mauna Kea
GEOLABS HAWAII
W Page 4
which have been Similarly dated at 0 6 million years Mac
donald and Abbott 1970 therefore these flows would be
approximately 0 7 million years of age
The Hamakua series is typically composed of basalt lava
flows which are mantled by a thin layer of palagonitized
ash locally referred to as Pahala Ash At the project
Site this mantle of ash is extremely dry and friable and
subject to wind erosion This wind erosion has resulted in
a desert pavement i e a surface layer of lag deposited
cobbles and gravel left behind as the finer grained ash is
blown away covering most of the Site
In the area of the project Site the lava flows are gen
erally oceanite basalt with a high percentage of well deve
loped olivine and augite crystals The flows are in the Aa
form which is characterized by a rough spiny or rubbly sur
face of fragmental clinker underlain by a dense core of mas
Sive basalt which frequently has a thinner less continuous
layer of clinker beneath it Lava tubes are generally not
encountered in Aa lava flows

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Visual examination of the project site and the surrounding
area including nearby road cuts lead to the conjecture
that the subsurface conditions throughout the site are rela
tively uniform
Underlying the surficial desert pavement is a layer of yel
lowish brown to reddish brown soil Pahala Ash This layer
is predominately a dry low plasticity silt which contains
occasional gravel and cobbles This silt has very little
cohesion and once exposed will be highly subject to erosion
by both wind and rain In some localized areas this layer
is covered with a thin crust of a calcareous evaporite depo
Sit and small localized areas of hardpan soils are antici
pated The thickness of this layer generally varies from 2
to 5 feet
The lower portion of this ash deposit probably grades into a
layer of volcanic clinker that varies from 3 to 8 feet in
thickness This clinker deposit consists predominately of
cobble and gravel sized scoraceous basalt fragments and
represents the top surface of the Aa basalt flows The
deposit also contains many accretionary lava balls boul
ders up to 5 feet in diameter Examination of the

this clinker layer is weakly cemented by calcareous leachate
from the overlying ash deposit
Below the clinker layer is the core of the Aa flow which
locally is composed of dense fractured to massive olivine
basalt It is anticipated that this core may be as much as
20 feet thick

Site Grading
Prior to commencement of roadway grading surface vegetation
and organic material should be removed and wasted off site
In areas to receive fill or at grade construction tree
roots should be removed to a mininun depth of 3 feet and the
resulting excavation backfilled and compacted to 90 of its
maximum density as established by ASTM D 1557
In relative level areas prior to receiving fill the exist
ing ground should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches
moistened and compacted to 90 maximum density as per ASTM
Due to the dry and windy site conditions the contractor
should have an adequate water supply for moisture condi
tioning the soils in the grading area It is also advisable
to institute a dust control program during grading to mini
mize wind erosion and airborne dust nuisance
If loose soil and or dumped fill is encountered within the
roadway grading areas it should be undercut to firm natural
material prior to fill placement

In general the excavated soil and rock materials less than
12 inches in size may be used as fill material The exca

subgrade

Excavation
The field exploration indicates that the Site is underlain
at shallow depths by massive dense basalt mantled with con
solidated clinker and a thin layer of ash soil
It is anticipated that the consolidated clinker may be exca

blasting may be required where large accretionary lava
balls boulders are encountered
The massive dense basalt will most likely require special
excavating procedures such as hoe ram or blasting to make
the proposed cuts or utility trenches
