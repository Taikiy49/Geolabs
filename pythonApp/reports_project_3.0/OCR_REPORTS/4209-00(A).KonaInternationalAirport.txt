REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Mauna Loa which has
formed the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the site
appears to be pahoehoe flow which spread and ponded as it approached the ocean
Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface with an internal
structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe
lava These cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and represent both
lava tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava
tubes intra flow cavities are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow
leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally
along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located within the existing Kona International Airport at Keahole
in the District of Kona on the Island of Hawaii The proposed overhead and cantilever
signage under the scope of this report are located alongside the airport terminal as shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 Based on our field observations the project site is relatively
level and is generally paved with asphalt concrete at the ground surface
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling two borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 extending to a depth of about 25 feet below the
existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered a surficial layer of silty sands and
gravel of approximately 1 5 to 2 5 feet thick The surficial layer was underlain by hard
basalt rock formation interbedded with some thin clinker layers extending to the maximum
depth drilled of approximately 25 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field
exploration However groundwater levels can fluctuate depending on factors such as
seasonal rainfall groundwater withdrawal and or injection and other factors
Detailed descriptions of the subsurface materials encountered from our field
exploration are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2 in Appendix A of
this report

Shallow Foundations
Based on the competent subsurface conditions encountered and the structural
loads anticipated for the subject project we believe that shallow foundations consisting of
spread footings may be used for support of the proposed signage An allowable bearing
pressure of up to may be used for the design of footing foundations bearing on
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4209 00 A Page 7
the basalt rock formation encountered at shallow depths This bearing value is for dead
plus live loads and may be increased by one half 50 percent for transient loads such as
those caused by wind or seismic forces
The bottom of the footings should be embedded a minimum of 3 feet below the
lowest adjacent finished grade Depending on the depth to the basalt rock formation
deeper excavations may be required at localized areas to provide a competent bearing
surface for the foundations
If the footings are designed in accordance with the recommendations presented
herein total settlement of footings is estimated to be on the order of one half 74 inch with
differential settlements on the order of one fourth 1 inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 6 may be used for footings bearing on the underlying basalt
rock formation Resistance to lateral loads due to passive pressure may be calculated
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 400 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf
These values assume that the concrete for footings is cast neat against the basalt rock
Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches
below finished grade should be neglected
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the
foundation bearing conditions and the required embedment depths
Foundation Probing
Based on our previous experience in the area voids and or cavities are common
in the basalt rock formation encountered at the project site To reduce the potential for
loss of foundation support resulting from the collapse of cavities below footings we
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4209 00 A Page 8
recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting be implemented during
construction
For estimating purposes we recommend that a probe hole be drilled at each
spread footing location one probe hole for every 50 square feet of footing area The
probe holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend at least 10 feet
below the planned bottom of footing elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or
suspected during the probing operation additional probes should be drilled at closer
spacing to aid in delineating the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The
probe holes and cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected
at low to moderate pressures We recommend that a low strength sand cement grout
with a slump range of 6 to 9 inches be utilized for the grouting operations In lieu of the
sand cement grout fluid lean concrete such as controlled low strength material flowable
fill may also be used for the grouting
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that the probe drill be available on site until the probing and grouting
operations are complete It should be noted that a longer lag time between
probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in the
construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of
cavities and or voids and to allow for additional recommendations to be made if excess
grout take and or changed conditions are observed

The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support from both
skin friction and end bearing between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable

and may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces
Lateral loads imposed on the foundations may be resisted by a combination of the
following 1 resistance of the shaft soil system to deflect and 2 passive resistance on
the vertical faces of shaft caps where applicable Lateral load resistance of the drilled
shaft is a function of the stiffness of the surrounding material the stiffness of the shaft
allowable deflection at the top of shaft and induced moment in the shaft Based on the
information provided each drilled shaft will be subject to a lateral load ranging between
about 1 and 6 kips and an overturning moment on the order of about 18 to 120 kip feet at
the ground surface Our lateral load analyses indicated that the estimated deflection at
the top of the drilled shaft should be less than one fourth inch
The anticipated settlements are estimated to be on the order of less than
one fourth inch We believe that a significant portion of the settlements is elastic and
should occur as the loads are applied

Torsion Resistance
In general the overhead cantilever sign foundations will be subjected to torsional
moments resulting in torsion on the drilled shaft foundations Torsion may be resisted by
the side shear along the drilled shaft surface and adjacent materials In our foundation
analyses we have evaluated the drilled shafts subject to the torsional moments provided
by the project structural engineer Based on our analyses we believe that the 5 foot
diameter drilled shafts extending to the lengths recommended should be able to resist the
torsional moments without significant movements We wish to emphasize that the sides
of the drilled holes should be relatively free of loose materials and the concrete for the
drilled shaft should have close contact with the in situ materials after the drilled shaft has
been constructed to provide the torsion resistance required
Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the subject project because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations
However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend upon the contractor s method
of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the following conditions
would have an effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations
Loose Subsoils Dense Rock Formation
Based on our field exploration the proposed new signage locations are generally
underlain by sandy gravel in the upper 3 feet and clinker layers in the underlying
basalt rock formation To reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes
temporary casing may be required during the drilled shaft installation
Based on our field exploration hard basalt rock formation may be anticipated at
shallow depths at the proposed signage locations Therefore coring into the hard
basalt rock formation will be required Appropriate drilling equipment and coring
tools should be utilized by the drilled shaft contractor to install the drilled shafts to
the depths and dimensions recommended herein

Workmanship
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding materials Therefore
proper construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care
in drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch range should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and surrounding materials The
concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for
segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix If pre cast concrete pole
bases are used the gap between the pole bases and the surrounding materials
should be backfilled with lean concrete A representative of Geolabs Hawaii
should be present to observe the installation of drilled shafts during construction

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings extending to a depth of about 25 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
