SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our field exploration, the drilled borings encountered residual soil 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 46.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The residual soil encountered generally consisted of medium stiff to very stiff 
clayey silt.  
Groundwater levels were encountered in the drilled borings at depths of about 37.5 
and 39.8 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration. It 
should be noted that groundwater levels may vary with seasonal rainfall, time of the year, 
surface water runoff, and other factors. 
We understand that it is desired to mitigate the slope erosion adjacent to the 
existing house. Based on our field exploration, we recommend a soil-nailed slope system 
to stabilize the slope. In general, a soil-nailed slope system consists of a series of 
individual reinforcing bars grouted into drilled holes to stabilize the slope. A shotcrete 
facing is generally applied to the face of the reinforced slope. 
The soil nails should be installed by drilling a minimum 6.0-inch diameter hole with 
an inclination of approximately 15 degrees from horizontal. The inclination of the nails may 
be increased to avoid existing underground structures. The soil nail bar should consist of 
an ASTM A615 Grade 75 solid threaded bar with a minimum bar diameter of 1.125 inches.  
Based on our soil nail analysis, we recommend using a design soil nail length of 
20 feet extending into the stiff residual soil encountered in our field exploration. The soil 
nails should be spaced 10 feet on-center vertically and 10 feet on-center horizontally. The 
first row of soil nails should be installed about 2 to 3 feet below the top of the slope. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and 
recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed Slope Stabilization at 3006 Kiele Lane project. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses. These recommendations are only intended for the design of slope 
stabilization. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project involves stabilizing the slope below the existing house at 3006 Kiele 
Lane in Lihue on the Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The existing slope height is approximately 
37 feet with slope inclinations ranging from about one and four-tenths horizontal to one 
vertical (1.4H:1V) to 0.6H:1V. A single-family residential structure is constructed near 
the top of the slope where the slope surface has eroded to encroach on the exterior 
deck foundations. We understand that a slope remediation measure consisting of the 
construction of a new retaining wall at the top of the slope was being considered. 
However, it is our opinion that the construction of a new retaining wall at the top of the 
slope would not mitigate the eroding slope problem. We understand that the 
development of slope remediation measure is desired for the project.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the 
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene 
Epoch (more than 2¼ million years before present). Following the cessation of this main 
shield building phase, renewed volcanic activity occurred with the extrusion of basaltic 
lavas of the post-erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the 
alluvial sediments of the Palikea Formation. 
Most of the soils in the project area consist of residual soils derived from the 
in-situ weathering of the Koloa Volcanic Series igneous rocks. In general, residual soils 
grade to saprolite and weathered basalt formations with increasing depth. Rocks of the 
Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt 
extruded from groups of vents aligned in north-south trends in various locales. 
Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials, which usually form low cinder cones 
at the vent. Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the 
Island of Kauai, including the site of the proposed project. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, many sea level changes occurred as a result of 
widespread continental glaciation. As the great continental glaciers accumulated and 
advanced, the level of the ocean fell since less water was available to fill the oceanic 
basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global sea levels rose because 
more water was available. The landmass of Kauai remained essentially stable during 
these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These glacio-eustatic 
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea, which were both higher and lower relative to 
the present sea level of the Island of Kauai.

Hawaii. The project site is bounded by Nawiliwili Stream to the north, Kiele Lane to the 
west and developed house lots to the south and east. The existing house is constructed 
on approximately the southern one-third of the property.  To the north of the existing

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
We understand that it is desired to mitigate the slope erosion encroaching on the 
existing house exterior deck foundations. Based on the existing site conditions and 
subsurface conditions encountered, we recommend utilizing a soil-nailed slope system 
to reduce the on-going slope erosion and to stabilize the slope in front of the existing 
house.  
Based on our field exploration and soil nail analyses, items pertaining to the 
soil-nailed slope design and installation are addressed in the following subsections: 
1. Slope Preparation 
2. Soil-Nailed Slope 
3. Soil Nails 
4. Soil Nail Installation 
5. Soil Nail Testing 
6. Shotcrete Facing 
7. Drainage 
8. Site Drainage 
3.1 
Slope Preparation 
At the on-set of construction, surface vegetation, debris, loose material, and 
deleterious material should be removed from the slope face and adjacent areas. Slope 
scaling should also be performed to remove loose rock fragments on the existing slope 
face. Care should be taken in removing material so that the stability of the existing slope 
is not compromised. Flash coats of shotcrete should be applied to the exposed slope 
surface to fill exposed voids and to prepare a relatively smooth slope surface for 
installation of the soil-nailed slope.  
3.2 
Soil-Nailed Slope 
A soil-nailed slope system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars 
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize the eroded slope. A shotcrete facing is generally 
applied to the face of the reinforced slope. To provide a natural rock appearance, the 
shotcrete facing may be sculpted and stained to match the appearance of the 
surrounding environment if desired. The reinforcing bars provide additional tensile and 
shear strength to the slope. The reinforced soil mass behaves in a manner similar to a

3.3 
Soil Nails 
The soil nails should be installed by drilling a minimum 6.0-inch diameter hole 
with an inclination of approximately 15 degrees from horizontal. The inclination of the 
nails may be increased to avoid existing underground structures. The soil nail bar 
should consist of an ASTM A615 Grade 75 solid threaded bar with a minimum bar 
diameter of 1.125 inches. We recommend using a double corrosion protection system 
for the nails consisting of epoxy-coated bars surrounded by neat cement grout or 
sand-cement mixture with a minimum 28-day compressive strength of 4,000 pounds per 
square inch (psi). 
Based on our soil nail analysis, we recommend using a design nail length of 
20 feet extending into the residual soil encountered in our field exploration. The soil 
nails should be spaced at 10 feet on-center vertically and 10 feet on-center horizontally. 
The first row of nails should be installed about 2 to 3 feet below the top of the slope. 
Based on the above design parameters, the soil-nailed slope system should have 
a factor of safety of 1.5 or greater for the combined internal and external stability for the 
static loading condition, considering a surcharge load of 150 pounds per square foot 
(psf) from the existing residence.  
3.4 
Soil Nail Installation 
To facilitate the installation of the soil nails, a shotcrete flash coat should first be 
applied to the face of the exposed slope. Soil nail installation activities should avoid 
causing instability to the existing slope and the house structure above.

3.5 
Soil Nail Testing 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated behind the soil-nailed slope, we 
recommend performing a minimum of one pre-production verification test on a sacrificial 
soil nail installed near the top of the slope. In addition, proof tests should be performed 
on at least 10 percent of the production soil nails during construction to confirm the 
bond stresses used in the design. 
The verification test should be loaded gradually to 150 percent of the design load 
(design load = 16 kips) for the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of 
about 200 percent of the design load. In addition, the load at every loading increment 
should be held for at least 10 minutes, and creep tests should be held for at least 60 
minutes. The proof tests should consist of subjecting the soil nail to at least 150 percent 
of the design loads and the load should be held for at least 10 minutes (until stable). 
The proof test nails may be incorporated into the permanent soil nail retaining slope 
provided they satisfy the test criteria. Pullout tests and creep tests on the soil nails are 
integral components of the design for the soil-nailed slope system. Therefore, we 
recommend conducting pullout tests and creep tests in the presence of a Geolabs 
representative. 
3.6 
Shotcrete Facing 
Shotcrete placement should be performed by an experienced nozzleman. Prior to 
production shotcreting, it is recommended that unreinforced test panels of shotcrete be 
constructed for inspection. We understand that the shotcrete facing may be sculptured. 
Therefore, adequate thickness of the final facing should be provided to accommodate 
the sculptured surfacing. A bottom key should be provided for the shotcrete facing.

3.7 
Drainage 
The soil-nailed slope should be well-drained to reduce the potential for the 
build-up of hydrostatic pressures. Drainage elements consisting of 2-foot wide strips of 
a prefabricated drainage composite product may be used on the face of the slope 
before the application of the shotcrete backfill or the shotcrete flash coat used for 
temporary stabilization. The prefabricated drainage composite product should extend 
from the top of the slope to the base of the slope and be hydraulically connected to a 
weep hole near the base of the soil-nailed slope. 
3.8 
Site Drainage 
We believe that one of the causes of the observed slope distress and erosion is 
the surface runoff water discharging over the slopes. To reduce the potential for slope 
erosion and instability, we recommend that the surface runoff water be directed away 
from the top of the slopes and into existing drainage structures.  
We recommend the installation of a concrete swale or drainage channel near the 
top of the slope to carry surface runoff water away from the slope face.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon 
information obtained from the field borings. Variations of conditions between and 
beyond the field borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may 
not become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it 
will be necessary to re-evaluate the recommendations presented herein. 
The field boring locations are approximate, having been estimated by using a 
hand-held Global Positioning System (GPS) device and shown on the 3006 Kiele Lane 
– Retaining Wall Plan prepared by Kodani & Associates Engineers, LLC in March 2023. 
Elevations of the borings were estimated based on spot elevations and contours shown 
on the same Plan. The physical locations and elevations of the borings should be 
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification breaks shown on graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between soil/rock types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown 
on the graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. These data 
have been reviewed, and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. 
However, it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variations in seasonal 
rainfall, surface runoff and other factors. These data have been reviewed and 
interpretations made in the formulation of this report.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to a depth of about 46.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers. 
 Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-2.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch O.D. Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch O.D. standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D2937) 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Six Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. Test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
test results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Two one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils. The test 
results are summarized on Plate B-2. 
Six Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) were 
performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the 
soils. The undrained shear strength test results along with the shear stress-strain curves 
are presented on Plates B-3 through B-8. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the in-situ soils. The test results are 
presented on Plate B-9. 
One Consolidation test (ASTM D2435) was performed on a sample of the 
potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the 
materials encountered.  Consolidation test results are presented on Plate B-10.
