MICROPILE FOUNDATIONS
Based on the column loads provided and the subsurface conditions encountered at
the project site, we believe that appreciable foundation settlements would occur if the new
structures were supported on a shallow foundation system. To reduce the potential for
appreciable differential settlements between the new columns and the existing structure,
we recommend using a deep foundation system to support the new columns. Considering
the space constraints and congested existing underground utility lines, we believe that
micropile foundations may be used to support the planned new structures.
In general, the micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter
(usually less than 12 inches) drilled and grouted pile with steel reinforcing. The micropile
foundation is typically constructed by drilling a borehole, placing reinforcing steel in the
hole, and then grouting. Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed readily in
access restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions. In
addition, micropile installation generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent structures,
the adjacent soils, and the environment.
We believe that a micropile system with a minimum grout bulb diameter of 5 to
7 inches may be used to support the new columns. We recommend that the following
allowable compressive load capacities with various pile length be designed for the
micropile. The allowable compressive load capacity for the piles is for dead-plus-live loads
and may be increased by 30 percent for transient loads, such as wind or seismic forces.
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the site, we recommend that the
capacity of the micropile foundation be derived primarily from skin friction between the
micropile pile and the marine deposits and mudrock formation underlying the coralline
detritus encountered in the borings.

MICROPILE LOAD TEST PROGRAM
It should be noted that the bond stress between the grout bulb and the soil is highly
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the
contractor. Therefore, the bond stress between the grout bulb and the soil may
considerably vary between different contractors and micropile foundation systems.
In order to determine whether the contractor's methods of micropile installation are
adequate and to determine the ultimate grout-to-soil bond stress, we recommend
performing at least one pre-production load test on a sacrificial micropile(s). Considering
the smaller size of the micropile, it may be difficult to maintain the compressive test loading
centralized during the load test. Therefore, our opinion is that a tensile load test may be
performed to evaluate the developed grout-to-soil bond stress.

In general, the pre-production load test should be performed in accordance with
ASTM D 3689. Based on our experience, we believe that the load test should be
conducted no earlier than seven days after completion of the micropile installation.
The load test micropile should be loaded gradually to at least 200 percent of the
allowable design load in tension. Each load increment should be about 10 percent of the
maximum test load and held for a minimum of 5 minutes. Each load increment should be
held until the rate of movement is no greater than 0.01 inch per hour, but each load
increment does not need to be held longer than 2 hours. We recommend holding the
maximum test load (200 percent of the design load) for a minimum of 1 to 2 hours
depending on the recorded movements of the load test micropile. The pre-production load
test is an integral part of the design of the micropile foundation system.
In addition to the pre-production load test, we also recommend performing pullout
tests (proof tests) on selected micropiles during production construction to confirm the load
carrying capacity of the installed micropiles. We recommend testing approximately
10 percent of the total number of micropiles or a minimum of 3 micropiles, whichever is
greater, for pullout.
The sacrificial pre-production load test and pullout tests on the micropiles are
integral parts of the design of the micropile foundation system. Therefore, we recommend
conducting the pre-production load test and pullout tests under the observation of a
Geolabs representative.
MICROPILE CONSTRUCTION CONSIDERATIONS
In general, corrosion protection is one of the concerns for the micropile foundation
system. Depending on the micropile foundation system used, there is a potential for
having inconsistent grout cover around the reinforcing steel of the micropile. We believe
that the potential for inconsistent grout cover could be significantly reduced if good
installation practices and good quality control measures are implemented.
We believe that a temporary steel casing should be used to reduce the risk of
inconsistent grout cover during the micropile installation. The minimum 5-inch diameter
(O.D.) temporary steel casing should be installed within the entire length of the micropile.
In addition, a grout with water-cement ratio of about 0.40 to 0.45 should be used.
Good quality control measures will assure the better quality of the micropile
product. We believe that a metering device to measure and record the flow rate of the
grout pumped into the micropile should be provided and should be working at all times.
The flow rate of the grout should be monitored carefully during the installation. The
contractor should slow down the temporary casing withdrawal rate when a cavity is
encountered, as evidenced by rapid grout level dropping.

SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration, the Third Level Sterile Corridor (TLSC) site was
generally underlain by fill and marine deposit overlying coral reef materials consisting of
coral formation and coralline detritus with a thin volcanic tuff layer interbedded. The top
elevation of the coral reef varied from approximately +7.5 to -2 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL)
and extended to about -48 feet MSL the maximum depth drilled. The thickness of the
volcanic tuff varied from 2 to 5 feet. Groundwater was encountered in the borings at
depths of about 10 to 15 feet below the existing ground surface during our field
exploration. The measured groundwater levels generally correspond to about +1 to
-2 feet MSL.
The boring drilled at the Enclosed Exit Stairs (EES) site encountered about 6 feet of
fill material over coral reef materials consisting of coral formation, sandstone and coralline
detritus. A thin layer of volcanic tuff formation was also encountered embedded in the coral
reef at approximately -27 feet MSL. Groundwater was encountered at depth of about
10 feet below the ground surface during our field exploration, equivalent to about +0.5 feet
MSL.
Based on subsurface conditions encountered in the borings at the TLSC site and
the structural load demands, we recommend that the new corridor structure be supported
by a deep foundation system consisting of 36-inch diameter cast-in-place concrete drilled
shaft. In general, the drilled shafts for the new corridor structure should extend to about
40 feet below the bottom of footing elevation. An allowable compressive load capacity of
up to 550 kips may be used in the design with a factor of safety of 2.0.
Based on the relatively competent subsurface conditions encountered at the EES
site, we recommend that the new stair structures be supported on a shallow foundation
system consisting of spread footings. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf
may be used for design of the stair structure foundations bearing on the compacted on-site
fill materials.
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific
recommendations for design of the airport improvements project.

SECTION 2.0 — SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2.1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes, Waianae and Koolau. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island.
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in
age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae became
extinct while Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below
Koolau lava.
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano, and its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio-eustatic
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the
Honolulu Coastal Plain.
During the later Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation. As the continental glaciers advanced, less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below its present
stand. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to above its present level.
Glacio-eustatic sea level fluctuations affected the processes of erosion and
deposition. The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal
deposits, and induration of the calcareous reef materials. In addition, renewed subaerial
erosion of the upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils
under relatively high-energy conditions.

During periods of no significant sea level changes, continued stream action
extended the alluvial delta and fans seaward by deposition of alluvium over the

The geology of the project area has been further complicated by the deposition of
recent pyroclastic materials and lava flows from eruptions of vents of the Honolulu

Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy
estuarine or lagoonal environments, resulting in thick deposits of soft harbor sediments
along the coast in areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways. Urban
development and reclamation projects have since altered the natural formation of the
area.

2.3. Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the new TLSC site were explored by drilling and
sampling four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, as shown on the Site
Plan, Plate 2.1. The borings were advanced to depths of about 60 feet below the
existing ground surface.
The TLSC site was underlain by surface fill over marine deposit and coral reef
materials (upper coral ledge) consisting of coral formation or coralline detritus with a thin
layer of volcanic tuff formation interbedded. The fill materials generally consisted of
mixtures of medium dense gravelly sands and stiff clayey silts extending to depths of
about 3 to 9 feet below the existing ground surface. The marine deposits generally
consisted of stiff to very stiff clayey soils extending to depths of about 9 to 16 feet below
the existing ground surface.

3.1.1 Uplift and Lateral Load Resistance
In general, uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead weight of the
drilled shaft and by shear along the shaft surface and the adjacent soils. The
ultimate uplift load capacities should be used for transient loads only. For sustained
uplift loads acting on the foundations, the ultimate uplift load capacities should be
reduced by a factor of safety of 2.0.
Lateral loads imposed on the new TLSC structure may be resisted by a
combination of the lateral load capacity of the drilled shafts and the passive
pressure acting against the near-vertical faces of the foundation caps.
In general, lateral load resistance of the drilled shafts is a function of the stiffness of
the surrounding soil, the stiffness of the shaft, allowable deflection at the top of
shaft, and induced moment in the shaft. In general, we recommend spacing the
drilled shafts a minimum of 2.5 times the diameter of the shaft from
center-to-center. The effect of group action should be considered in the lateral load
analyses.

3.1.6 Non-Destructive Integrity Testing
We recommend that non-destructive integrity testing be conducted on the
production drilled shafts. One of the non-destructive integrity testing
methods, Crosshole Sonic Logging (CSL), has gained widespread use
and acceptance for integrity testing of drilled shafts.
Crosshole Sonic Logging techniques are based on the propagation of
sound waves through concrete. In general, the actual velocity of sound
wave propagation in concrete is dependent on the concrete material
properties, geometry of the element and wavelength of the sound waves.
When ultrasonic frequencies are generated, Pressure (P) waves and
Shear (S) waves travel though the concrete. If anomalies are contained in
the concrete, the anomalies will reduce the P-wave travel velocity in the
concrete. Anomalies in the drilled shaft concrete may include soil particles,
gravel, water, voids, contaminated concrete, and highly segregated
constituent particles.
The transit time of an ultrasonic P-wave signal may be measured between
an ultrasonic transmitter and receiver in two parallel water-filled access
Hawaii ° California
SECTION 3 — DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
tubes placed into the concrete during construction. The P-wave velocity
can be obtained by dividing the measured transit time from the distance
between the transmitter and receiver. Therefore, anomalies may be
detected (if they exist).
To reduce the potential de-bonding between the access tube and the
surrounding concrete, we recommend that the access tubes consist of
standard steel pipe with a minimum inside diameter of 2 inches. In
addition, the access tube should be equipped with watertight couplings.
The access tubes should be securely attached to the interior of the
reinforcing cage as near to parallel as possible in the drilled shaft. We
recommend that a minimum of three access tubes be cast into the
concrete of the 36-inch diameter drilled shafts. Details pertaining to the
configuration of the access tubes for crosshole sonic logging tests are
presented on Plate 4.
In addition, the access tubes should extend from the bottom of the drilled
shaft reinforcing cage to at least 3.5 feet above the top of the shaft. The
bottom of the access tube should be permanently capped. It is imperative
that joints required to achieve the full length of the access tubes be
watertight. It is the contractor's responsibility to take extra care to prevent
damaging the access tubes during the placement of the reinforcing cage
into the drilled hole. The tubes should be filled with potable water as soon
as possible, but not later than 2 hours after the concrete placement.
Subsequently, the top of the access tubes should be capped with

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling five borings,
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to depths of about 56 to 60 feet below
the existing ground surface. The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown
on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2 and borings were drilled using truck-mounted
drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who observed the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic
representations of the materials encountered in the borings drilled are presented on the
Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-5.2.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving
a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In
addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 or 24-inch
drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths.
Core samples of coral and/or rock materials encountered at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral and/or rock
formation. The coral and/or rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically
recovered for examination and description.
Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of soil and/or rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material
recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if
3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be
74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of coral and/or rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run
that is sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937)
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties. The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B-1.
Eight Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 and C 136) were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution of the soils are
provided on Plates B-2 and B-3.
Two Unconsolidated Undrained  Triaxial Compression (TXUU) _ tests
(ASTM D 2850) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained
shear strength of the silty and clayey soils encountered. The approximate in-situ
effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the
relatively “undisturbed” soil sample. The test results and the stress-strain curve are
presented on Plates B-4 and B-5.
Seven unconfined compression tests (ASTM D 2938) were performed on the
selected core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the
weathered coral formation. The test results are summarized on Plate B-6.
