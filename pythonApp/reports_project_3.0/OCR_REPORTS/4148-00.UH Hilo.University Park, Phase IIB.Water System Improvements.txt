REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the eastern flank of Mauna Kea which makes up the
northeastern portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the site appears to
consist of pahoehoe flow which spread and ponded as it approached the ocean
Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure
of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas Cavities
are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow
cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes are formed when
molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4148 00 Page 5
extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally
smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted through
the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
clinker layers The permeability of the fractures and cavities is high therefore they serve as
the major water transmission features

Reservoir Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe that
the on site basalt rock formation would provide suitable foundation support for the
proposed reservoir tank structure We anticipate that the basalt rock formation at the
foundation elevation will likely consist of closely fractured basalt formation To provide
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 4148 00 Page 8
more uniform support for the shallow foundation system due to the variable hardness of
the rock we recommend that the subsurface materials below the bottom of the tank floor
and foundations be over excavated and be replaced with at least 12 inches of aggregate
base course
The 12 inches of compacted aggregate base course should extend a minimum of
2 feet laterally beyond the limits of the tank structure The aggregate base course should
consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Prior to placement of the aggregate base course we
recommend that the exposed basalt rock subgrade be proof rolled with a 15 ton vibratory
drum roller a minimum of six passes to assist in detecting and possibly collapsing
near surface cavities
For design of foundations bearing on the 12 inch aggregate base course placed
over basalt rock formation we recommend that an allowable bearing pressure of up to
6 000 pounds per square foot psf be used This bearing value is for dead plus live loads
and may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces For design of the tank floor a modulus of subgrade reaction of 250 pounds
per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the compacted aggregate base
course
Foundations and thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be
embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade If the
foundations are located adjacent to a slope sloping down away from the foundations the
foundations should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum 8 foot setback
distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of the foundations to the slope face
Where foundations are to be located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities the
foundations should extend to a depth below a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V
imaginary plane projected upward from the bottom of the below grade structure

Foundation Probing
Based on our site reconnaissance and previous experience in the vicinity cavities
and or voids are likely present in the basaltic lava flows encountered at the project site In
addition Boring No 1 encountered a cavity or void between about 6 and 8 feet below the
existing ground surface corresponding to about the foundation subgrade elevation To
reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the cavities
below foundations we recommend that a program of cavity probing and grouting at the
foundation locations be conducted during construction
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 4148 00 Page 10
For estimating purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each interior
foundation location and at 10 foot on center for the perimeter wall foundation The probe
holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the
planned bottom of foundation elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or
suspected during the probing operation additional probes should be drilled at closer spacing
to help delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and
cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate
pressures A typical sand cement grout should have a slump of about 6 to 9 inches In lieu
of the sand cement grout a highly fluid lean concrete such as controlled low strength
material flowable fill may also be used for the grouting operations
Because of the potential for encountering large cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during bidding
In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting
operations are complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer lag time
between probing grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in the
construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative from Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of
cavities and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or
changed conditions are observed

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
In general structural fills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above optimum moisture and compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The aggregate base course layer
below the reservoir should also be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 4148 00 Page 13
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where fills are composed of rock fragments and or cobbles such as the structural fill
conventional compaction testing is not practicable Therefore a testing program to
evaluate the number of passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level
of compaction should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project
under the observation of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii On the basis of this
testing program the number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for
adequate compaction
Excavation
Our field exploration disclosed that the reservoir site is underlain by medium hard
volcanic rock at shallow depths directly beneath the existing ground surface In
addition the near surface basalt rock formation appears to be vesicular porous in
nature It is our opinion that the volcanic rock at the site may be excavated with
normal heavy excavation equipment such as ripping with large bulldozers where the
rock is near the existing ground surface However excavations in some localized
areas exposing hard basalt formations will likely require the use of hoerams or
chipping
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials are based
on our visual observation of the existing basalt rock formations and field data from the
borings drilled at the site We recommend that contractors proposing to bid on this
project be encouraged to examine the site conditions and the boring data to make
their own interpretation

APPENDIX A

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the reservoir tank site were explored by drilling and
sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about
40 to 40 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the borings
drilled are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
