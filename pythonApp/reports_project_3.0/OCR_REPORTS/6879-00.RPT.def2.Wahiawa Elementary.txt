SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings generally encountered residual soils consisting of very stiff to hard 
clayey silts to the maximum depths explored of 6.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration.  
However, while groundwater levels can vary significantly depending on rainfall, 
temperature, surface runoff, groundwater withdrawal, and other factors, it is felt that the 
project site is situated at an elevation such that groundwater should not have a significant 
impact on the execution of the work. 
Based on our laboratory tests, the surface clayey soils exhibit a low shrink/swell 
potential if maintained at suitable soil moisture contents.  Therefore, special attention 
should be given to the subgrade preparation requirements for this project in order to 
reduce the potential of subsequent saturation and softening of the subgrade soils. 
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils and new fill 
materials are critical elements of earthwork, Geolabs should perform observations and soil 
density tests during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of 
compaction and the proper moisture content on each fill lift. 
Falling head infiltration tests performed at the site indicate that the subsurface 
infiltration rate at the site is on the order of about 0.13 inches per hour.  This rate is 
considered to be restrictive for the use of infiltration systems for the management of storm 
water runoff.  
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
recommendations for design of the project.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the new Parking and Student Drop-Off Area at Wahiawa Elementary 
School in Wahiawa on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project location and general 
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses.  These recommendations are intended for the design of site preparation and 
pavements only.  The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to 
the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
Based on the information provided, it is proposed to develop a new parking lot 
and student drop-off area at the existing Wahiawa Elementary School in Wahiawa on 
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project site is situated on the eastern side of the 
existing school campus fronting Glen Avenue, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
A detailed development plan was not available for this exploration; however, we 
anticipate that site grading will involve shallow cuts and fills on the order of less than 
about 1 to 3 feet in depth and thickness.  It is also anticipated that the parking and 
drop-off area will be paved with asphaltic concrete.  Concrete slabs-on-grade may be 
required for sidewalks and appurtenant structures, such as bus stop shelters. 
Based on our experience with other projects in the vicinity, we anticipated that 
the site would be underlain by stiff clayey residual soils grading with depth to saprolite 
derived from basaltic rock formation.  Key considerations for our geotechnical 
engineering exploration have included the following: 
Development of subgrade preparation procedures. 
Development of pavement design recommendations. 
Evaluation of the infiltration rates for the on-site shallow subsurface soils.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger 
Koolau shield is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct while 
Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas 
banking against its eastern flank.  The banking of the Koolau lavas against Waianae 
formed a broad plateau in the central portion of the island, which is now known as the 
Schofield Plateau.  The site is situated in the central portion of this plateau. 
In-situ chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred during the last 
1 to 2 million years.  The weathering process has transformed the surface lavas into a 
mantle of residual soils and saprolite.  In general, residual soils and saprolite are 
composed mainly of silty materials and are typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic 
rocks.  The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with depth. 
2.2 
Site Description 
The project site is located at 1402 Glen Avenue in Wahiawa on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii.  The site is situated on the eastern side of the existing school campus 
and is presently used as a playing field for the students.  During the time of our 
exploration, there was limited use of a portion of the site as parking and as a student 
drop-off area due to road work on Glen Avenue (i.e., an area in the vicinity of the large 
monkeypod tree to the north and west of the paved driveway had been coned off as 
parking and as a turnaround area for student drop off and pick up). 
Based on the topographic survey map provided, the project site is relatively level 
with existing ground elevations at the project site ranging from approximately +990 feet 
Mean Sea Level (MSL) along Glen Avenue to +998 feet MSL in the northeastern portion 
of the schoolyard.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration indicates that the project site is generally underlain by 
residual soils consisting of very stiff to hard clayey silts.  We did not encounter 
groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. 
Based on our laboratory tests, the surface clayey soils exhibit a low shrink/swell 
potential if maintained at suitable soil moisture contents.  Therefore, special attention 
should be given to the subgrade preparation requirements for this project in order to 
reduce the swelling potential of the subgrade soils.  Because moisture conditioning and 
compaction of the clayey subgrade soils and new fill materials are critical elements of 
earthwork, Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during site 
grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the 
proper moisture content on each fill lift. 
Falling head infiltration tests performed at the site indicate that the subsurface 
infiltration rate at the site is on the order of about 0.13 inches per hour.  This rate is 
considered to be restrictive for the use of infiltration systems for the management of storm 
water runoff.  
Our geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of site 
preparation and pavements are further discussed in the following sections of this report. 
3.1 
Site Preparation 
Based on the relatively flat topography at the project site, we anticipate that the 
majority of the site preparation work for the proposed project will consist of minor cuts 
and fills to achieve the design finish grades.  The project site is generally underlain by 
clayey silts with low shrink/swell potential if maintained at suitable moisture contents, 
i.e., not allowed to dry below optimum moisture content.  Therefore, special attention 
should be given to the subgrade preparation for the project construction. 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly.  Vegetation, organic debris, and other unsuitable 
materials, should be removed and disposed of properly off-site or in a designated area

to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials with the cleared 
and grubbed spoils.  Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing 
should be over-excavated to expose firm natural material, and the resulting excavation

Some trees were observed within the footprints of the new facility.  The growth 
and decay of the root system may cause potential distress to the future slab-on-grade 
and foundation systems, such as upheaval, cracking, and/or settlement.  Therefore, we 
recommend that the construction location is such that these trees are set back as far 
away as possible from the new site improvements.  In the event that an existing tree 
needs to be removed to accommodate the construction, the over-excavation resulting 
from the demolition and tree removal should be backfilled with compacted granular fill 
material.  The select granular fill material should be moisture-conditioned to above 
optimum moisture content, placed in 8-inch level loose lifts, and compacted. Areas that 
are more than 2 feet below finish subgrade should be recompacted to a minimum of 
90 percent relative compaction. Areas that are less than 2 feet below finish subgrade 
should be recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative density.  
After clearing and demolition, the subgrade areas should be scarified to a depth 
of about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture content, and recompacted.  Areas that are more than 2 feet below finish 
subgrade should be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.   
Areas that are less than 2 feet below finish subgrade should be recompacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative density.  Where shrinkage cracks are observed after 
compaction of the subgrade, we recommend preparing the subgrade soil again as 
recommended above.  Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade 
due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft 
areas and replacement with well-compacted fill.  The need for over-excavation due to 
soft subgrade soil conditions should be evaluated by a Geolabs representative in the 
field.

Fill materials required for the project construction should consist of 
non-expansive select granular material, such as crushed coral or basalt.  The material 
should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in 
largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the 
No. 200 sieve.  The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and 
should have a maximum swell of one percent or less when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D 1883. Aggregate subbase material required for the project should consist of 
crushed basaltic aggregates and should conform to Section 30 of the City and County of 
Honolulu, Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction (September 1986).  A 
qualified geotechnical engineer should test imported fill materials for conformance with 
these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. 
Fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture 
content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted.  
Areas that are more than 2 feet below finish subgrade should be compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.   Areas that are less than 2 feet below 
finish subgrade should be compacted  to a minimum of 95 percent relative density.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage 
of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with 
ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils are 
critical elements of the earthwork, Geolabs should perform observations and soil density 
tests during the site grading operations to assist the contractor in obtaining the required 
degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on each fill lift.  Where 
compaction is less than required, additional compactive effort should be applied with 
adjustment of moisture content, as necessary, to obtain the specified compaction.  It 
should be noted that the moisture content requirement of the general fills and 
subgrades (at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture) is an important requirement 
considering the moderately expansive nature of the clayey soils.

3.2 
Pavement Design 
It is anticipated that flexible asphaltic concrete pavements will be used for the 
new student drop-off and parking facility.  In general, we anticipate that the vehicle 
loading for the parking lot portion of this project will consist primarily of passenger 
vehicles, light trucks and vans.  The driveway and other pavements may be required to 
support heavier buses.  We have made our preliminary pavement design assuming the 
pavement subgrade soil will be the clayey soils encountered with a minimum California 
Bearing Ratio (CBR) of 20. 
Based on the site conditions encountered and the traffic loading conditions 
presented above, the following pavement sections may be used for preliminary design 
purposes: 
Standard Duty Pavement (Subjected to Passenger Cars, Light Trucks & Vans) 
3.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete 
6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
9.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade 
Heavy Duty Pavement (Subjected to Buses) 
4.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete 
  6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
10.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Compacted Subgrade 
The pavement subgrades should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction.  CBR and field density tests should be performed on the 
actual subgrade soils encountered during construction to confirm the adequacy of the 
above sections. 
The project site is located in an area with relatively high rainfall.  Accumulation of 
water in pavement subgrades can result in softening of the subgrade soils and pumping 
failure of the surface paving material.  Proper drainage of paved areas is critical to the 
long-term performance and service life of pavements.  Paved areas should be sloped, 
and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off the site.  
Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction.

3.3 
Walkways 
We anticipate that concrete walkways will be used for the proposed drop-off and 
parking facility.  The near-surface soils encountered at the site consist of clayey silts, 
which exhibit a low expansion potential if maintained at suitable moisture contents, 
(i.e., not allowed to dry below optimum moisture content).  Therefore, it is critical to 
provide special attention to subgrade preparation to reduce the potential for structural 
distress to lightly loaded slabs resulting from minor expansion or saturation and 
softening of the subgrade by accumulation of water. The subgrades for slabs-on-grades 
should be scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 
2 percent above the optimum moisture, and compacted to not less than 90 percent 
relative compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in 
accordance with ASTM D 1557.  Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by 
dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
We recommend a minimum 4-inch thick slab with 4 inches of aggregate subbase 
cushion below the walkways.  Control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the 
width of the walkways and at right angle intersections.  It should be emphasized that the 
areas adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled tightly against the edges of the slabs 
with low expansion, relatively impervious soils.  These areas should also be graded to 
divert water away from the slabs and to reduce the potential for water ponding around 
the slabs and foundations.

3.5 
Subsurface Infiltration 
It is understood that the project will include a system, or systems, for the on-site 
management of storm water runoff.  These systems may include, but are not limited to: 
detention basins, drywells or bioswales.  In order to obtain subsurface infiltration 
information in support of the design of such systems, we conducted falling head 
infiltration tests at two locations as provided by ParEn, Inc. dba Park Engineering. 
Two field infiltration tests were conducted at the site to evaluate the infiltration 
characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered at the boring locations. The 
infiltration tests were performed in Boring Nos. P-1 and P-2; and the approximate 
locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  These tests were 
performed in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D of the State of 
Maryland Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and 
II” (rev. 2009).  These procedures are consistent with other state’s procedures and may 
generally be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by augering a boring to the selected test 
depth of about 5 feet BGS.  Upon reaching the test depth, a 4-inch diameter PVC solid 
casing was set to the bottom of the drilled hole to allow infiltration only through the soil 
exposed on the bottom of the boring. Falling head infiltration tests were performed to 
determine the infiltration rates of the underlying subsurface materials.  Each test consisted 
of four trials of filling the casing with 24 inches of water and taking periodic readings over a 
one hour trial period.  The infiltration rates are then calculated based on the result of the 
fourth and last trial for each test location.  The results are summarized in the following 
table. Detailed information of the tests is presented in Appendix C.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based, in part, 
upon information obtained from field borings and bulk samples.  Variations of 
subsurface soil conditions between and beyond the borings and bulk samples may 
occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until 
construction is underway.  If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to 
re-evaluate the recommendations provided in this report. 
The field boring locations indicated in this report are approximate, having been 
estimated from the features shown on preliminary sketches provided by ParEn, Inc. dba 
Park Engineering on January 16, 2013.  The locations were recorded in the field using a 
hand help GPS receiver and plotted on the Topographic Survey Map transmitted by 
ParEn, Inc. dba Park Engineering on September 18, 2013. Elevations of the field 
borings were estimated by interpolation from the elevation points shown on the same 
plan.  The physical locations and elevations of the field borings should be considered 
accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling four borings, designated 
as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, extending to depths of about 6.5 feet below the existing 
ground surface.  In addition, four bulk samples of the near-surface soils, designated as 
Bulk-1 through Bulk-4, were collected at the boring locations to evaluate the pavement 
support characteristics of the near-surface soils.  The approximate locations of the 
drilled borings are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our engineer, who observed the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis.  These classifications were further reviewed by visual 
observation and testing in the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general conformance 
with the Unified Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A.  Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, 
Plates A-1 through A-4. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in 
general accordance with ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving 
a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches.  In 
addition, some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop.  The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) 
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and 
evaluation of soil properties.  The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits.  The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth.  Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D 3080) were performed on remolded samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics.  Direct shear test results are presented on 
Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Two Modified Proctor tests (ASTM D 1557A) were performed on selected bulk 
samples to evaluate the moisture-density relationships of the near-surface soils.  The 
test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D 1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils.  The test results are presented on Plates B-6 and B-7. 
One one-inch Ring Swell test was performed on a relatively undisturbed  sample 
to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils under a surcharge pressure 
roughly equivalent to a floor slab.  The test results are summarized on Plate B-8.

Infiltration Tests 
In order to provide information on the surface infiltration rates of the on-site soils, 
we conducted two falling head infiltration tests at selected locations at the site, as 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  These tests were performed in general accordance 
with the procedures in Appendix D of the State of Maryland Department of the 
Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009). These 
procedures are consistent with other state’s procedures and may generally be 
considered an industry standard.
