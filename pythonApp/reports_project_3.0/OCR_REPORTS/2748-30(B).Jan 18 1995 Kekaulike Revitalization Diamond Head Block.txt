INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our vibration monitoring services performed
during the test pile driving operations at the Kekaulike Revitalization Diamond Head
Block Project in the Chinatown area of downtown Honolulu on the island of Oahu
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1 This report summarizes the findings resulting from our field
monitoring work and presents our discussion and recommendations derived from our
engineering analyses for the subject project
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located in the Chinatown area of downtown Honolulu on the
island of Oahu Hawaii It is proposed to construct a 4 story commercial and residential
building complex on the site as a public housing development The proposed
development will also include one level on the Maunakea Street side and two levels
on the Kekaulike Street side of below ground parking which will extend to an
elevation of approximately 10 feet Mean Sea Level MSL or deeper
The project site is currently bounded by low rise buildings along King and
Kekaulike Streets and across Maunakea and Hotel Streets The adjacent buildings are
constructed of brick masonry rock masonry as well as concrete masonry units
Existing structural distresses such as crack openings were observed at various
locations of the building faces Due to the relatively poor condition and proximity of the

generated during the pile driving operation and evaluate their effects on the neighboring

TEST PILE PROGRAM
The field vibration monitoring work was conducted between December 15
through 23 1994 during the test pile driving operations performed by Hawaiian
Dredging Construction Company A diesel hammer with a rated
maximum driving energy of approximately 69 898 foot pounds was used to drive the
16 inch octagonal precast and prestressed concrete piles
A total of 24 test piles were driven to depths of about 75 to 134 feet below the
existing ground surface at the site Detailed observations of the test pile driving
operation were presented under a separate report dated prepared for
Mitsunaga Associates Inc The test pile locations were predrilled to a depth of
approximately 20 feet below the existing ground surface prior to the pile driving
operation The locations of the test piles for which monitoring was performed during
the pile driving operation are shown on the Foundation Plans Plates 2 and 3
METHODOLOGY
An was used to monitor the vibration generated during the
test pile driving operations A sensor geophone was placed at varying distances from
the test piles during the course of the driving operations in order to obtain vibration data
representative of different distances from the vibration source
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2748 30 B Page 4
The consists of a 3 component geophone and a signal
conditioner recorder unit The geophone senses ground motion along three mutually
perpendicular orthogonal axes thus providing detection of ground motion in the
vertical transverse and longitudinal directions The signal conditioner recorder unit
receives and processes these signals to provide a graphic record of vibrations The
Blastmate II permits scanning of vibration levels over extended periods when operated
in the Monitor Mode Resultant peak particle velocities RPPV in units of inches per
second ips are automatically computed by vector summation of the motions along the
orthogonal axes
The Blastmate II was set at a scanning interval of every minute during the
vibration monitoring process During the 1 minute scanning interval the Blastmate II
samples the vibration data at a rate of 1 024 data points per second and stores the
resultant peak level of vibration which occurs during that 1 minute interval At the end
of every scanning interval a stripchart record is produced on the recording paper by
the built in plotter The height of each bar on the stripchart record represents the
highest vibration level observed during each scanning interval The magnitude of the
highest vibration level observed and its corresponding time are shown on the stripchart
graph and data printout generated by the built in plotter at the completion of the driving
operation of each test pile A typical stripchart graph and the data printout are shown
on Plate 4 A 1 page summary of each test pile driving event can be produced by
connecting the Blastmate II to a computer A typical computer printout of the stripchart
summary event is shown on Plate 5
During the pile driving operations the times corresponding with every foot of
driven pile length were recorded in our field monitoring log This information was used
to evaluate the depth that produced the maximum vibrations during the driving of each
test pile when used in conjunction with the stripchart graph and data printout The
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2748 30 B Page 5
horizontal surface distance between the geophone and the test pile was also measured
and entered into the field log
FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS
A numerically ordered summary of the resultant peak vibration levels observed
during the test pile driving operations is presented on Plate 6 Two different distances
were used for the data processing one for horizontal surface distance between the pile
and the geophone and the other for the slant distance between the geophone and the
pile tip at the approximate depth at which the maximum vibration was observed
Since vibration amplitudes for a given energy source attenuate with increasing
distance in exponential form graphic representation of vibration amplitude versus
distance data will appear linear when plotted in log log graph form An attenuation plot
for the resultant peak vibration data obtained for the test pile driving operation is
presented on Plate 7 The round circles represent data based on surface distances
and the square symbols represent data based on slant distance between the geophone
and the pile tip at the time of the maximum observed vibration
A regression analyses power curve was performed on the vibration data to
evaluate representative rates of vibration attenuation with increasing distance from the
source One analysis was performed for data using surface distances and another
using slant distances These lines are also shown on the test pile attenuation plot on
Plate 7 Our evaluation of the distance versus particle velocity attenuation data sets
indicated that most of the peak vibrations were generated with the pile tip near the
maximum embedment depths However in some cases the measured peak vibrations
were encountered at shallower depths

The threshold for persons to perceive vibrations occurs at a relatively low level
of motion Depending on character duration and repetitiveness of vibrations
perception may occur at levels between 0 01 and These levels are well
below those required to cause damage to typical structures
Vibration damage thresholds for pile driving induced motions have not been
specifically established A number of researchers have presented a variety of proposed
criteria The criteria that we normally use are related to the type of construction age
and general condition of structures and are adapted from a variety of studies of actual
damage thresholds observed for blasting operations and other vibration inducing
sources
Based on Chae 1978 the following recommended threshold of damage
criteria should provide reasonable indicators for reducing the potential for direct
vibration damage
Class 4 inches per second
for structures of substantial construction
Class Il 2 inches per second
for relatively new residential structures in sound condition
Class Ill 1 inch per second
for relatively old residential structures in poor condition
Class 5 inch per second
for old residential structures in very poor condition
Reference Chae Y S 1978 Design of Excavation Blasts to Prevent Damage
Civil Engineering Vol 48 No 79

The structures adjacent to the Kekaulike Revitalization project site may be
classified as Class Ill to Class IV structures Because of the repetitive nature of pile
driving vibrations it has been recommended that the above categories be lowered by
one class Thus a vibration damage threshold criteria of 0 5 inch per second was used
for the subject project
RESULTS AND CONCLUSIONS
The attenuation plot for vibrations based on surface distance measurements is
presented on the Anticipated Pile Driving Vibration Levels Plate 8 Two lines that are
drawn parallel to the regression line were also constructed on the plot One represents
the upper bound of observed data and the other is the lower bound The upper bound
line approximates the maximum vibration level we might expect to reach during the
course of production pile driving operation for a specific distance from the vibration
source The Class III Reduced threshold value of was also drawn on this plot
The intersection of the upper bound line with the Class III Reduced criteria line occurs
at a distance of about 35 feet This suggests that damages may occur to structures
that are located within 35 feet of the vibration source While this distance refers to
maximum anticipated vibration levels we would expect average levels to be
considerably lower
Although the recorded data indicated that only five driven test piles resulted in
vibrations in excess of the threshold limit the distance of concern suggests that there
may be some possibility of reaching vibration values near or slightly above the
recommended threshold limits due to the proximity of the existing buildings to some of
the pile locations Because of the pre existing damage to some of the buildings it
should be expected that some further damage may be possible
