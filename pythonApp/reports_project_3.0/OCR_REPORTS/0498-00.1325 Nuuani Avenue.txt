SUMMARY OF RECOMMENDATIONS
The proposed buildings can be supported on shallow footings
with bearing values of 3000 psf for the sanctuary and two
story office buildings and 4500 psf for the three story
classroom and retaining wall An equivalent fluid pressure
of 35 psf is recommended for the design active lateral
pressure on the retaining wall Slabs on grade may be used
INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our foundation investigation
for the proposed church complex located on Nuuanu Avenue in
Honolulu Hawaii
The scope of this investigation was to investigate the site s
subsurface conditions determine foundation requirements and
foundation behavior for the proposed structures
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The present development plans call for four major structures

SITE CONDITIONS
Surface
The site approximately 300 by 900 feet in plan dimension is
located immediately east of Nuuanu Avenue The back portion of the
property is bounded by the Nuuanu stream the makai side by the
Royal Mausoleum and by a church on the mauka side Presently
a two story frame house is located in the center of the property
Page 3
W
The site is presently residential property with expansive lawns
and stately trees The elevation varies from approximately 304
feet at the front of the property and slopes down to 280 feet in
the rear where the site slopes sharply into Nuuanu stream There
is a 4 foot rock wall approximately 75 feet long parallel to the
stream A tributory stream runs along the mauka portion of the
property
Geology
The eastern portion of Oahu was formed by the Koolau volcano
Remnants of this volcano form the Kaolau range which stretches
from Kahuku to Makapuu Within this range lies Nuuanw Valley
carved by stream erosion since the Pleistocene Nuuanu s steep
walls and a flat broad floor are typical for a mature erosional
valley Later lava flows from the Honolulu series volcanics have
covered the older Nuuanu valley floor soils and diverted Nuuanu
stream to its present location

Our borings revealed that the site is immediately underlain by a
hard silty clay that varies from 2 to 20 feet in depth The
deposition of this silty clay is not uniform 6
7 9 and 11 indicate a buried stream bed or depression whose down
stream course is in the makai direction Underlying the silty
clay and 9 revealed significant amounts of chlorite
Serpentinized Basalt chlorite is a weak rock formed by volcanic
gasses Chlorite erodes easily and it appears that the stream
bed or depression was created by the fast erosion of this particular
mineral
Unweathered thin bedded hard fractured basalt underlies all
soils and varies in depth from 2 to 25 feet The basalt stratum
is closest to the surface in borings 1 and 2 Boring 4 encountered

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
It is our opinion that the site is suitable for the proposed
construction with conventional shallow foundations supported on
the site s stiff to hard silty clay or basalt rock Due to the
non conformity of subsurface conditions adjacent footings may
be supported on different materials and therefore the bearing
pressures for the silty clay will determine the recommended
bearing value

story office building may be supported on conventional spread
or continuous footings bearing in the undisturbed stiff to hard
silty clay A soil bearing value of 3000 pounds per square foot
for dead plus real live loads should be used for footings bottomed
a minimum of 18 inches below final adjacent grade Continuous
footings should have a minimum width of 12 inches Maximum
total settlements on the order of one inch may be anticipated
for footings bearing in the silty clay Differential settlements

inch Present plans show the Diamond Head side of the office
building will be on about 4 feet of fill Foundations should be
carried through the fill and bear in the undisturbed natural soils
Classroom Building The proposed three story classroom may also be
supported on conventional shallow foundations Foundations for
this structure will be bearing on basalt rock weathered rock ard
silty clay mixed with decomposed rock A bearing value of 4500
pounds per square foot should be used for dead plus real live loads
Footings should be bottomed a minimum of 18 inches below final
adjacent grade When the footings are bottomed in competent rock
a minimum embedment depth of one foot will be required Maximum
total settlements will be on the order of one inch with differential
settlements between column and wall footings less than one half inch

A one third increase in the above recommended values may be used
when considering wind or seismic loadings
Due to the nonconformity of subsurface conditions it is recommended
that a qualified soils engineer inspect the footing excavations to
confirm the conditions on which our recommendations are based
Any voids formed by the removal of rock in the footing excavation
should be filled with tean concrete or compacted granular material
Retaining Wall The retaining wall footings should be designed
for a bearing value of 4500 pounds per square foot bottomed a
minimum of 18 inches below final adjacent grade Maximum toe
settlements will be less than one half of an inch The active
lateral pressure developed against the structure by the weight of
the soil behind the wall will depend upon the unit weight of the
soil the inherent strength of the soil and rock and the angle of
internal friction between the wall material and the soil Geologic
instabilities due to beds dipping into the wall may also increase
the lateral pressure against the wall
For design simplification the lateral active pressure developed
behind the wall can be assumed to be equal to an equivalent fluid
pressure of 35 pounds per cubic foot
Page 7
W
The excavation behind the wall should be inspected by a geologist
from this firm to check for geologic instabilities that may cause
an increase in the active lateral pressure against the wall
The backfill immediately against the wall should be an open graded
compacted granular material Weepholes should be provided to prevent
additional fluid pressure building up behind the wall for the portion
of the wall beyond the classroom limits Within the classroom
building an adequate drainage system should be provided behind the
wall to prevent buildup of excessive pore pressures
Resistance to lateral and sliding forces can be resisted by either
passive pressure friction along the base of footings or by a
combination of both A coefficient of friction of 0 40 can be
assumed between concrete footings and the site materials Passive
pressures can be considered equal to the pressure developed by a
fluid with a density of 350 pounds per cubic foot
If the passive pressure and friction developed along the retaining
wall footing base is not sufficient to resist the horizontal
component of the active pressure it is then recommended a key be
constructed at the footing bottom

Slabs on Grade
The silty clay soil is slightly expansive It is therefore
recommended that the slabs on grade be placed on a minimum of
four inches of a granular type material In areas where dampness
in the floor slab would be undesirable or where moisture susceptible
floor coverings are to be used a plastic membrane should be placed
over the granular material
Site Preparation
After all debris and structures have been removed the ground
surface should be stripped to remove all vegetation All fill
areas exposed should be scarified to a minimum depth of six
inches brought to near optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum
of 90 of maximum density as determined by ASTM D 1557 70T Fill
may then be placed in maximum six inch lifts and also compacted
to at least 90 of maximum density
In excavating for the retaining wall and classroom building it is
anticipated that hard unweathered basalt will be encountered This
hard rock may require blasting The depths and locations of this
hard rock can be estimated from the boring logs and cross sections
on Plate 2 Where the core recovery was more than 90 then blasting
Page 9
will be required These recoveries were encountered in Borings
1 2 and 4 It may be possible to excavate the rock by methods
other than blasting where the core recovery is less than 90
Care should be taken during blasting operations so that excessive
fracturing does not occur in the foundation bearing rock

APPENDIX
_ FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface soil conditions at the site were explored by
drilling eleven test borings utilizing truck mounted rotary
auger equipment with rock coring attachments The depths of
the borings ranged from 10 to 25 feet below the existing
ground surface The locations of the borings are shown on
the Site Plan Plate 1
The soils and rock were classified by visual and textural
examinations in the field by our geologist who continuously
supervised the drilling operations The classifications
were verified by inspection and testing in the laboratory
All soils were classified in accordance with the Unified
Soil Classification System The description of rocks is
based on local geologic terminology Graphical representations
of the soils and rock encountered are presented on the Boring
Logs Plates A 1A through A 1K Soil samples were obtained
by driving a 2 4 inch diameter sampler with a 140 pound hammer
free falling a distance of 30 inches The blow counts to drive
the sampler and the percent of recovery of rock cores are also
shown on the Log of Borings at the sample depth
