SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated along the western side of Fort Weaver Road in Ewa
Oahu Hawaii and is divided by Geiger Road with Areas 6 through 11 on the north side
of the road and Area 12 on the south side of the road The site had previously been
used for the cultivation of sugar cane and has recently been cleared of the vegetation
with grading commencing shortly afterward At the time of this writing the areas of the
subject project north of Geiger Road are at or near finish grade The areas south of
Geiger Road are presently being graded

Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae ended lava flows from Koolau
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the
Schofield Plateau or Central Oahu
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating sediments
which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and further south

and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from

forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock over the
artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of these sediments has continued
from earlier geologic time through the present
Subsurface Conditions
Based on the preliminary field exploration the soil types at the project site may
be generalized into three distinct groups as follows
Fill
A surface layer of fill was encountered at various locations throughout the site and
primarily consists of medium stiff to very stiff brown to reddish brown clayey silt
This fill was probably derived from nearby alluvial deposits and was placed during
the early development of the site for the purpose of sugar cane Cultivation In
general this layer of cane fill was found to be approximately one 1 to two 2
feet in depth across the site
Alluvium
Alluvial deposits were encountered below the surface cane fills to depths of
approximately four 4 to eight 8 feet below the existing surface throughout the
site with the exception of Boring Nos 7 and 8 along the southern most portion
where coral formations were encountered immediately beneath the surface fills
The alluvium sampled primarily consists of stiff to hard dark brown to brown silty
clay to clay and clayey silt with some sand and gravel interbeds
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2443 00 A Page 5
In general most of the stiff to hard silts and clays may be classified as having low
to moderately high swelling expansion potential when wetted However clays
with high shrink swell potential have been encountered at nearby locations and
are anticipated to exist at the site
Coral Aigal Formation
Coral formations and loose coral sand and gravel was encountered in borings
below the overlying alluvial deposits and ranged from dense coral formations to
deposits of loose reef detritus
Groundwater was encountered in Geolabs Boring No 5 at a depth of 32 0 feet
below the existing ground surface at about elevation 2 0 feet above MSL In addition
seepage zones were encountered in Boring No 5 at 23 0 feet above MSL and in
Geolabs Boring No 1 at 26 0 feet above MSL It appears that the observed seepage
zones may reflect localized seepage conditions possibly originating from nearby
irrigation ditches reservoirs and other sources Rises in groundwater levels are
anticipated during and after periods of heavy rainfall
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
In general it is our opinion that the subject residential development project is
feasible from a geotechnical engineering point of view provided that our recommenda
tions are followed
The preliminary subsurface exploration generally disclosed that the explored areas
are underlain primarily by medium stiff to hard clayey silts silty clays and loose to
dense hard coralline deposits
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2443 00 A Page 6
As previously discussed the stiff to hard clay soils encountered at the site will
exhibit low to moderately high swelling expansion potential when wetted It is not
readily possible to distinguish between low and high expansion soils visually in the field
Such critical differentiation may be verified by further laboratory testing However it is
our opinion that special considerations be implemented during the mass grading of the
project site to reduce the potential for heaving stress with its resulting damage to the
slab on grade foundation and constructed residential structures
Site Grading
Based on the proposed site grading plan fill ranging from 0 to 5 feet is planned
for most of the site It is our understanding that fill material will be obtained from an
off site source at the proposed golf course located to the west of the project site
Based on the report by Soils International Inc entitled Soils Investigation
Proposed Borrow Site No 2 North of Geiger Road dated
August 7 1987 it appears that the proposed borrow material would consist primarily
of coralline deposits This coral material when it is broken down to 6 inch minus sizes
generally can be considered suitable for use as fill
The finally selected borrow materials should be tested and approved by the
geotechnical engineer prior to transportation to the site
The upper surface soils are clayey silt and silty clay soils which generally exhibit
moderate to high swelling potential wnen excavated and recompacted as fill Therefore
should this material be required for use as fill it should be used only in the deeper
portions of the level area fill a minimum of three 3 feet below the finished grade

Foundations
The field exploration disclosed that the site is underlain by a surface layer of
medium stiff to hard alluvial clayey silts and silty clays overlying loose to dense hard
coralline deposits The thickness of the upper surface clayey layer was found to vary
from approximately four 4 to eight 8 feet with the exception of the southern portion
of area 12 where coral was encountered within two 2 feet of the existing surface The
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2443 00 A Page 9
laboratory tests performed indicate that the alluvial soils are primarily clays with low to
moderate high swelling potential with some areas exhibiting critically high swelling
potential
The underlying coralline material generally has low expansion potential and is
considered an excellent source of fill and foundation support Normal slab on grade
foundation systems may be used when the mass grading is such that the upper clay
soils are removed in cut areas to expose native coral formations or the upper two 2
feet of fill consists of coral in the fill areas
Where the house foundations will bear on the surface clay soils special subgrade
preparation is recommended to reduce the effect of the swelling soils The subgrade
preparation would consist of removal and replacement of all surface clay soils or a
minimum of two feet below the house and driveway on slab in grade areas For more
details refer to the following section under Slab In Grade In Expansion Soil
In order to minimize the costs for the over excavation and replacement every
effort should be made to utilize the coralline material for fill within two 2 feet of finished
grade
For structures constructed in accordance with the above recommendations an
allowable bearing capacity of 2 500 p s f may be utilized

may be feasible for the proposed residential development

Slab On Grade on Low Expansion Soil
For house construction over coralline subgrade conditions i e areas where the
upper clay soils have been removed and fill areas where the upper two 2 feet
consists of coralline material conventional foundation design may be used
The conventional foundation design consists of a 4 inch thick slab on grade
placed over a four 4 inch layer of base course or 3B Fine gravel The
thickened edge around the slab on grade should extend a minimum of twelve 12
inches below the adjacent ground surface refer to Plate 3
To protect against moisture damage of the flooring material a polyethylene
moisture barrier should be considered
The slab subgrade soils should be kept moist prior to concrete placement
Slab On Grade On Expansive Clay
The expansion of the on site subgrade soils may be reduced by capping it with
a minimum of twenty four 24 inches of non expansive select material The
capping should extend at least three 3 feet beyond the perimeter of the
proposed structure
The select material for the non expansive capping layer should be low expansion
granular material such as crushed coral The material should be less than three
3 inch in size with a laboratory CBR swell value not exceeding 1 and CBR
value of 20 or more It should not contain large percentages of boulders
organics or other deleterious material
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2443 00 A Page 11
The select material should be examined and tested by the geotechnical engineer
prior to its use at the project site
Prior to the placement of the capping fill the bottom of the excavation should be
scarified to a depth of twelve 12 inches moisture conditioned to above optimum
moisture content and recompacted to 85 of its maximum dry density determined
by ASTM D 1557 78
The capping fill should be placed in horizontal lifts moisture conditioned to slightly
above optimum moisture content and compacted within a range of 85 to 90
of its maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D 1557 78
The capping fill should be placed as soon as possible after the scarification and
recompaction of the existing soil This will create a cap of moist soil above the
potentially expansive soils and thereby reduce the drying of the underlying clayey
subgrade soils The reduction in drying will reduce the potential swelling of the
expansive clay soils
Conventional slab on grade construction may then be utilized for the proposed
structures on the above recommended 24 inch thick capping select fill
The slab subgrade areas should be wetted and kept moist prior to concrete
placement Moisture barriers and capillary breaks should be incorporated below
the slab to prevent damage to the floor coverings by moisture infiltration
The thickened edges of the slabs should extend a minimum of twelve 12 inches
below the lowest adjacent outside grade

Utilities
Utility Excavation
In general a surface layer of clayey silt underlain by dense to medium dense
coralline sands may be encountered in the excavations for utility placement
Relatively easy digging is anticipated although local harder coral reefrock should
be anticipated and may require special excavating techniques such as hoe ram
excavation
Shoring of the trench walls are recommended to minimize caving in of the trench
walls in the vicinity of loose coralline deposits where they may be encountered
The excavation should be provided with proper bracing in accordance with the
applicable safety requirement The design and safety of the trench supports
should be the responsibility of the contractors
Heavy equipment and stockpiles should be kept away from the tops of the open
trenches
The ground adjacent to and near the top of the excavation should be continu
ously monitored for cracks or dips and other indications of movement and
possible sloughing of the trench walls Such monitoring is particularly critical in
the areas where existing structures are located adjacent to the trench excavation

Pipe Cushion
We recommend that a six 6 inch cushion consisting of clean granular material
such as 3B Fine gravel or sand should be provided below the utility pipes
Backfill
In general the trench backfill snould be in accordance with Section 11 of the
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction September 1986 City and
County of Honolulu
Granular material such as 3B Fine gravel or sand should be used for the
backfill around the bottom half of the utility pipes to at least the height of the
springline to reduce the compactive effort required and possible damage to the
pipes and to provide bearing support
Except for the boulders and expansive clay most of the excavated on site soil
can be re used in the trench backfill up to within two 2 feet of the finished grade
provided adequate compaction is achieved on each lift
Backfill should be compacted to a minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density
established by ASTM D 1557 78 Where the drain line crosses the existing
roadway the backfill within 2 feet of finished grade should consist of select
borrow and be compacted to 95 of its maximum dry density
The trenches should be kept free of water during backfilling
Pounding or jetting of the backfill materials may not be utilized as a method of
compaction

Pavement Design
Based on the information available previous experience and laboratory test
results provided by others it is estimated that the recommended preliminary pavement
section may be as follows
2 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 Inch Base Course
10 inch Select Borrow Sub base
18 Inch Total Pavement Thickness
Note Select borrow sub base course may be omitted where native coralline
sand and gravel are present or a minimum of two 2 feet of select
import soil is used below the road subgrade
We recommend that CBR tests be performed at regular intervals upon the
subgrade soils to determine the final recommended pavement section and to
confirm verify if the preliminary pavement section is adequate for soils in different areas
Select borrow should also be incorporated below sidewalks and driveways to
reduce potential heaving We recommend that a minimum 6 inch thickness of select
borrow cushion fill be placed under the sidewalks and driveway base course layer
unless coralline material is used within the top 2 feet of fill
The base course should be crushed rock or other granular material capable of
developing a field CBR value of 85 or more conforming with Section 31 Standard
Specifications for Public Works Construction The select borrow should be crusher run
mudrock coral sand or cinder with less than 15 passing the 200 sieve and
compacted to 95 of its maximum density The top six 6 inches of the subgrade soil
should be scarified and recompacted wet of optimum moisture to 95 compaction

The subgrade soil should not be allowed to become dry and to develop shrinkage
cracks prior to the completion of the pavement placement
The subgrade and pavement sections should be shaped to drain subsurface
water away from the pavement into suitable drain outlets We recommend that
weepholes be installed in the catch basins at the base course level in order to allow
drainage of accumulated subsurface water
