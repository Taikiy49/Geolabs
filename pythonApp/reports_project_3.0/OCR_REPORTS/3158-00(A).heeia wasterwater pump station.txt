PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located in the area of Kaneohe on the windward side of the
island of Oahu Hawaii It is proposed to construct a new wastewater force main at the
site The proposed force main will be approximately 1 850 feet long Site elevations
range from about 7 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the pump station to about 91 feet
MSL at the intersection of Lilipuna Road and Kamehameha Highway
It is anticipated that groundwater will be encountered during excavation for the
dewatering of the excavation near the vicinity of the pump station will likely be needed
to facilitate the force main construction

following work efforts

toning for underground utilities

to and from the project site
3 Drilling and installation of one groundwater monitoring well to a depth of
about 21 feet below the existing ground surface
4 Logging of the boring by a field engineer from our firm
5 Well development of the monitoring well

Monitoring Well No was installed near the entrance to the pump

In general MW 1 encountered a surface layer of man made fill of about 4 feet
in thickness The fill layer generally consists of medium dense coral gravel and stiff
clayey silt Underlying this fill layer our boring encountered soft to very stiff clayey silt
overlying very dense sandy gravel to the maximum depth drilled of 21 feet below
ground level The boring log of MW 1 is presented on Plate 3
Groundwater Conditions
Shallow groundwater condition was encountered in the drilled monitoring well at
a depth of about 7 feet below the existing ground corresponding to about 3 feet MSL
Due to the proximity of the shoreline and the Pacific Ocean it is likely that the
groundwater level measured in the boring during our field exploration may be affected
by tidal fluctuation of the sea level In addition the groundwater level at the project site
may be influenced by seasonal precipitation and storm surge conditions

After drilling to a depth of about 21 feet a monitoring well casing was lowered
into the borehole The casing consisted of 2 inch O D Schedule 40 flush threaded
pipe end cap made up the lower portion of the well Eleven feet of blank 2 inch O D
Schedule 40 flush threaded PVC pipe composed the upper portion of the well The
blank PVC casing extended upward to the ground surface The well was enclosed in
traffic rated hole covers The well was backfilled with a No 3 filter sand pack which
consisted of clean uniformly graded kiln dried Monterey Sands which should be
chemically stable within the monitoring well environment to reduce potential sorption
from the groundwater Typical grading parameters of the Monterey Sands are
presented on Plate 4 The upper 2 to 4 feet of PVC casing was sealed with bentonite
and the top 2 feet was sealed with grout The well housing was then cemented in
place The bentonite and grout were not in contact with the underlying groundwater
which is approximately 7 feet below the existing ground surface

In general test results from the additional sampling indicated a lower
concentration of total suspended solids turbidity and oil and grease than the initial test
in 1993 Our records indicated that standard environmental protocol was taken during
the drilling well installation and water sampling process in 1993 Therefore the slight
variance in the test results of oil and grease may be considered statistically
insignificant We also believe that the higher concentration of total suspended solids
and turbidity from the initial testing may be attributed to the waiting period following
development of the well Typically it is standard practice to perform sampling at about
3 to 5 days after well development In this case the initial sample was taken 5 days
after well development and the additional sample was taken about 8 months after the
well development The lower total suspended solids and turbidity values may be
attributed to the large difference in waiting times following well development
In general the test results also indicate the reporting limit which refers to the
detection limit of the laboratory equipment The reporting limit is dependent on the
testing equipment and the statistical control applied by the laboratory to the test results
Therefore laboratories adopting the same standard test method may have different
reporting limits
