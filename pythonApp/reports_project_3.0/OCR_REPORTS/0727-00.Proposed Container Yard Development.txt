INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our soil investigation
at the proposed container yard development site at the mauka
harbor portton of Sand Island in Honolulu Hawaii
The purpose of our study is to define existing subsurface
soil conditions at the site and to make recommendations for
the proposed contaitner yard pavement design and construction

PROPOSED CONTAINER YARD DEVELOPMENT P 2
SITE DESCRIPTION
The proposed new container yard site ts bounded by Kapalama
channel at the north Sand Island access road at the south
U S Coast Guard Station at the east and Matson Container
Yard Berth 51 B at the west The container yard consits
of a fairly level area of about 33 acres
The eastern half of the stte is paved and being used as a
new car storage yard
The western half of the site ts a warehouse and industrial
area Underground structures such as oi tanks and cesspools
were noted within this area
The Ewa boundary abuts the sluice area located east of the
recently completed Matson Container Yard expansion
PROPOSED CONSTRUCTION
Based on the preliminary plan prepared by William Hee
Associates Inc the project civil engineer pavement
will be at elevation 8 4 to 9 8 feet Minor site grading
will be required to obtain a level surface and to provide
drainage The improvements will include an asphaltic concrete
pavement and storm drains

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
In general sandy coral fill material was encountered at the
project site However about one half of the site area is
underlain by a 1 to 4 foot thick clay layer sandwiched in
between coral fill layers Due to these two different types
of subgrade soil encountered two site preparation procedures
are recommended

2 proof rolling for the area without the

Based on the planned container yard finish grade the major
portion of the clayey layer will be located about 3 feet below
the planned finish grade Our test results and analyses indi
cated that a 3 foot pavement section above the clayey subgrade
layer can provide adequate support for the proposed traffic
and loading Therefore the removal and replacement of the
clayey material would only be required within the pavement
section
The tn place dry density of the on site coral fill material
has a density of about 86 to 99 pounds per cubic foot which
is below 85 of its maximum dry density Proof rolling of the
existing coral sand fill will be required to improve the subgrade
condition
Based on our previous experience at the Berth 51 B container
yard construction with proper compaction equipment and water
flooding of the underlying coral material surface compaction
can densify a 2 feet prism of the in place coral fill and
increase its existing density to obtain 95 compaction

Pavement Design
Representative samples of the upper fill were obtained during
our subsurface tnvestigation and laboratory testing to evaluate
its subgrade support capacity Nine California Bearing Ratio
tests were performed tn accordance with the American Society
for Testing and Materials Test Method D 1883 utilizing a ten
pound surcharge
CBR values of 9 for the sandy coral material and 2 for the
clayey material were obtained from the on site soils tested
at their present tn place density It was found that additional
compaction to 95 compaction could produce higher CBR values of
35 for the sandy coral material and 8 for the clayey material
Based on the above CBR values produced by additional compaction
of the subgrade soils and by using single axle loads of 30 to
50 kips the recommended pavement sections are summarized as
follows

Construction Inspections cont d
The area of the proposed container yard expansion is man made
land which is generally not uniform Condittons quite different
from those encountered in the subsurface explorations may there
fore be disclosed during the construction operations
The construction inspections should also verify that the sub
surface conditions are consistent with the findings of the
investigation If unforeseen or undetected soil conditions
such as concrete rubble pockets of soft cohesive soils or
unusual water conditions are encountered corrective measures
should be made in the field as they are detected
