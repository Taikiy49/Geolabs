SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered a 6.5 to 15 feet 
thick fill layer consisting of soft to hard sandy/silty clays. Beneath the fill material, 
colluvium and clinker layers were encountered to depths of about 10 to 21.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The colluvium material generally consisted of medium stiff 
to very stiff silty clay and dense gravelly cobbles. Clinker materials generally consisted 
of medium dense to dense silty gravel and silty sands. The colluvium and clinker 
materials were generally underlain by medium hard to very hard basalt formation 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 52.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface.   
Twelve Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) tests were performed along the 
perimeter of the existing Kaha‘opulani and Nae‘ole buildings. Results of the DCP tests 
indicated that the subsurface soils along the perimeter of the existing buildings generally 
consists of stiff to very stiff soils except at three DCP test locations where soft to 
medium stiff material was encountered at depths of about 1 to 2 feet below the ground 
surface. 
The existing slope inclination along the slope distress areas typically ranged from 
about 0.5H:1V to 1.5H:1V.  Based on the borings, the near-surface  soils consisted of 
silty/sandy clay.  Permanent slopes for these silty/sandy clay soils are typically designed 
at a flatter inclination.  Based on our site observations, the existing concrete header 
adjacent to the slope does not extend along the entire roadway.  In areas with no 
concrete header, slope erosion caused by surface runoff water was observed.  We 
believe that a combination of the steep slopes and surface runoff water over the slopes 
may have caused the slope distress and erosion to occur. 
To minimize grading and the requirement to obtain a grading permit, the use of a 
soil nailed slope remedial measure is recommended to mitigate further erosion and 
failure of the existing slope.  The soil nailed slope system consists of a series of 
individual reinforcing bars grouted in to drilled holes to stabilize the slope. A shotcrete 
facing is applied to the face of the reinforced slope. The soil nailed slope may be 
capped with sculptured shotcrete to simulate a natural rock appearance, if desired. 
The soil nails should be installed by drilling a 5.5-inch diameter hole with an 
inclination of approximately 15 degrees from horizontal. The soil nail bars should consist 
of ASTM A615 Grade 75 threaded bar with minimum bar diameter of 1.125 inches.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
conducted for the Kamehameha Schools KSK/Erosion Control – Site 11, Vicinity of 
Kaha‘opulani and Nae‘ole Buildings project in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of the 
project, including soil nail installation, erosion control, and site grading. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located in the vicinity of Kaha‘opulani and Nae‘ole Buildings on 
the Kamehameha Schools Kapalama Campus in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. Erosion at the top of the slope adjacent to the roadway makai of the 
two buildings was observed. The slope grades downwards to existing homes along 
Makanani Drive. An existing tank storage cage and pad is located near the edge of the 
eroded slope. Longitudinal cracks were observed on the surface of the existing 
asphaltic concrete pavement adjacent to the top of slope. 
The eroded slope is located near the Kaha‘opulani and Nae‘ole Buildings which 
exhibited diagonal cracks in the first floor Concrete Masonry Unit (CMU) walls. There is 
concern that the eroded slope conditions may be attributed to the building distresses 
observed.  
To observe potential lateral movement with depth, two inclinometers were 
installed adjacent to the eroded slope and between the slope and buildings. The 
inclinometers were monitored for about 3 months. In addition, to determine the general 
consistency of the subsurface materials near the two buildings, Dynamic Cone 
Penetration (DCP) tests were performed at selected locations adjacent to the buldings.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to our geotechnical engineering analysis are the subsurface materials 
encountered at the project site, the engineering properties of the materials encountered, 
and the variability of the subsurface conditions across the project site. Therefore, the 
following subsections provide a description of the geologic setting of the project site, the 
surface and subsurface conditions encountered at the site, and a discussion on the 
items needed for seismic design, such as the seismicity, soil liquefaction, and soil profile 
characteristics for seismic analysis. 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
Extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes built 
the Island of Oahu. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be of middle to late 
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age 
and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Koolau Volcano 
reached the end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago.  
The project area generally is composed of basaltic rock built by extrusion of the 
lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series. These rocks generally are characterized by flows 
of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers. In-situ chemical 
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred in the last 1 to 2 million years. The 
weathering process has formed a mantle of residual and saprolitic soils.  
In general, saprolite is mainly composed of silty material and is typical of the 
tropical weathering of volcanic rocks. The saprolite grades to a basaltic rock formation 
with increased depth. Fills placed during the initial development of the campus may 
underlie the ground surface at portions of the project site.

Based on the International Building Code, 2018 Edition (IBC 2018), the project

2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by shifts 
in the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to 
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or

during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the 
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not 
erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but 
most of the earthquakes are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive 
instruments. However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a 
few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii 
(over 90 percent of earthquakes) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of high 
seismic activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance 
from the Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous 
earthquakes greater than Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not 
confined only to the Island of Hawaii. 
To a lesser degree, the Island of Maui has experienced numerous earthquakes 
greater than Magnitude 5. Therefore, moderate to strong earthquakes have 
occurred in the County of Maui. The effects of earthquakes occurring on the Islands 
of Hawaii and Maui may be felt on the Island of Oahu. For example, several small 
landslides occurred on the Island of Oahu as a result of the Maui Earthquake of 
1938 (M6.8). In addition, some houses on the Island of Oahu were reportedly 
damaged as a result of the Lanai Earthquake of 1871 (M7+). 
In the last 150 years of recorded history, we are not aware of earthquakes greater 
than Magnitude 6 that have occurred on the Island of Oahu. An earthquake of 
Magnitude 4.8 to 5.0 occurred along the Diamond Head Fault in 1948 on the Island 
of Oahu. The moderate tremor resulted in broken store windows, ruptured building 
walls, and broken underground water mains.

2.4.2 Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the International Building Code (2018 Edition), the project site may be 
subjected to seismic activity, and the potential for soil liquefaction at the project site 
will need to be evaluated. 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the 
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build‐up of excess 
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by 
earthquakes. In this process, when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to 
vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in 
volume, causing an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is unable to occur 
rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective stress 
(internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore water 
pressure build-up could equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the 
soil shear strength to near zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During 
liquefaction, the soil acquires sufficient mobility to permit both horizontal and 
vertical movements, and if not confined, will result in significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained 
sands and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the 
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows:

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
To mitigate further erosion to the existing slope and distress to the existing 
roadway, we recommend stabilizing and protecting the existing slope fronting the 
Kaha‘opulani and Nae‘ole buildings by using a soil nailed slope system. Detailed 
discussions and recommendations for these items and other geotechnical aspects of 
the project are presented in the following sections. 
3.1 
Potential Cause of Slope Distress and Remedial Measures 
The existing slope inclination along the slope distress areas typically ranged from 
about one half horizontal to one vertical (0.5H:1V) to 1.5H:1V.  Based on the borings, 
the near-surface soils consisted of silty/sandy clay.  Permanent slopes for these 
silty/sandy clay soils are typically designed at a flatter inclination. 
Based on our site observations, pavement distress consisting of longitudinal 
cracking was observed along the roadway fronting the Kaha‘opulani and Nae‘ole 
buildings, which may be evidence of ground movement along the adjacent slope. In 
addition, several areas of erosion were observed immediately adjacent to the existing 
roadway near the top of slope. The existing concrete header adjacent to the slope does 
not extend along the entire roadway.  In areas with no concrete header, slope erosion 
caused by surface runoff water was observed.  We believe that a combination of the 
steep slopes and surface runoff water over the slopes may have caused the slope 
distress and erosion to occur. 
To minimize grading and the requirement to obtain a grading permit, the use of a 
soil nailed slope remedial measure is recommended along the entire length of the slope 
fronting the Kaha‘opulani and Nae‘ole buildings. 
3.2 
Soil Nailed Slope 
We recommend using a soil nail slope system to mitigate further slope 
movement. Design of the soil nailed slope system considers both the internal and 
external stability of the reinforced mass. The design of the internal stability includes 
establishing the size, spacing, orientation, and length of the grouted reinforcing bars. 
The external stability includes slope stability of the reinforced mass.

3.2.2 Soil Nailed Slope 
A soil nailed slope system consists of a series of individual reinforcing bars 
grouted into drilled holes to stabilize the eroded slope. A shotcrete facing is 
generally applied to the face of the reinforced slope. The reinforcing bars provide 
additional tensile and shear strengths to the soil and reinforce the sidewall of the 
slope. The reinforced soil mass behaves in a manner similar to a gravity retaining 
wall but lacks a footing.  The face of the soil nailed slope system will have a 
finished slope of about 0.5H:1V to 1.5H:1V inclination with slope heights up to 
about 15 feet. A soil nail numbering plan is presented on Plate 3. A typical soil 
nailed slope system is presented on Plate 4. 
The soil nailed slope may be capped with sculptured shotcrete to simulate a 
natural rock appearance, if desired.

The soil nails should be installed by drilling a minimum 5.5-inch diameter hole 
with an inclination of approximately 15 degrees from horizontal. The soil nail bar 
should consist of ASTM A615 Grade 75 solid threaded bar with a minimum bar 
diameter of 1.125-inch. We anticipate that the subsoil may be very corrosive. 
Therefore, we recommend using a triple corrosion protection system for the nails. 
The epoxy coated bars should be surrounded by neat cement grout or 
sand-cement mixture with a minimum 28-day compressive strength of 4,000 
pounds per square inch (psi). 
Based on our soil nail analysis, we recommend using a design nail length of 
15 feet, and a design nail length of 25 feet for eight soil nails designated as A1 
through A8, extending into the stiff clay encountered in our field exploration. The 
soil nails should be spaced at 6 feet on-center horizontally and vertically in a grid 
pattern. The first row of nails should be installed about 2 to 3 feet below the top 
of the slope. 
Based on the above design parameters, the soil nailed slope system should have a 
factor of safety of 1.5 or greater for the combined internal and external stability for 
the static loading condition considering a surcharge load of 250 pounds per square 
foot (psf) applied on the roadway travel lanes. 
3.2.3 Soil Nail Installation 
Construction of the permanent soil nails should be performed by a specialty

drilling conditions may be encountered during the installation of the soil nails due 
to the presence of cobbles and boulders within the colluvium and clinker 
materials anticipated at the project site. Utilizing a temporary casing may be 
required to maintain an open hole for the soil nail installation. In addition, due to 
potential variations of basalt rock within the subsurface, hard drilling conditions 
should be anticipated. The contractor should fully investigate the project site and 
make his/her own interpretation as to the character of the materials to be

3.2.4 Soil Nail Testing 
Based on the subsurface conditions anticipated behind the soil nailed retaining 
slope, we recommend performing a minimum of one pre-production verification 
test on a sacrificial soil nail at the project site. In addition, proof tests should be 
performed on at least 10 percent of the production soil nails during construction 
to confirm the bond stresses used in the design. 
The verification test should be loaded gradually to 150 percent of the design load 
for the creep test and subsequently to the maximum test load of about 
200 percent of the design load. In addition, the load at every loading increment 
should be held for at least 10 minutes, and creep tests should be held for at least 
60 minutes. The proof tests should consist of subjecting the soil nail to at least 
150 percent of the design loads and the load should be held for at least 
10 minutes (until stable). The proof test nails may be incorporated into the 
permanent soil nail slope, provided they satisfy the test criteria. Pullout tests and 
creep tests on the soil nails are integral components of the design for the soil nail 
slope system. Therefore, we recommend conducting pullout tests and creep tests 
in the presence of a Geolabs representative. 
3.2.5 Shotcrete Facing 
Shotcrete placement should be performed by an experienced nozzleman. Prior to 
production shotcreting, it is recommended that unreinforced test panels of 
shotcrete be constructed for inspection. A bottom key should be provided for the 
shotcrete facing.

3.3 
Site Drainage 
As indicated previously, we believe that one of the causes of the observed slope 
distress and erosion is the surface runoff water discharging over the slopes.  To reduce 
the potential for slope erosion and instability, we recommend that the surface runoff 
water be directed away from the top of slopes and into the existing drainage structures. 
 Remedial measure consisting of replacement of the existing headers with a 
concrete curb along the roadway may be considered.  The runoff will flow over the 
roadway pavement to the existing drain inlet further down the access drive.  Portion of 
the driveway should be reconstructed to eliminate the low spot in the driveway.  Should 
it be desired to remove the runoff water on the roadway pavement, drain pipes and 
manholes may be installed and connected to the drain inlet at the bottom of the access 
drive.  
3.4 
Inclinometer Monitoring Program 
Two monitoring wells equipped with inclinometer casing were installed at Boring 
Nos. 2 and 3 to observe potential movement of the slope fronting the Kaha‘opulani and 
Nae‘ole Buildings. The inclinometer casings were installed in the drilled boreholes on 
November 22 and 23, 2022. The slope inclinometer used on this project was 
manufactured by Slope Indicator Company. The inclinometer casing was made of 
acrylonitrile butadiene styrene (ABS) semi-rigid plastic with an outside diameter of 
2.75 inches and was installed in the drilled borehole to a depth of 48 feet below the 
existing ground surface. The purpose of the inclinometer casing is to allow the lowering 
of the slope inclinometer device into the borehole to measure the lateral deflection of 
the soil at various depths in order to determine where the earth movements are taking 
place as well as how much movements have occurred.  The approximate location of 
the inclinometers is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

Our monitoring program included weekly inclinometer readings between

results of our inclinometer monitoring program are shown as plots of profile change versus

In general, the inclinometer records earth movements perpendicular to the slope

The results of the inclinometer monitoring indicates that minimal lateral ground 
movements have occurred during the 3-month monitoring period. In addition, the small 
movements are occurring at the surface to a depth of about 10 feet based on the 
inclinometer readings for Boring No. 2.  Based on the results of the inclinometer 
monitoring, the distance of the existing buildings to the slope, and the building 
distresses observed, we believe that the cause of the building distress is not associated 
with the slope distress and erosion.

Two site reconnaissance visits were performed by our engineers to observe the

December 21, 2022. Site reconnaissance photographs depicting the typical building

On the makai sides of the buildings, diagonal cracks in the CMU walls on the 
ground floor level were observed (Photograph Nos. 1 and 2, Plate 4.1). The cracks were 
primarily hairline in width, but some were widened up to about 1/16 inch

building residents, we understand that some of the cracks are wide enough for black 
ants to get through the walls. Based on our observations of the interior and exterior of

On the mauka sides of the buildings, observed building distresses were

foundation below.  In addition, another source of water entering into the gravel strip is 
rainwater.

structures may have occurred. Based on visual observation and our DCP test results,

water sources indicated above entering the gravel strip and softening the underlying

3.6 
Pavement Design 
We anticipate that flexible pavement will be required to repair the roadway 
adjacent to the slope. Traffic data was not available for the road at the time this report 
was prepared. Therefore, we have assumed the road to be a low volume traffic based 
on the “City and County of Honolulu Structural Design Requirements for New Asphaltic 
Concrete Pavements” dated February, 6 2006.  In addition, we have made our 
pavement design assuming the pavement subgrade soil will consist of the existing 
subsurface materials with a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 13 or 
higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D1883. Based on the above, the following section may be used for the 
preliminary design of pavement. 
Flexible Pavement Section 
  2.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete 
  3.0-Inch Asphaltic Treated Base (95% Relative Compaction) 
  6.0-Inch Aggregate Base Course (95% Relative Compaction) 
11.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade 
The subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum depth of about 8 inches, 
moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and 
compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. Where minor shrinkage 
cracks are observed after subgrade preparation, we recommend thoroughly moistening

compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction and meet the material

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
four borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 4, to depths of about 30 to 52.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. Twelve DCP tests extending to depths of about 2.1 to 
6.9 feet were performed around the existing buildings to assess the condition of underlying 
soil. In addition, two bulk samples were collected from the near-surface  soils to assist in 
design of the pavement structure.  The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill 
rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools. The approximate boring 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-4. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch O.D. Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch O.D. standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the 
publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in 
Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

Recovery (REC) is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses. Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered 
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. For example, if 3.7 feet of 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery would be 74 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities, discounting drilling 
induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5.0-foot 
core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of 
Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to describe the relative quality 
of the rock, based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and 
Minerals."

Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by performing Dynamic 
Cone Penetration (DCP) tests extending to depths of about 2.1 to 6.9 feet below the 
existing ground surface.  The approximate locations where DCP tests were performed are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The DCP tests were then performed in general accordance with ASTM D6951, 
Standard Test Method for Use of the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer in Shallow Pavement 
Applications. The Dynamic Cone Penetrometer device consists of two 16mm-diameter 
stainless steel rods with the lower rod containing an anvil, a replaceable 60° pointed tip 
and depth markings. The upper rod holds a 17.6-pound drop hammer with a 22.6-inch 
drop distance. At the beginning of a test, the cone is “seated” by dropping the hammer 
until the widest part of the cone is just below the testing surface, at which point the initial 
penetration is recorded as 0. The operator then lifts and drops the hammer one or more 
times depending on the strength of the soil at the test location. Following each 
sequence of hammer drops, a penetration reading is taken. The process is continued 
until the desired depth of testing is reached, or the full length of the lower rod is buried. 
Results are shown on the Logs of Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests, Plates B-1.1 through 
B-12.5.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Five Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate C-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM D6913) was performed on a selected soil sample 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. The 
graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate C-2. 
Four one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the soils under surcharge 
pressures. The test results are presented on Plate C-3. 
Three Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The results of the 
direct shear test are presented on Plates C-4 through C-6. 
Two Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) were

and moisture content relationships.  The test results are presented on Plates C-10 and
