REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
W O 4207 00 A Page 5
Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau banked
against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the Schofield
Plateau or Central Oahu
The forces of weathering and erosion attacked this plateau generating sediments
which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and further south these
alluvial sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and coral algal reef
formations to form a sedimentary wedge ranging in thickness from zero feet in the area of
the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the
Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal
aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of these sediments has continued from earlier
geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
growth and retreat of the great continental glaciers As the glaciers advanced less water
was available to fill the oceanic basins and sea levels fell below the current sea level As
the glaciers melted and retreated more water became available and sea levels rose to and
often above the current level When the stand of the sea was high and prolonged marine
deposits accumulated at elevations above the current sea level
At the project site below the near surface fill materials are primarily coral algal
deposits which are composed of medium hard limestone with an open lattice like structure
It is believed that these coral deposits were laid during the Kaena 95 feet Mean Sea Level
MSL stand of the sea and subsequently eroded to near their current elevation during the
Waimanalo 25 feet MSL stand The coral formation has also been subjected to subaerial
weathering
Coral algal materials are chemically composed of calcium carbonate During
subaerial weathering slightly acidic rain and groundwater can slowly attack the carbonate
dissolving the weaker portions of the formation This creates solution cavities ranging from
small pores to large caverns

Shallow Foundations
Based on the generally competent subsoil conditions encountered at the site we
believe that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the
proposed renovations We understand that the plans for the existing structures indicate
that a bearing pressure of 8 000 pounds per square foot psf was used in the design of the
existing foundations We envision that a probing and grouting program was likely
implemented at the existing foundation locations in order to achieve the relatively high
bearing pressures
Due to the limited number of new footings and the limited space in which to perform
construction work we believe that the probing and grouting program normally implemented
for new construction may be omitted for this renovation project However the allowable
bearing pressures wili need to be reduced from the relatively high bearing pressures used
previously Therefore an allowable bearing pressure of may be utilized for the
design of new foundations bearing on the on site medium dense to very dense coralline
materials on site stiff to very stiff sandy clays or weli compacted fill materials This bearing
value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces Footings should have a minimum width of
12 inches and should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the existing grade
New footings to be located adjacent to the existing footings should be lowered to
the same depths as the existing building footings or slightly deeper to avoid surcharging
the existing foundations In general the new footings should not extend deeper than the
existing footings by more than about 6 inches to avoid undermining the existing footings
lf foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should also be

from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines These

Soft and yielding areas encountered should be over excavated to expose firm natural

The excavated soft soils should not be used as fill and should be disposed of properly

Finished subgrades and areas designated to receive fills should be scarified to a

Fills and Backfills
In general the on site coralline materials should be suitable for use as fill materials
However the on site sandy clays are considered to be relatively expansive and
should not be used as fill materials Select granular fill should consist of
non expansive select granular material such as crushed coral basalt gravel or
cinder sand The material should be free of vegetation and deleterious materials
The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles
passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20
or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills backfills and aggregate base course should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers
to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test
procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of soils are critical elements of
earthwork observations and soil density tests should be performed by a
representative of Geolabs Hawaii during site grading to assist the contractor in

should be noted that the moisture requirement of the subgrades above the optimum

Slabs On Grade
Our field exploration disclosed that localized areas of near surface clayey soils with
relatively high swelling potential generally underlie the project site Unless floor slabs are
properly designed and constructed there is potential for future distress to the lightly loaded
slabs on grade resulting from swelling of these soils due to increases in the moisture content

To reduce the potential for distress to slabs on grade resulting from swelling of the sandy
clay materials we recommend that the sandy clay materials be removed from beneath
slabs on grade and replaced with 18 inches of non expansive select granular fill material
The on site coralline materials may be used as non expansive select granular fill material

systems be installed around the structures and that the discharge be diverted away from the
foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and
slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have
concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry
surface water off the site Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during
or after construction Due to the fairly level grade of the area construction of drain inlets

discharging to a subsurface drainage system may be necessary to accommodate proper
drainage of the surface water runoff
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above

excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the

as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water run off away from walls

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
12 borings designated as Boring Nos 1 to 12 extending to depths of about 1 8 to 21 5 feet
below the existing ground elevation at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight auger equipment and a concrete coring machine
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 12
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler and a 2 inch OD standard penetration
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 to 24 inch drive are shown on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
