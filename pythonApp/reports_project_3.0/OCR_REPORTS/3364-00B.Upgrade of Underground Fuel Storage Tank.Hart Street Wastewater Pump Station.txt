INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Upgrade of Underground Fuel Storage Tank at the Hart Street
Wastewater Pump Station located in the Kapalama area of Honolulu on the island of
Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recom
mendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses The recommendations presented herein are intended for design of founda
tion site grading and excavation and are subject to the limitations noted at the end
of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
We understand that it is proposed to install a new underground fuel storage tank
at the Hart Street Wastewater Pump Station in the District of Honolulu on the island of
Oahu Hawaii Based on the information provided to us we understand that the
proposed new fuel storage tank will be a fiberglass tank The new tank will have plan
dimensions of approximately 20 feet long by 8 feet wide and 6 feet high The
approximate location of the proposed new tank is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Detailed information for the project was not available at the time this report was
prepared Based on available information we understand that the top of the new fuel
tank will be embedded about 3 to 4 feet below the existing ground surface Therefore
to install the new underground fuel storage tank an excavation of up to 12 feet below
the existing ground surface will be required

In addition based on our previous experience in the area the project site is
underlain by loose sandy soils and coral formation at relatively shallow depths with high
groundwater levels Therefore we anticipate that shoring and dewatering of the
excavation for the new underground fuel storage tank will be needed for the proposed
construction

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be of the middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be of the late
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age When the building of the Waianae and Koolau
volcanoes came to an end only a slight embayment existed on the southern coast of
Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3364 00 B Page 5
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of
the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available
such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition caused the top of the basaltic lava
flows to be highly weathered and mantled by residual soils During the Pleistocene
Epoch the Kaena stand of the sea at approximately 90 feet Mean Sea Level a delta
of silt sand and gravel built out into the embayment near the project site Agricultural
land development and reclamation projects within the last century have brought the
project site to its present form

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsoil conditions at the subject site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings to a depth of about 26 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our exploration encountered a surface fill layer consisting of medium
dense to dense sandy and gravelly soils to a depth of about 5 feet below the existing
ground surface Below the near surface fill layer our borings generally encountered
loose to very loose sandy gravels and gravelly sands interbedded with occasional
layers of very soft clayey silts extending to about 25 feet below the surface Stiff to
very stiff silty clays were encountered from about 25 feet to the maximum depth
explored of about 26 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was encountered in all three borings at a depth of about 5 feet
below the ground surface at the time of our field exploration This groundwater level
corresponds to an elevation of about 1 feet MSL Due to the proximity of the site to
Honolulu Harbor it should be noted that groundwater levels measured in the borings
during our field exploration will likely be affected by tidal fluctuations In addition the
groundwater levels at the project site may also be influenced by seasonal precipitation
and storm surge conditions

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
In general our field exploration indicates that the proposed project site is
underlain by a medium dense to dense near surface granular fill layer over loose to
very loose sandy and gravelly soils extending to a depth of about 25 feet below the
ground surface Soft clayey silt lenses pockets were encountered within the loose
granular soil layers Groundwater was encountered at about 5 feet below the ground
surface at the time of our field exploration
We understand that the top of the proposed tank structure will be embedded
about 3 to 4 feet below the existing ground surface Therefore construction of the
underground fuel storage tank will involve an excavation on the order of 12 feet below
the surface Because construction of the underground fuel storage tank will replace the
soil with lighter material fuel there is a net decrease in stresses on the underlying
soils On this basis we recommend that a concrete mat be used to provide the
necessary bearing support and uplift resistance for the tank structure Because of the
soft soils anticipated at the foundation subgrade level we recommend that the concrete
mat bear on a minimum 2 foot gravel layer wrapped in a filter fabric for more uniform
bearing support The gravel layer would also serve as a working platform
We anticipate that an excavation of up to about 12 feet and subsequent
backfilling will be required for construction of the new tank structure Due to the
presence of loose to very loose subsoils and high groundwater levels we believe that
shoring and bracing will be needed to support the excavation and to aid in dewatering
during construction
Because of the loose soft subsoils encountered some movement of soils around
the new tank excavation should be anticipated due to changes in the earth stresses

during and after construction In addition it should be noted that dewatering for
construction of adjacent structures in the future may result in settlement of this
underground tank structure Therefore we recommend that some flexibility in the

in the design to accommodate possible earth movements

Foundations
We envision that a concrete mat will be needed below the proposed tank
structure to provide the necessary uplift resistance and foundation support An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 1 000 p s f may be used for the design of the
concrete mat bearing on a minimum 2 foot thick gravel layer wrapped in a filter fabric
This allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third for transient loads such as temporary wind and or seismic forces
Due to the presence of very soft loose subsoils near the foundation level we
recommend that a minimum 2 foot gravel layer wrapped with a layer of filter fabric
such as Mirafi 140N or equivalent be provided below the concrete mat for more
uniform bearing support and to serve as a working platform during construction The
gravel layer should consist of clean granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation The filter fabric should be lapped a minimum of 24
inches at the ends of each roll and along the sides Guide specifications for the filter
fabric are presented in Appendix C of this report
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3364 00 B Page 9
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the footing and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the foundation A coefficient of
friction of 0 35 may be used between the base of the mat footing and the supporting
materials Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated using an equivalent
fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f above the
groundwater table and 150 p c f below the groundwater for footings embedded in the
in situ soil These values assume that the concrete for footings is placed directly
against the footing excavations Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches below finished grade should be neglected
Due to the high groundwater levels encountered in our borings at the proposed
tank site it is anticipated that the proposed underground fuel storage tank will be
subjected to buoyant uplift forces Resistance to uplift loads may be mobilized by the
dead weight of the tank structure the concrete mat and the backfill material above the
concrete mat Contribution of dead weight from the backfill may be estimated using a
groundwater For evaluating uplift forces due to hydrostatic pressures a high
groundwater level elevation of 3 feet MSL may be used for design to take into
consideration tidal fluctuations
Settlements of the new tank structure may result from the compression of the
soft loose subgrade soil layer due to the placement of the heavier backfill material
and or disturbance of the soft loose subsoils during construction We estimate the total
settlement of the tank structure to be on the order of 1 inches We believe that most
of the settlements should occur during the construction period

Site Grading
Final grading plans were not available to us at the time this report was prepared
However we anticipate excavations and backfills of up to 12 feet relative to the existing
ground surface will be required for the project construction Site grading operations
should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii It is important that a
representative from our office observe whether the exposed soil conditions during
excavation are similar to those encountered in our exploration
We understand that additional fill placement is not planned for this project
Because of the presence of compressible soils at the site placement of additional fills
will induce consolidation of the compressible soils This consolidation will significantly
affect the foundations of the proposed tank structure and could induce areal settlements
which may also affect the surrounding existing structures and infrastructure The
following recommendations are intended to provide guidelines for the design of site
grading work
1 At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits
should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris
rubbish and other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed
of off site
2 The on site granular soils generated from the excavation may be reused
as a source of fill and backfill material However the on site soft and wet
clayey soils should not be reused as fill or backfill material It should be
noted that the on site soils are likely saturated and may require drying of
the soils prior to use as compacted backfill material
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3364 00 B Page 11
3 Imported fill and or backfill material should consist of well graded granular
materials of less than 3 inches in maximum dimension The material
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 15 or
more and should have a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when
tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 In addition
it should contain between 10 and 30 percent passing the No 200 sieve
Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for conformance
with our recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use In addition open graded material such as No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should not be used as backfill
material from the spring line of the tank structure up to the ground surface
to reduce the potential for migration of the surrounding fine soils into the
backfill material
4 All fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches
in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as
a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density Fill placement should commence at the lowest
point and continue upward in level compacted lifts

Dewatering
Dewatering will likely be necessary since the groundwater levels encountered in
our field exploration were above the bottom of the excavation Dewatering should be
the sole responsibility of the contractor The dewatering operations should be
coordinated with the shoring support so that the stability of the excavations is not
jeopardized and the operations should be carried out without softening the bottom of
the excavations
Because of the granular nature of the subsoil conditions it is anticipated that the
granular soils encountered are relatively permeable Therefore dewatering of the
excavation may involve large volumes of water It is our opinion that a cut off wall
system such as interlocking sheetpiles should be considered to aid in dewatering the
excavation
Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of the excavations
is generally not recommended The resultant areal depression of the natural
groundwater table could induce consolidation of the compressible subsoils resulting in
potential ground settlements which would affect the existing structures
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents
should be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural
groundwater table and to exclude the water from the excavations to permit construction
of the proposed underground fuel storage tank structure under safe and dry conditions
Therefore these works or systems should include but not be limited to pumping

Dewatering Method
The method and equipment to be used for the excavation dewatering should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considera
tions We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in
selecting a suitable method of dewatering
1 The method should result in the least disturbance or damage to
adjacent existing structures
2 The method should maintain stability of and also provide safe and
dry working conditions in the excavations
3 The method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modifications to
accommodate various ground conditions

Utility Trench
We envision that a new piping network will be required for the project It should
be noted that groundwater was encountered at about 5 feet below the ground surface
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding

under the pipes Where soft and or loose soil deposits are encountered near the pipe

18 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped in a geotextile
fabric Mirafi 140N or equivalent The backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes
should consist of free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the
potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill A typical section of
the trench detail is presented on Plate 4
