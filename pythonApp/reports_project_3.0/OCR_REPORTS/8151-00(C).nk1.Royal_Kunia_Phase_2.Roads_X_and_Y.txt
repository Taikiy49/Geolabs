grading, and underground utilities only. The findings and recommendations presented 
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene 
to early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava 
flows from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad 
plateau, now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is located on the western 
flank of the Koolau Volcano. 
The Schofield Plateau was formed when lava from the Koolau Volcano ponded 
against the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene 
Epoch. The dips of the lava beds are near horizontal (between 3 to 5 degrees from 
horizontal). The lava flows on the plateau have undergone in-situ weathering to depths 
of 50 to 100 feet. Deep narrow canyons, such as Waikakalua, Kipapa, and 
Kaukonahua, are the result of this in-situ weathering.  
In-situ weathering of the Koolau lavas on the Schofield Plateau generated a 
relatively thick mantle of residual soils generally consisting of reddish colored clayey 
silts. These residual soils grade with depth to saprolite, i.e., soil that retains the relict 
structure of the parent rock, which eventually grades to a basalt rock formation. In some 
portions of the residual soils and saprolite, remnant boulders, or “corestones” of 
weathering resistant rock are encountered.

3.1.5 Site Preparation Below Pavement Areas 
We anticipate that the majority of the new roadway pavements will be 
constructed at or near the existing grades with minimal fills or in a cut condition. 
In general, the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly 
cleared and grubbed at the on-set of earthwork. Vegetation, debris, deleterious 
material, and other unsuitable materials should be removed and properly 
disposed of off-site or disposed of in a designated area to reduce the potential for 
contamination of the excavated materials.  
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over-excavated to 
expose stiff and/or dense materials. The resulting excavation should be backfilled 
with aggregate base course materials. The excavated soft and/or organic soils 
should be properly disposed of off-site.  
After clearing and grubbing, the pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a 
depth of at least 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the 
optimum moisture content and compacted to no less than 95 percent relative 
compaction.  
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade, we 
recommend scarifying and re-preparing the soils as recommended above. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and 
replacement with well-compacted aggregate base course materials. Contract 
documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over-excavation 
and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the over-excavation 
quantities.

As an additional check for stability and uniform compaction, we recommend 
proof-rolling the subgrade prior to placing the aggregate base course materials 
using a pneumatic-tired vehicle with a gross vehicle weight of at least

3.1.6 Fill Materials Below Pavement Areas 
In general, the excavated on-site materials with particles no larger than 3 inches 
and minimum CBR values of 18 and 27 may be reused as a source of general fill 
material for Roads X and Y, respectively. Imported fill materials, if required, should 
consist of non-expansive select granular material, such as crushed coralline or 
basaltic materials. The materials should be well-graded from coarse to fine with 
particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The materials should have a 
laboratory CBR value of 27 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent 
or less. Imported fill materials should be tested for conformance with these 
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use.

3.2.2 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the excavated on-site clayey silt/silty clay soils should be suitable for 
use as general fill materials provided that the maximum particle size is less than 
3 inches in largest dimension and have minimum CBR values of 18 and 27 for

3.2.4 Excavations 
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by stiff to 
hard fill, residual soils, and saprolite. It is anticipated that the near-surface soils 
may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as excavating 
with a backhoe excavator or ripping with a bulldozer. However, it should be noted

saprolitic deposits that may require the use of hoerams or chipping. It should be

three of our borings in the residual and saprolitic soils. Deeper excavations may 
also encounter basalt formation that may also require the use of hoerams or 
chipping.

project vicinity. We recommend that contractors proposing to bid on this project 
be encouraged to examine the site conditions and the boring data to make their 
own interpretation.

3.3 
Slabs-On-Grade 
We anticipate that concrete slabs-on-grade, primarily consisting of exterior 
walkways, will be provided for the new roadway project. Based on the results of our field 
exploration and laboratory testing, the near-surface clayey soils exhibit a low expansion 
potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. To reduce the potential for structural 
distress resulting from swelling of these clayey subgrade soils, we recommend that the 
subgrade soils be kept within about 2 percent above the optimum moisture content prior 
to final placement of concrete. Conventional slab-on-grade design may be used above 
the properly prepared subgrade soils. 
We recommend scarifying the subgrade soils to a depth of about 10 inches, 
moisture-conditioning the soils to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and compacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The 
underlying subgrade soils and select granular fill should be wetted and kept moist until 
the final placement of slab concrete. Where shrinkage cracks are observed after 
compaction of the subgrade, we recommend preparing the soils again as 
recommended. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to 
inclement weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and 
replacement with engineered fill. 
Where the concrete slabs will be subjected to equipment vibration and/or 
vehicular traffic, we recommend placing the slab over 6 inches of aggregate subbase 
material. The aggregate subbase should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates 
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  
Control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways 
with expansion joints at right-angle intersections. The thickened edges of slabs adjacent 
to unpaved areas should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent 
grade. It should be emphasized that the areas adjacent to the slab edges should be 
backfilled tightly against the edges of the slabs with relatively impervious soils. These 
areas should also be graded to divert water away from the slabs and to reduce the 
potential for water ponding around the slabs.

3.4 
Underground Utility Structures 
Based on the information provided, we understand that new underground utility 
structures, such as sewer/drain manholes and catch basins, will be required for the new 
roadway project. Based on our field exploration, we anticipate the new underground 
utility structures will be constructed in the stiff to hard residual and/or saprolitic soils at 
the project site. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, the following general 
guidelines may be used for the design of the drainage structures at the project site. 
3.4.1 Underground Utility Structure Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, an allowable bearing pressure of 
up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be utilized for design of the 
underground utility structures bearing on the stiff to hard residual and saprolitic soils 
at the project site. To provide uniform bearing support for the new drainage 
structures, we recommend providing a minimum 6-inch thick layer of No. 3 Fine 
gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) below the bottom of the drainage structures. 
We anticipate the sewer/drain manholes and catch basin will be pre-cast concrete 
structures. 
Foundation subgrades should be recompacted to a firm and unyielding surface 
prior to the placement of the No. 3 Fine gravel. Soft and/or loose materials 
encountered at the bottom of the excavations should be over-excavated to expose 
the underlying firm materials or maximum 2 feet below the bottom of the footing. 
The over-excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill materials 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  
Based on our engineering analyses, we believe that the net increase in bearing 
pressure on the underlying stiff to hard residual and saprolitic soils resulting from 
the drainage structure construction would be minimal. Settlements on the order of 
less than 1 inch are anticipated for the drainage structures.

Underground Utility Lines 
We anticipate that underground utilities, such as drain, water, sewer, gas, and

open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes for uniform support.

or select granular fill material. The backfill should be placed in maximum 8-inch level 
loose lifts and mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction

3.7 
Corrosion Potential 
Laboratory corrosion tests were performed on soil samples from Boring Nos. 1 
through 8 and 61. In general, the minimum resistivity values ranged from about 
2,400 ohm-cm to about 6,000 ohm-cm. In our technical memorandum dated 
August 31, 2021, we discussed the overall corrosion potential of the in-situ soils within 
the Royal Kunia, Phase 2 project site.  
In summary, based on the updated Board of Water Supply, City and County of 
Honolulu Water System External Corrosion Control Standards dated July 28, 2021, 
most of the in-situ soils across the project site should be classified as Corrosion 
Category A (moderately to severely corrosive). The resistivity test results at 
Boring Nos. 1, 7, and 8 did not meet the criteria for Corrosion Category A. However, the 
resistivity test results were close to the threshold value of 5,000 ohm-cm, and the 
locations of these higher resistivity test results do not follow an obvious pattern. 
Therefore, consideration should be given to categorizing the entire Royal Kunia Phase 2 
project site as Corrosion Category A. It may be appropriate to consult with a 
professional corrosion engineer to review the test results and provide detailed 
recommendations for corrosion protection. 
The full corrosion potential discussion is included in our technical memorandum 
titled “Corrosivity Test Results, Royal Kunia Phase 2” dated August 31, 2021.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
16 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 8, 44, 46, 48, 50, 51, 57, 61, and 65, 
extending to depths of about 15.6 to 16.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In 
addition, 11 bulk samples of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 through 
Bulk-4, Bulk-12, Bulk-15, Bulk-19, and Bulk-25 through 28, were obtained to evaluate 
the pavement support characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring 
and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled 
using a truck-mounted drill rig with continuous flight augers.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-16. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550 Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Eighteen Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test 
results are provided on Plates B-1 and B-2. 
One sieve analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Twelve One-Inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-3. 
Four Unconfined Compression Strength tests (ASTM D2166) were performed on 
selected cohesive soil samples to evaluate the compressive strength of the soil. The 
test results are presented on Plates B-5 through B-8. 
Four Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression tests (ASTM D2850) were 
performed on selected in-situ soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strengths of 
the on-site clayey soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressures were 
used as the applied confining pressures for the relatively “undisturbed” soil samples.  
The test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on Plates B-9 through B-12. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested.  Results of the direct 
shear tests are presented on Plates B-13 and B-14. 
Seven sets of Corrosion tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0) were 
performed by our office and TestAmerica laboratories, Inc. on selected soil samples 
obtained from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-15. 
Seven Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557 A) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-16 through B-22.

(320-63721-A-1-A), (320-63721-A-1-B MS) and (320-63721-A-1-C MSD). Elevated reporting limits (RLs) are provided.

and precision. The associated laboratory control sample (LCS) met acceptance criteria.
