SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration at the project site generally encountered surficial fills and 
cultivated soils about 1 to 3 feet thick overlying stiff to hard silty and clayey alluvial soils 
extending down to the maximum depth explored of about 20 feet below the existing ground 
surface. Field observations during our exploration indicated the surface soils at the project 
site are dry and friable. In addition, we observed shrinkage cracks up to about 6 inches deep 
at the existing ground surface at various locations throughout the project site. We did not 
encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field exploration.  
Based on our laboratory test results, the near-surface clayey soils exhibit moderate 
to high shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture 
contents. Considering the lightly loaded structures planned for the Phase 4 development, it 
is anticipated that the moderate to high shrink-swell potential of the on-site soils could result 
in structural distresses to the new buildings and pavements. To reduce the risk of structural 
distress, we recommend implementing special subgrade preparations during construction. 
Special attention should be given to the site grading preparation and foundation design to 
reduce the risk of structural distresses due to the moderately to highly expansive soil 
conditions. 
We recommend the site grading preparation consist of the following procedures for 
creating a reworked soil layer within the entire grading limits. 
1. 
Removal of 12 inches of the near-surface cultivated soils after the clearing 
and grubbing operation. These soils should be considered “unsuitable” 
because they contain an appreciable amount of organic materials. These 
soils should be disposed properly off-site or used in landscaping areas, if 
appropriate. 
2. 
Over-excavate and remove an additional 12 inches of soil and moisture 
condition to about 4 percent above the optimum moisture content for use as 
general fill, provided the soils are free of vegetation, debris, deleterious 
material and other unsuitable materials. 
3. 
Prior to replacing the moisture-conditioned soil as fill, the bottom of the over-
excavation should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches, moisture-conditioned 
to about 4 percent above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted 
to a minimum of 85 percent relative compaction. 
The intent of the above site preparation is to create a 2-foot thick reworked soil layer 
within the entire grading limits. The reworked soil serves to close-up the shrinkage cracks 
observed throughout the site. It would also reduce the future soil swelling/heaving potential

underneath the Waffleboxes to a minimum of 2 feet beyond the slab perimeter to reduce 
water infiltration beneath the slab foundations. 
Generally, we anticipate the earthwork would likely involve two primary types of 
materials: select granular fill and general fill. Select granular fill should consist of 
non-expansive granular material, such as crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. 
General fill may consist of the on-site soils with a maximum particle size of 6 inches and 
should be free of deleterious material. In general, the excavated on-site materials 
(not including the 12 inches of soil materials removed after clearing and grubbing) may be 
re-used as a source of general fill material, provided they are free of vegetation, debris, 
deleterious material and other unsuitable materials. 
General fill material should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, moisture-conditioned to about 4 percent above the optimum moisture content, 
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The non-expansive select 
granular fill material should be placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to at least 90 percent 
relative compaction (or 95 percent relative compaction for finished subgrades subjected to 
vehicular traffic).

clayey clumps should be thoroughly broken down and mixed by the compaction equipment, 
such as large sheepsfoot compactors and/or large dozers, until achieving a relatively

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Ho`opili Development – Phase 4 project in the Ewa area on the Island 
of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project

This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting

These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of site 
grading, building foundations, slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, and pavements only. 
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted 
at the end of this report.

Project Considerations

H-1 Freeway and west of Fort Weaver Road in the Ewa area on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. The Ho`opili Development will encompass approximately 1,500 acres. The 
project site is gently sloping and is currently used for agricultural cultivation.

of future Phase 3. Details of the grading and layout were not available at the time this

park site, 1.7 acres for a community center, and road extensions through the site in 
generally north-south and east-west directions. Structural details were not available at the 
time this report was prepared; however, we anticipate the planned structures will be 
relatively light.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows 
from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, 
now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the southeastern flank of the 
Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering, followed by erosion of the Schofield Plateau, 
generated sediments which were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to 
the south, these sediments have accumulated and are interbedded with marine 
sediments and coral/algal reef formations, forming a sedimentary wedge. The 
thickness of the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate 
Route H-1 Highway to over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain 
and serves as the confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of 
Southern Oahu. Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through 
the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials.

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The project site is situated at the southwestern portion of Ho`opili Development, 
north of the Phase 3 site in the Ewa area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The site is 
bordered by cultivated agricultural land to the north, east, and west, and the future 
Phase 3 development to the south.  
At the time of our field exploration, the site was generally being used for 
commercial farming of various vegetable and herb crops; however, portions of the farming 
areas appeared to have been recently tilled possibly in preparation for future planting. In 
addition, several undeveloped pockets of vacant land were observed throughout the 
project site with light to heavy vegetation ground cover consisting of haole koa and weed 
grasses. Several unpaved access roads were observed traversing the site. Shrinkage 
cracks up to about 6 inches deep were observed on the existing ground surface at various 
locations throughout the project site. 
Based on our site observations and topographic survey map provided, the project 
site generally slopes gently down from north to south at an average slope gradient of 
about 2 percent. Based on the information provided, ground surface elevations range 
from about +114 to +84 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) at the northwestern and southeastern 
portions of the project site, respectively.

through the crack openings and soften the subsurface soils around the building 
foundations, thus affecting the foundation performance. Therefore, it is critical to keep the 
subgrade soil moist during the entire mass grading and site work phases. The individual 
homeowners and Homeowner’s Associations should be made aware of the potential for 
recurrence of shrinkage cracks, and appropriate maintenance measures should be 
implemented to reduce the potential for recurrence of shrinkage cracks around the 
building foundation.

Based on our field exploration, we believe the planned building structures may be 
supported on a shallow Wafflemat foundation system bearing on the 2-foot reworked soil 
layer or general fills placed below the finished pad grade. The Wafflemat is a 
post-tensioned slab-on-grade foundation system. This proprietary system utilizes a series 
of recycled plastic boxes, “Waffleboxes”, which are placed with the open side down on 
the soil. The Waffleboxes are arranged to form deep ribs or footings throughout the floor 
system, creating a very stiff foundation. A post-tensioned concrete slab is monolithically 
poured over and between the boxes. The empty space beneath the boxes allow for soil 
expansion, reducing uplift of the slab.

soil layer or general fills placed in accordance with the recommendations presented 
herein. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third 
(1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic forces. 
Generally, minimum subgrade preparation is required for the Wafflemat foundation 
system. Considering moderate to high expansive soil conditions, we recommend 
preparing the Wafflemat subgrade in accordance with Section 3.1.1 – Site Preparation

3.6 
Corrosion Potential 
Three sets of laboratory corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum 
Resistivity (ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), 
were performed (by our office and Test America Laboratories, Inc.) on selected soil 
samples obtained from our field exploration to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site soils. 
Results of the corrosivity tests are presented in Appendix B.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower 
the resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of 
metallic objects. 
On the basis of the laboratory resistivity and pH results, the subsurface soils at the 
project site have resistivity values ranging from approximately 600 to 1,300 ohm-cm and 
pH values ranging from approximately 6.8 to 7.6 within the upper 5 feet of soils, 
corresponding to a corrosion rating between 1 (Extremely Corrosive) and 2 (Very 
Corrosive) based on the guidelines provided by the City & County of Honolulu – Board of 
Water Supply.  
Based on the results of the laboratory corrosivity tests, we believe that a corrosion 
rating of 1 (Extremely Corrosive) may be used for the proposed development. Design of 
metallic substructures, such as water line pipes or other metallic piping, should consider 
the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. 
Levels of the chloride content of the on-site soils encountered near the existing 
ground surface are low on the order of 52 parts per million (ppm) or less, while the sulfate 
content is relatively low, less than 190 ppm. Based on the chloride and sulfate content 
tests performed on the on-site soils, we believe that either Type I or Type II cement may 
be used for concrete in contact with the ground planned for the project. It may be 
appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test results

3.7.3 Design Subgrade Conditions 
Based on our field exploration, the pavement subgrades along the proposed new 
roadway alignments are generally underlain by stiff to hard clay. Laboratory testing 
performed on the surface clayey soils at the project site generally indicated an 
average CBR value of about 5.4 and an average swell of about 1.5 percent.

Due to the relatively poor pavement support characteristics of the on-site clayey soils, 
we recommend placing the pavement sections on a minimum 18-inch thick layer of 
non-expansive, select granular fill.  
Alternatively, hydrated lime treatment of the upper 12 inches of roadway subgrade 
may be implemented to stabilize the in-situ moderate to highly expansive clayey soil. 
It is our opinion that the soil-lime stabilization is capable of mitigating in-situ clayey 
soil expansion when subjected to moisture fluctuations and thereby permanently 
increasing the bearing capacity of the soils for road pavement support. Details of the 
hydrated lime treatment procedures and requirements are presented in our letter 
report entitled “Geotechnical Engineering Evaluation, Ho`opili Development – Soil-
Lime Stabilization, Ewa, Oahu, Hawaii”, dated December 18, 2015. 
The select granular fill material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more 
and a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with 
ASTM D1883. Laboratory testing performed on the soil-lime mixture generally 
indicated a CBR value of 30 or more with a swell of less than 0.2 percent. Based on 
the test results, a design CBR value of 20 and a maximum swell of less than 
1 percent with a subgrade of resilient modulus (MR) of 17,380 pounds per square 
inch (psi) were adopted in our pavement analyses for the select granular fill and/or 
soil-lime mixture soils subgrade conditions.

3.7.5 Site Preparation Below Pavement Areas 
As mentioned previously, the near-surface clayey soils at the project site are dry and 
friable and exhibit moderate to high shrink/swell characteristics when subjected to 
fluctuations in the soil moisture contents. Prior to placing the 18-inch thick layer of 
non-expansive, select granular fill, we recommend that the subgrade be properly 
scarified to a depth of at least 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to about 4 percent 
above the optimum moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent 
relative compaction, as specified in Section 3.1.1 – Site Preparation herein. 
Where minor shrinkage cracks are observed after subgrade preparation, we 
recommend thoroughly moistening the soil to close the cracks prior to re-compacting.

3.7.6 Fill Materials Below Pavement Areas 
Select granular fill should consist of non-expansive granular material, such as 
crushed coralline and/or basaltic materials. The material should be well-graded from 
coarse to fine with particles no larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material 
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell 
of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.  
Aggregate base course and subbase course should meet the material requirements 
for Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in Sections 31 and 30, 
respectively, of the Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction, City & 
County of Honolulu, September 1986. Geolabs should test imported fill materials for 
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the 
intended use. 
3.7.7 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
The non-expansive, select granular fill should be placed in level lifts of about 8 inches 
in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. The compaction requirement 
for the final lift of the select granular fill should be increased to at least 95 percent 
relative compaction. Aggregate base course and subbase course and should be 
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a 
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) 
corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
As an additional effort for stability and uniform compaction, we recommend 
proof-rolling the non-expansive, select granular fill material prior to placing the

3.7.8 Pavement Drainage 
The recommended pavement sections assume that adequate drainage will be 
provided for the paved areas which should be sloped and drainage gradients 
maintained to carry surface water off-site. Surface water ponding should not be 
allowed on-site during or after construction. When concrete curbs are used to isolate 
landscaping in or adjacent to the pavement areas, we recommend extending the 
curbs a minimum of 2 inches into the soils below the aggregate base or subbase 
layer to reduce the potential for migration of excessive landscape water into the 
pavement section. Alternatively, a subdrain system could be constructed to collect 
the excess water from landscaping irrigation. For long-term performance, we 
recommend constructing a subdrain system adjacent to the paved/landscaped 
areas.   
3.8 
Erosion Control Measures (Landscape) 
As indicated previously, we observed shrinkage cracks at the existing ground 
surface of the project site. In general, shrinkage cracks will develop in the ground due to 
the lack of maintenance of the soil moisture content as the soils start to dry out under the 
sun and dry weather conditions. The development of shrinkage cracks in the ground was 
apparently intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions in the Ewa area resulting 
in large fluctuations in the soil moisture content. 
We recommend grassing the graded surfaces (including cut and fill slopes) to 
reduce the potential for significant soil erosion soon after completion of the site grading.

3.9 
Utility Trenches 
It is anticipated that the utilities for the proposed development project will consist 
of primarily electrical, water, sewer, and drain lines. In general, good construction 
practices should be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the 
proposed utilities. The contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used 
for excavation, subject to practical limits and safety considerations. The excavation 
should comply with all applicable local, state, and federal safety requirements. The 
contractor should be responsible for trench shoring design and installation. Trench 
shoring and bracing should conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements. 
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) for support of the utility lines. The 
initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of free-draining 
backfills, such as open-graded gravel, to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from 
compaction of the backfill. It is critical to use a free-draining granular material to reduce 
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate 
support for the sides of the pipes. The use of on-site clayey soils as backfill directly around 
the utility pipes is not recommended and should not be allowed. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the finished subgrade may consist of approved on-site soils or select granular fill material. 
The granular or on-site soil backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content or about 4 percent above the optimum moisture content, 
respectively, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for 
appreciable future ground subsidence. The finished subgrade of the trench backfill below 
the pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative 
compaction.

3.10 
Drainage 
The finished grades outside the building structures should be sloped to shed water 
away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding. Surface 
water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction. It is highly 
recommended to install gutter systems around the buildings and to divert discharge away 
from the foundation and slab areas. Excessive landscape watering near the foundations 
and slabs (within 3 feet of the building perimeter) should be avoided. Planters next to 
foundations also should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce the 
potential for significant water infiltration into the subsoils. 
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the 
building foundations and slabs-on-grade because ponded water could cause subsurface 
soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength. The foundation excavations 
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab edges immediately after setting of 
the concrete to reduce the potential for appreciable water infiltration into the subsurface. 
In addition, drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be 
maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the foundations and slabs.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
ten borings, designated as Boring Nos. 901 through 910, extending to depths of about 19 to 
20 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, five bulk samples of the near-surface 
soils, designated as Bulk-901 through Bulk-905, were obtained to evaluate the pavement 
support characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample 
locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-10. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Ten Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test results 
is provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) including four hydrometer tests 
[ASTM D422-63(2007)e2] were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the 
gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification. Graphic presentation 
of the grain size distributions are provided on Plate B-2. 
Ten one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-3. 
Sixteen one-dimensional swell or settlement potential tests (ASTM D4546 
Method A) were performed to evaluate the swell and/or compressibility characteristics of 
the remolded soil samples under different surcharge pressures. A soil sample is remolded 
to approximately 90 percent relative compaction (based on ASTM D1557) to simulate the 
anticipated field conditions. The remolded soil sample is allowed to settle until it has 
stabilized under a surcharge pressure. Water is then added to the soil sample to evaluate 
its collapse or swell potential in a saturated condition. The test results are presented on 
Plates B-4 through B-7. 
Three Expansion Index of Soil tests were performed on selected samples in 
general accordance with ASTM D4829. The test is a standardized test used to determine 
the expansive characteristics of the soil. The test results are summarized on Plate B-8. 
One Consolidation test was performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate the 
compressibility characteristic of the on-site soils. The test was performed in accordance 
with ASTM D2435, and the test results are plotted on Plate B-9. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested. The test results are 
presented on Plate B-10.

One Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression (TXUU) test was performed 
on a selected in-situ soil sample in accordance with ASTM D2850 to evaluate the 
undrained shear strength of the soils. The approximate in-situ effective overburden 
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the “undisturbed” sample. The 
test results and the stress-strain curve is presented on Plate B-11. 
Five Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557 Method A) were performed 
on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-12 through B-16. 
Five laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-17 through B-21. 
Three sets of Corrosivity tests, including pH (ASTM G51) Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were 
performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field exploration. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-22.
