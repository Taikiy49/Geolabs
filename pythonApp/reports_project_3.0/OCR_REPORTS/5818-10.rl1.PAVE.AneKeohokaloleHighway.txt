Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers
an area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of
the following five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the
site appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe type flows which spread and ponded as
they approached the ocean A a lava typically is characterized by a porous rough and
irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including
cobbles and boulders Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy
surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are encountered commonly in basaltic lavas especially pahoehoe type
lava Cavities form when lavas are still in a molten state and they represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes form
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow Blisters and pockets
generally are smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is
transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow
contacts cavities and along clinker layers Clinker and cavities serve as the primary
water transmission features because of their high permeability

Site Description
The general location and vicinity of the project limits are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1 The new project will stretch from Palani Road to Hina Lani
Street as shown on the General Site Plan Plate 2
A section of the Ane Keohokalole Highway was constructed previously from
about Puohulihuli Street to the water reservoir access driveway on the northern side of
Kealakehe Parkway Phase 6 30 The asphaltic concrete pavement
in this portion of the Ane Keohokalole Highway is generally in good condition
The remaining areas of the highway alignment are generally open undeveloped
land The ground surface is irregular consisting of a a and pahoehoe basalt lavas
Based on our site reconnaissance the majority of the ground surface from about
of Phase 1A to Hina Lani Street consist of a a basalt The majority of the
other areas of the project generally consist of dense pahoehoe basalt formation at the
ground surface

are present along the alignment typical of the basalt lava flows in this area Cavities
and lava tube features are encountered commonly in the basalt formation and should be
expected during the project construction
Vegetation at the project site varies along the project alignment In some areas
the vegetation is sparse consisting of scattered wild grasses In other areas thick
shrubs and small trees are present We also understand many archaeological features
are present along some areas of the highway alignment
Based on the topographic survey map provided the project site generally slopes

ground surface elevations range from about 250 to 400 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
along the Ane Keohokalole Highway alignment The existing ground surface elevations
along Palani Road range from about 180 to 290 feet MSL

Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling five borings designated
as Boring Nos 1 through 5 extending to depths of about 18 5 to 21 5 feet below the

Subgrade Preparation Below Pavement Section
We anticipate the majority of the new roadway pavements will be constructed on
compacted fill or in a cut condition In general the area within the contract grading limits
should be cleared and grubbed thoroughly at the on set of earthwork Vegetation
debris deleterious materials and other unsuitable materials should be removed and
disposed properly off site or disposed in a designated area to reduce the potential for
contamination of the excavated materials
In general we recommend the fill areas of the project site be ripped to a depth of
about 3 feet below the existing ground surface at the commencement of grading The
purpose of ripping the fill subgrades is to assist in detecting and collapsing cavities
and or voids contained in the porous near surface materials encountered at the project
site As a minimum ripping of the ground surface should extend at least 3 feet laterally
beyond the fill areas cut fill transition areas
After the fill surfaces have been ripped to a depth of about 3 feet below the
ground surface the ground should be leveled with the use of a Caterpillar D 10
bulldozer or similar size bulldozer working the surface a minimum of four passes to
provide a relatively level surface After leveling of the rugged surfaces with a bulldozer
we recommend proof rolling the fill subgrades with a large vibratory drum roller
minimum 20 tons static weight for a minimum of eight passes to help detect and
collapse near surface cavities and or voids The vibratory drum roller should be
operated at a speed of about 300 feet per minute Yielding areas loose areas or
cavities disclosed during the clearing and proof rolling operations should be
over excavated and backfilled with compacted fill materials The depth of
over excavation should be extended until dense underlying materials are exposed and
should be evaluated in the field by a Geolabs representative or a qualified geotechnical
engineer
As for the finished subgrades in cut areas the future pavement areas should be
excavated where necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the
aggregate subbase course layer The subgrade under the pavement areas should be

Fill Materials
In general we recommend capping the fill areas of the project site with a
minimum 2 foot thick layer of structural fill materials Structural fill materials should
consist of well graded granular materials generally less than 6 inches in maximum
dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted
mass In addition structural fill materials should contain less than 30 percent particles
passing the No 200 sieve by weight
Fill and backfill materials below the minimum 2 foot thick structural fill layer may
consist of general fill materials General fill and backfill materials should consist of
well graded granular materials the majority of which are less than 12 inches with an
absolute maximum dimension of 18 inches Materials between 12 and 18 inches in
particle size should be limited to about 15 percent or less of the total volume of general
fill materials
In general the excavated on site materials may be reused as a source of
structural and or general fill materials if they are processed to meet the maximum
particle size and gradation requirements presented above If the excavated materials do
not contain sufficient fines to produce a well graded material off site borrow or on site
rock crushing of larger rock fragments or boulders should be considered to provide the
required gradation and particle sizes to develop a well graded material
Imported fill materials if required should be free of organics and deleterious
materials and should be suitable for the intended use based on the recommendations
for fill materials provided herein

Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills should
be placed in level lifts of about 18 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as
a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with AASHTO T 180 ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction should
be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable
compaction equipment
Compaction equipment such as a Caterpillar D 10 bulldozer or similar heavy
compaction equipment providing suitable energy to achieve the required compaction
should be utilized The number of passes usually six to eight required to achieve
proper compaction should be determined at the on set of site grading by the
performance of a trial compaction test or by plate bearing tests Subsequent layers of
rock fill above the previously placed rock fill should provide a choking layer of
smaller sized boulder materials to fill in the voids between the boulders of the preceding
rock fill layer
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of
rocks boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program should be conducted at the
start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a qualified
geotechnical engineer to evaluate the number of passes needed to achieve the desired

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore special attention
should be given to the surface and subsurface drainage of the pavements As a
minimum the pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures
Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction
