PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the
subsurface conditions at the site to develop a generalized soils data
set for the formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations
pertinent to the design of the foundations and earthwork of the
proposed addition to the existing Hawaii State Public Library
facilities
Our scope of work included but was not limited to the following
efforts
1 Coordination logging and supervision of the drilling and
sampling of six 6 borings at the project site
2 Laboratory testing of selected samples obtained during the
field exploration to aid in classifying the materials
encountered and to evaluate their physical characteristics
3 Analyses of the field and laboratory data to formulate
geotechnical engineering recommendations pertinent to the
subject project
4 Preparation of this report summarizing our work on the project
and presenting our findings and recommendations

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a three story addition with basement to
the rear or mauka side of the existing Hawaii State Public Library
This proposed addition will be constructed of concrete and will be
designed to architecturally match the existing library building
It is anticipated that the column and line footing loads of the
proposed addition will be moderate to heavy Spread footing
foundations are planned in the current scheme
The proposed addition will include a basement level and three 3
buried utility mechanical vaults The proposed basement finish floor
elevation will be about seven 7 feet below the finished exterior
grade and the finish floor elevation of the vaults will be about eleven
11 feet below finish grade The exterior walls of these
substructures will function as retaining walls
Included in the project are renovations to the existing library
building These renovations involve strengthening and reinforcement of
portions of the building and most likely will require the installation
of new columns and footings
Also of consideration but beyond our scope of work are possible
plans to construct a mass transit station and or transit line
immediately adjacent to the mauka end of the proposed addition This
transit facility may involve underground construction or structures
which could potentially impact the library addition

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Oahu was built up during the late Pliocene and early
Pleistocene by the activities of two basaltic shield volcanoes
Waianae and Koolau Koolau is the younger of the two and reached the
end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago The
project site is located along the western flank of Koolau
As the main volcanic activity on the island of Oahu drew toa
close the island began to subside slowly While the island was
sinking fringing coral algal reefs began to grow on the seaward
margins of the island

During the Pleistocene or Ice Age global sea level rose and fell
in response to the great continental glaciers As the glaciers grew
less water was available to fill the oceanic basins and sea levels fell
below the present level As the glaciers melted and retreated more
water became available and sea levels rose to heights above the current
stand of the sea
During the higher stands of the sea coral algal reefs grew at
elevations above the present sea level These reefs became exposed
when sea level fell At the project site our borings encountered a
coral algal formation which is believed to have grown during the Kaena
95 feet MSI stand of the sea and to have been eroded to its current
elevation during the Waimanalo 25 feet MSL stand of the sea
Subsequent to the growth of this coral algal formation eruptions
of the Tantalus Roundtop and or Punchbowl members of the Honolulu
Volcanic Series mantled the area of the project site with deposits of
volcanic cinder and ash
During the development and urbanization of the area man made fills
were placed to raise grades and level the area

Soil Conditions
In general the drilled borings encountered a surficial fill layer
varying in thickness from 1 5 to 4 5 feet below the existing ground
surface This fill consists of medium stiff to very stiff brown clayey
silts and sandy silts with in situ moisture contents ranging between 10
and 20 percent of dry weight
Below this surface fill our borings encountered loose to medium
dense brown to black cinder sands with in situ moisture contents of
generally less than 10 percent of the dry weight of the soil These
sands ranged from 4 5 to 15 5 feet in thickness and are volcanic ash
and cinder deposits of the Honolulu Volcanic Series
Underlying the volcanic sands our borings encountered coral algal
reef formation and related detrital deposits to the maximum depths
drilled of 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface The upper 6 0
to 9 5 feet of this coral formation consisted of a relatively hard and
competent ledge of reef material which is underlain by dense white to
tan detrital sands with gravel
Groundwater was encountered in Boring Nos 1 through 4 at depths
ranging between 15 4 and 15 9 feet below the existing ground surface
No significant fluctuations in the groundwater levels were noted during

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based upon our field exploration laboratory testing and
engineering analyses it is our opinion that the proposed addition and
renovation project is feasible from a geotechnical engineering
point of view Conventional spread footing foundations may be utilized
provided that the recommendations presented herein are followed
The bottom of all footing excavations should be observed by the
geotechnical engineer prior to the placement of the reinforcement to
determine if adequate bearing has been obtained and that loose soils
are removed prior to concrete placement
It is our opinion that for footings designed and constructed in
accordance with the recommendations presented herein the potential
total and differential settlements should be less than one half inch
Foundation Support for Addition
In order to provide uniform support for the foundations of the
proposed addition we recommend that the foundations bear on in situ
coral formation

For the design of spread or wall footings an allowable bearing
capacity of 6 000 p s f may be utilized for footings bearing on
in situ hard coral formation This value may be increased by one third
when temporary wind and seismic loads are considered Footings should
be embedded a minimum of two 2 feet below the lowest adjacent grade
unless deeper embedment is required to reach the coral formation
If any soft or weak areas are encountered at the bottom of the
footing excavation they should be remedied by over excavation of the
soft and or weak materials to competent material and backfilling of the
resultant void with lean concrete
It is our opinion that for footings designed and constructed in
accordance with the recommendations presented herein the potential
total and differential settlements should be less than one half inch
Cavity Probing and Grouting
Solution cavities and other voids are commonly encountered in
coral algal formations To reduce the possibility of foundation
settlements resulting from the collapse of such voids we recommend
that a program of probing and grouting the footing locations be
implemented as part of the project
The probes should be drilled at each column location and on
10 foot centers for wall footings The probe holes should be a minimum
of three 3 inches in diameter and should extend a minimum of 8 feet
below the footing bottom The holes should be backfilled with
sand cement grout injected at low pressures
If a cavity or void is suspected at a footing location additional
probes should be drilled to attempt to delineate the extent of the
suspected void
GEOLABS HAWAII

Dk 501
In order to reduce mobilization and drilling costs it is
suggested that the probing and grouting work be performed after site
clearing but prior to the commencement of excavation work
The probing and grouting operations should be observed by the
geotechnical engineer to see that the above recommendations are
properly implemented and to evaluate other solutions to backfill
potential voids
Excavation
Based on our exploration it is our opinion that conventional
methods may be used to excavate the basement and vault areas If
properly designed open cut construction would likely be the most
economical method Based on our exploration a temporary cut slope of
1 5 horizontal to 1 vertical may be utilized except for where the
excavation will be bounded by the existing basement wall which may be
exposed vertically
Previous experiences with excavations in cinder sand indicate that
it may be possible to make an open cut excavation with near vertical
sidewalls However the boring data and other previous experiences
with cinder sand indicate that while such excavations will frequently
stand unsupported for a short period of time there is a tendency for
excavation sidewalls to unravel when exposed Therefore it is
recommended that the excavation sidewalls be stabilized or sloped back
to reduce the potential for sloughing or collapse
The actual design of shoring and or bracing should be left to the
contractor However it should be noted that there is a coral ledge at
a depth of less than 10 feet in the project area Conventional
sheetpiles will not be able to penetrate this ledge sufficiently to
provide an adequate toe in at the base Therefore soldier piles and
lagging or a tie back system may be required

Consideration should also be given to stabilization by pregrouting
or by the application of a binder to the sidewall faces immediately
after excavation Pregrouting of the excavation line with a weak
Portland Cement or chemical grout could cement or bind the sand soils
into a sufficiently competent mass to stand temporarily as vertical
sidewalls This work could be performed concurrently with the
foundation probing and grouting work recommended above
It should also be noted that if groundwater levels should rise to
within the vertical limits of the excavation during construction it is
our opinion that due to the nature of the formations encountered
there may be difficulties in dewatering the excavation
Retaining Walls
The basement and utility mechanical vault walls will function as
retaining walls as well as bearing walls The guidelines for design of
retaining walls are as follows
1 For lateral earth pressures the following values may be used
Walls unrestrained at the top 35 p s f equivalent
active condition level fluid pressure
backfill per foot of depth
Walls restrained at the top 50 p s f equivalent
at rest condition fluid pressure
level backfill per foot of depth
2 An allowable bearing capacity of up to 6 000 p s f is
recommended for foundations bearing on hard in situ coral
formation
3 A passive earth resistance of 300 p s f per foot of depth
may be used for the wall footing on level areas
4 A base friction factor of 0 4 may also be used to determine
the resistance of the wall against sliding

5 The wall foundations on relatively flat areas should be

6 A subsurface drainage system subdrains and or weepholes

Granular backfill material is recommended behind the retaining
walls to minimize excessive compaction required
New Foundations for Interior Renovations
New columns are planned within the front portion of the existing
library structure to help support the second floor beams Light
structural loads are anticipated
Boring Nos 5 and 6 which were drilled outside of the building
indicate that this portion of the building is underlain by medium stiff
fill and cinder sand Coral was encountered at depths of about 8 to 9
feet below the outside ground surface
For the light loads anticipated the new footings may bear on the
existing fill layer The bottom of the footing excavation should be
compacted to a minimum of 90 of ASTM D 1557 maximum dry density An
allowable bearing value of 2 500 p s f may be utilized
Slabs On Grade
It is anticipated that the floors of the proposed addition and
vaults will be concrete slabs on grade The slabs and their cushions
may be supported by the cinder sand provided the subgrade is first
scarified to a depth of ten 10 inches moisture conditioned to
near optimum moisture content and recompacted to 90 relative dry
density as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 A 4 inch layer of
3B Fine gravel should be provided below the slabs as cushion fill

In order to prevent future moisture infiltration and subsequent
damage to the floor coverings an impervious moisture membrane may be
considered below the slabs
Site Grading
_ Site grading for the proposed addition will be predominantly
excavation for the basement and vaults along with the placement of
related backfills However it is assumed that some minor amounts of
external grading work will be required
Prior to the commencement of any earthwork all areas within the
grading limits should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious
materials The resulting grub and spoil material should be disposed of
properly off site or stockpiled for use in landscaping Any soft
weak or yielding areas disclosed during the clearing operations which
are not scheduled for structure excavation should be over excavated to
firm ground and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted engineered fill
Generally the on site soils should be suitable for use as
engineered fill or backfills If additional imported material is
required the material should conform to the requirements stipulated in
Section 30 of the Standard Specifications for Public Works
Construction Select Borrow for Subbase Course dated September 1986
All fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not
exceeding eight 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density as established by ASTM Test
Method D 1557 78

Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the geotechnical engineer be retained to
provide geotechnical engineering services during construction of the
probing and grouting excavation and foundation phases of the work
This is to observe compliance with the design concepts specifications
or recommendations and to allow design changes in the event that
subsurface conditions differ from that anticipated prior to start of
construction The recommendations given in this report are contingent
upon such observations
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and
approved by the geotechnical engineer prior to hauling to the site
If actual exposed rock soil conditions encountered during
construction are different from those assumed or considered in this
report then appropriate modifications to the design should be made

Moisture content determinations were performed on selected samples as
an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The
results of these tests are presented on the Boring Logs at the appropriate
sample depths
Four 4 sieve analysis tests were performed on selected samples to
evaluate the gradation characteristics The test results are presented on
Plates B 1 through B 4
Two 2 swell tests were performed on selected samples to aid in
evaluating the shrink swell potential of the materials sampled The
results of these tests are shown on Plate B 5
Two 2 unconfined compression tests were performed on selected core
samples of the coral formation The results of these tests are shown on
Plate B 6
One direct shear test was performed on a selected sample to aid in
evaluating the in situ strength of the material sampled The result of
this test is shown on Plate B 7
