Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger Koolau
Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms
the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano became extinct while
Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau
lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad
plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau

The Waianae Mountain Range is composed of layered basaltic lava flows and
pyroclastic materials which are grouped and classified as the Waianae Volcanic Series
The Waianae Volcanic Series is divided into the lower middle and upper volcanic
members The lower member comprises lava flows and associated pyroclastic rocks that
built the main mass of the Waianae Shield Volcano The middle member consists of rock
that accumulated and gradually filled the vast volcanic caldera The upper member is a
relatively thin capping layer that covered the entire top of the shield volcano late in its
history of evolution
Once the Waianae Shield Volcano formed a long period of deep erosion
sedimentation and subsidence of the Island of Oahu occurred which produced the large
valleys of the western side of the shield volcano These erosional valleys were gradually
filled with enormous accumulations of alluvium and colluvium deposited as a combined
result of stream erosion base level rise sea level rise and subsidence of the island
mass Over time and as a result of sea level fluctuation some of the older alluvial and
colluvial deposits were buried and through consolidation and cementation formed
relatively dense deposits However the current surface soil deposits above these older
deposits are typically found to be unconsolidated
Site Description
As previously mentioned the site is located along Paheehee Ridge in the Waianae
area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project site is located approximately 500 feet to
the north from the end of Kuwale Road The project site is generally sloping from north to
south Based on the topographic survey map the ground surface elevations range from
about 180 to 220 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at the proposed tank location
Subsurface Conditions
We anticipate that the subsurface conditions at the current site will be similar to
subsurface conditions encountered at the previous site Based on the boring drilled for our
previous study located north of the current site we anticipate that the site is generally
underlain by colluvium and conglomerate deposits over basalt formation at greater depths
The colluvium consists of cobbles and boulders in a sandy silt matrix from the ground
surface extending to a depth of about 11 feet below the ground level Below the colluvium
layer conglomerate consisting of cobbles and boulders in a cemented soil matrix was
encountered from a depth of about 11 feet extending to about 26 5 feet below the ground
surface It should be noted that the cementation of the conglomerate ranged from weak to
moderate The conglomerate was underlain by another deposit of colluvium consisting of
loose silty sands with some cobbles unconsolidated Basalt formation was encountered
below the second colluvium layer from about 52 feet below the ground level extending to
the maximum depth explored of about 121 5 feet below the ground surface The basalt
formation encountered was generally slightly to highly weathered and ranged from
medium hard to very hard

generally feasible from a geotechnical engineering point of view However it should be

Fill
As previously mentioned significant amount of fill would be required to provide a
level pad for the reservoir tank structure We envision that the thickness of the fill would
range between 20 and 45 feet thick at the center and south side of the reservoir tank
structure respectively Based on our review of the boring log the colluvium and
conglomerate within the upper 25 feet contain boulders and cobbles The excavated
on site soils containing boulders and larger sized cobbles should not be used as fill
material since the fill may not be uniformly compacted resulting in future ground
subsidence
The fill material should consist of imported structural fill The structural fill material
should consist of well graded granular material with maximum particle size less than
3 inches in largest dimension The on site soils could be used as structural fill provided
that the boulders cobbles and particles greater than 3 inches are removed or crushed
and the on site soils are processed into a well graded material

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on available subsurface information it appears that the cut slopes at the site
would expose the colluvial soil deposits conglomerate and basalt formation Cut slopes
exposing the well cemented conglomerate and basalt formation may be designed based
on a slope inclination of one half horizontal to one vertical or flatter However
cut slopes exposing the unconsolidated soil deposits such as the loose to medium dense
colluvium and the weakly cemented conglomerate should be designed based on a slope
inclination of 2H 1V or flatter
Since majority of the site will require fill we anticipate that cuts into the hillside
would be significantly less than cuts required at the previous reservoir tank site located
north of the current site Based on the limited field exploration conducted previously we
believe that cut slopes would expose the colluvium and the conglomerate with varying
cementation
We recommend conducting additional borings during the design phase of the
project to further delineate the extent of the loose colluvium and weakly cemented
conglomerate that would require a flatter slope inclination
Permanent fill slopes constructed of structural fill may be designed with a maximum
slope inclination of 2H 1V All fills placed on existing slopes with inclinations steeper than
5H 1V should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of the new
fill against sliding
Rockfall Protection
In general structures situated below a hillside slope have an inherent potential
hazard from boulders rolling down the hillside Because the natural slopes at the site are
generally flatter than 1H 1V the boulders tend to roll along the ground surface and most of
the boulders are stopped by trees other boulders or come to rest on flatter areas
Man made structures such as fences or ditches may be installed to stop the occasional
boulders that may continue down slope
It should be noted that steep rock cuts generally increase the hazard from rockfall
Pieces of rock formation may break loose from the cut slope face and may roll or bounce

downslope depending on the steepness of the slope Extensive research by the U S
Department of Transportation Federal Highway Administration FHWA indicates that
boulders tend to roll down slopes flatter than 1H 1V bounce on the slope face for ratios of
about to 1H 1V and fall straight to the bottom for steeper slopes
Since the project site is adjacent to an existing hillside the potential for rockfall
hazard at the site is a significant design issue for the project Rockfall in the colluvium and
weakly cemented conglomerate is also more substantial due to the potential for erosion of
the weakly cemented soil matrix and loosening of the boulders over time Several
measures may be considered to reduce the potential hazard from rockfall at the project
site
1
In general the finished slope face should be carefully excavated to avoid
loosening of the intact rock and undercutting portions of the slope which
may create future instability Loose pieces of rock should be removed from
the slope as excavation of the cut slope progresses If the contractor is
unable to make a relatively smooth cut surface the use of chemical
expanders installed in predrilled holes may be considered
2
Mid slope benches for cut slopes should be avoided as recent research by
FHWA has indicated that the benches tend to accumulate debris and form
launching ramps for falling rocks which increase the hazard for potential
rockfall
3
A rockfall catchment ditch should be provided at the toe of the cut slope to
collect falling rocks and debris Based on guidelines from FHWA the
catchment ditch should be about 12 to 15 feet wide for a slope height of
about 20 to 40 feet
4
Structures should be set back from the slope as much as practicable to
provide for an additional safety zone
5
Lined surface water interceptor ditches should be constructed along the tops
of the cut slopes The lined interceptor ditches should be designed to
intercept and divert surface water runoff to appropriate drainage areas thus
reducing the amount of surface water that could percolate into the ground
and discharge as seepage from the cut slope face

Ground settlements are anticipated when new fills are placed over the existing
ground to raise the site to the finished elevations Based on the results of our previous
exploration the anticipated ground settlements are primarily the result of the following
two processes
compression of the fill material under its own weight
compression of the in situ soils induced by the new fill loads
Based on our analyses settlement from compression of the fill material under its
own weight and settlement of the in situ residual and saprolitic soils induced by the new fill
loads are on the order of about 6 inches Due to the significant amount of settlement
anticipated we recommend implementing a settlement monitoring program to evaluate the
magnitude and rate of the ground settlements prior to construction of the reservoir tank
structure
Reservoir Foundations
As previously mentioned we understand that the proposed 200 foot diameter
reservoir tank will be situated on fill about 20 to 45 feet thick Cuts will be required at the
north side of the site therefore the reservoir tank structure would bear on differing
subgrade materials consisting of engineered fill on the south and central sides and
colluvium on the north side Excessive differential settlement will likely occur if the
reservoir tank structure is bearing on differing subgrade materials
We envision that the reservoir tank structure will likely be supported on a concrete
floor slab with a thickened edge perimeter footing If the reservoir tank structure bears on
differing subgrade materials the reservoir tank structure could experience structural
distress due to excessive differential settlement To reduce the potential for structural
distress on the reservoir tank structure we recommend over excavating the reservoir
tank subgrade in cut areas to a minimum depth of about 10 feet In addition
over excavation should include areas where the fill thickness is less than 10 feet The
over excavated areas should be backfilled with structural fill material in order to reduce
the potential for excessive differential settlement and to provide a uniform bearing
surface for the reservoir tank structure Based on our preliminary foundation settlement
analyses total settlement on the order of about 1¾ to 2 inches is anticipated with
differential settlements on the order of about ¾ to 1 inch Due to the limited field
exploration conducted at this time we recommend drilling additional borings to further
evaluate the subsurface materials across the footprint of the proposed reservoir tank
location and to conduct a more thorough foundation settlement analyses
Settlement of the reservoir tank structure can be reduced by the use of deep
foundations However the cost of deep foundations will be much higher than footing or
mat foundations Driven precast prestressed concrete piles would not be an efficient
foundation system due to the need to pre drill through the compacted fill layer prior to
driving the piles Also the cobbles and boulders in the natural conglomerate layer would
limit the penetration of the piles and corresponding pile capacity Drilled shafts would be
