SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered fills placed over weathered coral and 
coral formation, coralline detritus, and alluvium at greater depths. We did not encounter 
groundwater in the borings drilled at the time of our field exploration. 
The coral formation encountered is soft, highly weathered, and breaks down 
readily into coralline sands and gravel. It should be noted that the strength characteristics 
of coral formation are highly variable. In addition, the depositional nature of the coral 
formation may create localized areas of hard, crystalline coral formation intermixed with 
soft, severely fractured detritus (debris). Our field exploration did not encounter large 
cavities or voids in the borings drilled; however, cavities are commonly encountered in 
the coral formations in the project vicinity. 
Based on our field exploration, we recommend utilizing shallow spread and/or 
continuous footings for foundation support of the new outpatient clinic. An allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used in the design of foundations bearing on 
the recompacted on-site sandy soils or new fills placed to achieve the design finished 
grades. 
As indicated previously, cavities and/or voids are commonly encountered within 
the coral formations in this portion of the Island of Oahu. To reduce the potential for loss 
of foundation support resulting from collapse of cavities below the foundations, we 
recommend conducting a program of cavity probing and grouting below the foundations 
of the structures. In addition, the excavated subgrades below future slabs and pavement 
structures should be proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller a minimum 
of eight passes to detect the presence of near-surface cavities or loose zones. 
The development site is underlain by interbedded coral reef formation and weakly 
cemented reef detritus at or near the existing ground surface. We envision that these 
coralline deposits may be excavated with heavy excavation equipment, such as a large 
backhoe excavator. However, coral formations typically contain localized hard and 
crystallized zones. Therefore, it is possible that the excavation for a portion of deep utility 
line trenches may require the use of hoerams or chipping.  
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
geotechnical recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed in support of the design of the proposed Department of Veterans Affairs Multi-
Specialty Outpatient Clinic design-build project located in the Kalaeloa area on the Island 
of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general site vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1.  
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses. These 
recommendations are intended for the design of building foundations, slabs-on-grade, 
retaining structures, site grading, pavements, underground utility lines, and underground 
detention/infiltration system only. The findings and recommendations presented herein 
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
The proposed design-build project site is located south of the intersection of 
Kamokila Boulevard and Roosevelt Avenue within the former Naval Air Station Barbers 
Point. The project consists of designing and constructing an approximate 90,000-square 
foot, one-story building structure for a Department of Veterans Affairs Multi-Specialty 
Outpatient Clinic. The project also will include a large parking lot consisting of over 
520 parking spaces, and the project also will entail supporting infrastructure, such as 
underground water, sewer, drainage, and electrical utilities to serve the development. In 
addition, we anticipate a stormwater runoff disposal system would be needed to meet the 
drainage requirements for Low Impact Development (LID). 
Based on the existing topography of the site and the adjacent roadways, we 
anticipate grading would be minimal consisting of minor cuts and fills of less than 2 feet 
to achieve the final design grades.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
Of interest to our geotechnical analysis for foundation design of the new outpatient 
clinic are the subsurface materials encountered at the project site, the engineering 
properties of the materials encountered, and the variability of the subsurface conditions 
across the project site. Therefore, the following subsections provide a description of the 
geologic setting of the project site, the surface and subsurface conditions encountered at 
the site, and a discussion on the items needed for seismic design, such as soil 
liquefaction, soil profile for the elastic response spectrum, etc. 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. 
The project site is situated on the Ewa Coastal Plain, which extends seaward off 
the southeastern flank of Waianae Volcano and the southwestern flank of Koolau 
Volcano. This plain was formed by the accumulation of a thick wedge of sediments and 
coral reef formations on the flanks of the two volcanoes.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous 
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous 
reef materials.

2.4 
Seismic Design Considerations 
It should be noted that the City and County of Honolulu adopted the 2012 Edition 
of the International Building Code (IBC 2012) with the City and County of Honolulu 
amendments in November 2020. Based on the IBC 2012, the project site would be 
subjected to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be 
addressed, as well as evaluating the potential for soil liquefaction. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to volcanic 
activity; therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during 
volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The 
Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are 
so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some 
of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate 
damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes 
on the Island of Hawaii. Because the majority of earthquakes in Hawaii (over 
90 percent) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree 
of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii.

2.4.2 Liquefaction Potential 
Based on IBC 2012, the project site may be subjected to seismic activity, and the 
potential for soil liquefaction at the project site will need to be evaluated. Soil 
liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near the 
ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of excess 
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by 
earthquakes.  
In this process, when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration 
(such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume 
causing an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is unable to occur rapidly 
enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective stress 
(internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore water 
pressure build-up could equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing the 
soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During

2.4.3 Soil Profile Type for Seismic Design 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, the 
geology in the area, and our engineering analyses, the potential for soil liquefaction 
at the project site is non-existent due to the presence of medium dense soil 
underlain by medium dense to very dense geo-materials and the absence of 
groundwater table within the upper 50 feet of the soil profile. Therefore, the 
potential for liquefaction is not a design consideration at this project site. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, we 
believe the project site may be classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as 
being a “Very Dense Soil and Soft Rock” site corresponding to a Site Class “C” soil 
profile in accordance with Chapter 20 of the American Society of Civil Engineers 
(ASCE) Minimum Design Loads for Buildings and Other Structures, 2010 Edition 
(ASCE 7-10).

Building Foundations 
We understand the finished floor elevation for the new outpatient clinic is set at 
+61.75 feet MSL. Based on the available topographic survey of the site, the existing 
ground surface generally ranges between about +58 and +64 feet MSL. Therefore, we 
anticipate cuts of up to about 2 feet and fills of up to about 4 feet will be required to bring

foundation system bearing on the re-compacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills.

spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the proposed structure. An allowable 
bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design

fills needed to achieve the finished grades. This bearing value is for supporting dead-
plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those 
caused by wind or seismic forces. In general, footings should be embedded a minimum 
of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades.

SECTION 4.  LIMITATIONS 
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from the borings drilled and bulk samples collected. Variations of the 
subsurface conditions between and beyond the field data points may occur, and the 
nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until construction is 
underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the 
recommendations provided in this report. 
The field boring locations are approximate, having been staked out in the field 
using a hand-held Global Positioning System (GPS) device. Elevations of the borings 
were interpolated based on the contour lines shown on the Development of Kalaeloa 
Parcel 1, Backbone Roadway Improvements plans dated May 4, 2020 prepared by 
R.M. Towill Corporation. The physical locations and estimated elevations should be 
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between the soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote 
a gradual transition. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings drilled at the time 
of our field exploration. It should be noted that groundwater levels may vary depending 
on rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and 
sampling seventeen exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 17, 
extending to depths of about 5 to 32.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings 
were drilled with truck-mounted and track-mounted drill rigs equipped with 4-inch OD 
solid-stem augers and coring tools. The borings were drilled at the approximate locations 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend (Plate A-0.1). 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Rock materials were described in general 
accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
(Plate A-0.3). The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested 
Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the 
International Society for Rock Mechanics (April 1977). Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-17. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance 
with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch 
OD standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In 
addition, some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance 
with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD modified 
California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the harder and/or rock materials encountered at the project site 
were obtained by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with 
ASTM D2113, Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling 
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation or harder materials. The rock

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting any 
drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. For example, if 2.5 feet of 
sound material is recovered from a 5.0 foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent 
and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is 
used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of 
Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture content (ASTM D2216) and unit weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate

Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil

results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and 
graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Seven Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & ASTM C136) were performed on 
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in 
soil classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on

Plate B-4.

evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-5 and B-6.

performed on a selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the

Three Uniaxial Compressive Strength tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were

One Consolidation test with time rates (ASTM D2435) was performed on a sample 
of the potentially compressible soil to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the 
materials encountered. Results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate B-9. 
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-10 through B-12. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557 Methods A and B) were 
performed on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and 
moisture content relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-13 and B-14.

Field Infiltration Tests 
Four field infiltration tests were conducted within four borehole locations, 
Boring Nos. 10 through 13, at the site to evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the 
subsurface materials encountered. The approximate field infiltration test locations are 
shown on the Site Plan (Plate 2). These tests were performed in general accordance with 
the procedures in Appendix D of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment 
“Stormwater Design Manual, Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009). These procedures are 
consistent with other state’s procedures and may generally be considered an industry 
standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by drilling and sampling boreholes to a 
selected test depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Upon reaching the 
test depth, a 4-inch diameter PVC solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled 
borehole to allow infiltration only through the soil exposed at the bottom of the borehole. 
Falling head infiltration tests were performed to determine the infiltration rate of the 
underlying subsurface materials. Each test consisted of four trials of filling the casing with 
24 inches of water and taking periodic readings until the water drained out or up to one 
hour. The test results are presented on Plates C-1 through C-4.
