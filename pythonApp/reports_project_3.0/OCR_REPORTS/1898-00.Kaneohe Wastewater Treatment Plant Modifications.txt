INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed modifications of the existing
Kaneohe Wastewater Treatment Plant
The general location of the project site and its vicinity is shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of this exploration was to explore the subsurface soil
and groundwater conditions at the site to provide soil data for use in
development of recommendations for the foundation design of the proposed
modifications to the Kaneohe Wastewater Treatment Plant
The following items were of particular concern during the
exploration
1 Presence of soft ground conditions which would consolidate under
the weight of the new building loads or new fill if any
2 Depth of competent bearing stratum for pile foundations if
necessary
3 Presence of high groundwater level which would affect dewatering
and shoring for excavation
The scope of our exploration was in general accordance with our
revised proposal dated March 7 1985 and included field reconnaissance
drilling and sampling of fifteen 15 borings laboratory soil testing
engineering analyses utilizing the field and laboratory data and report
preparation

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located within the existing 15 acre Kaneohe
Wastewater Treatment Plant south of Kaneohe Bay Drive in Kaneohe on the
island of Oahu Hawaii The treatment plant also lies to the west of
Waikulana Fishpond which opens to Kaneohe Bay
The treatment plant consists of a variety of existing structures
including sewage pump stations screening and grit chambers primary and
final clarifiers trickling filters sludge pump houses primary and
secondary digestors digestor control building chlorination building
contact tank effluent pump station sludge drying beds laboratory and
storage building and caretaker s cottage The existing 16 inch diameter
Ahuimanu Force Main and 36 inch diameter Kaneohe Stream Interceptor Sewer
are connected to the plant
It is anticipated that the sludge drying beds will be demolished to
rovide space for the proposed modifications
Except for a higher mound of fill at the central portion of the site
the topography at the plant site is generally flat varying from about 4
feet above Mean Sea Level MSL at the property lines to about 6 feet
near the interior of the site The higher fill below the clarifiers and
trickling filters covers an area of about 250 by 400 feet with ground
elevation as high as 13 feet

the existing sludge drying beds is presently covered with dense

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed Kaneohe Wastewater Treatment Plant modifications consist
primarily of four new structures three new diversion sewer lines and a
new asphaltic concrete driveway
The proposed structures include
1 An influent lift station with a floor bottom about 15 to 20 feet
below the existing ground and below groundwater level
2 A mechanical bar screen and grit chamber complex with a floor
bottom about 3 to 10 feet below the existing ground and partially
below groundwater level
3 A grit handling building approximately on the existing grade
4 A new junction box with a floor bottom about 11 feet below the
existing ground and below groundwater level
The proposed structures will be located to the north of the existing
final clarifiers and higher fill area
The three proposed diversion lines include
1 A 36 inch diameter Kawa Diversion line
2 A 16 inch diameter Ahuimanu FM Diversion line
3 A 27 inch diameter Kaneohe Bay South Diversion line

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The project site is located on the Mokapu Peninsula of the island of
Oahu The foundation of this peninsula was deposited during the Kahipa
low stand of the sea 300 feet by several vents of the Honolulu
Volcanic Series These vents laid down extensive deposits of pyroclastic
materials in the form of ash and cinder with some associated lava flows
Since the deposition of these materials during Kahipa time the sea
level has risen and fallen several times as a result of glacio eustatic
fluctuations
These changes in sea level have caused the deposition of alluviun
boulders and lagoonal deposits over portions of the peninsula
During the past century the efforts of man have shaped the land to
its present form by placement of fill in low areas
Subsoil Conditions
The subsurface soil conditions at the site were explored by drilling
and sampling fifteen 15 borings to depths of about 15 5 to 78 5 feet
below the existing ground surface
The borings were drilled at the originally proposed locations of the
new structures and diversion lines However subsequent to completion of
the field exploration the structures were modified and relocated
slightly while two of the new diversion lines were relocated south Asa
result some of the borings are not located exactly within the present
proposed structure sites and no borings are located along the two
relocated new diversion lines However it is our opinion that the
borings are still representative of the general subsurface conditions
below the structure
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 1898 00 Page 6
Dk No 39
The borings encountered a thin surface layer of fill underlain by a
thick layer of highly compressible lagoonal deposits overlying a weathered
boulder layer at the depth of about 63 feet below the existing ground
surface The boulder layer is underlain by stiff alluvial deposits
The surface fill layer generally consists of slightly to moderately
expansive clay with consistencies ranging from medium stiff to very stiff
The lagoonal deposits generally consist of soft to very soft silts
and clayey silts which have localized organic zones and are highly
compressible Based on our extensive laboratory consolidation testing it
appears that the lagoonal deposits may still be consolidating under the
influence of the existing high fill at the clarifier and filter tank
areas
The boulder layer encountered in our borings at the proposed
structure locations was about 7 feet thick and has a generally dense
consistency The alluvial sands and clays underlying the boulder layer
are generally medium dense or medium stiff
Groundwater Levels
The groundwater levels in the borings were monitored during our
exploration The highest groundwater level was encountered at about
elevation 1 6 feet MSL and the lowest level at about elevation 5 7
feet The low groundwater level readings lower than elevation 1 foot
are due to the low permeability of the subsoils which caused a long delay
time for equilibrium of the water level inside the drill holes
Therefore the low groundwater level readings in the borings do not
reflect the actual natural groundwater levels

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The borings encountered a thick layer of highly compressible subsoils
below the site It also appears that the compressible layer may still be
consolidating under the influence of the existing on site fill
Therefore ground settlement is a concern for the proposed construction
Based on our findings the proposed structures influent lift
station mechanical bar screen and grit chamber complex and grit handling
building should be supported by end bearing pile foundations bearing on
the weathered boulder layer to avoid excessive settlements However the
proposed new junction box may be supported by a mat foundation at the
present design finished floor elevation to support the relatively light
loads
Braced excavation is anticipated for both the construction of the new
structures and the installation of the new diversion lines in the very
soft lagoonal soils Deep sheetpile embedment below the excavation bottom
is recommended for the proposed structure construction A suitable
efficient dewatering system will also be required
Pile Foundation
It is our understanding that the proposed influent lift station and
grit chambers will be about 10 to 20 feet or between elevations 5 and
15 0 feet MSL below the existing ground surface and below groundwater
level Due to the soft and wet ground conditions anticipated a 2 foot
gravel cushion over a layer of filter fabric should be used as a working
platform below the design bottom elevation of each of these two
structures
A mat foundation had been considered to support these two proposed
structures however our analyses indicate that a heavy thick mat is
needed to offset the buoyant uplift forces on the submerged structure when
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1898 00 Page 8
Dk No 39
it is not holding sewage The weight of the structures including the
thick mat and influent loads is too heavy to be compensated by the
removed earth overburden resulting from excavation Estimated mat
settlements under the anticipated structure loads are as high as 1 foot
In addition our analyses indicate that more than 2 inches of additional
long term primary and secondary ground settlements may occur at the new
structure locations due to progressive consolidation of the underlying
soils
Shallow mat footing foundation had been considered to support the
proposed mechanical bar screen and grit handling structures However the
shallow foundations will exert additional bearing pressure on the
underlying soils and cause further ground settlement in addition to those
due to progressive consolidation of the underlying soils
As a result pile foundations are recommended to support the proposed
structures
Based on our findings we recommend that the proposed structure be
supported by pile foundations bearing on the underlying weathered boulder
layer Twelve inch square prestressed concrete piles are recommended
The piles should be driven to the boulder layer at about 45 to 50
feet below the lowest design bottom elevations of the proposed
structures Estimated total pile length below the existing ground surface
is about 65 to 70 feet
The piles would develop their vertical capacity primarily from
end bearing on the boulder layer The vertical uplift capacity of the
piles would be derived primarily from adhesion between the piles and the
lagoonal soils A design high groundwater table of 2 5 feet MSL may be
used for uplift stability check of the structures

The piles should be driven with a hammer delivering a minimm of
24 000 foot pounds of energy and to a resistance of about 6 blows per inch
for the last 2 inches or 70 blows per foot for the last foot
For the above recommendations the piles may be designed for a total
allowable vertical capacity of 60 tons per pile However due to the
ongoing consolidation of the soft subsoils downdrag loads as much as 30
tons per pile are estimated These loads should be deducted from the
allowable pile capacity Therefore a net allowable vertical compressive
load capacity of 30 tons per pile may be used for the design of the pile
foundations Pile settlements of less than one half of an inch are
anticipated The allowable vertical uplift capacity of the piles may be
taken as 0 4 ton per foot of the embedded pile length
Due to the soft ground conditions anticipated at the bottom of
excavation the piles would probably be driven from the existing grades
prior to excavation The piles should be carefully aligned during driving
to see that they are not offset from the design locations at the
excavation bottom
The pile driving should be monitored by a qualified geotechnical
engineer to see that the piles are driven to the required depth and that
sufficient end bearing and or adhesion have been developed
A test pile program should be undertaken to help evaluate and modify
if necessary the driving criteria and to more accurately determine the
production pile order lengths
To achieve these objectives we recommend that 10 percent of the
total piles be test driven at the site These test piles may later be
incorporated into the actual foundations

To resist the lateral loads on the structures passive earth pressure

Excavation Support
We recommend that excavation walls in the soft soils be supported by
sheetpiling It is important that adequate sheeting be installed
prior to excavation and maintained continuously tight against the
excavation walls with proper bracing during construction Properly
braced sheeting is essential to reduce lateral movements of the
ground adjacent to the excavation Such movement may cause
settlements and damages to the existing adjacent structures
The excavation should be provided with proper bracing in accordance
with the anticipated loading conditions construction traffic
materials stockpile and equipment and to satisfy applicable safety
requirements The design and safety of the support system s should
be the responsibility of the contractor The apparent earth pressure
diagram presented on Plate 3 may be used for preliminary design of
the excavation support system s as a guide for cost estimation
Due to the very soft subsoil conditions it is also important that
the sheeting should extend a sufficient depth below the excavation
bottom to reduce the possibility of bottom heave The required
sheetpile embedment below excavation bottom depends on the excavation
width Based on the anticipated variation in excavation width from
about 10 to 30 feet it is estimated that a minimm sheetpile
embedment of 15 feet below the excavation bottom should be used for
the wider influent lift station excavation and a minimm of 5 feet
sheetpile embedment below the excavation bottoms should be used for
the grit chamber and new junction box excavations
To minimize additional loads on the excavation walls and reduce
potential instability stockpiling of excavated material adjacent to
the excavation should be strictly avoided

Earth Pressure Loads on Pipe
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench the
size of the pipe the unit weight of backfill material and the
frictional resistance between the backfill material and the trench
wall To calculate the vertical loads on the pipe an average total
unit weight above water of 125 p c f for backfill material and a
coefficient of friction of 0 25 are recommended Earth forces upon
the pipe increase rapidly with the width of the trench and
therefore the width of the trench should be kept to a minimum
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great
effect upon the load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the
pipe bedding is an important design consideration
Based on the borings the anticipated pipe invert elevations lie
within soft to very soft lagoonal clayey silt Therefore a minimum
of twenty four inches 24 of compacted granular bedding material
should be placed below the pipe to provide uniform support The
granular bedding should extend a minimum of one fourth the outside
pipe diameter beyond the sides of the pipe and should extend halfway
up the sides of the pipe The bedding material should consist of
clean granular material such as 3B Fine gravel
Alternatively if a layer of geotextile fabric can be used to wrap
around the granular bedding material the minimum thickness of the
bedding material may be reduced to 18 inches

a ss i
Moisture content unit weight and Atterberg Limits determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are
presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Four 4 one inch ring swell tests were performed on natural and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the existing near
surface soils The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
Five 5 Atterberg Limit tests were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits The test results
are shown on Plates B 2 and B 3
Three 3 unconsolidated undrained triaxial tests were performed on
selected soil samples to evaluate their undrained shear strength
characteristics The test results are presented on Plates B 4 through
B 6
Seven 7 direct shear tests were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate their shear strength parameters The test results are
presented on Plates B 7 through B 13
Twelve 12 consolidation tests were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate their compressibility The test results are plotted
on Plates B 14 through B 25
