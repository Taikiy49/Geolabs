Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene Epoch in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in
age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island

Following the coalescence of the two volcanoes and after a long period of volcanic
inactivity during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield some
volcanic activity resumed with a series of basaltic lava flows followed by volcanic cinder
and tuff cone formations These more recent eruption series are referred to as the
Honolulu Volcanic Series The Honolulu Volcanic Series which began approximately
700 000 years ago MacDonald and Abbott 1970 produced several prominent cinder
and tuff cone structures with some basaltic lava flows that became inter layered with the
developing coastal plain deposits
Most of the southern Oahu coastal plain developed during the Pleistocene Epoch
when the sea level experienced significant fluctuations related to the continental glacial
stages As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the oceanic
basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the glaciers
melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose to
above the present sea level The processes of landform erosion and sediment reef
deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea level fluctuations
When the sea level was relatively lower the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and stream valleys were carved deeper into the island basaltic rock
the fringing coastal sediments and the offshore reef deposits Also during periods of
relatively lower sea level the sub aerial exposure of calcareous marine sediments caused
consolidation and cementation of the deposits to form hardened calcareous deposits In
contrast when the sea level was relatively higher the erosional base level was raised
such that marine sediments and coral reef formations filled the pre existing depressions
and accumulated as reef platforms at correspondingly higher elevations The higher sea
levels also caused the formation of deltas and alluvial fans composed of terrigenous
sediment in the head region of old bays
Finally the placement of man made fills associated with the development of the
urban area within the last 60 years has brought the southern Oahu coastal plain area to its
present form
The project site is situated near the mouth of Manoa Valley where the valley opens
onto the southeastern Oahu coastal plain The coastal plain formed along the eroded
southern flank of the Koolau Volcano which comprises the eastern portion of the Island of
Oahu Based on our research the Varsity Theatre project site is believed to mainly be
underlain by some man made fills and accumulations of soft alluvial soils which overlie
deeper and older calcareous marine sediments and some generally thin discontinuous
coralline limestone reef formations The buried marine deposits include layers of soft to
medium hard coralline limestone formation and various loose soft calcareous sediments
that are intercalated with other terrestrial sediments derived from inland erosion
Subsurface tunnel and cavern structures within the coralline limestone reef Karst
have been encountered and documented by previous exploration and construction

Existing Conditions
The project site is within the Moiliili area of the District of Honolulu on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii as shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The project site is bounded
by University Avenue toward the east Coyne Street toward the south and Kaialiu Street
toward the west The project site encompasses the existing oval shaped Varsity Theatre
building an adjacent circular shaped multi story commercial building and associated
ground level parking lot areas as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The project site is generally flat with existing ground surface elevations ranging on
the order of about 5 to 8 feet Mean Sea Level MSL The ground surfaces at the project
site are mostly paved or covered by the existing building footprints Observation of the
existing ground surfaces indicates no overt sign of ground distress such as cracking and
settlement deformation However based on our review of the available Varsity Theatre
foundation analysis report prepared by URS in November 2005 the theatre building has
experienced a long history of foundation distress evidenced by recurring cracks in the
walls and floors of the building Based on our review of the available soils data consisting
of previous boring and probe logs the foundation distress is believed to be caused by the
consolidation of the soft compressible alluvial soils which appear to extend to depths of
about 30 to 35 feet below the existing ground surface
Based on the available building construction plans provided for our review the
theatre walls utilize a pile foundation system for support Based on the test pile program
and construction plans the piles are believed to extend to depths of about 23 to 24 feet
below the ground surface It appears that the pile had targeted a relatively thin and
discontinuous medium hard coral layer encountered by the design test borings
by A H Hobart 1938 at depths of about 20 to 25 feet below the ground surface Other
lightly loaded portions of the theatre utilize near surface spread footings and slab on grade
construction We concur with the findings presented by URS and others that the
observed building distress is probably the result of subsurface soft soil consolidation pile
down drag and generally poor pile support on the targeted coralline bearing layer
The Varsity Theatre project site appears to be located in very close proximity to a
previously documented network of subterranean caves contained within shallow
subsurface coralline limestone strata The cave network is commonly referred to as the
Moiliili Caves or Moiliilli Karst The cave network is a naturally occurring dendritic
subterranean groundwater drainage system Karst that developed within the near surface
coralline reef limestone layer which is generally encountered near the mouth of Manoa
Valley in the University Moiliili area The approximate probable limits of the Moiliili Karst
system as mapped by other studies are shown on Plate 3 Because the project site is
within the previously mapped limits of the Moiliili Karst region it is desired to explore

Subsurface Conditions
Based on our review of available literature for the occurrence of karst caves in the
vicinity the Varsity Theatre project site was initially thought to be underlain by near surface
coralline reef limestone and various unconsolidated marine and terrestrial sediments The
coralline limestone was believed to be overlain by generally thin silty and sandy alluvial
soils and man made fills The coralline limestone in the project vicinity is known to contain
documented karst caverns the Moiliilli Caves connected by natural underground
drainages
Literature Research
The Moiliili Caves are a naturally developed karstic drainage system that formed
within the near surface coralline limestone materials residing within the University Moiliili
area The dendritic branching subterranean karst was formed by the dissolution of the
coralline limestone by fresh groundwater flowage The calcareous coralline limestone
materials are susceptible to slow dissolution erosion by percolating and flowing acidic
fresh groundwater
The coralline limestone containing the Moiliili karst features was deposited during
the Pleistocene Sangamon Interglacial Stage 25 feet Waimanalo Sea Level Stand
when the sea level was approximately 25 feet higher than the present day It has been
suggested that the karst feature may have developed preferentially along an abandoned
drainage path of the Manoa Stream The subterranean Moiliili karst encompasses the
highly urbanized area approximately centered about University Avenue extending from
about the H 1 Freeway in a southwesterly direction toward Kapiolani Boulevard as
generally shown on the Moiliili Karst Location Map Plate 3 It is postulated that the cave
system may extend all the way to the southern shoreline although it has not been
documented
A history of literature documentation by area residents and Board of Water Supply
personnel exists Documentation includes description of exploratory travel through the
underground tunnels that are partially filled with water Although many of the ground
surface features related to the buried karst topography have been covered over by
progressive urban development a few undisclosed entrances to the cave system remain
Previous exploratory observations performed by others indicate that some of the
interconnected tunnels have been partly filled with earth fill materials during development
of the surrounding area There are also reports of visible concrete pilings associated with
building construction and other underground support structures used for waterline
construction which breach and traverse portions of the caves The cave water quality has
been reported to generally be fresh in character and sustain aquatic life with little
discernable pollution from the surface infiltration Groundwater within the caves has been

Based on our review of available published reports pertaining to the Moiliili Caves it
appears that some of the larger branching network of conduits and passages range from
several feet wide up to 20 to 40 feet wide locally where caverns were encountered The
height of the passable conduits appears to range generally on the order of about 5 to
10 feet Estimates of the cave roof thicknesses are absent in the reviewed literature
however in some places the reported development of sinkholes at the ground surface
would indicate only limited overburden soil cover and cave roof thickness Based on our
review of available literature we anticipate that the overburden roof thickness composed
of thin surface soils and the underlying coralline limestone materials may range from
several feet to over 20 feet high
Based on our literature review at approximately 1 500 feet makai of the University
Avenue and King Street intersection a 1934 construction excavation encountered a large
cavern at depths between about 21 feet and 30 feet Mean Sea Level Construction
dewatering occurred continuously for four months and resulted in significant regional
up gradient ground deformations including additional sinkhole development settled broken
utilities and house foundations and the drainage of pre existing surface ponds Other
notable large cave breaches were reported just southeast of the South King University
Avenue intersection where extensive subsurface conduits were visible trending further
above South King Street
Based on our literature review detailed investigation including precise survey
mapping of the underground karst system has not been performed Therefore the exact
location of the conduits and passages are not well defined Some attempts have been
made by others to re create the subsurface trend of some larger passable conduits
Based on our review of the available drawings it appears that a portion of the primary
cave network may be in close proximity of the project site as shown on the Moiliili Karst
Location Map Plate 3 Details related to the potential location of other smaller voids and
conduits are not available

Current Subsurface Exploration
Subsurface Radar Technologies LLC performed a limited Ground Penetrating
Radar GPR geophysical survey in a preliminary effort to identify and locate the presence
of possible shallow depth caves in the near surface coralline limestone formation
potentially present below the project site Based on our review of the
report by Subsurface Radar Technologies the GPR survey identified several broad areas
of anomalous data above the groundwater table in the open parking lot area of the site
The groundwater level at the project site typically ranges between about 5 and 7 feet
below the existing ground surface The GPR survey did not identify the possible cause of
the anomalous recordings We attribute the anomalous GPR recordings to reflections
from contrasting clayey and gravelly fill layers residing above the existing groundwater
level
Following the GPR survey Geolabs performed a limited test boring exploration at
selected parking lot areas where anomalous GPR data was recorded We drilled four test
borings identified as Boring Nos 1 through 4 to depths of about 25 feet each at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings encountered surface
fills consisting of 5 to 7 feet of stiff clay and medium dense to dense gravel fills overlying
soft to very soft alluvial clays and silts containing some organic matter and sand The soft
clayey and loose sandy deposits extend to the maximum depth explored of about 25 feet
below the existing ground surface Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in
the borings are presented in the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
We did not encounter coralline limestone formation in the drilled borings We also
did not encounter subsurface voids and cavities in the test borings Based on our review
of available previous test boring data by others and our recent limited test boring
exploration we believe that coralline limestone containing possible voids is not present at
most of the Varsity Theatre project site We postulate that either the coralline limestone
material was not deposited in the local area of the project site or it was eroded away and
replaced by alluvial and marsh derived soils consisting of organic rich terrestrial silts and
clays Some early literature reports indicate that the Varsity Theatre site was once a
shallow pond which was subsequently filled
Groundwater

Discussions and Recommendations
We understand that the conceptual re development plan for the project site may
include some mid rise residential commercial buildings and a multi story parking structure
Details pertaining to the proposed redevelopment are currently not available for our
review
Based on our understanding of the subsurface conditions at the project site we
anticipate that new building construction is feasible but would need to be supported on
deep foundation systems because of the presence of loose soft unconsolidated and
compressible materials extending for considerable depth below the project site The deep
foundations may need to be extended to depths greater than about 70 feet depending on
the building loads
Based on the results of the field exploration we believe that coralline limestone
formation containing voids is not present at the majority of the project site It is possible
that coralline material with possible voids may exist at some portions of the site which
were not explored inaccessible areas among the existing buildings Therefore the
owner should be aware that there remains a relatively slight potential for encountering
some localized coralline formation containing possible voids at portions of the site
If voids are encountered in coralline formation material beneath the site then
consideration should be given to past evidence from earlier construction projects in the
South King Street University Avenue area that indicated construction dewatering and
foundation construction activity could potentially adversely affect the existing cave
structures and the groundwater contained therein by altering the established groundwater
flows The altered conditions as a result of excavation and pile installation activity may
cause new conduit obstructions or open new flow paths in the karst which in turn may
undesirably change the existing processes related to the coralline limestone dissolution by
groundwater Other undesirable effects could include the development of additional
sinkholes off site or the collapse of quasi stable existing cave structures beneath the site
Therefore we recommend performing additional geotechnical subsurface exploration to
specifically address the potential presence of voids and the requirements for utilizing deep
foundation systems at the project site
Deep Foundations
Based on the limited information provided we anticipate that redevelopment of the
project site may include new multi story buildings and a parking structure Based on our
preliminary review of the anticipated subsurface conditions at the project site we envision
the use of deep foundations such as piles or cast in place concrete drilled shafts bearing
on deeper competent strata

A detailed design level subsurface exploration consisting of test borings would
likely be required for the site specific design of the foundation system Furthermore the
subsurface exploration should also be performed to locate and identify the potential karst
caves underlying the entire project site to reduce the risk of encountering unforeseen voids
during construction which could pose significant construction delays and require the
implementation of construction contingency plans
Based on our experience in urban Honolulu we envision there may be concerns
regarding excess noise and vibrations generated during pile driving operations Therefore
we believe drilled shaft foundations may be desired to support the proposed structures
It is possible that subsurface cavities and voids will be encountered in coralline
material at the project vicinity Therefore the actual volume of concrete required to fill the
drilled shaft foundation may be appreciably more than the theoretical concrete volume
Based on our experience we envision concrete overpours of up to about 30 to 40 percent
over the theoretical concrete volume may be required for the drilled shaft foundations
We recommend evaluating the subsurface conditions in greater detail by
conducting a design level field exploration program consisting of drilling and sampling
additional borings during the project design phase when the site layout including support
facilities is better defined
Soft and or Loose Soils
Design of the new structures and improvements must consider the presence of the
soft and loose soils at the project site Significant fill thicknesses could initiate ground
settlements In addition drilled shaft excavations may require the use of temporary casing
to reduce the risk of the sidewalls caving in during construction As previously mentioned
a design level field exploration program should be conducted to confirm the presence of
and delineate the approximate limits of soft and or loose soils
Limitations
It should be noted that it is not possible to observe and or anticipate all site
conditions based on a review of literature and performance of preliminary geophysical
exploration Variations of subsurface conditions from those described in this letter report
may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until a
formal design level geotechnical engineering exploration is conducted or construction is
underway A design level geotechnical engineering exploration should be conducted to
confirm and or modify the preliminary recommendations provided herein Our services
were performed in accordance with generally accepted standards of geotechnical
