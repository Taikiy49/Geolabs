INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geologic and soils
investigation for the proposed townhouse development located
in Kamanaiki Valley Honolulu Oahu Hawaii
The scope of this study was to investigate the geologic and
subsurface conditions at the site evaluate overall site Stability
and determine foundation requirements for the proposed residences
and access bridge across Kamanaiki Stream

PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is presently planned to construct 38 townhouse cluster units
The buildings will be two and three story wood frame construction
utilizing post and beam construction Building loads are antici
pated to be normal for this type of construction with maximum
vertical column loadings on the order of 10 kips To prepare the
building site for construction minimal grading operations will
be required However cuts up to 12 feet in depth may be required
for roadway construction Access to the site will be obtained
by a concrete bridge over Kamanaiki Stream at Haaliki Street
It is anticipated that bridge abutment dead plus real live vertical
loadings will be on the order of 10 Kips per lineal foot of abut
ment No center piers will be required for the bridge
SITE CONDITIONS
Surface
The proposed townhouse development site comprising approximately
10 acres is located on the northern slopes of Kamanaiki Valley
Kamanaiki Stream provides the southern boundary of the site
The site is located at the foot of a steep ridge separating
Kamanaiki Valley from the main Kalihi Valley The site has
Slopes generally ranging from 20 percent to 40 percent becoming
nearly vertical at the higher elevations The surface has a heavy

Our borings revealed that the site is immediately underlain by
a clay that varies from 1 to 11 feet in depth These deposits
of clay were washed down from the ridge of weathered basalt
Weathered and unweathered basalt boulders varying in size up
to several feet in diameter are found throughout this upper
formation This type of material is usually subjected to creep
a very slow downhill movement Beneath this material and to
the depths explored a decomposed basalt formation was encountered
This consists generally of varying degrees of decomposed basalt
rock intermixed with a brown clayey silt that is usually found
along the joints and fractures inherited from the basalt rock
Our borings encountered decomposed material to the depths explored
It is expected that basalt bedrock would be found at considerable
depths perhaps as much as 50 to 80 feet below the existing surface
Groundwater was encountered at various depths within our borings
These are shown on the individual boring logs in the Appendix
Our borings were drilled during a wet rainy period and it is
our opinion that the groundwater may be at lower depths during
the drier seasons Subsurface flows may occur in the upper adobe
materials during rainy seasons

DISCUSSTON AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
It is our opinion that the site is suitable for the proposed
development However certain measures must be taken to insure
the overall stability of the site against creep Townhouse
foundations should be designed for expansive soil conditions
The bridge abutment footings should be bottomed in decomposed
basalt
Exterior eéotingé should be Founded a minimum of 24 inches and
interior footings a minimum of 18 inches below final adjacent
grade Continuous footings should be reinforced with a minimum
of two No 4 reinforcing bars placed at the top and bottom of
the footings A bearing value of 2500 pounds per square foot
may be used for continuous and spread footings bottomed in fill
or in natural ground The bearing value may be increased by
one third when considering wind and seismic Toads A minimum
set back distance of four feet should be maintained from the
outer edge of footing measured horizontally to the slope face

Bridge Foundations
Bridge abutments may be supported on spread footings bearing in
dense decomposed basaltic material The footings should be
designed to impose a dead plus live load of 4000 pounds per
square foot A one third increase may be used when considering
short duration lateral forces such as wind or seismic
In reviewing the proposed bridge site it appears that the bridge
will be on a bend in the stream channel The outer or northerly
side of the bend will be subjected to Tateral scour and should
be protected The abutment footings should be protected against
bottom scour Due to the large boulders and cobblés in the
stream channel bottom scour may not be critical However
abutment footings should be bottomed a minimum of three feet
below the Final Stream invert
The proposed footing excavations may be below the water table
observed during rainy periods The water level may be Tower
during periods of little or no rain Provisions should be made
for dewatering footing excavations
Footing excavations should be inspected by the Soils Engineer
to confirm the conditions on which these recommendations are
based

Slabs on Grade
It is our understanding that post and beam construction will be
used for the residences However slabs on grade will be utilized

Retaining Walls
It is our opinion that retaining walls parallel to contours
should be avoided wherever possible However if they are
required it is recommended an equivalent fluid pressure of
1000 pounds per square foot be used for design Retaining
walls acting perpendicular to the existing contours may be
designed to resist active lateral loads with an equivalent
fiuid pressure of 45 pounds per square foot Passive earth
pressures may be computed as an equivalent Fluid having a
density of 250 pounds per cubic foot An allowable coefficient
of friction between soil and concrete of 0 35 should be considered
The backfill immediately against retaining walls should be an
open graded compacted granular material Weepholes or a sub
surface drainage system should be provided to prevent the build
up of excessive pore pressures behind walls

Site Stability
Due to the heavy cover of vegetation it was difficult to determine
whether the site is undergoing creep However should the site
be in equilibrium at the present time residential development
will alter the equilibrium of the slope in that
1 Landscape watering will increase the amount of
moisture entering the creep mass which consists
of the adobe clays and reduce their shear strength
and overall stability
2 Concentration of stresses due to construction of
fills cuts walls and foundations may cause
settlements or lateral movements that may cause
cracking and allow water to enter the creep mass
3 Subsequent movements in the mass may break utility
lines that will supply substantial amounts of
water in the creeping mass and accelerate its
downward motion
It is therefore recommended that preventative measures be taken
to reduce or eliminate the potential of slow excessive downslope
movements
