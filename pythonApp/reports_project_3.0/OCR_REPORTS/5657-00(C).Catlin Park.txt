performed for the Catlin Park Hawaii Regional Housing Privatization Increment Il

slabs on grades retaining structures site grading and pavements only The findings
and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island As the
volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked
against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as Schofield
Plateau
After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time erosion incised deep
valleys into the Koolau Volcano Shield volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows
followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as the Honolulu
Volcanic Series
The Catlin Park project site is on the south side of the Aliamanu vent and on the
southwestern flank of the Salt Lake vent complex of the Honolulu Volcanic Series As
the molten magma ascended through these vents it reacted with the groundwater by
generating steam This steam caused explosive eruptions which deposited low cones
of ash material that have consolidated to form tuff formation locally referred to as
mudrock
The Aliamanu tuff is an older water laid ash deposit that was laid down when the
sea level was higher than the present level The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its
material over alluvium originating from Halawa Stream After this phase of volcanic
activity ceased sedimentary deposits accumulated over the Aliamanu tuff before the
next phase of volcanic activity began Then the Salt Lake eruptions began when the
sea level was at about 40 to 60 feet below the present level The Salt Lake eruptive
event deposited its material over earlier tuffs from the older Aliamanu events

3 1 Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered we envision that the new
dwellings at the Catlin Park site will be underlain by stiff dense surface fill materials or
hard volcanic tuff formation Therefore we recommend supporting the new dwellings on
a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings As an
alternative foundations for support of the structural loads of the residential structures
may also consist of thickened edge slab footings An allowable bearing pressure on
order of about 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used in the design of footings
bearing on a minimum of 12 inches of non expansive select granular fill materials or
directly on the in situ hard volcanic tuff formation This bearing value is for sizing
considering transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces The
minimum 12 inch thick non expansive select granular fill below the footings should
extend laterally a minimum of 2 feet on each side of the footing
Typically we recommend embedding the footings at 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent exterior grade based on the subsurface conditions at the site Due to localized
fine grading for the drainage swales we believe that the footing embedment may be
reduced to a minimum of 14 inches locally provided that the surface is adequately
sloped minimum 2 percent for drainage purposes and maintained at all times
Foundations next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded below an
imaginary 45 degree plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench
or the footings should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement
If the shallow foundations are designed and constructed in strict accordance with
our recommendations we estimate the total settlements of footings to be on the order of
about 1 inch or less Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on
similar materials should be on the order of about 0 5 inch

3 3 Garage Slabs and Concrete Driveways
We envision that concrete garage slabs and driveways will be required for the
new residential structures In addition we anticipate that the garage slabs and
driveways will be constructed near the existing grades In general we recommend
supporting the garage slabs and concrete driveways directly on the 24 inch thick layer

cushion fill layer The moisture barrier and the 2 inch moist fine sand layer may be
omitted for garage slabs and concrete driveways

intersections

3 9 Corrosion Potential
A total of 28 soil resistivity surveys were conducted at seven selected locations
designated as R 1 through R 7 on the Site Plan Plate 2 using the in situ
Wenner 4 Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance meter manufactured by
Nilsson Electrical Laboratory Inc The method utilizes four surface probes or pins
electrodes to evaluate the average resistivity of the underlying soil mass The average
resistivity values of the soil mass extending from the existing ground surface to the test
depth was assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 Results of the field soil resistivity surveys are presented on
Plate C 1 of Appendix C
In addition we conducted laboratory tests including pH minimum resistivity
chloride contents and sulfate contents to evaluate the corrosivity of the underlying
subsurface soils encountered at the project site Based on the test results presented on
Plate B 9 the subsurface soils at the project site generally exhibit a minimum resistivity
value of about 9 660 ohm cm indicating that the subsurface soils are moderately
corrosive to buried metallic structures Therefore we recommend properly designing
substructures especially metallic substructures for protection against potential
corrosion A corrosion engineer should be consulted for detailed recommendations on
corrosion protection

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the housing project site were explored by drilling
and sampling eleven borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 11 extending to
depths of about 20 to 20 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on the Log Legend Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 through A 11
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests performed on selected samples are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD also is a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and

APPENDIX C
Field Resistivity Testing
APPENDIX C
Field Resistivity Testing
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects In general the lower the
resistivity the greater the potential for corrosion to the buried metallic structure
Conversely the higher the resistivity the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of
metallic objects
Geophysical survey equipment i e resistance meter was mobilized to the project
site on September 20 and 21 2006 for the field survey Twenty eight soil resistivity
surveys were conducted at seven locations designated as R 1 through R 7 on the Site
Plan Plate 2 using the in Pin Method and a Nilsson Model 400 resistance
meter manufactured by Nilsson Electrical Laboratory Inc Our field resistivity work was
performed under sunny to cloudy conditions with generally damp surface conditions
The method utilizes four surface probes or pins electrodes to evaluate the
average resistivity an indication of corrosivity of the underlying soil mass The average
resistivity values of the soil mass extending from the existing ground surface to the test
depth was assessed by using this method at the approximate survey locations
The average soil resistance was evaluated at each survey location with the
electrodes aligned at equal spacings of various distances based on the anticipated utility
depths The depth of penetration of a resistivity sounding is a function of the electrode
spacing
The resistance meter Nilsson Model 400 provides a voltage between the outer
electrodes causing an electrical current to flow between them The voltage drop between
the inner electrodes is then measured by a sensitive built in voltmeter As a result the soil
resistance in ohms is obtained from the voltmeter dial reading The average soil
resistivity in ohms cm which is directly related to both resistance and spacing can then
be computed Results of the field resistivity surveys are presented on Plate C 1 It should
be noted that the soil resistivity may be influenced by temperature weather conditions soil
moisture existing underground utilities and other factors
