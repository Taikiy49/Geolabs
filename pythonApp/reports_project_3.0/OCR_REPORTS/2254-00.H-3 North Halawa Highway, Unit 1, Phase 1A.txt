SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
General
The project site is located on the island of Oahu the third largest
of the eight major islands of the Hawaiian Archipelago Oahu was formed
by the volcanic activity of two large shield volcanoes Waianae and
Koolau North Halawa Valley is an erosional feature which has been deeply
incised into the Koolau dome
Koolau was built during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene by
the accumulation of thinly bedded basaltic lava flows extruded from vents
along a fissure line over thirty miles in length which roughly paralleled
the trend of what is now the Koolau Range The main shield building
activity ended in the early Pleistocene approximately 2 5 million years
ago
After the end of the main shield building activity the forces of
erosion began to cut deep valleys into the volcanic dome During the
middle to late Pleistocene the Ice Age sea levels fluctuated
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2254 00 Page 5
eustatically in response to the cycles of continental glaciation As the
glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the ocean
basins so sea levels fell to stands below present sea level When the
glaciers melted and retreated an excess of water became available so sea
levels rose to elevations above present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were effected by these
glacioeustatic sea level fluctuations When sea level was low the
erosional base level was correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to
depths below present sea level When sea level was high the base level
was raised so that sediments accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii the erosional processes are
dominated by mass transfer i e masses of soil and or rock become
detached from the steep valley walls and are transported downslope toward
the axis of the valley primarily by gravity as colluvium Once this
material reaches the stream in the central portion of the valley alluvial
processes become dominant and the sediments are transported and deposited
as alluviun
In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy i e
the flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations
Because of this transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of a
stream s hydraulic load consists of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders
cobbles gravel sand and fines When base levels change these sediment
loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place long enough chemical processes begin
to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering
of the material and causing an induration or cementation of the coarse
portion of the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or
conglomerate Simultaneously erosion continues in the areas above the
valley floors and upstream in the headwaters This continued erosion
generates material which is transported downslope or downstream covering
the older alluvial deposits

Depending upon the local base level and the rate of transport these
newer sediments are generally transient in terms of geological time In
addition their consistency and density is generally less than that of the
older partially consolidated deposits
In general our exploration encountered several distinct types of
geological units The following are descriptions of the material types
se ecetantuanataantonatemmtaantaeatetententaetaaed
Koolau Basalts consist of thinly bedded basaltic lava flows with an
average flow unit thickness of 10 feet or less The flows occur both
in the a ta and pahoehoe forms with transitions between the two
forms The lavas are generally vesicular with jointing resulting
from contraction during cooling and solidification of the molten
rock
The Koolau Basalts grade with depth from extremely weathered
saprolites to relatively unweathered The basalts generally have
good strength characteristics and may be considered an excellent
bearing formation
Residual Materials
Residual materials are actually a subset of Koolau Basalts since they
are soil materials derived from the in situ chemical weathering of
the parent basaltic rocks These soils range from structureless
residual clayey silts or silty clays to saprolites extremely
weathered basalt which are soils that retain the relict structure of
the parent rock e g joints vesicles and or flow structures
These soils generally grade through the range with depth and continue
to grade to the underlying basalt The residual materials generally
exhibit good strength characteristics and are good bearing materials
for lightly to moderately loaded structures

Older Sediments
Se i ts ome
These materials were laid down during the later Pleistocene and are
poorly sorted boulderly alluvium and colluvium In situ weathering
and induration have altered the materials into a highly weathered
poorly consolidated conglomerate Core samples of these older
sediments are typified as highly weathered cobbles and gravel in a
matrix of cemented sandy silt
These materials generally have good to excellent strength
characteristics and provide an excellent bearing layer for foundation
loads
Recent Sediments
These materials are differentiated into two units Alluvium and
Colluvium The alluvium is composed of terrestrial sediments
deposited by the movement of water and is mostly silts and clays with
gravel cobbles and boulders The deposits are poorly sorted highly
variable in consistency density and are encountered in and adjacent
to active stream channels
Colluvium is detrital rock and soil which has been transported from
its original position by gravity This material is generally
bouldery Adobe clays and typically have very poor stability and
foundation support characteristics

Most of the potential embankment fill material will be generated from
the hillside cuts into the colluvium This material is predominantly
clay with moderate to high swelling potential and a significant
percentage of boulders The in situ material generally has a
moisture content in excess of 40 which is about 10 above the
optimum moisture content Therefore although use of the natural
on site material would result in a significant savings on material
costs there are definite limitations in the use of this materials as
described below
1 The excavated soil would need to be dried out to near optimm
moisture content prior to placement in the fill In light of the
relatively high rainfall normally experienced in North Halawa
Valley the drying of the soil would not be feasible during a
significant portion of the year thereby slowing construction of
the embankments
2 The boulders would need to be screened and separated from the
finer portions of the fill to exclude them from the embankments
This additional processing of the soil would slow the embankment
operations and increase costs
3 Due to the expansive potential of the clayey soil the on site
material should not be used within the top three 3 feet of the
embankment fill
In light of the above limitations consideration should be given to
the use of imported granular material for the embankment fills
Imported borrow should consist of non expansive granular material
generally less than three inches in size with less than 20 percent
passing a No 200 sieve The imported borrow material should be

Mechanically Stabilized Earth Retaining Wall MSERW

The proposed final grade of the highway necessitates the use of high
30 abutment and retaining walls at the Bridge No 1 site For
walls of this height MSE retaining walls are economically
competitive and are often less costly
The ability of the MSE structures to tolerate settlements are also an
important consideration for this area where 1 to 2 inches of
settlement may occur due to compression of the subsoils by the weight
of the new fill Since the bridge deck structure can be placed on
the MSE retaining walls after having sufficient time for the
settlement to take place the settlement of the bridge deck can be
greatly reduced
MSE retaining walls are composite soil reinforcing systems which
utilize layers of welded wire mesh or steel strips within the
backfill to improve the shear and compressive strength of the soil
The reinforcing strips are placed in horizontal layers as the fill is
placed and are connected to the precast wall facing members
The external stability of the structure is analyzed as a gravity
retaining wall for the following modes of failure 1 bearing
capacity 2 base sliding 3 overturning 4 deep seated slips

capacity of 150 tons per caisson may be used Settlements of the
caisson under the design loads are anticipated to be on the order of
less than one half of an inch
Serious construction difficulties are expected during installation of
the caissons since the boulders and cobbles will obstruct and slow
the caisson drilling Cobbles and smaller boulders particularly
when in a clayey silt matrix can generally be removed with
conventional caisson drilling equipment but drilling progress will
be slow Very large boulders up to two thirds the shaft diameter
may require removal by special tools like boulder bits or buckets
or in some cases require some manual effort The water table as
encountered in our field exploration would also hinder the progress
of the caisson drilling and subsequent tremie concrete placement
Particular attention should be paid to ensure the cleanliness of the
drilled shaft of the caisson To prevent caving in steel casing
would be required Careful removal of the casing during concrete
placement is essential to obtain a competent and continuous shaft

Laboratory Testing
a ee Se aS A Aa ee
The moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on
selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of the test are presented on the Boring Logs at
the appropriate sample depths
Seven 7 sieve analysis tests were performed on samples of the
underlying soil to evaluate its grain size distribution The test results
are summarized on Plates B 1 and B 2
Five 5 direct shear tests were performed on undisturbed samples of
the subsoils to evaluate the shear strength parameters The test results
are presented on Plates B 3 through B 7
One 1 consolidation test was performed on an undisturbed sample of
the fine grained subsoil to evaluate the compressibility of the soil The
test results are plated on Plates B 8 and B 9
Two 2 unconsolidated undrained UU triaxial tests were performed
on the deeper alluvial soils to evaluate the strength parameters of the
alluvium The test results are plated on Plates B 10 and B 11
Four 4 sets of resistivity and pH measurements were made on the
alluvium soils to evaluate the corrosion potential of the soils These
test results are summarized on Plate B 12
