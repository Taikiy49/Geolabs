REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu is comprised largely of the weathered remnants of the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of the western 
third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of the eastern 
two-thirds of the island. It is believed that the Waianae Volcano became extinct while the 
Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas 
in central Oahu. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet waters 
protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the

The project site lies on the Kahuku Coastal Plain and the pediment of the northern 
end of the Koolau Mountain Range. The project area is generally classified from a 
geological standpoint as ‘Qa’ for Quaternary Alluvium (Sinton, et.al.) which consists of 
alluvial soils deposited since the Pleistocene Epoch. Experience at the Turtle Bay Resort 
complex indicates that this alluvial material forms a thin mantle over residuum from 
Koolau Basalt at the southern side of the resort and over coralline and dune sand deposits 
to the north of Kamehameha Highway. 
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS 
The project site is situated along Kuilima Drive extending from Kamehameha 
Highway to the roundabout at Kaihalulu Drive East Roadway in the Kahuku area on the 
Island of Oahu as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. Kuilima Drive, as the main entrance 
to the Kuilima Estates and Turtle Bay Resort, had been widened to four lanes divided 
roadways with landscape median and concrete curb and gutter. The majority of Kuilima 
Drive was paved with asphaltic concrete except at the intersections with Kamehameha 
Highway and connecting roadways to the Kuilima Estates, where there are small 
segments of colored and stamped concrete pavements. Based on our field observations 
and experience in the area, the topography along the roadway is undulating with existing 
pavement surface elevations varying between +12 and +19 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
A site-specific field exploration program was not conducted for the electrical 
ductline improvement project. Instead, the available in-house subsurface boring 
information from our previous and current projects in the vicinity of the electrical ductline 
improvement project site was used in the formulation of our engineering 
recommendations for the project. 
Based on our review of available in-house soil boring information in the vicinity of the 
project site, we envision that the proposed electrical ductline improvement project site is 
likely underlain by alluvium consisting of both sandy and clayey soils. Below the alluvium, 
marine deposits, coralline detritus materials and basalt formation were encountered in the 
borings drilled during our previous field explorations.  
Shallow groundwater level conditions ranging from about Elevations +0 to +3 feet 
MSL are anticipated at the electrical ductline improvement project area. Considering that 
the project site is in proximity to the Pacific Ocean, the groundwater levels will vary in 
response to tidal fluctuations and storm surge conditions. In addition, water levels at the 
project site also may be influenced by seasonal precipitation, surface runoff, and other 
factors.

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on our research and review of available in-house soils and geologic 
information pertaining to the project site and its vicinity, we are providing the following 
detailed discussions and recommendations in support of the design of the proposed 
electrical ductline improvement project. 
Utility Trenches 
It is anticipated that the utilities for the proposed improvement project will consist of 
electrical ductlines. In general, good construction practices should be utilized for the 
excavation and backfilling of the trenches for the proposed utilities. The contractor should 
determine the method and equipment to be used for excavation, subject to practical limits 
and safety considerations. The excavation should comply with all applicable local, state, and 
federal safety requirements. We understand that the electrical ductlines will have a 3-foot 
minimum cover, with localized areas where the electrical ductlines will need to dive down 
deeper to cross under existing obstructions. The contractor should be responsible for 
trench shoring design and installation when the trench excavation is deeper than about 
5 feet below the existing ground surface. Trench shoring and bracing should conform to the 
appropriate health and safety requirements. 
In general, we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No. 3B 
Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) for support of the utility lines. The initial trench 
backfills up to about 12 inches above the pipes should consist of free-draining backfills, such 
as No. 3B Fine gravel, to reduce the potential for damaging the pipes from compaction of 
the backfill. It is critical to use a free-draining granular material to reduce the potential for 
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the 
sides of the pipes. The use of on-site clayey soils as backfill directly around the utility pipes 
is not recommended and should not be allowed. The bedding and initial trench backfills 
provisions may be eliminated if the electrical ductlines are concrete encased. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the finished subgrade may consist of approved on-site soils or select granular fill materials. 
Select granular fill materials should be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no 
larger than 3 inches in the largest dimension and contain between 10 and 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory California 
Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of 1 percent or less when 
tested in accordance with ASTM D1883. Geolabs should test the backfill materials for 
conformance with these recommendations. 
The on-site soil backfill and/or select granular fill materials should be 
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative 
compaction to reduce the potential for appreciable future ground subsidence. The finished 
subgrade of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to no less 
than 95 percent relative compaction.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density

Pavement Restoration 
Based on our experience with past and current improvement projects in the area, the 
following pavement sections are recommended for pavement areas damaged by trench 
excavations for the installation of the proposed electrical ductlines. 
Kuilima Drive (Clay Subgrade) 
  3.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete 
  6.0-Inch Asphalt Concrete Base (92 Percent Relative Specific Gravity) 
16.0-Inch Aggregate Subbase Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
25.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade 
Kuilima Drive (Sand Subgrade) 
  3.0-Inch Asphaltic Concrete 
  6.0-Inch Asphalt Concrete Base (92 Percent Relative Specific Gravity) 
10.0-Inch Aggregate Subbase Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction) 
19.0-Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade 
The small segments of concrete pavement that are damaged by trench 
excavations should be replaced with new concrete pavement sections matching the 
thickness of the existing concrete pavement section. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregates, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D2041. Asphalt concrete (AC) 
should consist of Mix #4 in accordance with the City and County of Honolulu Standard 
Specifications for Public Works Construction (September 1986). Asphalt concrete base  
and AC material should be constructed in accordance with Sections 32 and 34 of the 
Standard Specifications for Public Works Construction (September 1986), respectively. 
Aggregate subbase materials required for the project should consist of crushed 
basaltic aggregates and should conform to Section 30 of the Standard Specifications for 
Public Works Construction (September 1986). The imported subbase materials should 
be compacted to no less than 95 percent relative compaction. California Bearing Ratio 
and field density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered 
during construction to confirm the adequacy of the above sections.

Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be maintained 
to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. Surface water 
ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development of good 
shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage structures 
is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or premature 
failure of the pavements.

Construction Monitoring 
Geolabs should be retained to provide geotechnical engineering services during 
construction of the proposed project. The critical items of construction monitoring that 
require "Special Inspection" include observation of the trench excavation and compaction of 
trench backfill and subbase materials. 
A Geolabs representative should also monitor other aspects of earthwork 
construction to observe compliance with the intent of the design concepts, specifications, or 
recommendations and to expedite suggestions for design changes that may be required in 
the event that subsurface conditions differ from those anticipated at the time this report was 
prepared. The recommendations provided herein are contingent upon such observations. If 
the actual exposed subsurface conditions encountered during construction are different 
from those assumed or considered in this report, then appropriate modifications to the 
design should be made.
