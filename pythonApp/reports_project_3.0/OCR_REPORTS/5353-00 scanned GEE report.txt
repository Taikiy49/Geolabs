REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below the Koolau lavas in central
Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of the Koolau shield Its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu
Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil
Pleistocene Age coral algal reef deposits and related deposits which are intercalated with
terrigenous sediments laid during high stands of the sea The base of the stratigraphic
section in this area is Koolau Basalt
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the early Pleistocene by thinly bedded
basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities ceased approximately 2 million
years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicates that the Island of Oahu has subsided as
much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed behind
the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the lagoons

During the later Pleistocene Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers melted global sea levels
rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially
stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower
relative to present sea level on Oahu
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
corresponding higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the
sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and
induration of the calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of
upper areas of the volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high
energy conditions
During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward and deposited alluvium over the lagoonal
sediments During these stable periods the growth of fringing coral algal reefs formed
distinct ledges of reef limestone at elevations roughly conforming to the level of the sea
during that period About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level occurred
During that rise the valleys in the project area were submerged In the last 10 000 years
or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine sediments
have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments resulting
in thick deposits of soft loose harbor sediments along the coast in the areas that were
formerly valleys and drainageways
Land development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought
the Waikiki area to its present form Many of the resulting fills are of poor quality in terms
of supporting heavy structural loads

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our review of available subsurface information near the project site we
anticipate that the site is generally underlain by loose to very loose beach sand and
lagoonal deposits followed by a coral formation and medium dense reef detritus deposit
Shallow groundwater levels are anticipated at the site with groundwater levels anticipated
to be relatively close to the nearby tidal levels and fluctuations
Based on the highly compressible beach and lagoonal deposits anticipated at
shallow depths limited access area and the moderate to high structural loads anticipated
for the new elevator we believe that a deep foundation system consisting of micropiles
should be used to support the new elevator structure Micropile foundation
recommendations are provided below
Micropile Foundation
Based on the highly compressible subsurface conditions encountered at the project
site a deep foundation system is recommended to provide support of the new elevator
structure We believe that a deep foundation system consisting of micropiles may be
used In general a micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter usually less
than 12 inches drilled and grouted pile with steel reinforcing The micropile foundation
typically is constructed by drilling a borehole placing reinforcing steel in the hole and
grouting the borehole Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed readily in
access restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions In
addition installation of the micropiles generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent
structures the adjacent soils and the environment
Since field borings were not drilled at the site we recommend that some flexibility
be incorporated in the micropile design to accommodate varying subsurface conditions
The design flexibility consists of providing two micropile design lengths for the project The
micropile lengths will be for piles installed within the coral formation and for piles installed
within the medium dense detritus deposit should the coral formation not be encountered
during pile installation
We believe that micropiles with a minimum grout bulb diameter of about 5 to
6 inches may be used to support the concrete slab The micropiles should extend through
the loose to very loose beach and lagoonal deposits and into the underlying coral
formation or medium dense reef detritus deposit The micropiles will derive support
primarily in skin friction in the coral formation or medium dense reef detritus deposit

We understand that the total vertical load on the new slab foundation is about
550 kips We recommend that an allowable vertical load capacity of up to 46 kips may be
used per pile Based on the anticipated subsurface conditions we estimate that micropile
lengths of about 38 and 52 feet are needed to provide the recommended design allowable
compressive load capacity for piles installed within the coral formation and within the
medium dense reef detritus deposit respectively The micropiles should be spaced a
minimum of 2 feet from center to center The minimum pile spacing recommended would
avoid reduction in vertical capacity due to group action and would also facilitate pile
installation in the pile group
Micropile Load Test Program
It should be noted that the compressive load capacity of the micropiles is highly
dependent on the drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the
contractor to install the micropile Therefore the compressive load capacity of the
micropile may vary considerably between different contractors and micropile foundation
systems In order to evaluate whether the contractor s methods of micropile installation
are adequate and to evaluate the ultimate compressive load capacity we recommend that
at least two proof load tests be performed on the production micropiles
In general the proof load test should be performed in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 3689 Based on experience we believe that the load test should be
conducted no earlier than 7 days after completion of the micropile installation or when the
micropile grout has attained at least 75 percent of the specified 28 day compressive
strength
The load test micropile should be loaded gradually to at least 200 percent of the
allowable design load in compression We recommend that the allowable design test load
be held for a minimum of 10 minutes depending on the recorded movements of the load
test micropile Should the micropile movements between the one minute and ten minute
readings exceeds 0 04 inches the allowable design test load should be held for an
additional 50 minutes The proof load testing is an integral part of the design of the
micropile foundation system Therefore we recommend that the proof load testing be
conducted under the observation of a representative from Geolabs
Micropile Foundation Settlements
Settlement of the micropile foundation will result primarily from elastic compression
of the pile member and subgrade response We estimate the total settlement of the
pile supported foundations to be less than 1 inch with differential settlements between the
micropiles not exceeding about one half of the total settlement We believe that these
settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied

Construction Considerations
We understand that elevations of the existing structure are not available Based on
previous project performed in the general area we anticipate that the existing elevation at
the lobby area may be about 6 to 7 feet Mean Sea Level MSL In addition we
understand that the top of the basement slab is about 8 feet from the lobby floor level
Therefore it appears that the top of the existing basement slab may be below the existing
groundwater level
We envision that the micropiles will be installed from the lobby level and drilled
through the lobby and basement floor slabs During micropile installation we anticipate
that groundwater may enter the basement level through the drill hole in the basement slab
To reduce the potential for reduced grout strength due to flowing water during micropile
installation we recommend that micropile installation be performed after the groundwater
flowing into the basement level have reached a static condition The use of a drill casing
sealed on top of the basement slab may be considered to help contain the groundwater
inside the casing during pile installation
As previously mentioned a portion of the existing basement wall and slab will be
demolished for the new foundation slab Since the groundwater levels may be higher than
the basement slab groundwater may be encountered during demolition work and slab
construction Therefore dewatering may be required for the construction of the new
foundation slab The installation of continuously interlocking sheet piling may be
considered to reduce the amount of groundwater entering the excavation
Installation of micropiles should be performed by a specialty subcontractor
experienced in the construction of a micropile foundation system Due to the specialized
nature of the micropile foundation construction observation of the micropile foundation
installation system and testing of the micropiles should be designated a Special
Inspection item Therefore observation of the micropile installation operations by
Geolabs Special Inspector is necessary to confirm our design assumptions and should
be designated a Special Inspection item in accordance with Section 1701 of the Uniform
Building Code 1997
