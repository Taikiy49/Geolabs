INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Farrington Commercial Center project in the Kapolei area on the Island of
Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations derived from
our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These recommendations
are intended for the design of foundations slabs on grade retaining structures site
preparation and pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age
As the volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau banked against its eroded
eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the Schofield Plateau The project site
is located on the southwestern flank of the Waianae Volcano
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau generated
sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project site and to the
south these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine sediments and
coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the sedimentary
wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to over 1 000 feet at
Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or
caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of Southern Oahu Deposition of sediments has
continued from earlier geologic time through the present
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill the
oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When the
glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea levels
rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of accumulated
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits at correspondingly
higher elevations and deposited lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters protected by
fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and
GEOLABS INC
W O 4462 00 Page 5
reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials
Agricultural development within the last 100 years and recent mass grading work have brought
the Kapolei area to its present form

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
As described above the proposed commercial center is underlain by near surface fills
overlying saprolitic soils highly weathered basalt rock The near surface fills generally consist
of stiff clayey and dense sandy soils Based on the stiff and dense near surface fills at the site
we believe that a shallow foundation system bearing on the recompacted on site fill materials
may be used to support the proposed buildings planned at the site
Based on our laboratory test results the stiff clayey fill materials at the site may be
classified as moderately to highly expansive when subjected to fluctuations in the moisture
content Therefore we recommend that slabs on grade at the site be supported on at least
18 inches of non expansive select granular fill material capping fill to reduce the potential
shrink swell effects on the new slabs In addition special attention should be given to the
subgrade preparation requirements presented in the Slabs On Grade section of this report
to reduce the potential shrink swell effects on the concrete slabs
GEOLABS INC
W O 4462 00 Page 7
We wish to point out that there are other methods available in the local construction
market to reduce the potential effects of the expansive soils at the site As an alternate to the
conventional removal and replacement alternative consideration may be given to chemical
stabilization of the expansive soils at the site Based on the available information we believe
that chemical stabilization using the Electrochemical Soil Stabilizer EcSS 3000 may be an
effective alternative for the proposed commercial center project In general the EcSS 3000
chemical stabilization involves the injection of a diluted chemical diluted in water into the
ground to chemically stabilize the expansive nature of the soils The chemical injection reacts
with the clay particles in the soil to form a soil mass that is less susceptible to shrink swell
effects when subjected to moisture fluctuations

Foundations
Based on our field exploration we believe that a shallow foundation system consisting
of spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the proposed buildings and the
pump island canopy planned at the site An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 500 pounds
per square foot psf may be used for the design of foundations bearing on the re compacted
on site fill materials The allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as wind and or seismic forces
Foundations should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished
grade
The bottom of the footing excavations should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to
the placement of reinforcing steel and concrete Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Foundation subgrades should not be allowed to dry before placing concrete If shrinkage
GEOLABS INC
W O 4462 00 Page 8
cracks appear in the foundation excavations the soil should be thoroughly moistened to close
the cracks and recompacted to provide a firm base prior to concrete placement
Where foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the
utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to
avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to
reduce the potential for appreciable foundation settlement
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations we estimate that the total foundation settlements under the anticipated
design loads to be on the order of less than 1 inch with differential settlements on the order of
about 0 5 inches
We anticipate that the pump island canopy may be subjected to uplift forces created
by wind conditions Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the following 1 dead
weight of the structure and slab 2 weight of backfill material above the footing where
applicable and 3 side shear between the sides of the footings and surrounding soils
Contribution of dead weight from the backfill may be estimated using a unit weight of
110 pounds per cubic foot pcf For side shear between the sides of the footings and the
adjacent soils a shear value of 150 psf may be used
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 35
may be used between the base of the foundations and the supporting fill materials
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure
of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf These values assume that the concrete
for the foundations is placed directly against the foundation excavations and that the soils
around the foundations are well compacted Unless covered by pavements or slabs the
passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below the finished grade should be neglected

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed building floors
car wash facility pump island area and trash enclosure Our exploration indicated that the
site is generally underlain by moderately to highly expansive clayey soils at or near the existing
ground surface Unless slabs on grade supported by these expansive soils are properly
designed there is a potential for future distress to the floor slabs resulting from wetting and
subsequent swelling of the expansive clayey soils
To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from the swelling of the on site
expansive soils we recommend that non expansive select granular fill material capping fill
be provided for a minimum depth of 18 inches below the slab cushion The capping fill should
also extend beyond the perimeter of the building slab a minimum of 2 feet or to the edge of the
perimeter sidewalk surrounding the buildings The non expansive select granular fill should
be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level loose lifts of
about 8 inches and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction
The non expansive select granular fill should be placed as soon as possible after the
subgrade soils are prepared The subgrade soils below the select granular fill should be
scarified a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction
The capping fill and the underlying subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist until final
placement of concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrades
we recommend that the soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks or be prepared
again Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
GEOLABS INC
W O 4462 00 Page 10
and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well
compacted fill at no additional cost to the owner
For interior slabs such as the building slabs we recommend that a minimum
4 inch thick cushion layer consisting of free draining materials such as No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be placed below the slab to provide more uniform support of
the slab To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to
floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is
recommended on top of the cushion layer Two inches of moist fine sand may be considered
above the plastic membrane to provide protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the
slab concrete Provision of the 2 inches of fine sand above the vapor barrier will be left up to
the discretion of the project structural engineer Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or
sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking
Concrete for the exterior slabs such as for the car wash facility pump island and trash
enclosure may be placed directly on the compacted non expansive select granular fill
material The select granular fill should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction because these slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic For design of structural
slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction of 200 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection
pci may be used for the compacted select granular fill material
The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at least
12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade Areas adjacent to the slab should be backfilled
tightly against the slabs with relatively impervious soils on site clayey soils and graded to
divert water away from the slabs to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs
Chemical Stabilization of Expansive Soils
As previously mentioned we believe that chemical stabilization of the on site expansive
clayey soils may be an effective way of coping with the moderately to highly expansive nature
of the on site soils Based on our evaluations we believe that the locally available EcSS 3000
for chemical stabilization may be a technically and cost effective alternative to reduce the
GEOLABS INC
W O 4462 00 Page 11
shrink swell effects on the proposed slabs on grade With the chemical stabilization the
18 inch thick non expansive select granular fill below the building slabs may be omitted from
the design and construction However the 4 inch thick cushion fill below the building slabs is
still recommended and should be retained in the design and construction of the buildings
For preliminary estimating purposes we believe that the chemical stabilization would
need to be injected to a minimum depth of about 8 feet below the finished pad elevation In
addition the spacing of the injection holes should not be further than 3 feet on center in both
directions The chemical stabilization should also extend a minimum of 5 feet beyond the
edge of the building envelope
After injection of the chemical stabilizer a minimum curing time of 72 hours should be
allowed prior to post treatment sampling and testing for evaluation of the treated product The
post treatment sampling and testing would include drilling and sampling about six borings
extending to a depth of about 10 feet to obtain relatively undisturbed samples of the treated
zone Four relatively undisturbed samples of the treated zone should be obtained from each
boring The relatively undisturbed samples of the treated soils should be subjected to
one dimensional swell tests conducted in accordance with ASTM D 4546 Method B For
acceptance purposes the average swell in each of the post treatment borings should be
1 percent or less In addition the results of the swell test of each treated soil sample should
not exceed 2 percent
Based on our assessment the chemical stabilization alternative deserves a more
detailed evaluation and cost comparison If the chemical stabilization alternative is to be
implemented Geolabs should be consulted to provide supplemental recommendations for
implementation during the design and construction

Utility Lines
We envision that new utility lines and utility connections will be required for the
proposed project In general we recommend that granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be provided below the new utility pipes for
support Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes
It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper
backfill material around the pipes and placement could result in settlement of the backfill and
damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils or select
granular fill materials The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in about 8 inch loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less
than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence
Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the
pavement grade should be compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades adjacent to and beyond the slab areas and the building foundations
should be sloped to shed water away to reduce the potential for ponding Surface water
ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction These drainage
requirements are essential for the proper performance of the foundations and pavements
since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent heaving or loss
of strength In addition drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should
be maintained to drain surface water runoff away from the building and other improvements
Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling six borings
extending to depths of about 14 to 20 5 feet below the existing ground surface The
approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings
were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the
laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch OD
Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
