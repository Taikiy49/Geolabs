Geotechnical Engineering Exploration and Consuitation
Replacement Pipeline at Hanapepe Stream Crossing
Dear Mr Miyashiro
We have completed our field exploration for the above project This letter report
summarizes our work performed and the findings from our field exploration in situ testing
and engineering analyses for the proposed project
Field Exploration
Our field exploration consisted of excavating four test pits designated as TP 1 through
TP 4 at the proposed pipeline replacement project site to depths of approximately 5 to 6 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the test pits are shown on
the Site Plan Plate 1
Our field exploration indicated that the project site is generally underlain by medium
dense to dense streambed deposits consisting of rounded cobbles and boulders with fine to
coarse sand Cobbles and boulders up to 30 inches in diameter were encountered in the
excavation of the test pits Larger boulders were visible in the stream bed Groundwater was
encountered in the test pits at a depth of about 3 feet below the existing ground surface at
the time of our field exploration Descriptions of the materials encountered in the excavated
test pits are provided on the Log of Test Pits Plate 2 The test pits were backfilled using the
excavated material and compacted by tamping with the backhoe bucket
The test pits were excavated with a Cat backhoe to an average size of about 10 feet
long 5 feet wide and 5 to 6 feet deep The pits were excavated within 10 to 12 minutes
which indicates that a conventional open trench installation method might be an effective
construction method
In Situ Percolation Test
In situ percolation tests were conducted in TP 1 at depths of 4 and 5 5 feet below the
existing ground surface to evaluate the permeability of the existing ground for dewatering
A paddlewheel flow sensor was used to measure the pumping rate Three inch centrifugal
pump was used to pump water in or out of the test pit

The percolation tests were performed initially at a depth of 4 feet below the existing
ground surface by pumping water into the pit A hydrostatic equilibrium state was achieved
by a relatively constant pumping rate of 34 gallons per minute gpm to maintain the
differential water head of about 1 75 feet for approximately 5 minutes
The second percolation test was conducted at the depth of 5 5 feet below the existing
ground surface by pumping water out of the pit into a prepared seepage pit adjacent to TP 1
A hydrostatic equilibrium state was reached by a relatively constant pumping rate of about
55 gallons per minute The equilibrium state was maintain with a differential water head of
approximately 1 4 feet between the final water level in the pit and existing ground water table
for 10 minutes
Based on the in situ percolation test results the coefficient of permeability k was
evaluated The tests indicated that the coefficient of permeability could be on the order of
0 01 and 0 05 centimeters per second at depths of 4 and 5 feet respectively below the
ground surface The difference in k values may be caused by different percentage of fine
sand in the streambed deposits
It should be noted that the coefficient of permeability will vary primarily with the
percentage of sand and fines Therefore the pumping rate during dewatering could be
significantly higher than the equilibrium state rate obtained in the testing
