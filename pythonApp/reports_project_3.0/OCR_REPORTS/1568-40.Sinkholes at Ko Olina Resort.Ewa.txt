Geotechnical Recommendations
Sinkholes
Ko Olina Resort
In accordance with your request we have examined the sinkholes being
encountered in the vicinity of Lagoon 3 and other portions of the subject
project site The following report summarizes our current observations
and presents our recommendations regarding the treatment of the sinkholes
in the grading areas
Introduction
Sinkholes are a geologic feature commonly found in regions underlain
by calcium carbonate rocks such as coral and limestones Regions under
lain by such rocks develop a distinctive topography or terrain which is
referred to as a karst topography Karst terrains are typified by sink
holes and caves Manmoth Cave and Carlsbad Caverns are examples of karst
features which have developed in massive limestones The so called
Porsche eating sinkholes of Florida are examples of karst features which
appear to develop rapidly without warning and have catastrophic results

Calcium carbonate the principal component of corals and limestones
is insoluble in pure water However as rain falls to the earth carbon
dioxide reacts with water to form carbonic acid The carbonic acid in tum
reacts with the calcium carbonate causing the carbonate to go into solu
tion Dissolution of the carbonate rock first occurs along lines of weak
ness such as joints and bedding planes As the dissolution of the rock
continues these lines of weakness could enlarge into caves accompanied
by collapse of portions of the roof and walls of the cavities forming
along the lines of weakness Sinkholes occur when a cave broaches the
surface i e the roof of the cave becomes too weak to support itself and
any overburden
Regional Geology
The Ko Olina Resort project site is located at the southwest corner
of the island of Oahu on a coastal plain which developed on the south
flank of the Waianae range This coastal plain consists of intercalated
terrigenous and marine sediments During the Pleistocene epoch sea level
fluctuated in response to the waxing and waning of the glacial stages As
the great continental glaciers receded sea levels rose above current sea
level and conversely as the glaciers grew sea levels fell
In the seaward margins of the Ko Olina Resort large areas of coral
and coral algal limestones are encountered These formations grew or
were deposited during a higher stand of the sea
Coral and coral algal limestones are the result of the growth of
marine organisms which secrete calcium carbonate The formations con
structed by these organisms are frequently discontinuous in nature and
have voids where the organisms did not grow Often these voids are
filled with detritus However the voids also frequently act as lines of
weakness for the formation of solution cavities or caves and sinkholes

Field Observations
On a project geologist from our firm made a site visit
to the area in the vicinity of Lagoon 3 of the Ko Olina Resort project
During this site visit numerous holes were observed in the ground surface
which was composed of exposed coral algal reef formation The existing
surface of the formation showed evidence of solution reworking e g
smooth recemented faces The holes ranged from approximately one foot to
approximately 20 feet in surface diameter Closer visual inspection
revealed that many of the holes had greater diameters below the surface
and contained loose cobbles and boulders resulting from the collapse of
the roof of the underlying cavity It was also noted that most of these
holes contained other foreign materials such as wood tree limbs and
trunks leaves and dry grasses One hole was also noted to contain the
partial skeletal remains of a domestic animal believed to be bovine in
origin It was concluded from the field observations that the holes are
karst features or sinkholes that have developed in the coral algal
formation
Discussion
Sinkholes are the surface expression of subsurface voids or cavities
which have broached the surface The presence of sinkholes in the ground
surface is highly indicative of additional and often extensive voids in
the subsurface As evidenced in the karst regions of the southeastern
United States e g Florida and Georgia sinkholes resulting from the
sudden collapse of the roof of a cavity can have catastrophic effects
Sinkholes have opened suddenly to engulf automobiles house trailers
homes and commercial buildings Some sinkholes in the southeastern United
States attain diameters of 300 or more feet In Hawaii sinkholes are
generally smaller however there have been cases in Moiliili and other
areas where sinkholes have opened suddenly and resulted in property
damage

Therefore construction in an area known to have karst features
entails a certain degree of risk The ideal solution to the risks
involved in construction in karst areas is to locate fill and seal all of
the subsurface voids However this is generally not practical and is
frequently not possible
Recommendations
As mentioned above there is a certain degree of high risk involved
in developing land which is know to have subsurface voids Any method
used to remedy the situation will also involve a degree of risk as to the
completeness and future performance of the remedial efforts Each of the
following recommendations will have a certain level of risk associated
with the proposed remedy and the acceptability of that risk must be
evaluated with respect to the needs for performance and the costs
involved
General
No matter which one or combination of the following recommendations
is chosen the existing sinkholes encountered during clearing and grading
should be opened cleaned of loose and deleterious materials and then
properly backfilled in accordance with the appropriate procedures The
major difficulty in our opinion involves the location and detection of
caverns or large voids below the ground surface which may later open into
sinkholes
Following the backfilling of the existing open sinkholes a program
of cave or void detection should be undertaken As a minimm this
detection program should involve a dynamic detection method such as
striking the ground surface in sinkhole areas This dynamic detection
would involve using a large piece of earthmoving equipment e g a
track mounted backhoe to repeatedly strike the ground throughout the
entire area Any places which are then suspected of having shallow seated
voids as evidenced by hollow sounds or cracking of the coral should be
flagged and investigated in greater detail More detailed detection may
be obtained by probing of the subsurface with drilling equipment and or by
geophysical methods such as ground penetrating radar

The detection program should be closely monitored by a qualified

Cavity Probing
It is recommended that any areas having existing sinkholes or
suspected of having subsurface voids and cavities be probed by drilling
first to determine the depth and horizontal extent of the suspected voids
and cavities
The area should then be probed on minimm 10 foot centers using
equipment capable of drilling holes of three 3 inch diameter or greater
The probe holes should extend to depths of at least 10 feet below the
existing or finished ground surface whichever is deeper
When any probe encounters large voids additional probes should be
drilled to delineate the extent of the void
Once the void has been delineated it should be exposed opened
cleaned to solid coral formation and filled in accordance with one of the
methods given below
Depending upon the results of the probe drilling one or more of the
following remedial measures may be considered
Grouting
The drilled holes from the above probing could be pressure grouted to
locate fill and seal any subsurface cavities
Prior to the commencement of the probing and grouting operations the
existing sinkholes should be opened cleaned to solid coral formation and
backfilled with lean concrete

The probe holes should then be stage grouted from the bottom upwards
using a moderate pressure
The grout should consist of neat cement and water If grout uptake
exceeds more than 3 times the estimated volume of the drilled hole grout
placement should be stopped for a period of one hour and then a mortar mix
should then be pumped into the hole If grout uptake then exceeds 2 times
the estimated volume of the hole pumping should be stopped for an addi
tional hour and a masonry grout injected until no further grout uptake
occurs
This method will provide relatively complete filling and sealing of
the cavities and further provide a high degree of performance It is our
opinion that this method presents little risk in terms of future settle
ments and or collapse
Sand Backfill
The existing open sinkholes may be backfilled with sand Prior to
commencement of the backfilling operations the existing open holes should
be opened and cleaned to competent coral formation The holes may then be
backfilled by using a free running dry clean sand placed in maximum three
3 foot thick lifts Each lift should be thoroughly jetted and flooded
prior to the placement of subsequent lifts
This method does not assure the complete filling and sealing of all
subsurface voids and cavities nor is there any assurance that the sand
backfill will not migrate in the future to other openings The migration
of the sand could result in loss of support for any fill and or structures
placed over the backfilled sinkholes which could in tum result in
settlements and or collapse Therefore this method presents a
significant risk if used in structural areas

Bridging
The sinkhole areas may be bridged by the use of a strong rockfill
The existing open holes should be exposed or opened and cleaned to
competent coral formation The holes may then be backfilled using sand
as discussed above or with selected rockfill material The selected
rockfill should be well graded from coarse to fine and contain no
individual particles exceeding one 1 foot in maximm dimension The
selected rockfill should be placed in lifts having a maximm thickness of
eighteen inches and be thoroughly rammed and compacted with a large
backhoe or equivalent
When the holes are filled to the adjacent ground surface the area
should be covered with rockfill embankment placed in accordance with the
recommendations in our report dated The bridging
rockfill should be a minimum of four 4 feet thick choked and capped in
accordance with the previously cited recommendations
As with the sand backfill method this method does not assure
complete treatment of all the subsurface voids Although there is less
risk due to the bridging effect and less fines to migrate there is still
a risk of settlement and or collapse resulting from the enlargement of a
previously undetected cavity
Combined Methods
The above methods may be combined to yield a stronger ground
treatment and or a more cost effective treatment Possible combinations
are
1 the use of sand backfill and bridging rockfill
2 the use of grouting and sand backfill and
3 the use of sand backfill or bridging rockfill coupled with the
probing and grouting of the areas below the foundations of any
proposed buildings
