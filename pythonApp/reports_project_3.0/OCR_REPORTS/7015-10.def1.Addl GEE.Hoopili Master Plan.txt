SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration encountered a surface layer of clayey alluvial soils extending 
to depths ranging between about 28 and 44 feet below the existing ground surface.  These 
clayey alluvial soils have a limited capacity to transmit water (i.e., low to very low 
permeability). 
Below the clayey alluvial soils, our borings encountered more permeable coralline 
deposits that extended to depths of about 96 to 100 feet below the existing ground surface 
where they were underlain by another clayey alluvial sequence.  The coralline deposits 
ranged from loose sands to cemented detrital materials.  
We encountered groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. The groundwater levels were recorded at depths ranging between about 
35 and 38 feet below the existing ground surface at the times and dates noted on the 
boring logs presented in Appendix A of this report.  However, it should be noted that 
groundwater levels are subject to occur or change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Field permeability testing indicated that the coralline materials have an average 
coefficient of permeability, k, on the order of about 1.4 x 10-1 centimeters per second. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussions and specific 
geotechnical recommendations.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our additional geotechnical engineering 
exploration performed for the Ho`opili Master Plan Development project in Ewa on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The proposed development area encompasses approximately 
1,500 acres. The additional exploration was conducted to explore for more permeable 
horizons in the subsurface for potential use for the disposal of storm water runoff.  The 
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.

and geotechnical engineering information. The findings and recommendations 
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The project involves the development of a master plan for the site as a 
mixed-use residential and commercial project. As part of the overall development, it is 
planned to divert a portion of the storm water runoff generated in the Ho`opili area to the 
open area to the east of Fort Weaver Road and south of Arizona Road.  Our previous 
exploration in that area indicated that the near-surface soils were generally clayey in 
nature and exhibited poor infiltration characteristics.  This additional exploration has 
been conducted to explore the deeper subsurface conditions and evaluate whether 
more permeable materials are present at depths greater than the 20 feet explored in our 
preliminary exploration; and, to perform testing on the deeper materials to evaluate their 
permeability characteristics.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age. As the volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows 
from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, 
now known as the Schofield Plateau. The project site is on the southwestern flank of the 
Waianae Volcano. 
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau 
generated sediments, which were transported to the coast. In the project vicinity and to 
the south, these sediments have accumulated and are interbedded with marine 
sediments and coral/algal reef formations, forming a sedimentary wedge. The 
thickness of the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate 
Route H-1 Highway to over 1,000 feet at Ewa Beach. This wedge forms the Ewa Plain 
and serves as the confining formation, or "caprock," over the artesian basal aquifers of 
Southern Oahu. Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time 
through the present.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to 
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. 
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and

2.2 
Existing Site Conditions 
The development is on the northeastern portion of the Ewa - Kapolei area on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The Ho`opili development area is roughly bounded by the H-1 
Interstate Freeway to the north, Fort Weaver Road to the east, the new North – South 
Road (Kualakai Parkway) to the west and the new East – West Road 
(Keahumoa Parkway) to the south. The stormwater disposal project site is situated 
across Fort Weaver Road from the main Ho`opili area and is a roughly triangular area 
delineated by Fort Weaver Road and an unnamed plantation road to the west, Arizona 
Road to the north and the West Loch Branch of Naval Magazine Lualualei Explosive 
Safety Quantity Distance (ESQD) arc to the south and east.  The area is sometimes 
locally referred to as the “Blast Zone”. 
At the time of our field exploration, the site was generally being used for 
commercial farming of various vegetable and herb crops, with undeveloped pockets 
having a light to heavy cover of dry land vegetation, such as keawe, haole koa and 
grasses. Based on site observations and elevation data from Google Earth™, the 
project site generally slopes gently down towards the south with elevations ranging from 
about 33 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) along Arizona Road to about 32 feet MSL at the 
southern end of the site.

3.2 
Subsurface Permeability 
It is understood that the Ho`opili project will include a system, or systems, for the 
management of storm water runoff.  These systems may include, but are not limited to: 
detention basins, drywells or bioswales.   
Due to the low subsurface infiltration rates found in our preliminary site 
investigation for the overall development, one design scheme being investigated is the 
possibility of using the “Blast Zone” project site as a retention or detention area to 
accept the majority of the runoff generated within the Ho`opili area.  Although our 
previous exploration in this area indicated poor subsurface infiltration characteristics in 
the near-surface alluvial soils, there was a belief that more permeable materials may be 
present at depths greater than was explored in our preliminary investigation.

The field permeability testing indicates that the coralline materials have a 
coefficient of permeability, k, on the order of about 2.3 x 10-2 centimeters per second.  
Considering the coralline materials to be in a confined aquifer situation and using the 
method presented in “Groundwater and Wells, 2nd. Edition”, our analyses indicate that a 
drywell penetrating about 70 feet into the coralline materials with a diameter ranging 
between 12 and 24 inches should be able to accept between 0.9 and 1.2 million gallons 
per day.  It should be noted that this calculated value is based on testing conducted in 
undeveloped boreholes and does not allow for head losses associated with well 
screens, clogging or piping losses. 
The actual production disposal wells should be thoroughly developed and should 
consider screen losses in the design.  A regular maintenance program should be 
developed and implemented to address long term clogging losses.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by the drilling and sampling of 
three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 19A through 21A, to depths of about 102 feet 
below the existing ground surface using a truck-mounted drilling rig with continuous flight 
augers and rotary drilling tools. The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field and monitored the drilling operations on a near-continuous 
basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification 
System, as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1 and Soil Classification Log Key, 
Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the 
Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-3.3.  
Soil samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler or a 3-inch OD Modified California Sampler with liners using 
a 140 pound hammer with a 30-inch drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of 
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication 
“Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by 
the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used to 
describe the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical

were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of

soils.  Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plate B-1.

samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the coralline materials and to aid in 
soil classification. Graphic presentations of the test results are provided on Plates B-2 and

Field Permeability Tests 
The permeability characteristics of the coralline materials at the project site were 
evaluated by performing five constant head permeability tests at selected depths in the 
drilled borings. 
The field permeability tests were performed by pulling up the rotary casing to 
expose a length of open bore hole and pumping water into the boring at a metered rate. 
The head build up in the boring was measured and held at a constant level until the supply 
of water was exhausted.  
The permeability test data and analyses are presented on Plates C-1.1 through 
C-5.2.
