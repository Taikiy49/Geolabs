INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Proposed 8 Inch Water Main project along South Kalaheo Avenue
in the Kailua area on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of
the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design
input only and may not be appropriate to form the basis for construction cost estimates
nor for construction bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located along South Kalaheo Avenue near Kailua Bay on the
windward side of the island of Oahu Hawaii It is proposed to install approximately
2 500 lineal feet of new 8 inch water main along a portion of South Kalaheo Avenue
We anticipate that the depth of the water main invert will generally be approximately
6 feet below the existing ground surface with deeper inverts at some localized areas
The proposed new water main alignment starts from the intersection of South
Kalaheo and Kuulei Avenues runs along South Kalaheo Avenue to the intersection of
South Kalaheo Avenue and Kailua Road The approximate location of the proposed
alignment is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Because of the anticipated relatively shallow groundwater table dewatering of
the trench excavations may be necessary for the installation of the new water main
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3283 00 Page 3
Therefore the groundwater conditions and the relative permeability of the subsurface
soils will be the major geotechnical factors for design of this project
We understand that as part of this project it is desired to obtain an overview of
the electrical resistivity of the subsurface soils and their associated corrosivity rating
In general the scope of this work would conform to the requirements provided in the
Water System External Corrosion Control Standards Volume 3 dated 1991 and
subsequent amendments and additions

near Kailua Bay on the island of Oahu Hawaii The shoreline is approximately 400 to
500 feet away from South Kalaheo Avenue on the makai side The new water main
alignment starts from the intersection of South Kalaheo and Kuulei Avenues runs along

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling nine borings to depths
ranging between 8 and 15 feet below the existing ground surface Our borings were
drilled in the grass lawn adjacent to the shoulder area on the mauka side of South
Kalaheo Avenue The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan

encountered tannish brown medium dense fine sands in the upper 10 feet This was
underlain by very dense gray sands extending to the maximum depth of the borings
Groundwater was encountered in some of the drilled borings at depths ranging
from about 6 to 8 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field
exploration The groundwater levels corresponded to approximate elevations between
0 and 4 feet MSL It should be noted that groundwater levels can fluctuate depending
on factors such as tidal fluctuations seasonal rainfall groundwater withdrawal and or
injection and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions at the project site
consist generally of medium dense to very dense sands extending to the maximum
depth of the borings Relatively shallow groundwater conditions were generally
encountered during our field exploration Based on our evaluation of the subsurface
conditions with respect to the proposed water main construction the principal
geotechnical engineering considerations for the proposed 8 Inch Water Main project
include the following
1 excavation conditions and the potential need for support of the excava
tions along the proposed water main alignment
2 general dewatering conditions for the water main trench excavation and
the potential effects on the surrounding structures and
3 bedding requirements for support of the water main pipe
We understand that the water main pipe inverts will generally be about 6 feet
below the existing ground surface and the trench will be at a depth of about 7 feet
Deeper inverts may be required at localized areas when traversing other existing
utilities in the area Trenches extending to depths greater than 6 feet below the surface
will likely encounter the groundwater table and will likely require dewatering during
construction to install the proposed water main n addition shoring of the trenches will
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3283 00 Page 8
also be necessary to support the sides of the trenches Where dewatering of the
trenches will be necessary we believe that an adequately designed cut off wall such
as sheet pile shoring should be considered to reduce the influence of the dewatering
on adjacent existing structures due to the relatively high permeability of the subsoils
Recommendations addressing the above geotechnical engineering consider
ations and other geotechnical aspects of the proposed new water main project are
discussed in the following sections of this report
Trench Excavation

Our field exploration disclosed that the site generally is underlain by medium

groundwater conditions between 6 and 8 feet below the surface were encountered
Excavation Method
It is anticipated that conventional earthmoving equipment may be used to
excavate the trenches The method and equipment to be used for the
excavation and the installation of the water main should be determined by the
contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavated
soils should be stockpiled no closer than a horizontal distance equal to the depth
of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for
ground movement

Excavation Support
Based on the anticipated invert elevation of the new water main excavation of
the utility trenches to a depth of about 7 feet below the existing ground surface
will be required Based on the near surface soil conditions encountered in our
borings we believe that shoring of the sides of the trenches will be necessary
to protect personnel working in the trench Because the proposed new water
main will be along the roadway sloping of the sides of the trenches will not be
possible All trench excavation dewatering and construction works should
conform to Federal State and other safety requirements
Temporary shoring of the water main trenches will likely require the use of sheet
pile shoring especially where dewatering of the trenches will be necessary
However it should be noted that the proposed water main will likely traverse
other utility lines therefore care should be exercised to avoid truncating the
existing utility lines if sheet piles are to be used for shoring If sheet pile shoring
is used it would also serve as a cut off wall for the trench dewatering opera
tions Therefore to use the sheet piles or other mean as a cut off wall it should
be installed to a sufficient depth to reduce the potential for areal ground
subsidence and to reduce the amount of water to be dewatered within the trench
due to the relatively high permeability of the subsoils For preliminary design of
cantilever sheet pile shoring lateral earth pressures due to an equivalent fluid
weighing 35 pounds per cubic foot p c f may be used above the groundwater
may be used below the groundwater level to resist lateral loads
The contractor s excavation support and shoring system used must comply with
all applicable safety requirements It is recommended that a qualified
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3283 00 Page 10
geotechnical engineer be retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the
shoring system used The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should
be made the sole responsibility of the contractor His representative who should
be required to be continuously present on site during excavation and construc
tion work will have the best opportunity to promptly observe any changing
conditions during construction such as unforeseen subsoils unexpectedly high
groundwater table inappropriate construction sequence or techniques etc
which may affect shoring stability
Even if good construction procedures are followed some movement of the
shoring system and the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth
stresses during excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is
difficult to estimate accurately the magnitude of ground movement The
magnitude of ground movement also depend greatly upon workmanship such
as how quickly and tightly the shoring supports are installed the subsoil
conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be
installed properly and as early as practical and if necessary the adjacent
ground should be continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other
indications of movements with instruments
Dewatering
Since the excavation trenches will be about 7 feet deep and groundwater was
encountered at 6 to 8 feet below the existing ground surface we anticipate that
localized dewatering may be required for the installation of the new water main For
trenches that extend below the high ground water levels dewatering with a cut off wall

is recommended Therefore we recommend that dewatering provisions be included
in the contract documents for the proposed construction
Since the excavation dewatering will involve discharge of groundwater from the
dewatering operation into the adjacent drainage system a National Pollutant Discharge
Elimination System NPDES permit will be necessary The contractor should consult
their independent consultant or the State of Hawaii Department of Health for the latest
regulations and information pertaining to the NPDES permit application
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection

should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on the conditions encountered The dewatering method
selected should have minimal impact on the existing groundwater levels surrounding
the proposed excavation
Dewatering operations should be performed such that only clear water is
pumped Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in a blowing or
heaving of the excavation bottom or sides
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents
should be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural
groundwater table and or to exclude the water from the excavation to allow construction
of the proposed force main replacement structures under safe and dry conditions
It should be noted that the subsoil conditions at the site consist of medium dense
to dense sands which are generally highly permeable soils Because the proposed
new water main is located in a residential area and along a major roadway the

Subsoil Permeability
The in situ permeability of the underlying subsoils near the proposed invert
elevation of the new water main was evaluated by performing two percolation
tests to provide data for design of the proposed dewatering A constant head
percolation test was conducted in Boring No 1 and a falling head percolation
test was conducted in Boring No 7 The results of our percolation tests are
presented on Plates C 1 and C 2 of Appendix C in this report A hydraulic
conductivity of 0 05 and 0 002 centimeters per second were obtained from the
percolation tests results However it should be noted that the permeability of
the in situ soils may be highly variable and the actual permeabilities encountered
by excavations may vary in terms of orders of magnitude

Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils

coordinated with the shoring installation system such that the excavation stability

Any groundwater drawdown outside the excavation will cause settlement

Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering of adjacent and

drawdown resulting in settlements and potential damage to existing utility lines
and other existing adjacent structures
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important
design consideration For pipes bearing on the medium dense to very dense sands
a minimum of 6 inches of compacted granular bedding material should be provided
below the pipe If soft loose soils are encountered at the invert elevations an additional
18 inch of No 3B Fine gravel wrapped in a filter fabric Mirafi 140N or equivalent
should be provided below the bedding layer for more uniform support A typical section
of the trench is presented on Plate 3
The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width of
one fourth the outside pipe diameter and should extend half way up the sides of the
pipe The bedding material should consist of clean granular material such as No 3B
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3283 00 Page 15
Fine gravel Prior to placement of bedding material the excavated trench bottom
should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii to confirm that firm materials
are exposed at the bottom of the trench or if the bedding should be extended deeper
as recommended in this section
Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Standard
Specifications for Public Works Construction September 1986 The first zone of
backfill extending from the bedding material to at least 12 inches above the top of the
pipes should consist of select free draining well graded granular material to reduce the
compaction effort required and resulting stresses on the pipe The granular material
should be well graded less than 1 inch in maximum size and should have less than
10 percent passing the No 200 sieve Compaction by water ponding or jetting may be
allowed provided the granular material is self draining such as No 3B Fine gravel
The trench backfill from 12 inches above the top of the pipes to within 2 feet
below the finished subgrade may consist of the excavated on site sandy soils The first
lift of backfill should be thoroughly water tamped to force the sandy soils into the
underlying gravel layer thus reducing the potential for future downward migration of the
fine sands which may cause ground subsidence resulting in pavement distress The
backfill should be placed in thin lifts normally 8 inches in loose lift thickness with each
lift mechanically compacted to a minimum 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 The trench backfill from 2 feet below the finished subgrade
elevation up to the subgrade elevation should consist of select borrow compacted to
not less than 95 percent relative compaction
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3283 00 Page 16
Precautions should be taken against the occurrence of pipeline floatation before
and during trench backfilling due to possible influx of large quantities of groundwater
into the trenches It should be noted that the uplift buoyant forces due to groundwater
influx may lift empty or even partially filled pipes
Earth Pressure Loads on Pipe
Loads on buried conduits are influenced by the width of the trench the size of
the pipes the unit weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance between the
backfill material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads on the buried
water main pipes an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot p c f for
backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0 4 between the backfill and trench
sidewall are recommended Earth pressure forces upon the pipe increase rapidly with
increased width of the trench Therefore the width of the water line trench should be
kept to a minimum Traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be considered since
the proposed water line alignment is located along travelled ways
Settlements
Primary settlement of the new water main construction is normally caused by the
difference in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier
compacted backfill material placed over the water pipes The net increase in loading
will cause settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert Based on our
calculations primary settlement on the order of less than 1 inch is anticipated for the
project
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good
workmanship will be engaged during construction Additional settlement could occur
if improper trench support or dewatering procedures are used Therefore we believe
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3283 00 Page 17
that the proposed water main should be designed with sufficient flexibility to
accommodate possible settlements
Existing Utility Lines
Since the proposed water main alignment is located along a major roadway in
a developed residential neighborhood it is anticipated that many existing utility lines
may be encountered during construction of the new water main Therefore we
recommend that all existing utility lines along the proposed water line alignment be
located accurately prior to construction and that an adequate program be devised to
reduce potential damage to the existing utility lines during construction The contractor
has the sole responsibility to devise and implement an effective program to protect the
utilities during construction
Corrosion Control
Chemical analyses including pH value and minimum resistivity were performed
on selected soil samples obtained from the drilled borings to provide additional data for
corrosivity evaluation The soil samples were retrieved at depths ranging from 5 to 7
feet below the existing ground surface corresponding to approximately the proposed
invert elevation of the new water main
The pH value of the soil samples was obtained in general accordance with the
test procedures described in ASTM Test Designation D 2976 The resistivity testing
was performed utilizing the Soil Box method in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1125 using a Nilson model 400 Soil Resistivity Meter Results of the pH
value and minimum resistivity are presented on Plate D 1 of Appendix D
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil
characteristic with regard to corrosivity of the soil In general the lower the resistivity
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3283 00 Page 18
the greater the potential for corrosion of buried metallic pipes Conversely the higher
the resistivity the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion

APPENDIX C
Field Permeability Testing
Two permeability tests were performed in two selected borings during our field
exploration in order to evaluate the in situ permeability of the subsoil Both constant
and falling head test methods were utilized The tests were performed by advancing
a 3 inch D solid casing with flushed bottom to the desired depths
The constant head test was performed by pumping water into the cased borehole
until water surface reached and remained at equilibrium for a sufficient period of time
The depth to the water level and the rate of pumping were then measured
The falling head test was performed by measuring the rate of water level drop
after filling up the casing with water
The test data and the corresponding calculated permeabilities of the subsoils are
presented on Plates C 1 and C 2
