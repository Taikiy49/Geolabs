INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the pier and infrastructure improvements in support of the proposed
Domestic Commercial Fishing Village located at Honolulu Harbor Piers 36 through 38
on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design
of the pier bulkhead site grading pavements and infrastructure installation only
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3268 10 Page 8
The project site is located on the coastal plain of southern Oahu The coastal
plain was built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano which forms the eastern
two thirds of the island of Oahu The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation
of alluvium derived from erosion of the volcano interbedded with coral reefs and
associated deposits
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation Most of the coastal plains were developed during the
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly As the glaciers grew
and advanced less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels
fell below the present stands of the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an
excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose to above the present
sea
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations
The project site is generally underlain by deposits of calcareous sediments and
alluvial deposits associated with nearby Kapalama Channel and lagoonal deposits
from Honolulu Harbor A surficial layer of man made fill was placed over these deposits
for the development of Honolulu Harbor within the last century

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The borings drilled generally encountered near surface fills with variable
thicknesses ranging from about 4 to 26 feet below the existing ground surface across
the project site Boulders were encountered in the near surface fills throughout the
project area The fills were underlain by relatively compressible lagoonal sediments
ranging from about 2 to 20 feet in thickness Relatively stiff alluvium and dense
coralline detritus deposits were encountered below the lagoonal sediments extending to
the maximum depth explored of about 117 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Based on our laboratory test results of the soil samples retrieved from the project
site the near surface clayey fill soils are moderately expansive and the subsurface
soils have very high corrosion potential In addition the soft lagoonal deposits
encountered at the site are potentially compressible and will consolidate under the new
fill loads proposed Therefore ground surface settlements should be expected at the
site as a result of the filling operations Based on the results of our exploration the
principal geotechnical engineering considerations for the proposed Domestic
Commercial Fishing Village project include the following
e settlement of the underlying compressible lagoonal sediments due to
placement of new fills to attain the final design grades
e soil parameters for design of the earth retaining bulkhead structures

fabric as recommended above
Lateral loads may be resisted by a combination of soil friction between the base
of foundations and the supporting subgrade and passive earth pressures exerted
against near vertical foundation faces A base friction factor of 0 35 and a
passive earth pressure due to an equivalent fluid weighing 350 pcf above the
groundwater level and 200 pcf below the groundwater level may be used for

upper 12 inches should be neglected

Drainage
Headwall structures should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 cubic feet of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
wrapped around with a filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent discharging to
weepholes Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone should consist of
granular fill material less than 3 inches in maximum dimension Unless covered
by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of
non expansive impervious material to reduce water infiltration behind the
headwaill structures
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines such as sewer water and drain lines will be
required for the project In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes
We understand that the inverts for some utility lines may be as low as 4 feet MLLW
The borings indicate that the soft lagoonal deposits are present at as high as about
1 foot MLLW Therefore it is highly likely that the soft compressible deposits will be
encountered at some utility inverts If soft loose soils are encountered at the proposed
pipe inverts an additional 18 inches of No 3B Fine gravel wrapped in a filter fabric
Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be provided below the bedding layer for more
uniform support Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel should
be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes or groundwater
level to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce compaction of the
backfill thus reducing the potential for damaging the pipes

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 101 through 104 at the approximate locations
shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3 The depth of the four borings was about
41 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled with a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the
Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Relatively undisturbed soft soil samples were retrieved by hydraulically pushing a
3 inch O D thin wall Shelby tube into the soil formation The hydraulic pressures needed
to advance the sampler are recorded as the Penetration Resistance in pounds per square
inch psi per foot of penetration on the Logs of Borings
Pocket penetrometer and torvane shear tests were performed on selected
cohesive soil samples retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an
indication of the unconfined compressive strength of the sample The torvane shear test
provides a quick estimate of the undrained shear strength of the soil Results of the
pocket penetrometer and torvane shear tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths

Geotechnical Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX C
Geotechnical Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Twelve Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected
samples of the soils to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the test results are shown on Plates C 1 and C 2
Results of the tests are also summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Five sieve analyses ASTM C 136 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate C 3
One triaxial consolidated undrained CU compression test ASTM D 4767 was
performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate the drained and undrained shear
strengths of the soils The test results are presented on Plate C 4
Two triaxial unconsolidated undrained UU compression tests ASTM D 2850
were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained shear strength of the
soils The test results are presented on Plates C 5 and C 6
Three direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed to evaluate the shear
strength of the materials tested Results of the direct shear tests are presented on
Plates C 7 through C 9
Six consolidation tests with time rates ASTM D 2435 were performed on the
potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the
materials encountered Results of the consolidation tests are presented on Plates C 10
through C 15
Two California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on bulk
samples collected for pavement design Results of the CBR tests are presented on
Plates C 16 and C 17

Four one inch ring swell tests were performed on the near surface soils to evaluate

proposed piers The results are summarized on Plate C 19
