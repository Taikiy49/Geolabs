SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Komohana Heights Sunrise Estates Subdivision Kukuau Sewer
project located in the Hilo area on the Island of Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for
the sewer line installation project These recommendations are intended for the design
of pipe bedding trench backfill and manhole structures only In addition discussions
on the excavation requirements of the project are included in this report for information
purposes The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

The originally proposed project involves the construction of approximately 5 500

the project would include 1 abandoning Sunrise Ridge Sewer Pump Station SPS and

4 connecting the new gravity sewer to the existing sewer at Kukuau Street Based on
the information provided we understand that a new gravity sewer to include house lots
along Inia Lane has been deleted from the scope of work under this project The layout
of the proposed sewer improvements is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Based on the information provided the new gravity sewer lines will consist of
8 inch diameter Polyvinyl Chloride PVC pipes The depth of cover along the new
sewer line alignments generally ranges from about 3 to 13 feet below the existing

SECTION 2 0 SITE CHARACTERIZATION
2 1 Regional Geology
The project site is located on the northeastern flank of the Mauna Loa Shield
Volcano which has been built up by basaltic lava flows These lava flows are of the
Pleistocene Epoch and Recent Age and belong to the Ka u Volcanic Series The lava
flows were subsequently covered by volcanic ash deposits locally referred to as
Pahala Ash Pahala Ash is an air laid deposit of volcanic ash from the Hamakua
Volcanic Series of Mauna Loa Mountain during the Pleistocene Epoch Pahala Ash
generally has high in situ moisture contents and low in situ densities The ash is
characterized as having thixotropic properties i e it is susceptible to significant loss of
strength when remolded under equipment vehicular or seismic loads
The lava formation encountered at the site appears to be of pahoehoe flow
which is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal
structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe
lavas Cavities are formed when lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities blisters and pockets Lava tubes are
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like
structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow
cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent

3 1 Sewer Line Installation
Based on the competent subsurface conditions anticipated along the new sewer
line alignments it is our opinion that the following guidelines may be used in the design
and installation of the proposed sewer construction project

Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the
pipes the unit weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance between the
backfill material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads on the buried
sewer pipes we recommend that an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic
foot pcf be used for the compacted backfill material within the trench excavation

Trench Bottom Preparation
Prior to the placement of bedding material the trench bottom should be observed
by a representative from Geolabs to confirm whether firm materials are exposed at
the bottom of the excavated trench If clinker materials are exposed at the bottom
of the excavated trench the trench bottom should be recompacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Where basalt rock formation is exposed at the
bottom of the excavated trench the trench bottom should be proof rolled with a
rammax or similar equipment to help detect and collapse near surface cavities
and or voids
Cavities encountered during the trench bottom preparation should be ripped open
and cleaned of loose materials The cavity should be backfilled with well graded
granular fill materials in lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness The
backfill should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Alternatively lean
concrete or flowable fill may be used to backfill the cavity Due to the porous nature
of the clinker materials and pahoehoe lavas underlying the project site we
recommend that the backfill be allowed to set prior to pumping additional lean
concrete or flowable fill if needed Due to the potential for presence of cavities at
the site we recommend that contingencies be included in the budget estimates for
over excavation of these areas and compacted fill placement to account for
variations in the over excavation and backfill quantities

3 2 Excavation
Based on our field exploration at the project area we envision that excavations
for the new sewer lines will encountered hard to very hard basalt rock formation
interbedded with clinker layers within the depths of the sewer trench and manhole
structures
In general the method and equipment to be used for excavation should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
Because basalt formation was encountered at the ground surface trench excavation for
the sewer line installation likely will involve excavation in the basalt formation and will
require hard ripping or the use of hoerams Care must be exercised by the contractor to
avoid over ripping of the basalt formation which would disrupt the structure of the rock
formation resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for foundations in its vicinity
The excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance
equal to the depth of the excavation measured from the outside edge of the excavation
in order to reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement In addition the
excavated soils should be stockpiled at least 15 feet away from underground utilities to
reduce the potential for ground movement or subsidence which may damage the
existing underground utility lines
We anticipate that excavation depths up to about 15 feet may be required for the
project construction In general we believe that excavations into the hard rock
formation may be left unsupported at a near vertical slope However for the safety of
personnel working inside the trench and the surcharge load from the vehicular traffic
we recommend that the trench excavation be properly shored especially near the top of
the trench and where clinker deposits are encountered to reduce the potential for earth
movements toward the trench excavation

The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His her representative who should be required to be
continuously present on site during excavation and construction work will have the best
opportunity to promptly observe changing conditions during construction such as
unforeseen subsoils unexpectedly high groundwater table inappropriate construction
sequence or techniques etc which may affect shoring stability
However it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system
and the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during
excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to accurately
estimate the magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon
workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are
installed the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical if necessary and that the adjacent ground should be
continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of movements with
instruments
3 3 Existing Utility Lines
Since the general project site is in a well developed area it is anticipated that
numerous existing utility lines underground and overhead may be encountered during
construction of the new sewer lines Therefore we recommend that all existing utility
lines along the proposed sewer line alignments be located accurately prior to
construction and that an adequate program be devised to reduce the potential for
damage to the existing utility lines during construction The contractor has the sole
responsibility to devise and implement an effective program to protect the utilities during
construction

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the new sewer line alignments were explored by
drilling and sampling six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 extending to
depths of about 21 to 21 5 feet below the existing pavement ground surface The
borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight
solid stem augers and coring tools The approximate locations of the borings drilled are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 0 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
