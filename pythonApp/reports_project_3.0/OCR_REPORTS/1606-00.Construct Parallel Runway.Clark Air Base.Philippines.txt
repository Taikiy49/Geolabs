SUMMARY OF RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration disclosed that the general
runway site and borrow areas are underlain by medium
to fine sand which are suitable for embankment
construction pavement and foundation support The
field exploration and laboratory tests indicated that
the on site sandy soils can provide relatively high
CBR and subgrade modulus values
High water levels were encountered in the northern
half of the proposed runway embankment The
underlying sandy soils were found to have loose to
medium dense consistency within the upper 10 to
15 foot depth Although the near surface soil data
indicate suitable bearing support for the proposed 5
to 15 foot high fill embankment under a static loading
condition there is a critical concern due to the
effect of earthquake vibration on the loose sands
under water in an area of high seismicity

shallow depth boring data The preliminary analysis

embankment

along the proposed runway to obtain the necessary
information to complete the required seismic analysis

INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of a geotechnical engineering
investigation performed for the proposed Parallel Runway and related
structures at Clark Air Base The project site is located about 50
miles northwest of Manila on the Island of Luzon in the Republic of the
Philippines
The general location of the project site is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate l
PURPOSE AND SCOPE OF WORK
The purpose of our investigation was to evaluate the subsurface
conditions at the site to provide pertinent soil data such as shear
strength compressibility permeability CBR value subgrade modulus
and soil classification for the design of the proposed parallel runway
pavement site grading and foundations of the related structures
The following were the main objectives of this investigation
1 To determine the suitability of the on site soils near the
proposed finished grade along the runway taxiway and overrun
areas for pavement support
This involves the determination of the classification density
and modulus or CBR of the in situ soil
2 To determine the compaction characteristics of the on site cut
and off site borrow soils including maximum dry density
optimum moisture and laboratory CBR value
3 To evaluate the general suitability of available aggregate
sources for base course and concrete aggregate

I D 1773 Page 3
4 To determine the settlement characteristics of the in situ soil
under the loading of the proposed new fills
5 To determine the soil parameters for the foundation design of
the new buildings included in the project
6 To determine the percolation permeability rates of the existing
near surface soils for use in drainage design calculations
7 To develop recommendations for site preparation excavation
embankment construction and pavement subgrade design
8 To provide comments and recommendations on other geotechnical
considerations at the site including drainage slope stability
and erosion protection
The scope of the investigation was performed in general accordance
with our proposal of and included a review of
available geologic literature and soil reports of the general area
field reconnaissance drilling and sampling of 72 borings excavation of
28 test pits field CBR testing laboratory soils testing field

laboratory data and report preparation

and laboratory testing are presented in the appendices of the report

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration disclosed that the general runway site and
borrow areas are underlain by medium to fine sand alluvial deposits
generally in a loose to medium dense condition The on site sandy soils
are suitable for embankment construction and can provide suitable
support for pavement and light building foundations
The field exploration program concentrated on the shallow
near surface soil condition which would determine the pavement support
and corresponding pavement thickness required Although the
near surface field data obtained indicated suitable bearing support for
the airfield pavement the following conditions indicate that seismic
activity may be a significant concern
A High Water Level Groundwater level was found to be close to
the existing ground surface in the lower northern half of the
runway Based on the field exploration it appears that the
entire base area may be underlain by a perched water table
The water table is expected to fluctuate greatly between the
rainy monsoon and dry seasons
B Loose to Medium Density Soil Consistency The underlying sandy
soils were found to have loose to medium dense consistency
within the depth of the borings which were generally limited to
10 feet Only three borings were drilled to depths of 50 feet
and indicated variable consistency of the deeper subsoils The
actual densities of the underlying deeper soils will be a
critical item in the future study of a more detailed seismic
evaluation

Liquefaction Potential
When a loose saturated sand deposit is subject to vibration such
as during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in
volume causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is
unable to occur rapidly enough due to the large extent of the sand
deposit to dissipate the build up in pore pressure the effective
stress internal strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained
vibration the pore water pressure build up could equal the overburden
pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and
causing it to act as a viscous fluid This phenomenon is called
liquefaction The major factors affecting the liquefaction
characteristics of a soil are
1 Grain size distribution Fine and uniform sands are more prone
to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2 Initial relative density Loose sands are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential decreases with increased
relative density
3 Magnitude and duration of vibration Liquefaction potential
increases with increases in the magnitude and duration of the
earthquake

The present runway area is underlain by loose to medium density sand
and gravel The thickness of the loose portion of the deposit varies
from about 10 to 15 feet at the higher end of runway and increases up to
at least 40 feet in the lower end of runway area
A preliminary liquefaction analysis was performed using the
empirical method developed by Seed and Idriss Simplified Procedure for
Evaluating Soil Liquefaction Potential J Soil Mech and Found Div
No 1273 1971 The analysis used seismic
data from Reference No 1 indicating a maximum earthquake magnitude of
6 6 and a peak ground acceleration value of 0 20g at the site following
a seismic criteria of 50 probability of not being exceeded in 50
years This is based on an earthquake originating from the San Antonio
and or Iba Fracture Zones which varies from about 18 to 25 miles from
the site
The liquefaction analysis indicates that after site filling the
loose to medium density sands below the runway which would generally be
covered with a total of about 10 feet of fill may have sufficient
surcharge to resist liquefaction The calculated factor safety against
liquefaction is generally greater than 1 0 However below the sloping
portions of the embankment and within the temporary ponding areas where
the total fill height would be decreased from more than 10 feet to zero
the factor of safety against liquefaction would be reduced due to the
reduction in surcharge pressure Based on the present data our
analysis indicates the loose sands below this sloping area are
susceptible to liquefaction during an earthquake with a maximum ground
acceleration of 0 20g
GEOLABS HAWAII
I D 1773 Page 16
Liquefaction of the underlying sands would cause extensive
deformation and slope instability of the compacted fill for the runway
thus very serious damage to structural improvements near the edge of the
fill area could occur To minimize liquefaction potential
densification of the loose sands below the fill slope would be
required To achieve the required densification we believe special
deep compaction methods such as those listed below should be considered
a Vibrating Probe A 30 inch 0 D open ended pipe pile is
vibrated by a vibratory pile driver into the loose sands causing
densification of the sands
b Vibroflotation A special crane suspended cylindrical
penetrator vibroflot is advanced into the loose sand by a
combination of vibration and water jetting When the vibroflot
reaches the desired densification depth the water jet is closed
and the vibroflot is withdrawn with vibration while sand or
gravel backfill is dumped into the hole from the ground surface
c Dynamic Consolidation Heavy Tamping Heavy weights ranging
from 10 to 40 tons are dropped from a height of 50 to 130 feet
causing localized liquefaction and subsequent settlement
densification of the loose sands
d Compaction Piles Displacement piles are driven into the ground
at close spacing Granular backfill is then introduced into the
hole and compacted as the pile is withdrawn
GEOLABS HAWAII
I D 1773 Page 17
The method used would depend primarily on the cost effectiveness of
each alternative equipment availability and the results of the
additional investigation Our analysis indicates that densification of
the underlying soil to about 75 relative density would provide a factor
of safety against liquefaction of about 1 15 In our judgement each of
the above densification methods could increase the relative density of
the site sand to about 75 The critical area appears to be near the
toe of the slope area where the height of the proposed fill is limited

anticipated

edge of the runway embankment
Ground settlements caused by the new fill are estimated to be
about 1 to 2 inches in the existing ground which is 20 feet away
from the edge of the fill Beyond the 20 foot area away from
the edge of the embankment area the ground settlements would
reduce drastically Therefore it is our opinion that ground
settlements at the adjacent existing taxiway and structure areas
would be minimal

Additional Seismic Study
The above findings regarding the liquefaction potential and
earthquake settlements were based on very limited data derived from only
a few deep borings and therefore should be considered very
preliminary It is essential that additional field data on the in situ
density distribution of the deep sand deposit be obtained to better
define the potential for large ground settlements during earthquakes

APPENDIX Cc
Laboratory Testing
A A OS A ase
Most of the moisture content unit weight and classification tests
were performed by a local testing laboratory in the Philippines under our
supervision The strength tests and aggregate quality tests were
performed in our laboratory in Honolulu
The moisture content unit weight and Atterberg Limit
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the
classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of the
moisture content and unit weight tests are presented on the log of
borings at the appropriate sample depths The Atterberg Limits are
summarized on Plate C 6
Forty three sieve analysis tests were performed on samples of the
underlying sandy soils to evaluate its grain size distribution The test
results are summarized on Plates C 1 through C 11l and are plotted on
Plates C 12 through C 28
Five specific gravity tests were performed on typical samples of
the underlying soil The test results are presented on Plates C l
through C ll
Six laboratory CBR tests were performed on bulk samples of the
near surface soils to evaluate the suitability of the soil for pavement
support The test results are summarized on Plates C 9 and C 10
GEOCLABS HAWAII
Appendix C Cont d Page 2
Two full series CBR tests were performed on bulk samples from
the proposed borrow areas in accordance with the method outlined in AFM
88 6 Chapter 2 in order to evaluate the relationships between the
moisture density and CBR value of the compacted soil The test results
are plotted on Plates C29 through C 34
Six direct shear tests were performed on saturated samples of the
soil to evaluate the shear strength parameters of the soils The test
results are presented on Plates C 35 through C 40
Sulfate soundness abrasion absorption and bulk specific gravity
tests were performed on aggregate samples from a source in Manila to
evaluate the general suitability of the aggregate for base course and
concrete The test results are summarized on Plate C 41
Four laboratory permeability tests were performed on remolded
samples of the sandy soils to evaluate the permeability of the soils
The test results are summarized on Plate C 42
