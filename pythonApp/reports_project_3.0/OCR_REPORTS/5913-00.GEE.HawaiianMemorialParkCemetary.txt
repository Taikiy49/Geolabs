SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Hawaiian Memorial Park Cemetery Large Capacity
Cesspool Remediation project in Kaneohe on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering
recommendations derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of infrastructure
installation and site grading The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
Regulations promulgated by the US Environmental Protection Agency and the
Hawaii Department of Health prohibits the continued use of large capacity cesspools
At present the Hawaiian Memorial Park Cemetery in Kaneohe on the Island of Oahu
Hawaii is served by three existing large capacity cesspools at various facilities within the
cemetery The proposed project is being designed to bring the cemetery into
compliance It is anticipated that the facilities served by two of the existing cesspools
can be connected to an existing gravity sewer system for off site treatment and
disposal However due to the relatively low elevation of the maintenance yard cesspool
in comparison to its surrounding areas connection to the gravity sewer will be difficult
The installation of septic tank and absorption bed is being considered for this location in
the cemetery
In addition to the closure and replacement of the one cesspool preliminary
design indicates that connection to the existing gravity sewer system may require
excavation to depths of as much as 25 feet below the existing ground surface

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau
Volcano reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
The project site is situated within the rift zone and caldera complex of Koolau
During the active phase of Koolau hydrothermal action altered the rocks within the rift
and caldera complex to easily eroded materials As erosion on the windward side of the
Koolau dome breached the complex broad amphitheater headed valley formed and
coalesced to form the Koolau Pali Deep deposits of interbedded alluvium and
colluvium accumulated at the base of the pali and along the valley walls The site is on
a broad pediment of alluvial and colluvial deposits at the base of the pali between Pali
and Likelike Highways

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
a total of three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of
about 10 5 to 40 5 feet below the existing ground surface Boring No 3 was drilled to
the depth of about 40 5 feet below ground surface to provide information related to the
installation of the proposed gravity sewer line The approximate boring locations are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In Boring Nos 1 and 2 our field exploration generally encountered a surface
layer of soft to medium stiff alluvial soil to the maximum depth explored of 10 5 feet
below the existing ground In Boring No 3 the near surface alluvial soils were stiffer
and somewhat drier The alluvial soils extended to a depth of about 35 feet below the
surface where they were underlain by soft to medium stiff residual soils The apparent
moisture content in the residual soils was higher
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of our field
exploration However groundwater levels can vary significantly depending on rainfall
temperature surface runoff groundwater withdrawal and other factors Previous
experience in the area of the project site indicates that there may be lenses of perched
groundwater in the alluvial soils and there may be old buried channels which serve as
conduits for transient ground water flow or for seepage

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Disposal
As previously discussed the project site currently uses existing large capacity
cesspools LCC for the treatment and disposal of sanitary wastewater generated at
various facilities within the Hawaiian Memorial Park HMP complex The primary
consideration for this project is to bring HMP into compliance with federal and state
requirements that prohibit the use of large capacity cesspools It is our understanding
that two of the existing LCC will be connected to an existing off site sewer system for
treatment and disposal through a municipal wastewater treatment plant The location
and elevation of the HMP Maintenance yard presents constraints on connection to the
municipal system Therefore it has been proposed to replace the existing LCC at this
location with a new septic system consisting of a septic tank for treatment and a
relatively shallow leach field for disposal of the septic tank effluent
We conducted a field permeability test at a depth of about 4 2 feet below the
existing ground surface The test indicated an infiltration rate of about 24 minutes per
inch Under Hawaii Department of Health Administrative Rules Chapter 11 62
Wastewater Systems the site is considered suitable for leach field or similar infiltration
disposal methods

Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great effect upon the load
supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore pipe bedding is an important design
consideration In general granular bedding consisting of 12 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the sewer line pipe
and manhole structures The granular bedding should extend beyond the sides of
the pipe a minimum width of one fourth the outside diameter of the pipe or
24 inches whichever is greater A non woven filter fabric should wrap around the
bedding material and should extend up the walls of the trench excavation to a level
of about 12 inches above the pipes or 12 inches above the groundwater level
Refer to Plate 3 for details

3 3 Sewer Line Excavation
In order to install the sewer line and manhole structures we anticipate that
excavation on the order of about 25 feet below the existing ground surface will be
required Our field exploration at the project site encountered stiff alluvial soils which
grade to soft near the sewer line invert Therefore we anticipate that conventional
excavation techniques using backhoe equipment may be considered for the planned
excavations In general we believe that interlocking steel sheet piling or braced shoring
should be used to stabilize and support the trench sidewalls
The contractor should develop and implement a monitoring program to detect
ground movement and or subsidence adjacent to the excavations which may result in
damage to nearby structures and pavements It should be noted that minor settlements
may occur during and after installation of the trench support system Therefore the
contractor should retain a qualified geotechnical engineer to design and evaluate the
shoring system used
Excavation Method
In general the contractor should determine the method and equipment used for
excavation subject to practical limits and safety considerations Based on our field
exploration and the available information the surface fill soils and alluvial soils
encountered in the borings may be excavated with conventional earthmoving
equipment
The excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance
equal to the depth of the excavation measured from the outside edge of the
excavation in order to reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement In
addition the excavated soils should be stockpiled at least 15 feet away from
existing underground utilities to reduce the potential for appreciable ground
movement or subsidence which may damage the existing underground utility line

Excavation Support
We anticipate that excavation depths up to about 10 to 20 feet may be required in
localized areas for the project construction Based on the subsurface soil
conditions encountered in the borings we believe that shoring of the sides of the
excavations will be necessary to protect personnel working in the excavations
However it should be noted that the planned excavations may traverse other utility
lines therefore care should be exercised to avoid truncating the existing utility lines
if sheet piles are to be used as shoring The contractor should carefully evaluate
the potential for bottom heave and design the shoring system accordingly
It is important to install adequate sheeting prior to the excavation and to maintain it
tightly against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation The
properly braced sheeting is essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral
movements of the adjacent ground to occur in the excavation which may result in
potential settlements or distress to adjacent structures or other improvements such
as the roads or utilities

shoring installation His her representative who should be required to be

inappropriate construction sequence or techniques etc which may adversely
affect shoring stability

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of
approximately 10 5 and 40 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate

equipped with rotary coring tools

textural examination in the field He monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3

samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts

APPENDIX C

The permeability of the on site soils was evaluated by performing one field
permeability test in Boring No P 1 at a depth of about 4 2 feet below the existing
ground surface The percolation test was performed in general accordance with the
Wastewater Systems and Chapter 10 of the Ten State s Standards to evaluate the
permeability of the subsurface soils for wastewater disposal The location of the field
permeability test is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The test was performed in a 4 inch diameter borehole Approximately 1 inch of
gravel was placed at the bottom of the borehole to protect the bottom of the hole from
scouring and sediments During testing the hole was carefully filled with water to a
depth of about 6 inches above the gravel and refilled as necessary The time intervals
and water drops were then recorded to provide data upon which the calculation of the
percolation test was based The test results are presented on Plate C 1
