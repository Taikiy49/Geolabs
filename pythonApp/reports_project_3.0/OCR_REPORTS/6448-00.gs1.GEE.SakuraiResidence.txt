REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island As volcanic
activity in Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows from Koolau Volcano banked against its
eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau known as Schofield Plateau Both Waianae
and Koolau Ranges have lost most of their original shield outlines and are now long
narrow ridges shaped largely by erosion
The rocks of the Koolau Volcano are divided into the Kailua Volcanic Series and the
Koolau Volcanic Series The lava of the Kailua Volcanic Series has been altered by
hydrodynamic action of rising steam within the ancient Koolau caldera The lava of the
Koolau Volcanic Series lies outside the caldera
The project area is generally composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of
lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series In situ weathering of the lava flows has occurred for
the last 1 to 2 million years The weathered portions of lava area detached from the basalt
rock formation and fell to form colluvial deposits along the hillsides
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring and 2 to depths of about 2 0 to 10 25 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate boring locations are shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration generally encountered a colluvial deposit consisting of very
stiff to very hard clay with sand gravel and cobbles and dense basaltic cobbles and
gravel with boulders in a clay matrix extending to the maximum depth explored of
10 25 feet below the existing ground surface
We did not encounter groundwater or seepage water in the borings at the time of
our field exploration It should be noted that groundwater levels at the project site may
fluctuate depending on seasonal precipitation and other factors Detailed descriptions of
the materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1
through A 3 in Appendix A Laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples and
the results are presented in Appendix B

The existing conditions at the project site and the potential cause of seepage water

Existing Conditions and Potential Cause of Seepage Water
We drilled the borings at the rear of the lot adjacent to the crawl space area where
wet soil conditions were previously observed In addition the borings extended up to about
8 feet below the wet soil condition level The borings encountered colluvial deposits
generally consisting of sand gravel cobbles and boulders within a clay matrix The
colluvial materials were damp to moist instead of wet and saturated from seepage water
We did not encounter groundwater or seepage water in the borings It appears that the
underground seepage water may be occurring on an intermittent basis
The terrain slopes upward towards the hillside at the rear of the lot We anticipate
that colluvial material has been deposited at the base of the hillside down to the house lot
We believe that the intermittent underground seepage water may be caused from rain
water seeping into the underlying colluvial deposit along the hillside and slowly travelling
down the slope towards the lot
A downspout from the roof gutters was observed at the rear of the house Rain
water runoff from the downspout may also be contributing to the observed seepage water
Recommended Remedial Measures
To reduce the potential for underground seepage water from entering the crawl
space area at the rear of the house we recommend installing a subdrain to intercept the
seepage water The seepage water in the subdrain should be properly disposed away
from the house structure or into a drainage structure A typical subdrain detail is provided
on Plate 3
Another potential source of water may be from the existing downspout at the rear of
the house We recommend extending and tying the downspout into the subdrain system

Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the site by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring and 2 to depths of about 2 0 to 10 25 feet
below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using portable drilling equipment equipped with
continuous flight augers and coring equipment
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the borings in general accordance with
ASTM D 3550 84 and D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 to 24 inch drive are shown on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Pocket Penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides and indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
