Dispersive Clay Testing
As requested by 0 1 C C Manila additional laboratory testing was
performed on selected samples to determine whether the in situ soils
can be classified as dispersive clays Dispersive clays are clay
soils which disperse or deflocculate in the presence of water and
therefore are highly susceptible to erosion and piping
There are various types of soil tests which have been used to
attempt to identify dispersive clays However in the present
state of the art all of the tests have some limitations and variable
degree of success have been reported The generally recommended
procedure is to perform a minimum of two 2 different types of tests
on each sample in which the results can be compared
Parsons International
W O 1230 00
Page 3
The following three types of tests were performed in our
laboratory
1 Double Hydrometers This test consists of first performing a
standard hydrometer tests using a dispersing agent A second
test is performed without mechanical agitation and without a
dispersing agent The percent of dispersion is expressed as
the amount of particles smaller than determined in
the second test divided by the amount of particles smaller
than determined with the standard hydrometer test
2 Pinhole Erosion Tests This test is performed by causing
water to flow through a small 1 16 inch diameter hole
punched through a 4 6 inch long sample at water heads varying
from 2 to 15 inches The condition of the water flowing out
of the sample clear cloudy dark is used to determine if
the soil is dispersive intermediate or non dispersive
53 Chemical Tests Chemical analysis of the pore fluid in the

total salts and dispersion potential In general dispersion

Clarification of Trench Drain Location
Aircraft Hangar and Parking Apron
MCON P 516
NAS Cubi Point Philippines
Our investigation for the above project indicated that it was
essential that the upper portions of the soil below the slope face be
drained in order to maintain the desired factor of safety against slope
failure In our calculations we have assumed that the water surface
would be kept about 20 feet below the finished cut slope surface down to
the relocated Argonaut Highway The water surface was then assumed to
slope from 20 feet below the surface at the relocated Argonaut Highway to
5 feet below the finished grade at the toe of slope behind the hangar
area
The purpose of the trench and horizontal drains is to maintain the
water surface below the above critical level It is our opinion that the
trench drains as shown on Sheet 3 of the Civil drawings for the project
if installed and functioned properly could maintain the water level as
desired Of course if additional trench drains are installed they
would provide increased assurance that proper drainage would be
maintained Therefore we would not be opposed to addition of a trench
drain along the relocated Argonaut Highway
