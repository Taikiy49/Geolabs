INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Molokai Base Yard located within the property limits of the Molokai
Industrial Park in the District of Umipaa on the island of Molokai Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations retaining
walls below grade structures site grading and pavements only The findings and
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located at Lot Nos 9 and 10 of the Molokai Industrial Park
Subdivision Based on the information provided it is proposed
to construct a base yard for the maintenance of equipment and vehicles and the storage
of equipment vehicles and supplies for the State Department of Transportation For
storage maintenance and office operations three separate warehouse type buildings
are proposed within the base yard The majority of the site will be paved to handle the
anticipated vehicular traffic and to accommodate parking stalls Septic tanks are
proposed to handle the disposal of sewage A fuel station a washrack and oil water
separators are proposed for the project site Perimeter fence walls and low retaining
walls are proposed around the property limits

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Molokai was built during the early to middle Pleistocene Epoch by
basaltic andesitic lava flows from two shield volcanoes East Molokai Mountain and West
Molokai Mountain The project site is located at the south end of the Hoolehua Plain
between the East and West Molokai Domes The Hoolehua Plain or isthmus of Molokai
is composed of lava flows from the East Molokai Volcano which banked against the older
West Molokai Volcano
The West Molokai Volcano built a shield shaped dome of primitive basalts from a
northwest rift and a southwest rift After the dome was built about 1 400 feet above sea
level faulting dropped much of the northeast slope Prior to erosion the East Molokai
Mountain was a typical elongated basalt andesitic shield shaped dome It was built over
northwest and east trending rifts with a steep slope on the north side where the lava
flows plunged into deep water and a gentle slope on the west side where the lava flows
banked against the West Molokai dome
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell due to a lower quantity of
water available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted
global sea levels rose because of the increase in available water The land mass of
Molokai remained essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were
eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which
were both higher and lower relative to the present sea level on Molokai Subaerial

erosion of the emerging East and West Molokai Volcanoes and subsequent deposition of
the clastic debris as coalescing alluvial fans formed the present configuration of the
Hoolehua Plain
EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located at Lot Nos 9 and 10 of the Molokai Industrial Park
Subdivision These lots have been graded to relatively level
lots Roads sidewalks and the utilities within the roads have been constructed
previously The project site is located at the end of the main subdivision road
approximately 1400 feet in length which connects to Maunaloa Highway The project
site is bounded by the main subdivision road to the north a detention basin to the west
and a drainage easement to the south
The site is generally level with a ground elevation of about 74 feet MSL The
exception is at the rear southern 100 feet where the ground slopes downward to about
70 feet MSL No development is planned between the existing slope and the south
property line The dedicated industrial subdivision drainage easement is located within
the southern 25 feet of the property The site currently contains weeds and bare areas at
the surface

PERCOLATION TESTING
To obtain data pertaining to the septic tank design a falling head percolation test
was performed in Boring No 3 after soaking of the open borehole subgrade material
to evaluate the percolation of the upper soil materials The field percolation testing was
performed in general accordance with the State of Hawaii Department of Health s
Administrative Rules Chapter 2 Wastewater Systems and Chapter 10 of
the Ten States Standards to evaluate the permeability of the subsurface soils for
wastewater disposal Based on the test the percolation rate of the silty sand materials
was found to be about 40 minutes per inch The results of the test performed are
provided in Appendix C
if a higher percolation rate is desired the bottom elevation of the septic tank
should be lowered to the underlying basalt formation Due to the highly vesicular and
fractured nature of the basalt formation we anticipate higher permeability values as
evident by the water consumption rate of about 30 gpm within the borehole during the
drilling process Based on observations during our field exploration a percolation rate
of 5 minutes per inch may be used for the underlying basalt formation

Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe
that a shallow foundation system bearing on re compacted on site soils properly
compacted fill material or the underlying basalt rock formation encountered would
provide suitable foundation support for the proposed structures
Due to the dry and loose consistency of the on site soils we recommend that
footing bottoms be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4100 00 Page 8
to a minimum 90 percent relative compaction For silty cobble subgrades where
conventional compaction testing is not practical compaction should proceed on the
moisture conditioned material until a dense and unyielding surface is attained Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
lf proper compaction cannot be achieved with the on site soils at the bottom of
foundation excavations they should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials The over excavation should be backfilled with select granular material
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the foundation bottom
should be extended down to the underlying competent material
The bottom of foundations should be relatively level This may be difficult to
achieve within the silty cobble layer or the underlying basalt rock formation
Depressions may be filled with properly compacted on site soils or lean concrete
Due to the potential for presence of cavities loose zones and or voids we
recommend that a program of probing and grouting the building foundation locations be
implemented during construction A representative of Geolabs Hawaii should observe
the probing and grouting operation to evaluate the extent of cavities if encountered
Probing and grouting requirements are further discussed in the Foundation Probing
section of this report

compacted fill material an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square

where basalt rock is encountered at the foundation bottom These bearing

Foundation Probing
To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse of the
cavities below foundations and structures we recommend that a program of cavity
probing and grouting be conducted during construction at building locations
For estimating purposes we recommend that probe holes be drilled at each
isolated spread foundation location up to 50 square feet in area and at 10 foot on
centers for the continuous footings For mat foundations we recommend that a probe
hole be drilled for every 50 square feet of foundation footprint area The probe holes
should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the
planned bottom of foundation f cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected
during the probing operation additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to
help delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes
and cavities discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to
moderate pressures A typical sand cement grout should have a slump of about 6 to
9 inches
GEOLABS HAWAII

Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend that unit prices for additional probing and grouting be obtained during
bidding In addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and
grouting operation is complete The contractor should also be made aware that a longer
lag time between probing grouting operations and foundation construction may be
required in the construction schedule
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a
representative of Geolabs Hawaii This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of
cavities and or voids and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout
take and or changed conditions are observed
Foundation probe drilling and grouting will not be required for non building
foundations slabs on grade and pavements However proof rolling the excavated
subgrades with heavy equipment should be required for the finished subgrades to assist
in detecting and possibly collapsing near surface cavities and or voids and loose zones
The finished subgrade should be proof rolled with a Caterpillar D 8 bulldozer or similar
heavy equipment a minimum of 6 passes It is imperative that the proof rolling operations
be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii to evaluate the presence of cavities
and loose zones below the subgrade level
Retaining Structures
Retaining structures may be required for the perimeter fence wall septic tank
and oil water separator structures planned for this project In general retaining
structure foundations should be designed in accordance with the recommendations
presented in the Foundations section of this report In addition wall foundations
should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent grade should
have a minimum width of 18 inches and wall foundations parallel to the direction of the
slope should be constructed as stepped footings
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4100 00 Page 12
Retaining structures should be designed to resist lateral earth pressures due to
the adjacent soil and surcharge effects Free standing retaining walls should be designed
to resist an equivalent fluid earth pressure of 40 pcf Restrained retaining walls should be
designed to resist an equivalent fluid earth pressure of 55 pcf An active condition may
be used for walls that are free to deflect by as much as 0 5 percent of the wall height
Where the tops of walls are not free to deflect beyond this degree or are restrained the
walls should be designed for the at rest condition Surcharge and hydrostatic loads are
not accounted for in the above values Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges live
loads and point loads should be considered in the design
The values recommended above assume that the on site material and or select
granular fill will be used to backfill behind the wall It is assumed that the backfill behind
retaining walls will be compacted to between 90 and 95 percent relative compaction
Over compaction of the retaining wall backfill should be avoided
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads within a
horizontal distance equal to the depth of the wall should be considered in the design
For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on the loaded side of the wall a rectangular
distribution with uniform pressure equal to 55 percent of the surcharge pressure acting
on the entire height of the wall which is restrained at the top may be used in the
design For walls that are free to deflect cantilever a rectangular distribution equal to
40 percent of the surcharge pressure acting over the entire height of the wall may be
used for design Additional analyses during design may be needed to evaluate the
surcharge effects of point loads and line loads
Except for below grade structures the retaining wall structures should be
well drained to reduce the potential for build up of hydrostatic pressures A typical
drainage system consists of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable material such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation immediately around the perforated
pipe perforations down at the base of the wall discharging to an appropriate outlet or

Slabs On Grade
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by
near surface sandy clay material with low swelling potential In general we believe that
conventional slab on grade design may be used for interior slabs not subjected to
vehicular traffic and or vibrations due to machinery However to reduce the potential for
structural distress resulting from the swelling of the near surface soils we recommend
that the subgrade soils beneath the slab areas be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and recompacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction The slab subgrades should be kept moist prior to
placement of concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the
subgrade we recommend that the soils be thoroughly moistened to close all cracks
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well compacted engineered fill
For interior slabs on grades not subjected to vehicular traffic and or vibrations due
to machinery and with fioor coverings such as carpets vinyl flooring etc we recommend
that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill be placed below the slab The cushion fill
consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should provide more
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4100 00 Page 14
uniform support of the slabs and should serve as a capillary break To reduce the
potential for future moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top
of the gravel Consideration should be given to the placement of two inches of moist sand
above the plastic membrane to provide protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of
the slab concrete Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be
considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking It is also recommended that
interior walls be designed to incorporate some flexibility to accommodate small amount of
possible ground movements
For slabs subjected to vehicular traffic and or vibrations due to machinery we
recommend that the cushion fill consist of 4 inches of compacted select borrow subbase
materials The select borrow subbase material should be compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction For the design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade
reaction of 250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for the
compacted subbase material The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any unpaved
area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
The area adjacent to the concrete slabs walkways and driveways should be
backfilled tightly against the slab and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce
the potential of water ponding around the foundation
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the soils are critical elements

Fills and Backfills
In general the excavated on site soils and rock formations may be re used as a
source of fill material provided that the largest rock fragments in the material is less
than 3 inches in maximum dimension and that the materials are free of vegetation
and deleterious materials In addition the on site materials to be used as fill
should be relatively well graded have a minimum of 10 percent passing the
No 200 sieve size and have a CBR swell less than 2 percent
Imported fill should consist of non expansive select granular fill material such as
crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand The material should be well graded
from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and
should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve
The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883 Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Base course and
subbase materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4100 00 Page 18
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding
to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils are
critical elements of earthwork observations and soil density tests should be
performed by Geolabs Hawaii during site grading to assist the Contractor in
obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture content
Where compaction is less than required additional compactive effort should be
applied with adjustment of moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified
compaction It should be noted that the moisture requirement for fills and
subgrades above the optimum moisture is an important requirement for the use
of the on site clayey soils

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the generally flat terrain it appears that construction of slopes will likely
not be necessary or will be less than 3 feet in height However in the event that
slope construction will be performed we offer the following recommendations
In general we believe that cut slopes into the sandy clay and silty cobble material
may be made at an inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Cut slopes exposing the basalt
rock formations may be steepened to an inclination of 1H 1V or flatter Cavities
that may be exposed on the cut slope face should be backfilled or grouted
In the event that over cutting of a slope occurs or the cut slope is damaged by
erosion consideration should be given to leaving the slope as is rather than
attempting to backfill the slope to the design grade with sliver fills If backfilling of
an over cut slope is necessary over steepened or unstable the cut slope should
be completely over cut and buttressed with fill slope 10 feet wide constructed of
select granular fills cement treated if 1H 1V cut slope by keying and benching the
fill materials into the over cut slope
In general fill slopes should be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or
flatter Fills placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched
into the existing slope to provide stability of the new fill against sliding The filling
operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal

In general we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used for support of the utility lines
The initial backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes or 1 foot above the groundwater
level should consist of free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to provide

to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes The use of on site soils as backfill
immediately around utility pipes is not recommended
Soft or loose soils encountered at the bottom of trenches should be
over excavated to expose firm material or to a maximum 12 inches and backfilled with
select granular fill compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the
finish subgrade should consist of approved on site soils screened of materials larger than
3 inches in largest dimension or select granular material The backfill material should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence The upper
2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement finished grade should be compacted to
not less than 95 percent relative compaction Mechanical compaction equipment should
be used to compact the materials at the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding
should not be used to compact the backfill material

Drainage
The finished grades outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or perimeter footings immediately after
setting of the concrete to reduce waiter infiltration In addition drainage swales should be
provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away
from walls concrete pads and foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 extending to depths of about 3 to
16 5 feet below the existing ground elevation at the approximate locations shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
auger equipment and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the boring were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch O D Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of
an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

APPENDIX C
Percolation Testing
The percolation rates of the on site soils were estimated by performing one
falling head percolation test in Boring No 3 Field percolation testing was performed in
general accordance with the State of Hawaii Department of Health s Administrative
Rules Chapter 2 Wastewater Systems and Chapter 10 of the Ten States
Standards to evaluate the permeability of the subsurface soils for wastewater disposal
The test results are presented on Plate C 1
The percolation test was performed by drilling 4 inch diameter boreholes to a
depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface Six to nine inches of gravel was
then placed at the bottom of the borehole to protect the bottom of the hole from scouring
and sediments
During testing the hole was carefully filled with water to a minimum depth of
12 inches over the gravel pack The time intervals and water drops were then recorded
to provide data upon which to base calculation of the percolation rates
