INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed 250 000 gallon storage tank located in the Wailua area on
the island of Kauai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recom
mendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses The recommendations presented herein are intended for design input for
foundation site grading trench excavation and pavement and may not be appropriate
to form the basis for the preparation of construction cost estimates nor for construction
bidding purposes

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of the lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene
more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas
of the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic series cover most of the eastern half of the island
of Kauai Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic series are generally characterized as thick flows
of dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in north south trends in various
locales Associated with the vents are pyroclastic materials which usually form low
cinder cones at the vent
During the Pleistocene there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available
to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea
levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Kauai remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to present sea level on Kauai
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs

The project site is situated in the Makaleha Mountains Most of the soils in the
project area are residual soils derived from the in situ weathering of igneous rocks
Thin beds of ash and tuffaceous soils are intercalated with the flows

alignment were explored by drilling and sampling seven borings to depths ranging from
about 11 to 31 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations
of the borings are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3
Based on the three borings drilled at the proposed storage tank site we
generally encountered medium stiff silty clays with roots to depths of about 4 to 5 feet
below the existing ground surface Below the near surface clayey soils our borings
encountered soft to medium stiff silty and clayey soils with decomposed rock to depths
of about 16 to 24 feet below the ground surface The soft to medium stiff silty and
clayey soils were underlain by stiff to very stiff soils extending to the maximum depths
explored of approximately 31 feet below the existing ground surface

4 to 11 feet below the existing ground surface along the proposed water pipeline
Below the near surface clayey soils loose to medium dense silty sands and sandy
gravels were encountered in Boring Nos 4 and 5 and medium stiff clayey silts and silty
clays were encountered in Boring Nos 6 and 7 to the maximum depth drilled of
approximately 11 feet below the existing ground surface

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the proposed storage tank site is underlain by soft
to medium stiff soils extending to depths of about 16 to 24 feet below the existing
ground surface The soft to medium stiff silty and clayey soils are potentially
compressible under the anticipated tank loads Stiff to very stiff soils underlie the
potentially compressible soils As with any storage tank total and differential
settlements is of principal consideration in the design
Based on a shallow foundation system for support of the tank we considered
removal of up to 5 feet of the near surface soils and replacement with compacted
granular fills for support of the proposed tank Based on an allowable bearing pressure
of 2 000 p s f we estimate settlements of approximately 2 to 2 inches for this
alternative with differential settlements on the order 1 to 1 inches between the column
footings and the concrete slab on grade We believe that this amount of estimated
differential settlement is excessive for a storage tank foundation
To reduce the effects of potential differential settlement between the column
footings and the slab on grade we considered the use of a rigid mat foundation for
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3111 00 Page 9
support of the proposed storage tank We estimate the settlements for a rigid mat
foundation supported on at least 3 feet of compacted granular fill material to be on the
order of 1 to 2 inches with differential settlements between the center and the edge
of the mat to be on the order of less than 1 inch The mat should be rigid and may be
designed with an allowable bearing pressure of 1 500 p s f
If the estimated settlements for the shallow foundation alternatives above are not
acceptable a deep foundation system such as driven piles or drilled piers should be
considered Settlements of a deep foundation system are expected to be very small
and should result in satisfactory foundation performance If a deep foundation
alternative is desired additional geotechnical engineering exploration will be needed
to drill deeper borings for a deep foundation alternative
Detailed discussions and recommendations for design of foundation and retaining
wall site grading trench excavation and backfill pavements and other geotechnical
aspects of the project are presented in the following sections of this report
Mat Foundations
As previously indicated because of the potentially compressible subsoils
extending to depths of about 20 feet at the proposed storage tank site a shallow
foundation system consisting of column footings and slab on grade would result in large
differential settlements even with removal of up to 5 feet of the near surface soils As
a result to reduce the effect of large differential settlements between the column
footings and slab on grade we recommend that a rigid mat foundation be used to
support the proposed storage tank
The near surface soils are medium stiff and have high in situ moisture contents
Therefore to reduce the total settlements of the near surface soils and to provide more
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3111 00 Page 10
uniform support for the mat foundation we recommend that the mat foundation bear
on a minimum of 3 feet of compacted granular fills Concrete for the mat foundation
may be placed directly on the structural fill material The on site soils within 3 feet of
the bottom of the mat foundation should be removed and replaced with compacted
granular fill material The over excavation and structural fill should extend a horizontal
distance of 2 feet beyond the limits of the storage tank structure
Because of the high moisture content and relatively low strength characteristics
of the near surface soils we do not recommend that the on site soils be reused as
structural fill material Structural fill materials should consist of imported granular
materials which is discussed in the Site Grading section of this report The on site
materials may be used as a source of backfill material for the water line trench backfill
provided that they are processed and compacted as recommended herein Preparation
of the over excavated subgrade placement of structural fill and the requirements for
fill material are discussed in the Site Grading section of this report
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 1 500 p s f may be used for design of
the mat foundation bearing on at least 3 feet of compacted granular fill material The
allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one
third s for transient loadings such as temporary wind and or seismic forces A
modulus of subgrade reaction of 120 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection
p c i may be used in the design of the rigid mat foundation bearing on 3 feet of
compacted granular fills
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
between the base of the mat foundation and the bearing materials and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of
foundations A coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used between the base of the mat
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3111 00 Page 11
and the supporting granular fill Resistance due to passive pressure may be calculated
using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth
p c f for embedded foundations These values assume that the concrete for
foundations is placed directly against the footing excavations Unless covered by
pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below the finished
grade should be neglected
Where foundations will be located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities
the foundations should extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane
projected upward from the bottom of the below grade structure or utility line trench
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures
with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
It is important that a representative from Geolabs Hawaii observe the foundation

bearing materials and the embedment depths If unsuitable materials are encountered
in the excavations these materials should be over excavated to expose firm soils and
replaced with compacted engineered fill

shaft and the surrounding soils We estimate that an allowable compressive load

over excavation should extend a minimum of 2 feet beyond the limits of the
proposed storage tank structure After over excavation of the near surface soils
to the design subgrade elevation the subgrade should be proof rolled to attain
a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Proof rolling is recommended
because we anticipate the near surface soils to be very moist to wet Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
If the subgrade soils are observed to be pumping during proof rolling a filter
fabric such as Mirafi 140N or equivalent should be placed on the subgrade prior
to placement of the granular fill materials The filter fabric should also be
provided if proof rolling the subgrade cannot attain 90 percent relative
compaction The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with granular fill
material compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction The
granular fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in

Fills and Backfills
As previously indicated the on site materials should not be used as structural
fill However the on site materials may be used as backfill materials for the
proposed water line provided that they contain no lumps or fragments greater
than 3 inches in greatest dimension Cobbles and boulders if encountered
should not be allowed in the fill
Where used as backfill material the on site soils should be placed in level lifts
not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Backfills behind retaining walls should be compacted to between
90 and 95 percent relative compaction Over compaction of the retaining wall
backfill would exert additional lateral earth pressures on the walls and should be
avoided
Any imported soil and the structural fill recommended below the mat foundation
should be select fill Import materials should be tested and approved by
Geolabs Hawaii prior to delivery to the job site Imported granular materials
such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or cinder sand may be used The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than
3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a laboratory CBR value
of 15 or more with a maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 In addition the fill material
should contain a maximum of 20 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve
Imported soils should be brought to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction All surfaces should

Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important
design consideration For pipes bearing on the stiff silty and clayey soils a
granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation is recommended below the pipes For pipes bearing on the
soft to medium stiff silty and clayey soils we recommend that 12 inches of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped in a filter fabric
Mirafi 140N or equivalent be provided below the pipe
Trench Backfill
The first zone of backfill is the zone extending from the bedding material to at
least 12 inches above the top of the water line pipe Free draining granular
materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is
recommended for use as trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the top of
the pipe to provide adequate support around the pipe and to reduce compaction
of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes As an
alternative well graded granular material may be used for the first zone of
backfill The granular material should be well graded be less than 1 inch in
maximum size and have less than 10 percent passing No 200 sieve
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipe
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of select granular
material The on site soils may be used as a source of trench backfill material
however it should be noted that the on site materials have high in situ moisture

next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce
water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving because of the swelling potential of the near
surface soils The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the
walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce water
infiltration
Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain
all surface run off away from the slab and footing foundations

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the logs of borings at the appropriate sample depths
Two one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to
evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results are summarized
on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits test was performed on a selected sample of the soils to
evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of the test
are shown on Plate B 2 Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate
B 3
One unconfined compression test was performed on a selected sample of the
cohesive soils to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the material Results
of the unconfined compression test are summarized on the logs of borings at the
appropriate sample depth
One consolidation test with time rates was performed on the potentially
compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the materials
encountered Results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate B 4
One direct shear test was performed on a selected sample to evaluate the shear
strength characteristics Results of the test are presented on Plate B 5
One California Bearing Ratio test was performed on a bulk sample to evaluate
the pavement support characteristics of the near surface soils The test results are
summarized on Plate B 2
Two sets of pH and resistivity tests were performed on the soils within the invert
elevation to evaluate the potential corrosivity of the soils to the ductile iron pipe The
test results are summarized on Plate B 2
