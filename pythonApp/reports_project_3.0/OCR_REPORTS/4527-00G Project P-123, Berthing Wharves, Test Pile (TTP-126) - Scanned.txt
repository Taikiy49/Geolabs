Test Pile TTP 26 is located at Line C between Bents 36 and 37 Test Pile TTP 26 was
installed on The test pile was installed using a Delmag D 36 hammer
delivering a maximum rated energy of 113 160 Joules 83 820 foot pounds During the test
pile installation a record of the blow counts and field observations of the driving behavior was
maintained by a project engineer Geotechnical QC Specialist from our office The test pile
driving record is provided based on metric units Plate 2 1 and English units Plate 2 2
Test Pile TTP 26 was installed to a depth of about 16 15 meters 53 00 feet below the
existing deck surface The test pile was installed to a depth where approximately 1 25 meters
4 09 feet of the pile was left above the existing deck surface Therefore the total length of
the load test pile was 17 4 meters 57 09 feet measured from the top to the tip of the test pile
The eight embedment strain gages were connected using a multiplexer to a Geokon Micro 10
datalogger to record the strains in the eight embedment strain gages during the tensile pile
load test
The test pile was loaded in general accordance with the Standard Loading Procedure
of ASTM Test Designation D 3689 Based on the specifications Test Pile TTP 26 was to be
loaded to failure as defined by a pile head displacement equal to or greater than the following
Pile Head Displacement Pile Elastic Displacement for Full Pile Length mm
Plus Pile Diameter mm 120
Because there was no defined tensile pile design capacity variable load increments
were used in order to approach the failure gradually In general the test pile was loaded to
a load of kips in load increments varying from 44 5 to to 20 kips
Each load increment was held for at least 15 minutes or until the rate of pile head movement
was generally not greater than per hour 0 01 inch per hour but not longer than
2 hours based on ASTM D 3689 Individual Piles Under Static Axial Tensile Load
Additional settlement measurements were conducted on three scales mounted on a
driven pile reference point and two parallel reference beams by using surveyor s level to
monitor ground settlement during the load test At the beginning of the load test the readings
at the reference point and the two parallel reference beams were 49 and
72 and 2 69 inches respectively The measurements were carried out for load
increments at kN 135 and 165 kips respectively and at the end of the load test
Based on the measurements the readings remained unchanged throughout the test and
ground settlement was not observed during the pile load test
After loading the test pile to the total test load of kips the test load was
unloaded in approximately four equal decrements and was held at each decrement for at least
15 minutes The load test readings and the load displacement curve for the tensile load test
are presented on Plates 3 and 4 of this report The results of the tensile pile load test are
summarized as follows

The Models 4210 and VCE 4202
Vibrating Wire strain Gages are designed for direct em
bedment in concrete The VCE 4200 is six inches long
and is commonly used for strain measurements in found
ations piles bridges dams containment vessels tunnel
liners etc The VCE 4210 is 10 inches long and is design
ed for use in mass concrete with coarse aggregates It is
extra rugged to resist bending and has large flanges to
provide greater engagement area The VCE 4202 is
designed for laboratory use and or where there are space
limitations
The gages may be positioned either by means of wires
wrapped around the body or by attachment to a rosetie
which will hold the gage at a predetermined orientation in
the concrete One type of rosetie is shown overleaf
Strains are measured using the vibrating wire principle
A length of steel wire is tensioned between two end
blocks that are embedded directly m concrete Deform
ations i e strain changes of the concrete mass will
cause the two end blocks to move relative to one another
thus altering the tension in the steel wire The tension is
measured by plucking the wire and measuring its resonant
frequency of vibration using an electromagnetic coil
The Readout Box used in conjunction
with the Vibrating Wire Strain Gage will provide the
necessary voltage pulses to pluck the wire and will
convert the resulting frequency reading directly into strain
units by means of an internal microprocessor The strain
reading is displayed on a 5 digit LCD
The advantage of the vibrating wire strain gage over
more conventional electrical resistance or semiconductor
types lies mainly in the use of a frequency rather than a
voltage as the output signal from the strain gage Fre
quencies may be transmitted over long cable lengths with
out appreciable degradation caused by variations in cable
resistance contact resistance or leakage to ground
Vibrating wire gages also have excellent long term zero
stability For measurement of dynamic strains up to 100
Hz autoresonant versions are available
Where no stress strain gages are required a sheet steel
container is available which is lined with styrofoam This
container isolates the section of concrete around the gage
from the rest of the concrete so that no stresses can fall on
the gage yet apart from this it experiences the same
environmental conditions as adjacent gages and thus pro
vides a measure of effects due to temperature moisture
autogenous growth etc
The strain gages are provided with thermistors encap
sulated in the plucking coil The thermistor enables
temperatures to be measured Over vollage protection can
be provided in the gage and this can be supplemented by
gas tube surge arrestors af the termina box location
Installation procedures are fully explained in manuals
supplied with the gages
