SUMMARY
The site is underlain by loose backfill
firm residual soils and basalt In order
to provide uniform support with minimum
differential settlements for the proposed
structures a compacted fill is recommended
beneath many of the footing foundations
Net allowable bearing capacity up to 2 500
pounds per square foot can therefore be
used
The shallow on site soils are of moderate
expansion potential provided that fill is
compacted as recommended slab on grade
construction can be utilized
INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of a foundation investigation
for the above reference commercial and residential buildings
The work was performed according to our proposal dated May 1 1979
A preliminary soil investigation at this site was performed for
another development plan This preliminary report was performed
by their reference 478 00 dated June 29
1973

East commercial residential condominium complex The buildings
will be concrete frame with stucco walls and tile roofs

The purpose of the foundation investigation is to explore

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
During the late Tertiary Period the Koolau Volcanic Mountain
Range which forms the elongated northeastern portion of
Oahu was built mainly by eruptions of basaltic lava along
a northwest trending rift zone Subsequently erosion and
submergence produced the ancestral Manoa Valley
Approximately 10 000 to 20 000 years ago lava welled up
out of vents on the flank of Sugarloaf on the western side
of Manoa Valley The Sugarloaf flow spreaded across Manoa
Valley forcing Manoa Stream out of its former channel in
the center of the valley The stream now flows along the
eastern edge of the basalt flow and eastern edge of the
project site The basalt flow filled the lower end of
the valley and caused it to deposit alluvial debris in the
valley upstream Weathering of the lava flow was increased
adjacent to the stream the basalt flow on site was also
buried by alluvial debris
Subsequent channelizing of Manoa Stream excavation and
backfill operations have partly covered the site with fill
Soils
Excavations for underground gasoline storage tanks and
stream banks adjacent to the Manoa channel wall have been
backfilled Minor fill was also noted beneath paving and
in abandoned utility trenches These deposits of fill are

generally characterized by gray and brownish gray color
and by low density not a controlled fill not compacted

Based upon the borings the natural or residual clayey
sand and gravel strata generally divide into two layers
a shallow or upper and a deeper or lower stratum In some
cases the shallow horizon was absent in most cases the
intermediate B horizon was absent The upper stratum
consists of fine grained dark brown colored A horizon
it is underlain by firm reddish brown yellowish brown and
mottled clayey gravel C horizon The dark topsoil or
shallow A stratum is moderately expansive and will not
provide uniform footing support The underlying mottled
color or deeper C horizon will provide suitable support
Extremely or highly weathered basalt was encountered
adjacent to the Manoa Stream and in certain other borings
The underlying fresh basalt is hard and requires heavy duty
backhoe or jack hammers for excavation This fresh hard
basalt is generally gray in color
Groundwater
At the present time the water table on the entire site is
generally at more or less elevation 147 to 148 feet The
variations in water levels noted on the boring logs may be
due to water under artesian pressure Different water levels
noted in the preliminary 1973 report probably indicate that
water levels fluctuate with climatic conditions

CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the results of the field investigations laboratory
testing and engineering analyses the following conclusions
and recommendations are presented
The loose fill and dark A horizon are not recommended for
footing support The underlying C horizon soils weathered
and fresh basalt are capable of providing support for spread
type footings Fill soils near Manoa Stream extend well below
water level however removal of all fill near the wall is
not practical We have provided recommendations for the
depth of excavation and the degree of compaction required
in order to utilize spread type foundations Some differ
ential settlements should be anticipated using shallow foot
ings see Building 3 below
An alternate type of foundation might include using concrete
piles installed in holes predrilled near the Manoa Stream
This foundation system is anticipated to be somewhat more
expensive however it does offer the following advantages
negligible differential settlements are expected at Building
3 new active pressures would not be imposed on the existing
wall and underpinning of shallow footings would not be required
even in the event of modification to the adjacent Manoa Stream
channel wall We would be pleased to provide data for pile
foundation design if desired

The area of the former underground gasoline storage tanks
is also a potential problem Some extra expenditures will
be required to provide adequate foundation support Again
we generally recommend using shallow spread type foundations
underlain by compacted fill i e removal of all loose back

deep spread type foundations extending to underlying resi

Grading
The fill soil cobbles and boulders adjacent to the Manoa
channel wall and backfill puddled in the underground storage
tank excavations should be removed The excavation sides may
be sloped at 1 1 Groundwater will probably be encountered
adjacent to Manoa Stream below elevation 147 to 148 feet
In this and any other wet area 18 inches of drain rock
such as 3B Coarse Stone should be track rolled in the
bottom of excavations in order to provide a firm working
surface The on site soils can be utilized for compacted
fills after cobbles and boulders larger than 6 inches are
removed

Imported fill material should be tested and approved by
the soil engineer prior to importation Imported fill
shall be of a granular nonexpansive nature Fill soils
should be placed in maximum 9 inch thick lifts and each
layer compacted to 95 relative compaction using ASTM
D 1557 70 modified Proctor as a standard All lifts
must be placed horizontally 95 compaction has been
recommended to minimize differential settlements Also
for this project continuous inspection and frequent
density testing during grading should be performed under

that the subsurface conditions between the borings do not
substantially vary from those encountered during the explora

Shallow trenches excavated into the soil and rock formations
less than five feet deep may be left open for short term
construction periods with minor sloughing of the vertical
walls
Foundations
Spread footing foundations underlain by C horizon residual
soils weathered and fresh basalt and by compacted fill
soils may be used to support the proposed structures

Lateral Loads
Resistance to lateral loads may be provided by a combination
of passive pressure and friction Passive pressure may be
taken as that exerted by a fluid of density equal to 300
p c f Friction may be taken as normal dead loads times
a coefficient of friction equal to 0 5 five tenth
A combination of friction and passive pressure may be used
provided that passive pressure is reduced by one third
Walls Below Grade
Retaining walls may be designed to resist an active earth
pressure equal to a fluid having a density equal to 45
pounds per cubic foot An active earth pressure coefficient
equal to 0 3 may be used to compute resultant horizontal
earth pressures on the existing Manoa channel wall due to
new construction

APPENDIX A
FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by
drilling 12 borings using truck mounted rotary drilling equip
ment with continuous flight solid stem 4 inch auger The
borings were drilled to depths ranging from 6 5 to 26 5 feet
below the existing ground surface Undisturbed and disturbed
samples were obtained and penetration tests were performed
The soils encountered in the borings were classified
by visual and textural examination in the field by our
engineer who continuously monitored the drilling operations
The classifications were reviewed by visual inspection and
testing in the laboratory The soils were classified in
general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System A graphic presentation of the soils is presented
on the Boring Logs Plates A 1 through A 12
In the borings soil samples were obtained by driving
split barrel ring sampler Penetration tests were also
performed in coarse soils using a cone tip rod Samplers
and penetration tests were driven using a standard 140 pound
hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts used to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches are shown on the boring logs at
the appropriate depths
