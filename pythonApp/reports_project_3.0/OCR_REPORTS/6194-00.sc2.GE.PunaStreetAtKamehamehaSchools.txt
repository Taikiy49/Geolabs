The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and
Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late
Pliocene in age 4 million years ago and forms the bulk of the western one third of

the island The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age 5 million years ago and forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Waianae and Koolau shields volcanic activity
returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations mainly at
the southeastern portion of the Island of Oahu
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the
sea When the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such
that the sea levels rose to elevations above the present sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic sea
level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
filled pre existing depressions and accumulated at higher elevations
In the mountainous regions of Hawaii and at the heads of valleys erosional
processes are dominated by detachment of soil and rock materials from the steep valley
walls and transportation down slope toward a valley axis primarily by gravity as colluvial
earth materials Once these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley
stream flow processes become dominant and the sediments are transported and
deposited as alluvial earth materials

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Falling rocks are a special category within the large family of gravitationally driven
phenomena called landslides Rockfall events may involve various modes of detachment
and movement including detachment from the parent rock outcrop individual boulder
movement raveling of loose rock material from steep slope faces toppling of rock
outcrops and rock slides Rockfall is the fastest type of landslide and is primarily caused
by the loss of support and detachment of individual components of the rock mass under
the influence of gravity Complex interactions between the physical parameters of both the
rock and the slope may cause the falling rocks to move down the slope in a high velocity
seemingly random and erratic manner Rockfall is a natural and on going process that
occurs in steep terrain Rockfall source areas may be defined by topographically high
regions where the rock mass may contain discontinuities such as open fractures
undercuts or inclined bedding surfaces capable of producing sliding action In addition
source areas may include at rest boulder deposits where re activation of movement occurs

Destabilization of a rock mass may occur by mechanical weathering root pry and
expansion contraction along rock joints chemical weathering decomposition of the rock

events may include heavy precipitation high groundwater pressure decreased normal
rock friction extreme temperature fluctuations seismic shaking erosion by concentrated
surface runoff and prying and levering of tree roots within rock joints Human activities
such as excavation vibration traffic on the slope surface and development of
over steepened cut slopes may also trigger rockfall activity

Computer Rockfall Simulation Analysis
As part of our evaluation of the potential rockfall conditions at the site we
performed a computer simulation of potential falling rock activity using input data collected
during our site reconnaissance We used the Colorado Rockfall Simulation Program
CRSP Version 4 0 for our analysis The purpose for the CRSP analysis was to model the
typical slope and potential rockfall conditions to obtain a statistical analysis of the
simulation results to assist in the evaluation of risk and the design of appropriate rockfall
control measures
The CRSP is a computer program that is a widely accepted engineering tool used
to estimate potential rockfall behavior by simulating rockfall activity based on input
parameters that are assigned on a site specific basis The input parameters for this project
were assigned based on our observations and measurements collected in the field The
program provides a statistical analysis of potential rockfall activity based on hill slope
topographic profile and other specific input information such as rock size shape and
parameters used to quantify the typical ground surface conditions Information obtained
from the CRSP analysis includes the predicted falling rock velocity bouncing height
kinetic energy and roll out distance The output information is useful to assist in the
site specific design and location of various rockfall mitigation schemes such as rockfall
impact barriers rock catchment ditches or other rockfall containment systems
We developed typical slope profile representations consisting of a 15 foot high rock
shoulder swale width 5 feet and typical pavement width 25 feet at the base of the slope
Our CRSP analysis was performed using spherical shaped boulders
conservative scenario of 0 and 2 0 feet in diameter initiating from the model cut
slope face Numerical input coefficients were used which approximated the observed
typical ground surface relief and roughness conditions For each rockfall simulation run
1 000 simulated source rocks of each rock diameter were utilized to develop a statistical
distribution of the results
The purpose of our preliminary CRSP analysis was to statistically evaluate the
potential for falling rock to encroach on the roadway surface under the existing observed
site conditions In addition the probable rock bounce height and falling rock kinetic energy
was estimated for the slope model conditions Finally the CRSP analysis was used to
evaluate the effectiveness of a widened shoulder area that could be developed for the
catchment of rockfall from the cut slope
It should be noted that our rockfall protection criterion for the design of various
rockfall control systems is defined as the probable interception and catchment of
90 percent of possible rockfall hazards assessed by the computer rockfall simulation
analysis CRSP The 90 percent catchment criteria is a target that is commonly used in
engineering practice for evaluation criteria that can be quantified by a statistical and

Rockfall Mitigation Options
Based on our site reconnaissance we believe that the risk for potential falling rock
from the upper slopes is relatively low due to the existing limited source rock residing in
generally stable ground settings on the flatter graded slope surfaces In contrast we
believe that the risk for potential falling rock from the cut slopes is generally greater due to
the steep slope inclination and amount of visible loose fractured rock comprising the slope
face As a result of the observed difference between the cut slope and upper slope site
conditions we believe that upper slope rockfall hazards could be substantially reduced by
the removal and stabilization of the generally limited rock source In contrast mitigation of
the cut slope rockfall hazards could be accomplished by several alternatives
Based on our reconnaissance of the existing upper slopes we believe that potential
rockfall hazards consisting of widely scattered loose surface cobbles and boulders and a
few overhanging rock outcrop conditions could be effectively mitigated and the risk
substantially reduced or eliminated by performing a ground surface boulder sweep
combined with trimming of the fractured overhang conditions from a few selected rock
outcrops The remedial action is considered permanent and should not have to be
performed again unless significant changes to the slope occur
Based on our reconnaissance of the existing cut slopes we present four viable
alternatives for consideration to reduce the potential hazard associated with falling rock
from the cut slope
Option 1 Rock Slope Scaling
Option 1 is similar to a surface boulder sweep but consists of the prying and
detachment of loose rock material from the parent rock slope face by manual labor
Rock slope scaling is typically performed by experienced slope scaling crews who
are suspended by crane or by climbing ropes on the slope The crew works in a top
down procedure using pry bars and other hand tools to remove loose fractured rock
material that is poorly supported or in jeopardy of falling from the slope face The
scaling procedure may also encompass the cutting of undesired vegetation and the
removal of invasive tree roots and other debris accumulations from the slope face
The scaled material is moved to the bottom of the slope for collection and proper
disposal Scaling crews must exercise good judgment in the removal of rock
material so that adjacent rock material is not destabilized Loose fractured and
potentially unstable rock material should be scaled in a manner such that the
overall slope inclination is maintained and any unsupported fractured rock material
is not left in place At times the scaling procedure must be amended to chase
zones of loose material up the slope until a stable slope face free of rock overhangs

Option 2 Wire Mesh Drapery
Option 2 consists of the installation of special wire mesh panels on the slope face to
control the fall of rock beneath the drapery toward the base of the slope The wire
mesh panels cover the entire cut slope face and extend a short distance on the
upper slope towards the ground anchorage system The wire mesh is typically
coated with a black PVC material to provide coloration and reduce corrosion The
cut slope is prepared by cutting and removing the undesired vegetation and the
scaling of loose rock materials and debris from the slope The wire mesh panels are
typically anchored to the slope along the top of the mesh on the upper flatter slope
Supplemental ground anchors may be installed along the base of the wire mesh
drapery to help contain possible debris accumulation A boulder sweep of the upper
slopes would also be performed as part of this option

Option 3 Shotcrete Slope Facing
Option 3 consists of the construction of shotcrete on the cut slope face Shotcrete is
a wet concrete mix applied by a high pressure air spray onto the slope face
Shotcrete may contain fiber additives to provide extra strength and reinforcement if
needed The purpose of the shotcrete application is to cover the slope face to
reduce the surface erosion and detachment of rock fragments from the slope
Geocomposite drain board materials would typically be installed against the slope
face underneath the shotcrete to facilitate drainage Where flatter areas of soil
material are encountered on the slope face Grouted Rubble Pavement GRP may
be constructed to protect against surface runoff and reduce soil erosion A boulder

Recommended Conceptual Rockfall Protection Program
Based on our study we believe that the rockfall mitigation options presented should
provide a high level of effective long term protection against rockfall hazards emanating
from the adjacent slope
We believe that Option 1 Rock Slope Scaling is a cost effective plan to reduce the
potential for hazardous rockfall to encroach on the roadway In addition Option 1 is a low
maintenance alternative that best preserves the natural appearance of the rock slopes
along the roadway The primary disadvantage is that the rock slope scaling may require
repeat performances of the scaling effort to maintain effectiveness with the passage of
time We envision that the repeat scaling may be performed at a lower level of intensity
because the majority of the loose rocks and instability should be corrected by the initial
intensive rock scaling effort Based on our experience we envision that the scaling work
may need to be repeated at intervals of perhaps 5 to 15 years as natural erosion and
changes to the slope conditions occur It should be noted that in addition to the
recommended rock slope scaling localized slope stabilization such as the construction of
GRP or shotcrete applications may be necessary to slow erosion and stabilize some
specific zones of loose fractured rock and soil erosion that cannot be effectively mitigated
by scaling alone
We understand that it is desired to protect in place the documented archaeological
boulder feature identified as A 2 Although the existing A 2 boulder on the upper slope
appears generally stable in the present setting there is some potential for adjacent
down slope soil erosion to affect the future stability of the boulder We recommend
constructing an area of Grouted Rubble Pavement GRP on the saprolitic soil slope below
the boulder and extending towards the roadway elevation to reduce the potential for soil
erosion that may affect the future stability of the rock

analysis of potential rockfall activity only Variation in the surface and subsurface
conditions between our observations and analysis points may occur and the nature and
extent of these variations may not become evident until additional field exploration or

re evaluate the findings and recommendations provided herein
It should be noted that slopes composed of rock materials rock slopes deteriorate
with the passage of time due to natural weathering processes wet dry and hot cold
cycles and erosion conditions Due to the inherent deterioration of rock slopes resulting
from natural processes weathering wet dry and hot cold cycles and erosion conditions
the potential for rockfall hazard at any site changes with the passage of time Therefore
the findings and recommendations contained herein may be used only within a reasonable
time from the date of issuance of this letter report Land use site conditions and or other
factors may change with the passage of time Therefore additional work to evaluate the
applicability of the findings and preliminary recommendations contained in this letter report
due to changes with the passage of time will be required
