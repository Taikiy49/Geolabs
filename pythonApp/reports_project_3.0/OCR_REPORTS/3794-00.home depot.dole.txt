INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Home Depot Dole project located in the Iwilei area of
Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommenda
tions resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses
These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations site grading and
pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to construct a new Home Depot retail facility adjacent to the Dole
Cannery Outlet Center near the intersection of Alakawa Street and Nimitz Highway in the
Ilwilei area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the information
provided the Home Depot facility will consist of a retail building and garden center with
planned areas of approximately 130 453 and 15 128 square feet respectively In
addition driveways and a two story parking structure will also be constructed
We anticipate that the proposed new building will primarily be of tilt up concrete or
masonry construction with a sheet metal roof Based on the information provided we
understand that it is proposed to raise the finish grade elevation approximately 3 feet
above the existing ground surface for the proposed new building construction in order to
raise the floor above the design flood level and to accommodate the truck loading dock
_ elevation Therefore we anticipate that site grading consisting of about 3 feet of new fills
would be needed to achieve the design grades for the proposed project For the parking
structure we anticipate reinforced concrete construction Detailed information pertaining
to the proposed construction was not available at the time this report was prepared

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in central
Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology
and subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea
level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The
subsurface conditions within the project area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age
coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea which are
intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area
is Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower
relative to the present sea level on Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise
in sea level occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area were drowned In
the last 10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous
and marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal
environments resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in the
areas that were formerly valleys and drainageways
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3794 00 Page 5
With the urbanization of the area man made fills were placed to reclaim marshy
areas and lagoons The fill was placed upon lagoonal deposits consisting of silty sands
with coral fragments The lower clayey material was deposited during periodic influxes of
non marine alluvial deposits that apparently smothered the coral reef development Land
development and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought the area to
its present form Many of the resulting fills may be of poor quality in terms of supporting
heavy structural loads

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 to depths ranging from
about 56 to 61 5 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the
borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general the subsurface conditions varied from the northwestern boundary
along Alakawa Street to the southeastern boundary Along the northwestern boundary
our field exploration generally encountered a surficial fill crust about 2 to 3 feet thick
overlying compressible lagoonal deposits of clayey gravels to a depth of about 15 to
25 feet below the existing ground surface The loose and soft lagoonal deposits were
underlain by stiff to very stiff alluvial materials consisting of silty sands and gravels
extending to depths of about 45 to 50 feet below the existing ground surface Below the
stiff to very stiff alluvial materials very stiff clay was encountered to the maximum depth
explored of about 61 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Better subsurface conditions were encountered along the southeastern boundary
where the fill and lagoonal layers were much thinner Our field exploration generally
encountered a surficial fill crust of sandy gravel about 1 foot thick overlying soft lagoonal
clayey deposits to a depth of about 5 to 6 feet below the existing ground surface The
loose and soft lagoonal deposits were underlain by stiff to very stiff alluvial materials
consisting of silty sands and gravels extending to depths of about 40 to 49 feet below the
existing ground surface Very stiff clay was encountered to the maximum depth explored
of about 61 5 feet below the existing ground surface however in Boring Nos 4 and 6
sandy materials with shell fragments were encountered at a depth of about 55 feet below
the existing ground surface

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Variable soil conditions were encountered below the building site Relatively thick
compressible soft loose soils as much as 20 feet thick were found in the northwestern
portion of the site with decreasing thickness toward the southeast These compressible
soils have a major impact on the site grading and foundation design of the project
Additionally some of the near surface soils were found to be contaminated and may
require special treatment
Based on the proposed construction and the level of groundwater encountered in
the borings we do not anticipate the need for dewatering during foundation excavations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3794 00 Page 8
Old Foundations
It is our understanding that the foundations from the previous buildings and
warehouses at the site were not removed when the buildings were demolished It
appears that much of the Home Depot building site is situated over footing foundations
from the previous structure These footings are believed to be buried several feet
below the site near the present water table Additionally the proposed site for the
parking structure appears to be situated over footing foundations from a previous
structure as well as a concrete box drain currently in use
These existing foundations are obstructions to future pile foundations and
possibly to future utility excavations Recent attempts by others to locate and observe
the existing condition of these old foundations at the Home Depot building site revealed
old footings at depths of approximately 4 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface
near the water table Old footings removed would be classified as contaminated soil
which would be costly to dispose of offsite
Ground Settlement
The floor of the new building will be raised about 3 feet above the existing grade to
accommodate the truck loading dock and extend above the design flood level
Ground settlements are anticipated from the weight of the new site fill as well as
structural loads due to compression of the soft clay and loose sand layers below the site
Estimated settlements due to the various loading conditions anticipated are as follows
1 Three feet of new fill 6 to 12 inches
2 Storage or live loads of 650 p s f 6 to 12 inches
3 Structural column loads of up to 120 kips 1 to 2 inches

Due to the limited thickness of the clayey portions of the compressible layer and
the relatively high permeability of the loose sand it is anticipated that settlements will
occur relatively rapidly We estimate that 90 percent of the settlements may occur
within about 2 to 3 months
Foundation Alternatives Home Depot Building
Spread Footing Surcharge
Shallow spread and wall footing foundations may be used for the proposed
building if provisions are made to limit static settlements from the site filling and
storage loads and the structure can tolerate the estimated column loads The
static settlements may be reduced by first placing the 3 feet of permanent fill
with allowance for anticipated settlement and a temporary surcharge fill of
about 5 feet high which would be equivalent to the storage loads The
settlements of the fill and surcharge should be monitored to determine when the
settlements have essentially stopped and building construction may begin
Some settlement of the areas adjacent to the fill and surcharge should be
expected since the zone of influence of the new fill weight will extend beyond the
fill limits
Spread and wall foundations may be designed utilizing an allowable bearing
seismic loads The footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below
the lowest adjacent grade

Pile Foundation with Structural Slab
In light of the potential settlements at the site pile foundations may be utilized to
support the structure The use of a structural slab system would eliminate the
need for fill and the corresponding ground settlements The structural slab
system could provide a 3 to 4 foot high open space between the slab floor and
the ground which may be beneficial to the soil remediation gas release system
of the near surface contaminated soils
Based on the boring data the anticipated pile length varies from about 40 to
50 feet below the existing ground surface with an average length of about
45 feet
Calculations indicate that the 16 inch octagonal prestressed concrete pile
bearing into the stiff alluvial soils may be designed for a 50 ton allowable load
carrying capacity Since the pile capacities are derived primarily from frictional
resistance the allowable pile capacity may be varied by adjusting the pile
embedment
Pile Foundations with Slab On Grade
In order to reduce the pile foundation costs a slab on grade system in lieu of the
structural slab system may be considered For a slab on grade the site would
first have to be filled and surcharged as described in the Spread
Footing Surcharge subsection above
Surcharge Program
To aid in the acceleration of anticipated ground settlements we recommend a
surcharge program prior to construction The program should consist of the placement of
5 feet of temporary fill over the proposed fill area prior to building construction
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3794 00 Page 11
A slope ratio of up to one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V may be used for the
temporary surcharge material The surcharge fill should be placed in level lifts of 1 foot
high and extend laterally a minimum of 2 feet beyond the footprint of the fill area The
1 layer of the surcharge fill should be compacted to a minimum 90 percent relative
compaction Adequate precautions must be taken to preclude erosion of the surcharge
fill slopes during construction and the subsequent surcharge period to attain the
required consolidation
In general the surcharge material should be similar to the construction fill material
Ideally if the surcharge material is the same as the construction fill material it may be
used on other fill areas at the project site
Based on our engineering analyses we believe that a settlement period of about
8to 12 weeks would be adequate to achieve approximately 80 to 90 percent
consolidation However it should be recognized that it is very difficult to accurately
predict the exact time required for the filled ground to settle since the settlement rate will
be affected by variations of the subsoil structure and the history of the subsoil deposition
For the compressible subsoils encountered at the site we believe that the
estimated settlement period could vary as much as 50 to 100 percent from the actual
settlement period required Therefore the actual settlement rate should be monitored
and provisions should be made for potential delays in the construction schedule if a
longer surcharge period is required
Settlement Monitoring
To monitor the actual settlement rate during the surcharge program we
recommend that a minimum of eight settlement gauges be installed in the fill areas A
typical settlement gauge is shown on Plate 3 The gauges should be read optically by a
qualified professional surveyor and the readings should be transmitted to

Geolabs Hawaii for review The readings should be taken on a semi weekly basis during
the first 6 weeks and every week thereafter for the remainder of the anticipated settlement
period and or until the settlement rate has stabilized
A proper contingency plan should be devised to accommodate the potential delay

It should be recognized that it is very difficult to accurately predict the exact time

Site Grading
Site grading involving about 3 feet of fill is anticipated to raise the site to a finish
grade elevation of 7 0 feet Mean Sea Level MSL In order for the majority of the ground
settlements to occur a waiting period of 2 to 3 months is recommended after completion
of the initial site filling and prior to building construction
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Any unsuitable materials such as old footings
old slabs and pavement vegetation and rubbish should be removed and
disposed of properly off site
In fill areas the original ground should then be scarified to a minimum depth of
6 inches moisture conditioned and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent of
its maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 91
Loose or soft spots encountered at the subgrade level within the fill area should
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3794 00 Page 13
be recompacted if it is sandy or removed if it is clayey or organic and then
replaced with compacted non expansive select material
Fill Material and Placement
It is anticipated that off site borrow will be required for the fill material Imported
fill material should consist of non expansive select granular materials less than
3 inches in maximum size with less than 20 percent passing the No 200 sieve
The material should be tested and approved by the Geotechnical Engineer prior
to transportation to the site
The fill should be placed in maximum 10 inch thick loose level lifts and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of its maximum dry density as
determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 91
The upper 6 inches of subgrade should be compacted to 95 percent of its
maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 91 to
support the pavements
Fill Settlements
Due to soft and loose subsoil conditions the estimated general ground
settlements induced by the weight of the new fill are on the order of up to
12 inches It is estimated that 90 percent of the fill settlements may occur in
about 2 to 3 months Therefore the site filling should start as early as
practicable and the building construction activity should be delayed for a period
of 2 to 3 months after completion of the site filling and surcharge placement

It is further recommended that the construction of slab on grade and rigid
pavements be delayed toward the end of the project in order to allow maximum

A maximum slope ratio of two horizontal to one vertical is recommended for

Spread Footing Foundation
It is our opinion that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous
footings bearing on the recompacted fill soils encountered at shallow depths may be
used to support the proposed building provided that the anticipated settlement is
tolerable Based on the subsoil conditions encountered assuming the surcharge
program is followed and the structural loads anticipated for the proposed development
footing settlements on the order of about one half inch southern area to 1 inch
northwestern area may be anticipated for the column footings with differential
settlement of approximately one half the amount
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 500 pounds per square foot p s f may
be used for design of footing foundations bearing on the recompacted fill soils
encountered at shallow depths This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic
forces We recommend that the footing foundations be structurally connected together
with tie beams to improve the rigidity of the foundation thus reducing the anticipated
differential settlement
In general the footings should be placed as high as practicable to take
advantage of the fill crust thereby reducing the footing load applied over the underlying
compressible deposits To utilize the surficial fill crust we recommend that footings be
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3794 00 Page 15
embedded to a maximum depth of about 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished
grade If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of
the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is
necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that the
soil around footings is well compacted or undisturbed Unless covered by pavements
or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected A
coefficient of friction of 0 35 may be used for footings bearing on the recompacted
near surface soils
It is recommended that the bottom of all footings bear on the recompacted
near surface soils or compacted fill If soft and or loose materials are encountered at
the bottom of the footing excavations they should be over excavated to expose firm
materials The over excavation should be backfilled with granular material compacted
to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction or the footing bottom should be
extended down to underlying competent material Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of
the same soil established in accordance with procedures described in ASTM Test
Designation D 1557 91

Pile Driving Criteria
The piles should be driven by using a hammer with a rated energy of at least
32 000 foot pounds The hammer should be equipped with an energy level
control so that driving within the soft deposits may be achieved at a reduced
energy level to minimize the tensile stress within the pile which could cause
damage to the pile
The recommended pile driving criteria are as follows
1 The piles should be cast in one continuous section
2 The driving process should be continuous without interruption for each
pile A pile may be rejected or its design capacity modified when the
driving process is interrupted for more than 4 hours
3 During easy driving when the pile tip is still within soft soil the hammer
stroke should be carefully controlled reduced energy so that excessive
tensile stresses are not transmitted to the pile
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3794 00 Page 18
4 The pile driving process may be terminated when the blow counts steadily
increase with depth and reach 10 blows per inch for each of the last
2 inches or when aggregated blow counts are 50 blows for the last foot
5 This criteria is based on the use of a diesel driving hammer with a rated
energy of 32 000 foot pounds to drive a 16 inch octagonal concrete pile
with a design of 50 tons This driving criteria should be modified

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the proposed building
structures Based on the existing topography and the proposed finish floor elevation we
anticipate that fills up to about 3 feet high will be required to raise the existing ground
surface to the proposed finished subgrade Because of the soft loose deposits underlying
the site some amount of ground settlement may be anticipated under the new fill loads
Therefore in order to reduce the potential for distress due to differential movements
between the slab and foundations we recommend that free floating slabs on grade with
no structural connections to the wall and column foundations be used Joint filler and
sealant may be used to fill the opening between the edge of slab and other structural
elements In addition construction of the slabs on grade should be delayed as long as
practicable in order to permit maximum ground settlement to occur Alternatively the
slabs may be reinforced and structurally connected to resist the differential movements
Slab subgrades should be prepared in accordance with the recommendations
presented in the Site Preparation section of this report For slabs on grade that are not
subjected to traffic loading we recommend that a 4 inch cushion layer of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used below the slabs For slabs that will be
covered with moisture sensitive floor coverings a vapor barrier membrane may be
incorporated on top of the gravel cushion layer to reduce the potential for damage to the
floor coverings by infiltration of moisture A 2 inch layer of moist fine sand may be
considered above the vapor membrane to protect the membrane and to aid in concrete
curing

driveway a 6 inch layer of base course compacted to 95 percent relative compaction is

Liquefaction Potential
Liquefaction occurs when a build up of excess pore water pressure is induced on
saturated cohesionless soils resulting in a substantial loss of strength The most common
cause of this phenomenon is the cyclic loading due to earthquakes This process may
permit the soil to move both vertically and horizontally and if not confined can result in
significant deformations Loose uniformly graded fine grained sands and loose silts with
little cohesion are most susceptible to liquefaction Generally liquefaction is less likely to
occur if the loose deposit is deeper than 50 feet below the ground surface or the deposit
contains an appreciable percentage of clay
In general the subsurface information obtained from the drilled borings indicate
that the site is generally underlain by medium dense to dense well graded materials at
depths less than 50 feet below the existing ground surface or contain appreciable
amounts of clay with Liquid Limit LL greater than 35 and water content less than

W O 3794 00 Page 21
Based on this information we believe that the subsoils at this site are not highly
susceptible to liquefaction

Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter

and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
One consolidation test ASTM D 2435 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate compressibility characteristics of the on site soils Results of the
consolidation test are presented on Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples
of the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results
of these tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic representation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR test ASTM D 1883 was performed
on the near surface soil to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soil
Results of the test are presented on Plate B 3
Two sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Results of these tests are presented on Plate B 4
Two direct shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of the
soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics Results of the direct shear test are
presented on Plates B 5 and B 6
