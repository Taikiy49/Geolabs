In the Bingham Building area, our field exploration generally encountered fill, 
alluvium, cobbles and boulders, and volcanic cinder overlying basalt rock formation 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 35 feet below the existing ground 
surface. The surface fill layer, about 3.5 to 8.5 feet thick, consisted of medium stiff to

formation. Below the basalt formation, alluvium consisting of medium stiff to hard clay

2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The 
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) 
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Koolau 
Volcano reached the end of its main shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
The Koolau Volcano was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the 
northwest to southeast trending rift zones. These lavas consist of interbedded 
pahoehoe and a’a type flows with pahoehoe lava being the dominant form. “A’a” is the 
Hawaiian term for basaltic lava flow typified by a rough, jagged, spinose, clinkery 
surface. “Pahoehoe” is the Hawaiian term for basaltic lava flows typified by smooth, 
billowy, or ropy surfaces. After the cessation of the main shield-building phase of Koolau 
Volcano, there was a long period of erosion. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, renewal of volcanic activity occurred with the 
eruptions of vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series. Along the crest of the ridge along the 
westerly side of Manoa Valley, there are the Tantalus, Roundtop, and Sugarloaf vents. 
When these vents were active, one or more lava flows from these vents spilled down the 
westerly wall of the valley and moved across the valley floor. The lava flows ponded and 
filled the lower portion of Manoa Valley. The ponding allowed the lava to densify as it 
cooled, forming dense massive rock. These flows generally terminated in the Moiliili 
District, where the basalt rocks were quarried in the past. Reef limestone related to the 
Waimanalo stand of the sea underlies the basalt at the former Moiliili Quarry. Man-made 
fills were placed in the area and brought the project site to its current conditions.

2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by 
shifts in the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked 
primarily to volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally 
occurs before or during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result 
from the underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but 
does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each 
year, but most of the earthquakes are so small that they can only be detected by 
sensitive instruments. However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to 
be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on 
the Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement 
of magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa 
Volcanoes on the Island of Hawaii. Because majority of the earthquakes in 
Hawaii (over 90 percent of earthquakes) are related to volcanic activity, the risk 
of high seismic activity and degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased 
distance from the Island of Hawaii. The Island of Hawaii has experienced

2.4.2 Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the International Building Code (2012 Edition), the project site may be 
subject to seismic activity, and the potential for soil liquefaction at the project site 
will need to be evaluated. 
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near 
the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build-up of 
excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by 
earthquakes. In this process, when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected 
to vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and 
decrease in volume, causing an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is 
unable to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, 
the effective stress (internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under sustained 
vibrations, the pore water pressure build-up could equal the overburden 
pressure, essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to 
behave as a viscous fluid. During liquefaction, the soil acquires sufficient mobility 
to permit both horizontal and vertical movements, and if not confined, will result 
in significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained 
sands and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the 
liquefaction characteristics of a soil deposit are as follows:

2.4.3 Soil Profile Type for Seismic Design 
Based on the subsurface materials encountered and our engineering analyses, 
the project site is underlain by potentially liquefiable soils. Therefore, the project 
site would need to be classified as Site Class F based on the ASCE Standard 
ASCE/SEI 7-10 (Table No. 20.3-1), referenced by the International Building 
Code, 2012 Edition. Site Class F project sites normally require a site-specific 
response analysis to determine the spectral accelerations due to the potentially 
liquefiable soils. However, based on the information provided, we understand 
that the proposed structure will be a two-story building. Therefore, we envision 
that the fundamental period of the relatively short building may be less than 
0.5 seconds. In accordance with Chapter 20, Site Classification Procedure for 
Seismic Design, contained in ASCE Standard ASCE/SEI 7-10, a site-specific 
response analysis will not be required to determine spectral accelerations for

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a surface fill layer underlain by 
alluvium, cobbles and boulders, volcanic cinders and clinker, and basalt rock formation 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 35 feet below the existing ground 
surface. Groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings at depths of approximately 
7 to 26 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field exploration, 
corresponding to elevations of about +77 to +102.2 feet MSL. 
An evaluation of the use of a light structure consisting of steel frame or wooden 
frame structure and a heavier concrete and CMU structure was performed for the 
ground conditions anticipated at the project site. Detailed discussions and preliminary 
foundation recommendations for the project are presented in the following sections.  
3.1 
Groundwater Monitoring 
Monitoring of the groundwater at the project site and near the project vicinity has 
been performed by Tom Nance Resources Engineering (TNRE). In TNRE’s 
memorandum, dated September 8, 2021, high groundwater levels were encountered in 
Borings B-103 and B-104. In addition, it was noted that groundwater was measured 
above the existing ground level in Boring B-103 and flowing out of the existing spring. 
These high groundwater levels have occurred subsequent to rainfall events. 
Based on the groundwater monitoring, it was recommended to raise the existing 
spring wall to prevent surface runoff water from entering the spring. In addition, 
excavation in the spring area should be very limited or not done at all. Furthermore, the 
installation of a subdrain system should be carefully performed to reduce the potential 
for draining the existing spring. 
Based on the above, groundwater levels near the existing ground surface were 
encountered in the boreholes located near the existing spring and at the southwest end 
of the future track and field area. At the new Bingham Building and Swing Classroom 
area, the highest groundwater levels were measured at about 5 and 8 feet, and 
10.2 feet below the existing ground surface, respectively.

3.2 
Bingham Building Foundations 
We understand that the new Bingham Building will be a two-story structure. 
Further details of the building were not available at the time this preliminary report was 
prepared. However, we understand it is desired to evaluate the use of a lightly loaded 
structure (e.g., steel frame or wood frame) and a heavily loaded structure (e.g. 
reinforced concrete or CMU) as possible options for the new Bingham Building. 
Therefore, the following subsections discuss the advantages and disadvantages of each 
of these types of structures and foundation systems that would be appropriate for each 
structure type. 
3.2.1 Lightly Loaded Structures 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, we believe that lightly loaded 
structures, such as steel frame or wood frame buildings, may be supported on 
shallow spread and/or continuous footings. However, as previously discussed, 
portions of the project site may be susceptible to liquefaction during the design 
earthquake event and some seismically induced ground settlements (up to about 
0.5 inches) may occur. Due to the localized nature of the encountered potentially 
liquefiable material, differential settlement due to liquefaction may be up to 
0.5 inches during the design earthquake event. The structure may also 
experience up to 1 inch of static total settlement and 0.5 inches of static 
differential settlement. This could result in up to 1 inch of differential settlement 
when combining the static and dynamic conditions.  
The advantage of using a light structure is that these structures are generally 
more flexible and tolerable to movement compared to heavier structures, such as 
reinforced concrete or CMU buildings, which are generally more rigid and tend to 
show distress at smaller movements. We believe that the above anticipated 
settlements should be acceptable for these flexible structures but should be 
evaluated by a licensed structural engineer during the final design for the 
building. 
As indicated above, the highest groundwater levels measured at the Bingham 
Building area were about 5 to 8 feet below the existing ground surface. These

measured groundwater levels should not affect the foundation of the building. 
However, if groundwater levels should extend above the existing foundation 
level, uplift hydrostatic forces on the building foundations would need to be 
considered. The disadvantage of the lightly loaded structure can be that the 
structure is not heavy enough to counteract these uplift forces. Ground anchors 
may need to be installed to hold down the building, or subdrains can be installed 
to reduce the hydrostatic pressure on the building foundation. 
Typically, these lightly loaded structures are less costly, and the construction 
time is shorter compared to the concrete and CMU building structures. 
3.2.2 Heavily Loaded Structures 
Heavy structures, such as those constructed from reinforced concrete or CMU, 
are generally more rigid in nature and may not be able to tolerate significant 
differential settlement without cracking. Therefore, we believe the heavier 
structures should not be supported on shallow foundations but instead be 
supported by a deep foundation system.   
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the relatively heavy loads 
anticipated from the two-story reinforced concrete or CMU structure, the 
proposed heavier structure should be supported on a deep foundation system 
consisting of cased micropiles or drilled shafts. The load-supporting capacity of 
the micropile or drilled shaft foundation would be derived primarily from skin 
friction between the micropile or drilled shaft bonded zone, and the 
cobble/boulder layers and/or basalt formation anticipated at the project site. 
A micropile consists of a small diameter (usually less than 12 inches) drilled and 
grouted pile with a center reinforcing steel bar. The micropile foundation typically 
is constructed by drilling a borehole, placing reinforcing steel in the hole, and 
grouting the borehole. Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed 
readily in access restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and ground 
conditions. In addition, installation of the micropiles generally causes minimal 
disturbance to adjacent structures, the adjacent soils, and the environment.

A drilled shaft consists of a larger diameter (usually 3 to 12 feet) borehole 
constructed with a reinforcing steel cage and cast-in-place concrete within the 
borehole. A drilled shaft is capable of providing substantial resistance against 
vertical and lateral loading. Typically, the drilled shaft equipment is larger and 
heavier than the micropile equipment. Due to the presence of sensitive 
underground piping near the existing ground surface in the Bingham Building 
area, the use of the lighter micropile equipment may be more desirable.  
The groundwater levels measured at about 5 to 8 feet below the existing ground 
surface at the Bingham Building area should not affect the installation of the 
micropiles or drilled shafts. 
The installation of a deep foundation system will be more expensive compared to 
installing shallow foundations. Typically, the installation cost for the micropiles 
will be less costly compared to drilled shafts.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
seven borings, designated as Boring Nos. 5 through 7, MW-107, MW-107A, MW-108, 
and MW-109A, extending to depths of about 25 to 35 feet below the existing ground 
surface. In addition, Boring Nos. B-6A, B-6B, and B-7A were drilled at the direction of 
Tom Nance Water Resource Engineering for groundwater information purposes. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were 
drilled using both a truck-mounted drill rig and a portable drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers and/or rotary coring tools.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-10.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the 
publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in 
Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977).

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the

would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Seven Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits.  The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.  Graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Five Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM D6913) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in 
soil classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Eight one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils under 
different surcharge loads.  The results of these tests are summarized on Plate B-3. 
One Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression test (ASTM D2850) was 
performed on a selected in-situ soil sample to evaluate the undrained shear strength of 
the on-site clayey soil.  The approximate in-situ effective overburden pressure was used 
as the applied confining pressure for the relatively “undisturbed” soil sample.  The test 
results and the stress-strain curve are presented on Plate B-4. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested.  Results of the direct 
shear test are presented on Plate B-5. 
Seven Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012 Method C) were performed on 
selected rock cores to evaluate the uniaxial compressive strength of the rock formation 
encountered.  Results of the uniaxial compression tests are presented on Plate B-6. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships.  The test results are presented on Plates B-7 and B-8. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-9 and B-10.
