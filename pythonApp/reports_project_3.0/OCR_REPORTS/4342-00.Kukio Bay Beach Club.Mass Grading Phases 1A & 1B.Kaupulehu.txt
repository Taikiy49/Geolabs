INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Kukio Bay Beach Club Mass Grading Phases 1A and 1B project The
Kukio Bay Beach Club project is located adjacent to the existing Hualalai Resort in the
District of North Kona on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our preliminary field exploration performed
in 1991 and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed Kukio Bay Beach Club Mass Grading Phases 1A and 1B project
These recommendations are intended for design of site grading underground utility
installation and roadway pavements only The findings and recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both of which are still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the site
appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe flows which spread and ponded as they
approached the ocean A a lava is typically characterized by a porous rough and irregular
flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock fragments including cobbles and
boulders Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an
internal structure of vesicular porous rock
GEOLABS INC
W O 4342 00 Page 5
Cavities are commonly encountered in basalt lavas especially pahoehoe lava These
cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes
intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets Lava tubes are
formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure
which may extend for a large distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are
generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is transmitted through
the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve as
the major water transmission features

Site Plan Plate 2 It should be noted that the borings were drilled in 1991

generally encountered basalt formation interbedded with thin layers of clinker materials

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce the potential
for contamination of the excavated materials to be used as embankment fill materials
We wish to point out that caution should be exercised by the contractor while clearing
and grading near the collapsed features which may represent potential lava tubes or
cavities that may further collapse under the load of heavy equipment
Based on the borings and our site reconnaissance loose a a materials and
collapsible pahoehoe lavas are prevalent at the ground surface throughout the site
GEOLABS INC
W O 4342 00 Page 9
These near surface materials are relatively porous and will compress when
subjected to structural and vehicular loads Based on our boring information the
thickness of the loose a a materials may be on the order of about 6 feet thick At
the commencement of grading we recommend that the fill areas of the project site
be ripped to a minimum depth of 3 feet below the existing ground surface to assist
in detecting and collapsing cavities and or voids contained in these porous
near surface materials
After the fill surfaces have been ripped to a depth of at least 3 feet below the ground
surface the ground should be leveled with the use of a Caterpillar D 10 bulldozer or
similar size bulldozer a minimum of four passes to provide a relatively level surface
After leveling of the rugged surfaces with a bulldozer we recommend that the fill
subgrades be proof rolled with a large vibratory drum roller minimum 20 tons for a
minimum of eight passes to help detect and collapse near surface cavities and or
voids The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 100 feet
per minute Yielding areas loose areas or cavities disclosed during the clearing and
proof rolling operations should be over excavated and backfilled with compacted fill
materials The depth of over excavation should be extended until dense underlying
materials are exposed and should be evaluated in the field by a representative of
Geolabs
In the finished subgrades in cut areas we recommend that the cut subgrades be

requirement for ripping the ground surface by a minimum depth of 3 feet in fill areas

cut subgrades provided that the finished cut subgrades are relatively level to operate
the vibratory drum roller

Fills and Backfills
In general we recommend that the fill areas be capped with a minimum 2 foot thick
layer of structural fill material to serve as a choking layer for the larger sized general
fill materials below Structural fill materials should consist of well graded granular
materials less than 6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent
the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass In general the excavated materials
a a lava and pahoehoe basalt may be re used as a source of structural fill
materials provided that the materials are processed to meet the maximum size and
gradation requirements presented above Where appropriate such as within utility
corridors the thickness of the structural fill layer may be increased to a minimum of
1 foot below the invert elevation of the utilities to facilitate future construction of the
utilities
Fill and backfill materials below the minimum 2 foot thick structural fill layer may
consist of general fill materials General fill and backfill materials should consist of
well graded granular materials the majority of which are less than 12 inches in size
with an absolute maximum dimension of 18 inches The excavated on site materials
if less than 18 inches in maximum dimension may be used as a source of general fill
or backfill materials other than for trench backfill provided that they are processed
to meet the above gradation requirements If the excavated materials do not contain
sufficient fines to produce a well graded material off site borrow or on site rock
crushing of larger rock fragments or boulders should be considered to provide the
required gradation and particle sizes to develop a well graded material

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to
at least 95 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills should be placed
in level lifts not exceeding 18 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other
types of acceptable compaction equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
majority of the rocks or 5 feet whichever is less Placement should be by haul trucks
traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the currently placed lift
Spreading of the rock fill should be by a large bulldozer to facilitate seating of the
rocks The rock fill should be watered heavily during placement by water trucks
traversing in front of the current rock lift face and spraying water continuously during
placement Compaction equipment such as a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar
heavy compaction equipment providing suitable energy to achieve the required
compaction should be utilized The number of passes usually six to eight passes
to be made to achieve proper compaction should be determined at the on set of site
grading by the performance of a trial compaction test or by plate bearing tests
Subsequent layers of rock fill above the previously placed rock fill should provide
a choking layer of smaller sized boulder materials to fill in the voids between the
boulders of the preceding rock fill layer

Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rocks boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number
of passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should
be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation
of a representative from Geolabs Based on this testing program the number of
passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction For
preliminary estimating purposes compaction may be accomplished by using a large

of about 100 feet per minute The roller should pass over the fill surface at least
eight times for structural fill and six times for general fill For general fill a
Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar size equipment may be used in lieu of the
vibratory drum roller

Standard section is to serve as a foundation or stabilizing course such that the subsequent
aggregate base and surfacing layers may be constructed on top of an inferior subgrade soil
The standard pavement section does not take into consideration good quality subgrade
conditions Since the subgrade soils along the proposed roadway alignment at the project
site will consist of a minimum of 2 feet of compacted structural fill material or basalt rock
formation it is our opinion that a subbase course for stabilization foundation purposes is not
necessary due to the high quality of the subgrade materials anticipated at the subject site
If the roadway subgrade exposes weaker soils at the site we recommend that the weaker
soils be removed and be replaced with structural fill materials 6 inch minus materials
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
lf a subbase course is mandatory we believe that the compacted structural fill or
basalt rock formation below the aggregate base layer can be considered as a subbase
material since its pavement support characteristics with CBR value over 60 would be equal
to or better than the aggregate subbase course required by the County of Hawaii Standard
section On this basis we recommend that the following pavement design be used for the
subject site
Asphalt Concrete Pavement
2 0 Inch Asphaltic Concrete
6 0 Inch Aggregate Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings extending to depths of approximately 11 5 to 20 feet below the existing ground
surface The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight
augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3 of Appendix A
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of
the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would
be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical
Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
