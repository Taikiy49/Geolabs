INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the new Design Build Commissary and Exchange Mall project in the Pearl Harbor area
on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses for the
proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design of building
foundations slabs on grade retaining structures site grading and pavements only The
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the
end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
GEOLABS iNC
W O Page 5
Pleistocene in age As the volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the
Schofield Plateau
The project site is located near the southwestern flank of the Aliamanu vent and Salt
Lake vent complex of the Honolulu Volcanic Series As the molten magma ascended
through these vents it reacted with the groundwater generating steam This steam caused
explosive eruptions which deposited low cones of ash material that have consolidated to
form tuff locally referred to as mudrock
The Aliamanu tuff is an older water laid ash deposit that was laid down when the
sea level was higher than the present level The Aliamanu eruptive event deposited its
material over alluvium originating from Halawa Stream
After this phase of volcanic activity ceased sedimentary deposits accumulated over
the Aliamanu tuff before the next phase of volcanic activity began Then the Salt Lake
eruptions began when the sea level was at about 40 to 60 feet below its present level The
Salt Lake eruptive event deposited its material over earlier tuffs from the older Aliamanu
events

project site is generally blanketed by a surface fill layer on the order of about 2 to 6 feet thick

field exploration However petroleum odors were detected in the soil rock sample retrieved
at a depth of about 5 feet in Boring Nos 109 drilled at the site

foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings extending through the upper
fill soils and bear on the underlying dense volcanic tuff formation locally known as
mudrock may be used to support the proposed new commissary and exchange mall
facility An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for design of footing

to 6 feet below the existing ground surface

new commissary and exchange mall we anticipate that the new building slabs may

Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and the structural loads
anticipated for the subject project we believe that shallow foundations consisting of spread
and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed building structures
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 5 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used
for the design of foundations bearing on the underlying dense voicanic tuff formation
locally known as mudrock Based on the available information and our field exploration
results volcanic tuff formation may be anticipated at depths ranging from about 2 to 6 feet
below the existing ground surface The bearing pressure provided above is for dead plus
live loads and may be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as those caused
by wind or seismic forces

Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration results and the design finished floor elevation for the
project we anticipate that the proposed building slabs may straddle across new compacted
fills and in situ dense volcanic tuff formation To reduce the potential for cracking of the slabs
due to differential settlement resulting from differing subgrade materials we recommend that
a 12 inch thick non expansive select granular capping fill layer be provided below the slab
cushion for more uniform support
Our laboratory test results indicate that the upper clayey fill soils exhibit moderately
high shrink sweil potential when subjected to moisture fiuctuations Unless slabs on grade
supported by these expansive soils are properly designed there is a potential for future
distress to the slabs on grade resulting from wetting and subsequent swelling of the
expansive clayey soils Therefore the 12 inch thick non expansive select granular capping
fill recommended above will also serve to reduce the potential for structural distress resulting
from the swelling of the on site expansive soils if encountered at the slab subgrade level
Prior to placement of the 12 inch non expansive select granular capping fill the clayey
subgrades if exposed at the over excavated subgrade level should be scarified to a
minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The select
granular capping fill material should provide a capping layer of moist soil above the
underlying potentially expansive subgrade soil thereby reducing the loss of moisture from
the clayey subgrade soils In our opinion the reduction in potential drying and wetting of the
subgrade soils should reduce the impact of shrink swell effects on the slab due to potential
heaving of the underlying expansive clayey soils when wetted

Mechanically Stabilized Earth Retaining Walls
We understand that a Mechanically Stabilized Earth Retaining Wall MSERW
system is also desired as an alternate solution to the conventional concrete retaining wall
option for the subject project The MSE retaining wall system is a composite wall system
which utilizes welded wire mats galvanized steel strips high density polyethylene or other
reinforcing elements to reinforce the backfill zone and to improve the shear strength of the
reinforced soil zone This composite system essentially forms a gravity wall structure with
an ability to tolerate significant total and differential settlements Depending on the site
conditions and design requirements MSE retaining walls are sometimes more desirable
due to the flexibility of the wall ease of construction high load carrying capacity and cost
Design of the MSE retaining wall system will need to take into consideration both the
external and internal stability of the structure In evaluating external stability the MSE
retaining wall must satisfy four stability conditions 1 bearing failure 2 translational sliding
3 overturning stability and 4 overall slope stability Geotechnical design parameters to
evaluate these stability conditions are presented in the following subsections Some of the
geotechnical parameters necessary in evaluating the internal stability of the MSE retaining
wall are presented in the Reinforced Fill and Backfill Materials subsection The external
and internal stability of the MSE retaining wall system will need to be evaluated by the design
engineer We have evaluated the overall slope stability for the MSE retaining wall and the
results are summarized in the Overall Slope Stability subsection

or similar heavy equipment for a minimum of eight passes to detect the presence of
potential near surface loose zones Loose areas disclosed during ciearing and proof
rolling operations should be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill materials
Fill and backfill materials up to 12 inches below the bottom of the slab cushion should
consist of well graded granular materials less than 6 inches in largest dimension The on site
excavated volcanic tuff rock materials may be used as fill or backfill materials provided they
are processed and reduced to a relatively well graded select granular material with a
maximum size of about 6 inches The non expansive select granular fill required under the

value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of not more than 1 percent when tested in

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility connections will be required for the project A granular
bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is
recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel
should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort on the backfill thus
reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material
to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
adequate support for the sides of the pipes Improper backfill material and placement could
result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site soils or select fill material The
backfill should be placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted

Generally the near surface weathered volcanic tuff formation may be excavated with normal

into the deeper formations may require the use of hoerams or chipping Care must be
exercised by the contractor to avoid over ripping which would disrupt the structure of the
rock formation resulting in a potential loss of bearing strength for footings in its vicinity

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
13 borings designated as Boring Nos 101 through 113 extending to depths between about
19 and 30 5 feet below the existing ground surface In addition four probe holes designated
as Probe Nos 1 through 4 were drilled at selected locations especially along the proposed
retaining wall alignment to evaluate the depths of the underlying volcanic tuff formation The
results of our field exploration were used to supplement the boring information provided by
the government for this design build project The approximate locations of the drilled borings
and probe holes are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using
truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist engineer who monitored the drilling operations
on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 13 The Logs of
Probe Holes are presented on Plate A 14
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the volcanic tuff formation encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the volcanic tuff formation The material
left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and
classification

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would
be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the
QD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Three one inch ring swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the near surface soils The results of these tests are summarized
on Plate B 1
Two sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 ASTM D 1140 were performed on
selected soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B 2
Six unconfined compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected rock
cores to evaluate their unconfined compressive strength of the volcanic tuff formation
encountered Results of the unconfined compression tests are presented on Plate B 3
