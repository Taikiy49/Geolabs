This letter report presents the findings from our due diligence study conducted for
the proposed Puunene Commercial Development in the District of Puunene, on the Island
of Maui. This letter report summarizes our findings based on our limited site exploration
and reconnaissance and review of the site conditions. This information is intended to
inform the owner about the anticipated subsurface conditions at the property. The findings
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report.
Project Considerations
A retail commercial development is planned at the site. Two major anchor tenants
along with smaller commercial lots, parking areas and other infrastructure will be
constructed.
Details of the preliminary grading plan or other design concepts were not
available at the time this report was prepared. However, we anticipate that the larger
building structures may be a combination of concrete masonry shear walls and steel
columns supporting roof loads by means of steel joist girders and steel bar joists.
Based on our experience in the project vicinity, we anticipate that the subsurface
conditions at the property would likely consist of alluvial and residual soil over basalt rock
at shallow depths.

subsurface conditions based on limited exploration to develop a soil/rock data set to
assess the feasibility of the project from a geotechnical engineering standpoint. In order to
accomplish this, we conducted an exploration program consisting of the following tasks
and work efforts:

Existing Site Conditions
Site Description
The site is comprised of a single parcel encompassing 80 acres of land south of the
intersection of Mokulele Highway and Mehameha Loop. The site is bordered by sugar
cane fields to the south; Mokulele Highway, sugar cane fields, and the Old Maui Airport
(Naval Air Station Puunene) to the east; the Maui Humane Society facility to the north; and
Mehameha Loop and sugar cane fields to the west as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
The site is presently being used for sugar cane cultivation. There is only one
structure on the site which is of reinforced concrete construction with a concrete
slab-on-grade floor.

There are large piles of debris at the northern end of the site between the building
and the Maui Humane Society generally consisting of concrete, basalt boulders, metal and
some wood. Also, in the northern area is a bank of irrigation water filters which are part of
the HC&S drip irrigation system for this field.
The topography of the site and its vicinity has a gentle slope down towards
Maalaea Bay to the southwest. The ground surface elevation ranges from about 80 feet
above Mean Sea Level (MSL) along the southern boundary to about 90 - 95 feet MSL at
the northern end of the site.
Precipitation falling on the property generally infiltrates the soil. The nearest
permanent surface water body is Kealia Pond, about 2.4 miles to the south-southwest.
According to the Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) Flood
Insurance Rate Map (FIRM), the site is in an area where flood hazards are considered to
be minimal (Zone C), except for a narrow strip along Mokulele Highway, which is
considered to be between the areas with a one percent and a 0.2 percent chance of
annual flooding (Zone B).
Soil Conditions
The U.S. Department of Agriculture classified the surface soils in the area of the
property as Ewa silty clay loam (EaA) with minor areas of Pulehu cobbly clay loam (PrA)
and Pulehu silt loam (PpA). These soils are well drained soils developed in basins and
alluvial fans from material derived from the weathering of basic igneous rock. Runoff is
slow and erosion hazard is slight.
Regional Geology
The Island of Maui is composed of two (2) shield volcanoes - West Maui and
Haleakala. Of these, only Haleakala has been active during recent history, with a single
eruptive event in the area of La Perouse Bay in about 1793.
The assessed property is situated on the isthmus between West Maui and
Haleakala. Available geologic information indicates that the site is underlain by windblown
sands from the Kahului dune area, alluvium originating from the western slopes of
Haleakala and, at depth, rocks of the Kula Volcanic Series of Haleakala. At the site, the
surface soils are generally very fine silty sand representing a mixture of windblown sand
and alluvial soil.

Anticipated Subsurface Conditions
Four borings were drilled in the accessible portions of the site. Since the area is
currently under cultivation, boring locations were limited to existing dirt access roads.
Additionally, the building layout concept was revised after the borings were drilled. Based
on the limited boring data, we anticipate the project site may be underlain by 5 to 10 feet of
fill, alluvium, and residual soil over hard rock formation. The fill, alluvium, and residual soils
consisted primarily of stiff to hard clayey silt. The rock formation consisted of very hard
andesite and basalt. Groundwater level was not encountered in the borings during the field
exploration. However, groundwater levels may vary due to rainfall, irrigation, and other
factors.
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D 4318) was performed on a selected soil sample
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test result is summarized on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of the test result is
provided on Plate B-1.
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio Test (ASTM D 1883) was performed on a
bulk sample of the near-surface soils and potential borrow materials to evaluate the
pavement support characteristics of the soils. The CBR test result was also used to
evaluate the quality of the materials tested for use as fills. The test result is presented on
Plate B-2.
Preliminary Recommendations
Based on our preliminary findings, it is our opinion that the site is suitable for
development from a geotechnical engineering standpoint. We envision that the primary
geotechnical engineering considerations for this project may include:
° Building Foundation
° Removal of surface soil disturbed by cultivation
e Hard rock excavation
Building Foundation
Based on the subsurface information, we anticipate that spread footings may be
used to support the new building. The footing foundations would bear on the very stiff
near-surface soil or compacted fill.
For the preliminary design of foundations bearing on the in-situ silty clays and
clayey silts, an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may
be used to support the main building. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and
may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or
seismic forces.

In general, footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade. Foundations located next to utility trenches or easements should be
embedded below a one horizontal to one vertical (1H:1V) imaginary plane extending
upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines. This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures
with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement.
Our field exploration indicated that the surficial soils are generally dry. Therefore,
the subgrade soils at the bottom of footing and concrete slab elevations should be
scarified to a depth of at least 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above
optimum moisture, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction prior to
placement of reinforcing steel or concrete. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures.
The near-surface soils encountered at the proposed site consist of silty clays and
clayey silts, which exhibit a low to moderate potential for shrinking and swelling when
subjected to moisture fluctuations. Therefore, to reduce the potential for structural distress
to the slabs-on-grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the silty clays and clayey
silts, we recommend that the soils beneath the slab areas be over-excavated and replaced
with a minimum of 12 inches of non-expansive select borrow fill material below the
concrete slab.
The select borrow capping fill should be placed as soon as possible after the
subgrade soils are prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented herein.
The prepared capping fill and the underlying subgrade soils should be wetted and kept
moist until final concrete placement. To reduce the potential for future moisture infiltration
and subsequent damage to floor coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is
recommended below slabs that will have floor coverings.
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project. We
recommend supporting the concrete walkways on 6 inches of non-expansive select
granular fill or select borrow subbase compacted to at least 90 percent relative
compaction. Control joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the
walkways with expansion joints at right-angle intersections.
The thickened edges of slab adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at
least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade. Areas adjacent to the slab
should be backfilled tightly against the slab with on-site soil, and graded to divert water
away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the foundations.

Removal of Surface Soil Disturbed by Cultivation
The site has been in active sugar cane cultivation for many years. Therefore, we
anticipate that the near-surface soils have been disturbed and contain significant amounts
of organic materials, which render them unsuitable for structural support. We estimate that
the thickness of this unsuitable layer may be about 18 inches. This unsuitable material
should be stripped and disposed of off-site or placed in a non-structural area, i.e.
landscape.
Hard Rock Excavation
Hard rock formation is anticipated at depths of about 5 to 10 feet below the existing
ground surface. Utility excavations that extend below this level will probably require the
use of hoerams, or chipping. The rock material excavated may be too large for re-use as
trench backfill and on-site crushing and processing of the excavated materials may be
necessary.
Subgrade Preparation
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft and yielding
areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill
should be over-excavated to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well-compacted engineered fills. The excavated soft and/or organic soils
should be properly disposed of off-site and/or used in landscape areas, where appropriate.
General fill materials required to raise the existing ground surface to the design
finished subgrades may consist of the on-site excavated soils, provided that they are free
of vegetation, deleterious materials, and rock fragments no greater than 3 inches in
maximum dimension. The 12-inch non-expansive imported select borrow required under
the building slab should consist of materials such as crushed basalt. The material should
be well-graded from coarse to fine with particles no larger than 2 inches in largest
dimension and should contain less than 15 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve.
The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 25 or more and should have a
maximum swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883.
General fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The non-expansive select
granular fill materials should be placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness,
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to at least
90 percent relative compaction.
