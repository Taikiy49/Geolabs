2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes the older West Maui
Volcano Tertiary Epoch and the more recent East Maui Volcano also known as
Haleakala Volcano Pleistocene Epoch The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently
sloping plain located between these two volcanoes The Isthmus of Maui was created
by lava flows from Haleakala Volcano ponding against the West Maui Volcano and is
comprised of alluvium washed from the slopes of West Maui and East Maui Haleakala
Volcanoes
After the major formation of East Maui by the Honomanu Volcanic Series and the
Kula Volcanic Series a long quiescent period followed during which time canyons and
large valleys were carved in the East Maui volcanic shield Volcanic eruptions then
resumed creating new lava flows cinder cones and ash deposits which represented a
new series called the Hana Volcanic Series The cones built by the Hana Volcanic
Series eruptions ranged from small spatter cones a few feet high to large cinder cones
more than a mile across at the base and 600 feet high The resulting lavas poured
down the major valleys such as and Waihoi and along the
slopes of the older volcanic shield burying a large portion of the eroded topography on
the southern and eastern ends of East Maui
Although the Hana lavas were generally more fluid and the lava flows tended to
be thinner and less massive than the preceding Kula lavas thick accumulation of lava
did occur in the valleys and valley heads These eruptions were often separated by
intervals long enough to allow weathering and erosion of the lava surface before it was
buried by the next flow thus creating weathered layers between successive flows
The lava rock from these Hana eruptions comprises the rock encountered at the
project site beneath a relatively thin layer of soil This rock probably originated from the
Hana lava flows that came down the older volcanic slope surface and built its thickness
gradually by the hardening of successive lava flows adding a relatively thin lava layer

Predrilling
Our borings indicated that the project site is underlain by a relatively thick layer of
undocumented fill that contains cobble to boulder size particles The presence of
these over sized particles will likely impede the pile driving process and may
cause the piles to be damaged during driving In order to facilitate the pile driving
operations through these potential obstructions predrilling at the proposed pile
locations to a depth of about 30 feet below the water surface would be required
prior to pile installation
We wish to emphasize that the diameter of the predrilled holes should be limited
to the diagonal dimension of the pile to provide the driven piles with sufficient soil
contact for lateral load resistance In addition the annular space between the
piles and predrilled holes should be grouted with tremie concrete to provide the
recommended lateral load capacity
Uplift Load Resistance
Uplift loads imposed on the driven pile foundations may be resisted by a
combination of the dead weight of the driven pile and shear along the pile surface
and the adjacent soils In general an uplift load capacity of up to 30 kips per pile
may be used in design of the pile foundation The uplift load capacity provided
above includes the weight of the pile and is intended for transient load applications
Therefore the uplift load capacity should not be further increased for transient load
applications For sustained uplift loads we recommend reducing the above uplift
load capacity by 50 percent to resist sustained uplift loads The project structural
engineer should check the capacity of the pile member in tension
Lateral Load Resistance
Lateral loads imposed on the loading dock structure may be resisted by the lateral
load capacity of the driven piles Lateral load resistance of driven piles is a function
of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the pile allowable deflection
at the top of the pile and the induced moment in the pile The lateral load analyses
were performed using the program LPILE plus for Windows which is a

Pile Hammer Selection
The piles should be driven with a hammer capable of delivering a rated energy of
about 60 000 foot pounds of energy per blow The hammer should be equipped
with an energy control level such that driving within the loose soft soils may be
controlled at a lower energy to reduce the potential for tensile stress development
within the piles which could damage or cause cracking of the piles
Consideration should be given to the matching of the pile hammer with the weight
of the piles to be driven when determining driving equipment to be used This will
result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy to the top of the
pile and also reducing potential damage to the piles Geolabs should review the
pile driving equipment proposed to evaluate whether the equipment is capable of
installing the piles to the recommended capacity
The contractor should forward pile and driving equipment data forms for review
prior to construction Geolabs should review the submittal and assess the
driveability of piles with the use of the selected hammer A wave equation analysis
should be performed after selection of the pile driving equipment to assess the
driveability of the piles and to establish the preliminary pile driving criteria for the
pile installation program
Pile Driving Criteria
For preliminary design of the 24 inch square concrete piles pile driving may be
terminated when the estimated pile tip elevation of 30 feet MLLW has been
achieved for the loading dock structure The pile driving may also be terminated
when the aggregate blow counts are at least 10 blows per inch for the last 3 inches
or 100 blows for the last foot These preliminary driving criteria should be
confirmed and or modified after selection of the pile driving equipment and
performance of a wave equation analysis

order to reduce the potential for future distress to the reconstructed pavements a
pavement probing and grouting program should be implemented to locate potential
cavities and or voids that may exist due to undermining action from the high waves
For estimating purposes we recommend drilling probe holes at 10 feet on center
spacing in a staggered grid pattern across the pavement area The probe holes should
be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to a depth of at least 8 feet below the
existing pavement surface
If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing
operation additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacings to help delineate
the vertical and lateral extent of the cavities and or voids The probe holes and cavities
discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout with a slump of about 6 to
9 inches injected at low to moderate pressures
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and or voids at the site we
recommend obtaining unit prices for additional probing and grouting during bidding In
addition the probe drill should be available on site until the probing and grouting
operations are completed The contractor should also be made aware that a longer lag
time between probing grouting operations and pavement construction might be required
in the construction schedule
A Geolabs representative should observe the probing and grouting program This
would allow our firm to monitor the presence of cavities and to allow additional
recommendations to be made if excess grout take and or changed conditions are
observed

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling
three borings extending to depths of about 26 to 27 5 feet below the existing pavement
surface The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using truck mounted drilling equipment equipped with coring tools
Our engineer classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field and observed the drilling operations on a near continuous
basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation and testing in the
laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on the Log Legend Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method which uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and
Minerals
