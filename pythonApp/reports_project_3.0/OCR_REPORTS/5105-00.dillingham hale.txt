PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS ~
The project site is located at 524 Kaaahi Street in the District of Honolulu of the
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on
the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
Based on the information provided, we understand that it is proposed to construct a
new concrete stairway at the existing Dillingham Hale, a three-story apartment building.
We envision that only minor grading work will be involved in the construction of the new
stairway.

this, we conducted an exploration program consisting of the following tasks and work

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The project site is located at Kaaahi Street in the District of Honolulu on the Island
of Oahu, Hawaii. The existing ground surface at the project site generally consists of fairly
level asphaltic concrete pavement. The approximate location of the project site within the
Dillingham Hale property is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
FIELD EXPLORATION
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling one boring,
designated as Boring No. 1, extending to a depth of about 16.5 feet below the existing
ground surface. The boring was drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight, solid-stem augers. In addition, probing was conducted in the boring
extending to a depth of 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate
location of the boring drilled at the site is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.

The materials encountered in the boring were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer, who monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System, as shown on the Boring Log Legend, Plate 3. Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Log of Boring, Plate 4.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the boring drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling
30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the drilled boring in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel
Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
LABORATORY TESTING
Selected soil samples from the field exploration were tested in the laboratory with
an emphasis on evaluating the moisture, density, plasticity, and strength characteristics.
The following laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples obtained from the
field exploration.
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties. The test results are presented on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample
depths. — ;
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C 117 & C 136) was performed on a selected
sample of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate 5.
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D 3080) was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics. Results of the direct shear test are
presented on Plate 6.

underlain by a thin layer of fill material extending to a depth of about 4 feet below the
existing ground surface. Underlying the fill material were medium dense to dense silty
sand with some cementation extending to a depth of about 8.5 feet below the existing
ground surface. Below the sandy materials, soft/loose lagoonal deposits were
encountered, extending to the maximum depth drilled of 16.5 feet below the existing
ground surface. Probing conducted in the boring extended to 21.5 feet below the existing

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Detailed discussion of our geotechnical engineering recommendations for design of
structure foundations and site grading are presented in the following sections of this letter
report.
Structure Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we recommend
that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and/or continuous footings be used
to support the new stairway structure. An allowable bearing pressure of up to
2,500 pounds per square foot (psf) may be used for the design of foundations bearing on
the recompacted in-situ soils and/or compacted fills. This bearing value is for
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as
those caused by wind or seismic forces. The bottom of footings should be embedded a
minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent ground level.
The bottom of the footing excavations should be compacted to at least 90 percent
relative compaction prior to the placement of reinforcing steel and concrete. If soft and/or
loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavation, the soft and/or
loose materials should be over-excavated to expose firm and unyielding materials. The
resulting excavation should be backfilled with select granular fill material compacted to at
least 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry
density of the soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557.
Foundations. located next to utility trenches or easements should be embedded.
below a 45-degree imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility
trench. Alternatively, the footing should be extended to a depth as deep as the inverts of
the utility lines. This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade
structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable
foundation settlement.
If the foundation is designed and constructed in accordance with the
recommendations presented herein, we estimate total footing settlements under the
anticipated design loads to be on the order of less than 1 inch. Differential settlements
between adjacent footings supported on similar materials may be approximately
0.5 inches.

Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth
pressure developed against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient
of friction of 0.30 may be used for footings bearing on the compacted subgrade soils or
compacted fill. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf). This
assumes that the soil around the footings is well compacted. Unless covered by
pavements or slabs, the passive pressure resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should
be neglected. The passive pressure resistance should be further reduced for foundations
located on slopes.
We recommend that footing excavations be observed by a representative from
Geolabs prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm the foundation
bearing conditions and the required embedment depths.
Backfilling
In general, the excavated on-site soils may be re-used as a source of backfill
materials provided that they are free of deleterious materials (vegetation) and are
screened of particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension. If required, imported fill
materials needed for the backfilling should consist of materials with a low expansion
potential. Imported general fill materials should consist of materials with particle sizes of
3 inches or less in maximum dimension. The material should have a laboratory California
Bearing Ratio (CBR) greater than 20 and a swell potential of less than 1 percent when
tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883. Imported fill materials should
be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to
the project site for the intended use.
General fills and backfills should be moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. In general,
compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or other
similar types of acceptable compaction equipment.
A representative from Geolabs should observe the site grading and earthwork
operations for the project. It is important that a representative from our office be present to
observe the site preparation to evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered
during the excavation process, and whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to
those encountered in our exploration.

It is recommended that Geolabs be retained to provide geotechnical engineering

report, then appropriate modifications to the design should be made.
LIMITATIONS
The findings and discussions submitted in this report are based, in part, upon
information obtained from the field boring. Variations of subsoil conditions beyond the
boring may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident
until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to
re-evaluate the findings and discussions provided in this letter report.
The location of the field boring indicated in this report are approximate, having been
estimated by taping and pacing from the features shown on the Site Plan transmitted by
Bryce E. Uyehara, A.l.A., Inc. on June 20, 2003. The physical location of the boring —
should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used.
The stratification lines shown on graphic representations of the boring depict the
approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual transition.
As previously mentioned, groundwater was encountered in our boring at the time of our
exploration. However, it must be noted that groundwater level changes may occur due to
tidal fluctuations, seasonal precipitation, surface runoff, and other factors.
