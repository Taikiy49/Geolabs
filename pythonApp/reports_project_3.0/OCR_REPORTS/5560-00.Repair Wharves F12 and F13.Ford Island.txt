1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Repair Wharves F12 and F13 project on the northern side of Ford
Island on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations derived
from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses The findings
and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of
this report

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one third of the island The
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age
in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island The Koolau
Volcano reached the end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago
The geologic conditions underlying the project site are the result of a complex
series of deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio eustatic sea level
changes and post erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch Following
the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano alluvial soil materials were deposited in
a deltaic or estuarine environment in the Pearl Harbor area
The sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 300 feet below and
rising about 100 feet above the present sea level Concurrent with a rise in sea level
during the late Pleistocene era eruption of the Salt Lake Makalapa member of the
Honolulu Volcanic Series mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash
which consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally known as mudrock layers
Subsequent erosion dissected and thinned mantles of those volcanic tuff
Ford Island is a remnant of the hard reef rock that once covered a large portion
of the embayment and was subsequently carved away in many places by streams
during lower stands of the sea During the Recent Epoch as sea level rose to its current
stand the area was covered with soft and loose lagoonal marine deposits and
additional sediments have continued to accumulate along the lochs and channels of the
harbor These deposits were subsequently covered with man made fills placed during
the past 40 to 60 years for land development along the shoreline

2 2 Site Description
The project site is on the northern side of Ford Island and northeast of the USS
Utah Memorial at Pearl Harbor on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The existing
Wharves F12 and F13 are connected and extend in a southwest northeast direction
along the Ford Island shoreline as shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 through 2 3
The existing Wharves F12 and F13 consist of a concrete pier deck supported on
18 inch square concrete composite piles from Bent Nos 1 through 139 Based on the
information provided the top of deck elevation is at approximately 10 feet MLLW The
total length of Wharves F12 and F13 is about 1 400 linear feet Both Wharves F12 and
F13 have trestle approach structures for land access
The existing timber fender piles will be demolished and removed along the entire
Wharves F12 and F13 Based on our field observations the existing timber fender pile
system is spaced at about 5 feet center to center and appears to be in relatively poor
condition
The existing steel sheet pile bulkhead structures are at the end of the Wharf F12
trestle approach The existing bulkhead structures consist of one 30 foot long and
two 51 foot long segments installed along the shoreline for protection and grade
separation as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 1 The two 51 foot segments were
structurally connected by horizontal tie rods that are approximately 8 feet below the
existing ground surface

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The subsurface conditions along the existing Wharves F12 and F13 consisted of
very soft harbor deposits over lagoonal coralline detritus materials and stiff alluvium
extending to the maximum depth drilled of approximately 182 feet below the existing
concrete pier deck equivalent to about Elevation 172 feet MLLW The mudline was at
about Elevation 35 feet MLLW The subsurface conditions at the project site were
highly variable The thickness of harbor deposit ranged from less than 10 feet at
Wharf F13 to about 110 feet near the middle of Wharf F12
In general the subsurface conditions encountered in our borings are similar to
the information presented in the RFP contract documents However the drilled borings
at Wharf F12 shown in the RFP documents were terminated at depths of about 92 to

documents especially in the areas from Bent Nos 1 through 76 The recommended

probing program

Lateral Load Analyses
In general the concrete fender pile system will absorb the energy generated by
vessel berthing velocities Therefore the lateral load capacity of the 24 inch square
concrete fender pile is one of the primary design considerations for the fender pile
system Based on the information provided by the project structural engineer the
lateral load imposed on a single pile is approximately 49 kips The lateral load may
act at a depth between about 3 3 and 9 3 feet below the top of the pile Therefore a
distributed load of about 8 2 kips per lineal foot applied between about 3 3 and
9 3 feet below the top of pile was used in our analyses of the fender piles
Lateral load analyses were performed using the LPILE plus for Windows which is a
microcomputer adaptation of a finite difference laterally loaded pile program
originally developed at the University of Texas at Austin The program solves for
deflection and bending moment along a pile under lateral loads as a function of
depth Analyses were carried out with the use of non linear p y curves to
represent soil moduli The lateral deflection was then computed using the
appropriate soil moduli at various depths

3 2 Sheet Pile Bulkhead
Based on the information provided for the bulkhead repair we understand that
the new sheet pile will be installed in front of the existing sheet pile The new sheet piles
will be connected by the existing tie rods In general the new sheet pile bulkheads will
serve to provide lateral support for the shoreline protection and maintain stability of the
soil mass
Design of the sheet pile bulkhead will need to consider both overturning stability
and overall slope stability Geotechnical design parameters to evaluate these stability
conditions are presented in the following subsections herein The structural engineer will
need to evaluate the overturning stability We have evaluated the overall slope stability
for the new sheet pile bulkhead and the results are presented in Subsection
Lateral Earth Pressures
In general the bulkhead should be designed to resist the lateral earth pressures
due to the adjacent soils and surcharge effects Therefore we recommend
designing the new sheet pile bulkhead for lateral earth pressures due to the in situ
soils which include the fill materials weathered coral formation and coralline
detritus
Based on the borings drilled the loose harbor lagoonal deposits in front of the
sheet pile extend from the mudline Elevation 3 to 7 feet MLLW to about
Elevation 10 feet MLLW overlying about 7 feet of weathered coral formation and
medium dense coralline detritus material extending to about Elevation 39 feet
MLLW The fill materials behind the bulkhead extend to the top of weathered coral
formation at about Elevation 10 feet MLLW Based on these conditions the
recommended lateral earth pressure parameters for the new sheet pile bulkhead
design are provided on the Lateral Earth Pressure Diagram Plate 4 We also have

Overall Slope Stability
We have evaluated the overall slope stability of the proposed sheet pile bulkhead
Slope stability analyses consisting of two dimensional slope cross sections using
Bishop s simplified method of limit equilibrium analysis were performed on the
sheet pile bulkhead structure Based on our analyses the factors of safety for
overall slope stability of the sheet pile bulkhead are at least 1 5 which is the
minimum factor of safety normally recommended
Unbalanced Hydraulic Pressures
For waterfront structures such as sheet pile supported piers the maximum lateral
pressures may be expected when the tide is at its lowest level A receding tide may
cause higher water levels behind the sheet pile than in front of it depending on the
type of soils behind the sheet piles The high water level behind the sheet pile wall
will introduce additional seepage pressure due to hydrostatic load For the soils
encountered at the project site a hydrostatic pressure estimate based on the
maximum tide difference conservative may be used for the sheet pile design

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Corrosion Protection
Due to salt water exposure and the marine environment we envision that the new
sheet pile bulkheads would be subject to a highly corrosive environment Therefore
we recommend designing metallic substructures for protection against corrosion
Consideration may be given to the use of other types of sheet piles such as vinyl
composite or other types of non corrosive materials in lieu of the conventional
steel sheet piles If a steel sheet pile system is elected for the project a corrosion
engineer should be consulted for detailed recommendations pertaining to corrosion
protection of the steel sheet pile bulkhead
Construction Considerations
The borings generally encountered weathered coral formation consisting of densely
cemented coral and sand with localized hard zones and soft void pockets at
elevations varying from about 10 to 17 feet MLLW We envision that the tip
elevation of the new sheet pile bulkhead will extend into the coral formation
Therefore it should be noted that the presence of the hard coral formation may
pose some difficulties during sheet pile installation
As a result we believe that predrilling will likely be necessary to facilitate installation
of the new sheet piles through the weathered coral formation and or other
obstructions Based on the specified size of the sheet pile PZ 27 we believe that
the side of the predrilling hole should be limited to no more than 12 inches
Depending on the hardness of the coral formation the predrilling spacing may
varies as close as 2 feet for hard coral or further apart for relatively soft coral
The lateral earth pressure parameters provided on the attached Plate 4 have taken
into account the anticipated effect of predrilling through the weathered coral
formation

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling eight borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 8 In general the borings
extended to depths of about 56 to 182 feet below the existing pier deck surface The
borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with rotary wash coring
tools The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plans
Plates 2 1 through 2 3
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual
observation and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic
representations of the materials encountered in the borings drilled are presented on the
Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 8
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM Test D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 or 24 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of coral and or rock materials encountered at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the coral and or rock formation The
coral and or rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for
examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of soil and or rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material
recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if
3 7 feet of material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be
74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of coral and or rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run
that is sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting
drilling induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a
5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of
Borings as RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality
of the coral and or rock based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of
Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality
The rippability or excavation characteristics of a coral and or rock mass are a
function of the relative hardness of the coral and or rock its relative quality brittleness
and fissile characteristics A dense coral rock formation with a high RQD value would be
very difficult to rip or excavate and would probably require more arduous methods of
excavation

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Eight Sieve Analysis tests ASTM C 117 and C 136 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution of the soils are
provided on Plates B 1 and B 2
Five Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results
of the tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
and presented graphically on Plate B 3
Four Direct Shear tests ASTM D 3080 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the shear strength characteristics The results of the direct shear
tests are presented on Plates B 4 through B 7
Three Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression TXUU tests
ASTM D 2850 were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained
shear strength of the silty and clayey soils encountered The approximate in situ
effective overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the
relatively undisturbed soil sample The test results and the stress strain curve are
presented on Plates B 8 through B 10
