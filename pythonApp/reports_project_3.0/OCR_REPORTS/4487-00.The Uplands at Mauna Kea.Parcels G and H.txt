REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity during
recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea
which are both still active
The project site is situated on the northwestern flank of Mauna Kea which has formed
the west central portion of the island The project site appears to be underiain by basaltic lava
GEOLABS INC
W O 4487 00 Page 5
flows of the Hamakua Volcanic Series of Mauna Kea which were deposited during the
Pleistocene Epoch Stearns and Macdonald 1946
The Hamakua Volcanic Series is typically composed of basaltic lava flows which are
mantled by a relatively thin layer of palagonitized ash locally referred to as Pahala Ash The
ash soil at the project site differs from Pahala Ash found in other portions of the Island of
Hawaii in that the in situ moisture content is generally lower and the soil exists in a dry friable
state The soils at the site have been subjected to wind erosion which has resulted in the
formation of a desert pavement on the surface of the site This desert pavement is a surficial
layer of cobbles and gravel which were left behind as the finer ash was blown away
The lava formation at the site appears to be of a a and pahoehoe basalt type flows
which spread and ponded as they approached the ocean A a lava are typically characterized
by a porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock
fragments including cobbles and boulders Typically a more dense and layered lava rock
material is contained within the lava flow core Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth
rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are
commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas These cavities were formed when the lavas were
still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and
pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling
flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally
along the flow Blisters and pockets inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal
extent

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly
cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable materials
should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce the potential for
contamination of the excavated materials The near surface materials at the project
site generally consist of basalt cobbles and gravel mixed with varying amounts of
volcanic ash We anticipate that localized areas especially near the bottom of gullies
and topographic low areas may consist primarily of volcanic ash If soils consisting
primarily of volcanic ash is encountered the ash soils should be over excavated and
backfilled with well compacted fill materials
After clearing and grubbing finished subgrades in cut areas and areas designated to
receive fills should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches where possible
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum
of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to at
least 95 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills should be placed in
level lifts not exceeding 18 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the
optinum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density Compaction
should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other types of
acceptable compaction equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
majority of the rocks or 5 feet whichever is less Placement should be by haul trucks
traversing previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the currently placed lift
Spreading of the rock fill should be by bulldozer to facilitate seating of the rock
GEOLABS INC
W O 4487 00 Page 13
The rock fill should be watered heavily during placement by water trucks traversing in
front of the current rock lift face and spraying water continuously during placement
Compaction equipment such as a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy
compaction equipment providing suitable energy to achieve the required compaction
should be utilized The number of passes to be made to achieve proper compaction
usually six to eight passes should be determined at the on set of site grading by the
performance of a trial compaction test or by plate bearing tests Subsequent layers of
rock fill above the previously placed rock fill should provide a choking layer of
smaller sized boulder materials to fill in the voids between the boulders of the
preceding rock fill layer
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of rocks
boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of passes
usually six to eight passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of
compaction should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under
the observation of a representative from Geolabs Based on this testing program the
number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction

Cut and Fill Slopes
Based on the grading plans cut and fill slopes up to about 30 feet in vertical height are
planned at the project site Based on the available subsurface information it appears
that most of the cut slopes would expose the basalt cobbles and gravel with volcanic
ash material and closely to moderately fractured basalt formations ranging from
medium hard to hard with occasional clinker layers and potential small voids
In general we believe that the cut slopes planned at the site may be designed with a
slope inclination of or flatter It should be noted that on site ash soils are
highly susceptible to erosion by wind and surface water runoff Therefore freshly
graded cut slopes should be protected by appropriate slope planting or other means
to reduce the potential for erosion of the exposed materials as soon as possible after
the slope faces are exposed It is generally difficult to provide a smooth cut slope face
when excavating in basalt rock formations If over cutting of the slopes occurs
consideration should be given to flattening the slope or leaving the slope as is
Patching the over cut areas with sliver fills is not recommended due to the potential for
erosion of the fill materials in the patched areas
Permanent fill slopes constructed of the general fill and structural fill materials
recommended herein may be designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter Fills
placed on slopes steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched The fill slope face
should be finished to be relatively smooth and well compacted The fill slope face
should be free of large boulders greater than 18 inches in largest dimension and also
should be free of voids which would allow erosion and migration of fines to occur
As previously mentioned cut and fill slopes up to about 30 feet in vertical height may
be expected at this site In general we believe that slope benches may be omitted for
cut and fill slopes up to 40 feet in vertical height on this project For cut and fill slopes
GEOLABS INC
W O 4487 00 Page 15
with a vertical height of 40 feet or greater if considered for this project appropriate
measures should be considered to reduce the velocities of the surface water runoff and
potential erosion of the slope face One method to reduce the potential for erosion due
to surface water runoff is to provide a minimum 8 foot wide bench at the mid point of
the slope The cut slope bench may be omitted for cut slopes exposing dense to hard
basalt rock formation
Where slope benches are provided especially for fill slopes swales should be
provided on the benches to collect and divert surface water away from the slopes to
appropriate discharge outlets In addition construction of earth berms interceptor
ditches and the use of geotextile fabrics over the fill slope face may be considered to
reduce the potential for erosion thus enhancing the long term stability of the planned
fill slopes Appropriate slope planting or other erosion control measures to reduce the
potential for erosion of the exposed slopes should be implemented as soon as possible
after the finished slope faces are completed

In general the purpose of the subbase course required in the County of Hawaii
Standard section is to serve as a foundation or stabilizing course such that the subsequent
base and surfacing layers may be constructed on top of an inferior subgrade soil The
standard pavement section does not take into consideration good quality subgrade conditions
Since the subgrade conditions along the proposed roadway alignment at the proposed
Uplands at Mauna Kea Parcels G and H subdivision project will consist primarily of basalt
cobbles and gravel with some volcanic ash compacted granular fill material or basalt rock
formation it is our opinion that a subbase course for stabilization foundation purposes is not
necessary due to the high quality of the subgrade materials anticipated at the subject site If
the roadway subgrade exposes poorer subgrade soils volcanic ash at the site we
recommend that the poorer subgrade soils volcanic ash be removed and be replaced with
structural fill material 6 inch minus material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction

aggregate base course layer can be considered as a subbase material since its pavement

Four falling head and one constant head percolation tests were conducted in four drilled
borings Boring 4 and 5 to provide data for design of the proposed seepage pits

test the boreholes that appeared to encounter varying degrees of rock fractures and
embedded clinker seams The results of our percolation tests are presented in Appendix D
of this report
It should be emphasized that the hydraulic conductivity of the basalt rock formation at
the site is highly variabie due to the presence or absence of significant voids in the lava or the
presence of embedded clinker seams which greatly affect the disposal capacity of a seepage
well or seepage pit In general the greater the number of clinker seams and or voids
encountered by the seepage well excavation the higher the overall permeability and disposal
capacity

The finished grades outside the residential structures should be sloped to shed water

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 at the approximate locations shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 The depth of our borings extended to about 16 to 21 feet below the existing
ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers and rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 6
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using diamond
core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses
a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit
is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative quality
of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from a coring
attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material is recovered
from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs
of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced fractures or
breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the RQD would be
50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50 Generally the
following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the Practical Handbook
of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX D
Percolation Testing
APPENDIX D
Percolation Testing
Percolation tests were performed by using the constant and falling head methods in
four of the drilled borings Boring 4 and 5 The drilled borings have a diameter of
about 4 inches and depths of about 4 2 to 8 5 feet at the time of testing A water truck was
used to pump water into the test holes for the constant head test method The flow rate was
recorded by timing and calculating the volume as a relatively constant flow rate was achieved
The head levels within the test borings were monitored by an electric water level sounder
during the injection of water
For the falling head method the boreholes were filled with water and the water was
allowed to percolate into the ground The water levels and corresponding times were recorded
for the calculations of subsurface permeability The results of the percolation tests are
presented on Plates D 1 through D 5
