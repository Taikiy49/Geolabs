SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered stiff/dense fills and/or alluvial soils 
overlying residual/saprolite soils extending to the maximum depth drilled of 
approximately 21.5 feet below the ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in 
the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. However, it should be noted that 
groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Based on the generally competent stiff subsurface conditions, we recommend 
using shallow spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new building planned 
at the site. We recommend an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf to be used for 
the design of footings bearing on the recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills 
placed to achieve the design finished grades. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads 
and may be increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind 
or seismic forces.  
We anticipate the on-site clayey/silty soils may exhibit a low expansion potential if 
maintained near its natural moisture content. We recommend installing the slabs-on-grade 
with thickened-edge footing with the bottom of the footing to be embedded a minimum of 
12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade. 
We recommend the implementation of subgrade soil moisture-conditioning about 
2 percent above the optimum and compaction to at least 90 percent relative compaction to 
reduce the risk of potential soil expansion and settlement. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and 
recommendations.

SECTION 1.   GENERAL 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
conducted for the Rice Street Apartments, 3016 Umi Street project in Lihue on the 
Island of Kauai, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations resulting 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses for the project. 
These findings and geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of 
foundations, slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, site grading, and pavements only. The 
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at 
the end of this report. 
1.1 
Project Considerations 
It is proposed to develop a four-story apartment complex consisting of 
commercial spaces on the ground level and 66 residential units above. The project site 
is located at the east corner of Umi Street and Rice Street in the Lihue area on the 
Island of Kauai, Hawaii, encompassing approximately 1.73 acres of area. 
Based on the project plans provided, we envision majority of the site grading 
consisting of cuts up to about 4 feet and minus fills up to about 2 feet. 
The project site is currently occupied by a two-story commercial building on the 
western half of the property, surrounded by parking lots. Based on the project grading 
plans, we understand that the existing two-story building will be demolished.

revised fee proposal dated November 2, 2022. The scope of work for this exploration 
included the following tasks and work efforts:

SECTION 2.   SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Kaua`i is a dissected basaltic dome that was initially formed by a 
single large shield volcano, which has subsequently been deeply eroded and partially 
covered with later (younger) volcanic materials. The Island of Kaua`i is the oldest of the 
main Hawaiian Islands and today encompasses approximately 1,456 square kilometers 
(562 square miles) in area with a maximum elevation of about 1,569 meters (5,148 feet) 
above Mean Sea Level (MSL) at Mt. Wai’ale’ale near the center of the island. It is 
believed that during the Tertiary Period (about 5 to 6 million years ago in the Pliocene 
Epoch), basaltic lavas belonging to the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series built a roughly 
circular island volcano from the ocean floor that was estimated to have been about 
6,100 meters (20,000 feet) deep at the time. Near the end of the Pliocene Epoch, this 
main shield-building volcanic phase came to a halt. 
Following a long period of erosion and island mass subsidence, volcanism was 
renewed during the early Pleistocene Epoch (about 1.5 million years ago) with the 
eruption of the Koloa Volcanic Series from multiple vents throughout the island. This 
post-erosional volcanic activity laid a veneer of fresh lava and interbedded volcanic 
sediments over the older deposits of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series. Because of 
the long period of erosion between the eruption of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series 
and the Koloa Volcanic Series, deposits of the Koloa Volcanic Series typically filled the 
erosional valleys and depressions that existed at that time. Consequently, the basaltic 
rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series are commonly found overlying thick residual soil and 
alluvial deposits. 
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of 
Kauai. These rocks generally are characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded 
from groups of vents aligned in north-south trends in various locales. Associated with 
the vents are pyroclastic materials that usually form low cinder cones at the vent. 
The basaltic rock built by the extrusion of lavas of the Koloa Volcanic Series 
generally is characterized by flows of jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded

2.2 
Subsurface Conditions 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling five exploratory borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, and I-1 and I-2, extending to depths of 
approximately 5 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. In addition, two bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, were collected for 
laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) testing to evaluate the pavement support 
characteristics of the near-surface soils. The approximate locations of the borings drilled 
and bulk samples collected are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  
Based on our field exploration, the project site is underlain by fills and alluvium 
deposits overlying residual and/or saprolite soils extending to the maximum depth drilled 
of about 21.5 feet below the ground surface. The surface fills were relatively thin, dense 
base course material under about 3 inches of asphaltic concrete pavement. The 
alluvium deposits generally consisted of very stiff clayey/silty extending to a depth of 
about 13 feet below the existing ground surface. Underlying the alluvial soils, our 
borings encountered residual/saprolite soils generally consisting of medium stiff to stiff 
clayey/silty soils extending the maximum depth explored of about 21.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. 
We did not encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field 
exploration. However, it should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change 
due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other 
factors. Also, it should be noted that groundwater seepage and high-level perched 
groundwater are commonly encountered in the subsurface residual and saprolitic 
materials.

SECTION 3.   DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered relatively thin fills and/or alluvial soils 
overlying medium stiff residual/saprolite soils extending to the maximum depth drilled of 
approximately 21.5 feet below the ground surface. We did not encounter groundwater in 
the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration. However, it should be noted that 
groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, seasonal 
precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
Based on the generally stiff subsurface conditions, we recommend using shallow 
spread and/or continuous strip footings to support the new four-story apartment building. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design of footings 
bearing on the recompacted on-site soils and/or new compacted fills placed to achieve the 
design finished grades. This bearing value is for dead-plus-live loads and may be 
increased by one-third (⅓) for transient loads, such as those caused by wind or seismic 
forces.  
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and our experience within the 
project vicinity, the in-situ near-surface clayey and/or silty soils would likely exhibit 
relatively low expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations. We 
recommend installing the slabs-on-grade with thickened-edge footing with the bottom of 
the footing to be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade. 
Detailed discussions and recommendations for the design of foundations, 
slabs-on-grade, retaining structures, site grading, pavements, and other geotechnical 
aspects of the project are presented in the following sections.

Shallow Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we envision the 
proposed new apartment building may be supported on shallow foundations consisting of 
spread and/or continuous strip footings. 
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 pounds per square foot (psf) may be 
used for the design of footings bearing on recompacted in-situ soils. This bearing value is

If soft and/or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of footing excavations, 
the soft and/or loose materials should be over-excavated until dense and/or stiff materials 
are exposed in the footing excavations. The over-excavation should be backfilled with 
select granular fill materials moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, 
placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Alternatively, the bottom of the footing may be 
extended down to bear directly on the underlying competent materials. 
In general, the bottom of the footings should be embedded at least 18 inches below 
the lowest adjacent finished grade. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on 
sloping ground should be embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal 
setback distance of 6 feet measured from the outside edge of the footings to the face of 
the slope. 
Foundations next to other subsurface structures, including but not limited to existing 
foundations, utility trenches, and/or easements, should be embedded below a 45-degree 
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the subsurface structure, or 
the proposed footings should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. 
This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent subsurface structures with 
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation 
settlement. 
The bottom of the footing excavations should be moisture-conditioned to above the 
optimum moisture content and recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to 
provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement of reinforcing 
steel or concrete.  Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed 
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance 
with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture content is the water content (percentage by dry 
weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

3.2 
Slabs-On-Grade 
Based on the site grading plans provided, we envision the slabs-on-grade 
generally will be supported in a cut condition to lower the existing ground surface to the 
finished subgrades. Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and our 
experience within the project vicinity, we anticipate the alluvial and/or residual soils 
underlying the project site exhibit relatively low expansion potential when subjected to 
moisture fluctuations. 
We recommend installing the slabs-on-grade with thickened-edge footing with 
the bottom of the footing to be embedded a minimum of 12 inches below the lowest 
adjacent grade. We recommend scarifying the subgrade soils to a depth of about 
12 inches, moisture-conditioning to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture 
content, and recompacting to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. Where 
shrinkage cracks are observed after compaction of the subgrade, we recommend 
preparing the soils again as recommended. Saturation and subsequent yielding of the

Retaining Structures 
We anticipate retaining walls will be required for grade separation purposes. In 
general, retaining structures should be designed to resist lateral earth pressures due to 
the adjacent soils and surcharge effects. Based on the subsurface conditions 
encountered at the project site, the following general guidelines may be used for design 
of the retaining structures at the project site. 
3.3.1 Foundations 
Generally, we recommend retaining wall foundations be designed in accordance 
with the recommendations and parameters presented in the “Shallow 
Foundations” section herein. In addition, retaining wall foundations should be at 
least 18 inches wide and the bottom should be embedded a minimum of 
24 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grades. 
For sloping ground conditions, the bottom of the footing should extend deeper to 
obtain a minimum 6-foot setback distance measured horizontally from the outside 
edge of the footing to the face of the slope. Wall footings oriented parallel to the 
direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings.

3.3.3 Drainage 
The backfill immediately behind the retaining wall structure should be 
well-drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage 
system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of permeable material, such as 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), wrapped with geotextile 
fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) placed directly around a perforated pipe 
(perforations down) at the base of the retaining structure discharging to an 
appropriate outlet or weep holes. As an alternative, a prefabricated drainage 
product, such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used in lieu of the drainage 
material. The prefabricated drainage product also should be hydraulically 
connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall. 
A typical drainage system for site retaining walls would consist of 1 cubic foot of 
permeable material, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), 
wrapped with non-woven filter fabric placed at each of the weep hole locations. 
The weep holes should be spaced no more than 6 feet apart. 
Unless covered by concrete slabs or pavements, the upper 12 inches of backfill 
should consist of relatively impervious material to reduce the potential for 
excessive water infiltration behind the walls. In addition, the backfill below the 
drainage outlet (or weep holes) should consist of relatively impervious materials 
to reduce the potential for appreciable water infiltration into the footing subgrade. 
The relatively impervious material should be compacted to no less than 
90 percent relative compaction.

3.4.1 Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, the area within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site or 
in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated 
materials. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas 
designated to receive fill and/or future improvements should be over-excavated 
to expose firm material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with 
well-compacted fill. The excavated soft soils should not be reused as fill materials

3.8 
Corrosion Potential 
Chemical analyses, including pH, minimum resistivity, chloride contents, and 
sulfate contents, were performed on selected soil samples obtained during our field 
exploration for corrosivity evaluation. The test results are presented on Plate B-13 of 
Appendix B. 
Resistivity generally is recognized as one of the most significant soil 
characteristics with regard to the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. 
Generally, a lower resistivity value results in a greater potential for corrosion of buried 
metallic objects. Conversely, a higher resistivity value means the soil is less likely to 
contribute to the corrosion of metallic objects. Based on the test results presented on 
Plate B-13, the subsurface soils at the project site exhibit a minimum resistivity value of 
about 3,400 ohm-cm, indicating that the subsurface soils are corrosive (Corrosion 
Rating of 3) based on the guidelines provided by the City & County of Honolulu – Board 
of Water Supply. It should be noted that the resistivity results provided herein are based 
on bulk samples collected from two locations at the project site and the resistivity of the 
subsurface materials may vary with location and depth. Therefore, properly designing 
metallic substructures (such as piping) for protection against the potential for corrosion 
may be considered.

The method used to control the corrosion of underground concrete pipelines and 
structures is dependent on the pH and the chloride and sulfate contents found in the 
soil. In general, soils with a chloride content of less than 500 parts per million (ppm), 
sulfate content of less than 2,000 ppm, and a pH of greater than 5.0 may be considered 
“non-corrosive” to underground concrete pipelines and structures. Based on the 
laboratory tested values of pH, chloride content, and sulfate content of the in-situ soils, 
we believe the near-surface soils at the project site may be considered “non-corrosive”; 
therefore, either Type I or Type II (Type I/II) cement may be used for the concrete in 
contact with the ground.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
five boreholes, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, and I-1 and I-2, extending to 
depths of approximately 5 to 21.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers.  
Our logger classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and textural 
examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for 
Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-5. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows 
required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to 
obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples 
retrieved in the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the 
unconfined compressive strength of the sample. Pocket penetrometer test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. A graphic presentation of the test 
results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic Presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Two one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site soils. The test results 
are summarized on Plate B-3. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-4 and B-5. 
Three 
Unconsolidated 
Undrained 
Triaxial 
Compression 
(TXUU) 
tests 
(ASTM D2850) were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the undrained 
shear strength of the clayey silt soils encountered. The approximate in-situ effective 
overburden pressure was used as the applied confining pressure for the relatively 
“undisturbed” soil sample. The test results and the stress-strain curves are presented on 
Plates B-6 through B-8. 
Two Consolidation tests with time rates (ASTM D2435) were performed on 
samples of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility 
characteristics of the materials encountered. Results of the consolidation tests are 
presented on Plates B-9 and B-10. 
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics 
of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-11 and B-12. 
Two sets of Corrosion tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), were 
performed by our office and Eurofins TestAmerica on selected soil samples obtained 
from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on Plate B-13.

Field Infiltration Tests 
Two field infiltration tests were conducted at the site to evaluate the infiltration 
characteristics of the subsurface materials encountered. The infiltration tests were 
performed at selected locations, Borings I-1 and I-2, as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
These tests were performed in general accordance with the procedures in Appendix D 
of the State of Maryland, Department of the Environment “Stormwater Design Manual, 
Volumes I and II” (rev. 2009). These procedures are consistent with other state’s 
procedures and may generally be considered an industry standard. 
The field infiltration tests were performed by augering boreholes to a selected 
test depth of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Upon reaching the test 
depth, a solid casing was set to the bottom of the drilled hole to allow infiltration only 
through the soil exposed on the bottom of the boring. Falling head infiltration tests were 
performed to determine the infiltration rate of the underlying subsurface materials. The 
test consisted of four trials of filling the casing with 24 inches of water and taking 
periodic readings over a one-hour trial period. The infiltration rate is then calculated 
based on the result of the fourth and last trial for the test location. The test results are 
presented on Plates C-1 and C-2.
