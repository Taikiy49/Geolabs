INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed infrastructure improvements along Ilalo Street located in the
Kaka ako area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and
vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for the design
of the box culvert utility lines grading pavements and other infrastructure installations
only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated
to be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield volcanic activity returned with a
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series
The project site is located on the coastal plain of southern Oahu The coastal
plain was built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano which forms the eastern
two thirds of the Island of Oahu The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation
of alluvium derived from erosion of the volcano interbedded with coral reefs and
associated deposits
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age sea levels fluctuated in response to the
cycles of continental glaciation Most of the coastal plains were developed during the
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly As the glaciers grew
and advanced less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels
fell below the present stands of the sea When the glaciers melted and receded an
excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose to above the present
sea level
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio eustatic
sea level fluctuations When the sea level was low the erosional base level was
correspondingly lower and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level
When the sea level was high the erosional base level was raised such that sediments
accumulated at higher elevations

recommendations for stabilization of the soft loose soils below the proposed

Box Culvert
Based on the results of our borings deposits of very soft to medium stiff sandy
silts and very loose to medium dense silty sands are present at the project site Based
on the generally poor subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we recommend

on all sides with a layer of non woven filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent be
provided below the box culvert The clean granular fill material should consist of
free draining materials such as No 2 Rock ASTM C 33 No 4 gradation In addition
the stabilization layer should extend a minimum of 2 feet laterally beyond the edge of
the box culvert The non woven filter fabric is required to reduce the potential for

Box Culvert Foundation
For design of the box culvert foundation bearing on the 36 inch stabilization layer
an allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 500 pounds per square foot psf may be
used This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
For design of the concrete box culvert mat foundation a modulus of subgrade
reaction of 125 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used
above the stabilization layer Where foundations are to be located adjacent to
below grade structures or utilities the foundations should extend to a depth below
an imaginary 45 degree plane projected upward from the bottom of the
below grade structure
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with the
recommendations presented herein the total settlement of foundations is
estimated to be on the order of about 1 to 1 5 inches Differential settlements of
the foundations are estimated to be on the order of about 0 75 inches
Lateral loads acting on the box culvert structure may be resisted by frictional
resistance between the base of the foundation and stabilization layer and by
passive earth pressure developed against the near vertical faces of the embedded
portion of box culvert A coefficient of friction of 0 4 may be used for foundations
bearing on the No 2 Rock stabilization layer Resistance to lateral loads due to
passive pressure may be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of
180 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf for foundations embedded in
the soft loose lagoonal deposits Unless covered by pavements or slabs the

Earth Pressure Loads on Pipes
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the
pipes the unit weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance between
the backfill material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads on the
buried utility pipes we recommend that an average unit weight of 110 pounds
per cubic foot pcf for the backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0 25 be
used Earth forces upon the pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of the
trench Therefore the width of the trench should be kept to a minimum It
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3933 10 B DRAFT Page 15
should be noted that due to the subsurface conditions at the site the trench
excavations would need to be shored using sheet pile shoring or other similar
methods Therefore the width of the trench excavations will be wider than
normal and should be taken into consideration when designating the types and
class of pipes In addition traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be
considered for the portions of the pipes located in roadway areas
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a great effect upon the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore pipe bedding is an important
design consideration In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of

Pipe Settlement
Primary settlement of the new pipe installation is generally caused by the
difference in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the
heavier compacted backfill material placed over the pipeline The net increase in
loading may cause settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert
Based on our exploration and analyses small primary settlements on the order
of about 1 to 2inches may be anticipated for the new utility line alignments
Therefore some differential settlhements between pipe segments should be
anticipated and accounted for in the selection of the types and class of pipes
Construction Considerations
The settlement estimates presented in the Pipe Settlement subsection assume
that proper construction procedures and good workmanship will be consistently
practiced by the contractor during pipeline installation operations Based on our
knowledge and past experience with similar soft loose soils significant settlement
of the pipes may be attributed to the construction methodology and equipment
used by the contractor Therefore special attention should be given to the
installation procedure for the proposed utility lines especially in soft loose soil
conditions to reduce the potential for settlement distress in the future
As previously recommended the utility lines should generally be supported by
approximately 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel bedding layer over a subgrade
stabilization layer consisting of No 2 Rock wrapped in a non woven filter fabric
The non woven filter fabric is used to provide separation between the coarse
aggregate No 2 Rock and the fine grained soil In general a minimum overlap
of 2 feet should be provided between the ends of each roll of the filter fabric
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3933 10 B DRAFT Page 19
placed along the pipe alignment under dry conditions If the filter fabric is not
properly overlapped it is possible that the coarse aggregate placed may migrate
through the openings in the filter fabric and mix with the underlying soft soils
This may result in settlement of the pipes due to loss of cushion materials into
the soft loose subsoils
Considering the depth of the excavations some of the stabilization layer
installation below the pipe invert may be performed in wet conditions Therefore
we envision that it would be difficult to observe the fabric installation process below
the groundwater level In addition we believe that it would be difficult for the
contractor to assure that the proper minimum overlap is maintained Therefore we
believe that if placement of the filter fabric in wet conditions is necessary the
seams of the fabric should be sewn in lieu of overlapping to provide a higher
assurance that gaps will not occur in the filter fabric As a result we recommend
that sewing the seams of the filter fabric be incorporated into the construction
procedure to reduce the potential for large scale settlements of the pipes
Due to the soft loose subsoil conditions anticipated near the invert elevations care
should also be exercised during the backfilling operations of the trench excavation
Heavy equipment and vibratory action of the compactor may exert high pressures
onto the pipe and supporting soils during the trench backfilling operations resulting
in potential settlement of the soft loose deposits and pipe during construction
Therefore the size of the equipment used to compact the backfill around and
above the pipe should to be limited to less than 4 tons in weight

have a coefficient of permeability k in the range of about 0 009 centimeters per

APPENDIXA
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling five borings designated as Boring Nos 101 through 105 extending to depths of
about 50 to 53 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown
on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with continuous flight hollow stem augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD split barrel sampler
with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the second and third 6 inches a total of 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket
penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without discontinuities discounting drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the

APPENDIX B
Geotechnical Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1 Results of the test are also
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
One sieve analysis test ASTM C 136 ASTM D 1140 was performed on a
selected sample of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to
aid in soil classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B 2
One direct shear test ASTM D 3080 was performed on a selected soil sample to
evaluate the shear strength of the sample tested Results of the direct shear test are
presented on Plate B 3
One consolidation test with time rates ASTM D 2435 was performed on the
potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the
materials encountered Results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate B 4
Two California Bearing Ratio CBR tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils for pavement design Results of the CBR tests are
presented on Plates B 5 and B 6
One Modified Proctor compaction test ASTM D 1557 was performed on the near
surface soils to determine the moisture content versus the dry density relationship
