Regional Geology
atoll of the Chagos Archipelago located at 7 degrees south latitude approximately
1 368 kilometers south of India in the Indian Ocean The origin of coral atolls was first
postulated by Charles Darwin in 1842 His theory was that an atoll began as a fringing
reef around a volcanic island The island begins to subside tectonically at a slow rate
so that the coral growth maintains a reef line As the island subsides and becomes
smaller the reef grows larger and becomes a barrier reef When the island finally
disappears below sea level the upward growing reef encloses only a lagoon and
becomes an atoll
This basic theory has been somewhat modified by other considerations such as
eustatic sea level changes and cooled waters brought on by glacial activity Generally
the ocean side of an atoll is built up of larger pieces of rock that are ripped off of the
hard reef and worked up during storms while the lagoon side is composed of finer
material The usual atoll island stands between 1 5 to 10 meters above the sea level
The reef rock platform is generally about the low tide level The island detritus may spill
off the reef rock platform and build up towards the lagoon side of the island
The subsurface conditions in a coral atoll vary substantially in both the horizontal
and vertical dimensions Much of the large scale vertical variation is the result of
worldwide glacio eustatic sea level changes that occurred during the Pleistocene Epoch
Ice Age One significant low stand of the sea was a stand of relatively long duration at
about 18 meters below the present sea level Throughout the world many of the coral
atolls were eroded to this low stand and the materials above this level are the result of
rapid growth and accumulation of coralline deposits since the mid to late Pleistocene
Epoch The horizontal variation is largely dependent upon the environment in which the
coralline materials grew or were deposited

Soil Liquefaction
Soil liquefaction is a condition where saturated cohesionless soils located near
the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of strength due to the build up of excess
pore water pressures resulting from cyclic stress applications induced by earthquakes
In this process when the loose saturated sand deposit is subjected to vibration such as
during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume causing an
increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to occur rapidly enough to
dissipate the build up of pore water pressure the effective stress internal strength of
the soil is reduced Under sustained vibrations the pore water pressure build up could
equal the overburden pressure essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and
causing it to behave as a viscous fluid During liquefaction the soil acquires a mobility
sufficient to permit both horizontal and vertical movements and if not confined will
result in significant deformations
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose uniformly graded fine grained
sands and loose silts with little cohesion It is generally acknowledged that liquefaction
will not occur if the deposit is greater than 12 to 15 meters below the ground surface At
deeper deposits the greater overburden pressure is generally sufficient to prevent
liquefaction from occurring The major factors affecting the liquefaction characteristics
of a soil deposit are as follows
1
Grain Size Distribution Fine and uniform sands and silts are more
susceptible to liquefaction than coarse or well graded sands
2
Initial Relative Density Loose sands and silts are most susceptible to
liquefaction Liquefaction potential is inversely proportional to relative
density
3
Magnitude and Duration of Vibration Liquefaction potential is directly
proportional to the magnitude and duration of the earthquake

limited data obtained from our literature research However a review of the subsurface

Foundations
In general we believe that the proposed new construction may be supported on
a shallow foundation system with the understanding that appreciable footing settlements
may occur due to liquefaction under seismic conditions Based on this design
philosophy and the near surface subsoil conditions anticipated at the site we believe
that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for the foundation support
of the steam plant building We recommend structurally connecting the building
foundations with tie beams to reduce the effects of differential movements in the event
of liquefaction under seismic activities
An allowable bearing pressure of up to may be used for the
design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted in situ coral materials or new
compacted fills This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces

Slabs On Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be required for the construction of
the new steam plant building Based on the available soils boring data we believe that
new building slabs may be constructed on grade provided that the slab subgrade is
properly prepared in accordance with the recommendations presented below and in the
Site Preparation section herein
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery
vibration we recommend placing a minimum 100 mm thick layer of cushion fill
consisting of open graded gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation below the slabs The
open graded gravel cushion fill would provide uniform support of the slabs and would
serve as a capillary moisture break To reduce the potential for excessive future
moisture infiltration through the slab and subsequent damage to floor coverings an
impervious moisture barrier should be provided on top of the gravel cushion fill layer
We also recommend reinforcing the building slabs on grades with reinforcing
bars spaced at least 450 millimeters in each direction in lieu of the standard welded
wire fabric This recommendation is provided to reduce the potential for structural
distress due to differential movements of the slabs due to liquefaction under seismic
conditions It is also recommended that the interior wall design incorporate some
flexibility to accommodate a small amount of possible ground movements
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic or machinery vibration we
recommend providing a 150 mm layer of aggregate subbase below the slabs in lieu of
the 100 mm thick gravel cushion fill layer The aggregate subbase should consist of
crushed aggregates compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction For
design of structural slabs supported on 150 mm of aggregate subbase a modulus of
subgrade reaction of about per square meter per meter of deflection
kN m3 may be used for the compacted aggregate subbase

Based on the relatively flat topography at the project site we anticipate that the
majority of the site preparation work for the proposed project will consist of minor cuts
and fills to achieve the design finish grades As mentioned previously the project site is
generally underlain by near surface coral fills of unknown quality with the potential
presence for localized loose zones Therefore special attention should be given to the
subgrade preparation for the project construction
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation organic debris and other unsuitable

After clearing and grubbing the exposed subgrades should be proof rolled with a

detecting and compacting near surface loose zones The vibratory drum roller should

detect a near surface loose zone the loose materials should be removed to expose the
underlying firm ground and the resulting over excavation should then be backfilled with
compacted granular fill material After the proof rolling operations the excavated
subgrades underneath future slab and pavement areas should be scarified to a depth of
about 200 millimeters moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to a minimum of 90 and 95 percent relative compaction respectively

Excavation
In order to install the underground utility lines we anticipate that excavations
below the existing ground surface will be required for the project construction Our
review of available soils boring data suggest that the project area is generally underlain
by approximately 3 meters of dense surface fills over loose to medium dense coral
sands and gravel extending to depths of over 30 meters below the existing ground
surface
Excavation Method
Based on our experience the surface fill soils and underlying loose to medium
dense coralline materials anticipated at greater depths may be excavated with

The above discussions regarding the rippability of the subsurface materials are
based on our experience and the available soils boring data Sitework

In general the contractor should determine the method and equipment to be used
for excavation subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavated

reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement In addition the excavated
soils should be stockpiled at least 5 meters away from underground utilities to
reduce the potential for ground movement or subsidence which may damage the
existing underground utility lines
Excavation Support
In areas where open excavations are feasible we believe that a temporary cut
slope inclination of one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V or flatter may be used in
areas exposing the dense surface fills
In areas where open excavations are not feasible we believe that shoring of the
sides of the excavations will be necessary to protect personnel working in the
excavations based on the subsurface conditions anticipated and the excavation
depths Based on the competent subsoil conditions and the anticipated depth of
excavation we believe that steel plates with horizontal struts may be considered for
temporary shoring for the new utility lines planned for the project
It is important to install adequate shoring prior to the excavation and to maintain it
tightly against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation The
properly braced shoring is essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral

Dewatering
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater
level is above the bottom of the proposed excavation Groundwater is anticipated at
depths of about 1 to 2 meters below the existing ground surface based on the available
soils boring data in the project vicinity Based on the groundwater levels anticipated
and the permeable nature of the coralline deposits to be encountered during the
excavations dewatering will be required at the planned excavations of the project
construction
Because of the granular nature of the subsoils it is anticipated that the granular
soils encountered are relatively permeable Therefore dewatering of the excavation
may involve large volumes of water Dewatering by means of a well point system along

The contractor is responsible for dewatering for construction The selection of

excavations is not jeopardized The operations should be carried out without softening
the bottom of the excavations
Subsurface Soil Permeability
Percolation tests were not conducted at the project site However based on our
experience in the project vicinity we estimate that the subsurface permeability of
the on site soils may be on the order of about 0 01 to 0 5 centimeters per second
It is our opinion that the above estimated permeability value should be used with
caution due to the normally heterogeneous nature of the coralline subsurface soils
The actual subsurface soil permeability may range broadly and also vary locally in
terms of the orders of magnitude It should be noted that the permeability of the
subsurface soils at the site appears to be highly permeable based on field
observations and previous experience in the area Therefore the contractor should
give special attention to the site specific dewatering plan for the proposed project

Dewatering Considerations
We suggest considering the following three basic criteria in selection of a suitable
method of dewatering
a
The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or
damage to existing buildings roads and environment
b
The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also
provide safe and dry working conditions in the excavation
c
The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering
The contractor should be solely responsible for the impact and safety of the
dewatering operations His her qualified representative who should be continuously
present on site during dewatering activities will have the best opportunity to
promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and to implement
as soon as possible necessary precautionary or remedial measures including but
not limited to slowing down or stopping the dewatering operations
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils may
be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations should
be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the excavation
bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be performed without
pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and should be coordinated with
the shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not adversely affected
Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in blowing or heaving of
the excavation bottom or sides
Groundwater drawdown outside the excavation will cause additional settlement
resulting from consolidation of the soft loose compressible soils Therefore the use

Utility Trenches
We understand that new utility lines will be required to serve the proposed new
steam plant building In general good construction practices should be utilized for the
installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities at the site The contractor
should determine the method and equipment to be used for trench excavation subject
to practical limits and safety considerations In addition the excavations should comply
with the applicable local safety requirements The contractor should be responsible for
trench shoring design and installation
In general we recommend using granular bedding consisting of 150 millimeters
of coral sands or No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation for support under
the pipes If soft loose soils are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes a
stabilization layer consisting of additional 450 millimeters of No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation wrapped in a non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or
equivalent should be provided below the bedding layer for uniform support
Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about
300 millimeters above the pipes or 300 millimeters above the groundwater level to
provide adequate support around the pipes Select clean coral sand material may also
