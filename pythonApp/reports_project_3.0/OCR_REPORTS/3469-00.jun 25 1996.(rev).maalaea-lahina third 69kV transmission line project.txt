INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Maalaea Lahaina Third 69kV Transmission Line project
located between Maalaea and Lahaina in West Maui on the island of Maui Hawaii The
general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations for the
proposed transmission line project only The findings and recommendations presented
herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
We understand that Hawaiian Electric Company Maui Electric Company
HECO MECO plans to construct a new 69 kV single circuit line from the existing
Maalaea Power Plant Substation to the proposed Lahainaluna Switching Substation
which is approximately 1 5 miles northeast of the Town of Lahaina on the island of Maui
The proposed line is approximately 15 miles in length and will traverse rugged terrain of
the West Maui Mountain Range located on the south side of the island of Maui as shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Maui the second largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago was formed by the
activity of two volcanoes East Maui or Haleakala and West Maui The broad gently
sloping plain connecting the two volcanoes known as the Maui Isthmus was formed
when lavas of the Haleakala Volcano banked against the already existing West Maui
Volcano
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3469 00 Rev Page 8
The East Maui Volcano or better known as the Haleakala Mountain makes up the
eastern portion of the island Haleakala Mountain is considered to be dormant with the
last eruption taking place on the southerly side of the mountain around 1790 Haleakala
Mountain consists largely of the Honomanu Volcanic Series that built the mountain from
three rift zones probably during the Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epochs
The volcanic rocks of the West Maui have been divided into three volcanic series
The Wailuku Volcanic Series is the oldest and comprises of the basaltic lava flows and
associated pyroclastic and intrusive rocks that built the major shield volcano The
Wailuku Volcanic Series was covered by a thin discontinuous frosting of andesitic and
trachytic flows domes and pyroclastic deposits known as the Honolua Volcanic Series
After a long period of inactivity where erosion incised deep valleys into the volcanic flows
volcanic activity returned with the eruptions that produced the flows and cones of the
Lahaina Volcanic Series
The project site is situated on the southern flank of West Maui mountain The
processes of erosion and deposition caused the top of the basaltic lava flows to be highly
weathered and mantled by residual soils The coastal portions of the West Maui
mountain are flanked by fringing reefs and alluvial deposits

Based on our field exploration and geologic mapping performed for the subject
project the subsoils along the proposed transmission line alignment may be grouped into
three major stratigraphic units The engineering characteristics of these units appear to
be reasonably consistent for design purposes and are identified as follows
1 Recent Alluvium Ra
2 Quaternary Alluvium Qa
3 Tertiary Wailuku and Th and Thd
The approximate extent of these geologic units on the ground surface is also
shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3 The portion of the alignment between about
Sta 14600 and Sta 16200 was mapped as a combination of quaternary older alluvium
Qa and in situ basalt formation Tw Th and Thd because the boundary between the two
units nearly parallels the alignment and may grade between the two units Detailed
descriptions and discussions of these major stratigraphic units are presented in the
following subsections
Recent Alluvium R
The recent alluvium or younger alluvium generally consists of soft to medium stiff
brown to dark gray clayey silts and organic silts with varying amounts of sand and
infrequent pockets of gravels and cobbles These soils are generally the result of
sedimentation in a low energy environment such as a lake marsh low land and
coastal areas Frequently the soils assigned to this geologic unit accumulated
very rapidly under water and may not have been exposed to desiccation
Therefore there is a tendency to encounter underconsolidated sediments which
are still settling under their own weight or the weight of minimal overburden
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3469 00 Rev Page 10
Based on Boring No 1 drilled during our field exploration it appears that the recent
alluvial soils along the proposed transmission line alignment generally consists of
about 15 feet of stiff clayey silt soils overlying dense silty sands at greater depths
Our geologic mapping suggests that the recent alluvial soils are generally present
between the Maalaea Power Plant Substation and the Honoapiilani Highway as
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The quaternary alluvium or older alluvium generally overlies the basalt rock
formation and consists of terrigenous sediments which have been transported by
stream action from the upper reaches of the streams The older alluvium is
generally located at the foot of mountain ranges and steep slopes and at the
heads of large eroded valleys The material is more granular in nature when
compared to recent alluvium and consists of alluvial and colluvial fans talus and
debris flows of material generally consisting of sand gravel cobbles and boulders
with clays
Based on Boring through 10 14 and 15 drilled during our field
exploration it appears that the older alluvial soils along the proposed transmission
line alignment generally consists of medium dense to dense sands with basalt
cobbles and boulders throughout the depths explored The approximate extent of
the older alluvial soils is shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3
Tertiary Wailuku and Th and Thd
The Tertiary Wailuku Volcanics generally composed of in situ basalt rock with
interbedded clinker layers and their weathered products derived from lava flows
and volcanic eruptions The material varies in degree of weathering and is
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3469 00 Rev Page 11
typically overlain by residual or saprolite soils of extremely weathered rock Bare
rock may also be exposed at the ground surface with no soil cover The
Honomanu Volcanics generally composed of volcanic ash cinders basalt rock
and their weathered products The materials may have a chalky white or tan
appearance and are typically found near cinder cones and other volcanic vents
Based on Boring and 16 through 18 drilled during our field
exploration it appears that the volcanic rocks along the proposed transmission line
alignment generally consists of hard basalt rock formation blanketed by a cover of
saprolitic materials consisting of hard clayey silts of up to about 10 feet in
thickness The approximate extent of these volcanic materials is shown on the
Site Plans Plates 2 and 3

Construction Considerations
The performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures As a result we recommend that a
qualified representative be present to observe the installation of drilled shafts during
construction In our opinion the following may have a significant impact on the
effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in some of the borings drilled for
the project we anticipate that drilling through dense boulders or hard ledges will be
required The drilled shaft contractor will need to have the appropriate equipment and
tools to drill through the dense boulders Appropriate measures by the contractor will also
be needed to avoid dislodging any boulders into the drilled shaft excavation
Based on our field exploration soft loose soils may be encountered during the
construction of some of the drilled shaft foundations To reduce the potential for caving in
of the drilled holes temporary casing of the drilled holes may be needed during
construction The contractor should be made aware that temporary casing of the drilled
holes may be needed In addition care should be exercised during removal of the
temporary casing to reduce the potential for necking of the drilled shaft Therefore a
minimum 5 foot head of concrete should be maintained above the bottom of the casing
during removal of the casing
The load carrying capacity of drilled shafts depends to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling the
shaft holes and in placing concrete in the holes
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3469 00 Rev Page 21
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is generally not recommended Because
of the high groundwater anticipated near the Maalaea Power Plant Substation site
placement of concrete by wet construction methods using a tremie pipe will likely be
required for some of the drilled shaft foundations located in this area A low shrink
concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch slump range should be used to provide close
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils The concrete should be
placed in a suitable manner to reduce segregation of the aggregates from the concrete

Corrosion Control
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics
with regard to the corrosivity of the soil In general the lower the resistivity the greater
the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure Conversely the higher the
resistivity the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion
A total of 10 soil resistivity measurements using the Wenner 4 Pin method were
conducted at the location of Boring Nos 2 and 13 drilled for the subject project Results
of these tests are presented on Plate B 5 of Appendix B in this report Based on the
results of the field resistivity measurements the on site soils at the test locations range
from slightly to moderately corrosive

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the proposed transmission line alignment were
explored by drilling and sampling 15 borings to depths ranging from 15 to 31 5 feet below
the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plans
Plates 2 and 3 The borings were drilled using both truck mounted drill rig and skid
mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description Due to
the lack of water at the remote boring location the core barrel was advanced using
compressed air as a drilling fluid in lieu of water during our coring operations
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physicai Properties of Rocks and Minerals

MEASUREMENT OF THERMAL RESISTIVITY
The thermal resistivity or rho C cm W is measured by the transient thermal probe
technique Basically a slender cylindrical probe containing a precisely wound heater and a
temperature sensor is installed into the soil to be tested Constant power is applied to the
probe and the temperature time data is monitored From this curve the thermal resistivity is
calculated As long as certain theoretical assumptions and test procedures are met the
technique is equally applicable for small probes 3 mm diameter by 100 mm long in
laboratory soil samples and large probes 25 mm diameter by 2m long installed in situ A 6
mm diameter by 300 mm long thermal probe with single temperature sensor and the
Geotherm Thermal Property Analyzer Model TPA 1000 was used to perform thermal
resistivity measurements on this project
The EL 2128 manufactured by Geotherm Inc is a system that fully
automates the thermal resistivity test It is micro processor controlled and provides
programmable power to the thermal probes reads temperature sensors and probe power
and computes in real time the thermal resistivity The unit consists of a programmable
power supply a 6 channel data acquisition system a micro processor and an optional
printer A nominal 110 volt power source is required The entire test procedure is software
controlled in a totally interactive manner Various built in error reducing features ensure
that the test complies with the theoretical assumptions A statistical analysis of data
indicates whether an acceptable test has been accomplished

FACTORS AFFECTING THERMAL RESISTIVITY
Thermal resistivity depends on soil type texture water content density and various other
factors to a lesser degree Note that once a cable is installed the soil moisture is the only
parameter that changes significantly with time
SOIL COMPOSITION The thermal resistivity of a given soil mass is a function of the
intrinsic resistivities of its components These may range from 12 C cm W for quartz
TEXTURE This refers to the soil grain size shape and particle size distribution Since
most of the heat is conducted through the solid particles and their contacts the resistivity is
minimized for soil particle mixtures that maximize these contacts In engineering
applications a soil is often qualitatively categorized by a visual description using accepted
adjectives to indicate the fractional amount of each component i e gravel sand silt clay
WATER CONTENT For a given soil the major influence on the thermal resistivity is the
moisture content In a dry state the spaces between the solid mineral grains are filled with
substantially lowered as much as 3 to 5 times A soil that is better able to retain its
moisture as well as being able to efficiently re wet when dried will have better thermal
performance characteristics The soil water content is expressed as a percentage of the
weight of water to the dry weight of soil solids as determined by oven drying at 105 C in
accordance with ASTM
DRY DENSITY Soil densification improves soil grain contacts and displaces air therefore
reducing the resistivity most notably at low moisture contents Well graded soils are
potentially more dense because smaller grains can efficiently fill the spaces between the
larger particles Dry density is expressed as the ratio of the dry weight of the soil solids to
the total volume The total volume is taken as the initial volume of the undisturbed moist
soil in a sample tube

COMMENTS
The native soil at test location B 1 has very poor thermal characteristics especially with
natural moisture content ranging from 24 to 30 The totally dry thermal resistivity as
can be seen from the thermal dryout curves in Figure 1 is 275 C cm watt at 3 ft depth and
350 C cm watt at 5 ft depth
At location B 13 as described in your borehole log the overburden consisted primarily of
dry weathered basalt and weathered clinker This has very poor thermal characteristics as
can be seen from the thermal dryout curve in Figure 2 When reconstituted at the as
received moisture content of 6 2 and a dry density of 130 pcf the thermal resistivity was
found to be 80 C cm watt The rock core sample at 5 ft depth was fairly sound with some
fractures The bulk density was found to be 152 pcf and the as received moisture content
was 0 8 When fully saturated the moisture content was found to be 2 5 The thermal
performance of this core sample is fairly good with the thermal resistivity ranging from
low moisture content only two measurements were made fully saturated and totally dry

Please note that for the native soil at borehole B 1 at moisture levels below the critical
moisture content of about 12 where the curve begins to slope upwards more steeply
any amount of soil drying gives rise to a larger increase in the resistivity At this point the
heat from a cable can potentially dry the soil completely thermal runaway Thus if the
design resistivity i e at the driest expected soil conditions is in the stable region the soil
resistivity should remain stable under normal cable heat loads but if the design value
happens to be in the unstable region the soil can be expected to dry totally and the design
must be based on a value close to that of the dry resistivity Alternatively for such poor
conditions use of corrective thermal backfill FTB is strongly recommended This will
reduce the heat flux experienced by the native soil so that it will not dry out The corrective
thermal backfill should be able to better resist total drying and also have a lower dry
resistivity less than 100 C cm W even if it is completely dry
