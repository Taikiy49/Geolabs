In accordance with our fee proposal dated December 2, 2013, we performed a 
geotechnical engineering exploration for the Temporary Retention Basin/Kapolei 
Harborside Drainage Channel – Phase 3 project in the Kapolei area on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. This letter report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and 
laboratory testing and presents our recommendations for site grading. It should be noted 
that the recommendations provided herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of 
this letter report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
Based on the information provided, we understand that it is desired to alleviate 
the flooding problem in the Kapolei area by constructing a temporary retention basin 
along a portion of the future Kapolei Harborside Drainage Channel alignment situated 
between Kalaeloa Boulevard and Kalaeloa Harbor in the Kapolei area on the Island of 
Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project 
Location Map, Plate 1. The materials generated from the excavations into the future 
drainage channel alignment will be stockpiled as an embankment adjacent to the 
temporary retention basin. We envision that the excavated materials will be segregated 
into granular and cohesive soils (if encountered) during the embankment construction to 
facilitate future use as borrow soils.  
This report addresses the Phase 3 portion of the proposed temporary retention 
basin planned between about Hanua Street and Malakole Street.  The project site is 
about 2,700 feet long by about 80 to 150 feet wide at the bottom in plan dimensions.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling two borings, designated 
as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, each to a depth of about 27.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  In addition, our field exploration also consisted of excavating and sampling 
six test pits, designated as Test Pit No. 1 through 6, extending to depths ranging from 
about 6.5 to 15 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring and test 
pit locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, our field exploration conducted within the project limits generally 
encountered coralline deposits extending to the maximum depth explored of 
approximately 27.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The coralline deposits 
generally consisted of coralline detritus materials including very stiff sandy silts, dense 
to very dense silty sands, silty and sandy gravel, as well as medium hard to hard coral 
and sandstone formations. Although alluvial deposit consisting of clayey soils was not

GEOTECHNCIAL ENGINEERING RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the results of our field exploration as well as our knowledge and 
experience in the project vicinity, the proposed temporary retention basin site appears to 
be generally underlain by coralline deposits, and possibly alluvial soils in localized 
depths. These subsurface conditions should provide adequate support for the proposed 
retention basin/drainage channel project, provided that the recommendations presented 
herein are followed. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, we envision that conventional 
earthmoving equipment may be used for excavation of the coralline detritus materials. 
However, it is anticipated that the majority of the channel excavation will encounter 
medium hard to hard coral and sandstone formations. For the removal of these 
formations, hard excavation methods, such as hoerams and heavy ripping, will likely be 
required. 
The coral and sandstone formations at the site are variable, ranging from 
moderately to severely fractured, moderately to highly weathered, and medium hard to 
hard. Therefore, although most of the cut slopes should perform satisfactorily, provisions 
for slope protection such as riprap and permanent erosion mats may need to be included 
when the final configuration of the drainage channel is achieved. Detailed discussion of 
these items and our geotechnical engineering design recommendations are presented 
in the following sections herein.

a temporary retention basin and construction of an embankment with the excavated 
materials along the southeast side of the temporary retention basin. The following 
grading items are addressed in the succeeding subsections:  (1) Site Preparation,

Site Preparation 
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be 
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious material, and 
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off-site or 
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the 
excavated materials. 
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing in areas 
designated to receive fills should be over-excavated to expose firm natural 
material, and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted 
engineered fill. 
Areas to receive fill should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches, moisture 
conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum moisture, and compacted to at 
least 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place 
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the 
same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the 
water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory rollers, or 
other types of acceptable compaction equipment. These site preparation 
operations should be performed in the presence of our representative. 
Keying and Benching 
Although the topography at the site is relatively flat, there are local areas of 
steeper slopes. Therefore, keying and benching is recommended for fills that will 
be placed at the site in areas where the existing slope is steeper than 
five horizontal to one vertical (5H:1V). 
Based on the site conditions encountered, we recommend moisture-conditioning 
and proof-rolling the keys and benches prior to the placement of the 
embankment fill, with a Caterpillar D-9 bulldozer or similar heavy equipment for a 
minimum of six passes to help verify that adequate support for the fill has been 
achieved. The proof-rolling operations should be performed in the presence of a 
Geolabs representative. Yielding areas, loose areas, or cavities disclosed during

Fill Materials 
We envision that the temporary retention basin will be constructed by excavating 
into the existing ground. New fills to raise the existing ground surface for the 
retention basin/drainage channel construction are not anticipated for the project. 
If new fills are required for the retention basin/drainage channel construction, we 
recommend utilizing select fill material with proper slope and channel protection. 
Fill materials should consist of non-expansive materials less than 3 inches in 
largest dimension, with a minimum CBR value of 10. The fill material should also 
be free of vegetation and deleterious materials. Geolabs should observe and/or 
test imported fill materials for their suitability prior to being transported to the site 
for the intended use. 
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
Fills should be moisture-conditioned to about 2 percent above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 10 inches in loose thickness, and 
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers 
to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot 
rollers, vibratory rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. 
Geolabs should perform observations and soil density tests during site grading to 
assist the contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the 
proper moisture content. Where compaction is less than required, additional 
compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of the moisture content as 
necessary, to obtain the specified compaction. 
As mentioned above, the excavated materials should be segregated into granular 
and cohesive soils (if encountered) during the embankment construction to 
facilitate future use of the borrow soils.  
Excavation 
The temporary retention basin/drainage channel excavation will penetrate the 
near-surface soils and will involve cuts into the underlying coral and sandstone 
formations. It is anticipated that most of the excavation work may be 
accomplished with normal heavy excavation equipment, such as bulldozers and 
scrapers. However, excavations into the hard coral and sandstone formations 
may require the use of heavy ripping or hoerams. If heavy excavation is required, 
care should be exercised to reduce the potential of overbreak, particularly in the 
retention basin/channel walls and floor.

Cut and Fill Slopes 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration, it 
appears that most of the cut slopes would expose very stiff sandy silts, dense to 
very dense coralline sands and gravel, to medium hard to hard coral and 
sandstone formations. In general, we believe that cut slopes for the retention 
basin/drainage channel may be made at an inclination of 2H:1V or flatter 
provided that erosion control and slope protection is provided for the cut slopes in 
the final configuration, if deemed necessary. 
Fill slopes for the embankment construction should be designed with a slope 
inclination of 2H:1V or flatter. If necessary, slope benches are recommended at 
maximum 15-foot vertical height intervals. If the existing ground surface is 
steeper that 5H:1V, fills should be keyed and benched into the existing slope, as 
discussed above, to provide stability of the new fill against sliding. The filling 
operations should start at the lowest point and continue up in level horizontal 
compacted layers in accordance with the above fill placement recommendations. 
Fill slopes should be constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design 
slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face. Track-rolling of slopes should 
not be accepted. In the event that over-cutting of a slope occurs, keying and 
benching requirements should be implemented instead of backfilling the slope to 
the design grade with sliver fills. Water should be diverted away from the tops of 
slopes and slope planting should be provided as soon as possible to reduce the 
potential for erosion of the finished slopes. 
Slope and Channel Protection 
Although alluvial deposit consisting of clayey soils was not encountered during 
our field exploration, our experience in the area suggests that alluvial clays could 
be present below the coralline deposits in localized areas.  Where alluvial soils 
are exposed in the retention basin/channel walls and subject to water flow, the 
alluvial soils should be protected by riprap or other lining when the drainage 
channel is in its final configuration. 
It is our opinion that the overall stability of the proposed channel cut slope is 
satisfactory. However, erosion may occur where the coral formation has minimal 
cementation and the water velocity is high. Therefore, maintenance will be 
required following large storm events. This is typical of unlined channels in coral. 
If the channel is to be virtually maintenance free, then a structural lining should 
be considered.

If it is desired to keep the channel unlined, then periodic maintenance will be 
required. In order to reduce the maintenance, selective portions of the channel 
could be lined to reduce erosion. Slope protection/lining should be installed 
where the water velocities would be high, such as the outlets of culverts into the 
channel, and where large areas of coral with minimal cementation are 
encountered. 
Shotcrete, grouted rubble pavement (GRP), and articulating concrete blocks may 
be considered for the channel lining. The costs for these alternatives are believed 
to be similar, with shotcrete probably a little less costly than the others. The 
shotcrete and GRP are probably more flexible in terms of installation in localized 
areas since the articulating concrete blocks need to be installed in mats covering 
the entire slope. The articulating blocks are desirable due to its ability to allow 
water percolation into the subsurface. It is our opinion that shotcrete lining may 
be most appropriate for the drainage channel at its final configuration.

Field Exploration 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling two borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, each extending to a depth of about 27.5 feet below 
the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig 
equipped with continuous flight, solid-stem augers.  In addition, our field exploration 
program also consisted of excavating, sampling, and backfilling of six test pits, 
designated as Test Pit Nos. 1 through 6, extending to depths ranging from about 6.5 to 
15 feet below the existing ground surface. The test pits were excavated with a 
Caterpillar 345 excavator.  The approximate boring and test pit locations are shown on 
the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings and test pits by 
visual and textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, 
Standard Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling and 
excavation operations on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were 
further reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general 
accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for 
Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log 
Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with 
ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic 
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings and 
Test Pits, Plates A-1 through A-8. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in 
general accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
3-inch outside diameter (OD) Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 
30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general 
accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by 
driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The 
blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths.  Grab samples were also obtained from the excavated test pits at selected 
depths for further visual review and laboratory testing. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the

Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt. 
For example, if 3.7 feet of material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run, the recovery 
would be 74 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC = 74%. 
The Rock Quality Designation (RQD) is also a subjective guide to the relative 
quality of rock masses. RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run in rock that is 
sound material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities, discounting 
any drilling, mechanical, and handling induced fractures or breaks. If 2.5 feet of sound 
material is recovered from a 5.0-foot core run in rock, the RQD would be 50 percent and 
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD = 50%. Generally, the following is used 
to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the "Practical Handbook of Physical 
Properties of Rocks and Minerals” by Robert S. Carmichael (1989).

Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D4318) was performed on a selected sample 
collected to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. Test results are presented on

Nine Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 and 136) were performed on selected 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the test results are presented on Plates B-2 and 
B-3. 
Four Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012, Method C) were performed on 
selected core samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the coral 
formation encountered. The test results are presented on Plate B-4. 
Eight laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk sample of the in-situ coralline materials to evaluate their pavement support 
characteristics.  Results of the CBR tests were also used to evaluate the quality of the 
materials tested for use as fills   The test results are presented on Plates B-5 through 
B-12. 
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the in-situ soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships.  The test results are presented on Plates B-13 through B-16.
