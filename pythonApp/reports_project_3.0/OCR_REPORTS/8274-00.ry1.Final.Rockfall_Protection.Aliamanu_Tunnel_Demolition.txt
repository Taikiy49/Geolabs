between Rim Loop and Flame Lane in the Aliamanu Military Reservation on the Island of

Plate 1. In general, the objective is to evaluate potential rockfall conditions and protection 
measures to mitigate the rockfall activity that may impact construction activities, including 
tunnel demolition and waterline installation near the bottom of the sub-vertical cut slope. The 
primary area of potential impact extends approximately 400 linear feet around the perimeter 
of the existing roadway. In addition, another 200 linear feet of the sloping hillside around the 
perimeter of the existing roadway is also planned for access to the proposed staging area. 
The rockfall evaluation limits are shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
We understand that loose cobbles and boulders from the Aliamanu Crater wall have 
previously impacted the existing roadway. The falling rock is the result of natural 
erosion/weathering processes acting upon the volcanic tuff comprising the hillside.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae and 
Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age (2.7 to 3.4 million years ago) and forms the bulk of the western one-third of 
the island. The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene

(Ice Age) in age (2.2 to 2.5 million years ago) and forms majority of the eastern two-thirds 
of the island. The end of volcanic activity associated with the Koolau Volcano was followed 
by a long period of landmass erosion whereby deep stream valleys were carved into the 
dome-shaped Koolau Shield Volcano. 
Approximately 850,000 to 1,000,000 years ago, some limited volcanic activity 
resumed with the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series from secondary vents scattered 
across the eastern portion of the Island of Oahu. The eruptions occurred along the 
established secondary rift zones of the Koolau Volcano. Some of the most notable 
landmarks located on the Island of Oahu, such as Punchbowl and Diamond Head Craters, 
and including Aliamanu Crater, were created by the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic 
Series. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available 
to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to elevations above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea 
level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was correspondingly 
lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. When the sea level 
was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments filled pre-existing 
depressions and accumulated at higher elevations. 
The existing roadway connecting to the Aliamanu Tunnel is situated along the 
northern part of Aliamanu Crater in the Salt Lake area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, 
Hawaii. The slopes of Aliamanu Crater generally consist of southerly facing slopes 
towards Foster Village, which typically are composed of weathered bedded volcanic tuff 
rock.  The rock formations are mainly inclined downward towards the central volcanic vent. 
The volcanic tuff rock comprising Aliamanu Crater was created by the fall-out deposition of 
super-heated volcanic ash and cinders containing some entrained blocks of basaltic lava 
rock, which were emitted from the Aliamanu volcanic vent.

The project site is located northeast of the intersection of Rim Loop and Flame Lane 
in the Aliamanu Military Reservation area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The 
project site is bounded by Aliamanu Military Reservation in all directions, as shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1.

Site Reconnaissance 
Based on our field reconnaissance observation, the existing slope generally consists 
of two natural sub-vertical slopes and/or one sub-vertical cut slope connected by two steep 
natural tuff rock slopes. The upper natural slopes generally consisted of highly weathered 
friable formation extending from the east to the west with a near-vertical drop approximately 
15 to 30 feet high. The middle natural sub-vertical slopes encompassed between 
approximate Stations 0+32 and 3+56 with various heights of 10 to 40 feet and diminished 
beyond approximate Station 3+56 through the eastern end of the project site. The lower cut 
slopes were constructed during the original construction for the tunnel access roadway 
purpose with various heights between 30 and 60 feet. The lower cut slopes generally 
exposed massive formation. The approximate slope location and geometries are illustrated 
on the Site Plan (Plate 2) and CRSP Slope Profiles (Pates 3.1 through 3.3). 
Loose and/or eroded tuff rock fragments were observed from approximate 
Stations 0+32 to 3+56 near the middle sub-vertical slope. Majority of the rock fragments 
ranged about 2 to 3 feet long in dimension with a thin and slender shape. Based on our field 
observation, the potential rockfall sources may be represented from the upper to middle 
slopes. We believe the falling rocks to potentially be a rockfall hazard due to the taller height 
and adverse tuff rock bedding orientation. 
The middle sub-vertical slope is generally diminished near approximate Station 3+56 
towards the eastern end of the project limits at the proposed staging area. The potential 
rockfall hazard sources were identified from the upper slope to the top of the lower cut slope. 
We envision that the falling rocks have a high potential to be a rockfall hazard due to the 
taller height, less vegetation ground cover, and adverse tuff rock bedding orientation. 
Although the high potential of rockfall hazard is presented throughout the steep slope 
terrain within the project limits, the thin “laminated” volcanic tuff formation generally tends to 
break down to smaller fragments from falling impact due to the brittleness characteristics 
with low potential for rolling and/or bouncing as a hard basalt rock.

Our assessment of the potential rockfall conditions included the performance of site 
reconnaissance and computer simulation of potential rockfall activity. A detailed discussion 
of the computer rockfall simulation analyses is provided in the following section.

Based on our study, rockfall protection measures such as extensive rock slope 
scaling/rock demolition and the installation of wire mesh netting on the slopes are not 
recommended as viable rockfall mitigation alternatives for this project. We understand that 
the rockfall protection for the potential hazard conditions is only a temporary mitigation for 
safety concerns during construction activities and is not a permanent fixture in securing the 
slope. 
Rock slope scaling/rock demolition generally consists of the removal of potential 
rockfall hazards such as existing loose rocks and unstable rock outcroppings from the slope 
surface. Rock slope scaling is not a permanent mitigation alternative because the slope 
scaling must be repeated at future intervals to maintain the slope free of loose rock and keep 
pace with evolving slope erosion conditions. Furthermore, rock demolition work generally 
produces significant disturbance to the slope and existing vegetation. 
The installation and anchoring of wire mesh panels on sloped surfaces to control 
falling rock material beneath the net are not considered cost-effective due to the large slope 
area to be covered containing generally only widely scattered, small dimension loose rock 
materials. In addition, significant disturbance of the slope is anticipated due to the cutting of 
vegetation for mesh installation and the drilling and grouting of the anchorage system. 
Based on the results of the rockfall simulation analysis, we believe the rockfall hazard 
may greatly impact the construction operation along the tunnel access road near the bottom 
of the slope within the horizontal distance of 20 to 30 feet from the toe of the slope. In the 
areas further away from the toe of the slope, the potential impact from the rockfall may be 
diminished and may be protected by a low height barrier or earth berm, to retain smaller 
rock debris from rolling/bouncing into the working area. 
In addition to evaluating possible protection measures, the findings from our study 
were used to develop an appropriate conceptual site-specific rockfall hazard mitigation 
scheme. In general, the proposed rockfall hazard mitigation scheme involves the installation 
of a hybrid rockfall protection system, including protective chain-link fencing along the top of 
the lower cut slope and chain-link wire mesh extending down to the bottom of the lower cut 
slope, thus alleviating the need to perform extensive rockfall mitigation work on the hillside 
to stabilize the existing rock materials. 
Computer Rockfall Simulation Analysis 
As part of our evaluation of the potential rockfall hazard conditions at the site, we 
performed a computer simulation of potential falling rock activity using input data collected 
during our site reconnaissance. We used the CRSP Version 4.0 for our analysis. 
Topographic information of the existing steep slope was not available at the time this 
report was prepared. The slope sections were generated based on the topographic 
information provided by the United States Geological Survey (USGS) and Google Earth 
map. The slope sections were further modified/verified based on the visual 
measurements during the site reconnaissance. The purpose for the CRSP analysis was 
to model the existing slope and potential rockfall conditions to obtain a statistical analysis

of the simulation results to assist in the evaluation of potential rockfall encroachment and 
design parameters for an appropriate rockfall protection structure. 
The CRSP is a widely accepted engineering tool used to estimate potential rockfall 
behavior by simulating rockfall based on input parameters assigned on a site-specific 
basis. The input parameters for this project were assigned based on our field observations 
and measurements collected in the field. The program provides a statistical analysis of 
potential rockfall activity based on the slope topographic profile and other specific input 
information such as rock size, shape, and numerical parameters used to quantify the 
typical ground surface conditions. Information obtained from the CRSP analysis includes 
the predicted falling rock velocity, bouncing height, kinetic energy, and encroachment 
distance. The rock impact kinetic energy (reported in foot-ton units) can be computed 
from the rockfall simulation for any specified point on the slope. In this analysis, the 
possible rock impact energy was calculated at a designated analysis point set at the 
proposed fence location at the top of the lower cut slope. Colorado Rockfall Simulation 
Program calculates the impact energy based on the rock size and shape, rock density, 
slope inclination, and other input parameters that describe the slope surface conditions 
including vegetation and ground surface roughness and hardness. In addition, the CRSP 
computes the possible rock bounce height at a specified analysis point based on the 
model input information. The rockfall simulation output information, when used in 
conjunction with a good experienced understanding of the potential rockfall conditions 
and processes at a study site, is very useful to assist in the site-specific design and 
physical location of appropriate rockfall protection systems. 
Eight selected model slope profiles (identified as Slope Profiles “A” through “H”) 
were developed from the gathered topographic information and field visual 
measurements to support our CRSP analysis. The slope profile locations are shown on 
the General Site Plan, Plate 2. In addition, the graphic representation of the idealized 
slope profiles showing the typical character and general geometric condition of the slope 
used in our rockfall simulation analyses are shown on Plates 3.1 through 3.3. 
Our CRSP analysis was performed using discoidal shaped boulders (in respect to 
the general tuff material anticipated on-site) with a thickness of 1.5 feet. The diameter of 
the discoidal shaped boulders ranging from 1.0 to 3.0 feet was initiated from the potential 
rockfall source areas, as designated on the Slope Profiles, Plates 3.1 through 3.3. For 
each rockfall simulation run, 1,000 source rocks were released from random points within 
the rockfall source region to develop a statistical distribution of the results. We used 
selected numerical input coefficients to approximate the typical ground surface hardness, 
relief, roughness, and vegetation conditions.  
Our minimum rockfall protection criterion is defined as the probable interception 
and catchment of 90 percent of possible rockfall hazards assessed by the computer 
rockfall simulation analysis (CRSP). The 90 percent catchment criterion is a target that is 
commonly used in engineering practice for evaluation criteria that can be quantified by a 
statistical and probability analysis using model data for natural occurrences such as

rockfall activity. Because the analyses are based on statistics and probability, a

Based on the results of our CRSP analyses, for all rock sizes used, the possible 
rockfall encroachment to the access roadway (passing CRSP analysis point) was found 
to be 100 percent. The maximum impact energy at the proposed protective fence location 
was found to range from minimal to about 13 foot-tons. The higher kinetic energies and 
bounce heights produced by the rockfall simulation may be due to the reduced surface 
soil and vegetation cover on some of the graded slopes allowing a greater build-up of 
rolling velocity energy. 
For the design of a specialized hybrid rockfall impact system, the anticipated rock 
impact kinetic energy (calculated in foot-tons by CRSP) is used to select an appropriate 
supporting anchorage. The lowest capacity rockfall impact barriers typically are rated at 
about 30 foot-tons of design capacity. The probable impact energy calculated using the 
CRSP is substantially below the lowest rated capacity rockfall impact barrier fence. 
Therefore, it is our professional opinion that a traditional heavy-duty chain-link fence 
system may be used to contain potential rockfall at this low energy potential rockfall site. 
Based on the results of the CRSP analyses combined with our site reconnaissance 
and experience, we believe that an 8-foot rock impact system with a commercial/industrial 
heavy-duty chain-link fence along the top of the lower cut slope would comprise an 
appropriate rockfall protection system for this project site based on the conditions 
observed and analyzed. The chain-link fence wire mesh will be extended from the top of 
the rockfall impact barrier down to the bottom of the slope to retain the loose rock 
fragments from the upper slope and/or the face of the lower cut slope from encroachment 
to the construction working areas. The aboveground fence height is selected based on 
the probable rock bounce height computed by the CRSP at the desired fence location. 
Based on the results of our CRSP analyses, the calculated probable rock bounce heights 
at the fence location are generally low due to anticipated rock sliding and rock brittleness 
characteristics. Based on the CRSP results and our site observations, extreme rock 
bounce heights are not anticipated at this site.

Rockfall Impact Barrier 
Rockfall impact barriers are used worldwide as an effective rockfall mitigation and 
protection system. Rockfall impact barriers commonly are constructed on hillsides 
where the protection of downslope areas from various widespread and non-specific 
sources of potential falling rock is necessary. Rockfall impact barriers are considered 
to be a viable rockfall protection measure where a specific and limited source of 
rockfall cannot be readily identified and stabilized, or where the source area may 
encompass disturbance-sensitive land that makes other mitigation measures 
impractical to implement. 
The rockfall impact barrier is a specialized barrier structure designed to flex and 
absorb the impact energy of falling boulders. The barrier system essentially consists 
of a series of steel posts linked by wire rope cables with steel ring nets and wire mesh 
or chain-link fence material spanning between posts. Upslope wire rope anchors 
installed in drilled holes and grouted in-place with cement grout retrain the barrier 
posts. Rockfall barrier manufacturers fabricate the barrier components with varying 
capacities and dimensions based on the anticipated design load (rockfall impact 
energy) and probable rock bounce heights. Therefore, it is critical to carefully evaluate 
the existing site conditions and perform a statistical simulation of the rockfall activity 
to assist in the design of an appropriately sized rockfall impact barrier system. 
The impact barrier may be designed for varying load impacts that are estimated based 
on the existing site conditions such as boulder size, slope inclination and composition, 
computed rolling velocities, and possible boulder bounce heights from rockfall impacts 
analyzed using the CRSP computer software. 
After analysis using the CRSP, we recommend that 8-foot tall steel posts be placed 
two feet away from the edge of the lower inclined plane toward the top of the lower 
subvertical cut slope, and secured onto the slope using a base plate with four anchor 
bolts to be inserted 2 feet into the slope material. Upslope wire rope anchors of about 
25 feet that are installed in drilled holes and grouted in-place with cement grout retrain 
the barrier posts, providing an ultimate pull-out resistance of 27 kips. 
Draped Wire Mesh 
The installation of the chain-linked fence or wire mesh material extending from the top 
of the steel post of the hybrid rockfall fence to the bottom of the lower subvertical cut 
slope will serve as a draped wire mesh covering the slope face to control the release 
of loose rocks that may develop on the slope face. We anticipate that slope scaling 
efforts will not be necessary prior to the installation of the hybrid rockfall fence. 
Typically, we envision the draped wire mesh should retain rock (maximum of 3 feet in

The draped wire mesh cover essentially blocks rock material against the slope face

It is not the primary intent to closely pin the wire mesh to the slope face as a retention 
system. The draped mesh should provide positive rockfall control for the useful life of 
the wire mesh material. 
Rockfall Hazard Mitigation Design 
Our professional opinion is that the recommended rockfall protection system offers 
a fairly comprehensive and cost-effective mitigation system capable of providing a high 
level of short-term rockfall protection for the construction activities at the existing roadway 
from the potential rockfall hazard area identified in this study.

LIMITATIONS 
The findings and recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon 
information obtained from visual site observations and computer simulation and statistical 
analysis of potential rockfall activity only. Variation in the surface and subsurface 
conditions between our observations and analysis points may occur, and the nature and 
extent of these variations may not become evident until additional field exploration (such 
as site reconnaissance or near-surface probing) or construction is underway. If variations 
then appear evident, it will be necessary to re-evaluate the findings and recommendations 
provided herein. 
It should be noted that slopes composed of rock materials (rock slopes) deteriorate 
with the passage of time due to natural weathering processes, wet-dry and hot-cold 
cycles, and erosion conditions. Due to the inherent deterioration of rock slopes resulting 
from natural processes (weathering, wet-dry and hot-cold cycles, and erosion conditions), 
the potential for rockfall hazard at any site changes with the passage of time. Therefore, 
the findings and recommendations contained herein may be used only within a 
reasonable time from the date of issuance of this letter report. Land use, site conditions, 
and/or other factors may change with the passage of time. Therefore, additional work 
(such as site reconnaissance for review of the site conditions) to evaluate the applicability 
of the findings and preliminary recommendations contained in this letter report due to 
changes with the passage of time may be required.
