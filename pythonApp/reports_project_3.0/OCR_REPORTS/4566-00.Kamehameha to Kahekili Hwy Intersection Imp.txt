REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
two shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age As volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau banked
against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau known as Schofield Plateau
The project site is located on the easterly flank of Koolau and its geomorphology and
subsurface conditions are related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of sea level during the
Pleistocene Epoch and the location of the central caldera complex of Koolau The base of
the stratigraphic section in this area is composed of Koolau Basalt
The Koolau volcanic shield was built during the late Pliocene Epoch and early
Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows The main shield building activities
ceased approximately 2 5 million years ago Evidence from drilled wells indicates that the
Island of Oahu has subsided as much as 1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic
activity Macdonald and Abbott 1970
The central caldera complex of the volcano extended approximately from the area of
Mount Olomana northwesterly to the vicinity of Kualoa During the active phase of Koolau
hydrothermal activity by rising gases and fluids altered the basaltic rock into easily eroded
materials
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill
the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels rose
because more water was available The land mass of Oahu remained essentially stable
GEOLABS INC
W O 4566 00 Pavement Justification Page 4
during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to the
present sea level on Oahu
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the volcanic dome of Koolau
The headward erosion of these valleys reached the easily eroded materials in the caldera
complex and carved broad amphitheater headed valleys The heads of these valleys
coalesced to form a long steep cliff or pai
When the sea levels rose again sediments accumulated in the valleys In the vicinity
of the project site these sediments accumulated in a marshy low energy environment along
the shore of Kaneohe Bay The project site is situated in a transition area between shallow
basalt rock formation and man made fill over soft marshy sediments

Subgrade Preparation
In general the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared
and grubbed at the on set of earthwork Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site The existing AC underlying
base and subbase materials should also be completely removed and properly disposed
of off site Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to
receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or stiff material
and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted general fill The
excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
After clearing and grubbing the future pavement areas should be excavated where
necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the aggregate subbase layer
Clayey soil deposits may be exposed at the pavement subgrade level towards
Kamehameha Highway northwest leg as encountered in Boring No 4 drilled for the
project If clayey soils are exposed at the pavement subgrade level we recommend that
the clayey soils be removed to provide a minimum of 2 feet of compacted granular fill

material below the pavement structure bottom of the ASB layer Contract documents
should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and compacted fill
placement as a contingent item to account for the potential presence of clayey soil deposits
at the pavement subgrade level
The pavement subgrade soils or over excavated subgrades should be scarified
to a depth of at least 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
AASHTO T 180 or ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
If pumping conditions are encountered during compaction of the subgrade soils the
subgrade soils may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for pumping
subgrade conditions As a guide one sack of cement may be used for every 50 square
feet of subgrade area This preliminary mix should be adjusted in the field to suit the
exposed soil conditions Alternatively the subgrade may be proof rolled and a woven
geotextile fabric such as Mirafi 500X or equivalent should be placed on the subgrade prior
to placement of the aggregate subbase layer
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend
that the soils be scarified and prepared again as recommended above Saturation and
subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage
may require over excavation of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted fill
Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation
and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the over excavation quantities

Fill Materials and Compaction Requirements
In general the excavated on site materials may be re used as a source of general
fill and backfill materials up to the bottom of the aggregate subbase layer provided that
they are free of organic materials and contain no lumps or rock fragments greater than
6 inches in largest dimension The on site fill materials should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Imported fill materials below the aggregate subbase layer if required should consist
of non expansive select granular material such as crushed coral mudrock or basalt The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles greater than 3 inches
in largest dimension The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more with
a maximum swell value of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with
AASHTO T1193 or ASTM Test Designation D 1883 test procedures In addition select
granular fill material should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the
No 200 sieve Imported materials should be brought to above the optimum moisture
content and compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction Surfaces should
be wetted finished to create relatively smooth unyielding subgrades and kept moist until
covered by pavements or slabs Imported materials should be tested and approved by
Geolabs prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use
Aggregate subbase materials required for the new pavements should conform to the
requirements stipulated in the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and Public
Works Construction 1994 The aggregate subbase should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to
not less than 95 percent of the maximum density according to Section 106 09 Special
Test Methods of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works
Construction

accordance with Section 312 Plant Mix Glassphalt Concrete Base Course of the Hawaii

basalt aggregate placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness and

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement surface
should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry surface water off the pavement
to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should not be allowed on the site
during or after construction To collect infiltrated water into the pavement structural section
we recommend that a pavement edge drain system be provided along the sides of the new
travelway
We envision that an edge drain will be needed to collect and daylight the infiltrated
water into the pavement structural section from the untreated permeable base layer
The edge drain should consist of a 6 inch PVC perforated pipe with perforations facing
down surrounded by untreated permeable base A permeable separator non woven filter
fabric conforming to Section 648 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge
and Public Works Construction 1994 HSS should be placed around the untreated
permeable base to reduce the potential for migration of fines into the drainage material as
shown on Plate 3 In addition a permeable separator should also be placed between the
untreated permeable base and aggregate subbase Care should be exercised when placing
the subbase base and or construction of the surface course to avoid damaging the edge
GEOLABS INC
W O 4566 00 Pavement Justification Page 16
drain collector pipe A typical edge drain detail is provided on Plate 3 The edge drains
should be daylighted into appropriate drainage structures for proper discharge Development
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or
premature failure of the pavements

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the highway intersection site were explored by drilling
and sampling four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 extending to depths of
about 10 to 24 5 feet below the existing ground pavement surface A bulk sample
designated as Bulk 1 was obtained of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement
support characteristics The approximate locations of the test borings and bulk sample are
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
equipped with hollow stem augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the
field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive
strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock coral formations encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would
be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80

RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
