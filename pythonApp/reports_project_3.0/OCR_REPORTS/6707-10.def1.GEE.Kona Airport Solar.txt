performed for the proposed Kona Airport Parking Lot Solar Installation solar power

Island of Hawaii At Sunpower s request we have used two borings that were
previously drilled and sampled in support of the Underground Injection Control UIC
permit application for drywells located in the same parking lot as the basis of this

Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers
an area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of
the following five shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity during
recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the
project site appears to be predominately pahoehoe type flows which spread and
ponded as they approached the ocean It is believed that the site actually sits on part of
the lava flow Pahoehoe lava is characterized by a smooth
rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially in pahoehoe
type lava Cavities form when lava is in a molten state and they manifest as both lava
tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes form
when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure that
may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the flow Blisters and pockets
inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent

Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused by shifts in
the tectonic plates forming the earth s crust In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is
primarily linked to volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally
occurs before or during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may result from the
underground movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt
The Island of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so
small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments However some of the
earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the
Island of Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent
are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground shaking
diminishes with increased distance from the southern portion of the Island of Hawaii

Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the solar carport structure foundations
because of the significant increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when
compared to shallow foundations However the performance of the drilled
shafts will depend significantly upon the contractor s method of construction
and construction procedures In our opinion the following conditions would
have an impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft foundation
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore
proper construction techniques are important The contractor should
exercise care in drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the holes
A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch range should be used
to provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding rock
formation The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the
potential for segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix e g a
vertical drop without hitting the reinforcement It should be noted that clinker
zones encountered within the drilled borehole may result in significant
increases in the amount of grout required

Utility Trenches
We envision that new electrical duct lines will be required to service the
proposed new solar electric system In general granular bedding consisting
of 6 inches of clean sand or No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation is recommended under the duct lines
Free draining granular materials such as clean sand or No 3B Fine gravel
ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the initial trench
backfill up to about 6 inches above the duct lines to provide adequate
support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill
thus reducing the possibility of pipe damage It is critical to use this free
draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the
pipes Improper backfill material and placement procedures could result in
backfill settlement and pipe damage

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the
duct lines to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site
soils or imported select granular fill material The backfill should be placed in
maximum 6 inch level loose lifts moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content and mechanically compacted to no less than 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Foundation Probing
Our experience suggests that cavities and or voids are commonly
encountered in basalt rock formations To reduce the potential for loss of
foundation support resulting from the collapse of cavities and or sinkholes
below foundations a program of cavity probing and grouting at the
foundation locations is generally recommended However since no obvious
cavities were encountered at the time of our previous field exploration at the
site and the vertical loads are light it is likely that the risk of cavities under
the site is minimal If the owner is willing to accept the risk the probing
operation may be omitted

Excavation
The basalt formation in the project area appears to be slightly weathered and
medium hard to hard in nature We anticipate some difficult excavation conditions may
arise during construction It is possible that the excavation for utility line trenches will
require the use of hoerams or chipping The contractor must exercise care to avoid
over ripping which would disrupt the structure of the basalt formation resulting in a
potential loss of bearing strength for improvements in the vicinity
The above discussions regarding the rippability of the materials are based on our
visual observation of the existing rock formation and field and laboratory data from
borings drilled in the area We recommend encouraging contractors to examine the site

Field Exploration
No borings were drilled and sampled specifically for this exploration Instead at the
request of Sunpower Systems borings drilled in support of the UIC permit application for
new drywells in the parking lot were used as the basis of our analyses These borings
extended to depths ranging between 25 5 and 26 0 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our exploration
During our previous exploration at the site the subsurface conditions were explored
by drilling and sampling two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 The approximate
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
Mobile B 53 truck mounted drill rig equipped with wireline coring tools
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D 2488 Standard
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils and monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous full time basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and
by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D 2487
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Soil Log Legend Plate A 0 1 Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 and A 2
Core samples of the volcanic rock materials encountered at the project site were
obtained by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description Rock
cores were described in general accordance with the Rock Description System as shown
on the Rock Log Legend Plate A 0 2
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
