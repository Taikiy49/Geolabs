2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The project site is located in an area dominated by recent stream and lagoonal
deposits overlying formations of volcanic origin During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age
sea levels fluctuated and reached as much as 300 feet below the present sea level
Stream transported sediments consisting mainly of boulders cobbles and gravel were
deposited at a lower sea level As the sea level began to rise the capacity of the streams
was reduced and finer sands and silts were subsequently deposited At some point the
accumulation of sediments could not keep up with the rising sea level and the area
became flooded As a result very fine grained sediments were then deposited in marine
or estuarine environments Eventually the sea level stabilized while the sedimentation
process of fine grained sediments and shell beds continued Sediments with shell beds
are characteristic of near shore deposits Subsequently organic sediments which are
peaty and contain plant fibers and fragments accumulated and are indicative of marshy
conditions Land development within the last 100 years has brought the site to its present
form Many of the resulting fills over the marsh deposits are of poor quality in terms of
supporting heavy structural loads

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about

approximately 4 to 5 feet of surface fills consisting of very stiff to hard silty clays Below
the upper fill crust our borings generally encountered a relatively thick marsh deposit
consisting of soft to very soft organic clays extending to the maximum depth explored of
approximately 31 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Shallow groundwater conditions were encountered in the drilled borings at depths
between about 4 and 6 feet below the existing ground surface These depths generally
correspond to approximately Elevation 2 4 to 4 5 feet MSL Due to the proximity of the
project site to Wailani Stream and Pearl Harbor it is likely that the water levels measured
in the borings during our field exploration were affected by seasonal precipitation storm
surge conditions and tidal influences

3 1 Drilled Shaft Foundations
In order to develop the required bearing and lateral load resistances the proposed
new light pole structures may be supported by a foundation system consisting of
cast in place concrete drilled shafts Based on the concrete pedestal dimensions for the
new light pole structures we believe that a minimum diameter of 30 inches should be used
for the proposed drilled shaft foundations Based on the anticipated structural loads and
the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we recommend that drilled shaft
foundations with a minimum diameter of 42 inches and an embedment length of not less
than 6 and 7 feet below the design finished grades be used for the 26 and 40 foot high
new light poles located on level ground conditions respectively
The cast in place concrete drilled shafts would derive vertical support principally
from skin friction between the shafts and the surrounding soils An allowable compressive
load capacity of up to 15 kips per shaft may be used for dead plus live loads and may be
increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces
Lateral loads imposed on the light pole foundations generally may be resisted by
resistance of the shaft soil system to deflect Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is
a function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable
deflection at the top of the shaft and induced moment in the shaft Based on the
information provided each drilled shaft for the 26 foot high light pole will be subjected to a
lateral load of 780 pounds and an overturning moment of about 13 96 kip feet at the top of
the drilled shaft Based on our foundation analyses the maximum moment induced in the
drilled shaft increases slightly to 13 92 kip feet within a depth of about 6 inches below the
top of the shaft The drilled shaft for the 40 foot high light pole will be subjected to a lateral
load of 1 071 pounds and an overturning moment of about 26 14 kip feet at the top of the
drilled shaft Based on our foundation analyses the maximum moment induced in the
drilled shaft increases slightly to 26 17 kip feet within a depth of about 6 inches below the
top of the shaft Our lateral load analyses indicated that the estimated lateral deflection at
the top of the drilled shaft should be less than inch for both cases

3 2 Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the light pole foundations because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations
However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the
following conditions would have an impact on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled shaft
foundations
Soft Clayey Soils
Based on our field exploration the new light pole locations are generally underlain
by soft to very soft marsh deposits extending to depths over 31 5 feet below the
existing ground surface To reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes
temporary casing may be required during the foundation construction work
Therefore we recommend that casing of drilled holes be considered as a
contingent item with additive and deductive unit prices included in the contract
documents
Care should be exercised during removal of the temporary casing to reduce the
potential for necking of the concrete shaft Therefore a minimum 5 foot head of
concrete should be maintained above the bottom of the casing during removal of
the casing The anticipated high groundwater level along the shoreline may also
pose further construction difficulties since proper observation of the sides and
bottoms of the drilled shaft may not be possible
Workmanship
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper

3 3 Utility Trench
We envision that new electrical duct lines will be required to service the proposed
new lighting system In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or
S4C is recommended under the electrical duct lines Free draining granular materials
such as clean sand or S4C should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about
6 inches above the duct lines to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce
the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It
is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper backfill material and placement procedures could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the duct lines
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site soils or imported select
granular fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about
31 5 feet below the existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers The approximate locations
of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling
30 inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third
6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
