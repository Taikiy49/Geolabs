Dear Ms. Miyasato: 
This letter report presents the results of our permeability testing conducted for the 
Hawaii Department of Defense, Keaukaha Military Reservation Building 621, 
Underground Injection Control (UIC) project in the Hilo airport area on the Island of 
Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1. 
This report summarizes our field observations and presents the results of our 
permeability testing and engineering analyses for the project. These findings are intended 
to support the preparation of a UIC Injection Well Status Report for Injection Well Nos. 1 
and 2 at the project site. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject 
to the limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
Based on the information provided, the Hawaii Department of Defense, Building 
621 facility at the Keaukaha Military Reservation (KMR) operates two injection wells, 
identified as Well Nos. 1 and 2, for the on-site disposal of septic effluent. The KMR 
Building 621 facility is identified by UIC Facility I.D. No. 8-4203.19. Injection Well Nos. 1 
and 2 at the KMR Building 621 facility is regulated by UIC Permit No. UH-2509. 
We understand that observation and permeability testing services are desired in 
support of the preparation of a UIC Injection Well Status Report for Well Nos. 1 and 2 at 
the Hawaii Army National Guard Battery Building 621 facility at the Keaukaha Military 
Reservation in Hilo, Hawaii.

PURPOSE AND SCOPE 
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering services was to obtain an overview 
of the existing condition of the injection wells by visual observation and short-term 
permeability injection testing in support of a UIC Injection Well Status Report prepared by 
others for the project. Generally, the scope of work included the following tasks: 
1. 
Review of available documentations pertaining to the UIC permit 
application. 
2. 
Mobilization of a water truck with an operator to the project site. 
3. 
Performance of short-term duration permeability tests to evaluate the 
disposal capacity of the existing wells. 
4. 
Analysis of the field data by our engineer. 
5. 
Preparation of this letter report summarizing our work on the project and 
presenting our findings and recommendations. 
6. 
Coordination of our overall work on the project by our engineer. 
7. 
Quality assurance of our work and client/design team consultation by our 
principal engineer. 
8. 
Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting, word processing and clerical 
support. 
EXISTING WELL AND SITE CONDITIONS 
Well Nos. 1 and 2 are within the Keaukaha Military Reservation adjacent to 
Building 621, as approximately indicated on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. Well No. 1 
is on the southerly side of Building 621 and serves the women’s latrine and septic tank 
system. Well No. 2 is on the northerly side of Building 621 and serves the men’s latrine 
and septic tank system.  
Our engineer conducted the inspection and testing of Well Nos. 1 and 2 on 
November 18, 2020. The well covers were removed by MKR personnel prior to our site 
visit. Both wellheads are sited on generally level ground with lawn grass surrounding the 
wellheads and vicinity. 
We observed that the ground surface contained no ponded water accumulation. 
The surface completions of both wells consist of a steel manhole cover set flush with the 
ground surface. Below the manhole cover, access to the well is via a concrete plug cover 
measuring about 16 inches square.

Well No. 1 
Well No. 1 is on the southerly side of Building 621 in an open lawn area adjacent 
to the building. The wellhead is on generally firm level ground. The wellhead is generally 
constructed flush with the existing lawn surface and consists of a steel manhole cover at 
the ground surface with a recessed access port consisting of a concrete plug cover 
measuring about 16 inches square. 
We observed that the concrete plug cover seats firmly into the concrete wellhead 
surface with minimal gap space. We observed steel manhole covers upstream from the 
Well No. 1 wellhead that house access ports associated with the septic tank. No other 
existing aboveground appurtenances were observed associated with the well operation. 
With the well cover open, we sensed a mild to moderate sewage odor emanating 
from the well. The existing fluid level in the well was measured at 22.1 feet. We measured 
the existing depth of the well at about 22.3 feet below the existing ground surface. 
Therefore, there was an approximate 2 inches of fluid in the well prior to our testing. The 
accumulated fluid contained in the well appeared relatively clear. The rough and irregular 
floor of the well appeared to contain some limited accumulation of a dark-colored silty 
sand material. Some limited organic matter and debris were observed on the well floor. 
We observed a 6-inch diameter PVC effluent discharge pipe entering the well 
sidewall at about a 4-foot depth below the ground surface. No discharge fluid was 
observed emanating from the pipe during our inspection and testing. 
We observed that the circular-lined well diameter ranges from about 8.8 to 9.4 feet 
in the upper 11 feet of the well depth. Below about 11 feet in well depth, the well diameter 
appeared to taper to about 8 feet in diameter extending toward the well bottom. 
We observed that the well interior is lined to a depth of about 7 feet below the 
ground surface with grouted rock rubble generally consisting of hard basaltic cobbles and 
boulders containing some interstitial gaps between the rocks comprising the lining. Below 
about 7 feet in depth and extending toward the well floor, the well interior appears to be 
unlined and constructed in native hard basaltic rock condition. 
The integrity of the existing grouted rock rubble lining in the upper interior of the 
well and the exposed basalt rock formation in the lower interior of the well appear to be 
in good condition with no visible indication of well sidewall instability.

PERMEABILITY TESTING 
Our evaluation of the well permeability characteristics was conducted on 
November 18, 2020, between the hours of about 0745 and 1415.  The weather was cloudy 
during the majority of the testing, with some light rain in the afternoon. 
Permeability testing for Well Nos. 1 and 2 generally involved the short-term gravity 
injection of clear water from a water tanker truck, followed by measurement of the falling 
head in the well. The permeability testing was conducted in an effort to evaluate the 
drainage (infiltration) functionality of the existing wells. 
No septic effluent was observed discharging into Well No. 1 at the time of the 
permeability testing. A slight stream of effluent was observed discharging into Well No. 2 
during the test. The tests were conducted by discharging four loads of approximately 
2,500 gallons of water (resulting in about 10,000 gallons total injection) from a water truck 
under gravity feed through a 3-inch diameter hose placed in the lower portion of each 
well.  In between discharging of water truck loads and after discharging of the water, water 
level drops in the wells were measured.  It should be noted that Well Nos. 1 and 2 
contained about 1 to 2 inches in depth of fluid prior to the start of the test. 
Well No. 1 
Based on our measurements, approximately 10,000 gallons of clear water was 
discharged to the well from the water truck at an average discharge flow rate of about 
125 to 208 gallons per minute (gpm). The maximum accumulated head was measured at 
8.6 feet above the bottom of the well at the end of the short-term injection portion of the 
test.

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the results of our visual observations and the short-term permeability 
testing of Well Nos. 1 and 2 at the KMR Building 621 facility, the wells appear to be 
operating satisfactorily with no overt indication of potential failure. However, based on the 
testing, Well No. 1 appears to have a notable lower disposal capacity than Well No. 2.  
R.M. Towill Corporation

Based on the falling head permeability testing conducted, Well No. 1 has an 
estimated effluent capacity of about 33 gpm, equivalent to about 47,520 gallons per day 
(gpd), which exceeds the estimated design flow capacity of 3,000 gpd. A graphical 
presentation of the injection test results is presented on Plate A-1 in Appendix A. 
Based on the falling head permeability testing conducted, Well No. 2 has an 
effluent capacity of about 145 gpm, equivalent to about 208,800 gpd, which exceeds the 
estimated design flow capacity of 3,000 gpd. A graphical presentation of the injection test 
results is presented on Plate A-2 in Appendix A.  
Based on our observation of the existing well site and vicinity conditions, we 
recommend the following to maintain the performance of the wells. 
Perform routine inspection of the fluid levels contained in the septic tank to 
gauge when servicing may be needed and schedule servicing to include the 
pumping and cleaning of the septic tank system on a regular basis. 
Periodically inspect any upstream flow control valves to ensure operation 
and functionality. 
Continue to conduct annual injection well inspections as per the UIC permit 
conditions. 
Review and abide by the best practice guidelines for effluent discharge that 
was provided in the Environmental Notice dated December 2016 and 
provided to the facility operator by the State of Hawaii Department of Health, 
Safe Drinking Water Branch, Underground Injection Control (UIC) Program.
