EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
As a part of the Seismic Safety Program for the
U S Naval Base at Subic Bay Republic of the
Philippines a Geological Hazards Study was
performed to evaluate the geotechnical aspect of
the seismic safety of the existing buildings on
the base
The study was conducted primarily by means of
extensive research of existing boring geologic
and seismic data available at various
organizations in both the U S and the
Philippines Available soil and geologic data was
somewhat limited but adequate for the preliminary
nature of the study Detailed seismic data was
very limited with only one 1 Philippine
strong motion record available for the analyses
Therefore data from nearby countries with similar
tectonic settings were used to supplement the
Philippine seismic data
Based on the data collected a General Geologic
Map with generalized soil profiles and a design
earthquake for the base were developed for
evaluation of geological hazards including
liquefaction potential ground acceleration
response and seismically generated landslide
potential
The evaluation results are presented in three 3
Geological Hazards Maps which include
1 A Liquefaction Hazards Map delineating the
potential liquefaction areas and the
corresponding settlement estimates for the
design earthquake

soil type areas for which proposed ground

3 A Seismic Landslide Potential Map delineating

INTRODUCTION
This report and the attached set of Geological Hazards Maps present
the results of our geological hazards study for the U S Naval Base in
Subic Bay of the Republic of the Philippines The general location of the
study site is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report and the attached maps summarize the findings of our study
and present our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses of the data obtained during the study These recommendations are
intended for preliminary evaluation of the geotechnical aspects of the
seismic safety of the existing buildings in the naval complex
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Subic Bay Naval Complex is located in one of the most seismically
active areas of the world It has been categorized in the Navy Design
Manual NAVFAC P 355 as a Seismic Zone 4 area on a O to 4 zone scale
indicating the potential for great damage to buildings and structures due
to earthquakes
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1924 00 A Page 3
Dk No 64
Under the National Earthquake Hazard Reduction Program the
Department of the Navy has instituted a Seismic Safety Program to evaluate
and reduce the susceptibility of existing naval facilities to earthquake
damage A standard methodology has been established to first perform a
rapid evaluation of the structural stability of the structures during an
earthquake which is then followed by more detailed structural analysis for
selected structures and the normal MILCON funding processes and actual
strengthening of selected structures
A preliminary evaluation of the existing buildings in selected areas
of the Naval Complex had been performed by H J Degenkolb Associates
Engineers of California Recognizing that the poor soil conditions in
certain areas of the base would govern the amount and type of structural
damage caused by an earthquake it was proposed to conduct a geological
hazards study with the aim of preparing a Geological Hazards Map of the
entire base to enable a more detailed seismic analysis of selected
structures in these previously investigated areas and other facilities in
the base
Based on the current practice of geotechnical engineering it is
recognized that for detailed seismic evaluation the ground motion at the
site of each existing building in the base must be defined considering the
local soil conditions which may be categorized in accordance with the
Structural Engineers Association of California SEAOC and Applied
Technology Council ATC classifications The possibility of soil
liquefaction and seismically generated landslides in local areas of the
base should also be determined in the geological hazards study before any
detailed seismic evaluations of the existing buildings are performed
Therefore the proposed Geological Hazards Map for the U S Naval
Base in Subic Bay should define applicable zones for site specific ground
response spectra soil liquefaction and or seismic landslide potential
especially for the populated developed areas on the base

SITE DESCRIPTION
Subic Bay is situated on the northwestern shore of the Bataan
Peninsula on the island of Luzon Republic of the Philippines A natural
deep water harbor Subic Bay has been the site of naval activities since
1884 when the Spanish first selected Subic as their main Far East naval
port Today however Subic Bay provides support facilities for the U S
Navy s Seventh Fleet Over 36 000 personnel are employed by the Naval
Base including eight major commands and more than 355 smaller units and
detachments
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1924 00 A Page 9
Dk No 64
Most of the Naval Base Complex and support facilities are situated on
a relatively flat to gently sloping coastal plain along the bay
perimeter Other facilities including the existing hospital and most
housing areas are located on the relatively steep and densely vegetated
highland portions of the base
Located on the flanks of Mount Natib and Mount Balakibok major
volcanic centers of the Zambales Bataan Volcanic Highlands subprovince
the terrain of the Naval Base area consists of a northwest trending ridge
and valley morphology North of the Binictican River however the
terrain is generally irregular probably due to local volcanic activity
from Mount Santa Rita Link Station to the east
In general the topography slopes down towards the river and stream
channels associated valleys and bay perimeter at slope ratios of about
Relief is generally highest near the stream and river channels locally
exceeding 400 feet Elevations range from sea level near the bay
perimeter to greater than 1500 feet above Mean Sea Level MSL near the
eastern border of the base
The highland areas to the south and east as well as the lowlying
marshy areas and along slopes between the marsh and high ground are
densely vegetated with native and exotic tropical and sub tropical
vegetation These forested areas provide habitats for a variety of native
and feral animals including monkeys pigs lizards and snakes

Tectonic Setting and Regional Geology
The tectonic regime of Central Luzon is typical of an island arc
system In general island arcs occur along plate boundaries and are
accompanied by deep offshore oceanic trenches where subduction or the
sliding of one plate beneath another takes place Typically island arcs
are regions of extremely high seismic and volcanic activity for example
Taiwan Japan and Indonesia Earthquake data confirm that active
subduction is occurring along the Manila Trench and indicate that the
Eurasian Plate dips eastward beneath the converging Philippine Plate
Plate 7
Central Luzon lies within the Philippine Plate and is mainly the
product of island arc volcanic depositional and tectonic processes It
is segmented by several fracture zones all of which are the loci of
seismic activity
Physiographically Central Luzon consists of two mountain ranges
separated by a deep intermontane basin i e the Central Valley The
eastern margin is formed by the Sierra Madre Range a block of
metamorphic sedimentary and volcanic rocks formed before and during
the Eocene by westward dipping subduction of the Philippine Sea Plate
During the Oligocene and Miocene a shift in active subduction from
beneath the Sierra Madre to the west coast caused emplacement of the
ultramafic Zambales block western margin as well as subsidence in the
Central Valley area and the development of an associated volcanic arc
complex
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 1924 00 A Page 11
Dk No 64
Tectonic processes in Central Luzon are reflected in the form of
faulting throughout the southern Sierra Madre Range and boundaries of the
Central Valley The margins of the Central Valley consist of folded
tertiary sedimentary units and the entire valley fill which is more than
7 kilometers 4 3 miles deep in the center has been folded and faulted
into broad synclines and anticlines The Central Valley is bounded to the
north by the Philippine Fault a major tectonic feature that forms the
southern boundary of the Cordillera Central and Carballo Mountains and
separates the northern and southern segments of the Sierra Madre The
southern extension of the Central Valley lies to the east beneath Manila
Bay
The West Luzon Trough is a fault bounded basin 6 kilometers 3 7
miles deep filled with 4 kilometers 2 5 miles of horizontal sediments
of unknown age The east side of the West Luzon Trough is fault bounded
against a mass of ultramafic rock similar to that exposed in the Zambales
range to the north

zone is about 70 kilometers 44 miles

Local Geology
Subic Bay is located within the Western Volcanic Intrusive Province
of Luzon and comprises Recent to Pliocene Quaternary Volcanic deposits
The east west trending San Antonio Fracture zone is probably the closest
identified major fault zone approximately 4 to 10 miles northerly of the
Naval Base
The volcanic deposits in the Subic Bay Area are primarily of
pyroclastic origin and consist of lithic tuffs volcanic breccia
agglomerate and volcanic mudflows lahar believed to have been
generated by explosive eruptions from Mount Natib Mount Santa Rita and
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 1924 00 A Page 13
Dk No 64
Mount Balakibok three of the major volcanic centers which comprise the
Zambales Bataan Volcanic Highlands Subsequent chemical weathering of
these deposits has resulted in a mantle of chemically decomposed rock
residual soil saprolite overlying the relatively less weathered
tuffaceous rock
Numerous glacial eustatic sea level variations caused the emergence
and submergence of the land mass that produced soil deposits at various
levels both above and below the present sea level
Examination of the geomorphology of the area leads to the conjecture
that the highland portions of the area consist primarily of eroded
volcanic deposits which have been deeply incised by five north to
northwest trending streams the Boton and
Triboa rivers north to south respectively Alluvial and deltaic
deposits are primarily the product of stream deposition and accumulate
along the river mouths associated drainageways and valley entrances
Slopewash and colluvium are chiefly gravity driven deposits and form a
thin veneer over most of the slopes in the area and accumulate to
significant thicknesses at the base of steep slopes and cliffs The
materials encountered in the lowlying portions of the area are mainly of a
low energy deltaic estuarine or lagoonal depositional environment which
would be encountered in a sheltered bay area such as Subic Bay
Based on available geologic literature soil reports and field
observations the geologic materials have been divided into five
stratigraphic units From oldest to youngest these include
1 Andesite
The andesite unit dated at approximately 1 2 million years ESASCO
1976 is the oldest exposed unit at the Naval Base area and occurs
in the form of both vents and flows Mount Santa Rita Link Station
and a small nob near the POL storage area are both believed to be the

Dk No 64
source vents for the andesite flows The maximum exposed thickness
in these vent areas is approximately 300 feet The andesite flows
however are best exposed behind the Cubi Power Plant where they are
about 80 feet thick The total thickness of the andesite unit is
unknown since the basal contact is not exposed
The andesite is generally gray color where fresh and reddish brown in
moderately to highly weathered lava flows Fresh exposures are
generally porphyritic containing abundant hornblende and andesine
crystals in a glassy to partially devitrified groundmass In
general the lavas are thickly to thinly bedded and are moderately
fractured and moderately weathered However near the Cubi Power
Plant the lava flows are severely fractured and highly weathered
which may be the result of localized fault deformation
2 Tahar
The term lahar refers to a volcanic mudflow or landslide of
pyroclastic material on the flank of a volcano In the Subic Bay
area the lahars probably occurred contemporaneously with eruptions
of Mount Natib during Quaternary time within the last 1 6 million
years The resultant deposit generally consists of a jumbled
chaotic mass of boulder to gravel size clasts of basalt andesite
dacite and tuff in a matrix of sand silt ash and clay These
lahar deposits generally termed volcanic breccia or agglomerate
accumulated in topographic lows lying unconformably over the older
andesite lava flows
The lahar unit comprises a major portion of the stratigraphic section
and crops out extensively throughout the base On the eastern slopes
of the Naval Hospital the total thickness of this unit has been
estimated at 400 feet Other exposures are found in Kalayaan
Binictican and the Fuel Farm generally occurring in the form of
resistant ridges and cliffs The lahar unit also underlies the Subic
Power Plant site where an originally isolated hill was cut down
during the early development of the base

Dk No 64
Typically the lahar unit is thickly bedded relatively fresh and
ranges from poorly consolidated weakly cemented to very hard well
cemented When completely weathered the matrix is commonly a
yellowish tan stiff to very stiff sandy silt tuffaceous
containing completely to slightly weathered rock fragments ejecta
larger than 2 5 inches This material is relatively resistant to
surface erosion and commonly forms capping layers over other
underlying poorly consolidated materials Locally the lahar unit is
known as maritan and has been used extensively as an aggregate
source
Intercalated with the volcanic breccias and agglomerates of the lahar
unit are discontinuous lenses of lithic and lapilli tuffs The
lithic tuffs are generally whitish gray in color moderately to
highly weathered moderately to well cemented and contain various
sizes and amounts of lithic rock fragments and pumice The lapilli
tuffs however are only partially cemented and locally faintly
horizontal to cross bedded This may be due to fluvial erosion and
reworking of the pryoclastic material or the result of grain size
segregation due to air fall deposition or pulsations in the eruptive
event which caused the deposition of the pyroclastics
3 Interbedded Lahar and Saprolitized Tuff
Located in the northern and extreme southern portions of the area are
discontinuous interbeds of lahar deposits and saprolitized tuff The
lahar deposits are generally consistent with the lahar unit described
above The saprolitized tuff unit is generally a soil and is formed
from the in situ chemical weathering of air fall tuff and still
contains some of the compositional and structural characteristics of
the original parent rock Scattered spheroidally weathered boulders
and cobbles of andesite as well as clasts of the lahar unit are also
present in this unit

In the Kalayaan area a poorly exposed outcrop of latite lava flows
appears to lie stratigraphically above the lahar unit These lavas
are generally gray to purplish brown in color and are highly
weathered and leached The rock hardess of the latite generally

A Residual Soil Saprolitized Tuff
The residual soil saprolitized tuff is the most areally extensive
unit in the region and appears to be concentrated in the southern
portion of the base The chemical composition and structure of this
unit indicates that it was deposited as pyroclastic air fall
probably from one or more of the volcanic centers in the highland
areas These ashy deposits blanket most peaks and highland ridges in
the area However in the northern portion of the base it is
conspicuously absent possibly having been removed by erosion
Generally this unit is reddish brown to light yellow orange in color
and is easily recognizable where exposed It has been reported
throughout the region including north and west of Olongapo south of
the Naval Magazine area and on Grande Island Due to the large areal
extent of this unit and extremely high degree of weathering it is
highly susceptible to slope failure and is considered one of the
most hazardous units in the base area
The upper portion of the unit is a residual soil bearing little
resemblance to the parent material It is commonly a reddish brown
to orange clayey silt with localized white patches lobes of clay
Widely scattered weathered subrounded ejecta are also present
Generally the residual soil is only several feet thick but has been
reported to depths of 50 feet near Binictican Housing

Underlying the residual soil is the saprolite formed by complete
chemical decomposition of the parent rock but still retains the
original rock structures or fabric This material is predominantly
reddish brown in color and can be crushed by hand to a silty or
clayey soil and disintegrates when soaked in water Scattered lithic

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
The scope of our study was designed to complement the on going
structural seismic analysis at the base Since local areas in the Naval
Complex may be subject to large liquefaction settlement and or mass
instability during the design earthquake strengthening of existing
structures in these areas may be impractical Therefore prior to the
detailed seismic analysis of the structures it is important to delineate
these potentially hazardous areas
It must be recognized that this study is based on a limited amount of
existing data collected through an extensive data research effort at
various agencies and organizations mentioned previously in these report
A large portion of these data are out dated and did not contain the
pertinent information required for our study Much of the existing data
had to be interpreted and extrapolated Additionally most of the usable
data were from areas where recent construction or designs for future
construction have been made In the older developed areas and slightly
developed or undeveloped areas the existing information was minimal or
non existent Except a brief field reconnaissance trip no field
exploration was performed to identify subsoils or surface topographic
features in areas with sparse or absent data or to verify the accuracy of
the existing data Generally the spatial distribution and density of the
available data in relation to the areal extent of study area indicate that
although the existing data may be sufficient for preliminary assessments
more site specific data are required for individual project design

Design Earthquake
A site specific ground response analysis involves the determination

Design Earthquake Level
Based on historical earthquake data and empirical relationships
developed between the length of fault and the maximum possible
earthquake generated a statistical analysis can be performed to
determine the risk probabilities for the occurrence of various sizes
of earthquakes within a chosen distance from the site and their
attenuation to the site From this analysis a specific size of
earthquake with a corresponding risk level e g 20 probability of
exceedance in 50 years may be selected The design earthquake can
be represented by three major parameters which include the
magnitude intensity maximum ground acceleration and predominant
period
According to the proposed NAVFAC P 355 1 Seismic Design for
Essential Buildings in draft Reference 208 the seismic design for
construction of new facilities located in Seismic Zones 3 and 4

Liquefaction Potential
When a loose saturated sand deposit is subject to vibration such as
during an earthquake the soil tends to densify and decrease in volume
causing an increase in pore water pressure If drainage is unable to
occur rapidly enough due to the large extent of the sand deposit to
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1924 00 A Page 27
Dk No 64
dissipate the build up in pore pressure the effective stress internal
strength of the soil is reduced Under sustained vibration the pore
water pressure build up could equal the effective overburden pressure
essentially reducing the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to act
as a viscous fluid This phenomenon is generally called soil
liquefaction The major factors affecting the liquefaction
characteristics of a soil are
1 Grain Size Distribution
Fine and uniform sands are generally more prone to liquefaction
than coarse or well graded sands The presence of abundant silts
and clays may increase the liquefaction resistance of sands
2 Initial Relative Density
Loose sands are most susceptible to liquefaction Liquefaction
potential generally decreases with increases in relative density
3 Magnitude and Duration of Vibration
Liquefaction potential generally increases with increases in the
magnitude and duration of the earthquake
The existing data indicate that the lowland areas of Subic Bay are
underlain predominantly by loose to medium density saturated sand and
gravel The thickness of this layer varies from about 10 to 15 feet
inland near the hillsides and increases up to 140 feet near the existing
shoreline areas such as Rivera Point

Groundwater Conditions
High groundwater conditions would cause a decrease in slope
stability due to saturation of the soils which could reduce the
strength parameters of the soils and also in the effective
stress in the soil mass Additionally seepage forces would
increase the driving force resulting in a decrease in the slope
stability
The importance of the groundwater conditions is evidenced by the
fact that almost all of the landslides occur during or following
heavy rains which are prevalent during the annual monsoon
season To compound this problem the saprolite soils have a
relatively porous structure caused by relic joints from the
original rock mass dessication cracks and root tubes which
allow rapid infiltration of the surface water
The water migrates vertically through the saprolite zone until it
encounters a less permeable underlying unit such as the rock
formation This creates a perched ground water table in the
saprolite Continued rainfall will cause the rise of the perched
water table and a corresponding increase in piezometric head and
pore pressures thereby decreasing the shear strength of the
soils
During periods of high precipitation annual monsoon we believe
the groundwater levels could rise temporarily due to the large
infiltration of water and presence of some impermeable layers
below This is particularly true in the natural drainageway

Definition of Ground Response Spectra
Because of its geometry and composition each structure possesses
unique values of fundamental period and damping factor If we
consider a group of different structures with different fundamental
periods and damping factors excited by the same ground motion during
an earthquake the combined influence of the amplitudes of ground
acceleration and their frequency components will create different
responses in terms of accelerations velocities displacements
stresses strains bending moments shears and other parameters on
each of these structures Graphs showing the maximum responses
induced in the entire range of such structures i e different
fundamental periods and damping factors are called response spectra
In the present study our attention was mainly directed to the
development of the acceleration response spectra at ground surface of
the study areas

Computer Program SHAKE88
The computer program SHAKE88 computes the responses in a system of
homogeneous visco elastic and horizontal layers subjected to
vertically travelling shear waves The program incorporates the
latest average non linear relationships between the normalized
dynamic shear moduli and damping ratios of various soils as functions
of shear strain Thus by inputting an initial estimate of the
dynamic shear moduli of the soils the program can determine the
dynamic properties of the soils by using these functions with a
sufficient degree of accuracy
The program may be used to compute the responses anywhere in the
system from a selected design motion given also anywhere in the
system In the current study the selected ground motion of the
design earthquake was assigned at the outcropping rock of the project
site and the responses are computed for the existing grades of the
study areas

The liquefaction analyses for the current study were performed by
using Seed and Idriss s Simplified Method illustrated on Plates B 1
through B 3 At this stage a more elaborate analytical procedure is
deemed unjustified based on the limited amount of data currently
available
The analyses were performed by assuming an idealized homogeneous
sandy subsoil profile with groundwater depths ranging from 5 to 10 feet
below the ground surface The induced cyclic shear stress ratios at
different depths of the idealized soil profile under different groundwater
conditions were calculated by using the simplified method for the design
earthquake with a maximum ground acceleration of 0 28g and a magnitude of
6 5 Based on these ratios and the published relationships between the
cyclic stress ratios and the critical N for both clean
sands and silty sands Plates B 4 and B 5 the critical N q
blowcounts at various depths of the idealized soil profile under different
groundwater conditions were determined Conceptually speaking these
critical blowcounts are the minimum blowcounts resistance that the sandy
subsoils should possess at that depth in order to resist liquefaction
during the design earthquake
These critical blowcounts for different groundwater conditions were
then compared with the existing boring data to estimate the liquefaction
potential and if present to determine the thickness of the liquefaction
zones in various areas of the base
Based on these results areas with similar thickness of liquefaction
zones were grouped together and the approximate boundaries were delineated
to illustrate their general distribution To obtain a preliminary
seismic liquefaction settlement estimate for the current study the
thicknesses of the liquefaction zones were converted to the equivalent
liquefaction settlements based on an average estimate of 2 5 volumetric
strain for sandy subsoils with Nj 6q in the range of about 5 to 15
blows per foot Plate B 6
These estimated liquefaction settlements and their distribution are
presented on the Geological Hazards Map 3 Liquefaction Hazards Map
Plate 5 Their spatial relation with the generalized subsoil conditions
is illustrated on Plate 24

Slope Stability Analysis

The slope stability analyses for the seismic safety evaluation were
performed by computing both the static and pseudo static stability of the
idealized slope conditions The results of the static stability
calculations may aid in identifying marginally safe slopes under static
conditions and the results of the pseudo static stability calculations may
be used to identify potential landslide areas under seismic forces
The stability analyses were performed on a circular slip tangent toa
weakness plane through or near the toe of a slope which may represent the
typical condition of a steep hillside slip
Three 3 types of soil conditions were assumed for the stability
analyses including soil like saprolite rock like saprolite and Lahar
which represent a range of in situ geologic materials with poor to more
competent engineering properties The respective soil parameters used in
the analyses are presented on the corresponding charts
A seismic coefficient of K 0 18g was used to evaluate the seismic
safety of the slope conditions The coefficient is approxmiately equal to
65 of the anticipated peak acceleration of 0 28g of the design earthquake
based on the recommendation of Seed et al for determining the avarage
acceleration of an earthquake motion
The circular slip analyses were performed by using a computer program
STABRG originally developed by the Department of Civil Engineering at
the University of California at Berkeley The program employs the
Modified Bishop method for the circular slip evaluation with without
groundwater and or under seismic conditions
The resulted slope stability charts proposed for use are presented on

The relationship between magnitude of earthquake distance from
causative fault and the predominant periods for maximum acceleration
in rock as shown on Plate D 5 is available for earthquakes
occurring in the Western United States Reference 221 Sucha
relationship however is not available for Taiwan Japanese or
Philippine earthquakes Since these Asian earthquakes are typically
of the subduction type the relationship is anticipated to be
different from western United States earthquakes which are generated
by a strike slip fault zone between two plates
Therefore in this present study an attempt was made to evaluate a
similar relationship for the available Asian earthquake records
The predominant period for maximum acceleration in rock of a recorded
earthquake motion can be determined by performing a Fourier Spectrum
Analysis and a Response Spectrum Analysis on the recorded
acceleration time history data
The Taiwan earthquake strong motion data were recorded by a
seismograph station E 02 on a rock site As a result direct
Fourier and response spectrum analyses of the recorded motion could
be performed to determine the predominant periods of these collected
earthquake data Both types of spectrum analysis were performed by
using SHAKES88
The Fourier Spectrum analyses were performed by means of Fourier
transformation of the acceleration time histories and plotting of the
resulting Fourier amplitude spectra Usually an outstanding sharp
peak among the other peaks could be observed from the computed
Fourier Spectrum and the frequency at which the sharp peak occurs
would be used to calculate the fundamental period of the motion
which is equal to the inverse of the observed frequency If such an
outstanding sharp peak did not exist in the Fourier spectrum the
predominant period of the motion would then be determined from the
computed acceleration response spectrum of the acceleration time
history Accordingly the fundamental period corresponding to the
highest peak of the response spectrum was taken as the predominant
period of the motion

The calculated predominant periods for ten 10 selected Taiwan
strong motion records from the same rock site E 02 were plotted as
shown on Plate D 6
This spectrum analysis approach could not be used directly for the
collected Japanese earthquake records since nearly all of the records
were from soil sites and only two of the records from rock sites
Instead the spectrum analyses were performed with the subsoil
conditions at the recording stations which were approximately known
for the selected Japanese earthquakes used for this project Based
on the corresponding subsoil profile a recorded motion was
deconvolved using SHAKE88 to the rock outcrop and both the Fourier
and response spectrum of the rock outcrop motion were plotted The
frequency at which the highest Fourier amplitude occurred or the
fundamental period of the peak in the spectral acceleration response
was then taken as the predominant period for maximum acceleration in
rock of the earthquake record
Generally it is our opinion that this approximate procedure is
applicable for earthquake motions recorded on relatively thin and
stiff dense soil sites and the response spectrum of the rock outcrop
will usually show a distinct and easily identified peak However
for motions recorded on relatively deep and soft loose soil sites
the frequency components of the deconvolved motions may involve
considerable error Therefore only Japanese earthquake records from
thin stiff dense soil sites were used for analyses in this
evaluation

strong motion records from various soil and rock sites were plotted
