INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Hospital Road James P Turner Drive and Intersection
Improvements project in the area of Faga alu on the island of Tutuila American Samoa
The general location of the project site is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical engineering recom
mendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses The recommendations presented herein are intended for design input and
may not be appropriate to form the basis for preparation of final construction cost
estimates nor for construction bidding purposes

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Tutuila was built during the Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epoch
by the extrusion of basaltic lavas and pyroclastic materials from five volcanoes
Stearns 1944 Following the cessation of the main shield building activity there was
a long period of erosion accompanied by the growth of fringing coral reefs During the
late Quaternary volcanism was renewed by eruptions of lava and pyroclastic materials
at several vents on the south side of the island forming the Tafuna Leone Plain
The main mountain mass of Tutuila was divided into seven formations Stearns
1944 The rocks are thought to be of Pliocene Epoch consisting of thinly bedded to
massive flows of basalt andesitic basalt and andesite interbedded with cinder cones
and tuff beds with thrachytic dikes plugs and crater fills The rock units were erupted
from as many as five eruptive centers but the principal volcano was near the middle
of the island Pago Pago Harbor is believed to occupy part of the collapsed caldera of
that principal volcano Erosion along a major fault along the caldera wall formed the
near vertical cliff on the northeast side of the harbor
Prior to the eruption of the Leone Volcanics beach and lagoonal deposits
accumulated on and behind a submerged barrier reef at the present site of the Leone
Plain These deposits were later buried under several hundred feet of volcanic
materials These older beach and lagoonal deposits consist partially of sand and
coralline gravels
Three tuff cones and a thick sequence of lava flow form the plain between
Tafuna and Leone The rock is thought to be of Holocene Epoch possibly only a few
thousand years old as evidenced by geologic relationships in the area The lava flows
are mainly thinly bedded basalts with interbeds of tuff and breccia

located close to the ocean by alluvial processes In some valleys the alluvial deposits

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicates that fill materials consisting generally of sandy
soils with some clayey materials were encountered at the proposed project site below
the existing asphalt concrete pavement to depths of approximately 3 to 11 feet below
the ground surface The near surface fill materials are underlain by soft clayey deposits
and loose sandy soils with gravels extending to the maximum depth explored of
approximately 45 feet below the existing ground surface Shallow groundwater
conditions with depths ranging from about 3 to 8 feet below the ground surface were
encountered in the borings
Based on our field exploration and site reconnaissance the existing asphalt
pavement of Hospital Road has undergone significant distress with deterioration of the
surface pavements The serviceability of Hospital Road is currently poor to
unsatisfactory We believe that one of the most significant causes of the observed
pavement deterioration is a result of poor surface and subsurface drainage conditions
at the site In addition poor subgrade soil conditions including soft clayey deposits
and loose sandy soils have contributed to the pavement deterioration observed The
soft and loose subsoil conditions have also resulted in global settlement of the area and
potential lateral movements of the ground which could be a significant cause of the
observed pavement distress The lack of a proper base course layer below the asphalt
concrete pavement and the heavy truck traffic from the quarry using the road have also
contributed to the distress of the pavement
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3230 00 Page 10
Based on our observations at the site the surface and subsurface drainage
conditions of the roadway are poor as evidenced by ponding of rain water in depressed
areas where the pavement had settled The surface drainage conditions appear to be
poor as a result of the undulating pavement surface probably due to global ground
settlements and or lateral movements As a result we recommend that the existing
pavement surface be regraded to provide positive drainage and drainage ditches
should be constructed along the north side of the roadway to intercept runoff water from
upslope
As a result of the poor surface drainage water may be introduced into the
pavement section and subgrade from cracks and potholes in the pavement surface and
from seepage into the subgrade from the adjacent slopes In addition shallow
groundwater conditions were observed in the borings Water in the subgrade soils may
cause softening of the clayey subgrade soils which can result in pavement distress and
subsequent failure Therefore we recommend that the existing roadway pavements
be removed and be replaced with new pavements designed in accordance with the
recommendations presented herein
Because of the poor subsoil conditions and the relatively shallow groundwater
levels over excavation of the poor subsoils and replacement with compacted fill to
provide a firm base on which to construct asphalt concrete pavements would be
difficult In addition we understand that this road will be subjected to heavy traffic
loading Therefore we recommend that Portland Cement Concrete PCC pavements
be used for reconstruction of Hospital Road Use of PCC pavements would not require
significant over excavation of the soft subgrade soils to construct the rigid pavements
For the intersection of Hospital Road and the coastline highway asphalt concrete
pavements may be used for the reconstruction to match the existing asphalt concrete
pavement for the coastline highway

Pavement Drainage System
One of the primary distress mechanisms observed in pavement structures is
pumping due to saturation of the base course and or subgrade soils In addition free
water voids in the pavement section repeated heavy wheel loads and an erodible
base can also cause pumping
In general the primary source of free water in the pavement section is surface
water infiltration through cracks and joints in the pavement A major source of
infiltrated moisture is the longitudinal pavement shoulder joint Water also enters the
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3230 00 Page 15
pavement section through seepage from adjacent slopes To reduce moisture
infiltration into the pavement structure all pavement joints and cracks should be sealed
and pavement drainage systems should be installed to collect and divert free water
from the pavement section Therefore considering the high rainfall in this area we
recommend a subsurface drainage system be provided on the sides of the roadway to
divert subsurface water The pavement drainage system recommended consists of
three elements
1 A permeable base to provide rapid drainage of free water that may enter
the pavement structure
2 A longitudinal edge drain collector system to convey accumulated water
from the permeable base to appropriate outlet structures
3 A filter separator layer to reduce the potential for migration of fines into
the permeable base from the subgrade subbase or shoulder base
material
Permeable Base
A properly designed and constructed permeable base can rapidly drain water
that infiltrates the pavement structure and will not allow high water pressures to
build up beneath the pavement Therefore we recommend the use of a
permeable base for support of the pavements in lieu of the normal aggregate
base course The permeable base material would be more free draining for the
anticipated wet conditions As recommended in the Pavement Design section
we recommend 6 inch and 8 inch thick permeable base layers for the rigid and
flexible pavement sections respectively Where the pavements will be subjected
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3230 00 Page 16
to flooding conditions such as at stream crossings we recommend that the
thickness of the permeable base material be increased to a minimum of
12 inches
Due to the different thickness of the surface course for the new flexible and rigid
pavements where both new flexible and rigid pavements are used we
recommend that a transition section be provided between the flexible and rigid
pavement sections to allow better water flow within the pavement drainage
system A typical pavement transition section is illustrated on Plate 4
Two types of permeable base treated or untreated may be used for the new
pavements planned for this project depending on the site conditions Treated
permeable base is a highly permeable base course stabilized by either asphalt
or cement which would be known as asphalt treated permeable base ATPB
or cement treated permeable base CTPB In general treated permeable base
is recommended when there is lack of confinement for the pavement structure
Where there is confinement for the pavement section untreated permeable base
may be used For this project we understand that the pavement section
generally would be confined on both sides of the roadway by curbs therefore
an untreated permeable base may be used in the pavement section
Permeable base material should consist of a hard durable crushed angular
basalt aggregate with essentially no fines A permeable base consisting of
crushed basalt aggregate meeting the gradation requirements presented in
Appendix C of this report should provide sufficient stability on which construction
equipment can operate as well as provide good support for the pavement
surface course The permeable base material gradation should have good
aggregate interlock The material for the permeable base should be hard and

durable to prevent the aggregate from degrading and generating fines during
construction
To prevent rutting and displacing the permeable base during construction
rubber tired equipment should not be allowed on the permeable base Instead

If the working area is restricted and construction equipment must travel on the

Filter Layer
ms eo
The function of a filter is to provide both drainage and filtration by allowing water
to pass through drainage with minimal head loss while retaining soil particles
filtration The major criteria considered for a geotextile drainage and filtration
application include 1 soil retention piping resistance 2 permeability
3 clogging potential 4 chemical composition requirements considerations and
5 constructability and survivability requirements
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3230 00 Page 19
For a geotextile to perform effectively as a filter in a geotextile drainage system
it must remain free draining by having opening characteristics compatible with
the surrounding soils The choice of a correct geotextile is critical to the
formation of a stable and effective soil filter Unless proper fabric piping
resistance clogging resistance and constructability strength requirements are
obtained it is doubtful that the desired results will be obtained Construction
installation and monitoring must also be provided to ensure that the materials
have been installed properly
Therefore we recommend that a layer of geotextile fabric such as Mirafi 180N
or equivalent be provided between the upper permeable base and the
underlying subgrade material for the rigid pavement section to reduce the
potential for migration of fines into the permeable base from the underlying
subgrade or shoulder base material Generally non woven needle punched
geotextiles are better for pavement drainage applications than heat bonded
geotextiles Woven or slit film geotextiles should not be used The geotextile
fabric used should conform to the specifications as presented in Appendix C
For the flexible pavement section the aggregate subbase course layer see
Appendix C for gradation requirements would serve as a filter for the permeable
base therefore a geotextile fabric would not be required for the flexible
pavement section

of subgrade materials Therefore special subgrade preparation procedures
should be followed to provide a more uniform subgrade for support of the
proposed pavement structure
Subgrade Preparation
For rigid pavements recommended along Hospital Road between Stations 10 00
and 23 00 the subgrade should be proof rolled with a smooth drum roller
10 ton minimum a minimum of six passes If pumping subgrade conditions are
encountered during the proof rolling the pumping subgrade materials should be
removed to a maximum depth of 18 inches A filter fabric such as Mirafi 180N
should be placed over the resulting over excavation and backfilled with No 3B
Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation The top of the No 3B Fine gravel
should be proof rolled prior to placement of the geotextile filter fabric below the
permeable base course layer The geotextile fabric should have a minimum
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3230 00 Page 22
overlap of 2 feet and should extend at least 2 feet beyond the edge of the
pavement
To reduce the potential for subgrade pumping heavy or rubber tired equipment
are not recommended In addition vibratory action of the compaction equipment
should not be used to reduce potential pumping of the subgrade
For flexible pavements recommended at the intersection the exposed subgrade
should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches properly moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the
in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation
D 1557 91 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
If off site select material is required the imported fill material should be tested
and approved by Geolabs Hawaii prior to delivery to the site to confirm that it is
suitable for its intended purpose The requirement for imported fill material is
presented in the following subsection Detailed requirements for the permeable
base course material have been discussed in the Permeable Base subsection
of this report
Fills and Backfills
In general the on site granular soils may be used as fill and backfill material if
over sized particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension are screened
The on site soft and wet clayey soils should not be used as fill or backfill

material because we anticipate significant difficulties in achieving the desired
compaction Granular fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction
Where needed select fill material should be well graded from coarse to fine with
no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should have
a laboratory CBR value of 15 or more with a maximum swell of less than
1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 In
addition the fill material should contain a maximum of 20 percent particles

All permanent fill slopes should have inclinations of two horizontal to one vertical
2H 1V or flatter All fills placed on slopes with a 5H 1V slope inclination or
steeper should be keyed and benched Fill slopes should be constructed by

slope to provide adequate compaction at the edges of the slope

Wall Foundations
We believe that the planned CRM walls may be supported on shallow
continuous footings bearing on the medium dense to dense sandy soils
encountered at the project site An allowable bearing pressure of 2 500 pounds
per square foot p s f may be used for design of footings bearing on the stiff
and or dense soils The bearing value above is for dead plus live loads and may
be increased by one third s when considering transient loads such as wind or
seismic forces If the footings are designed in accordance with our recommen
dations total settlement of wall footings is estimated to be on the order of 1 inch
Differential settlements along the wall face generally should be less than about
1 inch in 20 feet of wall
Wall foundations should be embedded at least 2 feet below the lowest adjacent
finished grade If soft or loose materials are exposed at the footing subgrade
the soft or loose materials should be over excavated to expose firm material and
the over excavation should be backfilled with engineered fill compacted to at
least 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place
dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the
same soil determined in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Where footings are located adjacent to slopes or on slopes the footings should
be embedded to a depth to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of at
least 8 feet measured from the edge of the footing to the face of the slope
Where footings are to be located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities
the footings should extend to a depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane
projected upward from the bottom of the below grade structure or utility This
requirement is necessary to reduce the potential for surcharging adjacent below

embedded portion of footings A coefficient of friction of 0 30 may be used

the trench backfill from the level 1 foot above the pipes to the finish grade should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in 8 inch maximum
horizontal loose lifts and mechanically compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction since we anticipate the utility trenches to be below the roadway pavement

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The results of
these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
A one inch ring swell test was performed on a selected sample to evaluate the
swelling potential of the subsoils The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
Three Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected samples of the soils to
evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification Results of these
tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths and on
Plates B 2 and B 3 Graphic presentation of the test results is shown on Plate B 5
Four sieve analyses were performed on selected samples of the soils to evaluate
the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification Test results
are summarized on Plates B 2 and B 3 Graphic presentation of the grain size
distribution is presented on Plates B 6 and B 7
Two California Bearing Ratio CBR tests were performed on the bulk samples
to evaluate pavement support characteristics The test results are summarized on
Plate B 3
Two compaction tests ASTM D 1557 91 were performed on bulk samples of
the soils to evaluate the moisture and dry density relationship The maximum dry
density and optimum moisture content are summarized on Plate B 4
One consolidation test with time rates was performed on the potentially
compressible soil to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the materials
encountered Results of the consolidation test are presented on Plate B 8
