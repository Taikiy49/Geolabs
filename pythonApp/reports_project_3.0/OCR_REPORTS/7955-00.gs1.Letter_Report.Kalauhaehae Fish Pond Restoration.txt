PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located at 5841 Kalanianole Highway in the Honolulu area on 
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. We understand it is desired to attempt to locate the artesian 
water source on the mauka side of Kalanianaole Highway that fed fresh water to the pond 
at the 5841 Kalanianaole Highway residence prior to the widening of Kalanianaole 
Highway. We understand there is belief that the widening work may have cut off the 
supply of artesian water to the pond. 
We understand that it is desired to attempt to try and determine the location of the 
artesian water source on the mauka side of Kalanianaole Highway.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
We explored the subsurface conditions on the mauka side of Kalanianaole 
Highway by drilling and sampling seven borings, extending to depths of about 15 to 
15.5 feet below the existing ground surface. It should be noted that Boring Nos. 4 and 7 
were not sampled due to time constraints; the borings were primarily drilled to obtain 
groundwater level measurements. In general, the project site on the mauka side of the 
highway is generally underlain by a relatively thin layer of surface fill, consisting of stiff to 
very stiff sandy clay, measuring approximately 1.5 to 3.5 feet in thickness. Below the 
surface fill, a colluvial deposit was encountered to about 2.5 to 4.5 feet in depth.  The 
colluvium consisted of very stiff to hard sandy clay and clayey silt, and boulders. The 
colluvium was underlain by medium hard to hard highly weathered basalt formation 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 15.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  
It should be noted that Boring No. 6 (located near the western limit of the project 
boundary) encountered fill material, consisting of stiff to very stiff sandy clay and medium

PILOT TUBE MICROTUNNELING 
Based on the information provided, we understand that a trenchless utility 
installation method is desired in lieu of an open-trench (cut-and-cover) method for the 
Kalanianaole Highway crossing. Pilot tube microtunneling (PTMT) (a.k.a. guided auger 
boring) employs the pipe jacking and guidance system used for microtunneling, the 
steering method used for horizontal directional drilling, and the cuttings removal system 
used in auger boring for the installation of pipes up to 30 inches in diameter. This method 
of pipe installation is capable of installing the pipe to the current standard of line and grade 
requirements for gravity sewer lines. The maximum drive length using this method is 
about 400 feet and will depend on the ground conditions and PTMT operator’s skill. 
The PTMT method typically consists of drilling a pilot hole about 4 inches in 
diameter. A target with electric light-emitting diodes is mounted in the steering head and 
the head position is monitored using a camera-mounted theodolite, or laser, and TV 
monitor. The slanted surface of the drill head is used for steering. After completion of the 
pilot hole, the borehole is enlarged by a reamer and auger casing with augers.  
Anticipated Tunneling Conditions 
Evaluation of the subsurface conditions is important in assessing the viability and 
feasibility of PTMT techniques and in determining appropriate construction techniques. For 
the design and cost estimation pertaining to PTMT, special attention should be given to the 
characteristics of the soils that are likely to be encountered. Some of the important factors 
relating to the subsurface soil conditions that may significantly influence construction costs 
include face or heading stability, settlement potential and magnitudes, allowable passive 
pressures for providing thrust block resistance, groundwater control, muck characteristics, 
and/or special disposal requirements. 
The proposed gravity water line alignment was not available at the time this report 
was prepared. Based on the possible artesian water head measurements near the western 
portion of the project site, we envision that the new water line would begin in this area. We 
also envision that the gravity sewer line would daylight at the northern edge of the 
Kalauhaehae Fish Pond. Based on our field exploration, the mauka portion of the water line 
will likely traverse fill material and/or weathered basalt formation. The fill material consisted 
of stiff to very stiff sandy clay and medium dense silty gravel, and the weathered basalt 
formation graded from medium hard to hard. Based on our review of in-house geologic 
information, the makai portion of the water line may traverse coral and/or reef deposits 
and basalt formation with depth. The coralline reef deposits may consist of granular soils 
of varying relative density and cementation, and the basalt formation may vary in 
weathering and hardness.

Potential Tunneling Difficulties 
An assessment of the ground conditions likely to be encountered along the PTMT 
excavations is important for evaluating the face stability of the excavations and to provide 
information on the selection of appropriate equipment and methods for the tunnel and shaft 
construction. The ground conditions should be assessed to reduce the potential for loss of 
ground, surface settlement, and damage to existing utilities, buildings, roadways, and other 
improvements as a result of the planned tunneling operations. 
We anticipate that numerous existing underground utilities are present near the 
crossing along Kalanianaole Highway. Therefore, the new water line alignment should be 
designed to avoid the existing underground utility lines. Due to the relatively high 
groundwater levels measured, the presence of groundwater and the effect on the tunneling 
operation needs to be considered. 
Due to the potential for surface settlement to occur during the tunneling, we 
recommend that the ground surface along the new water line alignment be surveyed and 
monitored during the tunneling work. 
THRUST BLOCK RESISTANCE FOR JACKING PITS 
The thrust block at the jacking pit provides the reaction for the main jacks when 
pushing the pipe forward. The allowable passive pressure resistance of the soil, which is 
a function of the strength of the soil, will control the thrust resistance that can be 
developed. It seems reasonable to allow the thrust block to develop the full passive 
pressure resistance because this is a temporary construction loading condition.  However, 
it must be recognized that relatively large strains will be required in order to develop the 
maximum passive pressure resistance. At the jacking loads that impose stresses 
approaching the maximum passive pressure resistance, there may be large movements 
of the thrust block in response to the strains required to develop full passive pressure 
resistance. 
In addition, large movements of the thrust block are not desirable for pipe jacking 
because the movements are not uniform, and it becomes difficult to maintain the thrust 
block perpendicular to the pipe alignment. If the thrust block does not remain square, the 
force applied to the pipe may not be uniform; resulting in eccentric loading that could 
damage the pipe.  
Movements of the thrust block can also be controlled by limiting the allowable 
passive pressure resistance to about one-half of the maximum passive pressure 
resistance. This corresponds to a much lower level of strain and smaller soil movements 
(strain compatible design). If the project requires higher jacking forces than can be safely 
transferred to the soil mass behind the thrust block, then the soil mass should be improved 
or stabilized.

ARTESIAN WATER COLLECTION BOX 
We understand that the artesian water collection box on the mauka side of 
Kalanianaole Highway will consist of a manhole structure. Based on the existing 
topography and our field exploration, we envision that the top of the manhole structure 
will be at the existing ground surface (Elevations of about +8.5 to +9 feet MSL) and the 
bottom of the manhole structure will be set at about ±0 feet MSL. We anticipate that the 
manhole structure will be constructed in the fill material (stiff to very stiff sandy clay and 
medium dense silty gravel) and the highly weathered basalt rock formation. 
Manhole Structures 
In general, we recommend the bottom of the manhole structure bear on 6 inches of 
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) over the stiff to very stiff and medium 
dense fill or the weathered basalt rock formation encountered in our drilled borings. Based 
on our engineering analyses, we believe that the net increase in bearing pressure on the 
underlying soil deposits/weathered rock formation resulting from the manhole construction 
would be minimal. Settlements on the order of less than 1 inch are anticipated for the 
manhole structures. 
The lateral earth pressures acting on the proposed underground manhole structure 
will depend on the type of backfill used, the extent of backfill, and the compactive effort on 
the backfill material around the structure. We recommend designing the new manhole 
structures to resist the following lateral earth pressures (at-rest conditions) from the adjacent 
soils.

Backfilling of Manhole Excavations 
The manhole excavations will need to be properly backfilled. The excavated 
on-site fill material may be used as backfill above the cushion layer up to the finish grades. 
The backfill materials should have a maximum particle size of 3 inches. Some processing 
of the excavated material may be required to meet the 3-inch size requirement. 
The soil backfill above the No. 3B Fine gravel should be placed in thin lifts (normally 
8 inches in loose lift thickness) with each lift mechanically compacted to a minimum of 90 
percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in 
accordance with ASTM D1557. 
Should the manhole excavation to be backfilled be too small for a compactor or if 
groundwater is encountered, the excavation should be backfilled with lean concrete or 
Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM).  The CLSM should not be placed underwater. 
DEWATERING 
Dewatering of excavations will be necessary where the existing groundwater level 
is above the bottom of the proposed excavation.  Groundwater was encountered at 
depths ranging between about 3.2 to 6.3 feet below the existing ground surface in the 
borings drilled during our field exploration.  Based on the groundwater levels encountered, 
dewatering provisions will need to be included in the contract documents for the proposed 
construction.  Since the excavation may involve discharge of groundwater, a National 
Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permit may be required for this 
discharge.  The contractor should consult their independent consultant for the latest 
regulations and information pertaining to the permit application. 
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor.  The selection of 
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor, and he/she 
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during 
construction depending on the conditions encountered.  The dewatering method selected 
should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the proposed 
excavation.  The dewatering operations should be coordinated with the shoring support 
such that the stability of the excavations is not jeopardized.  The operations should be 
carried-out without softening the bottom of the excavations.
