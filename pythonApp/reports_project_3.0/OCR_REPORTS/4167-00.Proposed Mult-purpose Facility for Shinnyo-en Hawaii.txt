INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Multi Purpose Facility project for Shinnyo En Hawaii in the
Moiliili area on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses of the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located on the south side of the intersection of Isenberg and
Coyne Streets in the Moiliili area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii Based on the
information provided it is desired to expand the existing two story Shinnyo En Hawaii
Facility with the construction of a multi purpose facility on the north side of the church
premises The proposed expansion will comprise of a two story building with a basement
level for storage and future parking The remaining floor plans will consist of a
multi purpose room offices meeting rooms an instrument practice room and covered
lanais An overhead walkway bridge will be constructed to connect the new and existing
facilities on the second floor In addition a parking area with glassblock or glasscrete
paving is also planned to serve the new facility
Based on the information provided the new building will be of concrete walls and
slab on grade construction at the basement level The superstructure will be of CMU wall
construction with concrete topping on hollow core plank flooring Roofing for the building
will consist of steel framing with Monier Tile Based on the information provided by the
project structural engineer maximum column loads on the order of about 230 kips and
wall loads up to about 9 kips per lineal foot are anticipated for the proposed building

Based on the information provided we understand that the finish floor elevations

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located on the south side of the intersection of Isenberg and
Coyne Streets in the Moiliili area of Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The project
site is generally bounded by Coyne Street to the north Isenberg Street to the east the
existing Shinnyo En Hawaii building to the south and a single family dwelling to the west
Based on the information provided the project site is a corner parcel
approximately rectangular in shape comprised of three smaller lots with a total land area
of approximately 14 600 square feet Our field observations indicated that each lot is
currently occupied by a single family dwelling with concrete driveway and yard areas
Based on the Topographic Survey Map of the site the project site is relatively level with
existing ground surface elevations between about 11 and 12 feet MSL

Walkway Bridge
We understand that a walkway bridge connecting the existing and new facilities is
planned on the second floor Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the site we
believe that spread footings may be utilized to support the proposed overhead walkway
bridge In general we believe that walkway bridge foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the Building Foundations section of
this report Due to the high bending moments anticipated at the column bases we
recommend that the footings be embedded a minimum of 2 feet into the dense coral
material below the surficial topsoil layer To develop the required passive soil pressure
we recommend that the footing concrete be placed directly against the excavated dense
coral surfaces
Slabs On Grade
In addition to the basement level of the proposed new facility we anticipate that
concrete slabs on grade will also be required for the restroom and covered lanai areas of
the new facility The surficial topsoil layer encountered within slab on grade footprints
near the existing ground surface should be removed to expose the underlying dense coral
material during site preparation This serves to reduce the potential for future distress to
the slabs on grade resulting from the shrinking and swelling of the surficial clayey soils
due to moisture fluctuations
In general we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick cushion layer consisting of
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation or basaitic termite barrier BTB be
placed below the slabs to provide more uniform support of the slabs and to serve as a

provide protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Flexible
floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better

Where the concrete slabs on grade will be subjected to future vehicular traffic

Drainage
The basement walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic
pressures A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of
permeable material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of the
wall discharging to an appropriate outlet As an alternative a prefabricated
drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the
drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically
connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall A sump may be required to
drain the water collected in the subdrainage system
It should be noted that high shallow depth groundwater was encountered in one
of the borings indicating that there may be seepage or perched groundwater
conditions in the subsurface Therefore proper drainage and waterproofing of the
basement walls should be incorporated into the design and construction of this
project
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of the on site granular
soils or select granular fill material Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper
12 inches of backfill should consist of low expansion impervious soils to reduce
the potential for water infiltration behind the walls

Excavation Support
Our field exploration indicates that the site is generally underlain by dense to very
dense coral sand and gravel material extending to the maximum depth explored of
about 21 feet below the existing ground surface In general we believe that the
in situ dense coral may be able to stand unsupported at relatively steep angles
for temporary construction purposes However it should be noted that the quality
of the coral deposits could be highly variable Therefore where an open cut
excavation is not possible because of physical limitations or the presence of poor
quality coral deposits temporary shoring of the excavation may be required to
reduce the potential for adjacent ground movement The excavation support and
shoring system used must comply with all applicable safety requirements

Construction Precaution and Monitoring
The selected excavation shoring system if needed must comply with all
applicable federal state and local safety requirements The adequacy and safety
of the shoring installation should be made the sole responsibility of the contractor
His her representative who should be required to be continuously present on site
during excavation and construction work will have the best opportunity to promptly
observe changing conditions during construction such as unforeseen subsoils
unexpectedly high groundwater table inappropriate construction sequence or
techniques etc which may affect shoring stability
However it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and
the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during
excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to accurately
estimate the magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon
workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports
are installed on the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of
excavation
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical if necessary and that the adjacent ground
should be continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of
movements with instruments Such monitoring is particularly critical in areas
adjacent to existing buildings and utilities It is recommended that a qualified
geotechnical engineer be retained by the contractor to design and evaluate the
shoring system proposed

is above the bottom of the proposed excavation Based on a finish floor elevation of

dewatering may be required for the project construction Since the excavation may

Dewatering Method
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and
he she should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be
required during construction depending on the conditions encountered The
dewatering method selected should have minimal impact on the groundwater level
surrounding the proposed excavation The contractor should be made aware that
the coral and granular materials underlying the site are highly permeable and
capable of transmitting large quantities of water
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents
should be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural
groundwater table and or to exclude the water from the excavations to allow
construction of the proposed structures under safe and dry conditions These
works or systems may include but are not limited to grouting cut off walls tremie
concrete plugs or any combination of the above and or other possible methods
Therefore we recommend that provisions be made for localized dewatering
Dewatering Considerations
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of a
suitable method of dewatering
1 The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or damage to
existing buildings roads and environment
2 The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide safe
and dry working conditions in the excavation

3 The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modifications
to accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to
dewatering It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained
by the contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His her qualified representative who should be
required to be continuously present on site during dewatering activities will have
the best opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during
construction and to implement as soon as possible necessary precautionary or
remedial measures including but not limited to stopping or slowing down the
dewatering operations
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils
may be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations
should be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the
excavation bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be
performed without pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and should
be coordinated with shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not
adversely affected Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in a
blowing or heaving of the excavation bottom or sides
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches
connected to excavations If this occurs the granular bedding and or backfill in
the utility trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater
drawdown resulting in settlements and potential damage to utility lines and or
other existing adjacent structures

Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines will be required to serve the new facility It should
be noted that groundwater was encountered at depths of about 6 3 to 10 3 feet below the
existing ground surface Therefore trench excavations extending deeper than about
5 feet below the existing ground surface depending on location and surface elevation
may encounter the groundwater level
We envision that new utility connections may be required for the project A
granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as
No 3B Fine gravel should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches
above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the
compaction effort on the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes it Is
critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids
below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper backfill material and placement could result in settlement of the backfill and
damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site soils or select fill
material The backfill should be placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to
a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction

Drainage
The finished grades outside the new building should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs
should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete
bottoms and drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration Drainage swales should be
provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface runoff away from
the slab and footing foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling three
borings to depths of about 20 to 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the
approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled using a
truck mounted drill rig and portable drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight
augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler or a 3 inch OD Modified California
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
A pocket penetrometer test was performed on a selected cohesive soil sample
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Result of the pocket penetrometer
test is presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
