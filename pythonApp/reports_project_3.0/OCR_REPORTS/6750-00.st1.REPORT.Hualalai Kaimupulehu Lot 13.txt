This letter report presents the findings from our site reconnaissance to evaluate the 
existing conditions at Kaimupulehu Estates, Lot No. 13 within Hualalai Resort on the 
Island of Hawaii. The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the 
limitations noted at the end of this letter report. 
Project Considerations 
The project site is located at Lot No. 13 of the Kaimupulehu Estates (formerly 
Lot No. 6 of Parcel 13) of Hualalai Resort in the Kaupulehu area in the District of North 
Kona on the Island of Hawaii. We understand that excavation of a site retaining wall at the 
adjacent Lot No. 14 has encroached laterally onto the Lot No. 13 property. Therefore, it was 
desired to evaluate the current condition of Lot No. 13 and to provide recommendations for 
backfilling and restoration of the excavated areas. It should be noted that this letter report 
covers geotechnical engineering services for the restoration work at Lot No. 13 only. Design 
of proposed residences at Lot No. 13 or No. 14 is specifically excluded from this letter report. 
Existing Conditions 
Based on the grading plans, Lot Nos. 13 and 14 generally were graded to finished 
pad elevations of about +149.5 and +144 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL), respectively. A site 
visit was made on September 6, 2012 to evaluate the existing conditions at Lot No. 13 of 
Kaimupulehu Estates. Property pins marking the makai and mauka boundaries of 
Lot Nos. 13 and 14 were observed in the field. The makai corner property pin was located 
on the native pahoehoe lava beyond the limits of the building pads. The mauka corner 
property pin was located at the corner of the existing electrical box adjacent to the street.

Based on our observations, the excavation for the new retaining wall at Lot No. 14 
has encroached onto Lot No. 13 by about 6 feet at the mauka end of the property and up 
to about 12 feet at the makai end of the property. The excavation generally was cut to a 
near-vertical slope inclination with a height of up to about 6 feet. The materials exposed at 
the face of the excavation generally consisted of well-graded granular materials of less 
than about 6 inches in largest dimension placed during the original development of the 
lots. The surface materials at the excavated face were loose and have raveled off the face 
in many areas. Dense pahoehoe rock was exposed at the bottom of the excavated face in 
some areas.  
Discussions and Recommendations 
Based on the conditions at the site, we believe the excavated areas at Lot No. 13 
may be restored in several different ways. The subsections below discuss two options that 
may be utilized for restoration of Lot No. 13. 
Option A 
Option A involves immediate restoration of Lot No. 13. The property should be 
restored to the original condition, with a finished pad elevation of +149.5 feet MSL within 
the Lot No. 13 building pad areas and sloping down to a finished pad elevation of 
+149.5 feet MSL at the Lot No. 14 building pad limits. 
Fill materials should consist of well-graded granular materials generally less than 
6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in 
the compacted mass (structural fill). In addition, structural fill materials should contain less 
than 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve by weight. 
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in 
loose thickness and moisture-conditioned. Compaction may be accomplished by using a 
large vibratory drum roller having a static weight of at least 15 tons traveling at a speed of 
about 300 feet per minute (3.5 miles per hour). The roller should pass over the fill surface 
at least eight times. 
The excavation should be properly benched prior to placement of fills. A typical 
benching detail is provided as Plate 1. The fill slope face should be finished to a relatively 
smooth and well-compacted surface. The fill slope face should be free voids, which would 
allow erosion and migration of fines to occur. Fill slopes should be constructed by 
overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio to obtain a well-compacted slope face.

Option B 
A retaining wall is planned at the property line between Lot Nos. 13 and 14. 
Option A, as described above, would require restoration of the excavated slope followed 
by re-excavation and construction of the new retaining wall. Considering that a new 
retaining wall is planned at the property line, the restoration of Lot No. 13 may be 
conducted after the construction of the retaining wall. 
Option B consists of constructing the retaining wall first, then backfilling against the 
retaining wall and restoring Lot No. 13 to its original grades. Because of the sequence of 
work, we envision that only smaller compaction equipment would be able to operate 
behind the retaining wall. In addition, it is generally not desired to operate a large vibratory 
roller immediately behind a newly constructed retaining wall. Therefore, the maximum 
particle size of the backfills should be limited to 3 inches or less in order to facilitate 
compaction of backfill materials using smaller compaction equipment. The backfill 
materials should be well-graded from coarse to fine. In addition, the materials also may 
contain up to 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The materials should have a 
California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or higher and a swell value of 1 percent or less 
when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883. Excavated materials may be re-used as a 
source of backfill provided that the over-sized materials are screened and above 
requirements are met. 
The excavation should be properly benched prior to the placement of fills. A typical 
benching detail is provided as Plate 1. Backfill materials should be placed in level lifts not 
exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. 
Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of 
the maximum dry density as determined by ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Geolabs should be retained to provide earthwork observation and testing services 
during the restoration of Lot No. 13. The critical items of earthwork observation include 
observation of the benching, fill placement, and compaction testing. The recommendations 
provided herein are contingent upon such observations. 
In general, we believe that both Options A and B would result in a finished product 
similar to the original conditions of the lot. Considering that a site retaining wall is planned 
at the property line between the two lots, we recommend implementation of Option B. 
Limitations 
The recommendations submitted herein are based, in part, upon information 
obtained from our site reconnaissance only. Variations of the subsurface conditions may 
occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until 
construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be necessary to 
re-evaluate the recommendations provided herein.
