SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Irrigation Reservoir project located in the
Poipu area on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and
engineering analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of site
grading and other geotechnical engineering aspects of the project only The findings
and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of
this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The proposed project involves the old Manuhonuhonu Reservoir Dam located
above the proposed golf course Holes 5 and 6 in Kukui ula in the Poipu area on the
Island of Kauai Hawaii The project consists of removing and lowering a portion of the
dam and designing outlet works and spillways The remaining structure which is no
longer qualified as a dam structure will be improved to serve as a reservoir with varying
heights on an average of 6 feet for drainage detention Current design concept also
includes lowering the inside of the reservoir to maintain some capacity even as dam
embankment is lowered
Based on the topographic survey the existing dam has elevations of about
172 feet Mean Sea Level and 142 feet Mean Sea Level MSL at top and bottom
respectively The proposed finished grades are about 150 and 132 feet MSL at the top
and bottom of the reservoir respectively Cuts on the order of about 22 feet will be
required to achieve the design finished grades

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Kauai is composed of a single dissected basaltic shield volcano
built by the extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series beginning about
5to 6 million years ago The eruption of the Waimea Volcanic Series ended about
2 million years ago followed by a long period of erosion Following the cessation of
this main volcano shield building phase renewed volcanic activity about 1 million
years ago occurred with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of the post erosional Koloa
Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the thick alluvial sediments of the
Palikea Formation
Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic Series are generally characterized as
thick lava flows composed of dense basalt extruded from groups of vents aligned in
north south trends at various locales Associated with the Koloa Volcanic Series lava
flows are some deposits of pyroclastic materials volcanic ash and cinders which
usually form cinder cones surrounding the vent Rock formations of the Koloa Volcanic
Series cover most of the eastern half of the Island of Kauai including the project site
During the Pleistocene Epoch many sea level changes occurred as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the ocean level fell since less water was available to fill the
oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels rose
because more water was available The landmass of Kauai remained essentially stable
during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These
glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and
lower relative to the present sea level of the Island of Kauai
The project site is situated on ground underlain by hard basaltic rock lava flows
of the Koloa Volcanic Series In some places generally thin surficial soils consisting of
silty and clayey residual and alluvial materials may mantle the underlying rock
formation The basalt rock formation observed in the Koloa area appears to be

2 2 Site Description
The project site is located above the proposed golf course Holes 5 and 6 in
Kukui ula in Poipu on the Island of Kauai Hawaii The old Manuhonuhonu Reservoir
Dam is situated on the southern flank of an approximately 499 foot hill Manuhonuhonu
within the Kukui ula Development project
The project site is covered with short to medium tall grass of about 3 to 5 feet
high and the surface soil consists of mainly silty clay Two ridges and an embankment
bound the reservoir The bottom of the reservoir is generally flat and gently sloping
down from the north to the south We did not observe water accumulation at the bottom
of the reservoir at the time of our exploration Soft soil condition was not observed The
top of the embankment is accessible through a trail cut diagonally on the southern slope
of the embankment
Based on the topographic map provided the ground elevations range from
approximately 142 feet MSL at the bottom of the reservoir to 172 feet MSL at the top
of the embankment

2 3 Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four borings and
excavating and sampling three test pits Boring Nos 1 and 2 were drilled along the
crest of the dam extending to about 55 8 and 59 3 feet below the existing ground
surface Boring Nos 3 and 4 were drilled downstream of the dam extending to about
18 and 17 5 feet below the existing ground surface Three test pits designated as Test
Pit Nos 1 through 3 were excavated inside the reservoir to depths of approximately
6 to 11 feet below the existing ground surface The approximate boring and test pit
locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration along the dam crest generally encountered approximately
21 to 28 feet of fill consisting of very stiff to very hard silty clay clayey silt The fill was
underlain by residual soils consisting of very stiff to hard silty clay clayey silt extending
to about 50 5 to 53 feet below the existing ground surface Medium hard to hard basalt
formation was encountered below the residual soils extending to the maximum depths
explored of about 55 8 and 59 3 feet below the existing ground surface in addition
Boring No 2 encountered a layer of alluvial deposits between fill and residual soils The
alluvial deposits were 13 5 feet thick and consisted of very stiff silty clay clayey silt
Our borings at the downsiream side of the dam generally encountered 13 and
14 feet of residual soils consisting of medium stiff to very stiff silty clay clayey silt
overlying hard basalt formation extending to the maximum depths drilled of about
17 5 and 18 feet below the existing ground surface
Three test pits excavated inside the existing reservoir encountered 4 5 to 7 feet
of residual soils overlying saprolite extending to the maximum depths explored of about
6 and 11 feet below the existing ground surface Generally residual soils consist of
medium stiff to very stiff silty clay clayey silt and saprolite consists of medium stiff silt
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings or test pits at the time of our
field exploration However it should be noted that groundwater levels are expected to
vary with seasonal rainfall and time of year

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Excavation
Based on our field exploration the project site is underlain by medium stiff to very
stiff silty clay clayey silts at the ground surface The silty clay clayey silts are
underlain by highly weathered basalt formation We believe that the silty
clay clayey silt may be excavated by using regular equipment such as backhoes
and bulldozers Based on our logs of borings and proposed finish grade the
excavation for the project may not encounter the basalt formation However if
some excavations encounter basalt formation we believe that the highly weathered
basalt formation may be excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment such
as backhoes and ripped with large bulldozers However if some excavations
encounter dense basalt formations they may require the use of hoerams chipping
or blasting to remove the dense materials in localized areas The above
discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based on our
visual observation of the existing basalt formation at the site We recommend
encouraging contractors to examine the site conditions to make their own
interpretation
Cutand Fill Slopes
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered during our field exploration we
believe that the planned cut slopes will expose the medium stiff to hard silty
clays clayey silts at the site In general cut slopes for the réservoir project may be
designed with a slope inclination of 2H 1V or flatter To facilitate the installation of
the impervious liner and placement of the aggregate subbase course at the
reservoir a maximum slope inclination of should be used in the design of
the lined reservoir slopes
We do not anticipate permanent fill slopes for this project However if needed
permanent fill slopes may also be designed with a maximum slope inclination of
2H 1V or flatter As mentioned above a slope inclination of should be
used in the design to facilitate the installation of the impervious liner and placement
of the aggregate subbase course within the reservoir Fills placed on slopes
steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed and benched into firm and stable materials to

Fill and Backfill Materials
Fills and backfills to be used as general fills should consist of well graded materials
generally less than 3 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent
the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass In general the excavated
materials may be re used as a source of general fill materials provided that the
materials are processed to meet the maximum size and gradation requirements
presented above It should be noted that the on site silty clay clayey silt soils may
have high in situ moisture contents Therefore the contractor should anticipate
difficulties in achieving the specified compaction requirement using the on site soils
considering the relatively wet conditions at the site
The aggregate subbase course recommended below the impervious liner of the
reservoir should consist of well graded granular soils with a maximum particle size
of 3inches The aggregate subbase course should conform to the minimum
material requirements specified in Subsection 703 17 of the State of Hawaii
Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction 2005

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Fill Placement and Compaction Requiremenis
In general fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches
in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum
dry density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557
Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to
the maximum dry density
The aggregate subbase course should be placed in level lifts not exceeding
8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
3 2 Impervious Liner and Underlining
We understand that a synthetic liner will be provided for the reservoir to reduce
the potential for significant water infiltration through the underlying material It is our
opinion that the material type performance installation and protection details of the
impervious liner should be designed in accordance with recommendations by the liner
manufacturer It is critical that the liner maintain its integrity to prevent saturation and
build up of seepage water pressures
Our experience with similar synthetic liner installations indicates that there is
sometimes a tendency for the liners to become detached at the top of the water
impoundment It appears that this may be the result of inadequate anchoring of the top
of the liner and insufficient flexibility of the liner material We recommend consulting the
liner manufacturer regarding proper construction detailing and installation of the liner
with particular attention to proper anchoring to reduce the potential for liner failure
Effects of fluctuating water levels on the performance of the impervious liner near the
top anchor should also be considered The anchorage trench should be backfilled with
aggregate base course material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction

Technical representatives of the manufacturers of the impervious liner should be
required to be on site during the installation process to assure that proper construction
procedures and precautions are followed A sales representative of the supplier s may

An underlining consisting of a heavy duty geotextile fabric is recommended
beneath the impervious liner to increase the puncture resistance of the liner The use of
a geotextile fabric is preferred over a gravel cushion due to the ease of installation and
the increased puncture resistance of the synthetic liner when combined with a layer of
geotextile As a minimum requirement the geotextile fabric should have a minimum
weight of 8 ounces per square yard when tested in accordance with ASTM D 3776

four borings extending to depths of about 17 5 to 59 3 feet below the existing ground

depths

Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 determinations were performed on selected
soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The test
results are presented on the Logs of Borings and Test Pits at the appropriate sample
depths
Six Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B 1
Five Unconfined Compression tests ASTM D 2938 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate their unconfined compressive strength of the soils
encountered Unconfined compression test results are presented on Plates B 2 through
