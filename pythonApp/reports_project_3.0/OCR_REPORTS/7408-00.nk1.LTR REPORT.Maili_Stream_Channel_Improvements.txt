below the water table, it is desired to re-evaluate the repair scheme.

alignment. In addition, a 50-foot wide stream maintenance easement is also available on 
the northern side of the property boundary. 
The existing concrete lined channel was constructed at one and one-half horizontal

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu is largely composed of the weathered remnants of the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late 
Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and 
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. It is believed that Waianae Volcano 
became extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried 
below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu. 
The project site is on the southern flank of the Waianae Volcano. The rocks of the 
Waianae Volcanic Series are divided into Lower, Middle and Upper Members.  The lavas of 
the Lower Member consist largely of thinly bedded pahoehoe flows whereas the Middle 
Member consists of interbedded pahoehoe and a`a flows with a higher occurrence of the 
a`a form.  The Upper Member consists primarily of massive a`a lava flows. 
Erosion deeply dissected the Waianae Shield removing much of the western slope 
of the dome resulting in broad amphitheater headed valleys.  During the Pleistocene Epoch, 
sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew 
and advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell 
below the present stands of the sea.  When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of 
water became available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous 
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous 
reef materials. 
The project site is located at the foot of Puu o Hulu Kai and Puu o Hulu Uka that 
are elevational prominences on an erosional remnant ridge from the western flank of the 
Waianae Range.  Chemical and mechanical weathering of the volcanic rocks of the ridge 
has resulted in the deposition of an apron of colluvium and alluvium along the pediment 
of the ridge.  These deposits generally consist of remnant boulders and highly expansive 
clay. In the lower areas where the topographic gradient decreases, the clay soils 
frequently mantle the underlying coral formation.

SUBSURFACE CONDITION 
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling two borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of approximately 16.5 and 22.3 feet 
below the existing ground surface. The approximate drilled boring locations are shown on 
the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
In general, our field exploration encountered fill material about 5 feet thick over beach 
sand and/or coralline sand. The fills generally consist of medium stiff to very stiff clayey soils 
intermixed with coralline sand and gravel. Below the fill materials, loose to medium dense 
beach sand and/or coralline sand were encountered to the maximum depth explored of 
approximately 22.3 feet below the ground surface. 
Groundwater was encountered at a depth of about 5 feet below the ground surface 
at the time of our field exploration, corresponding to an elevation of approximately +0.5 feet 
MSL. It should be noted that the water levels are expected to vary with tidal fluctuation, 
seasonal rainfall, and time of year.

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the information provided, we understand that it is desired to repair the 
existing damaged concrete channel walls on the north bank of Maili Stream.  Sections 
of 
the concrete channel walls were corroded and broken. Consequently, the existing channel 
wall backfill was eroded leaving voids behind the damaged concrete sections. 
Additional channel cleaning efforts were conducted by the Department of Facility 
Maintenance, City & County of Honolulu, to remove the sand in the concrete channel and 
to observe the extent of the broken concrete channel walls. Based on the observation, it 
appeared that the cracks and voids may extend down up to an elevation of approximately 
+0 feet MSL. Loose sand was exposed in the voids behind the concrete channel walls about 
6 to 12 inches thick, as shown in the photo below for illustration. 
Based on our field exploration, the existing concrete lined channel structure may be 
underlain by soft/loose lagoonal/beach sand soils, extending down up to an elevation of 
about -5 feet MSL. The soft/loose soil conditions may also initially attribute to the voids 
behind the concrete channel wall due to the consolidation settlement. However, we believe 
the primary consolidation settlement from compression of the soft/loose soils under the 
existing concrete lined channel had completed since the original channel construction.

Based on our field observation, we believe the damaged concrete channel walls 
may be replaced after removal of sand, where accumulated behind the concrete lining, 
with a vacuum truck. It is critical to replace the sand with flowable concrete as necessary, 
to minimize future cracks that may be caused by sand migration and/or settlement. In 
addition, we recommend exposing the top of the boulder fills along backside of the 
channel wall lining and placing flowable concrete to fill up the voids between the boulders. 
Prior to removal of the fills behind the channel wall, the channel wall lining should 
be supported by temporary shoring and bracing. Therefore, the channel wall lining repair 
may be conducted to one panel at a time. Best Management Plan in the concrete channel 
should be installed sufficiently during the excavation and concrete placement, to prevent 
contamination to the ocean water. The contractor may consider placing concrete during 
low tide and during periods of minimal wave activity. 
Based on the subsurface soils encountered in the borings and our engineering 
analyses, we believe total of consolidation settlement from the loose lagoonal soils 
induced by the weight of flowable concrete backfill may be on the order of less than 1 inch. 
Majority of the settlement would likely occur during the concrete placement.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling two 
boreholes, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 16.5 and 
22.3 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped 
with continuous flight augers and coring tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 and A-2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive 
the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard 
Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.

Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths.

to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits.  The test results are summarized on the Logs of

provided on Plate B-1.

samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plate B-2. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected samples to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested.  Results of the direct 
shear tests are presented on Plates B-3 and B-4.
