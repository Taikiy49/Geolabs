PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located on the Windward side of the island of
Oahu to the north of the town of Kaneohe Hawaii The site encompasses
approximately 400 acres of rural land and has been used primarily for
agricultural purposes It is proposed to develop the site as a golf
course and recreational development
Previously portions of the site were explored by others to develop
geotechnical engineering recommendations for other types of development
The results of the previous explorations which were made available to us
were reviewed as part of our consultation efforts

preliminary engineering cost estimation of the proposed golf course
development

Regional Geology
The eastern part of the island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of
basaltic lavas from the rift zones of the Koolau shield volcano which
extended from Kahuku to Makapuu The project site is located roughly at
the northern boundary of the central caldera complex of Koolau near its
junction with the northeast rift zone
During the active phase of the volcano hydrothermal reactions caused
by steam and gases in the caldera complex altered the rocks into easily
eroded materials Headward erosion by the ocean to the east and from
streams with headwaters above the site breached the caldera and rift zone
boundaries exposing the easily eroded materials This resulted in the
rapid erosion of the eastern part of the Koolau dome
During the Pleistocene epoch or Ice Age global sea levels
fluctuated in response to the continental glaciers As the glaciers grew
and advanced there was less water to fill the ocean basins so sea level
fell to elevations below the current stand of the sea As the glaciers
melted and retreated sea level rose
During the low stands of the sea erosion occurred at elevations now
below sea level At the project site this caused the erosion of
relatively deep drainageways between resistant remnants of the original
Koolau dome and deposits of older alluvium Based on the available data
these drainageways appear to be as deep or deeper than 60 feet below
current sea level
When the level of the sea rose the deep drainageways became an
embayment or estuary off of what is now Kaneohe Bay Growth of corals and
accumulation of marine sediments across the mouth of the estuary resulted
in a lagoon or lake in what had been the drainageways

The depositional environment of lagoons or lakes is very low energy
This results in the accumlation of thick soft deposits of fine grained
sediments such as silts and clays Frequently a large portion of the
sediments are organic in origin As the sediments continue to accumulate
the lagoon or lake eventually becomes a marsh characterized by wet soft
surface soils with a dense vegetative cover of grasses rushes and similar
plants
Subsurface Conditions
The following are discussions of the generalized subsurface
conditions in each region of the project site
Steep Ridgelands
Our review of the available data and current field reconnaissance
indicate that most of the soils within this region are residual soils
derived from the in situ weathering of volcanic lavas Generally
these soils are clayey silt soils with relatively good strength
parameters and low swelling potential Soils of this type generally
provide adequate bearing for most types of infrastructure and golf
course developments
Previous experience in the vicinity of the project site also
indicates that the soils in this area generally have high in situ
moisture contents due to the wet climatic conditions in the area
The moisture contents are usually significantly in excess of optimm
moisture content Note The compactability of a soil is primarily
determined by its moisture content i e if it is too wet or too
ary proper compaction cannot be readily achieved The optimm
moisture content refers to the soil moisture content at which a soil
can be compacted to its highest density This high natural moisture
content could result in increased handling of the soils since they
are too wet to be properly compacted at their natural moisture
contents and would have to be aerated by blading with a bulldozer
until they have dried sufficiently for site fill placement

During our brief field reconnaissance of the project site and its
vicinity it was noted that there were several outcrops of in situ
basalt along the base of the main ridge to the north of the site
This indicates that some rock may exist at relatively shallow depths
within the steeper slope areas
Moderate Ridgelands
Our review of the available soil boring data and brief field
reconnaissance indicate that generally most of the soils within this
region are probably derived from the deep weathering of alluvial and
colluvial deposits In general these soils are classified as clayey
silt soils with moderate strength parameters and generally low
swelling potential due to the relatively high natural moisture
contents Previous experience with similar alluvial and colluvial
deposits indicates that bouldery horizons or isolated boulders are
frequently encountered Soils of this type generally provide
adequate bearing for golf course developments
Previous experience in the vicinity of the project site also
indicates that the soils in this area generally have high in situ
moisture contents due to the wet climatic conditions in the area
The moisture contents are usually significantly in excess of optimm
moisture content for compaction This will result in a need to dry
the wet soil prior to compacting it Generally local practice in
this high rainfall area has been to utilize slightly lower compaction
criteria 85 in comparison to the criteria used in drier parts of
the island 90 This reduced compaction criteria serves to
expedite site filling progress however the high rainfall conditions
still result in lower fill placement rates due to and increased
handling of the on site soils to allow drying

Central Lowlands
Our review of the available data and limited field reconnaissance
indicate that most of the soils within this region are recent
deposits of sediments in the central portion of the site which was
formerly an embayment or estuary Generally these soils are clay
and organic silt soils with low to very low soil shear strength
parameters In their present condition with a high groundwater
table the soft wet organic soils generally could not provide an
adequate bearing or playing surface for golf course development
Further soft compressible soils of the type which appear to exist in
most of this region generally will not provide adequate direct
support in their natural state for even lightly loaded structures
without requiring a site stabilization fill or using special grading
procedures and construction preparation
Previous experience in the vicinity of the project site and the
review of the available data indicate that the surface soils in this
area are generally saturated have very high in situ moisture
contents and are highly compressible when loaded due to the
near surface shallow generally 0 to 5 feet deep groundwater levels
and the continued alluvial siltation in the lowlying environment
These characteristics result in soft wet ground conditions which
generally require site stabilization by the placement of granular
fill to develop a firm fill crust Alternatively a dry more
competent surface crust could also be developed with time by
desiccation or drying of the surface soils if the high water levels
can be lowered Additional study should be made to explore the
possibility that there is sufficient hydraulic gradient to drain the
site through the mouth of Heeia Stream and that the high water levels
are not the result of leakage of an artesian aquifer below the site

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
In general it is our opinion that the project site is developable
from a geotechnical engineering point of view Based upon our review of
the available data the soil conditions encountered in the Steep
Ridgelands and Moderate Ridgelands can provide adequate support for a golf
course development From a geotechnical engineering point of view it is
feasible to stabilize the soft wet soils which appear to exist in the
Central Lowlands to support a golf course or development However other
constraints such as regulatory actions may preclude the site filling of
this lowlying portion of the site
Development Considerations
Major considerations with respect to development of the project site
are the steep existing slopes in the Steep Ridgelands region the soft wet
soil conditions that appear to exist throughout most of the Central
lowlands the regulatory designation of most of the Central Lowlands
region as Wetlands and the high in situ moisture contents of the
potential on site borrow fill soils in both of the Ridgelands regions
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2364 00 Page 10
Golf course development in the Steep Ridgelands would most likely
require extensive earthwork in terms of deep cuts and fills to provide
sufficient gently rolling or level areas for playing field
Development in the Central Lowlands would generally require site
stabilization earthwork in the form of fill placement to raise the site
above the water level to provide dry surface crust areas for playing
fields Generally the soils in this region are soft compressible
saturated clay and organic silt soils with a high groundwater level and
low to very low strength In their natural state soils of this type
generally do not provide adequate direct bearing for fills or structures
without special soft ground stabilization procedures prior to filling
The soft ground stabilization procedures are costly particularly for
large golf course developments Additionally it is our understanding
that most of the Central Lowlands have been designated as wetlands que
to the existing wet marshy surface conditions and that fill placement in
wetland areas is currently prohibited Therefore an alternative to
raising the site above the water table may be to lower the water level by
drainage of the site As previously mentioned the wet surface conditions
in the Central Lowlands could be the result of stagnant surface drainage
This stagnation could be due to a lack of maintenance of Heeia Stream and
of the previously existing ditches traversing the site We believe that
this should be further studied and further evaluated
The moisture contents of the underlying soils in both of the
Ridgeland regions are probably in excess of the ideal range of moisture
contents for quick and efficient fill placement This results in
decreased fill placement rates or workability resulting in increased
handling of the soils during earthwork due to the need to aerate the soils
to reduce their moisture content prior to use as compacted fill material
Based on previous experience in the vicinity of the project site we have
found that even if the on site soils are allowed to dry during normal work
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2364 00 Page 11
days the moisture contents are generally still high enough that it is
warranted to use a lower level of compaction for the fill placement
These factors lead to slower construction progress and higher grading
costs We believe that the normal 90 compaction standard used in drier
areas could be reduced to 80 to 85 maximum dry density per ASTM Test
Method D 1557 78 and that the fill could still provide adequate support
for the proposed golf course development The use of a reduced compaction
criteria would serve to expedite the progress of the earthwork somewhat
Development Capabilities
Generally it is believed that the soils in both of the Ridgeland
regions are clayey silt soils with good to moderate strength parameters
and low swelling potentials Soils with these characteristics generally
provide adequate bearing for most types of development particularly for
golf courses
In order to reduce the requirements for extensive earthwork the
Steep Ridgelands region could be developed as a challenging golf course
with islands of playing area surrounded by the existing rugged terrain
In that manner major grading work could be limited to tees landing zones
and greens
Grading for the Steep Ridgelands region would probably require
construction on steep cut slopes and the use of retaining structures
Based upon our review of the available data it is our opinion that the
on site soils will generally need to be graded with cut slope ratios of
about two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V and will most likely provide
adequate bearing support for retaining structures if they are properly
designed
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2364 00 Page 12
With respect to development of a golf course the Moderate Ridgelands
region probably has terrain that is much more conducive to the contour
grading and construction of a narrow golf course design The gentler
slopes would require less earthwork for the construction of open fairways
and gentler approaches to the greens
It is our understanding that some portions along the peripheries of
the Central Lowlands region may be excluded from designation as
wetlands These portions could be developed as a portion of the golf
course development together with the higher Moderate Ridgeland region It
is anticipated that it will be necessary to raise the grades in these
lower portions by fill placement Based on our review of the available
data and previous experience we anticipate that some amounts of site
stabilization using special grading procedures such as placement of
geotextiles and or an 18 inch thick granular fill blanket over the on site
soft wet soils may be needed in these portions of the Central Lowlands
region The underlying stabilizing granular fill will also serve as a
capillary break to promote the growth of the grasses used on the golf
course Alternatively if the site could be drained to lower the
groundwater level sufficiently to allow the surface to dry the
stabilization layer may be omitted
Recommendations
As previously discussed one of the major considerations and
constraints to development of the entire Heeia Meadowlands site is the
designation of the majority of the Central Lowlands region which
encompasses about half of the land area as wetlands Also as
previously discussed it appears that a major factor in the wet surface
conditions in this part of the site could be stagnant and or unmaintained
drainage The aerial photomap of the site prepared in 1969 shows that
most of the area was under cultivation or being used as pasture This map
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2364 00 Page 13
also shows a number of ditches traversing the site and a distinct channel
for Heeia Stream Therefore it is our opinion that the wetlands
condition at the site is to an extent artificial
To verify the above possibility we recommend that water surface
elevations within the project site be obtained We also recommend that
the tidal fluctuations in Heeia Stream and the open pond area within the
Central Lowlands be gauged and monitored If the water surface elevations
are appreciably higher than Mean Sea Level and there are no tidal
variations then the wet conditions are likely the result of impeded
drainage or artesian ground water seepage
We also recommend that additional studies and or exploration he
conducted to evaluate the potential or existence of any artesian
groundwater conditions occurring below the site or in its vicinity This
would aid in assessing the possibility that the wet surface conditions
could be the expression of a leaking artesian aquifer
If there is sufficient hydraulic gradient to allow the area to be
drained it is possible that the site conditions could be improved by
reestablishing and maintaining the drainage of this region Proper
drainage of this region could allow the formation of a drier more
competent surface crust which in turn could possibly reduce the amount
of land drea designated as Wetlands and aid in supporting any proposed
development

Assuming that some limited amount of site filling will be allowed
within the lowlying Central Lowlands region the following site fill
stabilization procedures are anticipated

1 Site preparation should consist of lightly grubbing areas
designated to receive fill The light grubbing should consist of
mowing the existing grasses without disturbing the root mat The
mown grasses may be left in place to form a mat Woody trees and
plants should be cut off at the ground surface and disposed of

2 A layer of Mirafi 140N or equivalent geotextile fabric should be

3 Limited fill may then be placed over the gravel blanket Due to

fill be compacted to between 80 and 85 of its maximm dry
density per ASTM D 1557 78 Lightweight equipment should
initially be used for the earthwork to reduce the potential of
inducing pumping failure in the underlying subsoils
Fill Settlements
We anticipate that site filling will induce settlements in the
underlying subsoils Settlements will occur both during construction
and as long term settlements Utility and surface structure
construction should be avoided on the new fill placed over the
underlying soft compressible soils
Due to the high in situ moisture contents generally encountered in
the vicinity of the project site we would anticipate recommending a
reduced level of compaction such as about 80 to 85 of the maximum
dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 to facilitate the
progress of the earthwork Previous experience indicates that these
reduced levels of compaction are generally adequate for golf courses
and residential developments

Infrastructure Construction
Based on the available data and our limited reconnaissance it is
anticipated that installation of infrastructure such as irrigation
water sewer and drainage in the Ridgeland regions will he
relatively conventional utility construction within the upper high
ground areas Groundwater will probably be encountered in
excavations in lowlying areas adjacent to the Central Lowlands and or
in isolated perched conditions Therefore some dewatering efforts
and sheetpile shoring should be anticipated for utility trench
excavations Except for flexible joint irrigation pipes general
major utility line construction should be avoided or delayed within
the newly filled sites located above the wetland areas underlain with
soft compressible soils
Additional Consultation
This report was prepared on a preliminary basis to aid in project
planning and engineering cost analyses The discussions presented herein
are based on previous experiences review of available boring data and a
recent field reconnaissance Therefore the recommendations presented
herein should not be considered adequate for detailed project design
purposes

consultation including formal exploration of the subsurface conditions at
the specific development sites This additional consultation is necessary
to allow the formulation of detailed design recommendations
