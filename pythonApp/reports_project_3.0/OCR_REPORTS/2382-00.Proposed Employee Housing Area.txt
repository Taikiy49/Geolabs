INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed employee housing area in Princeville on the
island of Kauai Hawaii The general location of the project site is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings of our exploration and our geotechnical
engineering recommendations derived from our analyses based on the data obtained
during the exploration These recommendations are intended for design input and are
not intended for construction estimation or bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is proposed to develop the project site as a multi family development to provide
units for employee housing at Princeville The project is still in the conceptual stage
therefore no site grading plans are currently availabie The current development scheme
is to provide 100 or more residential units in two and three story wood framed structures
Preliminary architectural sketches indicate post and beam foundation systems with some
of the units having below grade entrances
However the project site is situated in a drainageway having side slopes with ratios
as steep as two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V These slopes pose a constraint on the
project by limiting the amount of buildable area for residential units and parking areas
Therefore we anticipate that substantial site grading will be required for the proposed
project to provide building pads and level open parking areas It is also anticipated that
retaining walls will be required to provide grade separation

SITE DESCRIPTION
The Princeville development area is located on the northern coast of the island of
Kauai Hawaii The project site is located in a drainageway to the north of the existing
Hanalei Plantation Road and existing employee housing units and south of the Woods
Golf Course The general location of the site is shown on the Project Location Map Plate
1
The site is raw undeveloped land and is thickly vegetated with guava Java plum
brassia and other subtropical trees and shrubs A small stream meanders through the
central portion of the drainageway This stream has become choked and has overtopped
its banks resulting in several wet marshy areas

Based on our field reconnaissance the available topographic map and site plan
it appears that the site slopes towards the axis of the drainageway at ratios ranging
between about four horizontal to one vertical 4H 1V and two horizontal to one vertical
2H 1V or steeper The existing ground surface elevations range from about 150 to 225
feet above Mean Sea Level MSL The general direction of drainage is along the stream
running in a generally westerly direction from the existing golf course maintenance facility

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The project site is situated on a broad gently sloping plateau along the north shore
of the island of Kauai This plateau was built during the late Pliocene and Pleistocene by
the extrusion of basaltic lavas and related pyroclastics from the Pooku vent of the Koloa
Volcanic Series
Most of the soils in the project area are residual soils derived from the in situ
weathering of these basic igneous rocks The residual soils grade with depth to saprolite
a form of residual soil which retains the relict structures vesicles and joints of the parent
rock
In the bases of undeveloped drainageways sediments and organic matter have
accumulated and are generally soft with a high organic content

W O 2382 00 Page 6
Soil Conditions
The subsurface conditions were explored by drilling and sampling five 5 borings
to depths of 26 5 to 51 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate
locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The field exploration was conducted prior to
the formulation of conceptual site development plans and the borings were drilled and
sampled in locations intended to provide a general representation of the subsurface
conditions at the site
The areas which were noted during our field work to have soft wet surface soils
were explored by hand augering five 5 probes to depths of 2 5 to 8 0 feet below the
existing ground or water surface at the approximate locations also shown on the Site
Plan Plate 2
In general the soil conditions encountered throughout the project site appear to
be relatively uniform Most of our drilled borings encountered a near surface layer
approximately 4 0 to 10 0 feet thick of medium stiff to stiff brown to reddish brown clayey
silt These are residual soils derived from the in situ weathering of Koloa Basalts and are
generally structureless There is a thin layer about 2 to 3 feet thick of loose slopewash
material overlying the residual soils
Underlying the residual soils our borings encountered generally medium stiff to stiff
saprolite soils consisting of clayey silts and silts with sand and decomposed gravel
having predominantly brown color mottled with black spots or streaks to the maximum
drilled depth of the borings
During our field exploration groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings
at depths varying from 4 0 to 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface This
groundwater is most likely related to the stream running through the project site and may
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2382 00 Page 7
be in a perched condition as the result of low permeability ash beds or lenses within the
Koloa Volcanic Series The shallowest depths to groundwater were found in the borings
located in the lower areas near the stream Based on the current conceptual site plans
these areas are situated outside of the planned structural areas
It was also noted that a small portion of the site is occupied by an existing disposal
site for the disposal of vegetation wastes and miscellaneous rubbish from golf course and
grounds maintenance The current development plan does not show any proposed site
improvements in this existing disposal site area
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
In general it appears that the proposed multi family residential development is
feasible provided that the recommendations presented herein are followed
The project site is situated in a drainageway having side slopes with ratios as steep
as two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V Generally siting of the residential structures
should be limited to areas having existing slopes with ratios of three horizontal to one
vertical 3H 1V or flatter The steeper slopes pose a constraint on the project by limiting
the amount of buildable area for residential units and parking areas
Except for some areas of soft wet subsoils in portions of the base of the
drainageway the field exploration encountered generally competent subsoils within the
project site However modified grading techniques are required for the earthwork portion
of the proposed site improvements due to the relatively high in situ moisture content of
the subsoils and the relatively high rainfall occurring in the area
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2382 00 Page 8
The existing disposal site area should be excluded from the project development
area to reduce the potential of structural distresses resulting from settlements caused by
the decay of organic material buried in the site
Site Grading
The proposed development is in the conceptual stage and detailed grading plans
are not available However cuts and fills of up to about 10 feet in depth or thickness are
anticipated
Fill Compaction
Since the project site is located in a high rainfall area most of the subsoils within
the top 15 feet had in situ moisture contents of about 40 to over 60 per cent at the
time of our exploration These moisture contents are considerably higher than the
anticipated optimum moisture contents which range from about 32 to 36 per cent
for similar soils in the Princeville area Therefore considerable drying of the
excavated materials would normally be required prior to their use as fill materials
However in view of the relatively frequent rainfall that generally occurs in the area
throughout the year drying of large quantities of soils might not be readily
achieved during mass grading operations To facilitate construction it is our
opinion that the normal compaction standard of 90 of its maximum dry density
as determined by ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 used for general site fills in drier
areas may be reduced to 85 maximum dry density for this project Our previous
experience in Princeville and other similar areas indicates that 85 compaction is
generally adequate for site fills under roadway and light building loads
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2382 00 Page 9
Fills within 12 inches below roadway or footing subgrade should be compacted to
a minimum of 90 of its ASTM D 1557 78 maximum dry density or a geotextile
reinforcement should be used at the subgrade level
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Topsoil including the surface layer of slopewash
which is about 2 to 3 feet thick should be stripped and stockpiled for later use in
landscaping Any unsuitable materials such as debris rubbish vegetation and
other deleterious materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site
The area to receive fill should be scarified to a minimum depth of 6 inches below
the cleared grade level air dried if necessary and recompacted to the above
recommended minimum of 85 of its maximum dry density as determined by
ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
The ground surface below pavements or footings should be scarified to a minimum
depth of 6 inches below subgrade level air dried if necessary and recompacted
to a minimum of 90 of its maximum dry density as determined by ASTM Test
Method D 1557 78
Any loose soft or yielding areas encountered at the pavement or footing subgrade
level should be over excavated down to in situ firm ground and replaced with
air dried select on site soil or imported backfill placed in horizontal lifts not
exceeding 12 inches in loose thickness and then compacted to a minimum of 85
of its ASTM Test Method D 1557 78 maximum dry density Backfill within 12

Soft Wet Areas
Soft wet areas were encountered along portions of the stream and at the western
end of the project site An approximate delineation of the soft areas is shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 It is our belief that fills in these soft wet areas may cause
slope instability of the stream banks and induce long term ground settlements
The current conceptual development plans do not indicate any planned
development in the areas where our exploration encountered these soft wet soils
However if site grading will encroach on them we recommend that fills in these
soft areas should be limited to 5 feet maximum thickness to reduce the potential
of ground failure resulting from the weight of the fill
Fills in the soft areas should also be placed during the earliest part of the site
earthwork Settlement gauges should be installed at the base of the fills to monitor
ground settlements induced by the weight of the new fill
Construction of structures and the installation of utilities in fills underlain by the soft
wet soils should be delayed until most of the ground settlement has occurred as
determined by monitoring of the settlement gauge readings
Fills greater than 5 feet thick would require special design and construction
procedures such as setbacks and incremental placement If the site grading for
the development will require the construction of thick fills in these areas we
recommend that additional exploration and analyses be conducted to obtain data
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2382 00 Page 11
upon which to base development of additional recommendations for the final
design
Temporary diversion of the existing stream would be required for the construction
of a Culvert crossing for the proposed access road at the western end of the site
near the existing Lei O Papa Road Construction of the culvert should also be
delayed until the majority of ground settlement induced by the new fill at the culvert
site have reached tolerable rates
This would probably require at least a double barrel culvert with incremental
construction of the fill with diversion of the stream to one side for initial fill
placement on one bank When the settlements of the first side have become
tolerable the first barrel could be installed and the stream diverted through the
barrel Fill for the second part of the crossing could then be installed and the
remaining barrel installed when those settlements have reached a tolerable rate

Based on the field exploration and laboratory test results it is our opinion
that the on site soils have low expansion potential and may be used as a
source of fill and backfill materials if dried as indicated in the paragraph
above entitled Fill Compaction If additional off site borrow is required the
material should be tested and approved by the geotechnical engineer prior
to hauling to the site to see that it is suitable for the proposed construction
The material should generally be non expansive and should not contain large
percentages of boulders organic or deleterious matters

Subdrains
Due to the wet climatic conditions in the region of the project site and based
upon previous experiences of encountering seepage in drainageways in the
Princeville area we recommend that subdrains be installed to below new fill
embankments to intercept and relieve potential seepage which may occur or
accumulate along the interface between the existing natural ground and the
new fill Such an accumulation of water could create a plane of weakness
between the fill and the existing subsoils and potentially cause slippage or
creep of the new fill
The subdrains should be installed along the base axes of drainage paths and
along side slopes to intercept and drain any potential seepage flow below the
new fill See Plates 4 and 5 The final location and quantity of subdrains
should be determined in the field by the project geotechnical engineer
Reinforced Embankments
As previously discussed the proposed residential structures should only be
sited in areas where the existing slope is at a ratio of three horizontal to one vertical
or flatter 3H 1V One of the constraints or limitations on development of the site is
the limited amount of buildable area i e areas where the ground surface is at a slope
of three horizontal to one vertical 3H 1V or flatter In the current conceptual
development plan a relatively large amount of this space is consumed by parking
areas to provide the required number of parking stalls
More of the buildable area could be utilized for residential units if the parking
areas could be constructed in the steeper portions of the site i e downslope of the
residential units rather than upslope as indicated on the current conceptual plans
GEOLABS HAWAII
Page 13
W O 2382 00 Page 14
However since the slopes in the steeper areas are as steep as two horizontal to one
vertical 2H 1V it is not possible to construct conventionai fill embankments with
finished slopes of two horizontal to one vertical 2H 1V
The classical solution has been to construct retaining walls to support fills
placed in steep areas However the cost of retaining walls for an affordable housing
project is frequently prohibitive A potentially more economical method of supporting
fills in steep areas is by the use of reinforced embankments
Reinforced embankments utilize a special form of geotextile known as
geogrids to distribute tensional and shear forces through the embankment thereby
allowing the construction of steeper fill slopes with a greatly reduced potential of slope
failure It is our opinion that reinforced embankments could be used in the proposed
development to provide more buildable area by allowing the use of steeper one
horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V fill slopes With a steeper finished slope fills could
then be constructed in steeper areas See Plates 6 and 7
Based on our analyses using geogrids a one horizontal to one
vertical 1H 1V fill slope may be constructed of the on site soils placed as
recommended in Site Grading if geogrids are used in the embankment The vertical
separation of the geogrid layers should be about two 2 feet and the geogrids should
extend a minimum of 110 of the slope height into the fill at its base and a minimum
of 80 of the slope height at its top See Plate 8
The grids should be wrapped around the slope face between layers to provide
additional reinforcement to the slope face See Plate 6 A facing such as geotextile
sod straw or heavy paper should be incorporated below the grids on the slope face
to protect the soils until a vegetative cover has become established It is our belief
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2382 00 Page 15
that sod may be readily available in the vicinity of the project site and it is further our
opinion that use of sod would be more advantageous than geotextile straw or paper
since it is essentially a pre established vegetative cover
The new reinforced embankment fill should be keyed and benched into the
existing slope A drainage layer approximately one foot thick of ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation 3B Fine or a synthetic drainage mat should be provided between the
natural soils and the new fill The drainage should be provided at both the base and
the back of the fill and should be daylighted to drain downslope
Surcharge or structural loads applied to the top of the reinforced embankment
should be set back a minimum of six 6 feet from the top of slope Generally the
reinforced embankment should not be used to support foundation loads It is our
opinion however that the reinforced embankment if properly constructed could
provide adequate support for the typical loads imposed in a residential parking lot
Consideration could also be given to using reinforced embankments for grade
separation in lieu of retaining walls in other portions of the project
Foundations
Based on the soil conditions we believe that either shallow spread continuous
footing foundations bearing on recompacted in situ subsoils or drilled pier foundations
bearing on undisturbed in situ soils may be used to support the proposed residential
structures In order to provide uniform support for the foundations we recommend
that the foundations bear only on in situ residual soils and not on fill Care should be
taken during foundation excavation to bypass the surface layer of slopewash which
is about 2 to 3 feet thick
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2382 00 Page 16
Generally it is our opinion that continuous strip footings parallel to the trend of
the slope would be the most efficient system for the proposed residential structures
The strip footings could be stepped to follow the original ground surface The upslope
ends of the strip foundations should be tied together with a grade beam or strip
footing
For the design of spread continuous footing foundations an allowable bearing
value of up to 2 500 p s f with a minimum footing embedment of 24 inches below the
lowest adjacent grade is recommended The footings should bear on the residual
soils below the surface slopewash layer The recommended bearing value assumes
that the foundation subgrade has been recompacted as recommended above and is
for total dead plus live load only The allowable bearing capacity may be increased
by one third when considering short duration wind or seismic loading
As an alternate to spread continuous footing foundations consideration could
be given to the use of drilled pier foundations to support the proposed residential
structures For the design of drilled pier foundations an allowable bearing value of
up to 4 000 p s f with a minimum footing embedment of 48 inches below the lowest
adjacent grade is recommended The recommended bearing value assumes that the
foundation subgrade is undisturbed in situ soils and that there is continuous contact
between the shaft of the pier and the surrounding soils The recommended value is
for total dead plus live load only The allowable bearing capacity may be increased
by one third when considering short duration wind or seismic loading
For buildings to be constructed on or adjacent to slopes the building should
be set back or the depth of the footing embedment should be extended so that there
is a minimum of ten 10 feet horizontal set back distance between the toe of the
footing and the outside slope face
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2382 00 Page 17
If a foundation is located next to a utility trench the depth of the foundation
should be extended to the bottom of the utility trench or the structure should be
designed to span over the trench to reduce footing settlement due to settlement of the
trench backfill If the structure is designed to span the utility trench the trench area
below the structure should not be included in foundation support analyses
Based on the boring data estimated total footing settlements under the design
loads are anticipated to be on the order of less than one 1 inch total and one half
1 2 of an inch differential
Resistance to lateral loads on the proposed structures may be generated by
passive soil pressure against the buried vertical faces of the foundations and by
frictional resistance between the concrete foundation bottom and the subgrade soils
An equivalent fluid pressure of 300 p s f per foot of depth of the buried vertical faces
of the footings may be used to determine the passive soil pressure This assumes
that the footing backfill is well compacted The passive resistance of the upper twelve
12 inches should be ignored unless covered with a slab or pavement
Resistance to lateral loads on the proposed structures may also be generated
by friction between the concrete foundation bottom and the subgrade soils A
frictional coefficient of 0 4 may be used to compute the resistance to sliding between
the bottoms of the footings and the recompacted in situ soils

geotechnical engineering services during the excavation and foundation construction
phases of the work This is to observe compliance with the design concepts
specifications or recommendations and to allow design changes in the event that
subsurface conditions differ from that anticipated prior to start of construction The
recommendations given in this report are contingent upon such observations
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and approved
by the geotechnical engineer prior to hauling to the site
If actual exposed soil conditions encountered during construction are different
from those assumed or considered in this report then appropriate modifications to the
design should be made
LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part
upon information obtained from field data collection points such as borings
Variations of conditions between the borings may occur and the nature and extent
of these variations may not become evident until construction is underway _ If
variations then appear evident it will be necessary to re evaluate the
recommendations given in this report
The locations of the borings were determined by estimation from the
topographic map transmitted by the Princeville Development Corporation Elevations
of the borings were determined by interpolation from elevation points on the map

depths ranging between 26 5 and 51 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the

to refusal
