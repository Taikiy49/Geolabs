PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The proposed Maui Lani development consists of a planned residential
community with an 18 hole golf course and related facilities The project
will be developed in several phases
Phase I is situated in the eastern portion of the Maui Lani project
area and consists of about 100 acres of former sugar cane land to the
northwest of Kuihelani Highway The preliminary plans indicate that Phase
I will involve approximately 600 to 700 residential units around a portion
of the golf course Preliminary mass grading plans indicate cuts ranging
up to as much as 40 feet below the existing ground surface and fills of up
to about 25 feet deep The golf course area particularly the water
features will be used as a source of borrow for the mass grading of the
residential areas

GENERAL SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is situated between Wailuku and Kahului in the
Wailuku area of the island of Maui Hawaii The general location of the
site and its vicinity is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
The project site is fallow rural land that has previously been used
for the cultivation of sugar cane Most of the site is densely vegetated
by volunteer sugar cane haole koa and other semitropical trees shrubs
and grasses

In general the topography is nearly level to gently rolling with
locally steep relief where drainageways cross the site The overall slope
and drainage is towards the northeast
Elevations at the Phase I portion of the site vary from about 85 to
140 feet above Mean Sea Level
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Regional Geology
The island of Maui is composed of two shield volcanoes West Maui and
Haleakala built up by the extrusion of basaltic lavas during the
Pleistocene epoch The Maui Lani project site is situated on the isthmus
connecting the two volcanic domes
The isthmus was built by the deposition of sediments over the
underlying basalts of the two volcanoes During the Pleistocene sea
levels fell to stands lower than present sea level exposing large areas of
coralline sand on what was the ocean bottom Wind action transported
these coralline sands inland forming extensive areas of sand dunes
In some areas these dunes have been mantled by man made fill which
was apparently placed during the period that the area was used for the
cultivation of sugar cane
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by the drilling
and sampling of fourteen 14 borings in the accessible portions of the
project site to depths ranging between 11 5 and 56 5 feet below the
existing ground surface
In general our exploration encountered a relatively thin surface
layer of tan to brown medium dense fine silty sand Dune Sand ranging
in thickness between approximately two 2 to fifteen 15 feet In most
areas there was a thin layer of man made fill overlying the sand
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2413 00 Page 5
Below the sand strata our borings encountered stiff brown clayey
silts which contained gravelly and bouldery horizons Alluvium At
depths of about 38 feet below the existing ground surface our borings
encountered very stiff brown clayey silt Residual Soil which graded with
depth to saprolite or extremely weathered basalt Hard basalt formation
was not encountered in any of the drilled borings
No groundwater was encountered in the drilled borings However some
isolated moist to wet zones were noted in the borings These zones may
indicate possible zones of groundwater seepage
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration along with the boring
logs are presented in Appendix A of this report
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Based on our exploration and analyses it is our opinion that
development of Phase I of the proposed residential and golf course project
is feasible from a geotechnical engineering point of view The dune sand
and alluvial soils encountered at the site are generally competent and
should provide adequate bearing for the site improvements of the proposed
development The excavated materials should provide a suitable source of
fill material Some hard excavation and or ripping may be encountered in
localized cemented areas or where bouldery horizons are encountered
Grading
In general based on the results of our preliminary exploration it
appears that conventional grading methods may be used for the mass grading
of the Phase I portion of the Maui Lani development project
The following recommendations are general guidelines for the design
of the mass grading and for the development of general grading
specifications
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O
2413 00 Page 6
Prior to commencement of grading all areas within the proposed
grading limits should be cleared of all vegetation and deleterious
materials The resulting grub and spoil material should either be
disposed of properly off site or else the material should be
shredded chipped and stockpiled for use in landscaping
Due to the dry and sometimes windy site conditions moisture
conditioning of fill material will be necessary Because of silty
and fine sandy soil conditions it is also advisable to institute a
dust control program during grading operations
Soft or yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below
any areas designated to receive fill should be overexcavated to firm
natural material and the resulting depression backfilled with well
compacted engineered fill
In general the excavated soil materials should be suitable as fill
material Any boulders or cobbles larger than twelve 12 inches in
maximum dimension should be removed from the excavated material and
either disposed of off site or be placed in the deeper fills as
discussed below
The material should be adequate as a source of trench and structural
backfill if needed Trench and structural backfill should not
contain any rock or lumps larger than six 6 inches in maximm
dimension To densify the trench backfill water tamping could be
utilized selectively for the porous sandy and silty on site soils
Imported material if required should be non expansive with no
particles greater than six 6 inches in size and no more than
fifteen 15 percent passing the 200 sieve It is recommended that
if additional off site borrow is required it should be tested by the
geotechnical engineer to determine if it is suitable for its intended
use prior to its delivery to the jobsite
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 2413 00 Page 7
Iarge boulders or rock fragments should be buried in the deepest
portions of the fills provided that they are not nested and an
adequate amount of compacted finer grained soil fills the space
between the boulders to reduce the potential of loss of fines from
the upper portion of the fill Care must be exercised to avoid
placement of any boulders in locations which would conflict with
future utility lines In general boulder or rock fragments larger
than twelve 12 inches in size should be placed at depths greater
that four 4 feet below finish subgrade level
Generally fills required should be placed in level lifts not
exceeding twelve 12 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 90 maximum dry density as established by ASTM D 1557 78
Trench and structural backfills should be placed in level lifts not
exceeding eight 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 90 maximm dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Any fills to be placed on slopes having ratios steeper than 5 1
horizontal to vertical should be keyed and benched into firm
natural soils to lessen potential instability of the new fill see
Plate 3 The filling operations should start at the lowest point
and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance
with the above recommendations for fill placement
Excavation
Based on the results of our preliminary exploration it appears that
most of the materials encountered in the Phase I portion of the Maui Lani
development area may be conventionally excavated Some hard excavation
and or ripping may be encountered in localized cemented areas or when
bouldery horizons are encountered Generally the excavation method
should be left to the contractor within practical limits and safety
considerations However care should be exercised to avoid overcutting
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2413 00 Page 8
which would necessitate backfilling of the overcut area This is
particularly critical in slope areas to avoid the placement of sliver
fills on the disturbed sandy silty slope faces in an attempt to repair the
overcut area
Slopes
Provided that on site materials are utilized design cut and fill
slopes of 2 1 H V or flatter are recommended Fill slopes should be
constructed by overfilling and cutting back to the design slope ratio
Care should be exercised to avoid overcutting on cut slopes in order to
avoid the placement of sliver fills on the slope faces in an attempt to
repair the overcut area
Sandy and silty soils such as those encountered at the project site
are susceptible to erosion when exposed particularly in slope areas
Water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope planting
should be utilized to reduce potential drainage run off erosion and or
destabilization of the finished slopes For slopes steeper than three 3
horizontal to one 1 vertical the use of an erosion control mat such as
Miramat or similar should be considered to reduce the potential of slope
erosion until such time as the vegetative cover is well established
In general midpoint slope benches should be provided for slopes
steeper than three 3 horizontal to one 1 vertical and having vertical
heights greater than 20 feet Modification to the above could be made on
an individual basis by the project geotechnical engineer based on the
actual silt soil conditions and other design considerations
Infrastructure Installation
It is anticipated that underground utilities such as water sewer
drain and irrigation lines will be installed as part of the development
work Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that

W O 2413 00 Page 9
relatively conventional excavation methods may be utilized for utility
line trenching However bouldery horizons may be encountered in the
deeper excavations
In general the subsurface materials encountered at the site are
relatively dry and competent and would require minimal trench shoring
However areas of loose sand or gravel may be encountered Trench shoring
design and installation should be left to the contractor which should
conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements
Trench backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding eight
8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimm of 90 maximm
dry density per ASTM Test Method D 1557 78
Mechanical compaction equipment is highly suited to the materials
encountered at the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding should
not be used as a means to attempt to compact the on site soils
Sand Utilization
Our field exploration encountered poorly graded fine sand with grain
size Dune Sand at shallow depths throughout the Phase I area The
thickness of this sand varied a great deal at various locations ranging
between about two 2 and about fifteen 15 feet
Based on our laboratory testing this sand material should provide a
good source of fill and backfill material The material could be
exploited as export material if there is a surplus in the excavation vs
embankment balance
As far as using the material for concrete sand the gradation of the
on site dune sand is finer than the requirements of ASTM C 33
specifications for concrete fine aggregate However it is our
understanding that it has been the practice for several years on the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2413 00 Page 10
island of Maui to blend the dune sand with crushed basalt sand Man sand
to create fine aggregate According to Maui concrete suppliers this
blended aggregate has performed well in terms of workability and strength
Laboratory testing indicates that the sand is acceptable in terms of
organic content ASTM Test Method C 40
Ponds and Water Features
It is our understanding that the proposed golf course development
will involve ponds and water features Due to the dry site conditions and
the anticipated permeable nature of the materials at the site we
recommend that the ponds be lined with synthetic flexible membranes to
reduce the possibility of seepage To reduce the potential of punctures
in the flexible membranes a cushion layer of the on site sand is
recommended for the bottoms and sides of the ponds and water features and
should be installed prior to the placement of the flexible membrane The
cushion should be a minimum of six 6 inches thick
Although the flexible membranes are considered to be impervious
there is a possibility of seepage due to punctures in the liner or breaks
in the seams Therefore we recommend that a subdrain system be installed
below the sand cushion
Foundation Support
Our laboratory testing for this preliminary exploration indicates
that the on site soils generally have low to moderate expansion potential
when saturated from a dry and compacted condition It is our opinion that
shallow spread or strip footing foundations may be used to support
structures Alternately thickened edge slabs on grade may be ideal for
use to support the lightly loaded single family residential structures
For the design of the foundations of single family residential
structures an allowable bearing capacity of 2 500 p s f may be used for
footings bearing either on the in situ soils or on properly compacted fill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 2413 00 Page 11
consisting of on site materials The bottom of footing foundations should
be embedded a minimum of eighteen 18 inches below the lowest adjacent
grade
The thickened edges of slabs on grade should extend a minimum of
twelve 12 inches below the lowest adjacent exterior grade The slab
should be a minimm of four 4 inches thick and should be supported by a
four 4 inch thick cushion of 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 67B gradation A
moisture barrier membrane should be incorporated between the slab and
cushion if necessary to protect the interior floor covering from moisture
infiltration
Site specific exploration to provide data for foundation design
recommendations should be conducted for any multifamily commercial or
golf course structures when their final location and configuration have
been established

Additional Exploration
This report is based upon a preliminary exploration for the
subject project The majority of the project site is densely vegetated
and not accessible to truck mounted exploration equipment without
extensive trail clearing Therefore we recommend that additional
exploration work be performed when the grading design is completed in
critical areas which were not accessible such as the lake sites and
other deep cut areas to confirm the assumptions upon which our
preliminary recommendations are based The additional boring
exploration should also provide a better delineation of the sandy
deposit on site especially if sand mining is being considered
Design Review
Plans and specifications for the proposed construction should be
forwarded to the geotechnical engineer for review and written comment
prior to construction This review is needed to determine adherence to
the earthwork and foundation recommendations given herein If this
review is not made the geotechnical engineer cannot assume
responsibility for misinterpretation of the recommendations
Construction Monitoring
It is recommended that the geotechnical engineer be retained to
provide geotechnical engineering services during construction of the
sitework excavation and foundation phases of the work This is to
observe compliance with the design concepts specifications or
recommendations and to allow design changes in the event that
subsurface conditions differ from that anticipated prior to start of
construction The recommendations given in this report are contingent
upon such observations
Any imported material required should be non expansive tested and
approved by the geotechnical engineer prior to hauling to the site

Moisture content and unit weight determinations were performed on
selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil
properties The results of these tests are presented on the Boring Logs
at the appropriate sample depths
Five 5 Atterberg limits were performed on selected samples as an
aid to classifying and determining the index properties of the soils
These test results are presented on Plate B 1
Five 5 sieve analyses were performed on the underlying soil samples
obtained from the borings to evaluate the index properties of the soil
samples The test results are summarized and plotted on Plates B 2
through B 6 for visualization and comparison purposes
Seven 7 swell tests were performed on selected samples to determine
their swelling potential and as an aid in determining their foundation
support characteristics These test results are presented on Plate B 7
One 1 moisture density determination Modified Proctor was
performed to determine the compaction characteristics of the shallow
on site soils This test result is summarized on Plate B 8
Resistivity and pH tests were performed on selected tests to provide
information pertaining to the corrosion potential of the on site sand
soils These test results are shown on Plate B 8
Two 2 organic impurity tests ASTM C 40 were performed on selected
samples to determine the potential of deleterious organic matter These
test results are shown on Plate B 9
