INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Waialua Ward Improvements project for the Mililani Hawaii Stake in the
Waialua area on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project
site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
derived from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the design of foundations slabs on grade retaining
structures site preparation and pavements only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu encompassing approximately 604 square miles of land area
may be divided into 4 basic geographic provinces The provinces include
Mountains Mountains 3 Schofield Plateau and 4 Coastal Plain The Island
of Oahu was formed by the merging of basalt lavas erupted from the two prominent
volcanoes the Wai anae and Ko olau Volcanoes Of the two the Wai anae Volcano is the
older having breached the ocean surface nearly 4 million years ago The Ko olau Volcano
rose above sea level approximately 2 7 million years ago making it the younger of the
two volcanoes
In general the two volcanoes were built by the long term eruption of thinly bedded
aa and pahoehoe lava flows which were erupted from elongate and linear trending
volcanic vents associated with the prominent rift zones of each of the volcanoes The
Ko olau lavas banked against the lava flows of the Wai anae Volcano to form the Schofield
Plateau of the central interior portion of Oahu The Wai anae Mountains which form the
western third of the Island of Oahu contain the oldest basalt rock formations on the Island
of Oahu The Ko olau Mountains form the eastern two thirds of the island and include the
rock layers exposed along the extending from Waimanalo to Kahuku
During the final stages of eruption and continuing well after the cessation of the
Waianae and Ko olau eruptions the island mass began to subside thousands of feet under
GEOLABS INC
W O 4488 00 Page 5
the tremendous weight of the rock In addition to the subsidence of the island the force
of stream erosion worked in conjunction with the large scale fracture and slumping of
portions of the land mass to form the topography of today Between 850 000 to
70 000 years ago volcanic activity returned to the Island of Oahu with the eruption of ash
cinder and basalt lavas from vents located throughout the eastern and southeastern
portions of the island These most recent eruptions are collectively known as the Honolulu
Volcanic Series Subsequently erosion by streams and ocean waves have continued to
work on the exposed land surfaces to form the topography of the present day
The Island of Oahu is no longer sinking beneath the sea however the effects of the
early whole island subsidence combined with the added effect of major Pleistocene age
sea level fluctuations worked to create drowned river valley systems and the development
of coral reefs The coral reefs and lithified sand dunes may be observed exposed
subaerially on Oahu and also buried in submarine deposits well offshore of the island The
coral and sand dune deposits were formed in shallow offshore seas during periods of
higher and lower sea levels associated with the effects of the advance and retreat of
glaciers which controlled the volume of water contained in the seas Today some of the
shallow sea coralline deposits are visible on the flanks of mountains or buried beneath
terrestrial sediments evidence of the higher and lower stands of the sea in prehistoric time
GEOLOGY OF THE WAIALUA HALEIWA AREA
The Waialua Haleiwa area geology is typified by a section of recent alluvium and
calcareous marine deposits overlying the Koolau basalt basement rock The interbedding of
alluvial deposits of differing origins and the location of former ridges and stream valleys make
the geologic picture complex
The project site is located at the boundary between the Waialua Series WkA and the
Lahaina Series LaB The Waialua Series consists of moderately well drained soils on alluvial
fans developed from weathering of basic igneous rock In a representative profile the surface
layer is dark reddish brown silty clay about 12 inches thick The subsoil about 26 inches thick
is dark reddish brown silty clay that has subangular blocky structure The substratum is dark
GEOLABS INC

reddish brown mottled silty clay Permeability is moderate Runoff is slow and the erosion
hazard is no more than slight These soils are used for sugar cane truck crops orchards and
pasture
The Lahaina Series consists of well drained soils on uplands on the Island of Oahu
These soils developed in material weathered from basic igneous rock in a representative
profile the surface layer is dark reddish brown silty clay about 15 inches thick The subsoil
about 45 inches thick is dusky red and dark reddish brown subangular blocky silty clay and
silty clay loam The substratum is soft weathered basic igneous rock Permeability is
moderate Runoff is slow and the erosion hazard is slight These soils are used for sugar
cane and pineapple Small acreages are used for truck crops pasture home sites and wildlife
habitat

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsoil conditions at the subject site were explored by drilling and sampling five
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 5 to depths ranging from about 5 to 20 feet
below the existing ground surface A bulk sample designated as Bulk 1 was obtained from
the near surface materials to evaluate its pavement support characteristics The approximate
locations of the drilled borings and bulk sample are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered stiff to very stiff clayey silts to depths of
about 8 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface Below the upper clayey silt soils our
borings encountered very stiff to hard saprolite and highly weathered basalt rock extending to
the maximum depth explored of approximately 20 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration It
should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to fluctuation due to rainfall events
seasonal precipitation groundwater withdrawal and other factors occurring off site
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered in the borings are presented on the
Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5 of Appendix A Results of the laboratory tests
performed on selected soil samples are presented in Appendix B
DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by stiff to very stiff
Clayey soils overlying saprolite and highly weathered basalt rock at greater depths Based on
the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we believe that shallow foundations
consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing on the recompacted in situ stiff soils
or compacted fill materials may be used to support the proposed new additions planned for
the Waialua Ward facility New footings located adjacent to the existing building should be
lowered down to the same depths as the footings of the existing building to avoid surcharging
the existing foundations

Based on our laboratory tests the near surface clayey soils exhibit slight to low
shrink swell potential when subjected to normal fluctuations in the soil moisture contents To
reduce the potential for distress to the future lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from the
swelling of the near surface soils we recommend that the subgrade soils be scarified to a
depth of about 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Use
of the vibratory action of a compactor should be carefully controlled to avoid inducing a
pumping subgrade condition The slab subgrade should be kept moist and free of shrinkage
cracks prior to placement of cushion fill and or slab concrete Our geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertaining to the design of foundations slabs on grade retaining structures

Building Foundations
Based on the generally stiff subsoil conditions encountered at the site we believe that
shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used for support of the proposed new
construction An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may
be used for the design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted stiff clayey soils or
compacted fills needed to achieve the final design grades This bearing value is for dead plus
live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those caused
by wind or seismic forces
For footings bearing on the in situ stiff clayey soils or compacted fill material the bottom
of footing excavations should be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
prior to placement of reinforcing steel and concrete If soft and or loose materials are
encountered at the bottom of footing excavations the material should be over excavated to
expose the underlying firm materials The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with
select granular materials compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction In lieu
of backfilling the over excavation with compacted granular fill the footing bottom may be
extended down to the underlying competent material
GEOLABS INC
W O 4488 00 Page 9
It should be emphasized that if the footing subgrades are saturated or softened after
the visual observations probing or compaction testing due to inclement weather the footing
subgrades may need to be over excavated to expose firm ground and backfilled with
compacted fill As an alternative to backfilling with compacted fill the bottom of footing may
be extended deeper to bear on the firm over excavated subgrade
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finish grades New footings located adjacent to the existing building should be
lowered to the same depths as the existing building footings or deeper to avoid surcharging
the existing foundations The actual depths of the existing footings should be confirmed in the
field during construction by the project geotechnical engineer
Footings located adjacent to planned retaining walls should be embedded deep enough
to avoid surcharging the retaining wail foundations or the retaining walls should be designed
to resist the additional structural loads If foundations are located next to utility trenches or
easements they should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward
from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade structures with additional
structural loads and to reduce the potential for foundation settlement
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance developed
between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A coefficient of friction of 0 3
may be used for footings bearing on the in situ stiff soils or compacted fills Resistance due
to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds
per square foot per foot of depth pcf This assumes that the soil around footings is well
compacted The passive pressure should be reduced for foundations located on slopes
Unless covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil
should be neglected

Slabs On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by near surface
clayey soils with slight to low expansion potential when subjected to normal moisture
fluctuations To reduce the potential for future distress to the lightly loaded slabs on grade
resulting from shrinking and swelling of the clayey soils due to changes in the moisture
content we recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared in accordance with
the recommendations presented in the Site Preparation section of this report The prepared
subgrade soils should be wetted and kept moist prior to the final placement of concrete
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the
soils be thoroughly moistened to close the cracks Saturation and subsequent yielding of the
exposed subgrade due to inclement weather and poor drainage may require over excavation
of the soft areas and replacement with well compacted engineered fill
For interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a
minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation be placed below the slabs to serve as a capillary break and to provide more uniform
support of the slabs To reduce moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor
coverings an impervious moisture vapor barrier such as a plastic membrane is
recommended on top of the No 3B Fine gravel Two inches of moist sand may be considered
above the plastic membrane to provide protection of the membrane and to aid in curing of the
slab concrete Provisions of the 2 inches of moist sand above the vapor membrane will be left
up to the discretion of the project structural engineer Flexible floor coverings such as carpet
or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask minor slab cracking

The thickened edges of slab adjacent to unpaved areas should be embedded at
least 18 inches below the lowest adjacent finished grade Areas adjacent to the slab should
be backfilled tightly against the slab with on site soil and graded to divert water away from the
slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the foundations
Exterior concrete walkways will be required for the proposed project We recommend

aggregate subbase The select granular fill or aggregate subbase should be compacted to
at least 90 percent relative compaction Crack control joints should be provided at intervals
equal to the width of the walkways with expansion joints at right angle intersections
Other Design Consideration
Several large avocado and mango trees were observed within the footprint of the
proposed new addition on the northwest side of the existing building The growth and decay
of these root systems may cause potential distress to the future building slab on grade and
foundation systems such as upheaval cracking and or settlement Therefore we
recommend that the proposed addition be located such that these trees are set back as far
away as possible from the building footprints We understand that some of the existing trees
will need to be removed to accommodate the proposed construction The depression resulting
from the removal of the existing trees should be properly backfilled in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Site Preparation section of this report

Drainage
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build up of hydrostatic pressures
A typical drainage system would consist of a 1 to 2 foot wide zone of permeable
material such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation placed
immediately around a perforated pipe perforations facing down at the base of the wall
discharging to an appropriate outlet or weepholes As an alternative a prefabricated
drainage product such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain may be used instead of the
drainage material The prefabricated drainage product should also be hydraulically
connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of the on site clayey silt or
select granular fill material Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of
backfill should consist of low expansion relatively impervious soils to reduce water
infiltration behind the walls

landscaping irrigation For long term performance we recommend that a subdrain system be
constructed adjacent to paved landscaped areas
Surface Water Run Off Disposal System
Five in situ falling head permeability tests were conducted at Boring Nos 1 through 3
and 5 to evaluate the infiltration characteristics of the subsoils to provide data for design of the
facilities to be used for disposal of storm water run off
Based on the permeability tests conducted the calculated permeability of the tested
subsoils range from about 1 8 x 10 to 4 9 x 10 feet per minute The results of our
permeability tests are presented on Plates C 1 through C 4 of Appendix C Due to the
potential for variability of the subsoil conditions the absorption capacity of the proposed
disposal system should be confirmed by additional percolation tests during construction
The planned surface water run off disposal facilities should be located away from the
building foundations since water leaching into the ground from the disposal facilities could
cause subsurface soil saturation and subsequent loss of bearing strength of the foundation
soils resulting in foundation settlement and building distress
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the proposed project In general
for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches
of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used under the pipes Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also
be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate
support around the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the
possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce the
GEOLABS INC
W O 4488 00 Page 18
potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support
for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and
damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to the
top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of select fill material The backfill should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in maximum 8 inch level loose
lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce
the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to at
least 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems be
installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation and
pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also
be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to reduce the potential for water infiltration into the subsoils Drainage swales should
be provided as soon as possible and maintained to drain surface run off away from the slabs
and foundations
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation and
subsequent loss of strength and or heaving of the near surface clayey soils The foundation
excavations should be properly backfilled against the footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration

the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered are
provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 5

OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM
Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are

depths
