PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering reconnaissance was to obtain a general
overview of the surface conditions at the project site to develop a generalized soil rock data
set for the formulation of geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the site
grading and design of embankments To accomplish this we conducted an exploration
program consisting of the following tasks and work efforts
1 Research and review of available in house soils and geologic information
pertaining to the project site and its vicinity
2 Reconnaissance of the project site to observe the surrounding site conditions
by an engineer from our firm
3 Laboratory testing of the proposed fill materials obtained during the field
reconnaissance as an aid in classifying the materials and evaluating the
engineering properties
4 Analyses of the field and laboratory data for the formulation of geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertaining to the proposed project
5 Preparation of this letter report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and recommendations
6 Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting word processing and clerical
support
REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the northern flank of Hualalai Mountain which has
formed the west central portion of the island The project site is underlain by basaltic lava
flows of the Hualalai Volcanic Series which were deposited during the Holocene Epoch
Stearns and Macdonald 1946
The Hualalai Volcanic Series is typically composed of olivine basaltic lava flows The
project site is covered with ash soils The ash soils at the project site differ from Pahala Ash
found in other portions of the Island of Hawaii in that the in situ moisture content is generally
lower and the soil exists in a dry friable state

The soils at the site have been subjected to wind erosion which has resulted in the
formation of a desert pavement on the surface of the site This desert pavement is a surficial
layer of cobbles and gravel which were left behind as the finer ash was blown away
The lava formation at the site may consist of a a and pahoehoe basalt type flows
which spread and ponded as it approached the ocean A a is typically characterized by a
porous rough and irregular flow surface resembling a jagged accumulation of rock
fragments including cobbles and boulders Typically a more dense and layered lava rock
material is contained within the lava flow core Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth
rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are
commonly encountered in pahoehoe lavas These cavities were formed when the lavas were
still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and
pockets inter flow cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the
cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance
longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
SITE RECONNAISSANCE
The proposed irrigation lake is located in the Mauna Lani Resort in the Waikoloa area
in the District of South Kohala on the Island of Hawaii A general site reconnaissance was
performed on June 22nd for the irrigation lake to evaluate the existing site conditions and the
proposed fill materials for the construction of the embankments
The site is adjacent to the existing sewer treatment plant Based on our observations
the site is generally level Based on the available topographic survey map the existing
ground surface elevation ranges from about 47 to 54 feet MSL The surface conditions
at the southern portion of the site consist of volcanic ash with some basalt cobbles and
gravel The surface conditions at the northern portion of the site consist mainly of volcanic
ash with varying amounts of cobbles and gravel In localized areas the volcanic ash may
be about 2 feet thick An existing slope with an inclination of about 2H 1V was observed
along the chain link fence at the south end of the site The existing slope consists of basalt
fragments with some sand and silt The soils encountered at the project site were generally
dry in nature
A majority of the project site is covered by large kiawe trees and dried wild grasses
Stockpiles of dried kiawe wood were observed throughout the site A large pile of dried
kiawe wood was observed at the north central portion of the site Waste materials were
scattered throughout the north and central portions of the site Several small shacks were
noted near the northern portion of the project site A dirt road traverses the southern end of
the site
Based on our observation of the proposed fill materials the fill materials consist
mainly of basalt sandy gravel with cobbles Boulders about 3 feet in maximum dimension
were observed in the fill materials In addition PVC metals vegetation and asphalt and

LABORATORY TESTING
Soil samples from the proposed fill materials were tested in the laboratory with an
emphasis on evaluating the gradation and moisture density relationship The following
laboratory tests were performed on samples of the fill material obtained from the field
reconnaissance
One sieve analysis test was performed on a selected soil sample to evaluate the
gradation characteristics and to aid in soil classification The test was performed in
accordance with ASTM Test Designations C 136 and D 1140 Graphic presentation of the
grain size distribution is provided on Plate 3
One Modified Proctor compaction test was performed on a selected soil sample to
determine the dry density and moisture content relationship The test was performed in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Results of the test are presented on
Plate 4
FINDINGS AND DISCUSSIONS
Based on our site reconnaissance the project site is underlain by volcanic ash soils
with varying amounts of basalt cobbles and gravel near the ground surface Localized areas
at the northem portion of the project site may consist entirely of volcanic ash soils
We recommend that a minimum 12 inch thick choker layer be placed on the
embankment slopes and on the bottom of the irrigation lake In addition we recommend that
a minimum 24 inch thick choker layer to facilitate excavation of the anchoring trench for
the impervious liner be placed on the top of the irrigation lake embankments The choker
layer should be finished to a relatively smooth surface and free of sharp particles to prevent
puncturing of the impervious liner
Based on our observation of the proposed fill materials we believe that the
embankment slopes may be designed at a maximum inclination of 2H 1V However to
facilitate the placement of the impervious liner and geotextile fabric we recommend that
embankment slopes be designed at an inclination of 3H 1V Detailed discussion of these
items and our geotechnical engineering design recommendations are presented in the
following sections of this letter report

Site Grading
Based on the available plans we understand that embankment fill for the irrigation
lake will be on the order of about 8 feet high In addition some minor cuts will be required
to achieve the design finished grades In general a minimum 12 inch thick choker layer
is recommended on the embankment slopes and on the bottom of the irrigation lake To
facilitate the excavation of the anchoring trench for the impervious liner we recommend that
a minimum 24 inch thick choker layer be placed on the top of the embankments The
choker layer serves to choke off the larger sized voids in the underlying general fill and to
provide a relatively smooth surface for the placement of the impervious liner Items of

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site to reduce the potential
for contamination of the excavated materials
After clearing and grubbing finished subgrades in cut areas and areas designated to
receive fills should be scarified to a minimum depth of about 8 inches moisture
conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density
of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture
is the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry
density
lf soft and or loose soils are encountered at the finished subgrades in cut areas and
areas designated to receive fills these materials should be over excavated to the
underlying firm materials The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted granular fill materials We recommend that the contract documents

Fill Materials
Based on our observations of the proposed fill materials we believe that the materials
are suitable for use as fill for the proposed project provided that deleterious materials
are removed As previously mentioned deleterious materials such vegetation PVC
and metal pieces were observed within the fill materials In addition we recommend
that the stockpiles of broken up concrete pieces and volcanic ash with some cobbles
gravel and mulch should not be used as fill materials for the embankments
As previously mentioned we recommend that the slopes of the embankments and
the bottom of the irrigation lake be capped with a minimum 12 inch thick choker
layer over the larger sized general fill below and to provide a relatively smooth surface
for the placement of the impervious liner In addition a minimum 24 inch thick
choker layer is recommended at the top of the embankments to facilitate the
excavation of the anchoring trench for the impervious liners The choker fill
materials should consist of well graded granular materials less than 6 inches in
maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the
compacted mass Imported material should be observed and or tested by Geolabs
for its suitability prior to being transported to the site
General fill and backfill materials below the choker fill capping layer may consist of
well graded granular materials of which the majority portion are less than 12 inches
in size with an absolute maximum dimension of 18 inches
The fill materials consisting of boulders less than 4 feet in maximum dimension may
be used as rock fill or disposed of in the general fill provided that the top of the rock
fill layer is placed at least 4 feet below the finished subgrades In general placement
of boulders in fills should be in accordance with the procedures recommended in the
following Boulder Disposal subsection
Boulder Disposal
Boulders i e rock fragments larger than 12 inches in maximum size but less than

provided that the top of the rock fill layer is placed at least 4 feet below the
finished subgrades Boulders larger than 4 feet in maximum dimension should
not be used as boulder fill unless reduced in size

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirement
Choker fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture and compacted to
at least 90 percent relative compaction General fills and backfills should be placed
in level lifts not exceeding 18 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water
content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other
types of acceptable compaction equipment
Rock fills should be placed in lifts not exceeding the maximum dimension of the
rocks or 4 feet whichever is less Placement should be by haul trucks traversing
previously placed lifts and dumping at the edge of the currently placed lift Spreading
of the rock fill should be by bulldozer to facilitate seating of the rock The rock fill
should be watered heavily during placement by water trucks traversing in front of the
current rock lift face and spraying water continuously during placement Compaction
equipment such as a Caterpillar D 9 bulldozer or similar heavy compaction
equipment providing suitable energy to achieve the required compaction should be
utilized The number of passes to be made to achieve proper compaction should be
determined at the on set of site grading by the performance of a trial compaction test
or by plate bearing tests
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rocks boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number
of passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should
be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation

Fill Slopes
Based on our observation of the proposed project site and the fill materials we
believe that the fill slopes may be placed as steep as 2H 1V However to facilitate
the placement of the impervious liner and geotextile fabric on the slopes of the
irrigation lake we recommend that slopes be constructed with an inclination of 3H 1V
Fills placed on existing slopes having inclinations steeper than 5H 1V should be keyed
and benched into firm and stable materials to enhance the stability of the new
embankment fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest point
and continue up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the above
recommendations for fill placement Fill slopes should be constructed by over filling
past the design limits of the slope and cutting back to the design slope to provide
adequate compaction at the edges of the slope The embankment slopes should be
finished to a relatively smooth surface and free of sharp particles which may puncture
the impervious liner to be installed
Impervious Liner and Underlining
We understand that a synthetic liner will be provided for the irrigation lake to reduce
the potential for water infiltration through the underlying material It is our opinion that the
material type performance installation and protection details of the impervious liner should
be designed in accordance with the recommendations by the liner manufacturer It is critical
that the liner maintain its integrity to prevent saturation and build up of seepage water
pressures within the fill embankment
Our experience with similar synthetic liner installations indicate that there is sometimes
a tendency for the liners to become detached at the top of the water impoundment It
appears that this may be the result of inadequate anchoring of the top of the liner and
insufficient flexibility of the liner material We recommend that the liner manufacturer be
consulted regarding proper construction detailing and installation of the liner with particular
attention to proper anchoring to reduce the potential of liner failure Effects of fluctuating
water levels on the performance of the impervious liner near the top anchoring should also
be considered
Technical representatives of the manufacturers of the impervious liner should be
required to be on site at all times during their installation to assure that proper construction
procedures and precautions are followed A sales representative of the supplier s may not
have the technical expertise to adequately perform this function

An underlining consisting of a heavy duty geotextile fabric or 4 inches of No 3B Fine

materials The use of a geotextile fabric is preferred over the gravel cushion due to the ease

Slope Stabilit
Based on the soil rock conditions encountered in our field reconnaissance and the
maximum recommended fill slope inclination of 2H 1V it is our opinion that the proposed
slopes for the irrigation lake should have adequate factors of safety against sliding and
rotational failures during seismic activity
