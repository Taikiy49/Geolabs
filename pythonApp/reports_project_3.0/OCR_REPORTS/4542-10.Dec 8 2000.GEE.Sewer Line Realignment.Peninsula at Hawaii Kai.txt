INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed sewer line realignment project located in the Hawaii Kai area on the Island
of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the
Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering recommendations
resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses These
recommendations are intended for the design of the sewer line realignment only
Recommendations pertaining to mass grading and site improvements will be issued under
separate covers The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of the
western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still
active and its eastern flank is partially buried below the Koolau lavas in central Oahu
The project site is located on the southern flank of Koolau and its geomorphology and
subsurface conditions are directly related to glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea level
during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain The
subsurface conditions within the project area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age
coral algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea which are
intercalated with terrigenous sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is
Koolau Basalt
During the later Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to the
present sea level of Oahu About 15 000 years ago a relatively rapid rise in sea level
occurred During that rise the valleys in the project area were drowned In the last
10 000 years or so the sea level has adjusted to its present stand Terrigenous and marine
sediments have continued to accumulate in low energy estuarine or lagoonal environments
resulting in thick deposits of soft loose sediments along the coast in the areas that were
formerly valleys and drainageways
With the urbanization of the area man made fills were placed to reclaim marshy areas
and lagoons The fill was placed upon lagoonal deposits consisting of silty sands with coral
fragments The lower clayey material was deposited during periodic influxes of non marine
alluvial deposits that apparently smothered the coral reef development Land development
and reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought the area to its present form

Earth Pressure Loads on Pipes
Loads on buried pipes are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the
pipes the unit weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance between the
backfill material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads on the buried
sewer line pipes we recommend that an average unit weight of 110 pounds per
cubic foot pcf for the backfill material and a coefficient of friction of 0 25 be used
Earth forces upon the pipe generally increase rapidly with the width of the trench
Therefore the width of the trench should be kept to a minimum Traffic loads on the
buried pipes if applicable should also be considered for the portions of the pipes
located in roadway areas

and manhole structures The granular bedding should extend beyond the sides of

In general free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel

Pipe Settlement
Primary settlement of the new pipe installation is generally caused by the difference
in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted
backfill material placed over the pipeline The net increase in loading may cause
settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert Based on our
exploration and analyses small primary settlements on the order of about 1 inch
may be anticipated for the new sewer line alignment This is further discussed in
the Construction Considerations subsection of this report

Construction Considerations
The settlement estimates presented in the Pipe Settlement subsection assume
that proper construction procedures and good workmanship will be consistently
practiced by the contractor during pipeline installation operations Based on our
knowledge and past experience with similar soft loose soils significant settlement
of the pipes may be attributed to the construction methodology and equipment used
by the contractor Therefore special attention should be given to the installation
procedures for the proposed sewer lines especially in soft loose soil conditions to
reduce the potential for settlement distress in the future
As previously recommended the sewer lines should generally be supported by
approximately 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel bedding layer over a subgrade
stabilization layer consisting of No 2 Rock wrapped in a non woven filter fabric
The non woven filter fabric is used to provide separation between the coarse
aggregate No 2 Rock and the fine grained soil In general a minimum overlap
of 2 feet should be provided between the ends of each roll of the filter fabric placed
along the pipe alignment under dry conditions If the filter fabric is not properly
overlapped it is possible that the coarse aggregate placed may migrate through the
openings in the filter fabric and mix with the underlying soft soils This may result
in settlement of the pipes due to loss of cushion materials into the soft loose
subsoils
Considering the depth of the excavations we anticipate that the stabilization layer
installation below the pipe invert may be performed in wet conditions Therefore
we envision that it would be difficult to observe the fabric installation process below
the groundwater level In addition we believe that it would be difficult for the
contractor to assure that the proper minimum overlap is maintained Therefore we
believe that if placement of the filter fabric in wet conditions is necessary the seams
of the fabric should be sewn in lieu of overlapping to provide a higher assurance
that gaps will not occur in the filter fabric As a result we recommend that sewing
GEOLABS INC
W O 4542 10 Page 14
the seams of the filter fabric be incorporated into the construction procedure to
reduce the potential for large scale settlements of the pipes
Due to the soft loose subsoil conditions anticipated near the invert elevations care
should also be exercised during the backfilling operations of the trench excavation
Heavy equipment and vibratory action of the compactor may exert high pressures
onto the pipe and supporting soils during the trench backfilling operations resulting
in potential settlement of the soft loose deposits and pipe during construction
Therefore the size of the equipment used to compact the backfill around and above
the pipe should to be limited to less than 4 tons in weight

A monitoring program should be developed and implemented by the contractor to
detect ground movement and or subsidence adjacent to the existing sewer line considering
the soft loose subsurface soil conditions at the site Ground movement and or subsidence
which may result in damage to nearby sewer line and pavements may occur as a result
of the shoring and dewatering operations It should be noted that during and after
installation of the sheet piles minor settlements and ground movements might occur
Therefore it is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained by the
contractor to design and evaluate the shoring system used
Excavation Method
It is anticipated that conventional earthmoving equipment may be used to excavate
the sewer line excavations based on the subsurface conditions encountered
The method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the
contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations
The excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal
to the depth of the excavation measured from the edge of the excavation in order
to reduce the potential for appreciable ground movement Due to the presence of
soft loose subsoils at the site the excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer
GEOLABS INC
W O 4542 10 Page 18
than 15 feet from existing sewer line to reduce the potential for ground movement
or subsidence which may damage the underground sewer line
Excavation Support
We anticipate that excavation depths up to about 15 feet may be required in
localized areas Based on the subsoil conditions encountered in our borings we
believe that shoring of the sides of the excavations will be necessary to protect
personnel working in the excavations Temporary shoring of the excavations using
inter locking steel sheet piles especially where dewatering of the excavations will
be necessary may be considered Other methods of excavation support and
groundwater control methods such as jet grouting may also be considered
Where inter locking steel sheet pile shoring is used it would also serve as a cut off
wall for the dewatering operations Therefore the sheet piles should be installed
to a sufficient depth to reduce the potential for areal ground subsidence and to
reduce the amount of dewatering within the excavations due to the relatively
permeable subsoils at the site It should be noted that the deeper excavations will
likely encounter the soft loose soil deposits at the bottom of the excavation There
is a strong potential for bottom heave in these soft loose soil conditions Therefore
the contractor should carefully evaluate the potential for bottom heave and design
the shoring system accordingly A minimum factor of safety of 1 5 should be
provided in evaluating shoring stability and bottom heave of the excavation
It is important that adequate steel sheet piles be installed prior to excavation and
maintained tight against the excavation walls with proper bracing during excavation
The properly braced sheet pile is essential to reduce the potential for lateral
movements of the adjacent ground into the excavation which may result in potential
settlements or distress to adjacent structures or other improvements such as the
roads or utilities
GEOLABS INC
W O 4542 10 Page 19
The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His her representative who should be required to
be continuously present on site during excavation and construction work will have
the best opportunity to promptly observe changing conditions during construction
such as unforeseen subsoils unexpectedly high groundwater table inappropriate
construction sequence or techniques etc which may affect shoring stability
However it must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and
the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during
excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to accurately
estimate the magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon
workmanship such as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are
installed on the subsoil conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of
excavation In addition it should be noted that settlement of the new pipeline might
occur as a result of the vibrations generated during the extraction of the sheet pile
shoring Therefore special attention should be given by the contractor during the
sheet pile removal process to reduce the potential for pipeline settlement
Therefore it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed
properly and as early as practical if necessary and that the adjacent ground should
be continuously monitored for cracks dips and or other indications of movements
with instruments
Dewatering
We envision that dewatering will be required for the installation of the new sewer line
Groundwater was encountered at depths ranging from about 3 3 feet and 1 2 feet MSL in
the borings during our field exploration We estimate that the high groundwater table may
be as high as about 1 feet MSL Based on the groundwater levels anticipated and the
nature of the soils to be encountered during the excavations dewatering will be required for
this project Therefore dewatering provisions will need to be included in the contract
documents for the proposed construction Since the excavation will involve discharge of
GEOLABS INC
W O 4542 10 Page 20
groundwater a National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System NPDES permit may be
necessary The contractor should consult their independent consultant for the latest
regulations and information pertaining to the permit application
Based on the anticipated depths of the sewer lines and manhole structures and the
relatively shallow groundwater table dewatering with cut off walls such as inter locking
sheet piles with the use of tremie concrete plugs may be considered However sumps will
be needed to collect water that percolates up into the base of the excavation or infiltrates
through the sheet piles The sheet piles should be driven to a sufficient depth to reduce
the potential for areal ground subsidence and to reduce the amount of dewatering within
the excavations in areas underlain by granular subsoils with generally high permeability
Use of an inter locking sheet pile shoring support system with dewatering with tremie
concrete plug is relatively watertight which should allow the groundwater levels outside the
excavations to be maintained close to the original pre construction levels Therefore some
type of groundwater control requirement should be specified in the contract documents
Dewatering for construction is the responsibility of the contractor The selection of
equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor and he she
should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be required during
construction depending on the conditions encountered The dewatering method selected
should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the proposed
excavation The dewatering operations should be coordinated with the trench shoring
support such that the stability of the excavations is not jeopardized The operations should
be carried out without softening the bottom of the excavations
Dewatering Considerations
It is our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents should
be written to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater
table and or to exclude water from the excavations to allow construction of the
proposed structures under safe and dry conditions These works or systems may
GEOLABS INC
W O 4542 10 Page 21
include but are not limited to pumping We suggest that the following three basic
criteria be considered in selection of a suitable method of dewatering
a The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or
damage to existing roads utilities and the environment
b The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide
safe and dry working conditions in the excavation
Cc The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow
modifications to accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering
It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained by the
contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His her qualified representative who should be
continuously present on site during dewatering activities will have the best
opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during construction and
to implement as soon as possible necessary precautionary or remedial measures
including but not limited to stopping or slowing down the dewatering operations
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils may
be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations should
be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the excavation
bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be performed
without pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and should be
coordinated with the shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not
adversely affected Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in
blowing or heaving of the excavation bottom or sides

W O 4542 10 Page 22
Groundwater drawdown outside the excavation will cause additional settlement
resulting from consolidation of the loose soft compressible soils Therefore the
use of a deep well system outside the excavations to draw down the groundwater
level should not be allowed
Special caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches connected
to the excavations If this occurs the granular bedding and or backfill in the utility
trenches could act as subdrains and cause significant areal groundwater drawdown
resulting in settlements and potential damage to utility lines and or other existing
adjacent structures
Existing Sewer Force Main
As previously mentioned the new sewer line will be connected to the existing sewer
force main at the northwestern corner of the project site We understand that Pipe A 11 will
connect to the existing sewer force main on a bend without a manhole Installation of the
new sewer line will require excavation of up to about 15 feet deep and dewatering In
addition placement of new fills and a seawall are also planned near the existing sewer force
main The presence of additional loading may potentially cause substantial amount of
ground subsidence and have adverse effects to the existing sewer force main Therefore
special provisions should be made for the portion of the existing sewer force main to the west
of the Pipe A 11 connection
In order to provide support from the additional loads we recommend that pipe piles be
installed underneath the existing sewer force main These piles are designed to underpin
the existing sewer line to provide additional bearing support necessary to stabilize the pipe
The pipe pile system should be installed beneath the existing sewer force main from the
connection point of Pipe A 11 and the existing sewer force main to the western edge of the
property line
Based on our borings we anticipate that the site is underlain by surface fills and
soft loose lagoonal deposits at shallow depths The lagoonal deposits in this area are
GEOLABS INC

typically underlain by dense to very dense cinder sands at about Elevation 10 to 15 feet
MSL The cinder sands are generally underlain by medium hard to hard volcanic tuff
formation at about Elevation 15 to 20 feet MSL We estimate that pipe piles extending to
depths of about 10 to 15 feet below the existing sewer force main invert will be required to
underpin the sewer pipe An allowable vertical compressive loading of 15 kips per 6 inch
pipe pile may be used The pipe piles should be driven down into the hard volcanic tuff
formation
It should be noted that appropriate construction procedures and good workmanship
should be consistently practiced by the contractor during installation of the mini piles under
the live sewer force main Based on our knowledge and past experience with similar
soft loose soils significant settlement of the pipes may be attributed to the construction
methodology and equipment used by the contractor Therefore special attention should
be given to the pipe pile installation procedures under the existing force main especially
in soft loose soil conditions to reduce the potential for settlement distress in the future

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
ten borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 10 to depths of about 25 to 66 5 feet below
the existing ground surface Boring Nos 8 and 9 were drilled in Parcel B where the sewer
line will be realigned at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and
rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop
The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch
drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the volcanic tuff materials encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the tuff formation The tuff material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
