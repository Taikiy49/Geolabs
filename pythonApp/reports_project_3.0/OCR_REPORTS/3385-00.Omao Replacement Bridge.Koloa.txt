INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Omao Replacement Bridge project located to the north of the
intersection between Omao Road and Koloa Road in the Koloa area on the island of Kauai
Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical engineering recommen
dations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering analyses for
the proposed replacement bridge project These recommendations are intended for design
of foundations site grading and pavements only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Kauai is composed of a single basalt shield volcano built by the
extrusion of lavas of the Waimea Canyon Volcanic Series during the late Pliocene Epoch
more than 2 million years before present Following the cessation of this main shield
building phase there was renewed volcanic activity with the extrusion of basaltic lavas of
the post erosional Koloa Volcanic Series and the concurrent deposition of the alluvial
sediments of the Palikea Formation
Rocks of the Koloa Volcanic series cover most of the eastern half of the island of
Kauai and are generally characterized as thick flows of dense basalt extruded from groups
of vents aligned in north south trends in various locales Associated with the vents are
pyroclastic materials which usually form low cinder cones at the vent
During the Pleistocene Epoch there were many sea level changes as a result of
widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great continental
glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill
the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global sea levels rose
because more water was available The land mass of Kauai remained essentially stable
during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic
fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to
present sea level on Kauai

W Page 6
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and lagoonal marine sediments in the quiet waters
protected by fringing reefs
In the area of the project alluvial deposits have accumulated on the basalt rock
formation along Omao Stream The alluvial deposits generally consist of silty clays clayey
silts sands and gravels with some cobbles and boulders In addition some fills appeared
to have been placed in the area for the development of Omao Road

STREAM BED MATERIAL FOR SCOUR ANALYSIS
Scour is the result of erosive action of flowing water excavating and carrying away
material from the bed and banks of streams Total Scour generally consists of three
components 1 Aggregation and Degradation 2 Contraction Scour and 3 Local Scour
The rates of scour depend on a number of factors such as shape and dimensions of a pier
or abutment depth of flow velocity of approach flow size and gradation of stream bed
material bed configuration etc
One of the factors affecting the scour depth is the grain size characteristics of the
stream bed material The median diameter of the stream bed material Ds is used to
calculate the depth of flow the flow velocity and fall velocity of stream bed material in scour
depth analysis In order to evaluate the size and gradation of the stream bed material for
scour depth analysis at the project site a bulk sample of the stream bed material was
retrieved for our laboratory tests The stream bed material generally consists of brown silty
sand with gravel A sieve analysis with hydrometer test was performed on the retrieved
stream bed soil sample for grain size distribution of the material Based on the results of
our laboratory tests the median diameter of the stream bed material Ds is approximately
0 3mm The results of our laboratory tests on the stream bed material are presented on
Plate B 1 in Appendix B of this report and were provided to the project civil engineer for the
scour depth analysis

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our exploration the estimated scour depth and the structural loads
provided we recommend that a deep foundation system consisting of 48 inch diameter
cast in place drilled shafts be used to support the proposed replacement bridge The drilled
shafts would extend through the estimated scour elevation and be embedded into the stiff
alluvial soils and basalt rock formation
We envision that site grading would consist only of minor cuts and fills on the order
of less than 2 feet relative to the existing ground surface for the proposed project In
addition we anticipate that excavations on the order of 5 to 7 feet and subsequent
backfilling will be needed for construction of the new abutment walls

cast in place drilled shafts extending through the estimated scour elevation into the stiff

only from adhesion between the drilled shaft and the surrounding soils basalt rock The
end bearing component of the drilled shafts has been discounted due to the potential for
caving in of the loose soft soils which would preclude obtaining a clean bottom during
construction

Drilled Shaft Construction Considerations
In general the performance of drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of installation and construction procedures The load bearing
capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the contact between the shaft and
the surrounding soil rock Therefore proper construction techniques are important The
contractor should exercise care in drilling the shaft holes and in placing concrete into the
holes The subsoils at the bridge site consist predominantly of alluvial soils with cobbles
near the ground surface Drilling through the cobbles may be slow and may require special
tools In addition the drilled shafts are designed to be embedded into the basalt formation
therefore coring into the hard basalt formation may be necessary
Due to the relatively shallow groundwater and gravelly soils encountered cave in of
the material may occur during the drilling operation Therefore to reduce the potential for
caving in of the holes temporary casing may be needed The anticipated high groundwater
levels may also pose further construction difficulties since proper observation of the sides
and bottom of the shaft may not be possible Therefore a representative of
Geolabs Hawaii should be present at the site to observe the drilling and installation of drilled
shafts during construction Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is not recommended
because use of the drilling fluids may reduce the friction between the drilled shaft and
surrounding soils rock
A low shrink high slump 7 to 9 inch slump range concrete mix should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils and basalt
formation The concrete should be placed promptly after drilling within 24 hours to reduce
the potential for caving in The concrete should also be placed in a suitable manner to
reduce segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix Since groundwater will likely

Site Grading
The extent of grading required to construct the proposed replacement bridge will
consist of site preparation and minor grading to achieve final grades and excavation and
backfill for the abutment walls footings In general all grading work should conform to
Section 200 of the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works
Construction 1994 and the site specific recommendations contained in this report
Site grading operations should be observed by a qualified geotechnical engineer It
is important that the engineer observe the site grading to evaluate whether any undesirable
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3385 00 Page 16
materials are encountered during the excavation and scarification process and whether the
exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered in our exploration
In general areas that are to receive fills should be cleared of all vegetation and
deleterious materials The resulting grub spoil material should be disposed of properly off
site Any soft weak or yielding areas disclosed during clearing operations should be over
excavated to expose firm ground and the resulting excavation should be filled with
engineered fill compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction After clearing
and grubbing all subgrades should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches properly
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry
density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with AASHTO T 180 Test Methods Optimum moisture is the
water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
The on site materials may be reused as a source of fill and backfill materials
provided that they are free of organic materials and contain no lumps or fragments greater
than 3 inches in greatest dimension Cobbles and boulders if encountered should not be
used as backfill material Where used as fill material the on site soils should be placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Backfills behind abutment walls should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction since the backfill is intended to support the roadway
Any imported soil should be select fill such as crushed coral mudrock basalt or
cinder sand The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger

LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted in this report are based in part upon
information obtained from field borings Variations of conditions between the field borings
may occur and the nature and extent of these variations may not become evident until
construction is underway If variations then appear evident it will be necessary to
reevaluate the recommendations presented in this report
The locations of the field borings indicated in this report are approximate having
been estimated by taping from visible features shown on the topographic map dated
provided by Portugal Associates Inc Elevations of the borings were
interpolated between the contours shown on the same topographic map The locations and
elevations of the field borings should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling two
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 at the approximate locations shown on the Site
Plan Pilate 2 The depths of our borings were about 55 5 and 62 feet below the existing
ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing
in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the logs of borings at the
appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material in
excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

Laboratory Testing

samples of the soils to evaluate its strength characteristics Results of the test are
summarized on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths

soils to evaluate its strength characteristics Results of the test are presented on Plate B 2
