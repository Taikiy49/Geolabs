INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Proposed New Theaters project at the existing Koko Marina Shopping
Center in Hawaii Kai on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of
the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene in age When the building of the Waianae and Koolau volcanoes came to an
end only a slight embayment existed on the southern coast of Oahu
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Koolau Range and its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio eustatic
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3873 00 Page 5
fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age The base of the
stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt The Koolau volcanic shield was built by
thinly bedded lava flows during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epochs The
main shield building activities ceased approximately 2 million years ago
After a long period of cessation of the shield building volcanic activity returned
with the formation of a series of lava flows cinder cones and tuff cones such as those
associated with Koko Crater Many of the eruptions were rather explosive in nature due
to the interaction of groundwater with the rising magma resulting in steam explosions
which expelled large quantities of pyroclastic material predominantly ash and cinder
Some explosive eruptions produced ash that cemented together to form a firm rock
known as tuff locally known as mudrock
During the Pleistocene Epoch sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation As the glaciers grew and advanced less water was available to fill
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea When
the glaciers melted and receded an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present sea level
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays accumulated reef deposits
at correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The lower sea stands caused streams to carve
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials Land development and
reclamation projects within the last century have brought the project site to its present
form

Footings Above Elevation 3 feet MSL
For footing subgrades above elevation 3 feet MSL the footing subgrade should
be scarified moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the
maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Where soft and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of these footing
excavations they should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm
materials The over excavation should be backfilled with granular material
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction or the footing should
be extended down to the underlying competent material The over excavated soils
may be used as a source of backfill material provided that they are free of
deleterious materials and are not too wet to achieve the required compaction
Footings Below Elevation 3 feet MSL
For footing subgrades below an elevation of 3 feet MSL the footing subgrade
may encounter soft subgrade soils and or groundwater In order to reduce the
potential for appreciable settlements due to the compressible subgrade soils we

recommend that the loose soft lagoonal deposits encountered at the footing
subgrade level be completely removed and replaced with free draining granular
fill such as No 3B Fine gravel or No 2 Rock wrapped in a filter fabric In
general the depth of the footing over excavation may be on the order of 3 to
5 feet below the bottom of footing elevation in order to expose firm natural
ground Additional over excavation may be required at localized areas to
remove pockets of the highly compressible loose lagoonal deposits encountered
in our field exploration In addition the bottom of the over excavation should
extend laterally a minimum of 18 inches beyond the edge of the footings to

Excavation
Based on our field exploration excavation depths exceeding 5 to 6 feet below the
existing ground surface will likely encounter groundwater It is anticipated that
conventional earthmoving equipment may be used to excavate the stiff soils encountered
in our borings The method and equipment used for excavation should be determined by
the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations The slopes and sides
of the excavations must comply with all applicable federal state and local requirements
In general the excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a lateral distance
equal to the depth of the excavation measured from the top edge of the excavation to
reduce the potential for ground movement toward the excavation
Where an open cut excavation is not possible the excavation for construction of
the structure will need to be adequately shored to reduce the potential for adjacent
ground movement The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with
all applicable safety requirements The adequacy and safety of the shoring installation
should be made the sole responsibility of the contractor His her representative who
should be required to be continuously present on site during excavation and construction
work will have the best opportunity to promptly observe any changing conditions during
construction such as unforeseen subsoil conditions unexpectedly high groundwater
table inappropriate construction sequence or techniques etc which may affect shoring
stability
It should be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in the earth stresses during excavation
Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to estimate accurately the
magnitude of movement The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship such
as how quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed on the subsoil
conditions the size of the excavation and the rate of excavation

Where dewatering is needed for construction of the proposed project the
selection of equipment and methods of dewatering should be left up to the contractor In
addition he she should be aware that modifications to the dewatering system may be
required during construction depending on the conditions encountered The dewatering
method selected should have minimal impact on the groundwater level surrounding the
proposed excavation In addition dewatering operations should be performed such that
only clear water is pumped Excessive pumping which removes soil fines may result in
a blowing or heaving of the excavation bottom or sides
It should be noted that the subsoils in the zone where dewatering will take place
consist of loose granular materials which may be considered relatively permeable It is
our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents should be written
to include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater table and or to

exclude the water from the excavations to allow construction of the proposed structures
under safe and dry conditions These works or systems may include but are not limited
to well pumping grouting cut off walls tremie concrete plugs or a combination of the
above and or other possible methods
Dewatering Considerations
We suggest that the following three basic criteria be considered in selection of a
suitable method of dewatering
e The dewatering method should result in the least disturbance or damage to
existing buildings roads and the environment
e The dewatering method should maintain stability of and also provide safe
and dry working conditions in the excavation
e The dewatering method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modifications
to accommodate various ground conditions
Dewatering Precaution and Monitoring
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to
dewatering It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained
by the contractor to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His her qualified representative who should be
required to be continuously present on site during dewatering activities will have
the best opportunity to promptly observe the effects of dewatering during
construction and to implement as soon as possible necessary precautionary or
remedial measures including but not limited to slowing down or stopping the
dewatering operations

W O 3873 00 Page 19
Where encountered at the bottom of excavations permeable granular subsoils
may be susceptible to piping and quick conditions The dewatering operations
should be carried out without creating a quick condition or softening at the
excavation bottoms Therefore the project dewatering operations should be done
without pumping out soil fines pumping clear water only and should be
coordinated with shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not
adversely affected
Any groundwater drawdown outside the excavation will cause additional
settlement resulting from consolidation of the loose soft compressible soils
Therefore the use of a deep well system outside the excavations to draw down
the groundwater level should not be allowed Special caution should also be taken
to avoid dewatering utility trenches connected to excavations If this occurs the
granular bedding and or backfill in the utility trenches could act as subdrains and
cause significant areal groundwater drawdown resulting in settlements and
potential damage to utility lines and or other existing adjacent structures
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the proposed project and
some of the utility trenches may extend below the groundwater table In general a
granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33
No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes If loose soft soils are encountered at
or near the invert of the pipes an additional 18 inches of No 3B Fine gravel wrapped in a
filter fabric Mirafi 180N or equivalent should be provided below the bedding layer for
more uniform support Free draining granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel
should also be used for the trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes or about
12 inches above the groundwater level to provide adequate support around the pipes and
to reduce the compactive effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging
the pipes Itis critical that the free draining material be used to reduce the potential for
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3873 00 Page 20
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support around
the sides of the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes or
groundwater level to the top of the subgrade or below the non expansive capping fill layer
may consist of the excavated on site soils The backfill should be moisture conditioned to
above the optimum moisture placed in maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and
mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the
potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches will be located below paved
areas the upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction
