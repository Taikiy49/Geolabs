SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered fill and colluvium consisting of hard 
silty clay and clayey silt and cobbly boulders extending to the maximum depth explored 
of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface. Boring No. 1, near the Shaft building, 
encountered a 6-inch of concrete pavement underlain by fill material. We did not 
encounter groundwater in the drilled borings at the time of our field exploration.  It should 
be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, time of year, 
seasonal precipitation, surface water runoff, and other factors. 
We understand that the new utility line inverts will be about 2 to 5 feet below the 
existing ground surface. However, deeper excavations may be required where the new 
lines traverse under existing utilities. Installation of the proposed utility lines will involve 
excavation into the underlying fills and colluvial materials. In general, we anticipate that 
the in-situ soils along the proposed utility alignments may be excavated with normal heavy 
excavation equipment near the ground surface. Excavation into the underlying cobbles 
and boulders may require chipping and/or the use of hoerams to remove the materials 
and to achieve the design invert elevations. 
Where deep excavations (greater than 5 feet in depth) are planned, temporary 
shoring or sloping and benching should be implemented for the trench excavations. 
Where temporary cut slopes are not possible, trench walls would have to be cut near 
vertical, necessitating the use of temporary shoring during construction. 
Design of metallic substructures, such as waterline pipes or other metallic piping, 
should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure. Based on 
the laboratory resistivity test results, the subsurface soils at the project site have resistivity 
values ranging from about 900 to 2,100 ohm-cm; therefore, the on-site soil should be 
considered as Corrosion Category A (Moderately to Severely Corrosive) based on the 
updated BWS External Corrosion Control Standards, dated July 28, 2021. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
recommendations for the installation of the new waterlines.

Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the Board of Water Supply (BWS) Makaha Shaft Tunnel Rehabilitation 
project in Makaha on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general vicinity 
are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings and presents our geotechnical engineering 
recommendations derived from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering 
analyses for the rehabilitation project. These recommendations are intended for the 
design of site grading, trench excavation and backfilling, and corrosion potential only. The 
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at 
the end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The BWS Makaha Shaft Tunnel Rehabilitation project is located along Kili Drive in 
Makaha on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project includes the installation of new 
waterlines, drain lines, and sewer lines. We understand that the new utility lines will be 
installed 2 to 5 feet below the existing ground surface. The scope of work requires that 
the system be designed in accordance with the recently updated Honolulu BWS 
Standards dated 2021, including corrosion control. 
Based on the water system standards, corrosion testing should be performed at 
every 300 to 1,000-foot interval at approximately the pipe depth.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The 
younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age 
(Ice Age) and forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. Waianae became 
extinct while Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank was partially buried below 
Koolau lava banking against its eastern flank. 
The Waianae and Koolau volcanic shields were built during the late Pliocene and 
early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly-bedded basaltic lava flows. The main shield-building 
activities ceased approximately 2.5 to 3.5 million years ago. Evidence from drilled wells 
indicates that the island of Oahu has subsided as much as 1,200 feet since the cessation 
of this early volcanic activity (Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During that period of subsidence, coral-algal reefs began to grow on the southern 
coast of Oahu, forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth. The growth of the 
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence. A series of lagoons formed behind 
the barrier reefs, and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the lagoons 
(Macdonald and Abbott, 1970). 
During the late Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred as 
a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. As the great 
continental glaciers advanced, the level of the ocean fell since less water was available 
to fill the oceanic basins. Conversely, as the glaciers receded or melted, global sea levels 
rose because more water was available. The land mass of Oahu remained essentially 
stable during these changes, and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature. These 
glacio-eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea, which were both higher and 
lower relative to the present sea level on Oahu.

Our borings generally encountered fill and colluvial deposits consisting of hard silty

In general, the on-site soils encountered during our field exploration should be 
suitable for use as general fill materials provided that the maximum particle size is

fill materials should be tested for conformance with these recommendations prior to 
delivery to the project site for the intended use.

3.3 
Trench Excavation Support 
We anticipate excavation depths of up to about 5 feet deep below the existing 
ground surface will be required for the installation of the new utility lines. Where 
excavations greater than 5 feet deep are planned, temporary shoring or sloping and 
benching should be implemented for the trench excavations. Where open cut excavations 
are not feasible, the trench walls would have to be cut near vertical, necessitating the use 
of shoring during construction. 
The excavation support and shoring system used must comply with applicable 
safety requirements. The contractor should be solely responsible for the adequacy and 
safety of the shoring installation. The contractor’s representative should be on-site at all 
times during excavation and construction work for the opportunity to promptly observe 
changing or unforeseen conditions, such as high groundwater, inappropriate construction 
sequence or techniques, etc., which may affect the shoring stability. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal 
to the depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for 
excessive ground movement. It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it 
tight against the excavation walls with proper bracing during construction. The properly 
braced shoring is essential to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of 
the adjacent ground into the excavation, which may result in potential settlement or 
distress to adjacent structures or other improvements. 
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the 
adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. Due 
to the complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the magnitude 
of movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship, such as how 
quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed, the subsurface 
conditions, the size of the excavation, and the rate of excavation. 
Therefore, it is important to realize that the excavation shoring should be installed 
properly and as early as practical. The adjacent ground should be continuously monitored 
for cracks, dips and/or other indications of movements with instruments until the trench 
excavations are finally backfilled.

3.4 
Pipe Cushion 
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the 
load supporting capacity of the pipe. Therefore, the pipe cushion is an important design 
consideration. For pipes bearing on the underlying fills, colluvium, and alluvium, a 
minimum of 6 inches of granular cushion material, such as BWS S4C or No. 3B Fine 
gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 Gradation) should be provided below the pipe. Where soft 
and/or loose soils are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes, these soils should 
be over-excavated and replaced with select granular fill material compacted to a minimum 
of 90 percent relative compaction. 
The granular cushion layer should extend beyond the pipe sides a minimum width 
of one-fourth (¼) of the outside pipe diameter, or trench width, whichever is greater and 
should extend 12 inches above the pipe. Before the placement of cushion material, a 
Geolabs representative should observe the excavated trench bottom to confirm that firm 
materials are exposed at the bottom of the trench. 
3.5 
Trench Backfill 
In general, the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Water 
System Standards, State of Hawaii 2002. The first zone of backfill extending from the 
cushion material to at least 12 inches above the top of the pipes should consist of 
BWS S4C material or select free-draining granular material (ASTM C33, No. 67 
Gradation) to reduce the compaction effort required and resulting stresses on the pipe. In 
addition, it is critical to use this free-draining material to reduce the potential for the 
formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the 
sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill could result in backfill settlement and pipe 
damage. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from a level 6 inches above the pipes to 
the finished subgrade should consist of the excavated on-site soils that are free of 
vegetation, deleterious materials, organic materials, and clay lumps and rock fragments 
greater than 3 inches in maximum dimension or imported fill materials meeting the 
requirements of Section 30 - Select Borrow for Subbase Course of the City and County 
of Honolulu Standard Specification (September 1986). It should be noted that the fill

3.6 
Trench Backfill Compaction and Placement Requirements 
Backfill materials consisting of the on-site soils should be moisture-conditioned to 
at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches 
in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. The 
upper 3 feet below the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. 
Imported fill materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture, placed in level lifts of about 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a 
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base course and subbase 
materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed 
in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of 
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established 
in accordance with ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by 
dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.

3.7 
Thrust Blocks 
We envision that thrust blocks will be required at some locations to provide 
resistance to the thrust forces due to the pressurized pipes. The thrust force acting on the 
pipes may be resisted by passive earth pressure acting against the face of the thrust 
block. We understand that most of the planned inverts will be about 5 feet below the 
existing pavement surface. However, some of the thrust blocks may need to be located 
at deeper depths in areas where the new waterlines traverse existing utilities. 
Based on our field exploration, hard silty clay and clayey silt and very dense 
cobbles and boulders were encountered within the depths explored. We recommend 
using a lateral bearing pressure of 1,000 pounds per square foot (psf) for the design of 
thrust blocks bearing against the in-situ materials underlying the site. 
3.8 
Settlements 
Primary settlement of the water mains is normally caused by the difference in the 
unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier compacted backfill 
material placed over the pipes. The net increase in loading will cause the settlement of 
the underlying subsoils below the trench invert. Based on our engineering analyses, the 
settlement on the order of less than ½ inch is anticipated for the project. 
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good 
workmanship will be engaged during construction. Additional settlement could occur if 
improper trench support is used.

3.10 Corrosion Potential 
The design of metallic substructures, such as waterline pipes or other metallic

sets of laboratory corrosion tests, including pH and minimum resistivity, were performed 
by our office to evaluate the corrosivity of the on-site soils encountered. The test results 
are summarized in Appendix B. Detailed results of the Chloride Content (EPA 300.0),

Based on the updated version of the BWS, City and County of Honolulu Water 
System External Corrosion Control Standards, dated July 28, 2021, there are two soil 
corrosivity categories. Category A (Moderately to Severely Corrosive) applies to soil 
meeting any one of the following conditions below. Corrosion Category B (Negligibly to 
Mildly Corrosive) is applicable when none of the following conditions are met. The 
updated version of the standards is effective as of the publishing date for Oahu only. 
Resistivity ≤ 5,000 ohm-cm

Water-soluble chloride concentration ≥ 500 ppm 
Water-soluble sulfate concentration ≥ 1,000 ppm 
Within fluctuating saltwater table 
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics 
regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to the corrosion 
of metallic objects. Results of the laboratory testing indicate that the on-site soils have 
resistivity values of about 900 to 2,100 ohm-cm with pH values of about 7.39 to 7.93. 
Therefore, the on-site near-surface soils may be considered as Corrosion Category A 
(Moderately to Severely Corrosive) based on the updated BWS External Corrosion 
Control Standards, dated July 28, 2021. 
In addition, chloride content and sulfate content tests were performed by Eurofins 
and bicarbonate content tests were performed by Enthalpy Analytical to evaluate the 
corrosivity of the on-site soils encountered. Based on the chloride and sulfate content 
tests performed on the on-site soils, the test values are generally relatively low. The 
bicarbonate content of the on-site soil ranged from 150 to 600 parts per million (ppm). It 
may be appropriate to consult with a professional corrosion engineer to review the test 
results and provide detailed recommendations for corrosion protection.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions by drilling and sampling six borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending to a depth of about 5 feet below the 
existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The 
borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described 
in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-6. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples 
were obtained from the borings in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration 
sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler 
the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.
