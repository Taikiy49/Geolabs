Potential Boulder Hazard at 40 41
In accordance with the request of Kimo Vincent of DHHL a site visit was made to the
above referenced parcel on May 20 The current resident reported that a large
boulder located at the upper corner of his lot appeared to be precariously positioned
and may be subject to rolling down the backyard hill and impacting the house
Based on our observations the basalt rock boulder is approximately 6 feet in length
by about 3 feet in height and about 4 feet in depth The boulder is positioned on top
of a stable hard rock ledge outcropping located at the upper portion of the lot near the
property boundary The boulder does not appear to be loose however the resident
expressed concern that smaller supporting cobbles and boulders have slowly eroded
away creating voids under the base of the larger boulder Additional erosion may
allow the boulder to come loose and roll down the steep hill toward the house The
site is not accessible to heavy equipment for removal of the boulder
Based on our observation of the positioning of the boulder and the surrounding site
conditions an appropriate and least costly means of securing the boulder is to cement
the rock in place A good mason should be able to cement the boulder in place using
some angular shaped supporting rocks placed along the void space at the base of the
boulder
Another alternative is to break up the rock up using air percussion equipment
however doing so may destabilize the surrounding rock formation and other earth
materials currently supported by the boulder Another more costly alternative is to
construct an anchorage system consisting of a drilled anchor in the boulder and a tie
back anchor positioned at the top of the slope in the neighboring property
Should you have questions or require additional information please contact our office
for assistance

Cut Slope at 42 12
During the slope excavation work located at 42 12 the contractor over
excavated the proposed 5 foot wide buffer zone to approximately 12 feet in width in an
effort to make space for operation of the backhoe excavator The cut slope produced
by the excavation of the work area exposed highly weathered friable basalt rock and
saprolitic clinker materials
The wider buffer zone created by the over excavation is actually more beneficial to the
overall protection of the adjacent home from possible future rockfall Therefore we
concur that instead of backfilling to the original proposed toe of the 1H 1V cut slope
the present location of the toe of the existing cut slope exposing the highly weathered
rock and clinker may remain if the following actions are implemented to stabilize the
slope The approximate location of the subject cut slope segment is shown on the
attached Plate 1
The existing near vertical cut slope face should be cut back further to a 1H 2V
inclination to daylight with the existing natural slope condition A grouted rip rap
GRP lining should be constructed to face the exposed cut slope in an effort to
reduce raveling of the exposed highly weathered friable clinker materials The rip rap
should have a minimum thickness of 18 inches with a minimum 24 inch deep
embedment at the bottom of the slope At the top of the riprap the facing should be
constructed flush with the top of the cut slope and should be embedded a minimum of
36 inches End treatments at the lateral termination of the GRP should be
constructed with a minimum embedment of 24 inches Cut slope drainage should be
provided for by the installation of 4 inch diameter PVC pipes positioned at 6 feet on
center in the slope face A typical detail for the grouted rip rap is provided on the
attached Plate 2
