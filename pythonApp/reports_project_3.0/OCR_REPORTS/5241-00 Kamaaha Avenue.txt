Dear Mr. Hiu:
In accordance with our fee proposal dated December 30, 2003, we have
performed a geotechnical engineering exploration for the Kamaaha Avenue and
Manawai Street Extension project in the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing and
presents our findings and recommendations for the design of the project. The findings
and recommendations are intended for design input only and are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report.
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Based on the information provided, it is desired to extend the existing Kamaaha
Avenue from Manawai Street to Fort Barrette Road in the Kapolei area on the Island of
Oahu, Hawaii. The proposed roadway extension alignment will run between the Kapolei
Regional Park and Luana Koa development. In addition, the new roadway construction
will also connect to the existing paved portion of Manawai Street. The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
The new roadway extension for Kamaaha Avenue, measuring approximately
1,680 feet long, will consist of two 30-foot wide travelways with an 8-foot wide sidewalk on
both sides separated by a 20-foot wide median. The new roadway extension for Manawai
Street, measuring approximately 740 feet long, will consist of two 22-foot wide travelways
with an 8-foot wide sidewalk on both sides. Based on our field observations and the plan
and profile drawings provided, we anticipate that cuts and fills of less than about 3 feet
thick will be required to achieve the finished grades for the project construction. In
addition, new utilities, such as water, drain, and sewer lines, are planned as part of the
roadway construction project.

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration was to obtain an overview
of the surface and subsurface conditions at the site. The subsurface information obtained
was utilized to develop a soil data set for the formulation of geotechnical engineering
recommendations pertaining to the design of pavements and site preparation only. To
accomplish this, we conducted an exploration program consisting of the following tasks
and work efforts:
1, Mobilization and demobilization of a truck-mounted drill rig and two
operators to and from the project site.
2. Drilling and sampling of five borings extending to depths ranging from
about 5.5 to 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface for a total of
approximately 110 lineal feet of field exploration.
3. Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by a
geologist from our firm.
4. Laboratory testing of selected soil samples obtained during the field
exploration as an aid in classifying the materials encountered and
evaluating their engineering properties.
5, Analyses of the field and laboratory data for the formulation of
geotechnical engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of
pavements and site preparation for the project.
6. Preparation of this report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and recommendations.
7. Coordination of our overall work on the project by a senior engineer from
our firm.
8. Quality assurance of our work and client/design team consultation by a
principal engineer from our firm.
9. Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting, word processing, and clerical
support.
Detailed descriptions of our field exploration and the Logs of Borings are
presented on Plates 3 through 8 of this report. The results of the laboratory tests
performed on selected soil samples are presented on Plates 9 through 13.

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
As mentioned previously, the proposed new Kamaaha Avenue and Manawai
Street extension are located in the Kapolei area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The
alignments of the new roadway extension are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
Based on our field observations, the proposed roadway extension along
Kamaaha Avenue generally consists of vacant lands moderately vegetated with small
trees and tall grasses. Dry and loose soils were exposed at the existing ground surface
with potholes and trenches caused by soil shrinkage in the surface clayey soil. Based
on the plan and profile drawings provided, the existing ground elevations within the new
roadway alignment generally range from about +65 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) near the
proposed Manawai Street Extension to about +72 feet MSL near Fort Barrette Road.
Our field observations indicated that the proposed Manawai Street Extension has
been previously mass graded and is presently covered with a thin layer of gravel at the
existing ground surface. Based on the plan and profile drawings provided, the existing
ground elevations within this roadway extension alignment generally range from about
+72 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) near the existing paved portion of Manawai Street to
about +65 feet MSL near the proposed Kamaaha Avenue Extension.
FIELD EXPLORATION
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling five borings,
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending to depths ranging from about 5.5 to
26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using a
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight, solid-stem augers. Two bulk
samples, designated as Bulk-1 and Bulk-2, were obtained from the near-surface soils to
evaluate the pavement support characteristics. The approximate locations of the
borings drilled and bulk samples collected at the site are shown on the Site Plan,
Plate 2.
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our field representative, who monitored the drilling operations
on a near-continuous basis. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on the Boring Log Legend, Plate 3. Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are provided on the Logs of Borings,
Plates 4 through 8.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of
Soils, by driving a 3-inch outside diameter (OD) Modified California sampler with a
140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the
drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586, Penetration
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration

LABORATORY TESTING
Selected soil samples from the field exploration were tested in the laboratory with
an emphasis on evaluating the moisture, density, swelling potential, and strength
characteristics. The following laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples
obtained from the field exploration.
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937)
determinations were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and
evaluation of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths.
Four one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface soils. The test results are
summarized on the Summary of Ring Swell Tests, Plate 9.
One Atterberg Limits test (ASTM D 4318) was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth. Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate 10.
Two laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D 1883) were performed on
the bulk samples of the clayey soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils. The test results are presented on Plates 11 and 12 of this report.
Two sets of corrosion tests, including pH (ASTM G 51) and minimum resistivity
(ASTM G 57), were performed on selected soil samples obtained from our field
exploration. The results of these tests are summarized on Plate 13.
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
In general, our field exploration disclosed that the project site is underlain by hard to
very hard silty clays and clayey silts extending to the maximum depth explored of
approximately 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Dense to very dense sandy
gravel and gravelly sands were encountered at shallow depths in Boring No. 3 and
extended to the maximum depth explored of about 25 feet below the existing ground
surface.
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of our field exploration.
However, groundwater levels are subject to change due to rainfall, seasonal precipitation,

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings, the principal
geotechnical considerations for the design of the Kamaaha Avenue and Manawai Street
Extension project include proper site preparation requirements to take into consideration
the expansive nature of the near-surface soils and the relatively dry condition of the soils
and the site. Detailed discussion of our geotechnical engineering recommendations for
design of pavements and site preparation are presented in the following sections of this
report.
Pavement Design
Based on our field exploration, the proposed new roadway alignments are
generally underlain by hard to very hard silty clays at the ground surface. Laboratory
testing performed on the surface clayey soils indicated CBR values of about 3.7 and 4.9
and swells of 2.6 and 4.3 percent. Based on the field exploration and laboratory test
results, we recommend that the following new pavement sections be used for
preliminary design and cost estimating purposes:
Pavement Design (Kamaaha Avenue Extension)
4.0 Inches Asphalt Concrete
6.0 Inches Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction)
18.0 Inches Aggregate Subbase (95 Percent Relative Compaction)
28.0 Inches Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
Pavement Design (Manawai Street Extension)
3.5 Inches Asphalt Concrete
6.0 Inches Aggregate Base Course (95 Percent Relative Compaction)
18.0 Inches Aggregate Subbase (95 Percent Relative Compaction)
27.5 Inches Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
The subgrade soils below the pavement sections should be scarified to a depth of
about 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture, and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. The aggregate base course and aggregate
subbase should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction. Our experience in the Kapolei area suggests that the
swelling potential of the in-situ soils could be highly variable in nature. Therefore, CBR
and field density tests should be performed on the actual subgrade soils encountered

Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due
to saturation of the base course, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the
pavement surface should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to
carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. Surface water
ponding should not be allowed on the site during or after construction.
Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or adjacent to the
pavement areas, we recommend that the curbs extend a minimum of 2 inches into the
subgrade soils below the base and subbase course layers to reduce the potential for
migration of excessive landscape water into the pavement section. Alternatively, a
subdrain system could be constructed to collect excess water from the landscaping
irrigation. For long-term performance, we recommend that a subdrain system be
considered along the sides of the roadway.
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the anticipated design finish grades for the
proposed roadway project, we anticipate that site grading work consisting of cuts and
fills of less than about 3 feet thick will be required for the project construction. In
general, the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and
grubbed at the on-set of earthwork. All vegetation, debris, deleterious material and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed of properly off-site. Soft
and yielding areas encountered during clearing below areas designated to receive fill or
future improvements should be over-excavated to expose firm and/or stiff material, and
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well-compacted fill. The excavated
soft and/or organic soils should be properly disposed of off-site.
Existing structures and pavements that are to be demolished should be
completely removed. The over-excavations resulting from demolition should be
backfilled with compacted granular fill material. Existing utilities to be abandoned
should be removed, and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with
granular fill material moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content,
placed in 8-inch level loose lifts, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction. Utilities that are to be abandoned in-place under the proposed structures
should be backfilled by pumping lean concrete under low pressure.
Our field observations indicate that shrinkage cracks and ground depressions are
present at the existing ground surface across the project site. In general, shrinkage
cracks will develop in the ground due to the lack of maintenance of the soil moisture
content as the surface soils start to dry out under the sun and dry weather conditions.

The development of shrinkage cracks in the ground was apparently intensified by the
prolonged dry climatic conditions characteristic of the Kapolei area resulting in large
fluctuations of the soil moisture content.
If the existing ground cracks are left unattended for an extended period of time
and are allowed to penetrate deeper below the surface, the ground cracks could
adversely affect the performance of the future roadway. The ground cracks could serve
as conduits for water to percolate into the pavement subgrade soils and cause loss of
strength of the subgrade soils resulting in potential settlement of the pavement
structure. Therefore, remedial work to correct or mitigate the observed ground cracks
should be implemented prior to fill placement and construction of the proposed new
roadway.
After clearing and grubbing, the exposed subgrades should be proof-rolled with a
minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of six passes to assist in detecting and
collapsing near-surface voids and loose zones created by the shrinkage cracks and
ground depressions. The exposed subgrades should be proof-rolled without
moisture-conditioning of the subgrade soils (at the pre-existing moisture contents).
Loose areas disclosed during the proof-rolling operation should be backfilled with
general fill material compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. The
proof-rolling operations should be conducted under the near-continuous observation of
a representative from Geolabs.
Subsequent to the proof-rolling operations, the subgrades should then be
scarified to a minimum depth of 12 inches, moisture-conditioned to at least 2 percent
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures. Optimum moisture is the water content
(percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density.
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade, we
recommend that the soils be scarified and re-prepared as recommended above.
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement weather
and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and replacement with
well-compacted fill. Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit
prices for over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the
over-excavation quantities.
The aggregate base course and aggregate subbase materials should meet the
requirements of Sections 30 and 31 of the City and County Standard Specifications
(September 1986). The materials should be moisture-conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content, placed in 8-inch level loose lifts, and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.

Imported fill materials below the subbase course layer should consist of
non-expansive select granular material, such as crushed coral or basalt. The material
should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in
largest dimension. In addition, the material should contain between 10 and 30 percent
particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory CBR value
of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or less when tested in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883. The materials should be
moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in 8-inch level
loose lifts, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Imported fill
materials should be tested by Geolabs for conformance with these recommendations
prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use.

is important that a representative from our office observe the site preparation to
evaluate whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and
scarification process and whether the exposed soil/rock conditions are similar to those
encountered in our field exploration.
Utility Trenches
We anticipate that the utilities for the new roadway project will consist primarily of
water, drain, and sewer lines. In general, good construction practices should be utilized
for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities at the site. The
method and equipment to be used for trench excavation should be determined by the
contractor, subject to practical limits and safety considerations. In addition, the
excavations should comply with the applicable federal, state, and local safety
requirements.
Based on the results of our field exploration, it appears that conventional
excavation methods may generally be utilized for utility line excavation at the site. The
upper clayey soils encountered at the site are generally very stiff to hard. However, some
of the utility trench excavations will likely encounter the dense to very dense coralline
materials as described in the boring logs, which may pose some excavation difficulties
during the trench excavation.
In general, we recommend that granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of
No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) be used for support under the pipes.
Free-draining granular materials, such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67
gradation), should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above
the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes. It is critical to use this
free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of
pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes. Improper trench backfill
could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes.

The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to

Where trenches are below pavement areas, the upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below
the pavement finished grade should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction.
Corrosion Protection for Water Lines
Two sets of pH and minimum resistivity tests were performed to evaluate the
corrosivity of the on-site clayey soils generally encountered at the subject site. The
results of these tests are presented on Plate 13 of this report. The minimum resistivity
values of the near-surface soil samples tested are on the order of about 930 and
980 ohm-cm, which correspond to a Corrosion Rating of 1 (extremely corrosive) based
on the guidelines provided by the City & County of Honolulu — Board of Water Supply.
Therefore, we recommend that all metallic substructures, such as ductile iron water
lines, in contact with the soils be properly designed to be protected against potential
corrosion.

encountered during construction are different from those assumed or considered in this
report, then appropriate modifications to the design should be made.

LIMITATIONS
The findings and discussions submitted in this report are based, in part, upon
information obtained from the field borings. Variations of subsurface conditions between
and beyond the borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not
become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be
necessary to re-evaluate the findings and discussions provided in this report.
The locations of the field borings indicated in this report are approximate, having
been estimated by taping and pacing from the features shown on the Topographic
Survey Maps provided by Engineering Concepts, Inc. on April 26, 2004. Elevations of
the field borings were estimated by interpolation from the spot elevations shown on the
same plans. The physical locations and elevations of the borings should be considered
accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used.
The stratification lines shown on graphic representations of the borings depict the
approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual transition.
As mentioned previously, groundwater was not encountered in the borings at the time of
our exploration. However, it must be noted that groundwater level changes may occur due
to seasonal precipitation, surface runoff, and other factors.
This report has been prepared for the exclusive use of Aina Nui Corporation, and
their consultants for specific application to the Kamaaha Avenue and Manawai Street
Extension project in the Kapolei area in accordance with generally accepted
geotechnical engineering principles and practices. No warranty is expressed or implied.
This report has been prepared solely for the purpose of assisting the engineer in
the design of the proposed project. Therefore, this report may not contain sufficient
data, or the proper information, to serve as the basis for preparation of construction cost
estimates. A contractor wishing to bid on this project is urged to retain a competent
geotechnical engineer to assist in the interpretation of this report and/or in the
performance of additional site-specific exploration for bid estimating purposes.
The owner/client should be aware that unanticipated soil conditions are
commonly encountered. Unforeseen soil conditions, such as perched groundwater, soft
deposits, hard layers, or loose fills, may occur in localized areas and may require
additional probing or corrections in the field (which may result in construction delays) to
attain a properly constructed project. Therefore, a sufficient contingency fund is
recommended to accommodate these possible extra costs.
