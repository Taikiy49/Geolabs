Drilled Shaft Foundations
Based on the anticipated structural loads and the subsoil conditions encountered at
the project site we recommend that drilled shaft foundations be used for the support of the
new light poles planned for the project development The cast in place concrete drilled
shafts would derive vertical support principally from skin friction between the shafts and
the surrounding soils For the 7 and 14 foot light poles an allowable compressive load
capacity on order of about 15 kips per shaft may be used for dead plus live loads and may
be increased by 50 percent for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces The
allowable compressive load capacity may be increased to 50 kips per shaft for the 35 foot
light pole planned for the project
Lateral loads imposed on the light pole foundations generally may be resisted by
resistance of the shaft soil system to deflect Lateral load resistance of the drilled shaft is
a function of the stiffness of the surrounding soil the stiffness of the shaft allowable
deflection at the top of the shaft and induced moment in the shaft Based on the
foundation design parameters presenied in the following table our lateral load analyses
indicated that the estimated lateral deflection at the top of the drilled shaft should be less
than 1 inch

Construction Considerations
Drilled shafts are desirable for the light pole foundations because of the significant
increase in lateral and uplift load capacities when compared to shallow foundations
However the performance of the drilled shafts will depend significantly upon the
contractor s method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the
following conditions would have a substantial effect on the effectiveness and cost of the
drilled shaft foundations
Loose Sandy Soils
Based on our field exploration the proposed new light pole locations are generally
underlain by medium dense coralline sands and gravel extending to a depth of
about 71 5 feet below the existing ground surface However localized zones of
loose sandy subsoils may be encountered during the drilled shaft installation To
reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes permanent casing may be
required during the foundation construction work Therefore we recommend that
casing of drilled holes be considered as a contingent item with additive and
deductive unit prices included in the contract documents
Workmanship
The load bearing capacities of drilled shafts depend to a large extent on the
contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in
drilling the shaft holes and placing concrete into the holes Drilling by methods
utilizing drilling fluids is not recommended

A low shrink concrete mix with high slump 6 to 9 inch range should be used to
provide close contact between the drilled shafts and the surrounding soils
Concrete should be placed in a suitable manner to reduce the potential for
segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix If precast concrete pole
bases are used the gap between the pole base and the surrounding soils should
be backfilled with concrete or lean concrete A representative from Geolabs should
be present to observe the installation of drilled shafts during construction to confirm
the subsoil conditions and embedment depths
Utility Trench
We envision that new electrical duct lines will be required to service the proposed
new lighting system In general granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of clean sand or
S4C is recommended under the duct lines Free draining granular materials such as
clean sand or S4C should also be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 6 inches
above the duct lines to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the
compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the possibility of damaging the pipes It is
critical to use this free draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below
the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes
Improper backfill material and placement procedures could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 6 inches above the duct lines
to the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of on site soils or imported select
granular fill material The backfill should be placed in maximum 6 inch level loose lifts
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and mechanically
compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the upper 3 feet of
the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
corresponding to the maximum dry density
