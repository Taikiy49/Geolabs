This letter report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Rainbow Tower Loading Dock project located within the
Hilton Hawaiian Village in Waikiki on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and recommendations for design of the new
loading dock based on our field exploration and engineering analyses performed for the
proposed project The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this letter report
Please note that the soil samples recovered during our field exploration
remaining after testing will be stored for a period of two months from the date of this
report The samples will be discarded after that date unless arrangements are made
for a longer sample storage period Please contact our office for alternative sample
storage requirements if appropriate
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The new loading dock project site is located within the Hilton Hawaiian Village
complex in the Waikiki area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii In general the
new loading dock area is adjacent to the existing Rainbow Tower and the existing porte
cochere within the Hilton Hawaiian Village complex The new loading dock will consist
of a partially embedded loading dock structure with a finished floor elevation of 0 feet
Mean Sea Level MSL The loading dock will be an enclosed structure with a concrete
ramp leading to the entrance of the enclosed loading dock structure In order to
construct the loading dock structure and related foundation caps we envision that the
bottom of footing for the loading dock structure likely will be placed at about

FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface conditions at the new loading dock project site were explored by
drilling and sampling one test boring designated as Boring No 5 B 5 In general the
test boring drilled extended to a depth of about 53 feet below the existing ground
surface The boring was drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with rotary
wash coring tools The approximate location of the test boring drilled for the project is
shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the boring drilled were classified by visual and
textural examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on
a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on the Boring Log Legend Plate 3 Graphic
representations of the materials encountered and water levels observed in the boring
drilled are provided on the attached Log of Boring Plate 4
Soil samples were obtained from the boring drilled in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches in
addition some samples were obtained from the boring drilled in general accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of
Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and
drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an
18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Log of Boring at the
appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are summarized on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
One boring was drilled specifically at the proposed loading dock location for the
design of the loading dock In general the loading dock project site generally is
underlain by about 6 feet of surface fills consisting of dense gravelly and silty sands
The surface fill layer generally was underlain by lagoonal deposits and coralline
materials The lagoonal deposits generally consisted of loose to medium dense silty
sands and gravel and extended to a depth of about 45 feet below the existing ground
surface
Coralline materials consisting of interbedded dense sandy and gravelly coralline
detritus generally were encountered below the lagoonal deposits extending to the
maximum depth explored of about 53 feet below the existing ground surface

Groundwater was encountered in the boring drilled at approximately 6 3 feet

Based on the information provided we understand that the new loading dock will
have a finished floor elevation of 0 feet MSL Therefore we envision that the bottom of
footing for the loading dock likely will be placed at about Elevation 3 feet MSL As
mentioned previously we believe that the groundwater levels at the project site may rise
up to about Elevation 3 feet MSL on a periodic basis under extreme high tide conditions

Micropile Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site and the
anticipated bottom of footing elevation about 3 feet MSL we believe that the underlying
compressible lagoonal deposits would not provide adequate foundation support for the
new structures bearing directly on the lagoonal deposits without appreciable settlements
under the structural loads In addition the project site may be subjected to appreciable
ground settlement in the event of liquefaction during a moderate earthquake Therefore
we recommend that a deep foundation system be used to support the loading dock
structure Considering the effects of noise and vibrations generated from a driven pile
foundation system during construction on the adjacent structures we recommend that
micropiles be used to support the new loading dock structures
In general the micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter usually
12 inches or less drilled and grouted pile with steel reinforcing The micropile foundation
GEOLABS INC
Hawaii California
Hilton Hotels Corporation c o Rider Hunt Bailey Page 5

typically is constructed by drilling a borehole placing reinforcing steel in the hole and
grouting the borehole Micropiles are desirable because they can be readily installed in
access restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions In
addition micropile installation is less noisy and generally less costly compared to driven
piles and or drilled shaft foundations Furthermore use of micropiles will reduce the
current footing sizes for the project
Based on experience we believe that a micropile system with a minimum grout
bulb diameter of 5 inches minimum drill bit size may be used to support the new loading
dock structure The micropiles should extend through the loose lagoonal deposits and into
the underlying dense coralline materials The micropiles would derive support primarily
through skin friction in the dense coralline materials
Based on our analysis we recommend that each micropile be designed based on
an allowable compressive load capacity of up to 70 kips The allowable compressive load
capacity for the micropiles is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by 50 percent
for transient loads such as wind or seismic forces Based on the subsurface conditions
encountered at the project site we recommend that a minimum embedment of about
10 feet into the underlying dense coralline materials be provided for the micropiles
Based on the field exploration and bottom of footing elevation of about 3 feet MSL
we estimate that a micropile length of about 50 feet below the bottom of footing elevation
estimated tip elevation of about 53 feet MSL would be needed to provide the
recommended allowable compressive load capacity for micropiles installed within the
dense coralline materials The micropiles should be spaced a minimum of 2 feet from
center to center The minimum pile spacing recommended would avoid further reduction
in vertical capacity due to group action and also would facilitate micropile installation in a
pile group
In the event of seismically induced liquefaction settlement of the loose lagoonal
deposits will occur at the project site When these loose deposits move downward relative
to the micropile the downward movement will result in negative skin friction forces
generally known as downdrag forces acting on the micropile The magnitude of the
downdrag force depends on the thickness of the liquefiable layer relative density of the
potentially liquefiable lagoonal deposits and the pile perimeter
Due to the potential for liquefaction of the loose lagoonal deposits at the site
settlement of the loose deposits will result in downdrag loading of the micropile
foundations extending through the potentially liquefiable materials Based on our
foundation analyses we estimate that the downdrag loads imposed on the micropiles due
to liquefaction of the loose deposits may be on the order of about 15 kips per micropile It
should be noted that the recommended allowable compressive load capacity of the
micropiles 70 kips has been reduced by the expected downdrag loads in the micropile
foundation analyses

For resistance to uplift loads an uplift load capacity of up to 30 kips per micropile
may be used in the design for sustained uplift loads such as from buoyant uplift forces
The sustained uplift load capacity provided above may be increased by 50 percent when
considering transient loads The project structural engineer should check the structural
capacity of the micropile member in tension
It should be noted that the micropile foundation system provides low lateral load
resistance due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile Therefore the lateral load
resistance contributed by the micropiles should be neglected Lateral loads imposed on
the loading dock structure may be resisted primarily by passive pressure resistance acting
against the vertical faces of the footings and grade beams and by shear acting along the
sides of the footings and grade beams Passive pressure resistance against the vertical
faces of the footing and grade beams may be estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure
of 150 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf below the groundwater level A side
shear resistance of up to 150 pounds per square foot psf may be used for transient loads
such as wind or seismic forces only
Shallow Foundations
We understand that some of the new structures will be extensions of the existing
structures We also understand that the existing structures are supported on shallow
footings bearing in the fill materials existing at the project site To match the performance
of the existing structures supported on shallow foundations we believe that shallow
spread footings and or continuous footings may be used to support the new structures
extending from the existing structures Use of shallow footings to support the new
Structures should be limited to structural loads less than about 20 kips per column In
addition the bottom of footing elevations of the new footings should be at an elevation
higher than 3 feet MSL It should be noted that these minor structures supported on a
shallow foundation system should not be structurally connected to the loading dock
structure which will be supported on micropiles or structurally connected to existing
structures that are pile supported
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2 000 pounds per square foot psf may
be used for the design of shallow footings bearing on the recompacted on site fill soils
for transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces In general footings
should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent exterior grade
however the bottom of footing should not extend below Elevation 3 feet MSL
Foundations located next to other foundations utility trenches or easements
should be embedded below a 45 degree imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench or the footings should be extended to a depth as deep
as the inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the
potential for appreciable foundation settlement

Site Preparation
Based on the existing grades we believe that only minor site grading work will be
required to achieve the finished grades at the project site In general areas within the
contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared at the on set of earthwork
Vegetation debris deleterious materials and other unsuitable materials including the
existing lawn area and asphaltic concrete pavement should be removed and disposed of
properly off site
Soft loose and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below
areas designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to
expose firm or dense material and the resulting excavation should be backfilled with
well compacted fill The excavated soft loose and or organic soils should be properly
disposed of off site In addition contract documents should include additive and deductive
unit prices for over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variation in the
over excavation quantities
Existing structures and pavements to be demolished should be completely
removed The excavations resulting from the demolition work should be backfilled with
select granular fill material compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
Existing utilities to be abandoned should be removed and the resulting excavation should
be properly backfilled with select granular fill material moisture conditioned to above the

After clearing and grubbing the areas to receive fill and finished subgrades in cut
areas above the groundwater level should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The compaction requirement for the finished
subgrades in areas subjected to vehicular traffic should be increased to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrade due to inclement
weather and poor drainage may require over excavation of the soft and or loose areas and
replacement with well compacted fill at no additional cost to the owner The need for
over excavation should be evaluated by a representative from Geolabs in the field
In general the excavated on site soils may be re used as a source of general fill or
backfill materials provided that they are free of deleterious materials vegetation and are
screened of particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension Imported fill materials
needed for the site filling should consist of non expansive granular materials such as
crushed coral basalt or cinder sand The materials should be well graded from coarse to
fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between
10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The materials should have a
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of 1 percent or
less Aggregate base course materials should consist of crushed basaltic aggregates and
should conform to Subsection 703 06 the State of Hawaii Standard Specifications for
Road Bridge and Public Works Construction 1994 Imported fill materials should be
tested by Geolabs for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the
project site for the intended use
Fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a
minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Aggregate base course required under the
pavement areas should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction In general compaction should be accomplished by
sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers or other similar types of acceptable compaction

Underground Utility Trenches
We envision that some new utility lines and or utility connections may be required
for the project We envision that most of the new utility lines and or utility connections for
the project will be relatively shallow within 5 to 8 feet of the ground surface In general
we recommend that granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of free draining granular
materials ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be provided under the pipes for uniform support
Where soft and or loose soils are encountered at or near the invert of the pipes pipe
inverts below about 3 feet MSL a stabilization layer consisting of an additional 24 to
30 inches of open graded gravel wrapped in a non woven filter fabric Mirafi 180N or
equivalent should be provided below the bedding layer for uniform support
Free draining granular materials ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation also should be
used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide
adequate support around the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to reduce
the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate
support for the sides of the pipes Improper trench backfill could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of the excavated on site soils with a
maximum particle size of 3 inches The backfill should be moisture conditioned to above
the optimum moisture content placed in about 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the potential for
appreciable future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas the
upper 3 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
