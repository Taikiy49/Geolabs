PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of this investigation is to provide general geologic
information regarding the subsurface conditions in the area of Pearl
Harbor Oahu Hawaii This generalized subsurface information is to
be used to aid in the development of a design feasibility study for
future improvements and rehabilitation projects for the naval facili
ties at Pearl Harbor Of primary concern are possible access routes
from the Navy property surrounding Pearl Harbor into Ford Island and
waterfront improvements at Ford Island
The scope of this investigation involved the review of existing soils
and geologic information including
1 Boring logs and pile driving records from U S Navy
construction drawings
2 Boring logs and reports by other soil consultants
3 Soils and geological data from our own files on projects
located within the Study area and vicinity
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1191 00 Page 2
4 Published and unpublished source materials concerning
the geology and soils of the area under study and
5 Field visits made to portions of the study area to
confirm surfical geologic conditions
A list of the reports maps and literature used in our study is pre
sented in the Reference Section at the end of the report It should
be noted that no borings were drilled for this study
AREAL DESCRIPTION
The area under study is located in the central portion of the south
coast of the island of Oahu and generally forms the division between
the Honolulu and Ewa coastal plains
The study area is roughly diamond shaped in plan view with the bounds
being imaginary lines as follows
1 Southeast limit runs from Waipio Point to the Makalapa
Gate of Pearl Harbor
2 Northeast limit runs from Makalapa Gate to the inter
section of McGrew Loop and Kamehameha Highway
3 Northwest limit runs from the intersection of McGrew
Loop and Kamehameha Highway to the mouth of Waiawa
Stream and
4 Southwest limit runs from the mouth of Waiawa Stream to
Waipio Point
The study area encompasses approximately 6 5 square miles of which
about 60 percent is covered with water The location of the study
area in relation to the island of Oahu is shown on the Project
Location Map Plate 1

Pearl Harbor is probably one of the finest natural harbors in the
world It consists of 4 inland basins or lochs connected by channels
and has a main entrance channel leading to Mamala Bay and the open
ocean This configuration provides a sheltered haven for the U S
Navy Pacific fleet
The harbor is a vast estuary into which empties the drainage of about
one quarter of the island of Oahu
Due to the extent of the area and the intent of the study the emphasis
of our study has been on the areas which have been previously developed
and which may be developed in the future for naval facilities The
greatest amount of development has been done in the eastern half of
the study area therefore the concentration of our report will be in
this portion of the Pearl Harbor area Since little development has
taken place in the western half of Pearl Harbor Waipio Peninsula
there is very little information regarding subsurface conditions in
this area so the western portion of the study area will be discussed
only in cursory detail

GEOLOGIC HISTORY OF PEARL HARBOR
The origin of Pearl Harbor has been discussed in almost every treatise
involving the geology of the island of Oahu Various authors have
attributed its origin to a multitude of different processes Stearns
and Vaksvik believed that the Pearl Harbor Lochs are drowned river
valleys as did Macdonald and Abbott J Pollack felt that Pearl Harbor
is wholly of marine origin and that the lochs are the result of a lack
of growth of coral materials due to inhibition by fresh water entering
a lagoonal environment
More recent evidence from foundation investigations and a master s
thesis by K Munro indicates that the lochs are primarily of stream
origin It is most likely that marine processes and lack of coral
growth are only partially responsible for the morphology of Pearl
Harbor
The stratigraphic history of Pearl Harbor is quite complex due to
its large areal extent the large number of streams and drainageways
entering it and a complex series of sea level changes The base of the
Stratigraphic section is Koolau Basalt which underlies the Pearl Harbor
area at depths which range from about 50 feet to about 1000 feet below
sea level
GEOLABS HAWAII
The Koolau Volcanic Shield was built during the late Pliocene and
early Pleistocene by thin bedded basaltic lava flows The main
shield building activity ceased approximately 2 5 million years ago
Evidence from drilled wells indicates that the island of Oahu has
subsided up to 1200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic
activity Macdonald and Abbott 1971
During this period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on
the southern coast of Oahu forming a broad bay with a barrier reef
across its mouth The growth of the reef essentially kept pace with
the rate of subsidence A lagoon formed behind the barrier reef and
both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the lagoon
Macdonald and Abbott 1971
During the Pleistocene there were many eustatic sea level changes that
were the result of wide spread glaciation in the continental areas of
the world i e as the great continental glaciers accumulated the
level of the ocean fell since there was less water available to fill
the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted
sea level rose because more water was available The land masses
remained essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations
were eustatic in nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted
in stands of the sea which were both higher and lower relative to
present sea level
GEOLABS HAWAII
The sequence and ages of these fluctuations is quite complex and
lengthy therefore for the sake of brevity we will not go into great
detail regarding the individual events The estimated significant sea
level stands are indicated on Plate 3
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans
of terrigenous sediments in the head of the old bay and accumulation of
reef deposits at higher elevations The lower stands carved valleys
through the sediments and reef deposits Subaerial exposure of the
sediments and calcareous materials caused consolidation of the soft
deltaic materials and induration of the reef deposits
At some point during the growth of the lagoon and accumulation of
sediments the several streams emptying into the Pearl Harbor area
coalesced to form a single stream leading to the ocean The resistant
barrier reef materials kept the channel of this stream narrow so that
it now forms the entrance to Pearl Harbor Erosion of the softer
deltaic sediments in the head of the lagoon during low stands of the
sea formed broad shallow valleys which have become drowned to form the
Pearl Harbor lochs
The geologic history of the Pearl Harbor area has been further compli
cated by the deposition of volcanic materials from members of the
Honolulu Volcanic Series These deposits originated from post erosional
volcanic events which were contemporaneous with the Pleistocene sea
level fluctuations
GEOLABS HAWAII
The eruptions were rather explosive in nature owing to the interaction
of ground water with the rising magma which resulted in steam explosions
that expelled large quantities of pyroclastic materials such as ash
and cinder These deposits of pyroclastic materials have consolidated
to form tuff locally referred to as mudrock The oldest of these
eruptions at Aliamanu caused a diversion of Halawa Stream towards the
north and into area of what are now the southeast lochs during the
Kaena 95 feet high stand of the sea
The remaining two events Salt Lake and Makalapa were essentially
simultaneous and have been assigned to the stand of the sea which was
at between 40 and 60 feet below present
These last two eruptions caused further diversion of Halawa Stream and
deposited a mantle of tuff over much of the Shipyard and Submarine Base
areas of Pearl Harbor
In the last 10 000 years or So sea level has adjusted to its present
stand Terrigenous and marine sediments have continued to accumulate
in the low energy estuarine or lagoonal environment of Pearl Harbor
resulting in thick deposits of soft sediments in the lochs and channel
areas
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1191 00 Page 9
Land reclamation projects within the last 100 years have brought the
Pearl Harbor area to its present form A great many of these projects
were done in the 1940 s to handle fleet activities during World War
II For these projects expediency took precedence over quality and
many of the resulting fills are of poor quality
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Since this study is of a preliminary nature and the methodology has
been research of existing information no borings were drilled
specifically for this Study Much of the data came from outside
sources and was interpreted with respect to our own information and
experiences Many of the older boring logs were maintained by the
drillers rather than by an engineer or geologist Even the logs which
were maintained by professional personnel are subject to interpretation
since each firm will describe the materials encountered slightly
differently In addition quite frequently in foundation investiga
tions no attempts are made to describe the geologic origins of the
materials encountered nor is any attempt made to assign a stratigraphic
age to any particular stratum Therefore subsurface conditions
described in this report are of a generalized nature based on our
interpretations of the available data and may not be wholly accurate
on a site specific basis

Koolau Basalt
As previously discussed this unit is the oldest geologic unit within
the study area It consists of interbedded pahoehoe and a a flows of
basaltic lavas and may have a mantle of Tesidual soil overlying the
unweathered rock
Structurally Koolau basalt is the strongest of all the units
encountered within the study area However its use for foundation
Support in the Pear Harbor area is severely limited by certain
considerations
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1191 00 Page 11
First as described earlier basaltic bedrock is encountered at
depths usually in excess of 100 feet at the development sites being
considered Based on the drilling records of artesian wells the
estimated depth to Koolau basalt is indicated on Plate 4 The
impracticality and expense of extending piles or caissons to
end bearing on this formation may be sufficient cause to rule out
Koolau basalt as a foundation material
Secondly Koolau basalt is the aquifer material of the Pearl Harbor
Artesian Basin This basin is one of the main groundwater bodies
which is developed to supply water for the island of Oahu The
driving of end bearing Piles to basalt may damage the overlying
confining sedimentary strata resulting in losses of groundwater
Supplies
Coral Algal Reef De osits
The reef and related deposits are considered the second oldest
Stratigraphic assemblage in the Study area although they are
partially contemporaneous with the older alluvial deposits and have
continued to accumulate even through the present This unit consists
of the fossil remnants of coral algal reefs and their related detrital
deposits In Hawaii the detrital deposits tend to be the dominant
portion of any coralline assemblage and only isolated hard ledges of
intact reef limestone are encountered usually marking the seaward
margin of a lagoon
GEOLABS HAWAII
The detrital deposits consist of clastic fragments of coral algal
colonies which have been broken to gravel sand and silt sized
particles and then deposited by wind wave and tidal action Many of
the older deposits became indurated or cemented during lower stands of
the sea by the action of water percolating through the deposits
Structurally the coral algal reef deposits can be quite strong and
capable of supporting high capacity end bearing piles However
coralline deposits tend to be discontinuous particularly in the
channel areas where the erosive effects of the paleo streams tended to
remove unconsolidated deposits during low stands of the sea During
high stands of the sea the influx of fresh water and terrigenous
sediments tended to inhibit the growth of coral algal organisms
particularly in the northern area of the study area where reef
deposits are essentially absent
Where competent coral algal reef deposits are found at reasonable
depths by exploratory borings they can generally be counted upon to
provide good support for foundations
Older Alluvium
The older alluvium is assigned the position being the third oldest
unit in the stratigraphic column of the Pearl Harbor area eventhough
portions of it are coeval with the coral algal reef deposits
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1191 00 Page 13
This unit consists of terrigenous sediments which have been transported
by stream action from the upper areas of the Koolau Volcano to the
northern portion of the Pearl Harbor area The particle sizes run the
complete range from boulders to Clays and the deposits sometimes
consist of poorly sorted mixtures of different particle sizes
During the low stands of the sea the members of this unit were exposed
to subaerial weathering which caused consolidation of those soft soils
which were not removed by stream action In some places the consoli
dation was sufficient to create a weak siltstone or conglomerate In
other areas the deposits were deeply weathered into a uniform stiff
Clayey silt
With respect to structural considerations the formation is generally
not suitable to develop high Capacity end bearing however the older
alluvium is usually stiff enough to develop sufficient resistance to
Support low to moderate capacity friction Piles
Design of such friction piles will require consideration of many
factors which can be determined only from boring data for each
Proposed project These factors include the depth to friction
material the effects of overlying soft sediments and the possible
presence of artesian water in the older alluvium
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1191 00 Page 14
Sometimes gravel beds containing water under a hydrostatic head are
encountered in alluvial deposits If a pile penetrates such a
feature it could result in a loss of frictional capacity However
these gravel beds are usually isolated occurrences and the older
alluvium is usually sufficiently competent to develop friction piles
Honolulu Volcanic Series Tuff
This unit is the second youngest of the materials encountered in our
Study area It consists of volcanic ash which has consolidated or
hardened to form a medium hard rock which resembles siltstone or
sandstone usually overlying the coralline deposits or older
alluvium As previously discussed there are at least two members of
this unit however it is not practical to differentiate between them
Structurally the tuff is very competent and capable of Support ing
heavy structural loads on either spread foundations or end bearing
piles depending upon its depth from the surface However most of
the tuff is restricted to the eastern portions of the study area with
little or none encountered in the central and eastern areas Addi
tionally the tuff tends to thin out towards the west so that in some
areas it is less than 10 feet thick It is conceivable that a high
Capacity pile or pile cluster bearing on a thin layer of tuff could
result in punching failure through the thin layer
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 1191 00 Page 15
Any boring encountering this material should have sufficient penetra
tion into the tuff to see that there is adequate thickness to support
the foundation or that the tuff is underlain by competent subsoils
which will aid in distributing the foundation loads
Recent Alluvium and Fill
This unit is the youngest of the units encountered in our Study area
We have combined the recent alluvial deposits with the man made fills
since in most areas the fill was placed over the softer alluvial and
lagoonal sediments and therefore for heavy structural loads the
soft sediments are the dominant material for foundation design consi
deration
In general the recent alluvial and lagoonal deposits consist of very
soft gray to black organic silts and clays In terms of structural
usage these soft sediments have very little capacity for foundation
support Usually piles or caissons must be extended through the
recent sediments to underlying stiffer material
In areas near active streams such as Halawa Landing the soft
sediments are as thick as 130 feet It is possible that within the
channel areas they may extend to depths in excess of 200 feet below

pile foundations would be required for all of the bridge crossing

Previous boring data is not available in the central portion of
the harbor Most of the available boring data is along the
existing harbor facilities Based on the geology of the area
supplemented by well logs and near shore borings the basalt

2 Pile Capacity
For frictional piles the allowable pile capacity is related
primarily to the shear strength of the soil depth of penetra
tion into the stiff soil and allowable settlement of the pile
Therefore the frictional pile capacity could vary considerably
from 40 tons to in excess of 100 tons
Closer to the shoreline pile Capacities of about 75 to 100 tons
may be utilized if sufficient thickness of coral or tuff is
encountered
GEOLABS HAWAIl
W O 1191 00 Page 18
3 Lateral Loads
Lateral loads due to wind seismic and harbor traffic would have
to be considered in the design The harbor muds are soft and
can generally provide only low lateral pile resistance
Due to the long pile lengths deep soft soil and relatively hard
driving anticipated through the stiff alluvium special splice
design may be necessary for this project to insure that the
vertical and horizontal loads are properly transmitted through
out the pile lengths
4 Downdrag
When the ground surface surrounding a pile settles the soil
adjacent to the pile adheres along the length of pile surface
and exerts a downward load on the pile called negative skin
friction or downdrag This downdrag load reduces the available
pile capacity for structural loading and therefore is a very
important consideration in the Pile design The downdrag force
is a function of the shear strength of the surrounding soil
thickness of the settling soil layer amount of settlement and
Size of pile and pile group
GEOLABS HAWAII
The downdrag forces are particularly important near the bridge
approaches if abutment fills are required The new fills could
initiate additional settlements in the soft harbor muds and
produce significant downdrag forces Downdrag forces could also
affect the piles in the harbor since the upper harbor muds may
be underconsolidated and still undergoing settlement due to its
own weight
Special design such as using an outer casing sleeve using a
bentonite slurry or bitumen coating around the pile preconsoli
dating the soft soils or reducing the allowable pile capacity
may need to be considered
Pile Driving
Most of the bridge structure will be over water therefore pile
driving from a barge would be required Substantially slower
pile driving progress would occur due to the difficulty in
moving the pile driver setting up and locating the pile
locations
Relatively hard driving is anticipated through the stiff
alluvium to develop sufficient frictional resistance Sub
stantial deposits of boulders are not anticipated however
layers of gravel may be interbedded in the upper alluvial soils
which also could create hard driving

Abutments
The design of the bridge abutments will also be greatly affected by
the soil conditions Special considerations for the abutment design
are as follows
a Bearing Retaining structures for the bridge abutments would
require suitable bearing layers such as coral tuff or stiff
alluvium at shallow depth At Ford Island coral formation
suitable for bearing is anticipated along most of shoreline
areas In general along the shoreline areas of Pearl Harbor
competent bearing soils are relatively deep therefore it is
advisable to locate the abutments relatively far away from the
shoreline areas which are underlain by soft compressible
deposits
Filling If abutment fills are Placed over the soft recent
alluvial soils along the shoreline significant ground settle
ments would be anticipated Special filling procedures
including surcharging may be required As discussed previously
settlements of the soft soils could also initiate downdrag on
the nearby piles
For the above reasons it may be advisable to locate the bridge
abutments to where more competent soils are present and to minimize
new fills along the shoreline areas
