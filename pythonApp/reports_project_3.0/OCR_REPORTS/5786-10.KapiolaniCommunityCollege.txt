SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
A slope assessment and rockfall evaluation was conducted for the proposed
Culinary Institute of the Pacific at Diamond Head development in an effort to study and
provide mitigation recommendations for the potential rockfall hazards present at the
property Our ground surface reconnaissance at the project site consisted of assessing
the existing site conditions and identifying sources of potential rockfall hazards on the
hillside In addition rockfall simulation using computer modeling was performed to
estimate the rockfall behavior emanating from the identified source areas and to assist in
the selection and design of an appropriate rockfall hazard mitigation scheme for the
proposed development
Due to the relatively homogenous character of the volcanic tuff rock material
differential erosion of the natural crater slopes is limited Therefore the natural side slopes
of the crater typically are devoid of local ground surface relief and hence rockfall
producing rock outcrops and loose boulders Weathering and erosion of the natural
volcanic tuff slope surfaces typically produce soils gravel and smaller rock fragments
which are angular or tabular in shape The down slope movement of these rock fragments
generally is not conducive to increasing rolling momentum and the rock fragments
typically lodge on the slope in the existing vegetation within close proximity to their source
production
Based on our assessment of the potential rockfall conditions at the project site we
believe that the potential risk for dangerous rockfall conditions to affect the project site is
relatively low The slopes above the project site appear to harbor limited potential rockfall
sources because of the generally smooth low relief character of the exposed volcanic tuff
rock material The anticipated potential rockfall conditions are perceived to be more of a
minor debris accumulation issue which may occur along the upper boundary of the
property Therefore we believe that special provision for dangerous rockfall hazard
mitigation at the project site is not necessary Instead we recommend a 6 foot high
heavy duty chain link fencing be considered as a mitigation measure to reduce the
potential for small rock fragment debris to encroach upon the upper property boundary
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and proposed rockfall
hazard mitigation measure

The project site is at the former U S Army Cannon Club at the foot of Diamond

former U S Army Cannon Club site into the Culinary Institute of the Pacific at Diamond
Head In addition to the new building construction asphalt concrete paved parking lots
access roads site retaining walls drainage ditches stairs walkways and underground
utilities are also planned to serve the proposed new development
The existing hillside to the south side of the proposed development is on the order
of about 400 to 450 feet high which is considered average to moderate for the slopes on
the Island of Oahu The average hill slope inclination is approximately 1 7 horizontal to 1
vertical

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The younger
Koolau shield is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene Ice Age in age and
forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island Waianae Volcano became
extinct while Koolau Volcano was still active and its eastern flank was partially buried
below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank Both Waianae and Koolau
Ranges have lost most of their originai shield outlines and are now long narrow ridges
shaped largely by erosion
Following the cessation of the Koolau Volcano and about a 2 million year period
of volcanic inactivity dominated by erosional processes volcanic activity on the
southeastern end of the Koolau Range resumed with the eruption of some 30 or so
vents which represent the Honolulu Volcanic Series These secondary eruptions are
believed to have occurred scattered throughout a period of hundreds of thousands of
years extending from the late Pleistocene Age to the Recent Age
The project site is on the lower north facing slope of Diamond Head Crater
Diamond Head is a tuff cone formed by hydromagmatic eruption and consists of
volcanic ash that has been altered by weathering to palagonite Furthermore there are
scattered blocks of Koolau basalt material and fragments of coral limestone embedded
in the tuff deposits These materials were entrained by the hydromagmatic explosions
as the vent blasted through the underlying and older age Koolau basalt and coal reef
deposits
2 2 Site Geology
As previously mentioned the project site is located adjacent to Diamond Head
Road on the lower northerly facing slopes of Diamond Head Crater Diamond Head is a
remnant volcanic cone generally composed of layered volcanic tuff rock mudrock and

3 1 Rockfall Mechanisms
Rockfall involves the detachment and fall of rock material from the slope face
Rockslide is another term that may be used to describe the detachment from a slope
and fall of a mass of rock material Rockfall is a more general term used to describe the
process of falling rock from a hillslope Rockfall may encompass the fall of a single
boulder or rock fragment or a shower of boulders or rock fragments Some of the
applicable mechanisms generating rockfall events are described below
Loosening of rocks from the slope may occur due to a multitude of causes
including water pressure behind the rock differential weathering or erosion along
geologic contacts faults or shear zones weakening of rock due to temperature and
moisture cyclic fluctuations loosening by animals trees wind and ground vibration due
to earthquake seismic forces construction activity and vibration from loud sound
waves
A frequent cause of rockfalls is by invasive tree roots prying on the existing
cracks located in the rock slope surface Tree roots frequently develop and grow larger
in the discontinuities of rock materials The added force of wind action on tree branches
may create leveraged forces that are transferred to the roots lodged within the
discontinuity Over time the repetitive leveraging of the discontinuity can loosen rock
and cause it to fall
Inter layered rock such as lava flows with variable strength and rates of
weathering commonly exhibit accelerated erosion and rockfall The weaker layers
clinker tend to erode and undercut the more weathering resistant rock located above
This may lead to failure of undercut blocks of rock as support from the underlying
weaker layers is removed It is an especially important failure mechanism where thick

The construction of cut slopes create external disturbance to the slopes by

The daily cyclic temperature change from alternating exposure to sunlight and
shade as well as air temperature changes can accelerate erosion and weathering to
produce rockfall and landslide activity The effect of rock surface exposure to sunlight
does cause a significant fluctuation of temperature on the rock surface
Large trees that are dead or weakened by disease can come loose and fall from
the slope When the entire tree falls from a steep slope a potentially large amount of
entrained soil or rock material may accompany the root system The tree fall and
entrained earth material produces substantial debris that may encroach the lower
slopes In addition to the fall of the tree the movement of the tree downslope may
trigger additional slope instability and cause additional fall of loose rock and debris
Furthermore the portion of the steep slope that the tree once occupied may be left in a
state of instability The slope instability may consist of a pocket or void space and the
loss of the pre existing root network may upset the equilibrium of stability that existed on
the hillside
Groundwater seepage from a slope and high pore water pressure in a slope
reduces the shear strength of soil and rock materials leading to potential slope failure

Adverse geologic structures discontinuities may include the presence of

Adverse geologic structures have the potential to act as a focal point for the initiation of
rockfall and landslides as they are typically locations of low shear strength with the
potential for earth movement
3 2 Rockfall Hazard Analyses
The Colorado Rockfall Simulation Program CRSP is a computer program that is
a widely accepted engineering tool used by experienced personnel to estimate potential
rockfall behavior by simulating rockfall activity based on input parameters that are
selected on a site specific basis The input parameters for this project were selected
based on the observations and measurements collected in the field Basically the
program provides a statistical evaluation of potential rockfall behavior based on the
hillslope topographic profile and other input information such as rock size rock shape
and other parameters used to characterize the ground surface _ irregularities
Information obtained from the CRSP analysis included the predicted falling rock
velocity bounce height kinetic energy and roll out distance The output information is
useful to assist in the site specific design of various rockfall mitigation schemes such as
rockfall impact barriers or other rockfall containment systems
The CRSP was run utilizing four selected slope profiles located above the project
site as indicated on the Site Plan Plate 2 The slope profiles were created by
