REGIONAL GEOLOGY
Hawaii the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea which are both still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain which makes
up the west central part of the island The lava formation encountered at the site appears
to be of pahoehoe flow which spread and ponded as it approached the ocean
Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or billowy surface and an internal
structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are commonly encountered in pahoehoe
lavas These cavities were formed when the lava was still in a molten state and represent
both lava tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such as blisters and pockets
Lava tubes intra flow cavities are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow
leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large distance longitudinally
along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt are considered to be relatively permeable rock and can transmit water quite
readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions Water is transmitted through the
porous rock matrix along joints fractures and inter flow contacts cavities and along
clinker layers The permeability of the clinker and cavities is high therefore they serve
as the major water transmission features

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling four borings to depths
ranging between 5 and 10 feet below the existing ground surface The boring locations
were surveyed by a professional surveyor and the approximate locations of the borings
are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
In general our field exploration encountered volcanic rock consisting of hard basalt
from the surface to the maximum depths explored of about 5 to 10 feet below the existing
ground surface The basalt encountered ranged from very closely to widely fractured and
ranged from slightly to moderately weathered The basalt formation encountered included
dense and vesicular porous basalt
Cavities and clinker zones were not encountered in our borings within the basalt
formation at the site during our field exploration However based on previous experience
in the adjacent areas it is our opinion that cavities voids and clinker zones may exist in
the basalt formation and should be expected at the subject site

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the subsurface conditions at the project site consist
generally of basalt rock formation In general the basalt formation may have cavities and
potential clinker layers although they were not encountered in our borings Based on our
evaluation of the subsurface conditions with respect to the proposed development the
principal geotechnical engineering considerations for the proposed roadway and sewer
line construction at the Keahuolu Makai Area Development project include the following
1 grading of the proposed project site and pavement design for the
roadways
2 trench excavation and pipe installation for the proposed sewer line and
3 potential cavities and clinker zones beneath the subgrade and or fills for the
roadways and sewer line
Because of the substantial fills planned at the site we understand that fill materials
will need to be imported from adjacent or other areas Therefore proper specification of
the fill materials for the construction of the roadway embankment will be necessary

Detailed discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design
recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report
Site Grading
As previously mentioned construction of the proposed roadways will involve
embankment fills on the order of 5 to 20 feet in thickness Only minor cuts are
anticipated with some relatively deep excavations for the proposed sewer line trenches
Therefore we envision that the fill materials to be used for construction of the roadway
embankment will likely be imported from adjacent sites or other sources The
embankment fill material should consist of material with a maximum size of 12 inches
To reduce the potential for migration of fines from the roadway pavement section through
the voids of the embankment fill we recommend that the top of the fill be capped with a
12 inch layer of choker fill
Items of grading that are addressed in the following subsections include 1 Site
Preparation 2 Fills and Backfills 8 Boulder Disposal and 4 Excavation
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii
It is important that a representative from our office observe the site grading to evaluate
whether any undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and clearing
process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those encountered
in our exploration
Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork all areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3171 00 Page 9
unsuitable materials such as volcanic ash should be removed and disposed of
properly off site to reduce potential contamination of the excavated materials
In areas to receive fill the subgrade should be scarified to a depth of 12 inches
if possible and compacted to 90 percent relative compaction Otherwise the
subgrade should be proof rolled a minimum of four passes with heavy construction
equipment such as a D 9 bulldozer to help detect and collapse near surface
cavities Any loose areas or cavities disclosed during clearing operations should
be backfilled with compacted engineered fill Due to the dry and sometimes windy
site conditions it may be advisable to institute a dust control program during site
grading operations
Fills and Backfills
The fill materials to be used as general fill material in the roadway embankment
should consist of granular materials less than 12 inches in maximum dimension
Excavated on site material with maximum dimensions of less than 12 inches may
be used as source of fill or backfill materials In general we do not anticipate that
boulders materials greater than 12 inches in diameter will be used to construct
the embankment fill However if boulders are to be used in the embankment fill
placement of boulders in fills should be in accordance with the procedures
recommended in the Boulder Disposal subsection of this report
As previously indicated we recommend that the upper 12 inches of fill below the
finished subgrade consist of a choker fill layer The choker fill should consist
of well graded granular materials of 6 inches in maximum dimension with sufficient
fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the compacted mass If the rock
material does not contain sufficient fines to produce a well graded material off site
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3171 00 Page 10
cinders or soil may be mixed into the material to increase the percentage of fines
to provide a well graded material for compaction and to increase the stability of the
fills Imported material should be observed and or tested by Geolabs Hawaii for
its suitability prior to being transported to the site
Fill slopes should be constructed at slope inclinations of two horizontal to one
vertical 2H 1V or flatter Fill slopes should be constructed by over filling past the
design slope and cutting back to provide adequate compaction of the side slopes
If boulders will be used in the embankment fill it is recommended that the sides
of the fill slopes be capped with a 12 inch layer of choker fill material Fills placed
on slopes steeper than five horizontal to one vertical 5H 1V should be keyed and
benched
In general fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches
in loose thickness moisture conditioned and compacted to a minimum of
90 percent relative compaction The upper 2 feet of the fills and backfills below the
road subgrade level should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative
compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil
established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practicable in fills composed of
rock boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number
of passes of a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction
should be conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the
observation of a representative from Geolabs Hawaii On the basis of this testing
program the number of passes may then be used as the field criterion for
adequate compaction

Utilities
We understand that a sewer line of about 3 800 feet in length is planned for this
development We envision that most of the trenches for the sewer line will be excavated
in hard basalt rock or embankment fill As previously recommended placement of
boulders within the sewer line trench corridors and within the anticipated depths of the
sewer line should be avoided

To provide more uniform support for the sewer pipes and to reduce migration of
fines into the embankment fill we recommend that a granular bedding consisting of 12
inches of choker fill or 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
be provided below the pipes Self compacting granular materials such as No 3B Fine

generally less than 6 inches in maximum size such as the choker fill material and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Where trenches will be located in
paved areas the upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the road subgrade should
consist of select borrow material and should be compacted to not less than 95 percent
relative compaction
Pavement
We anticipate that asphalt concrete pavement will be used for the proposed
roadways We anticipate that the vehicle loading for the roadways of this project will
consist of mixed traffic usage with frequent passes Therefore we have assumed
moderate to heavy traffic loading conditions for pavement design purposes In general
we believe the paved roadways will be constructed on the embankment fill Since the
embankment fill will consist of at least 12 inches of choker fill layer we have assumed
that the choker fill or imported select granular material with CBR greater than 25 would

Percolation Testing
Constant head percolation tests were conducted in Boring Nos 2 and 3 to provide
data for design of the proposed drainage facilities The results of our percolation tests

formation ranged from 0 02 to 1 7 feet per minute Where drainage facilities will be
constructed in fills we believe that the fills would be more permeable than the in situ
basalt rock formation However it must be noted that the permeability of the basalt rock
is highly dependent upon the presence of fractures and clinker layers Therefore the

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling four
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 at the approximate locations shown on
the Site Plan Plate 2 The depths of our borings range from about 5 to 10 feet below the
existing ground surface The borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped
with rotary coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a near
continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A of Appendix A Graphic presentation of the
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in
the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Boring as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
Rock Quality RQD
Very Poor 0 25
Poor 25 50
Fair 50 75
Good 75 90
Excellent 90 100
