2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene in age and the younger shield Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to
early Pleistocene in age After a long period of volcanic inactivity during which time
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau shield volcanic activity returned with a series
of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations These series are referred to as
the Honolulu Volcanic Series
Schofield Plateau was formed when lavas from the Koolau Volcano ponded against
the already eroded slopes of the Waianae Volcano in the late Pleistocene Epoch The
dips of the lava beds generally range from 3 to 5 degrees The lava flows on the plateau
have weathered to depths of 15 to 30 meters and are characterized by the red colors of
the soil The deep narrow canyons such as Waikakalaua Kipapa and Kaukonahua are
a result of this weathering The brown and reddish brown soils generally consist of ancient
alluvial deposits overlying the weathered rock

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by stiff to very
stiff clayey silt soils extending to the maximum depth explored of 15 7 meters below the
existing ground surface Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project
site we believe that shallow foundations consisting of perimeter ring footing and individual
spread footing bearing on the recompacted in situ stiff soils may be used for support of the
proposed new reservoir
Based on our laboratory tests the silty soils exhibit a slight potential for shrinking
and swelling when subjected to moisture fluctuations To reduce the potential for distress
to the future slab on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of the silty and clayey
soils we recommend that the subgrade soils underneath the slab areas be properly
prepared and capped with at least 6 inches of compacted base course below the
reservoir slab areas The aggregate base course should be compacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction The subgrade soils should be scarified to a depth of
about 200 millimeters moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
The slab subgrade should be kept moist and free of shrinkage cracks prior to placement
of base course and concrete slab Our geotechnical engineering recommendations
pertaining to the design of foundation site grading and pavements are further
discussed in the following sections of this report
3 1 Reservoir Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site we believe that
the underlying very stiff to hard clayey silt residual soils would provide suitable foundation
support for the proposed water reservoir structure It is our opinion that perimeter ring
footing and spread footings with concrete slab on grade foundations may be used to
support the proposed reservoir structure

3 2 Slab On Grade
Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by clayey silty
soils with a slight expansion potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations To reduce
the potential for future distress resulting from shrinking and swelling of these clayey soils
due to changes in the moisture content we recommend that the subgrade soils below
the slab concrete be properly prepared and capped with at least 100 millimeter thick base
course materials The 100 millimeter compacted base course will also serve to provide
more uniform support for the reservoir slab The base course materials should be
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent of the maximum dry density as determined by
ASTM D 1557 Test Methods for Laboratory Compaction Characteristics of Soil Using
Modified Effort The in place density of the compacted base course shall be determined in
accordance with Standard Test Methods for Density and Unit Weight of
Soil in Place by the Sand Cone Method or ASTM D 2922 Standard Test Methods for
Density of Soil and Soil Aggregate in Place by Nuclear Methods Shallow Depths The
area adjacent to the reservoir slab should be backfilled tightly against the slab with
compacted on site soils and graded to divert water away from the slab to reduce the
potential for water ponding around the slab and foundation

3 3 Site Grading
Based on the proposed finish floor elevation and the existing grades at the reservoir
site we anticipate that site grading work for the proposed project will consist of major cuts
and fills within the limits of the reservoir structure to achieve the design finish grades
At the on set of earthwork the areas within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site Soft and yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should be
backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic soils
should be properly disposed of off site or used in landscaping areas if appropriate
The on site clayey soils generated from the cut areas should not be used as fill and
backfill materials below the reservoir footprint They should be disposed of properly
off site or used in landscaping areas if appropriate Aggregate base course material
should meet the material requirements for Aggregate for Untreated Base as specified in
the Hawaii Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works Construction
1994 Imported fill material if required should consist of non expansive imported
granular material such as crushed coral basalt or cinder sand The material should be
well graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 75 millimeters in largest
dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200
sieve The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a
maximum swell of less than 1 percent Imported fill material should be tested by Geolabs
for conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for its
intended use
We recommend that select borrow be used as backfill material around the tank and
compacted to a minimum of 90 percent of maximum dry density The top 600 millimeters
of the back fill should be impervious materials to limit the filtration of surface water The
impervious material should consist of fined grained soil with more than 70 percent passing
the No 200 sieve and a CBR swell less than 1 0 percent

Base course and imported granular fill materials should be moisture conditionéd to

3 4 Drainage
The finished grades outside the reservoir structure should be sloped to shed water
away from the foundations and slabs and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive
landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the above
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce water infiltration In addition drainage swales should be provided as
soon as possible and should be maintained to drain all surface run off away from slabs
and foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring and 3 under the reservoir footprint
extending to depths of approximately 15 7 meters below the existing ground surface and
one boring designated as Boring No 4 for the proposed drainage channel extending to a
depth of approximately 3 5 meters below the existing ground surface The borings were
drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight solid stem augers
The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil
Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 thru A 4
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
75 millimeter OD Modified California sampler with a hammer falling
760 millimeters and ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 50 millimeter OD standard penetration sampler with a
pound hammer falling 760 millimeters The blow counts needed to drive the
sampler the second and third 150 millimeters of an 750 millimeter drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Boring at the appropriate sample depths
