2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two main shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene Epoch in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
volcanic materials erupted from the Waianae volcano is collectively referred to as the
Waianae Volcanic Series
The younger shield volcano Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
Pleistocene Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island
The volcanic materials erupted from the Koolau volcano are collectively referred to as the
Koolau Volcanic Series Waianae became extinct while Koolau remained active Thus
Waianae s eastern flank was partially buried below the younger Koolau lavas that banked
against its eastern flank These banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred
to as the Schofield Plateau of Central Oahu The present day form of the Island of Oahu is
a result of stream dissection and mass wasting erosion of the steep valley walls
During the history of the evolution of the Island of Oahu multiple global
fluctuations in sea level occurred as a result of the advance and retreat of the great
continental glaciers These sea level changes occurred more substantially in the
Pleistocene Epoch and had some significant effect on the geologic evolution of the
Island of Oahu The changes in worldwide sea level affected the erosional baseline of
streams and caused local submergence and emergence of the coastal landforms with
respect to the level of the sea
The end of volcanic activity associated with the Koolau volcano was followed by
a long period of erosion whereby deep valleys were carved into the Koolau mountain
range The valleys were filled with thick accumulations of alluvial sediment as the island
slowly sank under the weight of the new landmass

The project site is located along the southern flank of Koolau The
geomorphology and subsurface conditions at the site are directly related to the
glacio eustatic fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the
genesis of Pearl Harbor and the Honolulu Coastal Plain The subsurface conditions at
the project site are dominated by fossil Pleistocene age alluvial soil deposits and related
deposits laid during high stands of the sea that are intercalated with terrigenous
sediments The base of the stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt
Evidence from wells drilled indicate that the Island of Oahu has subsided by as much as
1 200 feet since the cessation of this early volcanic activity Macdonald and
Abbott 1970
During that period of subsidence coral algal reefs began to grow on the southern
coast of Oahu forming broad bays with barrier reefs across its mouth The growth of the
reefs essentially kept pace with the rate of subsidence A series of lagoons formed
behind the barrier reefs and both terrigenous and marine sediments accumulated in the
lagoons Macdonald and Abbott 1970
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers advanced the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded and melted
global sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Oahu
remained essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in
nature These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both
higher and lower relative to the present sea level of Oahu
The higher sea level stands resulted in the accumulation of deltas and fans of
terrigenous sediments in the heads of the old bays accumulation of reef deposits at
correspondingly higher elevations and deposition of lagoonal marine sediments in the
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs The concurrent growth of reefs and lagoonal
sediments may have also resulted in deposits of coral algal limestone and marls within
the predominantly lagoonal sedimentary units

Method
Two categories of trenchless methods are available conventional and rehabilitation
trenchless Conventional trenchless methods consist of installing a replacement pipe
generally parallel to the existing pipe that includes horizontal directional drilling and
microtunneling We understand that conventional trenchless methods are not considered
for this project Trenchless rehabilitation consists of reusing the existing pipe or pipe
alignment and includes pipe bursting slip lining and Cured In Place Pipe CIPP methods
Slip lining consists of inserting a new pipe of smaller diameter into the deteriorated
host pipe CIPP consist of inserting a liquid thermoset resin saturated material into the
host pipe The liquid thermoset resin saturated material is inserted into the host pipe by

Pipe Bursting
Pipe bursting consists of winching a pneumatic powered bursting tool through an
old section of pipe breaking and cracking the old pipe as it progresses A new
pipe section is pulled into place behind the bursting tool replacing the old pipe
section High Density Polyethylene HDPE pipe is typically used with pipe
bursting
We believe that conventional open trench method was used for the installation of
the existing sewer line The open trench method consists of excavating a trench
wide enough for the sewer line installation placing the sewer pipe and backfilling
the open trench with backfill material During pipe bursting the soil surrounding the
bursting tool will be compressed In a backfilled trench compaction of the backfill
material will generally occur
Damage may occur to existing utilities generally within about 3 feet of the bursting
tool In areas where the existing utilities are within about 3 feet the utility crossings
may be exposed and the crossing utility moved further away from the bursting tool
during the pipe installation or the utility crossing may be supported in place to
reduce the potential for damage
For conditions where the bursting tool is close to the existing ground surface
surface heave may occur Minimum depth of ground cover should be provided to
reduce the potential for ground heave Generally minimum depth of ground cover
is determined by 10 times pipe upsize Pipe upsize is defined as the difference
between the inside diameter ID of the existing pipe and the outside diameter OD
of the new pipe Pipe bursting may be used in areas where the existing sewer line

Excavation Support
We envision that the proposed construction is located in city streets with various
traffic conditions Because of the limited space sloping of the sides of the
trenches may not be possible Based on the relatively deep excavations
anticipated at localized areas shoring of the sides of the trenches will be
necessary to facilitate the proposed trench excavations and to protect personnel
working in the trench
As previously mentioned various hard materials were encountered in our borings
Therefore we believe that it is not practical to use interlocking sheet piles to shore
the trench excavations Trench shields or other methods may be required The
excavation support and shoring system used must comply with all applicable safety
requirements It is recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained
by the contractor to design and evaluate the shoring system used The adequacy
and safety of the shoring installation should be made the sole responsibility of the
contractor
Even if good construction procedures are followed some movement of the shoring
system and the adjacent ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses
during excavation Due to the complexity of the stress changes it is difficult to
estimate accurately the magnitude of ground movement The magnitude of ground
movements also depends greatly upon workmanship such as how quickly and
tightly the shoring supports are installed the subsoil conditions the size of the
excavation and the rate of excavation Therefore it is important to realize that the
excavation shoring should be installed properly and as early as practical and if
necessary the adjacent ground should be continuously monitored for cracks dips
and or other indications of movements with instruments such as inclinometers

Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937
determinations were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification

Three Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are presented on the

results is provided on Plate B 1

samples of the soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in
soil classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B 2

evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested Results of the direct
shear tests are presented on Plates B 3 thru B 5
