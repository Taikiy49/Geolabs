INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Gymnasium Addition and Renovation project within the
Naval Computer and Telecommunication Area Master Station NCTAMS in the
Wahiawa area on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses of the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design
input only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be of
the middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island
The younger shield Koolau is estimated to be of the late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Epoch Ice Age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island
Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank was
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3419 00 Page 6
partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank These banked
or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau
The project site is composed of basaltic rock built by the extrusion of the lavas
of the Koolau Volcanic Series These rocks are generally characterized by flows of
jointed dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers In situ chemical
weathering of the Koolau lava flows has occurred for the last 1 to 2 million years The
weathering process has formed a mantle of residual soils which grade to saprolite with
depth In general saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the
tropical weathering of volcanic rocks The saprolite grades with depth to basaltic rock
formation

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration generally encountered stiff residual soils and hard saprolites
throughout the depths of the borings drilled at the subject site Based on the existing
topography and the proposed finish floor elevation new fills on the order of about 0 5
to 1 5 meters will be required to attain the design finish grades for the proposed
construction Based on the relatively competent subsoil conditions encountered in our
borings and the anticipated light structural loads it is our opinion that shallow
foundations bearing on the undisturbed in situ stiff soils or new engineered fills may be
used for support of the proposed gymnasium additions The proposed retaining wall
for the new access road at the north side of the project site may require drilled pier
foundation support due to the presence of the existing steep slope bank which may
present potential design and construction constraints with the use of shallow footing
foundations
Our laboratory swell test results indicate that the near surface soils exhibit slight
to moderate swelling and shrinkage potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations
Therefore we recommend that building slabs be supported on at least 300 millimeters
of select granular fill material for more uniform support Based on the CBR values for
the near surface soils we also recommend that a minimum 150 mm thick layer of
select borrow subbase material be provided below the new pavement areas Detailed
discussion of these items and our geotechnical engineering design recommendations
are presented in the following sections of this report

Slab On Grade
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by near
surface silty clay soils with swelling potential ranging from slight to moderate when
subjected to moisture fluctuations Unless floor slabs constructed above the moderately
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3419 00 Page 11
expansive soils are properly designed there is a potential for future distress to the
lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of these expansive
clayey soils due to changes in the moisture content To reduce the potential for
structural distress resulting from the swelling of the expansive subgrade soils we
recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with at least 300
millimeters of non expansive select granular fill material placed in accordance with the
recommendations presented in the Site Grading section of this report
For the interior building slabs not subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend
that a minimum 100 mm thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel

recommended on top of the gravel cushion layer Fifty millimeters of moist fine sand
should be placed above the plastic membrane to provide protection of the membrane
and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or
sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask any minor slab cracking
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic or equipment operation a
150 mm layer of base course compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative
compaction is recommended in lieu of the 100 mm thick cushion layer The 50 mm

relative compaction For the design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade reaction

Drilled Pier Foundations
The 2 5 meter set back requirement between the slope face and the retaining
wall footing may require deep footing excavations due to the relatively steep slope bank
encountered at the project site Therefore it is our opinion that structural slabs
supported by a foundation system consisting of cast in place concrete drilled piers
embedded in the stiff silty clay deposits may be considered for the new access
roadway The cast in place drilled piers would derive support principally from skin
friction between the pier shafts and the surrounding soils
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3419 00 Page 15
For the design of the drilled pier foundations an allowable skin friction value of
up to 40 kPa may be used for the stiff silty clay material The allowable skin friction
value may be increased by one third for transient loads such as those caused by wind
or seismic forces Load bearing contribution from the upper 1 5 meters of subsoils
below the top of the drilled pier should be neglected The drilled piers should have a
minimum diameter of 500 millimeters Drilled piers should have a minimum center to
center spacing of three times the diameter to avoid capacity reduction due to group
action
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the face of piers Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be
estimated using an equivalent fluid pressure of per square meter per
meter of depth down to a depth of 3 meters below the ground surface Below a depth
of 3 meters the passive resistance may be estimated based on a uniform pressure
distribution equal to 142 kilonewtons per square meter These values are ultimate
values therefore a safety factor should be included by the designer Unless covered
by slabs or pavements the passive resistance in the upper 2 meters of soil should be
neglected The passive resistance may be assumed to act over two times the diameter
of the piers
Uplift loads may be resisted by a combination of the dead weight of the structure
the weight of the pier and pier cap and by side shear along the shaft surface and the
adjacent soils Contribution of uplift resistance from the upper 1 5 meters should be
neglected A shaft resistance of 25 kPa may be used for the stiff clayey soils to resist
intermittent uplift forces

Construction Considerations
Performance of the drilled piers will depend significantly upon the contractor s
method of construction and construction procedures In our opinion the following
conditions would have a significant effect on the effectiveness and cost of the drilled
pier foundations
Soft ioose subsoils and or boulders may be encountered in the upper portion of
the existing steep slope To reduce the potential for caving in of the drilled holes
temporary casing for the upper 3 meters may be needed during construction of the
drilled piers Care should be exercised during removal of the temporary casing to
reduce the potential for necking of the concrete pier Therefore a minimum 2 meter
head of concrete should be maintained above the bottom of the casing during removal
of the casing Therefore we recommend that unit prices for casing and coring should
be obtained during bidding
The load bearing capacities of drilled piers depend to a large extent on the
contact between the pier shafts and the surrounding soils Therefore proper
construction techniques are important The contractor should exercise care in drilling
the pier holes and in placing concrete into the holes
Drilling by methods utilizing drilling fluids is not recommended A low shrink
concrete mix with high slump should be used to provide close contact between the pier
shafts and the surrounding soil The concrete should be placed in a suitable manner
to reduce segregation of the aggregates from the concrete mix

Site Grading
Based on the existing topography and the finish floor elevation of the proposed
facility we anticipate that site grading work for the proposed construction will consist

overlay to the existing pavements Pavement reinforcing fabric should be aligned and
placed with no wrinkles that lap If manual laydown methods are used the fabric
should be unrolled aligned and placed in increments of approximately 9 meters
Adjacent borders of the fabric should be lapped between 50 and 100 millimeters
Pavement reinforcing fabric should be manufactured from polyester polypropylene or
polypropylene nylon material The fabric should be non woven and heat treated on at
least one side Guidelines for specification of pavement reinforcing fabric are presented
in Appendix C of this report
Where the pavement has deteriorated the portion of the deteriorated pavement
should be repaired by removing the deteriorated pavement section together with any
loose soft subgrade soils The resulting excavation should be backfilled with aggregate
base course compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines may be required for the project A granular
bedding consisting of 150 millimeters of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67
gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining granular materials such as
No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation should also be used for the trench
backfill up to about 300 millimeters above the pipes to provide adequate support around
the pipes and to reduce the compaction effort of the backfill thus reducing the
possibility of damaging the pipes It is critical to use this free draining material to
reduce the potential of formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3419 00 Page 22
adequate support for the sides of the pipes which could result in settlement of the
backfill and damage to the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 300 millimeters above the
pipes to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of on site soils or
select granular fill material The backfill should be moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture placed in maximum 200 mm level loose lifts
and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction to reduce
the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches are below pavement areas
the upper 600 millimeters of the trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should
be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Drainage
The finished grades outside the gymnasium additions should be sloped to shed
water away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised
that gutter systems be installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted
away from the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near
the foundations and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should
be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the
subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting
of the concrete to reduce water infiltration

Drainage swales should be provided as soon as possible and should be
maintained to drain all surface run off away from the slab and footing foundations
