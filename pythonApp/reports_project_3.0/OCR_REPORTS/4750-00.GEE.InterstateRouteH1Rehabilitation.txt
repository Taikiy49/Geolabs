This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering evaluation and
analyses for the Interstate Route H 1 Rehabilitation project in the vicinity of Ola Lane to
Kalihi Street in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity

This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory

findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from two main shield
volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be middle
to late Pliocene Epoch in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island The
volcanic materials erupted from the Waianae Volcano are collectively referred to as the
Waianae Volcanic Series
The younger shield volcano Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene
Epoch to early Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern
two thirds of the island The volcanic materials that erupted from the Koolau Volcano are
collectively referred to as the Koolau Volcanic Series Waianae Volcano became extinct
while Koolau Volcano remained active Therefore Waianae s eastern flank was partially
buried below the younger Koolau lavas that banked against its eastern flank These
banked or ponded lavas formed a broad plateau referred to as the Schofield Plateau of
Central Oahu The present day form of the Island of Oahu is a result of stream dissection
and mass wasting erosion of the steep valley walls
During the history of the evolution of the Island of Oahu multiple global
fluctuations in sea level occurred as a result of the advance and retreat of the great
continental glaciers These sea level changes occurred more substantially in the
Pleistocene Epoch and had a significant effect on the geologic evolution of the Island of
Oahu The changes in worldwide sea level affected the erosional baseline of streams
and caused local submergence and emergence of the coastal landforms with respect to
the level of the sea
The end of the volcanic activity associated with the Koolau Volcano was followed
by a long period of erosion whereby deep valleys were carved into the Koolau Mountain
Range The valleys were filled with thick accumulations of alluvial sediments as the
island slowly sank under the weight of the new landmass

Approximately 850 000 to years ago volcanic activity resumed with
the eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series from secondary vents scattered across the
southeast portion of the Island of Oahu Some of the most notable landmarks of Oahu
such as Salt Lake and Diamond Head were created by the eruption of the Honolulu
Volcanic Series which included cinder and ash cones with some basaltic lava flows
The eruption of the Honolulu Volcanic Series continued over a period of several
hundred thousand years which ended about 30 000 years ago
The project site is located along the southern flank of the Koolau Volcano
adjacent to the coastal plain comprising the southern portion of the Island of Oahu The
Interstate Route H 1 Highway project site traverses east to west across the mouth of
Kalihi Valley which trends toward the southwest from the summit region of the Koolau
Mountain Range Portions of the floor and mouth of Kalihi Valley were covered by
basaltic lava flows Quaternary Honolulu Basalt associated with the more recent
Honolulu Volcanic Series The veneer of Honolulu Basalt is underlain by thick alluvium
and volcanic materials associated with the Koolau Volcanic Series Thus it is believed
that the hard basalt rock encountered at relatively shallow depths of the project site
represent the Honolulu Basalt geologic unit Because the Honolulu Basalt materials
were deposited over the dissected alluvial terrain the basalt rock may be encountered
sporadically and at variable elevations

Pavement failures were observed along some portions of the Interstate Route
H 1 Highway We believe that the pavement failures may be attributed to the softening
of the clayey subsurface soils encountered at the pavement subgrade level resulting
from moisture infiltration In general complete removal of the underlying clayey soils
and replacement with select granular fill materials would be required for the
reconstruction of the highway pavements exhibiting pavement failures However we
recognize that complete removal of the underlying clayey soils and replacement with
select granular fill materials would significantly increase the construction time and may
not be feasible nor practical due to the high traffic volumes of the highway at this
location Therefore we recommend that the existing pavements at these areas be
replaced with a new pavement structural section consisting of 2 5 inches of AC over
14 inches of asphalt concrete base ACB

area for long term clogging resistance The geotextile fabric would serve to provide

subgrade We recommend that a contingency fund be allocated for additional distressed

membrane for effective repair of pavement cracks and joints and for sealing of bridge
decks A unique asphaltic coating on the non woven fabric provides for a good bond
between the concrete bridge deck and the AC pavement overlay In addition the
Petrotac should extend a minimum of 10 feet beyond the bridge deck over the
expansion joints to aid in reducing the potential for reflection cracking along the
expansion joints in the bridge structure The approximate limits of the bridge area are
shown by the notation Il on the Site Plan Plate 3 3
3 7 New Pavement Construction
As previously discussed in the Existing Site Pavement Conditions section of this
report some of the observed distresses along portions of the subject highway are likely
caused by failures in the underlying base subbase and or subgrade materials In
general we recommend that these areas be reconstructed with a new pavement section

the softening of the clayey subsurface soils encountered at the pavement subgrade

3 8 Surface Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the subbase and base course and or subgrade soils Therefore the
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be maintained
to carry all surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface
water ponding should not be allowed anywhere during or after construction Development
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the Interstate Route H 1 Rehabilitation project
were explored by drilling and sampling eleven borings designated as Boring Nos 1
through 11 extending to depths of about 0 3 to 18 5 feet below the existing pavement
surface The approximate locations of the test borings are shown on the Site Plans
Plates 3 1 through 3 5 The borings were drilled by Hawaii Test Borings Inc using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous solid stem augers and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1
through A 11
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils
by driving a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30
inches In addition some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel
Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same
hammer and drop The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6
inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of
Boring at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any

5 foot core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 and Unit Weight ASTM D 2937 determinations
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties The results of these tests are presented on the logs of borings at the
appropriate sample depths
One One Inch Ring Swell Test was performed on a relatively undisturbed sample
to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface clayey soils The result of the test
is summarized on Plate B 1
One Atterberg Limits Test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected soil
sample to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits of the near surface clayey soils The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Two Sieve Analysis Tests ASTM C 117 C 136 were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on
Plate B 3
