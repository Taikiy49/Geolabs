PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located near the Sand Island State Recreation Area at the 
southern tip of Sand Island in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project 
location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that a shoreline condition 
assessment indicated that dramatic erosion and recession of the shoreline were 
observed along the on-land portion of the Sand Island Wastewater Treatment Plant 
(SIWWTP) 84-inch ocean outfall near the pipe stop gate structure.  Therefore, it is 
desired to construct a rock revetment for shore protection along the on-land portion of 
the buried outfall. 
In order to provide information to assist in the design of the shoreline revetment, 
a total of three on-land borings extending to a depth of about 38.5 feet below the 
existing ground surface were drilled along the proposed revetment alignment.  To 
supplement the subsurface data obtained from the on-land borings, Dynamic Cone 
Penetration (DCP) testing was also conducted at eleven selected locations along the 
revetment alignment.

SUBSURFACE CONDITONS 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling three on-land 
borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extending to a depth of about 38.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface.  The approximate locations of the drilled borings are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site generally is underlain by a 
surface fill layer, consisting of medium dense to dense silty sands, extending to a depth 
of about 5 feet below the existing ground surface.  Below the near-surface fill layer, 
lagoonal deposits consisting of loose to very loose silty sands and gravel and soft sandy 
silts were encountered to depths of about 29 to 32 feet below the existing ground 
surface.  Below the compressible lagoonal deposits, the drilled borings generally 
encountered a relatively thin coral ledge, on the order of about 3 to 4 feet thick, 
overlying loose to medium dense coralline detritus materials to the maximum depth 
explored of approximately 38.5 feet below the existing ground surface. 
We encountered shallow groundwater conditions in the drilled borings at depths 
of about 3.8 and 4.4 feet below the existing ground surface at the time of our field 
exploration.  These groundwater levels generally correspond to approximately Elevation 
+0.9 to +1.1 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL).  Due to the proximity of the site to the Pacific 
Ocean, groundwater levels at the site likely will be affected by tidal fluctuations and 
other factors, such as groundwater withdrawal, seasonal precipitation and storm surge 
conditions. 
In addition to borehole drilling and sampling, our field exploration program also 
included the performance of Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) testing at eleven 
selected locations along the proposed revetment alignment to provide additional data 
and information for our evaluation and analyses. 
The DCP test provides a measure of a material’s in-situ resistance to 
penetration. The test is performed by driving a metal cone into the ground by repeatedly 
striking it with a 17.6-lb weight dropped from a distance of 22.6 inches. The penetration

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site, we believe that 
the proposed new revetment could have a significant impact on the existing SIWWTP 
84-inch ocean outfall pipes.  Our field exploration results suggest that the existing ocean 
outfall pipes and manhole structures are generally underlain by highly compressible 
lagoonal deposits consisting of loose to very loose silty sands and gravel and soft sandy 
silts.  Due to the heavy weight of the armor rocks, the highly compressible lagoonal 
deposit could consolidate under the new revetment loads, resulting in potential 
settlement of the existing ocean outfall pipes. 
Based on our engineering analyses, we estimate the potential ground settlement 
below the existing ocean outfall pipes may be less than about 1 inch provided that the 
additional surcharge load on top of the existing ocean outfall pipes resulting from the 
revetment construction is less than about 400 pounds per square foot (psf).  Where 
there is new surcharge load being placed on the ground, a steel sheetpile wall should 
be installed alongside the existing ocean outfall pipes (front and back sides) to serve as 
a slip plane to reduce the potential impact of the new surcharge load on the existing 
pipeline.  Based on our field exploration results, we estimate that the sheetpile wall 
should bear on the hard coral formation anticipated at depths of about 30 to 35 feet 
below the existing ground surface, corresponding to about Elevations -25 to -30 feet 
MSL.  Where there is no new surcharge load placed on the back side (landside) of the 
existing ocean outfall pipes, the sheetpile wall on the back side of the existing pipeline 
may be omitted along those areas.

Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the proposed revetment alignment 
by drilling and sampling three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, 
extending to a depth of about 38.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The 
approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were 
drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers and coring 
tools. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through 
A-3.2. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained 
by using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, 
Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that 
uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core 
of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were 
described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the 
Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the 
publication “Suggested Methods for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in 
Rock Masses” by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Recovery (REC) may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the 
relative quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Recovery is defined as the actual 
length of material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.

A P P E N D I X   B 
Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the ocean side of the proposed 
revetment alignment by performing Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) tests at 11 
selected locations, extending to depths of about 55 to 120 inches below the existing 
ground surface.  The approximate locations where DCP tests were performed are 
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. Results are shown on the Dynamic Core Penetrometer 
Data Sheets, Plates B-1 through B-11. 
The procedures used in our DCP tests are in general accordance with 
ASTM D6951, Standard Test Method for Use of the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer in 
Shallow Pavement Applications.  The DCP apparatus consists of a 5/8-inch diameter 
steel rod with a 60-degree conical tip. The rod is topped with an anvil that is connected 
to a second steel rod. This rod is used as a guide to allow a 17.6-lb hammer to be 
repeatedly raised and dropped from a height of 22.6 inches. The connection between 
the two rods consists of an anvil to allow for quick connections between the rods and for 
efficient energy transfer from the falling weight to the penetrating rod. 
Once the test apparatus is assembled, the DCP is placed at the test location and 
the initial penetration of the rod is recorded to provide a zeroing scale. While holding the 
rod vertically, the weight is raised to the top of the rod (22.6 inches above the anvil) and 
dropped. The penetration of the rod is measured after each drop. 
In the DCP tests conducted by Geolabs, the same equipment described above 
was used to record the number of blows required to advance the cone in 1-inch 
(25.4-mm) depth increments, as shown on the field logs provided in Appendix B.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Six Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on 
Plates C-1 and C-2. 
One Direct Shear test (ASTM D3080) was performed on a selected sample to 
evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the material tested.  Direct shear test 
results are presented on Plate C-3. 
One Consolidation test with time rates (ASTM D2435) was performed on a 
sample of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility 
characteristics of the materials encountered.  Consolidation test results are presented 
on Plate C-4.
