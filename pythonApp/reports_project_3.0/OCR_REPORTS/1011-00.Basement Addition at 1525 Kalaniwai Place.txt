SITE CONDITIONS
The subject residence is situated on the eastern mountain
side slope up from the valley where Kalani High School is
located
The surface slope of the site varies from about 2 1 H V
in the higher rear portion of the house to about a 5 1
H V in the area under the house It varies to about
3 1 H V further down slope A 4 foot high retaining
wall separates the adjacent lower residence from the sub
ject property
The existing residence is a one story wood frame structure
primarily of post and beam construction on concrete footings
A 2 to 4 foot high retaining wall supports the house along
the entire up slope side of the house
The existing area under the house where the proposed basement
addition is located has a difference in elevation of about
5 to 6 feet
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS AND OBSERVATIONS
A soft and moist gray brown clay with cobbles and gravel was
encountered in all of the hand probings in the proposed base
ment addition area The thickness of this clay layer varies
from 1 to 2 feet Beneath this surface clay layer rocky
material was encountered in all of the hand probings These
hand probings were not able to penetrate into the rocky material

LABORATORY TESTS
Laboratory soil tests performed on the representative samples
consisted of Atterberg Limits one inch thick ring swell test
and natural moisture content determination
Our laboratory test results indicate that the existing sur
ficial soils can be classified as expansive Adobe clay
CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
The field exploration and laboratory testing disclosed a thin
surface layer of clayey soil underlain with hard basalt rock
formation Because of the inaccessible site for drilling
equipment no samples were taken of the rocky material It
is anticipated that jackhammer excavation into the underlying
basalt rock will be required for the basement foundation at
the subject site
Proper support and underpinning should be provided to the
existing house during the basement addition work
Foundation
Spread or continuous footing are recommended for the proposed
basement addition Footing should be placed upon the rocky
material for support An allowable bearing value up to
10 000 p s f is recommended for footings bearing on the
underlying rock material The above values are for total
dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third when
temporary loads are considered
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1011 00 P 5
Retaining Walls
Retaining walls and basement wall up to about 10 feet high
are planned for the proposed addition Well graded granular
material such as base course rock should be used as backfill
material behind the walls A good waterproof system should
be utilized to prevent moisture intrusion into the basement
walls All water run off should be diverted away from the
basement area
The retaining walls should be designed to resist the following
lateral earth pressure
Walls unrestrained at top 30 p s f equivalent
fluid pressure
Walls restrained at top 35 p s f equivalent
fluid pressure
Additional loads due to surcharge should be included where
applicable A friction factor of 0 65 can be utilized to
determine the sliding resistance of the wall
Additionally a passive soil resistance of 600 p c f can be
used for the underlying rocky material However the passive
resistance should be reduced by one third when combined with
frictional resistance
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1011 00 P 6
Slab On Grade
For slab on grade bearing on the rocky subgrade it is
recommended that a 4 inch rock cushion be provided under
a 4 inch concrete slab to evenly distribute the floor load
into the rock foundation
Where the lower portion of the slab may still bear on
the upper adobe clay it is important that the remaining
adobe clay below the cushion should be over excavated
and replaced with a select borrow material down a maximum
depth of 3 feet below the slab subgrade elevation
