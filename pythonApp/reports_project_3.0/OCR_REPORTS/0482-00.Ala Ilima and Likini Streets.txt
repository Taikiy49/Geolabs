SUMMARY
The proposed high rise apartment building can be
supported on piles bearing on mudrock the surface of
which is found from elevation 5 to 20 Sixteen inch
octagonal piles are recommended with a capacity of
100 tons The parking structure can be supported on a
mat foundation with a bearing value of 1500 psf
INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our preliminary soils and
foundation investigation for the proposed project The study
consisted of subsurface borings laboratory testing and engineer
ing analyses as outlined in our proposal dated
The scope of the investigation was to evaluate the nature of the
subsurface conditions and develop recommendations for use in
positioning the structures in the most feasible location on
the site and in the design of foundations

PROJECT DESCRIPTION
The property is located between Ala Ilima and Likini Streets
1 4 block east of Ala Nanala Street in the Salt Lake area of
Honolulu The site is relatively flat and split level with
the northern portion being about 4 feet below the southern
portion
A 24 story condominium tower and an adjacent parking facility
are presently planned The high rise tower located along the
southern portion of the property is proposed to be of concrete
construction Underground basements for the high rise tower are
being considered The parking structure would be located in the
northern portion of the property and two stories are presently
planned for with a partial basement Due to preliminary status
of this project the precise building loads are not known at this
time but relatively heavy column and wall loading should be
anticipated for the structures
FIELD EXPLORATION
The subsurface exploration consisted of nine 9 borings ranging
in depth from 35 to 67 5 feet The drilling was accomplished
using truck mounted rotary drilling equipment with continuous
flight solid stem auger The borings were continuously inspected
by an engineer or geologist from our firm

The approximate boring locations are shown on Plate 1 and the
boring logs are presented in Appendix A
In all borings either Standard Penetration Test or 2 4 inch
I D ring samples were taken at selected depth intervals not
exceeding 5 feet The Standard Penetration Test consists of
driving a 2 inch 0 D split spoon sampler 18 inches with a
140 pound hammer free falling a distance of 30 inches The
number of blows required to drive the sampler the last 12 inches
is termed Standard Penetration Resistance N and is an
approximate measure of the relative density or consistency of
a soil These resistance values are plotted on the boring logs
Samples obtained were visually classified in the field and repre
sentative material was placed in air tight containers and returned
to our laboratory for inspection and testing

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
Late in Oahu s geologic history hot magma rising in fissures
along the coast encountered sea water The subsequent hydro
magmatic explosions generated a series of tuff cinder and
breccia volcanic cones Salt Lake Crater is one of these cones
The cone rests on an old brown soil which can be seen in a
series of recent 1973 road cuts through its outer flanks
The ash and rock fragments were often welded together by heat
to a fairly tough rock Further consolidation of the tuff
occurred by alteration of the glassy material to the mineral
palagonite Since much of the original pore space was blocked
by welding and palagonization Salt Lake acted as a large reser
voir Rain water carrying sediment and dissolved minerals into
this basin The sediments were almost immediately deposited
The dissolved carbonates and salts were deposited during periods
of dessication and possibly by calcareous algae There is no
evidence of a Coral Reef underlying the subject site The
carbonate minerals are mixed with land derived debris in many
places beneath the site and are essentially unconsolidated
Later a lakeside marsh apparently covered the site as is evidenced
by carbonized root and stem fragments in some of the borings

ENGINEERING RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Our study indicates that the high rise apartment building should
be supported on a pile foundation bearing on the underlying
mudrock The parking structure may be supported on a mat founda
tion Additional subsurface information will be required after
the building locations and loads are finalized
Pile Foundations
Pile lengths on the site will be highly variable throughout
the site The surface of the mudrock varies from elevation 5
to 20 feet The depth of the mudrock is shallower in the south
east portion of the site hence the length of piles will be
shorter in this section The apartment building should be located
over this portion of the site to realize savings in foundation
costs
Pre drilling at pile locations will be necessary and difficult
due to the presence of boulders in the upper fill However if
a basement can be provided below the existing ground elevation
this will remove or reduce the depth of pre drilling through
the boulder fill This will also shorten pile lengths in addition
to providing more usable space e
Page 9

August 22 1973
The high rise tower should be supported on piles and bearing
on mudrock Sixteen inch diameter octagonal prestressed
concrete piles driven to firm bearing on the hard volcanic
mudrock should be capable of supporting allowable design loads
on the order of 100 tons each Settlement of individual piles
should not normally exceed 3 4 inch However once building
locations and magnitudes of foundation loads are determined
additional analyses should be performed This will require
drilling of deep borings in the high rise tower area to determine
more precisely the elevation of mudrock and the thickness of
soil below the mudrock layer to study the anticipated settlements
A static load test should be performed on a test pile prior to
ordering production piles in order to establish pile driving
criteria and to confirm the calculated pile capacity The test
pile should be placed near one of the subsurface borings The
test pile may be used for the foundations provided the test is
not carried out to failure
The final driving resistance for the recommended pile capacity
will be on the order of 100 blows for the last foot of penetra
tion using a hammer with a minimum driving energy of 24 000 foot
pounds

Mat Foundation
The two level parking structure may be supported on a mat type
foundation provided a full or partial basement level is incor
porated in the design It will be difficult to develop soil
bearing criteria at this stage however an allowable bearing
value of 1500 psf can be considered A more exact value can
be recommended when loads and depth of basement are determined
Dewatering
Dewatering of the basement excavation may be necessary if base
ments extend below Elevation 8 Dewatering if required
should be continuous during basement construction and as long
as necessary to ensure safe and satisfactory construction The
method of dewatering may be left open to the contractor provided
the excavation stability and satisfactory drawdown are maintained
and nearby structures are not damaged
Retaining Walls
Basement walls should be designed to resist lateral active earth
pressures For laterally restrained walls that will be back
filled after being tied into the structure the walls should be
designed to resist an equivalent fluid density of 55 pcf for
the portion of wall above and 90 pcf for the portion below the
highest anticipated groundwater level which is Elevation 8
Page 11
W O 482 00
August 22 1973
Cantilevered walls or those backfilled prior to connection to
the structure should be designed to resist fluid densities of
35 pcf and 80 pcf respectively for the portion of wall above
and below water level
Resistance to the lateral load may be provided by a combination
of passive pressure and friction Passive pressure may be taken
as that exerted by a fluid of density equal to 300 psf The
upper one foot of soil should be neglected for passive pressure
unless it is confined beneath a concrete floor slab The co
efficient of friction can be taken as 0 5 Lateral resistance
of piles will be provided in our additional study after founda
tion designs are established
Structural Fill
A six inch layer of compacted free draining granular fill should
be placed beneath all floor slabs Backfill for walls and uti
lity trenches etc should also be compacted free draining
granular material Compacted structural fill should be placed
in 6 inch layers and compacted to 90 of the laboratory maximum
density using ASTM D 1557 70 as a standard

LABORATORY TESTING
A laboratory testing program was developed to establish the
engineering properties of the soils and rocks encountered at
the project site The tests where applicable to the subject
project are included in this Appendix

Visual Classification All soil samples obtained from
the borings were brought to our laboratory where they
were visually reclassified to confirm or modify the
field classification prior to finalizing the Logs of

Water Content Determinations In addition to visual
classification typical samples were tested for natural
water content as an aid in soil classification and in
evaluating soil properties The water content values
are based upon the dry weight of the soil
Atterberg Limits Atterberg limit tests were performed
on selected clay and silt samples obtained from the
borings for the purposes of identification and correla

3 Consolidation Test Consolidation tests were performed on
selected undisturbed ring samples of the compressible
soils to provide basic data for making settlement calculations
Porous stones were placed on the top and bottom of the samples
to allow drainage Vertical loads were applied in increments
with each load increment being allowed to consolidate prior

and rebound was measured during the unloading portion
Consolidation test results are plotted in terms of percent
settlement versus applied loads
Swell Test The ring sample was placed between porous
stones and a 60 psf load was applied to the sample for a
24 hour period The difference between the initial and
final sample heights is the amount of swell which is expressed
as a percentage of the initial height of the sample

density of the soil At least three trial points at dif

density and optimum moisture content The test is run in
