INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed
for the proposed Manor at Kapiolani Green project located at TMK 3 Division 14
along Kinoole Street in Hilo on the Island of Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical recommendations based on our
review and analyses of the site conditions plans and subsurface information obtained for this
project These geotechnical recommendations are intended for the design of building
foundations slabs on grade retaining structures site preparation pavements and utility lines
for the subject project only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject
to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Hawaii the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago covers an area of
approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five shield
volcanoes These are Kohala which is long extinct Mauna Kea which has had activity during
recent geologic times Hualalai which last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and Kilauea
which are both still active
The project site is situated on the northeastern flank of Mauna Loa The site is
underlain by alluvial deposits and some fill placed during the development of the existing
structure The lava formation encountered at the site appeared to be pahoehoe flow which
spread and ponded as it approached the ocean Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth
rope like or billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock Cavities are
commonly encountered in pahoehoe lava These cavities are formed when the lava is still in
a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and inter flow cavities such
as blisters and pockets Lava tubes intra flow cavities are formed when molten lava drains
from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large
distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal
extent

Building Foundations
Based on the subsoil conditions encountered and the light structural loads anticipated
for the subject project it is our opinion that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread
GEOLABS INC

and or continuous footings may be used to support the proposed building Based on the
existing topography and the proposed finished floor elevation we anticipate that the new
building will straddle across cut and fill areas Due to the varying thickness of the soil cover
over the basalt rock formation we believe that there is potential for differential settlements of
the new structure Therefore we recommend that a 12 inch thick layer of select granular fill
be placed below the footings to provide more uniform support The select granular fill material
should be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures
An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 500 pounds per square foot psf may be used for
the design of footing foundations bearing on the select granular fill material This bearing
value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads
such as those caused by wind or seismic forces
The footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest adjacent
finished grade Where footings are located adjacent to slopes or on slopes the footings
should be embedded to a depth to provide a minimum horizontal setback distance of at least
6 feet measured from the edge of the footing to the face of the slope If foundations are
located next to utility trenches or easements they should be embedded below a 1H 1V
imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the
inverts of the utility lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent
below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for
foundation settlement
If the footings are designed and constructed in accordance with the recommendations
presented herein total settlement of footings is estimated to be on the order of 1 inch with
differential settlements on the order of inch

Foundation Probing
As mentioned previously the new building will straddle across cut and fill areas The
new footings near the existing building will bear on about 1 to 2 feet of compacted soil
underlain by the basalt formation Based on our previous experience in the vicinity cavities
and or voids may be present in the basaltic lava flows encountered at the project site
Due to the low structural loads and the thickness of the fill and alluvium over the basalt
formation we believe that the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from collapse
of the cavities below the foundations is relatively small If it is desired to further reduce the
potential for loss of foundation support then a program of cavity probing and grouting at the
foundation locations near the existing building may be conducted during construction
For estimation purposes we recommend that probes be drilled at each interior
foundation location and at 10 foot on center for the perimeter wall foundation The probe
holes should be at least 3 inches in diameter and should extend to at least 10 feet below the
planned bottom of foundation elevation If cavities and or voids are encountered or suspected
during the probing operation additional probes should be drilled at closer spacing to help
delineate the vertical and lateral extent of the cavity and or void The probe holes and cavities
discovered should be backfilled with sand cement grout injected at low to moderate pressures
A typical sand cement grout should have a slump of about 6 to 9 inches

The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of a

Underpinning of the Existing Structure
We understand that the basement level for the proposed building will be adjacent to the
existing structure Construction of the basement level will require excavation adjacent to the
existing structure which will likely expose the existing footings Excavations of up to about
12 feet below the existing ground surface will be required for the construction of the basement
Our field exploration encountered loose gravelly soils in our boring adjacent to the existing
structure Based on the materials encountered we believe that there is potential for
settlement of the existing structure due to the adjacent lateral ground movement caused by
the migration of the gravelly soils into the basement excavation
Based on our site reconnaissance and boring boulders were exposed at the ground
surface and hard to very hard basalt formations were encountered at a depth of about 13 feet
below the existing ground surface Due to the difficulty of installation through the basalt
boulders and penetration into the hard to very hard basalt formations it is not practical to use
sheet pile shoring to reduce the settlement of the existing structure due to the adjacent lateral
ground movement Therefore we recommend that underpinning of the existing structure be
utilized to reduce settlement of the existing structure
There are several different methods that can be implemented to underpin the
foundations Some of the common types that are used include helical anchors and steel pipe

layer In this case the underpinning system should extend down to the hard to very hard

be made the responsibility of the contractor

Retaining Structures

Retaining Structure Foundations
In general we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed in accordance
with the recommendations presented in the Building Foundations section of this
report Wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches Wall foundations on

extend deeper to obtain a minimum 8 foot setback distance measured horizontally from

to the direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings

Cut and Fill Slopes
Cut and fill slopes planned for the subject project should be constructed at an inclination
of 2H 1V or flatter Based on the boring cut slopes will likely expose the loose to dense silty
and clayey basalt cobbles and gravel
Fill slopes should be keyed and benched into the existing slope to provide stability of
the new fill against sliding The filling operations should start at the lowest point and continue
up in level horizontal compacted layers in accordance with the fill placement recommendations
presented above Surface water should be diverted away from the tops of slopes and slope
planting should be provided as soon as the cut and fill slopes are completed to reduce the
potential for erosion of the finished slopes

Drainage
The finished grades outside the structure should be sloped to shed water away from
foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems be
installed around the building and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation
areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be avoided
Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce
water infiltration into the subsoils
The foundation excavations should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab
footings immediately after setting of the concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales
should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface run off
away from the slabs and footing foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to depths of about 18 5 to
20 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 The borings were drilled using both portable and truck mounted drilling equipment
equipped with continuous flight augers wash boring and coring tools
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who observed the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation
and testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered is presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Core samples of the dense basalt rock formations encountered at the site were
obtained using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with
ASTM Standard Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core
drilling is a rotary drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation
The material left in the hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and
classification
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered from
a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 4 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 foot core run the recovery would be 80 percent and would be shown
on the Logs of Borings as REC 80
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative quality
of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling induced
fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 foot core run the
RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as RQD 50
Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
