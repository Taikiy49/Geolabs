This letter report presents our findings and recommendations resulting from our 
field dynamic cone penetrometer (DCP) testing program and our engineering analyses 
performed for the Alu Like Building Foundation Evaluation project in the Kakaako area 
of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is located at the existing Alu Like Building at 458 Keawe Street in 
the Kakaako area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The existing building is a 
two-story structure supported on shallow spread and/or continuous footing foundations. 
The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, 
Plate 1. 
Based on the information provided, it is desired to renovate the existing building 
by converting the second floor into dining, restaurants, and possibly light storage which 
will increase both the dead and live loads of the second floor. The Alu Like Building is 
supported on shallow footing foundations and our experience in the area suggest that 
the project site is likely underlain by soft and/or loose lagoonal deposits at relatively 
shallow depths; therefore, it is desired to evaluate the magnitude of potential footing 
settlement due to the increased loading resulting from the future planned use of the 
building. 
REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from 
the Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to 
be middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene

to early Pleistocene in age. After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time 
erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Volcano, volcanic activity returned with a 
series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These series are 
referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series. 
The project site is on the coastal plain of Southern Oahu. The coastal plain was 
built on the eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano, which forms the eastern two-thirds of 
the Island of Oahu. The coastal plain was built by extensive accumulation of alluvium 
derived from erosion of the volcano, interbedded with coral reefs and associated 
deposits. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. Most of the coastal plains were developed during the 
Pleistocene Epoch when the sea levels fluctuated significantly. As the glaciers grew and 
advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell 
below the present stands of the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess 
of water became available such that the sea levels rose to above its present level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. 
When the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations. 
The project site generally is underlain by deposits consisting of calcareous 
sediments and lagoonal deposits. A surface layer of man-made fill was placed over 
these deposits for development of the Kaka’ako area within the last century. 
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
A field exploration program consisting of the performance of Dynamic Cone 
Penetration (DCP) testing was conducted at selected locations within the building 
structure to evaluate the subsurface conditions underlying the project site. 
Three footings were exposed by cutting through the existing ground floor 
slab-on-grade and excavating the fill material down to approximately the bottom of 
footing elevation. Three DCP tests, designated as P-1, P-2, and P-3, were conducted at 
the exposed footing near the southwestern entrance, an interior footing near the 
northwest wall, and a wall footing along the northwest wall, respectively. The DCP tests 
were extended to depths of about 10.4 to 11.1 feet below the bottom of footing 
elevation, corresponding to elevations of about -7.9 to -9.1 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL), 
respectively. The approximate DCP test locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Results of the dynamic cone penetrometer tests performed are presented on Plates 3.1 
through 5.4.

Based on our field observations and measurements, it appears that the top of the 
ground floor slab-on-grade is approximately 2.5 feet above the exterior sidewalk 
elevation, corresponding to a top-of-slab elevation of about +7.5 feet MSL. Beneath the 
approximately 4-inch thick concrete slab, a mix of fill material, consisting of silty/clayey 
sands with some coralline gravel and cobbles, was observed extending to the bottom of 
footing elevation, at about +2.7, +2.2, and +3.0 feet MSL for P-1, P-2, and P-3, 
respectively. Based on our review of available subsurface data in the proximity of the 
project site and the results of our DCP testing, we envision that the project site is 
underlain by approximately an additional 2 feet of fill materials followed by relatively 
loose lagoonal deposits extending to approximately -15 feet MSL.  
We did not observe groundwater during our DCP testing. However, based on the 
existing ground surface elevations and our experience in the area, we envision that 
groundwater may be encountered at elevations between about -2 and +2 feet MSL.  
OBSERVED DISTRESSES 
In general, building distresses were observed near the northeastern wall adjacent 
to Pohukaina Street. The observed distresses included cracks in the existing 
slab-on-grade, cracks in the CMU walls, and apparent “bowing” of the slab-on-grade 
around gridline intersection C-8. Separation between the northeastern stairway wall and 
the slab-on-grade was also observed. Based on the observed distresses, it appears that 
some settlement has occurred at the project site, especially within the northeastern 
portion of the Alu Like Building.
