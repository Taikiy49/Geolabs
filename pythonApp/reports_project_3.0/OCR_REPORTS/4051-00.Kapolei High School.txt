INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Kapolei High School within the Villages of Kapolei
development in the District of Ewa on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations site
grading and pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are
subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from two
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae is estimated to be
middle to late Pliocene in age and Koolau is estimated to be late Pliocene to early
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4051 00 Page 6
Pleistocene in age As the volcanic activity in Waianae ceased lava flows from Koolau
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau now known as the
_ Schofield Plateau The high school site is located on the southwestern flank of the
Waianae Volcano
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of Schofield Plateau
generated sediments which were transported to the coast In the vicinity of the project
site and to the south these sediments accumulated and are interbedded with marine
sediments and coral algal reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of
the sedimentary wedge ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway
to over 1 000 feet at Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the
confining formation or caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu
Deposition of sediments has continued from earlier geologic time through the present
Agricultural development within the last 100 years and recent mass grading work have
brought the Villages of Kapolei area to its present form

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling 30 borings designated as
Boring Nos 1 through 30 at the proposed high school site ranging in depths from about
11 5 to 31 5 feet below the existing ground surface In addition four bulk samples of the
near surface soils were collected at selected locations across the project site to evaluate
the pavement support characteristics of the near surface soils The approximate
locations of the borings and bulk samples are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
Our field exploration indicates that the majority of the project site is generally
underlain by near surface fill materials consisting of very stiff to hard silty clays and clayey
silts extending to depths of about 6 to 10 feet below the existing ground surface Below
the near surface clayey fill soils medium dense to dense coral sands and gravel were
encountered in some of the drilled borings extending to the maximum depth explored of
approximately 31 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Toward the northwestern portion of the project site our field exploration
encountered different near surface fill materials consisting of medium dense to dense
coral sands and gravel extending to a depth of about 10 feet below the existing ground
surface The surface granular fills were underlain by stiff to very stiff sandy silts overlying
dense to very dense coral sands and gravel extending down to the maximum depth
explored of about 26 5 feet below the existing ground surface
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled borings during our field
exploration In general the site is at an elevation several tens of feet above the highest
basal water table in the area However groundwater levels can vary significantly
depending on rainfall and other factors

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration indicates that the project site is generally underlain by very
stiff to hard silty clays and clayey silts and medium dense to dense coral sands and
gravel extending to the maximum depth explored of about 31 5 feet below the existing
ground surface Based on the subsoil conditions encountered at the project site we
believe that shallow foundations consisting of spread and or continuous footings bearing
on the recompacted in situ stiff dense soils or new compacted fills needed to achieve the
final design grades may be used for support of the proposed new facilities
Based on our laboratory tests the near surface clayey soils exhibit moderate
shrink swell characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents
As a result we recommend that the moderately expansive clayey soils be capped with a
minimum of 12 inches of non expansive select granular capping fill material below the
building slab areas In addition special attention should be given to the recommended
procedures for subgrade soi preparation below slabs and pavements in order to reduce
the potential shrink swell effects of the near surface clayey soils
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils are
critical elements of earthwork observations and soil density tests should be performed by
Geolabs Hawaii during site grading to assist the contractor in obtaining the required
degree of compaction and the proper moisture content on each fill lift Where compaction
is less than required additional compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of
the moisture content as necessary to obtain the specified compaction Our geotechnical
engineering recommendations pertaining to the design of foundations site grading and
pavements are further discussed in the following sections of this report
Foundations
Based on the current grading concept and the generally stifffdense subsoil
conditions encountered at the site we believe that shallow spread and or continuous
footings may be used for support of the proposed new building construction An
allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 pounds per square foot psf may be used for
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4051 00 Page 9
the design of shallow foundations bearing on the recompacted stiff dense surficial soils or
compacted fills needed to achieve the final design grades This bearing value is for dead
plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 3 for transient loads such as those
caused by wind or seismic forces
For footings bearing on the stiff dense soils the bottom of the footing excavations
should be recompacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction to provide a relatively
firm and smooth bearing surface prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete If soft
and or loose materials are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations they
should be over excavated to expose the underlying firm materials The over excavation
may be backfilled with on site soils compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative
compaction or the footing bottom may be extended down to the underlying competent
materials For footings bearing on new compacted fills the bottom of the footing
excavations should also be recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction
prior to placement of reinforcing steel or concrete
Lateral loads acting on the structures may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system A
coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used for footings bearing on the in situ stiff dense soils
or compacted fills Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of depth pcf This
assumes that the soil around footings is well compacted The passive pressure should
be reduced for foundations located on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slabs
the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 and 24 inches below
the lowest adjacent finished grades for the one and two story buildings respectively For
footings constructed near tops of slopes or on sloping ground the footings should be
embedded deep enough to provide a minimum horizontal set back distance of 8 feet
measured from the outside edge of the footings to the slope face Footings located

surcharging the retaining wall foundations or the retaining walls should be designed to
resist the additional structural loads

lines This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below grade

Slabs On Grade
As previously indicated the majority of the project site is generally underlain by
moderately expansive near surface clayey soils Unless slabs on grade constructed
above these expansive soils are properly designed there is a potential for future distress
to the lightly loaded slabs on grade resulting from shrinking and swelling of these clayey
soils due to changes in the moisture content To reduce the potential for structural
distress resulting from the swelling of the subgrade soils we recommend that the
subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with at least 12 inches of non expansive
select granular fill material below the slab cushion The capping fill should also extend
beyond the building perimeter a minimum of 24 inches
GEOLABS HAWAITI
W O 4051 00 Page 11
In accordance with the requirements of D A G S Technical Memorandum No 61
dated a basaltic termite barrier BTB will need to be incorporated into the
slab on grade design of new facilities Therefore for the interior building slabs not
subjected to vehicular traffic we recommend that a minimum 4 inch thick layer of cushion
fill consisting of BTB be placed below the slabs to provide more uniform support of the
slabs and to serve as a termite barrier To reduce the potential for future moisture
infiltration and subsequent damage to floor coverings an impervious moisture vapor
barrier such as a plastic membrane is recommended on top of the BTB cushion layer
Two inches of BTB should be placed above the plastic membrane to provide protection of
the membrane and to aid in curing of the slab concrete Flexible floor coverings such as
carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since they can better mask minor slab
cracking
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic or equipment operation such
as the Central Plant Building a 6 inch layer of base course compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction is recommended in lieu of the 4 inch thick BTB cushion
layer The 2 inch BTB and vapor barrier may also be omitted for these slabs The base
course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction For the design of structural slabs a modulus of subgrade
reaction of about 250 pounds per square inch per inch of deflection pci may be used for
the compacted base course Where slabs are intended to function as rigid pavements a
minimum slab thickness of 6 inches may be used for preliminary design purposes
Provisions should be made for proper load transfer across the slab joints which are
subjected to vehicular traffic The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to unpaved areas
should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
To reduce the potential for distress to the building slabs on grade and foundations
resulting from moisture infiltration we recommend that a concrete skirt be provided
around the building perimeter Concrete sidewalks walkways are anticipated for the
project and should be supported on 6 inches of non expansive select granular fill or select
borrow subbase The select granular fill or select borrow subbase should be compacted
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4051 00 Page 12
to at least 90 percent relative compaction Construction joints should be provided at
intervals equal to the width of the sidewalk walkway with expansion joints at right angle
intersections
Based on our experience with the Kapolei Middle School project minor differential
slab movement between the building slab and the abutting sidewalk walkway slabs were
observed in some localized areas We believe that this may be attributed to the lack of
maintenance of the sidewalk walkway subgrade moisture content after its initial
preparation It should be noted that the moisture requirement of the on site fills and
subgrades at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture is an important requirement
considering the moderately expansive nature of the on site clayey soils Therefore the
subgrade soils underneath the sidewalk walkways should be _ properly
moisture conditioned and maintained until the placement of the capping fill and concrete
To further reduce the potential for differential slab movement of the proposed
development we believe that the non expansive select granular fill or select borrow
subbase underneath the abutting sidewalk jwalkway may be increased to 12 inches
matching the thickness of the non expansive select granular fill recommended
underneath the building slabs Alternately consideration may be given to structurally
connecting the two abutting slabs together with dowels or other structural connections
it should be emphasized that the area adjacent to the slabs should be backfilled

from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the slabs and foundations

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed Vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of properly off site The near surface
soils encountered in cut areas may be stockpiled and re used as a source of
general fill material provided that they meet the requirements for general fill
presented in the Fills and Backfills subsection of this report
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill should be over excavated to expose firm material and
the resulting excavation should be backfilled with well compacted fills The
excavated soft and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site and or
used in landscape areas where appropriate Contract documents should include
additive and deductive unit prices for over excavation and compacted fill
placement to account for variations in the over excavation quantities

fills should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at

density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same

maximum dry density

particle sizes of 6 inches or less in maximum dimension The material should have
a laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 8 or higher and a swell
potential of not more than 3 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1883
The 12 inch non expansive select granular fill required under the building slabs
should consist of non expansive select granular material such as crushed coral or
basalt The material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles
larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and
30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material should have a
laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum swell of
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883
Aggregate base course and select borrow subbase materials should meet the
material requirements for Base Course and Subbase Course as specified in
Sections 30 and 31 respectively of the Standard Specifications for Public Work
Construction Department of Public Works City and County of Honolulu
September 1986 Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs Hawaii for
conformance with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for
the intended use
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
General fill materials should be moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above
the optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose
thickness and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The
non expansive select granular fill materials should be placed in level lifts of about
8 inches in loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 4051 00 Page 18
and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Select borrow subbase
and base course materials should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum
moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction Relative
compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of
the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance with
ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Because moisture conditioning and compaction of the clayey subgrade soils are
critical elements of the earthwork observations and soil density tests should be
performed by Geolabs Hawaii during the site grading operations to assist the
contractor in obtaining the required degree of compaction and the proper moisture
content on each fill lift Where compaction is less than required additional
compactive effort should be applied with adjustment of moisture content as
necessary to obtain the specified compaction It should be noted that the
moisture requirement of the on site fills and subgrades at least 2 percent above
the optimum moisture is an important requirement considering the moderately
expansive nature of the on site clayey soils

Utility Trench
We anticipate that the utilities for the proposed campus development will consist
primarily of water drainage and sewer In general good construction practices should
be utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trench for the proposed utilities The
method and equipment to be used for excavation should be determined by the contractor
subject to practical limits and safety considerations The excavation should comply with
the applicable local state and federal safety requirements Trench shoring design and
installation should be the responsibility of the contractor Trench shoring and bracing
should conform to the appropriate health and safety requirements
Based on the results of our field exploration it appears that in general
conventional excavation methods may be utilized for utility line trenching at the site The
near surface soils encountered at the site are generally stiff to medium dense However
deeper excavation may encounter dense to very dense coral sands and gravel as
described in the boring logs
In general for support of the utility lines we recommend that a granular bedding
consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation be used

under the pipes The initial trench backfill up to about 1 foot above the pipes should
consist of free draining backfills such as No 3B Fine gravel to reduce the potential for
damaging the pipes from compaction of the backfill It is critical that a free draining
granular material be used to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the
haunches of pipes and to provide adequate support for the sides of the pipes Use of the
on site clayey soils as initial trench backfill immediately around utility pipes is not
recommended
We recommend that a minimum cover of 3 feet be provided between the top of the

compaction of the trench backfill The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level

trench backfill below the pavement subgrade should be compacted to not less than
95 percent relative compaction Mechanical compaction equipment should be used to
compact the materials at the project site Water tamping jetting or ponding should not
be used to compact the backfill material considering the expansive nature of the on site
soils
Drainage
As previously indicated proper surface drainage is critical to the performance of
foundations constructed on expansive soil conditions Therefore the finished grades
outside the structures should be sloped to shed water away from foundations and to
reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter systems be installed around
the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from the foundation and pavement
areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations and slabs should also be
avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or have concrete bottoms and
drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
GEOLABS HAWAIi
W O 4051 00 Page 23
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce the potential for water infiltration In addition drainage swales
should be provided as soon as possible and should be maintained to drain surface water
run off away from slabs and foundations

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Twenty two one inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near surface soils under
different surcharge loads The results of these tests are summarized on Plates B 1
through B 3
Five Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are presented on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B 4
Four sieve analysis tests ASTM C 136 were performed on selected soil samples
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Graphic presentations of the grain size distribution are provided on Plate B 5
Four laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed on
bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of
the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 6 through B 9
Four Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates B 10 through B 13
