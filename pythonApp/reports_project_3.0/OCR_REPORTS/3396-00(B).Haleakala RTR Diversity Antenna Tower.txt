INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the Haleakala Remote Transmitter Receiver RTR Antenna Tower
located in Haleakala on the island of Maui Hawaii The general location and vicinity
of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
GEO ARGC_HAWAII
W O 3396 00 B Page 2
This report summarizes the findings and geotechnical engineering recom
mendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These geotechnical engineering recommendations are intended for the
design of foundations and site preparation only The findings and recommendations
presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The Haleakala RTR antenna tower site is located approximately one mile
westerly of the summit of Haleakala on the island of Maui Hawaii The site is situated
near the end of a narrow ridge dropping off to the northwest and southeast
It is proposed to construct a 20 foot high diversity tower down the hill on the
northwest side of the existing equipment building We understand that a specific model
for the proposed antenna tower has not been selected at the time this report was
prepared Based on the information provided we anticipate that the antenna tower will
be relatively light in weight but will be subjected to high uplift forces and moment at the
foundation level
Detailed information pertaining to site grading were not known at the time this
report was prepared However we understand that the Federal Aviation Administration
FAA generally plans to construct on a level site for the installation of the antenna
tower Based on the existing topography at the project site we anticipate that the site
grading work would involve cuts into the existing steep slope to create a level pad for
the proposed antenna tower Therefore a retaining wall may be required for the project
construction

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Maui is composed of two shield volcanoes West Maui and
Haleakala built up by the extrusion of basaltic lavas during the Pleistocene Epoch
East Maui or Haleakala Volcano which is much larger was formed later and banked
against the West Maui mountains A flat isthmus connects the two volcanoes
The primitive shield of Haleakala volcano is composed of pahoehoe and a a
flows Near the end of the Kula eruptions the Haleakala volcano rose to a summit
possibly 1 000 meters higher than the present crater rim At the end of the
accumulation of the Kula Series volcanic activity became very infrequent and stream
erosion began to make progress in wearing away the mountain and carved master
valleys Finally after this period of erosion volcanic activity resumed The principal
rift zones reopened right across the depression at the top of the mountain and on down
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3396 00 B Page 5
the flanks and the great erosional valleys were flooded with lava flows and filled with
cinder cones
SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located at the summit of Mount Haleakala at the Federal
Aviation Administration RTR site on the island of Maui Hawaii The proposed antenna
tower will be situated on a one horizontal to one vertical 1H 1V slope below the
existing RTR facility on the northwest side Our field observations indicate that the
steep slope is generally composed of loose volcanic cinder sands and gravels with
some cobbles Local outcrops of welded cinder sand and gravel were noted
approximately 5 feet upslope of the proposed antenna location These outcrops exist
as vertical exposures of the welded cinder material standing approximately 5 to 7 feet
in height above adjacent grade Excavation of the outcrops with a pick reveals the
material is cemented welded and difficult to pick by hand The existing ground
surface elevation of the site varies from approximately 9 600 to 9 700 feet Mean Sea
Level MSL
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Since vehicular access to the proposed antenna location was not possible due
to the very steep and loose nature of the slope surface we have modified our field
exploration program at the site by hand digging a shallow test pit at the proposed
antenna tower location to observe the shallow subsurface conditions This was
supplemented with drilling and sampling one boring to a depth of approximately 26
feet below the existing ground surface in the vicinity of the proposed antenna tower
location The approximate location of the boring is shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The test pit at the proposed antenna tower location was excavated by pick and
shovel to a depth of approximately 3 feet below the existing ground surface Brown
GEOLABS HAWAII

cinder sand and gravel with some cobbles that appeared to be weakly welded were
encountered to the maximum depth excavated by hand digging
The boring was drilled approximately 30 feet to the northwest of the existing
paved access roadway edge in a level area at the top of the slope Horizontal distance
from the boring to the proposed antenna location was estimated to be about 150 feet
The vertical elevation difference between the boring and the proposed antenna site was
estimated to be about 10 feet The ground surface appeared uniform all across the site
with the exception of the localized outcrops of welded cinder material previously noted
In general our boring disclosed that the area is underlain by a deposit of medium
dense cinder sands and gravels extending to the maximum depth explored of
approximately 26 feet below the existing ground surface Cave in of the drilled boring
was observed upon retrieval of the augers at the completion of the drilling operations
Groundwater was not encountered in the drilled boring at the time of our field
exploration The groundwater level at the project site may be influenced by seasonal
precipitation and subterranean seepage conditions

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
On the basis of our geotechnical engineering exploration we encountered
medium dense cider sands and gravels at the site These cinder sands and gravels
generally have low densities and are slightly cemented In general we believe that the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3396 00 B Page 7
proposed antenna tower may be supported on a mat foundation bearing on the
recompacted cinder sands and gravels encountered at shallow depths We estimate
the total foundation settlements under the anticipated light structural loads to be less
than 1 inch with differential settlements between the center and the corner of the mat
to be on the order of inch Detailed discussion of our geotechnical engineering
design recommendations are presented in the following sections of this report
Mat Foundations
Based on the generally medium dense near surface soil conditions encountered
at the project site we believe that a mat foundation bearing on the recompacted near
surface soils may be used to support the proposed antenna tower structure The
requirements for subgrade preparation are discussed in the Site Preparation section
of this report
Based on our field exploration an allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000
pounds per square foot p s f may be used for design of shallow foundations bearing
on the recompacted cinder sand and gravel material encountered at shallow depths
This bearing value is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third 1 for
transient loads such as those caused by wind or seismic forces For mat foundation
design a modulus of subgrade reaction of 200 pounds per square inch per inch of
deflection p c i may be used for the mat foundation bearing on the recompacted
cinder sands and gravels The subgrade modulus is the modulus of vertical subgrade
reaction for a 1 foot square test plate
The bottom of the mat foundation should be embedded a minimum of 2 feet
below the lowest adjacent finish grade Deeper embedment may be required to provide
the required capacity against uplift and moment reactions at the base of the tower
Resistance to uplift loads may be provided by the dead weight of the structure and the
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3396 00 B Page 8
mat foundation Additional resistance to uplift loads may be provided by the weight of
the backfill material placed above the footings if applicable It should be noted that
cinder materials are relatively light Therefore a unit weight of 80 pounds per cubic
foot p c f may be used for the on site compacted cinder sand and gravel materials
We estimate the total foundation settlements under the anticipated light structural loads
to be less than 1 inch with differential settlements between the center and the corner
of the mat to be on the order of inch
If foundations are located next to utility trenches or easements they should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the bottom edge of
the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility lines
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by passive earth pressure
acting against the near vertical faces of the foundation system and by frictional
resistance developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil
Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an equivalent fluid
pressure of 250 pounds per square foot per foot of depth p c f This assumes that
the soils around the mat are well compacted The passive pressure should be reduced
for foundations located on slopes Unless covered by pavements or slab the passive
resistance in the upper 12 inches of soil should be neglected A coefficient of friction
of 0 4 may be used between the mat and the recompacted cinder sand and gravel
material
If loose or weak areas are encountered at the bottom of the footing excavations
the loose materials should be over excavated to expose the underlying dense cinder
sands and gravels The resulting over excavation should be backfilled with on site soil
placed and compacted in accordance with the recommendations presented in the Site
Preparation section of this report

Foundation excavations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs
Hawaii prior to the placement of reinforcing steel or concrete to confirm foundation
bearing conditions and the embedment depths
Retaining Wall
We anticipate that a retaining wall may be required to provide retention along the
cut face of the steep slope at the project site The following guidelines may be used
for preliminary design of retaining walls
1 An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3 000 p s f may be used for the
design of wall footing foundations bearing on recompacted cinder sands
and gravels encountered at the project site This bearing value is for
dead plus live loads and may be increased by one third for transient
loads such as wind or seismic forces
2 For lateral earth pressures the following values may be used
Walls unrestrained atthe top 25 p s f equivalent
active condition level fluid pressure
backfill per foot of depth
Walls unrestrained atthe top 40 p s f equivalent
active condition up to 2H 1V fluid pressure
sloping backfill per foot of depth
Walls unrestrained at the top 55 p s f equivalent
active condition up to 1H 1V fluid pressure
sloping backfill per foot of depth
Level backfill condition may be used for 5H 1V slopes or flatter
Surcharge stresses due to areal surcharges line loads and point loads
within a horizontal distance equal to the height depth of the wall should
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3396 00 B Page 10
be considered in the design For uniform surcharge stresses imposed on
the loaded side of the wall a rectangular distribution over the height of the
wall with a pressure equal to 30 percent of the surcharge load may be
used for the active condition
3 For resistance to lateral loads a passive earth pressure of 250 p s f per
foot of depth may be used for wall footing on level areas The passive
pressure should be reduced for walls located on slopes A base friction
factor of 0 4 may also be used to evaluate the resistance against sliding
of the walls
4 Wall footings should have a minimum width of 18 inches Wall
foundations on relatively flat areas should be embedded a minimum depth
of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent finish grade For sloping ground
the footing should extend deeper to obtain a minimum 8 foot set back
distance measured horizontally from the outside edge of the footing to the
face of the slope Wall footings oriented parallel to the direction of the
slope should be constructed in stepped footings
5 Wall foundations located next to sewer drainage easements should be
embedded below a 1H 1V imaginary plane extending upward from the
bottom edge of the utility trench or as deep as the inverts of the utility
lines
6 Unless covered by concrete slabs a subsurface drainage system
subdrains or weepholes should be utilized behind the walls to reduce
potential build up of hydrostatic pressures A typical drainage system
consists of 1 to 2 foot wide zone of No 3B Fine gravel material wrapped
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3396 00 B Page 11
in a filter fabric Mirafi 140N or equivalent immediately adjacent to the
wall with a perforated pipe with perforations down at the base of the
structure discharging to a storm drain or other discharge facility As an
alternative a prefabricated drainage product such as MiraDrain or
EnkaDrain may be used in lieu of the drainage material with filter fabric
The prefabricated drainage product should also be _ hydraulically
connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall
7 Backfill behind the No 3B Fine gravel drainage zone may consist of the
on site soils Unless covered by concrete slabs the upper 12 inches of
backfill should consist of non expansive impervious material to reduce
water infiltration behind the walls
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography at the proposed antenna tower location we
anticipate that site grading for the subject project will involve excavation into the
existing steep slope for the construction of the mat foundation
At the on set of earthwork the area within the contract grading limits should be
thoroughly cleared and grubbed All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of off site
All areas beneath the future tower mat should be over excavated to the future
foundation subgrade level If zones of soft loose or saturated soils are encountered
at the over excavated subgrade level deeper over excavation may be required to
expose firm material The cinder sand and gravel material resulting from the over
excavation may be stockpiled and re used as a source of backfill material provided that
they are Jess than 3 inches in maximum particle size
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3396 00 B Page 12
Foundation subgrade should be proof rolled with a vibratory drum roller and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1557 91
Generally the on site soils should be suitable for use as engineered fill If
additional imported material is required the material should conform to the require
ments for Select Borrow for Subbase Course stipulated in Section 30 of the Standard
Specifications for Public Works Construction dated September 1986 Any imported fill
material should be tested and approved for its intended use by our firm prior to delivery
to the project site
All fills and backfills should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction Backfills behind conventional
retaining wall should consist of the on site soils compacted to between 90 and 95
percent relative compaction Over compaction of the retaining wall backfill would exert
additional lateral earth pressures on the wall and should be avoided
Site grading operations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs
Hawaii It is important that a representative from our office observe the site grading to
evaluate whether any undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and
scarification process and whether the exposed soil rock conditions are similar to those
encountered in our exploration

Drainage
The finished grades outside the antenna tower should be sloped to shed water
away from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding Excessive landscape
watering near the foundations should also be avoided Drainage swaies should be
provided as soon as possible especially along the retaining wall and should be
maintained to drain all surface run off away from the foundation slab
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of the
above foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil
saturation and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations
should be properly backfilled against the foundation slab immediately after setting of
the concrete to reduce water infiltration
