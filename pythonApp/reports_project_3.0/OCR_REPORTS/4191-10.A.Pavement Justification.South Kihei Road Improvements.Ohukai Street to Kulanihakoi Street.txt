INTRODUCTION
This pavement justification report presents the results of our analyses for the design
of new pavements for the South Kihei Road Improvements project The South Kihei Road
Improvements project is located along the existing South Kihei Road from Ohukai Street to
Kulanihakoi Street in the District of Kihei on the Island of Maui Hawaii The general location
and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory testing
and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for
the design of pavements The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject
to the limitations noted at the end of this report
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
Based on the information provided we understand that it is proposed to improve the
existing South Kihei Road from Ohukai Street to Kulanihakoi Street in the Kihei area on the
Island of Maui Hawaii The proposed improvements will generally include road widening
curbs and gutters sidewalks relocation of utilities and drainage improvements along an
approximate 0 94 mile stretch of South Kihei Road Based on the information provided the
new roadway will consist of a 12 foot travelway with a paved shoulder in each direction for a
total of 60 or 70 foot wide right of way
Based on the guidelines provided in the Pavement Design Manual prepared by the
State of Hawaii Department of Transportation Highways Division Material Testing and
Research Branch dated May 1995 we understand that a detailed economic comparison of
pavement types is not required if the area of new pavement is less than 4 lane miles and or
a short highway to highway connection is being made between pavements of the same type
Since the new pavements will be less than 2 lane miles and the existing roadway is currently
paved with asphaltic concrete AC we believe that recommendations for new pavements may
be limited to pavement sections with an asphaltic concrete riding surface

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Maui was built by two major voicanoes the older West Maui Tertiary
Epoch and the more recent East Maui also known as Haleakala Pleistocene Epoch The
Isthmus of Maui is a narrow gently sloping plain located between these two volcanoes The
project site is located at the northern portion of this gently sloping plain
The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows from East
on West Maui It is comprised of alluvium washed from the slopes of West Maui and
Haleakala The erosional processes are dominated by the detachment of soil and rock
masses from the mountain walls and the soil materials are transported downslope toward the
isthmus primarily by gravity as colluvium Once these materials reach the stream in the central
portion of a valley alluvial processes become dominant and the sediments are transported
and deposited as alluvium In general stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy such
that the stream flows transmit large volumes of water for very short duration Because of this
the transport of sediments is intermittent and the bulk of the stream s hydraulic load consists
of a poorly sorted mixture of boulders cobbles gravel sands and fines When the erosional
base levels change these sediment loads are left as deposits
When deposits are left in place for long periods of time chemical processes begin to
alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of the materials
Chemical processes also cause induration or cementation of the coarse grained portion of
the sediment into a poorly consolidated sedimentary rock or conglomerate Simultaneously
erosion continues in the areas above the valley floors and upstream in headwaters This
continued erosion generates material which is transported downslope covering the older
alluvial deposits Depending on the local base level and rate of transport these newer
sediments are generally transient in terms of geologic time In addition their consistency and
density are generally less than those of the older partially consolidated deposits
Underlying the alluvial deposits are overlapping lavas from the West Maui and East
Maui volcanoes The bulk of the Haleakala Shield was built during the late Pliocene and early
Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows of the Honomanu Volcanic Series

During the Pleistocene Epoch the characteristics of the lavas changed to very hard thickly
bedded flows of andesitic composition These lavas have been grouped as the Kula Volcanic
Series Typically the basalt consists of thinly to thickly bedded a a and pahoehoe type flows
EXISTING SITE AND PAVEMENT CONDITIONS
The project site is located along the existing South Kihei Road from Ohukai Street to
Kulanihakoi Street in the District of Kihei on the Island of Maui Hawaii The existing South
Kihei Road is a two way asphaltic concrete paved travelway with one lane in each direction
In general the project site is relatively level with existing ground elevations ranging between
about 4 5 and 8 feet Mean Sea Level MSL The overall conditions of the existing
pavements were generally fair to moderate with distresses such as localized potholes low to
moderate severity fatigue cracking and settlement of the pavement surface At the time of
our field exploration traffic flow was relatively heavy and consisted primarily of passenger
vehicles pick up trucks and occasional heavy trucks

Design Subgrade Conditions
Our field exploration program along the proposed roadway improvement alignment
consisted of drilling and sampling six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6 The
borings were extended to depths ranging from about 11 5 to 21 5 feet below the existing
ground surface The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
Based on the borings drilled along the proposed roadway improvement project silty
sand soils were generally encountered at shallow depths below the existing ground surface
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered from our field exploration are presented on
the Logs of Borings Plates B 1 through B 6 of Appendix B Laboratory testing of similar
subgrade soils from the Phase 4 portion of the project resulted in modified California Bearing
Ratio CBR values of about 9 and 18 which correspond to a design Resistance R value
based on correlations by the State of Kentucky of 22 and 47 respectively Based on our field
exploration and the current design concept we anticipate that the pavement subgrade soils
will likely consist of imported or in situ sandy soils along the proposed roadway alignment
Therefore a design Resistance R value of 22 has been adopted for the subgrade materials
in our pavement analyses

Based on our field exploration and our experience in the adjacent areas we anticipate
that the subgrade material below the new pavements shouid consist of relatively permeabie
silty sand soils We believe that groundwater or surface water runoff trapped below the
pavement section should dissipate fairly rapidly through the sandy subgrade material
Therefore it is our opinion that the incorporation of a permeable base course would not be
necessary for the proposed roadway improvement project
Due to the heavy traffic flow in the Kihei area we envision that the progress of the
roadway construction would be of critical concern In order to reduce the potential for
disturbance to vehicular traffic asphalt concrete base ACB is generally used as the base
material to facilitate the pavement construction work to be performed in a timely manner
Therefore the use of untreated aggregate base course was not considered in our pavement
design analyses
Rehabilitation of Existing Pavements
We understand that it is also desired to evaluate the feasibility of rehabilitating the
existing pavements without reconstruction In general the type and severity of the distresses
exhibited in the pavement areas would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of
the existing pavements should be performed Existing pavements with minor distresses of low
severity are generally rehabilitated with an AC overlay with cold planing
Based on our field observations the existing pavements within the roadway
improvement limits were observed to be in fair to moderate condition with localized potholes
as well as cracking and settlement of the pavement surface For the AC overlay design the
strength of the existing AC layer has been reduced to that of asphalt concrete base ACB
based on the fair to moderate conditions of the existing pavements observed at the subject
roadway
Based on the results of the AC overlay analyses it appears that an AC overlay on the
order of about 9 to 10 inches thick will be required under the anticipated future traffic
conditions for the existing pavement sections based on a design life of 10 years Results of

opinion that re surfacing of the existing pavements would not be feasibie and the existing
pavements should be replaced with a new pavement section as recommended above for the

Site Preparation
In general the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and
grubbed at the on set of earthwork The existing AC underlying base subbase materials and
other unsuitable materials should be completely removed and properly disposed of off site
The exposed subgrade materials in areas designated to receive fill prior to construction of the
new pavements and in cut areas should be scarified to a depth of about 8 inches
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and recompacted to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of
soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with AASHTO T 180 or ASTM D 1557 91 test procedures Optimum moisture is
the water content percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
If pumping conditions are encountered during compaction of the subgrade soils the
subgrade soils may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for pumping
subgrade conditions As a guide one sack of cement may be used for every 50 square feet
of subgrade area This preliminary mix should be adjusted in the field to suit the exposed soil
conditions
Aggregate subbase materials required for the new pavements should conform to the
requirements stipulated in the Standard Specifications for Road Bridge and Public Works
Construction 1994 The aggregate subbase should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content placed in 8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction The asphalt concrete base ACB material should consist of
asphalt treated basalt aggregate placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted
thickness and compacted to a density of at least 91 percent of the maximum theoretical
GEOLABS INC
W O 4191 10 A Page 9
specific gravity determined in accordance with AASHTO T 209 or ASTMD 2041 test
procedures
In general the excavated on site materials may be re used as a source of general fill
and backfill materials up to the bottom of the aggregate subbase layer provided that they are
free of organic materials and contain no lumps or rock fragments greater than 6 inches in
largest dimension The on site fill materials should be moisture conditioned to above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Imported fill materials below the aggregate subbase layer if required should consist
of non expansive select granular material such as crushed coral mudrock or basalt The
material should be well graded from coarse to fine with no particles greater than 3 inches in
largest dimension The material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more with a
maximum swell value of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with AASHTO T 193 or
ASTM Test Designation D 1883 test procedures In addition select granular fill material
should contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve Imported
materials should be brought to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to not less
than 95 percent relative compaction Surfaces should be wetted finished to create relatively
smooth unyielding subgrades and kept moist until covered by pavements or slabs Imported
materials should be tested and approved by Geolabs prior to delivery to the project site for the
intended use

Pavement Surface Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the subbase and base course and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement
surface should be adequately sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry
surface water off the pavement to appropriate structures Surface water ponding should not
be allowed on the site during or after construction Development of good shoulder drainage
along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage structures is strongly
recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or premature failure of the
pavements

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the proposed roadway improvement project were
explored by drilling and sampling six borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 6
extending to depths ranging from about 11 5 to 21 5 feet below the existing ground surface
The approximate locations of the borings drilled are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
borings were drilled using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our engineer who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in
the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification
System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the materials encountered in the
borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates B 1 through B 6
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 3550 84 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 3 inch
OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In addition some
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM Test
Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a 2 inch
OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow counts needed
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown as the
Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
