In accordance with our fee proposal dated January 26, 2007, we recently
completed the field exploration work for the new Kihei Information Center & Administrative
Building project located in the Kihei area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. This letter
summarizes the work performed thus far on the project and presents our preliminary
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for the project.
Project Considerations
The project site is within an existing vacant parcel [TMK: (2) 3-9-04: Portions of 01
& 06] off Kihei Road in the Kihei area on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. It is approximately
bound by Kihei Road to the east, an existing roadway easement along the south, open
land to the north, and the Pacific Ocean to the west.
Based on the information provided, we understand that it is desired to develop the
parcel into the Kihei Information Center & Administrative Building for the Kaho’olawe
Island Reserve Commission (KIRC). The proposed construction will consist of a KIRC
building, educational/performance sacred gathering area, water feature, walkways, and an
asphaltic concrete paved parking lot. Detailed information for the proposed construction
was not available at the time this letter was prepared.
Based on our experience with other projects in the Kihei area and the recently
completed geotechnical exploration, the project site is underlain by basalt rock formation
near the existing ground surface. Therefore, special attention was given to the potential
presence of cavities and/or voids in the basalt rock formation, which may collapse under
structural loads or vehicular traffic. Primary considerations for our geotechnical
engineering exploration included the following:

Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling six borings, designated as
Boring Nos. 1 through 6, extending to depths ranging from about 5 to 20 feet below the
existing ground surface. In addition, a bulk sample of the near-surface soils, designated as
Bulk-1, was collected for laboratory CBR test to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics.
Based on our field exploration, the project site is underlain by a surface soil layer,
on the order of about 3 to 6 feet thick, consisting of dense to very dense silty sands. Below
this layer, dense basalt rock formation was generally encountered and extended to the
maximum depth explored of about 20 feet below the ground surface. The dense basalt
formation encountered was generally hard to very hard and moderately weathered.
Cavities and/or voids were not observed within the basalt rock formation in the drilled
borings. However, it should be noted that cavities and/or voids are commonly present in
the basaltic lava flows in the vicinity. Therefore, the presence of cavities and/or voids
should be expected during project construction.
Groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled at the time of our field
exploration. However, groundwater levels may change due to seasonal precipitation,
surface water runoff, and other factors.
Preliminary Recommendations
Based on our field exploration, the project site is generally underlain by a surface
soil layer, on the order of about 3 to 6 feet thick, consisting of dense to very dense silty
sands. Below the surface soil layer, dense basalt rock formation was encountered and
extended to the maximum depth explored of about 20 feet below the ground surface. The
dense basalt formations encountered generally were hard to very hard and moderately
weathered. Groundwater was not encountered in the borings drilled at the time of our field
exploration.

Building Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, we recommend using shallow
spread and/or continuous footings to support the new two-story structure planned at the
project site. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 3,000 psf may be used for the design
of footings bearing on the recompacted on-site soils, basalt rock formation, or new
compacted fills placed to achieve the finished grades. This bearing value is for
dead-plus-live loads and may be increased by one-third (1/3) for transient loads, such as
those caused by wind or seismic forces.
For footings bearing on the on-site soils or new compacted fills, the bottom of the
footing excavations should be recompacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction to
provide a relatively firm and smooth bearing surface prior to the placement of reinforcing
steel or concrete. Soft and/or loose materials encountered at the bottom of the footing
excavations should be over-excavated to expose the underlying firm materials. The
over-excavation may be backfilled with imported fill materials compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction, or the bottom of footing may extend down to bear directly
on the underlying competent materials. Where both existing soils and basalt rock
formation are exposed at the foundation subgrade level, we recommend over-excavating
the basalt rock formation by 12 inches and replacing with compacted fills for uniform
bearing support.

Footings should be embedded a minimum of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent
grade for the two-story structure. Footings constructed near the tops of slopes or on slopes
should be embedded deep enough such that the horizontal distance measured from the
outside edge (at the base of the footing) to the slope face is no less than 8 feet.
In addition, foundations located next to other foundations, utility trenches,
easements or other retaining walls should be embedded below a 45-degree imaginary
plane extending upward from the bottom edge of the structure or utility trench, or the
footings should extend to a depth as deep as the inverts of the utility lines. This
requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging adjacent below-grade structures with
additional structural loads and to reduce the potential for appreciable foundation
settlement.
If the foundations are designed and constructed in accordance with our
recommendations, we estimate the total settlements of the foundations to be less than
1 inch. Differential settlements between adjacent footings supported on similar materials
may be on the order of about 0.5 inch or less.
Due to the potential cavities as commonly encountered in basalt rock formations,
we recommend implementing a program of probing and grouting of the foundation
locations during construction. Probing and grouting requirements are discussed further in
the following "Foundation Probing" section of this letter report.
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by friction developed between
the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive earth pressure acting
against the near-vertical faces of the foundation system. A coefficient of friction of 0.4 may
be used for footings bearing on the recompacted on-site soils, basalt rock formation, or
new compacted fills. Resistance due to passive earth pressure may be estimated using an
equivalent fluid pressure of 350 pounds per square foot per foot of depth (pcf), assuming
that the soils around the footings are well compacted or the footings are cast neat against
the basalt rock formation. The passive resistance in the upper 12 inches of the soil should
be neglected unless covered by pavements or slabs.
A Geolabs representative should observe footing excavations prior to the
placement of reinforcing steel and concrete to confirm the foundation bearing conditions
and the required embedment depths.
Foundation Probing

voids are commonly encountered in the pahoehoe lava flows that characterize the project
site. To reduce the potential for loss of foundation support resulting from the collapse of
cavities below foundations, we recommend implementing a cavity probing and grouting
program for the new structure foundations during construction.

For estimating purposes, we recommend drilling probe holes at 10-foot on centers
for the continuous strip footings. In addition, probe holes should be drilled at each isolated
spread footing location (one probe per 50 square feet). The probe holes should be at least
3 inches in diameter and should extend to a depth of at least 10 feet below the planned
bottom of foundation.
If cavities and/or voids are encountered or suspected during the probing operation,
additional probe holes should be drilled at closer spacing to help delineate the vertical and
lateral extent of the cavity and/or void. The probe holes and cavities discovered should be
backfilled with Controlled Low Strength Material (CLSM) with a slump of about 6 to
9 inches. The CLSM should be injected at low to moderate pump pressures. As an
alternate to CLSM, sand-cement grout with a slump of about 6 to 9 inches may also be
used for the grouting operations.
Because of the potential for encountering cavities and/or voids, we recommend
obtaining unit prices for additional probing and grouting during bidding. In addition, the
probe drill should be available on-site until the probing and grouting operations are
completed. The contractor should also be made aware that a longer lag time between
probing/grouting operations and foundation construction might be required in the
construction schedule.
The probing and grouting program should be conducted under the observation of
our representative. This would allow our firm to monitor the presence of cavities and/or
voids and to allow additional recommendations to be made if excess grout take and/or
changed conditions are observed.
Slabs-On-Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs-on-grade will be used for the ground floor of the
new structure planned for the development. For the interior building slabs (not subjected to
vehicular traffic), we recommend providing a minimum 4-inch thick layer of cushion fill
below the slabs for uniform support. The cushion fill should consist of open-graded gravel
(ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) and would also serve as a capillary moisture break. To
reduce the potential for excess moisture infiltration and subsequent damage to floor
coverings, an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel cushion
layer. Flexible floor coverings should be considered above the floor slab because they can
better mask minor slab cracking.
Where slabs are subjected to vehicular traffic, such as forklifts, or sustained
equipment vibrations, a minimum of 6 inches of compacted aggregate subbase should be
provided below the slabs in lieu of the 4-inch thick cushion fill layer. The aggregate
subbase should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. For the design of structural
slabs supported on 6 inches of aggregate subbase, a modulus of subgrade reaction of

Retaining Structures
Based on the information provided, we understand that site retaining structures or
basement walls are not planned for the project development. However, the following
general guidelines may be used for design of retaining structures, such as elevator pit
walls, required for the project construction.
Wall Foundations
In general, we believe that retaining wall foundations may be designed in
accordance with the recommendations presented in the “Building Foundations”
section of this letter report. In general, retaining wall footings should have a
minimum width of 18 inches. In addition, wall foundations on relatively flat areas
should be embedded a minimum depth of 24 inches below the lowest adjacent
finished grade. For sloping ground conditions, the footing should be extended
deeper to obtain a minimum 8-foot setback distance measured horizontally from the
outside edge of the footing to the face of the slope. Wall footings oriented parallel to
the direction of the slope should be constructed in stepped footings.

Site Preparation
At the on-set of earthwork, areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly. Vegetation, debris, deleterious materials, and
other unsuitable materials should be removed and properly disposed off-site to
reduce the potential for contamination of the excavated materials. The contractor
should exercise caution while clearing and grubbing near-collapsed lava tube
features and/or cavities. These areas may represent potential lava tubes or
cavities, which may further collapse under the load of heavy equipment.
In general, cut subgrade and areas designated to receive fills should be scarified to
a depth of about 12 inches, where possible, moisture-conditioned to above the
optimum moisture, and proof-rolled with a large vibratory drum roller (minimum
20 tons static weight), a Caterpillar D-10 bulldozer, or similar size bulldozer a
minimum of eight passes to help detect and collapse near-surface cavities and/or
voids. The vibratory drum roller should be operated at a speed of about 300 feet
per minute. The proof-rolling operations should be performed in the presence of a
qualified geotechnical engineer or our representative.
Yielding areas (which may be indicative of subsurface cavities and/or voids), loose
areas, or cavities disclosed during the clearing and proof-rolling operations should
be over-excavated and backfilled with compacted fill materials. The depth of
over-excavation should be extended until dense underlying materials are exposed
and should be evaluated in the field by a qualified geotechnical engineer or our
representative. The excavated materials should be properly disposed off-site or in a
designated area. Due to the potential for presence of cavities (lava tubes, blisters,
pockets, etc.), we recommend including contingencies in the budget estimates for
over-excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the
over-excavation and backfill quantities.

Fills and Backfills
Imported fills required to raise the existing ground surface to the design finish
grades should consist of materials with particle sizes of 3 inches or less in
maximum dimension and should be free of deleterious materials. The material
should have a laboratory California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 12 or higher and a
swell potential of less than 1percent when tested in accordance with
ASTM D 1883. Imported fill materials should be tested by Geolabs a minimum of
seven days prior to being transported to the project site for the intended use. The
excavated on-site soils may be reused as a source of fill materials, provided that
they are processed to meet the above requirements.
Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in loose
thickness, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and
compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction. Compaction requirement for
fills placed within 3 feet below the pavement grade should be increased to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the
in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density
of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum moisture
is the water content (percentage by weight) corresponding to the maximum dry
density. Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers, vibratory
rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment.
Excavation
Based on our field exploration, the project site is underlain by basalt formation at
shallow depths. It is anticipated that the near-surface soils and weathered basalt
formation may be readily excavated with normal heavy excavation equipment, such
as excavators, and ripped with large bulldozers. However, excavations into the
dense pahoehoe lava may require the use of hoerams or chipping.
The discussions regarding the rippability of the surface materials are based on our
visual observation of the existing basalt formation and field and laboratory data
from the borings performed at the project site. We recommend that the contractors
be encouraged to examine the site conditions and the subsurface data to make
their own reasonable and prudent interpretation.

primarily consist of passenger vehicles, light pick-up trucks, and handivans. Vehicle
loading for the access driveways and loading zones will generally consist of heavy
vehicular traffic, including delivery and container trucks.

The subgrade soils under the pavement areas should be scarified to a minimum
depth of 8 inches, where possible, moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture,
and compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction. Where scarification of the
subgrades is not practical, subgrade soils should be proof-rolled with a minimum 20-ton
