PROJECT DESCRIPTION 
The project is situated at the former CompUSA site in the Kaka`ako area of 
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The site is bounded by Auahi Street to the north, 
Keawe Street to the east, Ala Moana Boulevard to the south, and South Street to the west. 
In general, the project involves the construction of a 43-story high-rise building, three and 
four-story mid-rise residential buildings, and a seven-level parking structure.

In order to reduce the adverse effects resulting from the anticipated ground 
settlements and reduced ground stiffness during an earthquake, stone columns were used 
to strengthen the potentially liquefiable soils under the high-rise residential tower. The 
stone column installation consisted of 23 test stone columns and 415 production stone 
columns, for a total of 438 stone columns installed within the footprint of the 43-story 
high-rise residential structure.   
Hawaiian Dredging was the general contractor for the project and Condon-Johnson 
& Associates, Inc. was the stone column installation subcontractor for the project. 
STONE COLUMN INSTALLATION 
Geotechnical recommendations for the stone columns were presented in the 
afore-mentioned report dated September 8, 2014 and our subsequent stone column 
design consultation services. The purpose of our geotechnical engineering observation 
and consultation services was to observe the stone column installation for general 
conformance with our recommendations. 
The stone column installation program consisted of performing 23 test stone 
columns and 415 production stone columns.  Detailed discussions are provided in the 
following subsections. 
Vibro Stone Columns 
This ground improvement technique uses specialty purpose-built depth vibrators to 
densify and reinforce the soils while constructing a stone column of about 3 feet in 
diameter. The installation process consists of imparting energy by means of vibrations that 
are generated close to the tip of the vibrator and are produced by rotating eccentrically 
mounted weight mounted on a shaft that is powered by an electric motor. Follower tubes, 
which have visible markings at regular increments that enable measurement of penetration 
and re-penetration depths, were added to achieve the desired penetration depth of about 
25 feet below the working grade. 
To install a stone column, the vibratory probe is suspended from a crane and 
lowered into the ground under the action of its own weight, vibrations, and air jetting. Upon 
lowering the vibratory probe to the required depth, the vibratory probe is lifted in 2 to 3-foot 
stages by raising the vibratory probe up and down, releasing the gravel through the 
specially shaped gravel displacement nose cone at the tip of the vibratory probe.  While 
the vibratory probe is being retracted, positive air pressure maintained in the tremie pipe 
pushes the aggregate out of the nose cone and prevents the surrounding soil from 
entering the gravel tube.

Stone Column Test Section Program 
In order to evaluate the effectiveness of the stone columns, a stone column test 
section program was implemented by installing 23 test stone columns on October 17 and 
18, 2014 under the near-continuous (full-time) observations by a Geolabs field 
representative. The stone columns were installed in groups, with each group containing 
varying amounts of aggregates resulting in a variety of area replacement ratios.  The area 
replacement ratio is defined as the ratio of the stone column area to the tributary area of 
the stone columns. Verification borings were performed at the center of select groups to 
determine the effectiveness of the various area replacement ratios. Results of the stone 
column verification borings are transmitted under separate cover. 
Based on the results of the stone column test section program, five buckets of 
aggregate per stone column (approximately 4,000 lbs of aggregate per bucket) was 
selected to provide the optimal treatment. The identifications of the test stone columns 
installed are shown on the Stone Column Location Plan, Plate 2. 
Production Stone Column Installation 
The production stone column installation program, consisting of installing 415 stone 
columns (5 buckets of aggregate per column), was conducted from November 4 through 
22, 2014 under the near-continuous (full-time) observations by a Geolabs field 
representative. Condon-Johnson & Associates used the same stone column equipment 
utilized for the test section program to install the production stone columns. Automatic 
monitoring equipment was mounted on the stone column equipment with the intent of 
providing vibrator probe depth, vibrator amperage, number of bucket of aggregate, total 
weight and volume of aggregate installed, penetration and compaction time, etc., on a 
near-continuous basis.  
During the stone column installation operation, logs were maintained for each stone 
column, which included information pertaining to stone column number, date installed, 
depth of stone column, amount of buckets, amperage at bottom depth, etc. The 
identifications of the production stone columns installed are shown on the Stone Column 
Location Plan, Plate 2. The production stone column installation summary is presented by 
stone column number on the attached Plates 3.1 through 3.13. Daily field reports 
summarizing the stone column activities on a daily basis are presented on the attached 
Plates 4.1 through 4.16. 
Based on our observation of the equipment performance and subsurface conditions 
encountered, it is our professional opinion that the production stone columns have been 
properly installed in general conformance with the project plans, specifications, and our 
recommendations to meet the design requirements.
