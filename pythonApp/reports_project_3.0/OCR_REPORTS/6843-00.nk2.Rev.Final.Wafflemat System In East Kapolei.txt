SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
The Ewa Beach and Kapolei areas on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii have had 
documented cases of expansive soils causing damage to architectural finishes in both 
residential and commercial structures. Wafflemat is a proprietary post-tensioned slab-on-
grade foundation that is designed to mitigate vertical movements due to expansive soil. 
Three Wafflemat test slabs, two with different vapor barriers and one with no vapor barrier, 
were constructed in the East Kapolei area where a large residential development is planned. 
The slabs were monitored for vertical movement by taking spot elevation readings of the 
slab surface along a gridline system. Moisture sensors were installed in the soil around the 
perimeter of each slab. To simulate extreme wet weather conditions, the soil around the 
perimeter of each slab was soaked with water on a regular basis to induce movement of the 
foundations from the expansive soil heaving. 
The presence of a vapor barrier did not have a significant effect on reducing 
differential movement of the Wafflemat slab. The maximum vertical and differential 
movements were determined to be less than 1.0 and 0.5 inches, respectively, with a 
maximum angular distortion of less than 1/400.  These measurements are well below values 
that would cause architectural damage to the light residential structure supported by the 
Wafflemats. 
Based on our field observations and analyses, the soil in the vicinity of the test site is 
sensitive to both collapse and expansion when subjected to changes in soil moisture 
content. To minimize this behavior, we recommend removing the upper 1 foot of surface soil 
and moisture-conditioning the next 2 feet below bottom of the Wafflemat. Although the 
moisture barrier did not appear to impact the performance of the Wafflemat, we believe that 
the vapor barrier would help maintain the near-surface soil moisture content for a longer 
period of time and reduce potential soil expansion.  
We believe that the Wafflemat slab is a preferred foundation system for residential 
development in the East Kapolei area to mitigate soil movement due to expansive soil heave 
and collapse. To provide further stiffness and resistance to architectural damage, we 
recommend using the 12-inch Wafflebox in lieu of the tested 8½-inch Wafflebox. By treating 
the upper 3 feet of existing subgrade as described above, incorporating the 12-inch 
Wafflebox and utilizing a vapor barrier to retain moisture in the prepared subgrade during 
construction, we believe that the Wafflemat will provide improved performance and be more 
cost effective than traditional post-tensioned foundations or non-post-tensioned spread 
footing foundations. 
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and 
recommendations.

1.1 
Project Considerations 
Projects in the area have experienced variable performance such as heaving of 
lightly loaded concrete slabs and sidewalks/roads. Also, some instances of 
settlement/collapse were reported. The East Kapolei area has been designated as the 
next large residential development area on Oahu. Conventional foundation design for 
expansive soils in the area consists of over-excavating 1 to 5 feet of expansive clay and 
replacing with non-expansive structural fill. Due to a lack of readily available sources of 
structure fill, an alternative foundation system is desired. The objective of the evaluation 
was to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed Wafflemat slab-on-grade foundation 
on expansive soils in the East Kapolei area. 
The project site is located at the northeast end of Keahumoa Parkway (East-West 
Road), as shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2, in Kapolei on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. 
Three Wafflemat testing slabs were constructed using the following three different 
subgrade preparation methods: 
• Slab No. 1 – 10-mil vapor barrier over prepared and recompacted subgrade. 
• Slab No. 2 – 6-mil vapor barrier over prepared and recompacted subgrade. 
• Slab No. 3 – No vapor barrier over prepared and recompacted subgrade.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age. 
The project site is situated on the Ewa Coastal Plain, which extends seaward off 
the southeastern flank of the Waianae Volcano and the southwestern flank of the Koolau 
Volcano. This plain was formed by the accumulation of a thick wedge of sediments and 
coral reef formations on the flanks of the two volcanoes.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When 
the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea 
levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous 
materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of the calcareous 
reef materials. The project is situated on a broad alluvial apron that flanks the southern 
margin of Schofield Plateau. Agricultural activities in the project vicinity have brought the 
surface to its current form. 
The surface soils underlying the project site are classified as Honouliuli Clay (HxA) 
by the U.S. Soil Conservation Service in their publication "Soil Survey of Islands of Kauai, 
Oahu, Maui, Molokai and Lanai, State of Hawaii," (1972). These soils consist of well-
drained alluvium derived from basic igneous rocks. The soils have generally high shrink-
swell potential and low shear strength. The boundaries of the Honouliuli Clay and other 
nearby soils are illustrated on the Soil Map, Plate 3. A Geologic Map of the project site is 
presented on Plate 4.

2.3 
Historical Use of The Ewa Beach Plain 
The Ewa Coastal plain has had a long history of agricultural use. In 1890, the Ewa 
Plantation Company was incorporated by Mr. B.F. Dillingham and large scale sugar cane 
production commenced on approximately 11,000 acres of land. Artesian wells throughout 
the area fed the numerous sugar cane fields. The company prospered and continued to 
produce large quantities of sugar cane for several decades. Castle and Cooke Ltd. then 
bought and maintained majority control of the Ewa Plantation stock in 1962. Eight years 
later, in 1970, the Ewa Plantation Company merged with the Oahu Sugar Company who 
then managed agricultural operations (HSPA, 2004). By 1995, the sugar cane industry in 
Hawaii declined into non-existence. Aloun Farms began leasing portions of the land in 
increments and has expanded to its present size of approximately 3,000 acres (Aloun 
Farms, 2015). The current project site is located on previously farmed land in close 
proximity to the Aloun Farms property. D.R. Horton purchased 1,600 acres of agricultural 
land from the Estate of James Campbell in March of 2006 to develop the new Ho’opili 
subdivision.

SECTION 3.  WAFFLEMAT TEST SECTION 
3.1 
Wafflemat Construction 
Wafflemat is a post-tensioned slab-on-grade foundation system. High-strength 
plastic hollow boxes are positioned and connected in a grid arrangement on top of the 
prepared subgrade. Post-tensioning tendons are placed through the spaces between the 
Waffleboxes. When the concrete is poured over the boxes, ribs are formed between the 
Waffleboxes. After the concrete is cured, post-tensioning tendons are loaded to stiffen 
the concrete ribs in both directions.  
The hollow Waffleboxes are the key component of the Wafflemat system and have 
been designed specifically to mitigate the effects of expansive soils. By decreasing the 
amount of slab contact area to the underlying expansive soil, the Wafflemat system 
provides increased bearing stress to counteract soil swelling pressure. The voids created 
by the Waffleboxes also allow soil to expand unimpeded. In addition, the Waffleboxes 
form ribs in two orthogonal directions with post-tensioned reinforcing strands that provide 
greater stiffness to the foundation in comparison to conventional slab foundation.

3.5 
Testing Instruments 
 ZIPLEVEL® 
The ZIPLEVEL® is a high precision pressurized hydrostatic altimeter. The device 
utilizes a pressurized system that measures the weight of a proprietary liquid sealed 
within a cord relative to a reference cell located in the hub of its reel (ZIPLEVEL® 
2012). Measurements are taken by placing the measurement module at points of 
interest. A telescopic monopod may also be attached to the back of the measurement 
module to take spot elevations in locations that the measurement module may have 
difficult access. The ZIPLEVEL® was used to measure the elevation at each survey 
point on the test slab to a tenth of an inch. Due to the ZIPLEVEL® working off of a 
pressurized hydraulic and pneumatic system, temperature changes and movement 
of the device’s cord affected the record elevation reading. Experience with this device 
in both indoor and outdoor environments indicated a potential error up to 
approximately 0.25 inches.

SECTION 4.  DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS 
The soil moisture content around the Wafflemat slabs has a direct relationship to 
the induced soil and slab foundation movement. Based on the results of laboratory testing 
and the trends in moisture content change measured by the moisture sensors, we believe 
that the upper 3 feet of soil at the project site may dry up at a rapid rate after being 
saturated, while the moisture content remains relatively constant at greater depths. This 
could be due to existing large shrinkage cracks extended through the upper 3 feet. The 
rapid moisture content changes in the upper 3 feet of soil contribute to the movement of 
the test slabs. 
The elevation measurements were taken on the surface of the Wafflemat test slabs 
during the wetting cycles. In general, the test slabs experienced initial settlement (due to 
soil collapse) after the first soaking cycle followed by heave after additional soaking cycles 
(due to soil swell). 
We did not observe soil heave within the Waffleboxes during the wetting cycles. 
Therefore, the Wafflebox mechanism of mitigating soil swell could not be verified through 
this evaluation. 
Total vertical movement of the test slabs induced by the wetting cycles was less 
than 1 inch with differential movement of less than 0.5 inches and angular distortion of 
less than 1/400 during the 10-month testing period. No crack or distress was visually 
observed on the test slab surfaces. We believe that the Wafflemat is a viable foundation 
system to support the light residential structures in the East Kapolei area.

clayey soil condition, the moisture sensors were not able to be properly installed with full 
sensor prong penetration. Therefore, the data collected from the moisture sensors were 
questionable.

within 3 feet below the ground surface experienced a rapid change after each soaking; 
the environment of the test site may be so arid that the near-surface soil dried out between 
the time of soil wetting and the time the soil samples or moisture readings were taken. 
However, the soil at 5-foot depth retained relatively higher moisture content with a small 
increase at the end of the wetting cycle. We believe that this behavior could be due to the 
existing shrinkage cracks that extended to no deeper than 3 feet.

test site are sensitive to collapsing and swelling, which would adversely affect the 
foundation’s performance. Under normal environmental conditions, the near surface soil 
at the test site is generally in a very dry state and shrinkage cracks are common. When 
the dry soil is subjected to inundation, the soil collapses. Upon increasing moisture, soil 
swelling occurs. The Wafflemat slabs were designed to mitigate soil expansion but not 
necessarily soil collapse. Therefore, it is critical to provide moisture conditioning to the 
soil subgrade and moisture protection to the near-surface soils. We recommend 
performing soil subgrade moisture conditioning up to 3 feet below the ground surface in 
conjunction with the Wafflemat installation to mitigate potential structural movement and 
distress due to soil collapsing and swelling.

4.3 
Foundation Performance 
Foundation built on expansive soils are subjected to movement induced by the 
shrinkage and swelling of the soils due to moisture variations. Post-tensioned slabs are 
designed to be sufficiently stiff such that the movements do not cause distress to the 
structure or damages to architectural finishes. Architectural finishes are more sensitive to 
movement and will crack when the foundation moves inducing excessive wall distortion. 
Industry standards have been established to limit the distortion minimizing cracks in 
finishes. 
To evaluate the performance of foundations, two measures are commonly used, 
differential movement and angular distortion. For typical house foundations, differential 
movement should be limited to 1.25 inches to prevent damage. Angular distortion is the 
critical factor that results in damage to finishes. Angular distortion is defined as the 
difference in movement divided by the distance between any two points. The maximum 
angular distortion may be estimated by dividing the maximum vertical differential 
movement, by the horizontal distance from the closest edge of the slab.  The maximum 
angular distortion, designated as max, should be limited to 1/300 (~0.0033) to prevent 
damage to architectural finishes. Depending on finishes used, the limit may be relaxed or 
made more restrictive. For the finishes anticipated in the Ho’opili development, 1/300 is 
a reasonable benchmark. 
Over a period of about 1 year, movements were measured for the three test 
Wafflemats subjected to extreme moisture variations. Upon establishing a baseline 
reading, the elevations at the slab surface along the gridline system were measured at 
various time periods to determine differential movements and angular distortions, as 
presented on Plates C-8.1 through C-10.2 in Appendix C. The maximum calculated 
differential movement and angular distortion were about 0.5 inches and 1/400, 
respectively, less than the above-noted limits, while the maximum vertical movement was 
less than 1 inch. The calculated maximum differential movements and maximum angular 
distortions for the three test slabs are also summarized in the table below. No crack was

4.4 
Conclusions and Recommendations 
a. 
The test Wafflemat system met the required performance standards for the 
post-tensioned slabs-on-grade. Therefore, we believe that the Wafflemat 
system is a viable foundation to resist the expansive soil swelling for light 
residential structures in the East Kapolei area.  
b. 
The test slabs experienced initial settlement upon wetting. This is likely a 
result of soil collapses due to the friable nature of the upper several feet of 
soil caused by past agricultural activities and dry climactic conditions. To 
reduce these effects, the upper 3 to 5 feet of soils should be over-excavated 
and moisture-conditioned during the earthwork phase of the development. 
The re-moisture conditioning procedures include the following. 
After clearing and grubbing, remove the upper 12 inches of the 
cultivated soils below the existing ground surface and haul off the site 
or screen for top soil; 
Over-excavate at least another 12 inches of dry and friable soils for 
moisture conditioning to at least 4 percent above the optimum moisture 
content and replace as compacted fills;

At the bottom of the 12-inch over-excavation, scarify to a depth of at 
least 12 inches, moisture-condition to at least 4 percent above the 
optimum moisture content, and compact to no less than 85 percent 
relative compaction; 
Place the moisture conditioned material in the upper 12-inches, 
provided the material is free of deleterious materials, and compact to 
no less than 90 percent relative compaction. 
c. 
The presence or thickness of the vapor barrier below the Wafflemat boxes 
did not seem to have a significant effect on the performance of the slabs. 
Although the vapor barrier may be optional, we recommend using the vapor 
barrier to assist in keeping the subgrade soils below the Waffleboxes moist 
and to create a water barrier beyond the edges of the Wafflemat. Details of 
the vapor barrier are presented on Plate 10. 
d. 
The 8.5-inch high Waffleboxes used for this evaluation performed 
adequately. However, in order to reduce differential movement and 
resulting angular distortion, a 12-inch high Wafflebox should be considered. 
e. 
Given the extreme moisture variation simulated during the duration of the 
tests and the lack of soil preparation, the test Wafflemats performed very 
well. By incorporating 12-inch Waffleboxes and soil preparation, as 
planned, we believe that the Wafflemat system will provide additional 
stiffness and improved soil behavior, to optimize the foundation 
performance.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site by drilling and sampling 
twenty-one boreholes, designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 105, 1 through 10, 
201 through 203, and 301 through 303, extending to depths of approximately 2.5 to 25.9 
feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the 
Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers.  
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and 
by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to 
the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-21. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550-01(2007), Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed 
to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the 
“Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The 
penetration resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows required 
for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the 
field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive 
strength of the sample.  Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the 
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Six Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test results are 
provided on Plate B-1. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on a selected soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soil and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentations of the grain size distributions are provided on Plate B-2.  
One laboratory California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D1883) was performed on a 
bulk sample of the near-surface soils to evaluate the compressibility and expansion 
characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plate B-3. 
Twelve one-inch Ring Swell tests were performed on both relatively undisturbed 
samples and remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the near-surface 
soils. The test results are summarized on Plate B-4.  
Four One-Dimensional Swell or Collapse of Cohesive Soils Tests (ASTM D4546) 
were performed to measure the amount of swell pressure exerted by a one-inch tall and 
2.42-inch diameter sample of cohesive soil. The sample is first allowed to swell under 
saturated conditions and then compressed back to the original height by incrementally 
adding an increasing surcharge load. Graphic presentations of the test results are 
presented on Plates B-5 through B-8. 
One Expansion Index of Soils test was performed on a bulk sample of the 
near-surface soils in general accordance with ASTM D4829.  The test is a standardized 
test used to determine the expansive characteristics of the soil.  The Expansion Index of 
Soil test is the same test (UBC Standard 18-2) described in the Uniform Building Code 
(UBC) to determine the expansive characteristics of soils.  The test results are 
summarized on Plate B-9. 
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships. The test results are presented on Plates B-10 and B-11.
