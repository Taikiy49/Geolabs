SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, our borings drilled for the roadway rehabilitation project encountered a 
pavement section consisting of approximately 3 to 8 inches of AC over 3 to 8 inches of 
aggregate base course materials.  Below the existing pavement section, the borings 
generally encountered stiff to very stiff clayey silts extending to the maximum depth 
explored of approximately 6.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.   
We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow borings drilled at the time of our 
field exploration.  It should be noted that groundwater level may vary with seasonal rainfall, 
time of the year, and other factors. 
Our field survey indicated that the existing pavements of Makawao Avenue from 
Apana Road to Piiholo Road under consideration for this project appear to exist in fair to 
poor conditions. Based on our engineering evaluation and construction considerations, we 
believe that the areas in fair condition may be rehabilitated with a regular AC overlay 
consisting of cold-planing 3.0 inches of the existing pavements and replacing with 
3.0 inches of new AC overlay. 
We observed pavement failures along various portions of Makawao Avenue within 
the project limits. These highly distressed pavements should be reconstructed with a new 
pavement structural section designed based on a 30-year pavement life, in accordance 
with the guidelines presented in the pavement design manual prepared by the State of 
Hawaii - Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Material Testing and Research 
Branch. In general, the existing highly distressed pavements may be reconstructed with a 
new pavement structural section consisting of 3.0 inches of AC on 6.0 inches of ACB over 
6 inches of AB.   
For Makawao Avenue between Maha Road and Baldwin Avenue, a layer of 
reinforcing geogrid (such as Tensar TriAx Grid, TX7 or equivalent) on a layer of permeable 
separator (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be provided below the new pavement 
structural section.  The reinforcing geogrid would serve to facilitate compaction of the 
aggregate base and to enhance the R-value of the subgrade materials by providing 
stabilization and reinforcement to the underlying subgrade materials. 
We also recommend allocating a contingency fund for additional distressed areas 
requiring reconstruction that may develop between the time the construction plans are 
prepared and the actual commencement of the project construction.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This pavement justification report presents the results of our geotechnical 
engineering analyses performed for the Makawao Avenue Pavement Rehabilitation 
(Apana Road to Piiholo Road) project in Makawao on the Island of Maui, Hawaii. The 
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory 
testing, and presents our geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for 
the rehabilitation and reconstruction of the existing pavements only. The findings and 
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this 
report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The roadway rehabilitation project is along Makawao Avenue from Apana Road 
to Piiholo Road for a distance of approximately 1.85 miles. The approximate limits of the 
roadway rehabilitation project are shown on the Site Plan-1 through Site Plan-20, Plates 
2.1 through 2.20. 
In general, the existing pavements showing minor distresses and severity will be 
rehabilitated with cold-planing and an asphaltic concrete (AC) overlay. Reconstruction 
of the existing pavements will be performed for those areas where the observed 
distresses are likely caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or 
subgrade materials. We understand that the proposed pavement rehabilitation project 
will be federally funded. Therefore, a pavement justification report conforming to the 
requirements and guidelines of the State of Hawaii - Department of Transportation will 
be required. The new pavements will be designed based on the methodology described 
in the Pavement Design Manual dated March 2002, prepared by the State of Hawaii - 
Department of Transportation, Highways Division, Materials Testing and Research 
Branch. 
Special attention was given to the potential presence of near-surface soft and/or 
loose soils, which may be a contributing factor to the pavement failures observed along

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui was built by two major volcanoes, the older West Maui 
(Tertiary Epoch) and the more recent East Maui, also known as Haleakala (Pleistocene 
Epoch).  The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located between these 
two volcanoes. 
The bulk of the Haleakala shield was built during the late Pliocene and early 
Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows of the Honomanu Volcanic 
Series.  During the Pleistocene Epoch, the characteristics of the lavas changed to very 
hard, thickly bedded flows of andesitic composition.  These lavas have been grouped as 
the Kula Volcanic Series.  Typically, the basalt consists of thinly to thickly bedded a’a 
and pahoehoe type flows. 
These lava flows have undergone in-situ weathering over time.  This in-situ 
chemical weathering of the lava has formed a mantle of residual soils that grade to 
saprolite with depth.  In general, saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is 
typical of the tropical weathering of volcanic rocks.  The project site is located on the 
western slopes of the Haleakala shield.  
2.2 
Existing Site/Pavement Conditions 
The existing Makawao Avenue within the project limits is generally a two-lane 
undivided roadway between Apana Road and Piiholo Road except for the additional 
turn lanes at the intersection areas and shoulder parking lanes in Makawao town. The 
approximate project limits of the roadway are shown on the Site Plan-1 through Site 
Plan-20, Plates 2.1 through 2.20. 
The shoulder areas beyond the edge of pavement generally consist of asphalt 
concrete, short cut grass, barren subgrade soils, traffic signs and utility posts. The 
surface soils along the shoulder areas below the vegetation generally consist of brown 
mixture of sand, gravel, silt, and clay. The existing Makawao Avenue roadway 
alignment traverses in a general northwest-southeast direction and is relatively level 
with rolling terrains between Laie Drive and Alana Place, and between Baldwin Avenue

2.2.2 Areas with Structural Defects

distressed areas. Alligator and alligator block cracking were observed to be 
widespread and generally moderate to high in severity. The poor areas consisted of 
elongated, severely distressed zones of alligator block cracking with depressions of 
1 to 2 inches, rutting and some shoving of the pavement within the tire lanes. 
Potholes patched with asphalt concrete (AC) material were observed at various 
locations along the alignment. The ride characteristic of the roadway was observed 
to be generally fair to irregular and bumpy in the areas of poor pavements.

3.4 
Rehabilitation of Existing Pavements 
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement 
areas would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing 
pavements should be performed.  Existing pavements with minor distresses are 
generally rehabilitated with an AC overlay with cold-planing.  Based on our evaluation, it 
is our opinion that pavement areas with minor distresses may be rehabilitated with an 
AC overlay consisting of cold-planing 3.0 inches of the existing pavements and 
replacing with 3.0 inches of new AC overlay. 
In general, the contractor should vacuum-sweep the pavement areas 
immediately after cold-planing to remove gravel-sized materials and dust not picked up 
by the cold-planing operation.  It should be noted that the vacuum-sweep should not 
preclude the dust control operation during cold-planing. In general, the contractor 
should begin paving no later than three working days after cold-planing begins.  Finally, 
vacuum sweeping of cold-planed surfaces should also be performed immediately prior 
to paving. 
3.5 
Design Pavement Sections 
As discussed in the “Existing Site/Pavement Conditions” section herein, majority 
of the observed distresses along the roadway are likely caused by failures in the 
underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials. In general, these areas should be 
reconstructed with a new pavement section in lieu of an AC overlay section.  
Our field exploration and laboratory testing indicate that the roadway alignment 
under consideration for this project is generally underlain by clayey silt soils.  We 
understand that placement of ACB directly on these clayey subgrade is not 
recommended.  Based on our evaluation and these construction considerations, we 
recommend providing a layer of aggregate base between the upper ACB layer and the 
underlying subgrade soils.  The aggregate base will serve to provide a compacted 
granular surface for the placement of the ACB.

The thickness of the flexible pavement section, especially the aggregate base 
layer, for Makawao Avenue between Maha Road and Baldwin Avenue may be reduced 
by incorporating a layer of triaxial geogrid between the upper aggregate base and 
underlying clayey silt subgrade soils. To maximize the benefits of the triaxial geogrid, 
we understand that aggregate base course with nominal maximum size of 1.5 inches 
should be used. In general, the triaxial geogrid will interlock with the aggregate base 
course, resulting in two benefits during initial construction and for the life of the project: 
1) lateral confinement – increasing the modulus of the aggregate base course, and 
2) subgrade bearing capacity enhancement.  Based on the full scale performance test 
results by Tensar as shown on Plate 3, we believe that the R-value of the subgrade 
soils may be enhanced from a value of 6 to 35 (conservative side) with the incorporation

Aggregate base course materials required for the project should consist of 
crushed basaltic aggregates with a 1.5-inch maximum nominal size and should conform 
to Subsection 703.06 of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and 
Bridge Construction (2005).  Aggregate base material should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent 
relative compaction. Relative compaction refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in 
accordance with AASHTO T-180 or ASTM D1557. Optimum moisture is the water 
content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry density. 
Asphalt concrete base (ACB) material should consist of asphalt-treated basalt 
aggregate, placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness, and 
compacted to no less than 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity 
determined in accordance with AASHTO T-209 or ASTM D2041. Asphaltic concrete 
(AC) material should be constructed in accordance with Section 401 Asphalt Concrete 
Pavement of the State of Hawaii, Standard Specifications for Road and Bridge 
Construction (2005) and its amendments.

Sam Kalama Bridge and Emill Balthazar Bridge 
It is our understanding that pavements in the shoulder areas along both bridges

grades.  Therefore, the removed pavement sections should be replaced with pavement 
sections of same thickness.  Based on the shallow borings drilled over the existing 
bridges, a new pavement section consisting of 3 inches of AC over 7 inches of ACB for 
the Sam Kalama Bridge and a new pavement section consisting of 3 inches of AC over 
5 inches of ACB for the Emill Balthazar Bridge should be considered for the repair 
areas. 
For majority of pavements over the existing bridge deck that do not need to be 
removed, the existing pavements should be rehabilitated with 3 inches cold-planning 
and resurfacing with 1.5 inches of AC over a layer of paving fabric (Glasgrid 8511TF or 
equivalent) over 1.5 inches of AC on top of the cold-planed surfaces. 
3.7 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due 
to saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the 
pavement surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be 
maintained to carry surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. 
Surface water ponding should not be allowed during or after construction. Development 
of good shoulder drainage along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage 
structures is strongly recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration 
or premature failure of the pavements.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Field Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the roadway alignment by drilling and 
sampling ten borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 10, extending to a maximum 
depth of about 6.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. In addition, two shallow 
borings, designated as Boring Nos. 2A and 9A, were drilled/cored over the existing bridge 
decks at the Sam Kalama Bridge and Emill Balthazar Bridge, respectively, to evaluate the 
thickness and composition of the pavement sections. The approximate boring locations 
are shown on the Site Plan-1 through Site Plan-20, Plates 2.1 through 2.20. The borings 
were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight augers. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed 
visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes 
(Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. 
Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the 
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-12. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified 
California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 
2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow 
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive 
are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. The penetration resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the 
number of blows required for the specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need 
to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
Pocket Penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in 
the field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined 
compressive strength of the sample.  Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized 
on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Six Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits of the near-surface fine-grained soils. 
The test results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depth. Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Five laboratory Resistance (R) Value tests (ASTM D2844) were performed by 
Ninyo & Moore on bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement 
support characteristics of the soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-2 through 
B-6.
