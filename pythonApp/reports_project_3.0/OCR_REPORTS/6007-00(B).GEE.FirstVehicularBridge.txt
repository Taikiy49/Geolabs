In accordance with our revised fee proposal dated December 21, 2007, this letter
report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration performed for the
First Vehicular Bridge, United States Coast Guard (USCG) Wailupe Family Housing
project.
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering
analyses. These recommendations are intended to assist in the foundation design of the
first vehicular bridge structure only. The findings and recommendations provided herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report.
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located on the northern side of Kalanianaole Highway across
from Wailupe Beach Park on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location and general
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.
Concrete bridge slabs are used to span over an existing drainage ditch for vehicular
traffic at the project site. We understand that the first existing vehicular bridge crossing will
be replaced. The bridge slab has a span of about 10 feet 4 inches. We understand that
the replacement bridge will be approximately 14 feet wide and 18.5 feet long. In addition,
the replacement bridge will consist of two abutment structures.
We understand that the vertical load per abutment is 48 kips dead load and 60 kips
live load. In addition, the strength limit state abutment load is 166 kips. Longitudinal and
transverse seismic loading on the abutment structure are 10 and 11 kips, respectively.

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the surface and
subsurface conditions at the site to develop a soil/rock data set to formulate geotechnical
engineering recommendations for the foundation design of the first vehicular bridge at the
project site. Our work was performed in general accordance with our revised fee proposal
dated December 21, 2007. The scope of our work included the following tasks and work
efforts:
1. Application for permits from the applicable agencies and coordination of
underground utility toning, and site access by our engineer.
2. Mobilization and demobilization of a truck-mounted drill rig and
two operators to and from the project site.
3. Drilling and sampling of two borings at the project site each extending to a
depth of about 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface.
4. Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by our field
engineer.
5. Laboratory testing of selected soil samples obtained during the field
exploration as an aid in classifying the materials encountered and evaluating
their engineering properties.
6. Analyses of the field and laboratory data to formulate geotechnical
engineering recommendations for the bridge foundation design.
7. Preparation of this report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and recommendations.
8. Coordination of our work on the project by our project engineer.
9. Quality assurance of our work and client/design team consultation by our
principal engineer.
10. Miscellaneous work efforts, such as drafting, word processing, and clerical
support.
REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to
late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island. The younger

Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age (Ice Age) and
forms the majority of the eastern two-thirds of the island. The Koolau volcano reached the
end of its main shield building phase about 2 million years ago.
The project site is located on the southeastern flank of the Koolau Range, and its
geomorphology and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio-eustatic
fluctuations of sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age). The base of the
stratigraphic section in this area is Koolau Basalt. The Koolau volcanic shield was built by
thinly bedded lava flows during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene Epochs. The main
shield-building activities ceased approximately 2-1/2 million years ago. In the
mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosional processes are dominated by detachment of
soil and rock mass from the slopes and movement of the material downslope toward the
axis of the valley by gravity to form colluvial deposits. Once the colluvium reaches the
streams in the valley, alluvial processes become dominant, and the sediments are
transported and deposited as alluvium. Large areas of alluvium can be found in the
lowland areas and areas bordering streams.
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of
continental glaciation. As glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill the
oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When
glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea
levels rose to above the present sea level.
The higher sea level stands caused the formulation of deltas and fans of
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits at
corresponding higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet
waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys
in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous
materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits, and
induration of the calcareous reef materials. Land development within the last century has
brought the project site to its present form.
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling
two borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, each to a depth of about 26.5 feet below
the existing ground surface. The approximate boring locations are shown on the
Site Plan, Plate 2.
Our field exploration generally encountered a surface fill layer about 6 to 7 feet thick
underlain by alluvial deposits to about 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface, the
maximum depth explored. The fill layer consists of medium stiff to very stiff silty clay with
sand and gravel, and medium dense silty coral sand with gravel. The alluvial deposits

BRIDGE FOUNDATION
We anticipate that the bottom of spread footings for the new bridge foundation will
be situated near the bottom of the existing concrete channel! structure to reduce the
potential for loading the existing channel wall. Based on the very soft alluvial deposit
encountered below the footing level, settlement of about 10 inches is anticipated for the
spread footing foundation. Because of the excessive settlement of the spread footing
foundation, we recommend using a deep foundation system to provide support to the new
bridge structure.
Micropile Foundation
We believe that a deep foundation system consisting of micropiles may be used. In
general, a micropile foundation system consists of a small diameter pile (usually less than
12 inches), drilled and grouted, with steel reinforcing. The micropile foundation typically is
constructed by drilling a borehole, placing steel casing and reinforcing steel in the hole,
and grouting the borehole. Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed readily
in access restrictive environments and in numerous soil types and ground conditions. In
addition, installation of the micropiles generally causes minimal disturbance to adjacent
structures, the adjacent soils, and the environment.
We believe that a micropile system with a minimum grout bulb diameter of about
7 inches may be used to support the abutment footing. The micropiles should be installed
into the underlying stiff to hard and medium dense to dense alluvial deposits. Permanent
steel casing should be used within the very soft alluvium in the upper portion of the
micropile. Therefore, the steel casing should extend to at least Elevation —5.5 feet MSL.
The steel casing will facilitate pile construction through the very soft deposit, increase the
corrosion resistance of the micropile, and reduce the amount of grout used within the very
soft deposit. The micropiles will derive its support primarily from side resistance within the
underlying stiff to hard and medium dense to dense alluvial deposits.
We understand that the strength limit state vertical load on the abutment is about
166 kips. For five micropiles per abutment footing, the ultimate vertical load is about

57 kips per pile. The micropiles should be spaced at least 3 feet center-to-center. The
minimum pile spacing recommended would avoid reduction in vertical capacity due to
group action and would also facilitate pile installation in the pile group. We anticipate that
the bottom of the pile cap will be about 2 feet below the existing ground surface. Based on
the anticipated pile cap depth and subsurface conditions, we estimate that a micropile
length of about 27 feet is needed to provide the recommended design compressive load
capacity.
Micropile Foundation Settlements
Settlements of the micropile foundations will result primarily from elastic
compression of the pile member and subgrade response. We estimate the total
settlement of the pile-supported foundations to be less than 1 inch with differential
settlements between the micropiles not exceeding about one-half of the total settlement.
We believe that these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are
applied.
Construction Considerations
Installation of micropiles should be performed by a specialty subcontractor
experienced in the construction of a micropile foundation system. Due to the specialized
nature of the micropile foundation construction, observation of the micropile foundation
installation system and testing of the micropiles should be designated a “Special
Inspection” item. Therefore, observation of the micropile installation operations by
Geolabs (Special Inspector) is necessary to confirm our design assumptions and should
be designated a “Special Inspection” item in accordance with Section 1701 of the Uniform
Building Code (1997).
LIMITATIONS
The analyses and recommendations submitted herein are based in part upon
information obtained from field borings. Variations of subsurface conditions between and
beyond the field borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not
become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, Geolabs,
Inc. should be contacted to re-evaluate the recommendations presented in this report.
