INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our preliminary geotechnical engineering
exploration performed for the proposed Kau olu Crown Elderly Housing project in the
Waipahu area on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and preliminary geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the preliminary design of
foundations site preparation and pavements only The findings and preliminary
recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this
report

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western third of the island while the younger shield Koolau forms the majority of the
eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct while Koolau
was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in central Oahu
The project site is located in an area dominated by recent stream and lagoonal
deposits overlying formations of volcanic origin During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age
sea levels fluctuated and reached as much as 300 feet below the present sea level
Stream transported sediments consisting mainly of boulders cobbles and gravel were
deposited at a lower sea level As the sea level began to rise the capacity of the streams
was reduced and finer sands and silts were subsequently deposited At some point the
accumulation of sediments could not keep up with the rising sea level and the area
became flooded As a result very fine grained sediments were then deposited in marine
or estuarine environments Eventually the sea level stabilized while the sedimentation
process of fine grained sediments and shell beds continued Sediments with shell beds
are characteristic of near shore deposits Subsequently organic sediments which are
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3651 00 Page 7
peaty and contain plant fibers and fragments accumulated and are indicative of marshy
conditions Land development within the last 100 years has brought the site to its present
form Many of the resulting fills over the marsh deposits are of poor quality in terms of
supporting heavy structural loads
SITE HISTORY
The project site is part of a larger property formerly owned by the Alii Plantation in
the 1960 s The property was later acquired by the Crown Corporation and subsequently
purchased by the State of Hawaii in 1974 Based on our review of the reports prepared
by Dames Moore we understand that the development history of the project site and its
vicinity dates back to the 1960 s A summary of previous studies performed by
Dames Moore in the vicinity of the project site available for our review is listed on
Plate 3 The approximate locations of the borings and test pits performed in these
studies that are relevant to the subject site are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
corresponding logs of borings and test pits were reproduced and presented in Appendix B
of this report for reference only The information contained in these documents was used
to supplement our engineering analyses in the formulation of the preliminary geotechnical
engineering recommendations presented in this report Geolabs Hawaii is not
responsible for the accuracy of the information provided in these reports
We understand that much of the project site and its vicinity were believed to be
used for rice paddies and taro patches before the 1960 s When the first study was
conducted by Dames Moore in 1966 for Alii Plantation the area was being used as
grazing land for cattle At the time the area was generally level with a few ditches and
drainage conditions appeared to be poor Site investigations conducted by
Dames Moore indicated that the area was generally underlain by thick deposits of soft
and compressible soils beneath a surficial fill crust Because of the soft soil conditions a
surcharge program for site stabilization was developed to induce the potential ground
settlements anticipated from the consolidation of the soft soil deposits under the new fill

Subsoil Conditions
Based on the previous soil boring test pit data obtained by Dames Moore and
the information from our current field exploration the subsurface conditions at the project
site may be generalized by the following four layers in the same order from the ground
surface as encountered in our borings
1 Fill
The project site is generally blanketed with an upper surcharge fill and an
underlying older fill consisting of stiff to very stiff silty clays and clayey silts
The total thickness of the surficial fill layer varies with time and locality
across the site as a result of the various grading activities in the past
Based on the previous soil boring information obtained by Dames Moore
in 1966 the surficial fill layer was on the order of about 6 feet thick prior to
the implementation of the surcharge program Subsequent soil boring data
obtained by Dames Moore in 1990 indicated that relatively competent
materials were encountered to depths of about 11 5 to 15 feet below the
existing ground surface We speculate that this increase in depth of the fill
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3651 00 Page 12
layer may be attributed to the placement of surcharge fill as well as to the
increase in strength of the underlying soft subsoils under the influence of
the surcharge fill loads
Our recent field exploration encountered relatively stiff subsoils to depths of
about 12 5 to 14 feet below the existing ground surface in Boring Nos 1
through 3 which are comparable to the results obtained by Dames Moore
in 1990 In Boring No 4 which is located to the east of the project site and
close to Wailani Stream the surficial fill layer encountered was on the order
of about 8 5 feet thick which would be expected due to the poorer subsoil
conditions in the area and a lesser amount of surcharge fills placed
alongside Wailani Stream
2 Marsh Deposit
The surficial fill layer is generally underlain by a relatively thick marsh
deposit consisting of dark gray very soft and highly compressible organic
silts with decayed vegetation near the interface of this layer and the upper
fill soils Based on the soil boring data obtained by Dames Moore and the
results from our current field exploration it appears that the soft soil deposit
ranges from approximately 20 to 30 feet thick along the northern portion of
the site to about 40 to 50 feet thick in the south southwest side of the site
near Wailani Stream
3 Old Alluvium
This layer generally laid down during the Pleistocene Epoch consisted
mainly of poorly sorted boulder alluvium in a fine grained soil matrix at the
time of deposition Subsequent in situ weathering has altered the materials
to finer grained materials consisting of stiff to very stiff silty clays and sandy
silts This layer is generally encountered between depths of approximately
50 and 65 feet below the existing ground surface
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3651 00 Page 13
4 Basalt Formation
Hard basalt rock formation is encountered below the old alluvium deposit at
depths ranging from about 70 to 85 feet below the existing ground surface
at the Phase Ill development site It appears that the top of the bedrock
tends to be shallower along the north northwest side of the site and
becomes deeper toward the south southeast portion of the project site
Groundwater Conditions
Artesian groundwater conditions were reported in previous reports prepared by
Dames Moore The hydraulic head was reported to be about 0 7 feet above the old
ground surface old fill in 1974 or estimated to be about elevation 10 feet MSL The
groundwater level measured in previous borings at the site was reported to be between
elevations 5 and 8 feet MSL
It appears that the underlying basaltic formation is part of the Pearl Harbor Aquifer
which is confined by overlying deposits of marine and alluvial deposits with low
permeability commonly referred to as caprock Groundwater has been heavily
developed within the Pearl Harbor Aquifer in the last 100 years The hydraulic head in
the vicinity of the site was approximately 33 feet MSL in 1879 23 feet MSL in 1958
and 17 feet MSL in 1978 as published by the Board of Water Supply 1980 We
believe that further head declines may occur and this head decline could initiate more
areal and local ground settlements due to the artesian head loss
Groundwater levels were generally encountered at depths of about 6 5 to 11 6 feet
below the existing ground surface at the time of our recent field exploration These
groundwater levels correspond to elevations between 3 8 and 4 2 feet MSL Due to
the proximity of the project site to Wailani Stream and Pearl Harbor it is likely that the
water levels measured in the borings during our field exploration were affected by
seasonal precipitation storm surge conditions and tidal influences

DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our current field exploration and the information contained in the
various reports prepared by Dames Moore the project site is generally blanketed with
an upper surcharge fill and an underlying older fill on the order of about 8 to 15 feet
thick The surficial fill layer is generally underlain by a marsh deposit consisting of very
soft and highly compressible organic silts varying from about 20 to 50 feet in thickness
Hard basalt rock formation was encountered at depths ranging from about 70 to 85 feet
below the existing ground surface at the Phase III development site
Because of the moderate to high structural loads anticipated from the mid rise
building planned for the Phase III development a shallow foundation system would be
subjected to appreciable total and differential settlements due to the consolidation of
the underlying soft marsh deposits As a result we recommend that a deep foundation
system consisting of 16 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles end
bearing on the hard basalt formation underlying the site be used to support the
proposed mid rise apartment building for the Phase III development
An allowable compressive load capacity on the order of about 100 to 120 tons
with a preliminary pile length of about 75 to 90 feet below the existing ground surface
may be used for preliminary pile foundation design Because of the presence of the
soft compressible soils underlying the site at shallow depths we recommend that the
floor slabs for the proposed mid rise building be structurally supported on pile
foundations to reduce the potential for differential settlements between the structure
supported on piles and the floor slab supported on grade
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3651 00 Page 15
We believe that the on site soft and compressible deposits may still be
undergoing long term settlement from the surcharge fill loads and lowering of the
regional artesian head Therefore structures constructed over these soft soil deposits
on shallow foundations should take into account the potential impacts resulting from
these future ground settlements
For the one to two story residential structures planned for the Phase IV
development we believe that a shallow foundation system may be utilized for support
of the structures provided that the following measures and recommendations are
implemented during the project development to reduce the potential impacts on the
future performance of the residential structures and foundation systems from the on
going long term ground settlements These may include but are not limited to the
following 1 re establishment of a settlement monitoring program 2 removal and or
replacement of the previously placed surcharge fill 3 avoiding new site filling above
the existing ground elevations 4 use of reinforced grid or mat foundations and
5 use of flexible structural systems such as wood or steel framed construction
Alternatively the one to two story residential structures may be supported on driven pile
foundations embedded in the stiff alluvial soils and saprolite underlying the soft marsh
deposits to reduce the adverse effects which may result from potential future ground
settlements

Pile Foundations
We believe that 1674 inch octagonal precast prestressed concrete piles may be
used to support the proposed mid rise building The piles would derive support
principally from end bearing on the hard basalt rock formation underlying the site
at greater depths as encountered in our borings drilled for the subject project An
allowable compressive load capacity on the order of about 100 to 120 tons may be

Downdrag
Where the regional artesian head is lowered or fill is placed over a compressible
soil the compressible deposit consolidates and moves downward relative to the
pile The downward movement results in negative skin friction forces generally
known as downdrag forces on the pile shaft The magnitude of the downdrag
force depends on the thickness of the compressible layer adhesion between the
soil and pile and the pile perimeter area
With a structurally supported floor slab as recommended for this project potential
ground settlements would only be induced by any new fills needed to raise the
existing ground surface to the bottom elevation of the structural slab Based on the
existing topography at the site we believe that site filling would not be required to
attain the design finish subgrade Therefore we anticipate the potential for ground
settlements resulting from new fills would be low In any case the design build
team should re evaluate the potential for ground settlements and downdrag forces
based on the specific development plan proposed
The other source of potential ground settlements would be from the on going
secondary settlement resulting from the previous surcharge fills placed in the area
However we believe that a substantial amount of the secondary compression
should have occurred since the surcharge fills were placed almost 18 years ago
Therefore we do not believe that significant downdrag forces would be imposed
on the pile as a result of these settlements

Pile Installation
Determination of driving equipment to be used for this project should take into
consideration the matching of the pile hammer with the weight of the piles to be
driven This will result in the pile hammer efficiently delivering the required energy
to the top of the pile and also reducing damage to the piles The hammer should
be equipped with an energy level control such that driving within the loose soft soil
may be controlled at a lower energy to reduce tensile stress development within
the piles which could damage or cause cracking of the piles
In general each pile should be driven continuously without interruption A pile may
be rejected or its design capacity may be reduced when the driving resistance is
interrupted for more than four hours and the pile cannot be driven to the required
depth and or meeting the pile driving criteria Because variations in soil strength
characteristics are likely to exist in the bearing materials the supporting capacity of
each pile must be confirmed in the field during driving A test pile program should
be carried out to confirm the pile design and to establish pile driving criteria The
production pile driving criteria should be evaluated and or revised based on the
test pile program

Settlements
In general three types of potential ground settlements may result from the
consolidation of the soft soil deposits underlying the project site including total
differential and long term settlements Substantial total settlements are
anticipated under any new fill above the existing ground surface and significant
building loads because of the highly compressible nature of the thick and soft
marsh deposit underlying the site at shallow depths The amount of settlements
would vary from location to location depending mainly on the thickness of the fill
required for site grading and the thickness of the compressible deposit underlying
the site
As previously indicated settlements interpreted from the contour comparison of
the 1979 and 1988 topographic plans indicate that total settlements on the order of
1 to 4 feet appeared to have occurred over most of the surcharge areas during the
1980 s with up to 5 to 7 feet in Area G and a portion of Area F To further
evaluate the ground settlement history at the project site since 1988 we have
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3651 00 Page 24
conducted a contour comparison of the 1979 and 1997 topographic survey maps
Based on the results from our interpretation we believe that the primary
consolidation process of the soft soil deposits resulting from the surcharge loads
appears to be essentially completed
Differential settlements are generally caused by the variations or the
non uniformity in the thickness and the compressibility of the soft soil deposits
Any variation in the applied loads from the building structures could also cause
differential settlements As a general rule of thumb differential settlements may
range from about 50 to 70 percent of the total settlement and could occur over
relatively short distances
After completion of the relatively large primary consolidation the soft and
compressible soils would undergo a slow and continuous viscous compression
under any given load The rate of continuing settlement after the completion of the
primary consolidation would be relatively uniform In addition it would take a
relatively long period of time to achieve stabilization in ground settlement The
magnitude of the long term settlement is difficult to estimate depending on the
amount of new fill placed the building loads applied as well as the thickness and
compressibility of the soft soil deposits

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling
four borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 4 to depths ranging from about 51 5 to
99 5 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate locations shown on the
Site Plan Plate 2 The borings were drilled with a truck mounted drill rig equipped with
continuous flight augers and rotary tools as necessary
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Relatively undisturbed samples of the soft soil deposits were obtained by
hydraulically pushing a 3 inch O D thin wall Shelby tube into the soft soil deposit The
penetration resistances measured in p s i needed to push the Shelby tube sampler into
the soil deposit are also recorded on the Logs of Borings
Pocket penetrometer and torvane shear tests were performed on selected
cohesive soil samples retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer and torvane shear
tests of the selected samples are shown on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description

Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX C
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX C _
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Two 1 inch ring swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate the
swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are summarized on
Plate C 1
Six Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected samples of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits The test results are presented on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic presentation of the test results
is provided on Plate C 2
To evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the on site soils strain controlled
consolidated drained direct shear tests were performed on selected soil samples in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3080 The test results are shown on
Plates C 3 and C 4
To evaluate the undrained shear strength of the clayey soils unconsolidated
undrained triaxial compression tests were performed on selected samples in accordance
with ASTM Test Designation D 2850 The approximate in situ effective overburden
pressure was used as the applied confining pressure The test results and stress strain
curves are presented on Plates C 5 and C 6
Three consolidation tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the
compressibility characteristics of the on site soils These tests were performed in
accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 2435 and the test results are plotted on
Plates C 7 through C 9
Two California Bearing Ratio CBR ASTM D 1883 tests were performed on bulk
samples collected for preliminary pavement design Results of the CBR tests are
presented on Plates C 10 and C 11
Two Modified Proctor compaction tests ASTM D 1557 were performed on bulk
samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content
relationships The test results are presented on Plates C 12 and C 13
