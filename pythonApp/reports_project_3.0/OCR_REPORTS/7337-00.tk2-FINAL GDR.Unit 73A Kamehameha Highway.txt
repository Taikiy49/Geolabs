SECTION 1.  
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project is along Kamehameha Highway between about Likelike Highway and Haiku 
Road in the Kaneohe area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project location and general 
vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
Based on the information provided, we understand that it is proposed to rehabilitate 
and/or reconstruct approximately 7,300 linear feet of the existing asphaltic concrete pavement 
along Kamehameha Highway between about Likelike Highway and Haiku Road.  The approximate 
limits of the project alignment are shown on the General Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Based on our experience in the project vicinity, we anticipate the subgrade materials 
under the roadway would primarily consist of residual soils and alluvial deposits.  Our 
exploration services will consist of drilling and sampling eight borings at selected locations 
along the roadway alignment.  In addition, dynamic cone penetration (DCP) testing will be 
performed adjacent to the drilled borings to provide additional data and information for 
evaluation and analyses.  Laboratory testing will be conducted on the soil samples collected to 
determine their physical and engineering properties.  We understand that pavement design will 
be performed by the City and County of Honolulu – Department of Design and Construction.   
The objective of the geotechnical exploration program is to collect information to support 
the pavement design of the roadway rehabilitation and/or reconstruction project. A summary of 
the field exploration program conducted for the project is presented below. Detailed information 
and discussions are provided in Appendices A through C of this geotechnical data report.

SECTION 3.  
SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
3.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae and 
Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late Pliocene 
in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the island.  The younger Koolau Volcano is 
estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age and forms the majority of the 
eastern two-thirds of the island.  The Koolau Volcano reached the end of its main 
shield-building phase about 2 million years ago. 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation.  As the continental glaciers advanced, less water was available to fill the 
oceanic basins such that the sea levels fell to below the present stand.  When the glaciers 
melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose to above 
the present level.  
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic sea level 
fluctuations.  The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of 
terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulation of reef deposits at 
correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in the quiet 
waters protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the 
sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials 
caused desiccation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the 
calcareous reef materials.  In addition, renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of the 
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions. 
In some of the mountainous regions of Hawaii, chemical weathering has altered the 
basaltic flows to a soil-like condition.  Erosional processes, consisting of detachment of soil and 
rock masses from the valley walls and transport downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily 
by gravity as colluvium, have also altered the natural condition of the volcanic flows.  Once 
these materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become 
dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium.

When alluvial/colluvial deposits are left in place for long periods of time, chemical 
processes begin to alter the materials simultaneously causing a breakdown or weathering of 
the material.  Erosion continually deposits newer material covering the older alluvial/colluvial 
deposits.  Depending on the rate of transport, the newer sediments are generally transient in 
terms of geologic time.  
The project area is generally underlain by these older and more recent alluvial soil 
deposits or by the soil-like, highly weathered basaltic lava flows/pyroclastic ejecta.  
Development has since altered the natural formation of the area to its present form. 
3.2 
Site Description 
The project site is along a portion of Kamehameha Highway extending between about 
Likelike Highway and Haiku Road in the Kaneohe area on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The existing 
Kamehameha Highway alignment under consideration for this project generally is a four-lane 
roadway with two travel lanes in each direction.  Based on our field observations, it appears that 
the roadway has received multiple AC overlays in the past.  
Based on our site reconnaissance and the topographic survey maps provided, the 
highway generally slopes down from the south towards the north with existing ground surface 
elevations ranging from a topographic high of about +113 feet Mean Sea Level (MSL) at Likelike 
Highway to a topographic low of about +54 feet MSL near Station 44+00. 
3.3 
Subsurface Characteristics 
The primary surface soils in the area are classified in the “Soil Survey of Islands of Kauai, 
Oahu, Maui, Molokai, and Lanai, State of Hawaii” issued August 1972, by the U.S. Department 
of Agriculture Soil Conservation Service, and is described as follows: 
Lolekaa silty clay, 3 to 8 percent slopes (LoB) 
This soil is on terraces and fans.  In a representative profile, the surface layer is 
dark-brown silty clay about 10 inches thick.  The subsoil is 46 to more than 70 inches 
thick. The upper part is dark-brown silty clay that has subangular blocky structure, and 
the lower part is dark yellowish-brown loam that has subangular blocky structure.  The 
substratum is strongly weathered gravel.  The soil is strongly acidic in the surface layer

GEOTECHNICAL EXPLORATION PROGRAM 
The geotechnical exploration program conducted for the highway pavement 
rehabilitation and/or reconstruction project generally consisted of the following field and 
laboratory work components. A brief description of each work component is presented below. 
Detailed discussions, findings, and the test results of the various components are provided in 
the respective appendices herein. 
4.1 
Soil Borings, Rock Coring, and Associated Field Testing 
The field exploration is described in detail in the introduction of Appendix A. The 
following discussion provides an overview of the methods and procedures used. 
Typically, soil borings were advanced with 4-inch diameter continuous-flight solid-stem 
augers (SSA).  Sampling of the soils was performed at continuous penetration from near the 
existing pavement surface. Drive samples were obtained using the modified California sampler 
(ASTM D3550) and the Standard Penetration Test (SPT) split-spoon sampler (ASTM D1586).  
In-situ testing of the subsurface materials in the borings included the SPT, which was 
performed in accordance with ASTM D1586. SPT sampling provides a measure of the resistance 
or consistency of a soil and is reported as the uncorrected N-value, which is defined as the 
number of blows required to drive a standard split-spoon sampler 12 inches with a 140-lb 
hammer, after 6 inches of initial seating. The penetration resistance of the modified California 
sampler was recorded in a similar manner. 
During the field exploration program, our geologist performed pocket penetrometer 
tests on selected cohesive soil samples. These test values are shown on the Logs of Borings at 
the appropriate sample depths in Appendix A. 
4.2 
Dynamic Cone Penetration Testing 
In addition to borehole drilling and sampling, our field exploration program also 
included performance of Dynamic Cone Penetration testing adjacent to the drilled boring 
locations to provide additional data and information for pavement evaluation and analyses.

The Dynamic Cone Penetration test provides a measure of a material’s in-situ resistance 
to penetration. The test is performed by driving a metal cone into the ground by repeatedly 
striking it with a 17.6-lb (8-kg) weight dropped from a distance of 22.6 inches (575 mm). The 
penetration of the cone is measured after each blow and is recorded to provide a continuous 
measure of shear resistance up to 72 inches (6 feet) below the pavement surface. Test results 
can be correlated to California Bearing Ratio, in-situ density, resilient modulus, and bearing 
capacity. 
The DCP tests were generally conducted at 1-inch (25.4-mm) discrete depth intervals 
between depths of about 6 to 72 inches below the existing pavement surface at the test 
locations.  Field logs of the DCP tests are included in Appendix B of this report for reference.  
4.3 
Laboratory Testing 
Laboratory testing was performed on soil samples to determine the physical and 
engineering properties. Laboratory tests performed included moisture content, unit weight, 
Atterberg limits, grain size distribution, and laboratory CBR tests. The laboratory test results are 
provided in Appendix C.

LIMITATIONS 
The geotechnical field and laboratory test data submitted in this geotechnical data 
report are based upon information obtained from the field exploration points. The field boring, 
dynamic cone penetration test, and bulk sample locations indicated herein are approximate, 
having been estimated by taping from visible features shown on the Reconstruction Plans 
downloaded from AECOM project website on June 15, 2016. Elevations of the field data points 
were interpolated from the contour lines and spot elevations shown on the same plans. The 
field exploration locations and elevations should be considered accurate only to the degree 
implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the field exploration 
points depict the approximate boundaries between the soil and rock types and, as such, may 
denote a gradual transition. Water level data presented on the logs of borings in this report 
were based on measurements recorded in the borings at the times shown on the graphic 
representations. It should be noted that fluctuation of the water level may occur due to 
variation in tides, rainfall, temperature, and other factors.

A P P E N D I X   A 
Test Boring Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the highway alignment by drilling and 
sampling eight borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 8, extending to depths of about 
5 to 5.4 feet below the existing pavement surface using truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous-flight augers. The field exploration was conducted from May 18 through 
June 8, 2016. The approximate boring locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 
3.9. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and textural 
examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for Description 
and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a near-continuous basis. These 
classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified 
in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for 
Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.1.  Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described in the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2.  Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-8. 
Soil borings were advanced with 4-inch diameter continuous-flight solid-stem augers 
(SSA).  Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general 
accordance with ASTM D3550-01(2007), Standard Practice for Thick Wall, Ring-Lined, Spilt 
Barrel, Drive Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD modified California sampler with a 
140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the 
borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D1586, Standard Test Method for Penetration 
Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler 
using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second 
and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the 
field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive 
strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are summarized on the Logs 
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests 
We explored the subsurface conditions along the highway rehabilitation and/or 
reconstruction project by performing Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) tests at eight locations, 
extending to depths of about 60 to 72 inches below the pavement finished grade.  The 
approximate DCP test locations are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.9. Results are 
shown on the Dynamic Core Penetrometer Driving Logs, Plates B-1 through B-8. 
The procedures used in our DCP tests are in general accordance with ASTM D6951, 
Standard Test Method for Use of the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer in Shallow Pavement 
Applications.  The DCP apparatus consists of a 5/8-inch diameter steel rod with a 60 degree 
conical tip. The rod is topped with an anvil that is connected to a second steel rod. This rod is 
used as a guide to allow a 17.6-lb (8-kg) hammer to be repeatedly raised and dropped from a 
height of 22.6 inches. The connection between the two rods consists of an anvil to allow for 
quick connections between the rods and for efficient energy transfer from the falling weight to 
the penetrating rod. 
Once the test apparatus is assembled, the DCP is placed at the test location and the 
initial penetration of the rod is recorded to provide a zeroing scale. While holding the rod 
vertically, the weight is raised to the top of the rod (22.6 inches above the anvil) and dropped. 
The penetration of the rod is measured after each drop. 
In the DCP tests conducted by Geolabs, the same equipment described above was used 
to record the number of blows required to advance the cone in 1-inch (25.4-mm) depth 
increments, as shown in the field logs provided in Appendix B.

Laboratory Tests 
Samples recovered from our field exploration program were visually examined and 
classified in our laboratory in Oahu, Hawaii. Selected samples were tested to evaluate the 
physical characteristics and engineering properties of the materials encountered. The 
laboratory tests performed included determination of dry density and natural moisture 
content, Atterberg Limits, sieve analysis, and laboratory CBR tests. Results of the tests 
performed are presented on Plates C-1 through C-10. Detailed descriptions of the various tests 
performed for this exploration are presented below. 
Moisture and Density 
Moisture content and dry density tests were performed on selected soil samples to aid 
in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and correlating the layers 
between test borings. The moisture content tests were performed in general accordance with 
ASTM D2216. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. 
Atterberg Limits 
Eight (8) Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the 
liquid limit (LL), plastic limit (PL), and plasticity index (PI), as an aid in classifying the soils. The 
Atterberg Limits tests were performed in general accordance with ASTM D4318. The test results 
are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation 
of the test results is provided on Plate C-1. 
Sieve Analysis 
To provide information on the particle size distribution of the soils encountered, one (1) 
Sieve Analysis test was performed to evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil 
classification. The test was performed in accordance with ASTM C117 and C136. Graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate C-2. 
California Bearing Ratio 
Eight laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on bulk 
samples of the in-situ clayey soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils. 
The test results are presented on Plates C-3 through C-10.
