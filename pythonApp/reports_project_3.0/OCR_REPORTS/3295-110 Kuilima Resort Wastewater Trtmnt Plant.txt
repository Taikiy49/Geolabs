INTRODUCTION
This report presents the findings of our injection well performance evaluation for the
Kuilima Resort Wastewater Treatment Plant in Kahuku on the Island of Oahu Hawaii
The findings and recommendations presented herein are based on observations recorded
during our site visit the performance of short term injection testing of the wells and
communication with the operators and manager of the treatment plant facility The
approximate location of the treatment plant facility and the injection well field are shown on
the Project Location Map Plate 1
Our work was performed in general accordance with our fee proposal dated
and the State of Hawaii Department of Health Underground Injection
Control UIC Injection Well Status Report Guidelines dated October 1999 This injection
well performance evaluation report covers the current monitoring period which extends
from June 2004 through May 2005
END OF INTRODUCTION

SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Project Considerations
The Kuilima Resort Wastewater Treatment Plant injection wells are utilized for
the disposal of secondary treated effluent generated within the Turtle Bay Resort
development The majority of the effluent treated at the plant may be used for irrigation
of the resort golf courses located within the Turtle Bay Resort complex Therefore the
wells are used for the injection of treated effluent on a stand by basis during periods of
time when the effluent is not being utilized for irrigation purposes
As a condition of the Underground Injection Control UIC permit UIC Permit
No UO 1532 issued for the facility it is required that routine monitoring of the wells be
performed by a professional geologist or engineer familiar with injection well operations
Geolabs Inc has prepared this injection well evaluation report to provide the owner the
operator and the regulatory agency with information regarding the performance of the
injection wells to aid in the prevention of adverse public health effects from an injection
well system malfunction or failure This evaluation is intended to assist in ascertaining
that the well system remains operational

2 2 Well Field Description
The wells are located on the easterly side of the tee boxes for the 10th Hole of
Golf Course G 2 The wells are situated in an open grass area between the tees and
the moat around Punahoolapa Marsh The topography of the well field area is generally
level with some topographic mounds and depressions associated with the adjacent golf
course terrain Based on a topographic map of the well field and vicinity ground
surface elevations range from about 5 to 6 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
The well heads are enclosed in below ground sump vaults of approximately
4 feet by 4 feet in plan dimension and about 5 feet in depth The vaults are covered
with hinged steel plate doors and are approximately flush with the adjacent
grass covered ground surface
Inside the vaults the well heads consist of steel tees with flange plates bolted to
their tops for security and to allow the build up of additional head if needed These
plates were removed by plant personnel for the well inspection and performance of the
injection tests In 1994 two inch diameter inspection ports were installed in the well
covers to eliminate the need for removal of the entire top plate during future
inspection testing However to allow complete access to the well interior for the
performance of the short term injection tests and visual inspection of the exposed well
casing the top plate of each well was removed

3 1 Well Field Usage
In general only two of the three wells are placed in use for effluent disposal
when the well system is activated The third well is taken out of operation on a rotating
basis so that at any given time only two wells are in operation Flow to an individual
well is controlled by a valve located adjacent to each well head Each valve is housed
in a steel cased vault of about 12 inches in diameter A main control valve diverts the
effluent flow from the treatment plant to either the well field or the irrigation pond which
serves as a Storage reservoir for golf course irrigation water
The following information pertaining to the effluent flow to the well field is based
on a review of the available WWTP Daily Operating Log records for the period of

elevated rainfall when the golf course irrigation needs were typically reduced

3 5 Short Term Injection Tests
Short term injection tests were performed in each of the three wells by diverting
the effluent into each well separately Each well was isolated from the others for the
duration of each test by adjusting the valves controlling the effluent flow to individual
wells
During injection testing the head level response was continually measured and
recorded using an electronic piezometer pressure transducer and data logging
recorder linked to a portable computer Thus the head level response could be
monitored continuously and simultaneously to record the fluctuation of head levels
within the wells Manual readings collected from the totalizing flow meter were recorded
during the short term injection test to obtain the total quantity of injected effluent and to
estimate the average rate of flow into each well The duration of the short term injection
tests ranged between approximately 16 and 57 minutes The average incoming flow
rate to the well field from the WWTP was calculated to range between about
550 gallons per minute and 700 gallons per minute during the injection testing

Based on the short term injection test results and the well usage data we believe
the injection wells have generally maintained their disposal capacity relative to the

SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND FINDINGS
Injection wells sustain losses in capacity over time due to natural biological and
chemical fouling processes or other introduced factors such as suspended solids or debris
contained in the effluent In addition extended periods of well inactivity and the
introduction of poor quality effluent containing excessive suspended solids may cause
acceleration of the normal losses in capacity It should be noted that some loss in capacity
may be permanent and irreversible however it has also been proven that redevelopment
of injection wells can aid in the recovery of lost capacity provided that the proper methods
are utilized on a regular basis
Based on our injection well evaluation we recommend that the owner operator
continue to operate the injection wells on a routine rotating basis in an attempt to maintain
the disposal capacity of the system In addition we recommend that the owner operator
closely monitor and document the operation of each of the wells to assure that overflows
or back ups related to a well failure are detected prior to their occurrence In the event an
overflow of a well or other failure occurs and the situation cannot be remedied the
owner operator should promptly notify the Department of Health Safe Drinking Water
Branch
We believe that by maintaining the use of the wells on a rotating basis especially
during periods of low effluent availability the current operational performance of the wells
should be maintained Keeping the wells operational is not normally a problem however
we believe it is crucial to maintain rotating operation of all the wells during the periods of
time when low effluent flow are experienced
We recommend that the owner operator continue recording field observations such
as the following 1 notes regarding the overall system operation 2 documentation of well
maintenance performed including well cleaning and replacement of faulty parts and
instruments 3 observation of the head levels within the wells prior to and during injection
events 4 daily flow volumes 5 a log of individual well usage and 6 notes pertaining to
the increase or decrease of the total flow to the plant for treatment This information will be
used to evaluate the future performance of the injection wells In addition the results of

Generally redevelopment should be considered if head levels continue to increase

However when the wells begin to fail it may often be too late to restore the capacity

Based on our observation of the valves that control the flow to each injection

2 2 Well Field Description
The wells are located on the easterly side of the tee boxes for the 10th Hole of
Golf Course G 2 The wells are situated in an open grass area between the tees and
the moat around Punahoolapa Marsh The topography of the well field area is generally
level with some topographic mounds and depressions associated with the adjacent golf
course terrain Based on a topographic map of the well field and vicinity ground
surface elevations range from about 5 to 6 feet Mean Sea Level MSL
The well heads are enclosed in below ground sump vaults of approximately
4 feet by 4 feet in plan dimension and about 5 feet in depth The vaults are covered
with hinged steel plate doors and are approximately flush with the adjacent
grass covered ground surface
Inside the vaults the well heads consist of steel tees with flange plates bolted to
their tops for security and to allow the build up of additional head if needed These
plates were removed by plant personnel for the well inspection and performance of the
injection tests In 1994 two inch diameter inspection ports were installed in the well

3 6 Recommendations
Based on the short term injection test results and the well usage data we believe
the injection wells have generally maintained their disposal capacity relative to the
available historical well capacity data for the well system The maintenance of the well
system injection capacity is believed to be a result of the annual well cleaning
performed on the wells and the continued rotation of the injection well usage However
Well No 1 appears to have experienced some decline in disposal capacity relative to
the prior performance evaluation The decline in disposal capacity at Well No 1 may
have resulted from the overall lower usage of the well field system during the past year
which could have resulted in additional growth of biological material In addition the
wells were cleaned only once during the past year as opposed to twice per year in
previous years To maintain the disposal capacity of the well system we recommend
continued well cleaning well operation rotation and well usage at least once per week
especially for Well No 1
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND FINDINGS
Injection wells sustain losses in capacity over time due to natural biological and
chemical fouling processes or other introduced factors such as suspended solids or debris
contained in the effluent In addition extended periods of well inactivity and the
introduction of poor quality effluent containing excessive suspended solids may cause
acceleration of the normal losses in capacity It should be noted that some loss in capacity
may be permanent and irreversible however it has also been proven that redevelopment
of injection wells can aid in the recovery of lost capacity provided that the proper methods
are utilized on a regular basis
Based on our injection well evaluation we recommend that the owner operator
continue to operate the injection wells on a routine rotating basis in an attempt to maintain
the disposal capacity of the system In addition we recommend that the owner operator
closely monitor and document the operation of each of the wells to assure that overflows
or back ups related to a well failure are detected prior to their occurrence In the event an
overflow of a well or other failure occurs and the situation cannot be remedied the
owner operator should promptly notify the Department of Health Safe Drinking Water
Branch
We believe that by maintaining the use of the wells on a rotating basis especially
during periods of low effluent availability the current operational performance of the wells
should be maintained Keeping the wells operational is not normally a problem however
we believe it is crucial to maintain rotating operation of all the wells during the periods of
time when low effluent flow are experienced
We recommend that the owner operator continue recording field observations such
as the following 1 notes regarding the overall system operation 2 documentation of well
maintenance performed including well cleaning and replacement of faulty parts and
instruments 3 observation of the head levels within the wells prior to and during injection
events 4 daily flow volumes 5 a log of individual well usage and 6 notes pertaining to
the increase or decrease of the total flow to the plant for treatment This information will be
used to evaluate the future performance of the injection wells In addition the results of
Hawaii California
SECTION 3 DISCUSSION AND FINDINGS
our short term injection testing of the wells may be used to evaluate the operation of the
wells with respect to prior well inspection data
Generally redevelopment should be considered if head levels continue to increase
over time or when well failures such as overflow or system back ups may be anticipated
However when the wells begin to fail it may often be too late to restore the capacity
Therefore based on the effectiveness of the recent well redevelopment we believe it may
be prudent to continue scheduling well redevelopment at annual intervals
Well redevelopment should be performed by a professional experienced with
coastal injection well construction and rehabilitation The redevelopment method selected
should be carefully evaluated based on the existing well design proper containment and
disposal of wastewater generated by the activity and other factors specific to the site and
operation of the wells
Based on our observation of the valves that control the flow to each injection
well the valves appear to be working satisfactorily however the valves are not visible
due to their continual submergence below water Therefore the valve cannot be
visually inspected In addition the long term and continual submergence of the valve
may accelerate corrosion of the hardware We do not believe that this is a critical
concern however consideration may be given to reconstructing the valve vault so that
it is sealed from the infiltration of groundwater to facilitate inspection for signs of wear
In consideration of other possible long term improvements that could facilitate
monitoring of the injection well performance it is suggested that individual flow metering
devices be installed between each well control valve and well head The installation of
additional flow meters would provide direct readout of flow data for each individual well
which would improve the direct correlation between the flow rate and the head level
response in the well As a result changes in the well flow rate could be monitored and
adjustments to the control valve could be made to achieve the desired flow rates that
can be compared from year to year Currently only the total flow to the well field is
known therefore when more than one well is operating simultaneously it is not possible
to estimate the actual flow rate into the individual wells This scenario occurs when the

KAHULUI ATRPORT

we have conducted an engineering study of the adequacy of the existing
taxiway and apron pavements at Kahului Airport to serve heavy aircraft of

Existing Pavement Surface Condition
The 1970 Dames Moore report indicated that the then existing
concrete pavement surface was seriously cracked and the asphaltic
concrete surface was also oxidized and cracked at random locations
In 1982 approximately 8 years since the apron rehabilitation
project was carried out in 1974 a survey of the then existing crack was
carried out by Geolabs Hawaii The observations indicated that cracking
existed in most parts of the concrete apron while the asphalt concrete
surface was in generally good condition The most severe cracking
occurred at the central section of the apron area especially on location
nearest to the terminal building Very little to no cracking occurred at
both ends of the apron
The current surface crack condition follows similar pattern as
observed in 1982 with the exception that new cracks was found in the
asphaltic apron area The current observations also indicated that most
of these cracks had been backfilled

Existing Subgrade Condition
It appeared that most of the borings indicated that the subgrade was
formed by a residual soil consisting mainly of clayey silt with some
highly decomposed basalt gravel Under the FAA classification system
the material belonged to the Soil Group E 7 and hence together with
poor drainage condition was classified as F 5 subgrade The CBR FAA
Subgrade Class Comparison Chart indicated that the corresponding CBR
value for F 5 class subgrade was between 8 and 9
Field and laboratory CBR tests had been performed by both Dames and
moore and Geolabs Hawaii The results indicated a large variation
Laboratory values varied from 6 5 to 41 0 with a mean of 21 and a
standard deviation of 13 In accordance with the FAA Advisory Circular
design criteria the recommended design CBR value is
generally selected as the value which is one standard deviation below the
mean of the test values Therefore based on the laboratory test
results the design CBR value for the residual silt subgrade soil would
be 8

Discussion and Conclusion
General
The pavement analysis carried out earlier in this report indicated
that the existing pavement thickness is Significantly less than that
390 000 and 460 000 pounds respectively Therefore unless further
improvement can be carried out to the existing pavement the latter
should be considered inadequate to serve the abovementioned aircrafts
Methods of Improvement
The methods of improvement can in general be divided into the
following main catagories
1 Improvement of the subgrade so that a higher subgrade CBR value
or modulus value can be achieved
2 improvement to the base course material so that a reduction in
the design pavement thickness can be achieved and
5 overlay the existing pavement so that the required adequate
pavement thickness is achieved
These three methods will be discussed individually and presented as
follows
Improvement of Subgrade
Improvement of the subgrade within the study indeed can only be
considered hypothetical First of all it will be unlikely to
perform grouting to the existing subgrade soil because of its fine
grain nature Secondly it would take a major effect also almost
impossible to convince the FAA to reclassify the existing subgrade
so that a higher subgrade CBR value can be used in the design
Furthermore the deteriorating condition at the airport simply
suggests that such an attempt will meet with refusal
W O 1446 00 Page 8
Improvement of Base and Subbase
Successful improvement to the pavement of Keahole Airport at the
island of Hawaii by injecting grout into the base and subbase
material have been carried out
The structural benefit imparted to a pavement section by such
improved base and subbase is reflected in the modulus of subgrade
reaction assigned to the foundation Workable as it is the
improvement method by grouting will face some significant drawbacks
when applied to the study area in the Kahului Airport The major
reason will be the already existing poor drainage condition of the
subgrade soil Grouting of the base course layer will further
deplete the chance of proper drainage Subsequently the subgrade
soil will be saturated and weakened from moisture that migrated
through cracks and construction joints in the pavement surface
during rainy season causing deterioration to the pavement surface
Besides the drainage problem it is anticipated that the possible
amount of improvement for the base and subbase will be insufficient
to achieve a significant increase in strength of the pavement for
the design aircraft types with their maximum loadings

airport pavement can provide an assuring solution to the problen

Conclusion
The study concluded that the existing pavement thickness at the
apron and taxiway A area of Kahului Airport is inadequate to serve the
weight
Different methods of improvement to the existing pavement had been
considered and it appeared that pavement overlay will provide the most
assuring solution For the concrete apron depending on different method
of overlay 6 to 10 inches of concrete overlay would be required For
the flexible pavement the bituminous overlay required would be 7 inches
