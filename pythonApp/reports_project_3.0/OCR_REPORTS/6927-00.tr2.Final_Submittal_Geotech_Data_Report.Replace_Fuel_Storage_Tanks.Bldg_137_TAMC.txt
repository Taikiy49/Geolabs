This geotechnical data report presents the results from our geotechnical 
engineering exploration performed for the Building 137 Replace Fuel Storage Tanks 
project at the Tripler Army Medical Center (TAMC) on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The 
project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1.

vicinity derived from our field exploration and laboratory testing only. The subsurface 
information presented herein is subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project site is at the Tripler Army Medical Center (TAMC) in Honolulu on the 
Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project site is situated along the south side of the Building 
137 Access Road and is shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The proposed project will provide for replacement of two existing underground 
storage tanks (UST) with new aboveground storage tanks (AST) or supplementing the 
existing UST capacity with new ASTs. Based on the information provided, a new tank 
pad is planned alongside the existing driveway leading from Krukowski Road to 
Building 137. In order to provide level area for the new ASTs, it will be necessary to 
grade the area by cutting the existing slope and/or construct a retaining wall.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu is comprised largely of the weathered remnants of the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk of 
the western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of 
the eastern two-thirds of the island. It is believed that Waianae became extinct while 
Koolau was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lavas in 
central Oahu. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to 
fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. 
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that 
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposition of lagoonal/marine sediments in 
the quiet waters protected by fringing reefs. The lower sea stands caused streams to 
carve valleys in the sediments and reef deposits. Subaerial exposure of the sediments 
and calcareous materials caused consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal 
deposits and induration of the calcareous reef materials. 
Following the extrusion of lavas in the early Pleistocene Epoch, the island 
underwent a long cycle of erosion and weathering forming the prominent ridgelines and 
summits as we know today. During the erosion period, the inter-fluvial ridges and areas 
such as the Schofield Plateau, were subjected to deep chemical weathering, forming a 
mantle of residual soil and saprolite, which grades with increasing depth to saprolite and 
weathered basalt formation. 
The project site is situated on an inter-fluvial ridge on the southeastern flank of 
Koolau Mountain Range. The majority of the project area is underlain by fill, recent 
alluvium, and residual clayey soils, which grade with increasing depth to saprolite and 
basalt formation. 
SOIL SAMPLING AND FIELD TESTING 
A total of three borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 3, extended to 
depths of about 19 to 23 feet below the existing ground surface. The approximate 
boring locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
The borings were drilled using truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous 
flight augers and HQ coring equipment. Our geologist classified the materials 
encountered in the borings by visual and textural examination in the field and observed 
the drilling operations on a near-continuous basis. These classifications were further 
reviewed by visual observation and laboratory testing. Soils were classified in general

Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler, and ASTM D1586 by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. The blow counts 
needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are 
shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths.  
In general, when the blow counts exceed about 50 blows per 6 inches or when 
the sampler does not advance with repeated hammer blows, driving is discontinued and 
the blow counts are shown on the logs with the distance penetrated. The penetration 
resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts.  
LABORATORY TESTING 
Laboratory testing was performed on selected soil and/or rock core samples 
obtained during the field exploration as an aid in classifying the materials and evaluating 
their engineering properties. The tests performed are described below. 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate 7. 
One Sieve Analysis test (ASTM C117 & C136) was performed on the bulk soil 
sample to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the near-surface soils and to aid in 
soil classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on 
Plate 8. 
Two one-inch ring swell tests were performed on relatively undisturbed and 
remolded samples to evaluate the swelling potential of the on-site clayey soils. The test 
results are summarized on Plate 9.

Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate their shear strength characteristics. The direct shear test results are 
presented on Plates 10 and 11. 
Two Unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D7012) were performed on selected 
rock cores to evaluate their uniaxial compressive strength of the basalt formation 
encountered. Unconfined compression test results are presented on Plate 12. 
One Modified Proctor compaction test (ASTM D1557, Method C) was performed 
on a bulk soil sample to evaluate the relationship between the moisture content and the 
dry density of the near-surface soils. The test results are presented on Plate 13. 
One laboratory soaked California Bearing Ratio test (ASTM D1883) was 
performed on a bulk soil sample to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the 
soils. The test results are presented on Plate 14. 
LIMITATIONS 
The geotechnical field data submitted herein is based upon information obtained 
from field borings and a surface bulk sample. Variations of the subsurface conditions 
between and beyond the exploration points may occur, and the nature and extent of these 
variations may not become evident until additional exploration and/or construction is 
underway. 
The field boring and surface bulk sample locations indicated herein are 
approximate, having been estimated by our field measurements using a surveyor’s wheel 
and tape measure and using the topographic survey map provided by HDR, Inc. on 
January 27, 2014. The boring elevations indicated herein were estimated based on 
interpolation between the contour elevations from the aforementioned topographic survey 
map. The physical locations and elevations of the borings should be considered accurate 
only to the degree implied by the methods used.  
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict 
the approximate boundaries between soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote a 
gradual transition. Water level data presented in the text herein were based on 
measurements recorded in the borings at the times shown on the graphic 
representations of the borings. These data have been reviewed and interpretations 
made in the formulation of this report. However, it must be noted that fluctuation may 
occur due to variation in tide, rainfall, temperature, and other factors.
