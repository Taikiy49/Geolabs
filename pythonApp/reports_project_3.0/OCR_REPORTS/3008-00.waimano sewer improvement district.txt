INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed sewer line project located at Pearl City on the island of Oahu
Hawaii The general location of the project site is shown on the Project Location Map
Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analysis based on the data
obtained from the field exploration and laboratory testing These findings and
recommendations are intended for design input Therefore they may not be appropriate
to form the basis for preparation of construction cost estimates nor for construction
bidding purposes
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
The project site is located within the Waimano District of Pearl City on the island
of Oahu Hawaii The site is bounded generally by Kamehameha Highway H 1 Freeway
and the former Hale Mohalu Hospital site It is proposed to install approximately 900
linear feet of new sewer line to service the area as shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 The
inverts of the new sewer line are generally about eight 8 to fifteen feet 15 below the
existing ground surface It is our understanding that the new sewer lines will be
connected to the existing 36 inch sewer line that runs parallel to the H 1 Freeway at the
southern portion of the site
PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our exploration was to obtain an overview of the subsurface
conditions at the project site to develop an idealized soil rock data set for the formulation
of geotechnical engineering recommendations for the design of the proposed sewer lines

SITE DESCRIPTION
The project site is located in the Pearl City area on the island of Oahu Hawaii The
site is located south of Kamehameha Highway and to the east of Sunset Memorial Park
The Waiau power plant of Hawaiian Electric Company is located to the south of the
project site
The site generally slopes down towards the south with existing ground elevations
ranging from about 45 feet Mean Sea Level MSL to 10 feet MSL A watercress farm
was noted in the central portion of the site near Boring Nos 4 and 5 Elevations near the
watercress farm generally ranged from 8 to 10 feet MSL Another watercress farm
was noted to the southeast of Boring No 5 An existing fuel line crosses the sewer line
alignment near Boring No 4
The alignment of the proposed sewer line improvements borders both commercial
and residential developments Portions of the proposed alignment travels parallel to the
existing asphaltic concrete paved roadways while other portions travel along property
easements adjacent to existing homes
An existing stream which drains into the East Loch of Pearl Harbor borders the
western side of the project site From the exposed unlined stream bank below the
existing freeway basalt was noted near the bottom the stream Alluvial deposits were
noted above the basalt formation

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
General
Several different soil conditions ranging from poor to good were encountered near
the proposed invert elevations for the sewer lines Groundwater levels above the
proposed sewer invert elevations and soft to very soft clayey silt deposits were
encountered in the lowlying areas at the central portion of the site These conditions
high groundwater and soft soils are anticipated near the watercress farm at the southern
portion of the site Therefore proper dewatering trench shoring with interlocking steel
sheetpiling and bracing will be needed in these areas during construction of the
proposed sewer lines Adequate shoring and bracing will be needed to reduce lateral
movement of ground with resulting potential damage to existing facilities
A hard basalt formation was encountered near the proposed sewer line invert
elevation in Boring and 9 at the southern portion of the site Difficult excavation

may be anticipated in this area In addition installation of the sheet piles shoring will not
be able to penetrate the basalt formation In these areas other methods of trench
shoring such as the use of trench shields may be considered Determination of the
method and equipment used for the trench shoring should be made by the contractor
within practical limits and safety considerations
An underground concrete structure was encountered at a depth of about eight feet
8 below the existing ground surface in Boring No 2 Since the anticipated sewer line

Trench Support
It is our opinion that the trench walls in the soft to very soft clayey silt deposits and
shallow groundwater level conditions will require continuous sheet piling support for the
proper installation of the sewer line It is important that adequate interlocking steel sheet
piling be installed prior to the excavation and sheet piling should be maintained tight
against the trench walls with proper bracing to reduce potential lateral movements of the
adjacent ground into the trench excavation resulting in potential settlements of adjacent
structures This is particularly critical since some of the existing adjacent structures may
be supported on spread footings bearing on the near surface soils
The steel sheet piling should be properly driven with a suitable hammer to a depth
sufficient to provide stability to the trench bottom and to reduce the possibility of bottom
heaving or quick conditions which may be created by upward seepage pressure during
dewatering Based on our estimate the steel sheet piles should be driven to a minimum
penetration depth of seven feet 7 below the stabilization layer or the trench bottom
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3008 00 Page 9
along the proposed alignment of the gravity sewer line for this purpose A trench width
of less than eight feet 8 has been assumed greater trench widths will require deeper
sheet pile penetration A factor of safety of 1 5 was assumed for estimating the design
sheetpile penetration
The sheet pile penetration depth recommended is based on the assumption that
sump pumping inside the excavation is used with no groundwater drawdown outside the
excavation The use of a deep well system outside the excavations to draw down the
natural groundwater level to reduce the required sheeting embedment is not
recommended
The sheet piling and bracing system used must comply with all applicable safety
requirements The adequacy and safety of the sheet piling and bracing installation
operations should be the responsibility of the contractor His representative who should
be required to be continually present on site would therefore have the opportunity to
observe changing conditions which might affect trench stability during construction
For estimating earth loads in the struts of the trench bracing the apparent pressure
diagrams presented on Plates 4 and 5 may be used as a guide An appropriate safety
factor should be included by the shoring designer
Appropriate loads due to surcharge traffic or sloping ground conditions adjacent
to the trench excavations should be added to the recommended earth pressures for
design purposes
Even if good construction procedures are followed some movement of the trench
support bracing system may be anticipated because of changes in earth stresses during
excavation Movements depend greatly on workmanship such as how quickly and
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3008 00 Page 10
tightly the support is installed It also depends on the types of subsoils the depth and
width of excavation and the type of support In view of the complexity of the problem
it is difficult to accurately estimate the amount of movement to be anticipated of a bracing
system and the adjacent ground Therefore it is important that the trench support be
installed as early as practical and that measurements of vertical and horizontal
movements be taken
The ground adjacent to the trench excavations should be continuously monitored
for cracks or dips and other indications of movements and possible sloughing of the
trench walls Such monitoring is particularly critical in areas adjacent to existing structures
and utilities
Dewatering
As indicated dewatering will be necessary for the gravity sewer line since the
groundwater level encountered in our field exploration were above the proposed sewer
pipe inverts The dewatering operations should be coordinated with the trench shoring
support such that the stability of the excavations is not compromised
The method and equipment to be used for the excavation dewatering should be
determined by the contractor subject to practical limits and safety considerations It is
our opinion that the definition of Dewatering in the contract documents be written to
include all works or systems required to lower the natural groundwater table and to
remove the water from the excavations to permit construction of the proposed waterline
structures under safe and dry conditions These works or systems should include but
should not be limited to pumping We suggest that the following three 3 basic criteria
be considered in selecting a suitable method of dewatering
1 The method should result in the least disturbance or damage to the
adjacent existing structures
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3008 00 Page 11
2 The method should maintain stability of and also provide safe and dry
working conditions in the excavations
3 The method should be sufficiently flexible to allow modifications to
accommodate various ground conditions
Based on these criteria it appears that sump pumping inside the excavations may
be used if the steel sheet pile shoring is driven sufficiently deep to reduce potential piping
and or heaving of the excavation bottoms
Dewatering by means of a well point system along the outside of the excavations
is generally not recommended even though this method could reduce the required sheet
pile penetration The resultant areal depression of the natural groundwater table could
induce consolidation of subsoils resulting in potential ground settlements affecting the
adjacent roadways and structures
If however the use of a well point system is elected recharging by timely injecting
water back into subsoils may be considered to balance the groundwater table and reduce
areal drawdown in areas near the existing adjacent structures We believe however that
the risk of causing settlements to adjacent buildings will generally be higher by using the
well point method than using the sump pumping method
Construction Considerations
The potential impact of the dewatering system selected on depressing the natural
groundwater table must be carefully evaluated by the contractor prior to dewatering It
is also recommended that a qualified geotechnical engineer be retained by the contractor
to design and evaluate the dewatering system used
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3008 00 Page 12
The impact and safety of the dewatering operations should be made the sole
responsibility of the contractor His qualified representative who should be required to
be continuously present at the site will have the opportunity to promptly observe the
effects of dewatering during construction and to implement as soon as possible
necessary precautionary or remedial measures including but not limited to stopping or
slowing down the dewatering operations
Where encountered at the excavation bottoms permeable granular subsoils may
be susceptible to piping and quick conditions Therefore the project dewatering
operations should be done without pumping out soil fines bumping clear water only and
should be coordinated with shoring installation such that the excavation stability is not
compromised The dewatering operations should be carried out without creating a
quick condition or softening at the excavation bottoms
Extra caution should also be taken to avoid dewatering utility trenches that connect
to the excavations If this occurs the granular bedding and or backfill in the utility
trenches could act as subdrains and cause areal settlements and damage to the utility
lines and or other existing adjacent structures
Pipe Bedding
The stress distribution against the bottom of a pipe has a significant effect on the
load supporting capacity of the pipe Therefore the pipe bedding is an important design
consideration
For pipes bearing on the stiff to hard alluvial and residual deposits or basalt
formation a minimum of six inches 6 of compacted granular bedding material should
be placed below the pipe The granular bedding should extend beyond the pipe sides
a minimum width of one fourth the outside pipe diameter and it also should extend half
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3008 00 Page 13
way up the sides of the pipe The bedding material should consist of clean granular
material such as 3B Fine gravel A typical cross section is shown on Plate 3
For pipes bearing on the soft to very soft alluvial deposits an additional 18 inch
layer of clean granular material wrapped in filter fabric should be provided below the pipe
and bedding layer to provide more uniform support of the pipe The recommended
bedding detail is shown on Plate 3
Backfill
In general the trench backfill should be placed in accordance with the Standard
Specifications for Public Works Construction September 1986
The first zone of backfill extends from the bedding material to at least 12 inches
above the top of the sewer line pipes Select free draining well graded granular material
is recommended for the first zone of backfill to reduce the compaction effort required and
resulting stresses to the pipe The granular material should be well graded less than
one inch 1 in size and should have less than ten percent 10 passing No 200 sieve
Compaction by water ponding or jetting may be allowed provided the granular material
is self draining such as 3B Fine gravel
The trench backfill from 12 inches above the top of the pipes to within two feet 2
of the finished ground elevation may consist of the excavated on site soils with the
exception of the soft alluvial deposits The backfill should be placed in thin lifts normally
8 inches in loose lift thickness with each lift mechanically compacted to a minimum 90
percent of its maximum dry density as determined in accordance with ASTM Test Method
D 1557 78
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3008 00 Page 14
The trench backfill up to a level within two feet 2 of the design pavement
subgrade elevation in paved areas should consist of select borrow compacted to not
less than 95 percent of its maximum dry density as determined in accordance with ASTM
Test Method D 1557 78 A typical section of the pipe trench showing the compaction
requirements is presented on Plate 3
Precautions should be taken against the occurrence of pipeline floatation before
and during trench backfilling due to possible influx of large quantities of groundwater into
the trenches The uplift buoyant forces due to groundwater influx may lift empty or even
partially filled pipes
Earth Pressure Loads on Pipe
Loads on buried conduits are influenced by the width of the trench the size of the
pipes the unit weight of backfill material and the frictional resistance between the backfill
material and the trench walls To calculate the vertical loads on the buried sewer pipes
an average unit weight of 120 pounds per cubic foot p c f for backfill material and a
coefficient of friction of 0 25 between the backfill and trench sidewall are recommended
Earth pressure forces upon the pipe increase rapidly with increased width of the trench
Therefore the width of the sewerline trench should be kept to a minimum
Traffic loads on the buried pipes should also be considered for the gravity sewer
line since the proposed sewer line alignment is located within travelled ways
Settlements
Primary settlement of the new sewer line construction is normally caused by the
difference in the unit weight of the lighter excavated original earth and the heavier
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3008 00 Page 15
compacted backfill material placed over the sewer pipes The net increase in loading will
cause settlement of the underlying subsoils below the trench invert Based on our
calculations primary settlement on the order of 2 inch is anticipated for the project
The above estimate assumes that proper construction procedures and good
workmanship will be engaged during construction Additional settlement could occur if
improper trench support or dewatering procedures are used Therefore we believe that
the proposed sewer lines should be designed with sufficient flexibility to accommodate
possible settlements
Joints and Connections
Due to the various subsoil conditions along the proposed sewer line alignment we
recommend that flexible pipe joints be used to connect the sewer pipes to accommodate
some amount of differential settlement
Manhole Structures
Structures supported on the stiff to hard alluvial and residual deposits may be
designed using an allowable bearing value of up to 2000 pounds per square foot p s f
Settlements on the order of inch are anticipated for these structures
If it is necessary to place the manhole structures on the very soft alluvial deposits
an 18 inch layer of gravel such as 3B Fine gravel or ASTM C 33 No 67 wrapped in a
filter fabric Mirafi 140N or equivalent should be provided below the structure to provide
uniform bearing support An allowable bearing pressure of 1000 pounds per square foot
p s f may be used on the 18 inch gravel layer The increase in load on the subsoils due
to the manhole structure should be small Therefore large settlements are not anticipated
if proper shoring and dewatering techniques are utilized in construction

W O 3008 00 Page 16
The lateral earth pressures acting on the proposed underground manhole
structures will depend on the type of backfill used the extent of backfill and the
compactive effort on the backfill material around the structures Therefore granular
material with less than ten percent 10 passing the No 200 sieve is recommended for
use as backfill around the structures to reduce the compactive effort required which may
cause additional lateral earth pressures We recommend that the new manholes be
designed to resist the following lateral earth pressures provided granular backfill is used
around the structures
1 Above water level 60 p c f equivalent fluid pressure
2 Below water level 90 p c f equivalent fluid pressure
The above values assume that the backfill around the manhole structures is
properly placed and compacted Surcharge loading due to traffic and other applicable
sources should be added to the above values for design
Existing Utility Lines
Since the general project site is in a developed urban area it is anticipated that
many existing utility lines may be encountered during construction of the sewer lines
Therefore we recommend that all existing utility lines along the proposed sewer line
alignment be located accurately prior to construction and that an adequate program be
devised to reduce potential damage to the existing utility lines during construction

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling nine 9 borings to
depths of approximately 8 to 41 feet below the existing ground surface at the approximate
locations shown on the Site Plan Plate 2 A truck mounted rig was used to drill the
borings
The materials encountered in the borings were examined and classified in general
accordance with ASTM Standard Practice D 2488 84 Description and Identification of
Soils Visual Manual Procedure by a geologist from our firm The logs of the borings
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in accordance with ASTM
Standard Method D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by
barrel sampler with a 140 pound hammer free falling 30 inches The blow counts needed
to advance the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Boring
Logs at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of soil and rock materials encountered at the site were obtained
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard
Practice D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary
drilling method which uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material
left in the annulus of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination classification and
testing
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt such as if 3 7 feet of material
is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Boring Logs as REC 74
The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses This designation is only applicable to cores retrieved from in situ
rock formations RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound material
in excess of four inches 4 in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Boring Logs as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality based on the
Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Laboratory testing was performed on selected samples obtained during our field
exploration A laboratory testing program was developed to schedule appropriate tests
to aid in evaluating pertinent engineering properties of the soil materials sampled
Classification Tests
Frequently the classification of a material provides a correlative index to certain
engineering properties of the material
Moisture Content ASTM Test Method D 2216 80 and Unit Weight Determinations
are used as an aid to soil classification and to aid in evaluating certain properties such
as relative saturation The moisture content expressed as a percentage of the dry weight
of the tested sample and dry unit weights are shown at the appropriate sample depths
on the Boring Logs
Atterberg Limits test ASTM Test Method D 4318 84 are used to assess the
plasticity of the fine grained portion of soils The Plasticity Index of the material is used
to classify the soil under the Unified Soil Classification System The results of these tests
are shown at the appropriate sample depths on the Boring Logs and are summarized on
Plate B 1
Physical Property Tests
Tests are performed to develop a correlative index of certain physical parameters
of asoil These physical parameters may be used in engineering analyses pertaining to
the performance or suitability of a soil
Torvane Shear tests are used to evaluate the cohesive strength of a soil sample
The results of the torvane shear tests are presented on Plate B 1
Direct Shear tests ASTM Test Method D 3080 72 are used to determine the angle
of internal friction and cohesion in a soil sample These data are used in various
engineering calculations The results of the direct shear test are presented on Plate B 2
Consolidation tests ASTM Test Method D 2435 80 are used to evaluate the
consolidation characteristics of the soil The results of the test are summarized on Plate
B 3
