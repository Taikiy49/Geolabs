SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered a surface fill layer about 3 to 5 feet 
thick underlain by very soft to soft and very loose marine deposits (swamp, estuarine and 
river). Groundwater was encountered in the borings at depths ranging from about 2.5 to 
6 feet below the existing ground surface.  These depths correspond to elevations ranging 
from about +3.2 to -1.1 feet MSL.   
Based on the borings, the project site may be classified as being a “Soft Clay Soil 
Profile” or Site Class “E” soil profile.  Based on the subsurface conditions encountered, 
the granular portions of the marine deposits generally are susceptible of soil liquefaction.  
The estimated seismically induced ground settlement due to soil liquefaction is 3 to 4 
inches at the site.  It should be noted that the liquefaction may only occur in pockets at 
the project site. 
Due to the underlying thick compressible deposit, the thickness of new fills should 
be kept at a minimum to reduce the amount of settlement at the project site.  After clearing 
and grubbing, areas designated to receive fills should be scarified to a minimum depth of 
about 8 inches and moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, and 
recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction. 
In general, the excavated on-site fill materials may be reused as a source of 
general fill material provided the material does not contain organic matter and have a 
maximum particle size of 3 inches.  Imported fill material should be well-graded from 
coarse to fine with no particles larger than 3 inches in largest dimension and should 
contain between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No. 200 sieve.  The imported fill 
material should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum 
swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.

design recommendations.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lava from the Waianae 
and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to 
late Pliocene in age (2.7 – 3.4 million years ago) and forms the bulk of the western 
one-third of the island. The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene (Ice Age) in age (2.2 – 2.5 million years ago) and forms the majority of 
the eastern two-thirds of the island. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), sea levels fluctuated in response to the 
cycles of continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was 
available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the 
sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such 
that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition were affected by these glacio-eustatic 
sea level fluctuations. When the sea level was low, the erosional base level was 
correspondingly lower, and valleys were carved to depths below the present sea level. When 
the sea level was high, the erosional base level was raised such that sediments 
accumulated at higher elevations.

In the mountainous regions of Hawaii, the erosional processes are dominated by 
detachment of soil and rock masses from the valley walls and are then transported 
downslope toward the axis of a valley primarily by gravity as colluvium. Once these 
materials reach the stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become 
dominant, and the sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. 
In general, stream flows in Hawaii are intermittent and flashy, such that the stream 
flows transmit large volumes of water for very short durations. Because of this, transport 
of sediments is intermittent, and the bulk of the stream's hydraulic load consists of a poorly 
sorted mixture of boulders, cobbles, gravel, sands, and fines.  When the erosional base 
levels change, these sediment loads are left as deposits. 
Depending on the local base level and rate of transport, the newer sediments are 
generally transient in terms of geologic time. In addition, their consistency and density are 
generally less than those of the older, partially consolidated deposits. 
The project site is located on the Mokapu Peninsula on the island of Oahu. The 
foundation of this peninsula was deposited during the Kahipa low stand of the sea (-300± 
feet) by several vents of the Honolulu Volcanic Series. These vents laid down extensive 
deposits of pyroclastic materials in the form of ash and cinder with some associated lava 
flows. 
Since the deposition of these materials during Kahipa time, the sea level has risen 
and fallen several times as a result of glacio-eustatic fluctuations. These changes in sea 
level have caused the deposition of alluvium, boulders and soft/loose marine deposits 
over portions of the peninsula. During the past century, the efforts of man have shaped 
the land to its present form by placement of fill in low areas. 
After a long period of volcanic inactivity, during which time erosion incised deep 
valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic activity returned with a series of lava flows 
followed by cinder and tuff cone formations.  These series are referred to as the Honolulu 
Volcanic Series.  The Honolulu Volcanic Series, which began less than a million years

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition), the project site may be 
subject to seismic activity, and seismic design considerations will need to be addressed.

design, and the potential for liquefaction at the project site. 
2.3.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused largely by shifts 
in the tectonic plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily to 
volcanic activity. Therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or 
during volcanic eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground 
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island 
of Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most of the 
earthquakes are so small that they can only be detected by sensitive instruments. 
However, some of the earthquakes are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause 
minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes (associated with volcanic activity) are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent 
of earthquakes) are related to volcanic activity, the risk of high seismic activity and 
degree of ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the earthquake 
epicenter. The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater than 
Magnitude 5 (M5+); however, earthquakes are not confined only to the Island of 
Hawaii. 
It should be noted that some earthquakes in Hawaii are caused by the burden of the 
island masses overlying the lithosphere, which cause the lithosphere to sag beneath 
the immense weight. The isostatic adjustment of the Pacific lithosphere beneath the 
islands’ overburden causes some moderate to strong intensity earthquakes. The 
1973 Honomu Earthquake, Magnitude 6.2 (M6.2), beneath the Hamakua Coast of 
the Island of Hawaii and the October 15, 2006 Kiholo Bay earthquake, Magnitude 6.7

2.3.3 Liquefaction Potential 
Based on the International Building Code (2006 Edition), the project site should be 
evaluated for the potential for soil liquefaction. Soil liquefaction is a condition where 
saturated cohesionless soils near the ground surface undergo a substantial loss of 
strength due to the build-up of excess pore water pressures resulting from cyclic 
stress applications induced by earthquakes. When the loose saturated sand deposit 
is subjected to vibration (such as during an earthquake), the soil tends to densify and 
decrease in volume causing an increase in pore water pressure. If drainage is unable 
to occur rapidly enough to dissipate the build-up of pore water pressure, the effective

stress (internal strength) of the soil is reduced. Under sustained vibrations, the pore 
water pressure build-up could equal the overburden pressure, essentially reducing 
the soil shear strength to zero and causing it to behave as a viscous fluid. During 
liquefaction, the soil acquires a mobility sufficient to permit both horizontal and 
vertical movements, and if not confined, will result in significant deformations. 
Soils most susceptible to liquefaction are loose, uniformly graded, fine-grained sands 
and loose silts with little cohesion. The major factors affecting the liquefaction

Because the project site will be subjected to seismic activity, we have evaluated the

of soil liquefaction caused by a peak ground acceleration of 0.271g, the estimated 
seismically induced ground settlement due to soil liquefaction is 3 to 4 inches at the 
project site.

After clearing and grubbing, areas designated to receive fills should be scarified to a 
minimum depth of about 8 inches and moisture-conditioned to above the optimum 
moisture content, and recompacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction.  
Relative compaction, in this report, refers to the in-place dry density of soil 
expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil 
established in accordance with ASTM D1557 test procedures. Optimum moisture 
is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the maximum dry 
density. 
Soft and/or loose, weak, or yielding areas (or cavities) disclosed during site 
preparation operations should be over-excavated to expose firm ground, and the 
resulting excavation should be backfilled with select fill materials (well-graded 3-inch 
minus materials) compacted to a minimum 90 percent relative compaction. 
Saturation and subsequent yielding of the exposed subgrades due to inclement 
weather and poor drainage may require over-excavation of the soft areas and 
replacement with compacted fill material. 
3.1.2 Excavation 
Based on the anticipated grading and our field exploration, excavation for this 
project generally will consist of excavations for foundations and other 
infrastructure. Some of the grading and infrastructure work may encounter 
cobbles. It is anticipated that the majority of the material may be excavated with 
normal heavy excavation equipment. 
3.1.3 Fills and Backfills 
In general, the excavated on-site fill materials may be reused as a source of general 
fill materials above the groundwater level (Elevation +2 feet MSL), provided they 
meet the requirements stated below. Because of the relatively shallow groundwater 
and the proximity to the shoreline, drying or aeration of the excavated materials may 
be required prior to their use as compacted fills. Therefore, a drying area should be

designated for the stockpiling of the usable excavated granular materials. Some 
aeration of the stockpile material likely will be necessary in order to reuse the 
excavated materials as compacted fills. 
It should be noted that excavated organic clayey silt soils (or other cohesive soils) 
should not be used as a source of select fill material due to the poor pavement and 
foundation support characteristics of the soils. Organic soils should be disposed of 
off-site. However, other cohesive soils may be reused as a source of general fill 
for the project construction. 
If imported fill materials are needed for the project construction, imported fill 
materials should be well-graded from coarse to fine with no particles larger than 
3 inches in largest dimension and should contain between 10 and 30 percent 
particles passing the No. 200 sieve. The material should have a laboratory 
California Bearing Ratio (CBR) value of 20 or more and should have a maximum 
swell of 1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM D1883.  
In general, drain rock fill consisting of relatively clean, free-draining granular fill 
material (2-inch maximum size granular materials) may be used for backfill placed 
below about Elevation +2 feet MSL due to the presence of groundwater unless efforts 
are made to dewater the excavations to enable compaction of standard backfill 
materials. 
Imported fill materials, if needed, should be tested for conformance with these 
recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended use. An 
accredited testing laboratory should test the imported fill materials for conformance 
with these recommendations prior to delivery to the project site for the intended 
use. 
3.1.4 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
As indicated above, fills and/or backfills below the water level should consist of 
free-draining granular materials, such as the surge rock or drain rock fill, as 
recommended above. Mechanical compaction of these materials will not be required 
during placement. However, densification by pounding the material with the bucket

of a backhoe, track-walking or by other acceptable means should be done in a 
consistent manner during placement. The last lift of the material above the water level 
should be proof-rolled a minimum of eight passes with the use of a vibratory plate or 
similar equipment prior to the placement of the non-woven geotextile fabric, such as 
Mirafi 180N or equivalent, and capping with compacted fill to the finished subgrade. 
General fill and select granular fill materials above the water table should be placed

the optimum moisture content, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction. The compaction requirements for finished subgrades subjected to 
vehicular traffic, such as the pavement subgrade, should be increased to a minimum 
of 95 percent relative compaction. Aggregate base or subbase course materials 
should be moisture-conditioned to above the optimum moisture content, placed in 
level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction. 
3.2 
Utility Trenches 
New underground utilities, such as sewer and water, are planned for the project. 
In general, we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
free-draining materials, such as bedding sand or open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 
gradation), below the pipes for uniform support.  
If loose and/or soft soils are encountered at the proposed inverts, a stabilization 
layer consisting of 12 to 18 inches of open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) 
wrapped in a non-woven geotextile fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) may be necessary 
below the bedding material. Free-draining granular materials, such as bedding sand or 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation), also should be used for the initial 
trench backfill (pipe cover) up to about 12 inches above the crown of the pipes or the top 
of the groundwater level to provide adequate support around the pipes and to reduce the 
need for substantial effort in compacting the backfill, thus reducing the potential for 
damage to the pipes. 
The trench backfill above the pipe cover or groundwater to the finished subgrade 
or finished grade may consist of the excavated on-site soils (excluding the organic clays).

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
five exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 5, extending down to depths 
of about 26.5 to 43.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled with a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with augers. The borings were drilled at the approximate 
locations shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 and 2.2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard 
Practice for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually 
and by testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM 
D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified 
Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations 
made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil 
Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered 
are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-5. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD 
standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with ASTM 
D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD modified California 
sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the 
second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on 
the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration resistance shown 
on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the specific sampler 
type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard Penetration 
Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Please note that the efficiencies of the safety hammers used for this project were 
measured in May 2015 and the resulting energy transfer ratios are shown on the Logs of 
Borings. The soil consistencies and densities presented on the boring logs have been 
adjusted based on the energy transfer ratio, as appropriate.  
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the 
field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive 
strength of the sample. The pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on the Logs 
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of 
soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Ten Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and 
graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification. The fines content of the samples tested (percent particles passing the 
No. 200 sieve) are indicated on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B-2. 
Three Consolidation tests (ASTM D2435) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the compressibility characteristics of the encountered material. The 
consolidation test results are presented on Plates B-3 through B-5. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate shear strength characteristics. The direct shear test results are presented on 
Plates B-6 and B-7. 
Three Modified Proctor compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on the 
bulk soil samples to evaluate the relationship between the moisture content and the dry 
density of the near-surface soils. The test results are presented on Plates B-8 through 
B-10. 
Three  laboratory-soaked California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were 
performed on bulk soil samples to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils. 
The test results are presented on Plates B-11 through B-13.
