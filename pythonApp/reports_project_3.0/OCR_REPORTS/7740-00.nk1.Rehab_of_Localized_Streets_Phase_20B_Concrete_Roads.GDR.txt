SECTION 1.  
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The project involves rehabilitating the following Portland Cement Concrete (PCC) streets in 
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii: 
Coyne Street from Artesian Street to 2121 Coyne Street 
Dole Street from Metcalf Street to 2145 Dole Street 
Farrington Street from Wilder Ave to H-1 Freeway/1413 Farrington Street 
Farrington Street from Bingham Street to South Beretania Street 
The project and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 1. Based on 
conversations with a local resident, the initial homes in the area were built in the 1920’s. It 
appears that the concrete pavement may have been installed at that time. The concrete pavement 
panels along the streets vary from about 30 to 60 feet in length. No drainage structures were 
observed along the concrete paved roadways except at the end of Farrington Street near the 
northern edge of the Interstate Route H-1 Freeway. 
Numerous cracks were observed on the surface of the existing PCC pavement. In addition, 
multiple depressions and areas of frequent asphalt patching were also observed along the 
roadways.

SECTION 3.  
SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
3.1 
Regional Geology 
Extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from the Waianae and Koolau Shield Volcanoes 
built the Island of Oahu. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late Pliocene 
Epoch in age and forms the bulk of the western one-third of the Island of Oahu. The younger 
Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (latest Ice Age) in age and 
forms the bulk of the eastern two-thirds of the Island of Oahu.   
Following the coalescence of the two volcanoes and after a long period of volcanic 
inactivity, during which time erosion incised deep valleys into the Koolau Shield, volcanic 
activity returned with a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formations. These 
late series of eruptions are referred to as the Honolulu Volcanic Series, which are confined to 
scattered areas of the Koolau Volcano footprint. The Honolulu Volcanic Series, which began 
approximately 700,000 years ago (MacDonald and Abbott, 1970), produced several prominent 
cinder and tuff cone structures with some basaltic lava flows that became inter-layered with 
the developing Southern Oahu coastal plain deposits. 
The project site is on the lower southern flank of the Koolau Volcano. As a result, the 
geomorphology and subsurface conditions of the area are directly related to glacio-eustatic 
fluctuations of the sea level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu 
Coastal Plain. The subsurface conditions in the Moiliili area include various marshland and 
alluvial soil deposits overlying fossil Pleistocene Age coral/algal limestone reef and related 
marine/lagoonal deposits laid during higher stands of the sea. These deeper marine deposits 
are intercalated with other terrigenous derived sediments. The base of the stratigraphic section 
in this area is Koolau Basalt. 
Most of the Honolulu Coastal Plain developed during the Pleistocene Epoch when the 
sea level experienced significant fluctuations related to the continental glacial stages. As the 
glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill the oceanic basins such that sea 
levels fell below the present stands of the sea. When the glaciers melted and receded, an 
excess of water became available such that the sea levels rose to above the present sea level.

The processes of landform erosion, sediment deposition, and reef development were affected 
by these glacio-eustatic sea level fluctuations. 
When the sea level was relatively lower, the erosional base level was correspondingly 
lower and stream valleys were carved deeper into the island basaltic rock, the fringing coastal 
sediments, and the offshore reef deposits. Also, during periods of relatively lower sea level, the 
sub-aerial exposure of calcareous marine sediments caused consolidation and cementation of 
the deposits to form hardened calcareous deposits. In contrast, when the sea level was 
relatively higher, the erosional base level was raised such that marine sediments and coral reef 
formations filled the pre-existing depressions and accumulated as reef platforms at 
correspondingly higher elevations. The higher sea levels also caused the formation of deltas and 
alluvial fans composed of terrigenous sediment in the head region of old bays. 
Finally, the placement of near-surface man-made fills associated with the development 
of the urban area within the last 80 years has brought the Honolulu Coastal Plain to its present 
form. In the early part of this century, much of the Moiliili area consisted of low elevation 
marsh wetlands. As the City of Honolulu grew and the Waikiki/Moiliili areas were urbanized, 
man-made fills were placed to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons for development. It should 
be noted that much of the resulting fill materials placed are of poor quality in terms of 
supporting heavy structural loads. 
3.2 
Subsurface Conditions 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
15 borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 15, extending to depths of about 5 to 6.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface. Prior to soil sampling, each boring location was cored using a 
6-inch concrete core barrel to retrieve intact pavement cores. In addition, five bulk samples of 
the near-surface soils, designated as Bulk-1 through Bulk-5, were collected near select boring 
locations to evaluate the pavement support and swelling characteristics of the near-surface 
soils. The approximate boring and bulk sample locations are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 1.  
Our field exploration indicates that the existing pavement structure generally consists of 
about 5.3 to 6.8 inches of PCC pavement. The base material was not encountered below the

SECTION 4.  
GEOTECHNICAL EXPLORATION PROGRAM 
The geotechnical exploration program conducted for the Rehabilitation of Localized 
Streets, Phase 20B Concrete Roads project generally consisted of the following field and 
laboratory work components. A brief description of each work component is presented below. 
Detailed discussions, findings, and test results of the various components are provided in the 
respective appendices herein. 
4.1 
Soil Borings, Pavement Coring, and Associated Field Testing 
The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight 
augers and concrete coring equipment. Our geologist classified the materials encountered in 
the borings by visual and textural examination in the field and observed the drilling operations 
on a near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed by visual observation 
and laboratory testing. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard 
Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), 
as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in 
accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. 
Graphic representations of the materials encountered in the drilled borings are presented on 
the Logs of Borings in Appendix A. 
In-situ testing of the subsurface materials in the borings included the Standard 
Penetration Test (SPT), which was performed in accordance with ASTM D1586. Standard 
Penetration Test (SPT) results are reported as the uncorrected N-value, which is defined as the 
number of blows required to drive a standard split-spoon sampler 12 inches with a 140-lb 
hammer, after 6 inches of initial seating. Appropriate hammer energy calibrations and 
corrections should be used to obtain N60 values. The penetration resistance of the modified 
California sampler (ASTM D3550) was recorded in a similar manner, and the modified California 
sampler blows per 12 inches can be approximately related to the SPT N-value through 
correlation.

4.2 
Dynamic Cone Penetration Testing 
In addition to borehole drilling and sampling, our field exploration program also 
included the performance of one DCP test at each boring location for a total of 15 DCP tests. 
The DCP tests, designated as DCP Nos. P-1 through P-15, extended to depths of about 2.4 to 5.5 
feet below the pavement subgrade surface. 
The DCP test provides a measure of a material’s in-situ resistance to penetration. The 
test is performed by driving a metal cone into the ground by repeatedly striking it with a 17.6-lb 
(8-kg) weight dropped from a distance of 22.6 inches (575 mm). The penetration of the cone is 
measured after each blow and is recorded to provide a continuous measure of the shear 
resistance of the underlying soil. Test results can be correlated to California Bearing Ratio (CBR), 
in-situ density, resilient modulus, and bearing capacity. Results of the dynamic cone 
penetration tests are presented in Appendix C. 
4.3 
Laboratory Testing 
Laboratory testing was performed on soil samples to determine the physical and 
engineering properties of the materials. Laboratory tests performed included moisture content, 
unit weight, Atterberg Limits, grain size distribution, ring swell, and CBR tests. The laboratory 
test results are provided in Appendix B.

LIMITATIONS 
The  geotechnical  field  and  laboratory  test  data  submitted  in  this  geotechnical  data 
report  are based  upon  information  obtained  from  the  field  exploration  points.  Variations  of 
subsurface conditions between and beyond the exploration points may occur, and the nature 
and  extent  of  these  variations  may  not  become  evident  until  additional  field  investigation 
and/or construction is underway. 
The  test  boring  and  bulk  sample  locations  indicated  herein  are  approximate,  having 
been estimated by taping from visible features. The field boring locations were estimated using 
Google™  Earth  Pro.  The  field  boring  locations  and  elevations  should  be  considered  accurate 
only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The  stratification  lines  shown  on  the  graphic  representations  of  the  field  exploration 
points  depict  the  approximate  boundaries  between  soil  and  rock  types  and,  as  such,  may 
denote a gradual transition. We did not encounter groundwater in the borings at the time of 
our field exploration. However, it must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in 
seasonal rainfall and other factors. The data has been reviewed and interpretations made in the

Test Boring Exploration 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the project site for the Rehabilitation of 
Localized Streets, Phase 20B Concrete Roads project by drilling and sampling 15 borings, 
designated as Boring Nos. 1 through 15, extending to depths ranging from about 5 to 6.5 feet 
below the existing ground surface using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous-flight augers and pavement coring tools. The drilling and sampling work was 
conducted from October 24, 2018 through October 31, 2018. The boring locations are shown 
on the Site Plan, Plate 2. The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers and pavement coring tools. 
Our geologists/engineers classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for 
Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a near-continuous 
basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in the laboratory. Soils 
were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of 
Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are 
described on the Soil Classification Log Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-15. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general 
accordance with ASTM D3550, Standard Practice for Thick Wall, Ring-Lined, Spilt Barrel, Drive 
Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer 
falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general 
accordance with ASTM D1586, Standard Test Method for Penetration Test and Split-Barrel 
Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer 
and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 
18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths.  
Please note that the efficiencies of the safety hammers used for this project were 
measured in May 2015 and the resulting energy transfer ratios are shown on the Logs of Borings. 
The soil consistencies and densities presented on the boring logs have been adjusted based on the 
energy transfer ratio, as appropriate. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples retrieved in 
the field. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths.

Laboratory Tests 
Samples recovered from our field exploration program performed for the Rehabilitation 
of Localized Streets, Phase 20B Concrete Roads project were visually examined and classified in 
our laboratory in Honolulu, Hawaii. Selected samples were tested to evaluate the physical 
characteristics and engineering properties of the soils encountered. The laboratory testing 
program was formulated with the emphasis on evaluating the moisture, density, plasticity, 
gradation, expansion potential, and pavement supporting characteristics of the soils 
encountered. 
The laboratory tests performed included determination of dry density and natural 
moisture content, sieve analysis, Atterberg Limits, ring swell, and CBR tests. Results of the tests 
performed are presented on Plates B-1 through B-9. Detailed descriptions of the various tests 
performed for this exploration are presented below. 
Moisture and Density 
Moisture content and dry density tests were performed on selected soil samples to aid 
in defining the interface between the various soil layers encountered and correlating the layers 
between test borings. The moisture content tests were performed in general accordance with 
ASTM D2216. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. 
Atterberg Limits 
Twelve (12) Atterberg Limits tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate 
the liquid limit (LL), plastic limit (PL), and plasticity index (PI) as an aid in classifying the soils. 
The Atterberg Limits tests were performed in general accordance with ASTM D4318. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic 
presentations of the test results are provided on Plates B-1 and B-2. 
Sieve Analysis 
To provide information on the particle size distribution of the soils encountered, four (4) 
Sieve Analysis tests were performed to evaluate the gradation characteristics and to aid in soil 
classification. The tests were performed in accordance with ASTM C117 and C136. Graphic 
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B-3.

Ring Swell 
Fourteen One-Inch Ring Swell tests were performed on selected soil samples to evaluate 
the swelling potential of the on-site soils under surcharge pressure. These tests were

California Bearing Ratio (CBR) 
Five California Bearing Ratio (CBR) tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on bulk samples 
of the near-surface soils to evaluate the strength characteristics for pavement subgrade 
support. The CBR test results are presented on Plates B-5 through B-9.
