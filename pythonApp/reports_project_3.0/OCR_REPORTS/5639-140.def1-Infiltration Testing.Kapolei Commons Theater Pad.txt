This report presents the results of our infiltration tests performed for the Kapolei 
Commons Theater Pad in Kapolei, on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The project location 
and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map (Plate 1) and Site Plan 
(Plate 2). 
This report summarizes the results of our infiltration testing and engineering 
analyses for the storm water detention/infiltration system.  The recommendations 
presented herein are intended for the design of the storm drain system and the possible 
use of a detention/infiltration system for the subsurface disposal of storm water runoff.  
The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at 
the end of this report. 
Project Considerations 
The site was previously graded with the placement of about 18 to 20 inches of 
coralline capping fill over the native clayey soils.  While the capping fill would be relatively 
permeable, it would have limited storage capacity and infiltration into the underlying clayey 
soils would govern the design and function of a detention/infiltration storm water 
management system.  Therefore, this testing was conducted to determine the infiltration 
rates of the underlying clayey soils. 
In order to provide information regarding the infiltration rates, we conducted 
double-ring infiltrometer testing in a total of 10 relatively shallow test pits excavated using a 
front end loader-backhoe (“hopto”) provided by Royal Contracting Co. Ltd. 
Purpose and Scope 
The purpose of our work was to obtain infiltration data of the in-situ soils to assist 
in the storm water disposal design.  In order to accomplish this, we performed an 
exploration and testing program consisting of the following tasks and work efforts:

Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to 
early Pleistocene in age.  As the volcanic activity in Waianae Volcano ceased, lava flows 
from Koolau Volcano banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau, 
now known as the Schofield Plateau.  The project site is located on the southwestern flank 
of the Waianae Volcano. 
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation. As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to 
fill the oceanic basins, such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea. 
When the glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available, such that 
the sea levels rose to above the present sea level. 
The higher sea level stands caused the formation of deltas and fans of 
accumulated terrigenous sediments in the heads of old bays, accumulated reef deposits 
at correspondingly higher elevations, and deposited lagoonal/marine sediments in the 
quiet waters protected by fringing reefs.  The lower sea stands caused streams to carve 
valleys in the sediments and reef deposits.  Subaerial exposure of the sediments and 
calcareous materials caused consolidation of the lagoonal deposits and induration of 
the calcareous reef materials.  Agricultural development within the last 100 years and 
recent mass grading work brought the Kapolei area to its present form.

Site Description 
The project site is located in the northwestern portion of the City of Kapolei. The 
Theater Pad is situated on the southwest corner of the Kapolei Commons commercial 
development area. 
At the time of our field testing, the project site was largely vacant with the northwest 
corner being used as a construction storage area.  The ground surface of the interior of the 
site was generally level and had been raised by the placement of about 18 to 20 inches of 
coralline capping fill.  Along Kapolei Parkway and the western access road, little or no fill 
had been placed and the grade was roughly the same as the road grades. A temporary 
detention basin was present in the southeastern part of the interior pad area. 
Infiltration Testing 
The water infiltration rates into the subsurface soils were tested by performing 
double-ring infiltrometer tests at the bottom of each test pit in accordance with 
ASTM Standard Test Method D3385.  The tests were performed by driving two open 
concentric cylinders into the ground, partially filling the rings with water and maintaining a 
near-constant water level. 
The double-ring infiltrometer test estimates the vertical movement of water through 
the bottom of the test pit in the inner cylinder, while the outer ring helps to reduce the 
lateral movement of water in the soil. The volume of water added to the inner ring is 
measured as the infiltrated water volume.  The tests were carried out in several increments 
until achieving a steady-state condition with a relatively constant water infiltration rate.  
Detailed testing data and results are presented in Appendix A.

Based on the conditions encountered at the site, it appears that the infiltration rates 
are restrictive for the use of subsurface infiltration systems for the management of storm 
water runoff.  The average rate for the tests was 0.67 in/hr and the lowest feasible rate is 
0.5 in/hr.  It should be noted that the highest rate of 1.6 in/hr was measured in 
Test No. P-10; and, that the soil at this location had a visibly higher sand content than 
other locations at the site.  Without this test, the average infiltration rate would be 0.5 in/hr, 
which is the lower limit of feasibility for subsurface disposal systems.  Based on this, we 
recommend that off-site disposal be considered for this location.

evident, Geolabs should be contacted to re-evaluate the recommendations presented 
herein.
