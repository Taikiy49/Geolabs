2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest in the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers an
area of approximately 4 000 square miles The island was formed by the activity of five
shield volcanoes which include the following Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 and Mauna Loa and
Kilauea both still active
The project site is situated on the western flank of Hualalai Mountain which
makes up the west central portion of the island The lava formation encountered at the
site appears to be of both a a and pahoehoe type flows which spread and ponded as
they approached the ocean A a lava is typically characterized by a porous rough and
irregular flow surface which resembles a jagged accumulation of rock fragments that
include cobbles and boulders Pahoehoe is characterized by a smooth rope like or
billowy surface and an internal structure of vesicular porous rock
Cavities are commonly encountered in basaltic lavas especially in
pahoehoe type lava Cavities are formed when the lava is still in a molten state and
represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities and blisters and pockets inter flow
cavities Lava tubes are formed when molten lava drains from the cooling flow leaving
a hollow tube like structure that may extend for a large longitudinal distance along the
flow Blisters and pockets inter flow cavities are generally smaller in horizontal extent
Basalt is considered to be a relatively permeable rock and can transmit water
quite readily in both the horizontal and vertical directions In general water is
transmitted through the porous rock matrix along joints fractures and _ inter flow
contacts cavities and clinker layers Because of the high permeability clinker and
cavities serve as the primary water transmission features

2 4 Earthquakes and Seismicity
In general earthquakes that occur throughout the world are caused solely by
shifts in the tectonic plates In contrast earthquake activity in Hawaii is linked primarily
to volcanic activity Therefore earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or
during volcanic eruptions In addition earthquakes may be a result of the underground
movement of magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt The Island of
Hawaii experiences thousands of earthquakes each year but most are so small that
only sensitive instruments can detect the small tremors However some of the
earthquakes are strong enough to be felt and a few cause minor to moderate damage
In general earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on
the Island of Hawaii Earthquakes directly associated with the movement of magma are
concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on the Island of
Hawaii Because the majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii over 90 percent of
earthquakes are related to volcanic activity the risk of seismic activity and degree of
ground shaking diminishes with increased distance from the southern portion of the
Island of Hawaii The Island of Hawaii has experienced numerous earthquakes greater
than Magnitude 6 M6 Based on information obtained from the United States

2 5 Site Specific Response Spectra Analysis
We performed a seismic hazard analysis and site response evaluation with
respect to seismic design considerations The purpose of our seismic hazard analysis
and site response evaluation is to establish appropriate design elastic response spectra
taking into consideration the proximity of the site to seismic sources regional seismicity
and site specific subsurface conditions The structural engineer uses response spectra
to perform a structural analysis given the dynamic loading conditions and the ground
motions at the foundation elevations
The response spectra are graphic representations that relate to the maximum
response of a single degree of freedom damped elastic system with different
fundamental periods to dynamic forces The anticipated earthquake ground motions and
dynamic response of the subsurface materials are represented by site specific elastic
response spectra
Design Ground Motion
Based on the 1997 Uniform Building Code UBC the Island of Hawaii is located
within Seismic Zone 4 with an associated peak ground acceleration of 0 40g
Alternatively site specific design ground motions may be developed based on a
site specific probabilistic seismic hazard analysis PSHA or determined based on
seismic hazard maps In any case the site specific design ground motions should
be based on a 10 percent probability of exceedance in 50 years This level of
seismic hazard is equivalent to an annual probability of occurrence of
0 002107 events per year or a return period of approximately 475 years
As previously mentioned the subsurface conditions generally consist of a surface
layer of volcanic ash materials overlying basalt formation and a a basalt Based on
the subsurface conditions encountered in our field exploration the project site may
be classified from a seismic analysis standpoint as being a Very Dense Soil and
Soft Rock site corresponding to an SC soil profile type based on the Uniform
Building Code Table No 16 J 1997 Edition

3 4 Slabs on Grade
We anticipate that concrete slabs on grade will be used for the floors of the new
control building and other minor structures For the interior building slabs not subjected
to vehicular traffic we recommend providing for uniform support a minimum 4 inch
thick layer of cushion fill below the slabs The cushion fill should consist of open graded
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation and also would serve as a capillary moisture
break Where slabs are subjected to vehicular traffic or sustained vibrations a minimum
of 6 inches of compacted aggregate base course should be provided below the slabs
in lieu of the 4 inch thick cushion fill layer The aggregate base course layer should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and compacted to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
To reduce the potential for excess moisture infiltration and subsequent damage
to floor coverings an impervious moisture barrier is recommended on top of the gravel
cushion layer Flexible floor coverings should be considered above the floor slab
because they can better mask minor slab cracking

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Structural fill materials should be placed in level lifts not exceeding 12 inches in
loose thickness moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction Relative compaction refers to
the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 Optimum
moisture content is the water content percentage by weight corresponding to the
maximum dry density Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers
vibratory rollers or other types of acceptable compaction equipment

Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of rocks
and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of passes
needed by a compactor to achieve the desired level of compaction should be
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a
Geolabs representative Based on this testing program the number of passes may
then be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction
For preliminary estimating purposes compaction may be accomplished by using a

Based on our field exploration the project site is generally underlain by surface
volcanic ash materials over fractured pahoehoe lavas and a a basalt It is

However deeper excavations in the pahoehoe formations may require the use of

3 7 Pipelines
We envision that new water pipelines will be installed It is anticipated most of the

Free draining granular materials such as open graded gravel ASTM C 33

of the backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to the pipes
As an alternative the granular bedding and the initial trench backfill up to about
12 inches above the top of the pipes may consist of 1 inch minus well graded granular
materials aggregate base course Where used the 1 inch minus well graded granular
bedding and the initial trench backfill should be moisture conditioned and compacted to
not less than 90 percent relative compaction

to the top of the subgrade or finished grade should consist of well graded granular
materials less than 6inches in maximum size The backfill should be
moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in about 12 inch loose lifts

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling seven borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 7 The borings were
advanced to a maximum depth of about 41 feet below the existing ground surface using
a truck mounted drill rig equipped with coring tools The approximate locations of the
borings drilled for the project are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representations of the
materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 7
Relatively undisturbed soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM D 3550 Ring Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving
a 3 inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
addition some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance
with ASTM D 1586 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils by driving a
2 inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop The blow
counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18 inch drive
are shown as the Penetration Resistance on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate
sample depths
Core samples of the rock formations encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The material left in the hollow core of the bit is
mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt For example if 3 7 feet of
material is recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and
would be shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74
