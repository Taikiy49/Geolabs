EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
This basis of design report was developed in support of the preliminary design
concept level of a rockfall protection system for falling rock hazards along sections of the
roadway within the limits of the Route 030 Ta u Road Rockfall Hazard Evaluation project
The slopes extending above the roadway at the project site typically consist of steepened
cut slopes adjacent to the roadway that merge with the upper steep slopes comprising the
native mountainside The slopes generally consist of layered a a and pahoehoe basalt
formations containing alternating seams of weathered clinker and weathering resistant
hard basaltic rock materials The basalt rock comprising the cut slopes and natural hillside
is actively eroding and producing falling rock ranging from very large boulders and blocks
of dense basalt rock to small stones that encroach on the paved roadway area
Based on our observation of the existing site conditions we believe the potential
exists for falling rock to occur throughout the length of the study area Considering budget
constraints that may preclude the construction of a complete and comprehensive rockfall
protection system throughout the length of the project site we anticipate the site will need
to be broken down into priority areas Therefore we suggest that priority be assigned to
the implementation of the rockfall protection for selected portions of Route 030 identified
as probable higher risk potential rockfall hazard areas
Based on our reconnaissance of the project site it appears that there are
three main rock failure mechanisms generating rockfall events at the site The
three mechanisms related to rockfall are raveling failure boulder fall and differential
erosion of the alternating volcanic rock layers
Based on the three mechanisms of rockfall observed at the project site in
conjunction with the topographic character of the source slopes we believe the most
viable alternatives for rockfall mitigation consist of the implementation of various site
specific rockfall hazard controls The rockfall hazard controls proposed for this project
include the following 1 rock slope scaling and boulder sweep of the cut slopes and
portions of the natural slopes adjacent to the roadway 2 the installation of anchored wire
netting on the steep cliff faces and 3 the installation of rockfall impact barriers on some
selected higher elevation natural slopes above the lower elevation slopes where scaling
and netting may be proposed
In addition to prioritizing the overall mitigation work into phases in accordance with
our assessed risk ranking we suggest that each work phase could be further subdivided
into two separate rockfall mitigation construction projects The first of the two construction
projects which is recommended as priority and should require less intensive field study to
develop the final design could encompass the rock scaling and boulder sweep activities
on the lower elevation slopes immediately adjacent to the roadway The second of the
two construction projects which is recommended if additional field study confirms our
preliminary assumptions regarding the presence of significant rockfall sources on the
higher elevation slopes could encompass the installation of rockfall impact barriers on
selected upper slopes The purpose for developing two construction projects per

SECTION 3 REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The Territory of American Samoa is an island archipelago located approximately
2 300 miles toward the southwest of Honolulu Hawaii at about 14 degrees latitude
south of the equator The total land area of American Samoa consists of about
76 square miles and includes five volcanic islands Tutuila Aunu u Ofu Olosega and
Ta u and two remote atolls Rose and Swains The Samoan archipelago was formed
by submarine volcanic activity that built up to form the volcanic mountains which
emerged from the ocean about 1 5 million years ago during the late Pliocene Epoch
Stearns 1944
The American Samoa island archipelago formed as an elongated island chain
that grew as the Pacific Plate moved westward in relation to a nearly stationary hot
spot of thermal activity located deep in the earth s mantle Therefore the easternmost
Samoan Islands are the youngest and least eroded islands of the Samoan chain As the
entire Samoan Island archipelago shifts westward on the Pacific Plate it is subducted
under a bend in the Australian Plate at a junction approximately 100 miles toward the
south of American Samoa This proximal plate interaction is believed to be responsible
for some earthquakes and renewed volcanic activity in the area The current hot spot
volcanism is centered over Vailuluu Seamount 28 miles east of Ta u Island The last
known eruption of the seamount occurred in 1975 and was suspected again in 1995
when an earthquake swarm was recorded in the area The most recent documented
eruption in the main Samoan Islands occurred in 1866 along the submarine ridge
connecting Olosega Island with Ta u Island
The islands of American Samoa are slowly subsiding into the sea with the
passage of time The land mass subsidence is generally due to the lack of thermal uplift
associated with active volcanism deflation of magma chambers beneath the islands
and the weight of the island mass pressing on the ocean floor As the islands slowly
subside low elevation coastlines are slowly being submerged leading to changes in the
shoreline dynamics

Island of Ta u
The Island of Ta u is one of three islands comprising the Manu a Island group
The Island of Ta u encompasses a land surface area of approximately 29 square miles
Lata Mountain shield volcano forms the highest peak on the island with a maximum
elevation of about 3 200 feet MSL The Island of Ta u generally consists of a
rectangular shaped volcanic island with the highest ground elevation corresponding with
the volcanic crater located near the central portion of the island Lata Mountain The
island slopes are generally radial and steep with very limited flatter terrain generally at
the shoreline margins as depicted on the Project Location Map Plate 1 The island has
numerous volcanic cones and is generally comprised of thick and thin bedded a a and
pahoehoe lava flows interspersed with volcanic tuff beds The southern shoreline is
comprised of tall sea cliffs rising to heights of over 2 000 feet above mean sea level
The combination of an active tectonic setting close proximity to the oceanic
Tongan Trench and a tropical climate in American Samoa presents many natural and
geologic hazards including earthquakes volcanic eruptions tsunamis slope failures
rapid mass wasting cyclones drought and flooding As a result falling rock hazards
associated with erosion and mass wasting of hillslopes contributes to falling rock
hazards especially where roadway development encroaches on the adjacent steep
slopes

As mentioned previously the typical basalt rock formation comprising the
mountain slopes appears to be composed of mainly a a lavas thicker more viscous
flows with some inclusions of pahoehoe type lavas thinner more fluid flows In
general the basalt rock appears to be slightly weathered and ranges from dense
massive lava flow cores containing limited fracture discontinuities to random closely
fractured medium hard to hard rock that appears loose broken and potentially
unstable Observed interbedded clinker materials appear slightly to moderately
weathered severely fractured rubbly and very porous The thick a a lavas are prone to
differential erosion between the interbedded hard and soft rock layers The differential
erosion promotes the development of overhanging blocks of dense rock that produce
boulders once detached from the slope surface The thinner a a and pahoehoe lava
flows are more prone to raveling of individual rock fragments bounded by open fractures
and the production of rockslides involving larger masses of closely fractured rock Rock
fractures typically range from hairline discontinuities to wide open dilated fractures

rock weathering resulting from exposure to the elements of erosion as well as the

stability of the slopes composed of the volcanic materials

Climate Considerations
The climate generally is classified as tropical and is affected by the close
proximity of the inter tropical convergence zone ITCZ which is characterized by
weaker wind fields higher air temperatures and increased humidity Rainfall averages
about 125 inches per year at the lower elevations increasing to as much as about
300 inches per year at the inland mountain summit regions Thunderstorm activity
commonly is accompanied by briefly heavy rainfall rates with sudden and intense
surface water runoff generated from the steep mountain slopes The island may be
affected by hurricanes and other tropical weather systems which typically approach
from the north
The average annual air temperature is near 80 degrees Fahrenheit with
maximum temperatures recorded in the lower 90s and minimum temperatures near
60 degrees Fahrenheit The island is densely vegetated with tropical flora with many
perennial streams flowing from the typically small and steep drainage basins located
within the interior sections of the island
Due to the high rainfall and humid climatic environment groundwater seepage
from the slopes is anticipated to occur Groundwater seepage may be responsible for
some occurrence of rockfall within rock materials that are weakened by rock fractures
and the effects of deep weathering In addition the type of rainfall experienced on the
Island of Ta u frequently can be intense and or prolonged which contributes to heavy
surface water runoff and flash flooding The effects of intense rainfall and heavy runoff
have the potential to accelerate surface erosion and loosen rocks by infiltrating rock
fractures and washing away loosened materials

Rockfall Mechanisms
Based on our reconnaissance of the rockfall hazard areas along Route 030 it
appears that there are three main mechanisms generating rockfall events at the site
The three mechanisms related to rockfall are raveling failure boulder fall and
differential erosion of alternating volcanic rock layers
Raveling
Raveling of one or several rocks from the slope may occur due to a multitude of
causes The hazard develops when the raveling rock has sufficient velocity and
volume to reach the roadway surface Raveling may be caused by water pressure
behind the rock ice jacking not possible at this location differential weathering or
erosion along faults or shear zones weakening of rock due to temperature and
moisture cyclic fluctuations and loosening by animals or trees and wind Raveling
also may result from ground vibration Due to the steep exposed condition of some
of the basalt rock slope surfaces at the rockfall hazard areas raveling is an
important process that continually works to shed weathered and broken rock
materials from the slopes
One of the most frequent causes of raveling is by invasive tree roots prying on the
existing cracks located in the rock slope surface Tree roots especially of the larger
trees frequently develop and grow larger in the discontinuities of rock materials
The added force of wind action on tree limbs and branches may create leveraged
forces that are transferred to the roots lodged within the discontinuity Over time
the repetitive leveraging of the discontinuity can loosen rock and cause it to fall
Because of the numerous large and leaning trees at the rockfall hazard areas we
believe that tree root pry is a significant and real mechanism for creating rock slope

Boulder Fall
On many of the lower height cut slopes abutting the roadway excavations have
exposed rocky colluvial talus composed of accumulations of fallen basaltic boulders
and rock fragments with some entrained soil material The typical size of the talus
boulders visible on the ground surface vary between about 2 and 6 feet in
dimension and include some very large blocks of rock locally up to about 10 to
15 feet or larger in dimension
Loose unsupported or precariously positioned boulders on the slope have potential
to fall and roll from the short length but steep cut slopes Surface boulders lining the
cut slope brow are most at risk for movement due to limited rock support and
greater exposure to impact from other large rocks and levering trees and storm
water runoff during intense rainfall Unsupported boulders protruding from the cut
slope face are also at risk of developing instability and popping out from the slope
face
In addition many of the steep natural upper slopes and the very steep slopes
cliffs encompass weathered basalt rock formation containing embedded boulders
more weather resistant blocks of rock contained within the overall basalt rock
formation The less weather resistant basalt rock surrounding the more weather
resistant basalt boulders may be scoured away by intense surface water runoff
causing the unsupported boulder to dislodge and fall from the slope Because of the
tall height of the very steep slopes the dislodged boulders often have the potential
to develop substantial momentum from rolling without breaking apart The boulders
may readily roll through a ditch or hardened barriers therefore falling and rolling
boulders are a significant and real rockfall hazard at the project site

crane baskets Rock slope scaling work typically proceeds by starting from the top of

scaling should be performed typically on slopes with an inclination steeper than about

slope face Anchored netting is recommended for selected slopes at Rockfall Hazard

Rockfall impact barriers typically are designed to span across the slope along a

constructed between about 8 and 20 feet above ground level All galvanized steel
barrier components may be powder coated black in coloration for blending with the

The rockfall impact barrier system should be inspected periodically for signs of
excessive corrosion anchor and net damage and undesired loading to maintain the
desired level of protection with the passage of time Additional guideline information
pertaining to periodic inspections and maintenance after installation is included in

6 Slope scalers are defined as persons performing the slope
scaling trimming activity directly on the slope face Slope scaling activity
may include slope scalers hanging from ropes attached to the top of the
slope face rappelling using a man lift bucket to allow the slope scalers
to reach the slope face or by any other means that can place slope
scalers directly on the slope face Slope scaling is performed by using
hand tools such as small power tools crowbars prybars shovels etc
and rolling or pushing scaled materials down toward the highway below
the slope Each slope scaler shall be equipped with communications
equipment to enable direct communication with the scaling supervisor
and or a designated person on the ground Excavation using heavy
equipment to scrape the slope face by the use of a backhoe or etc is
prohibited except for the areas accepted by the Engineer in writing
7 Start all slope scaling at the top of the slope and proceed down slope
removing loose rock and other debris as the work progresses All
material on the slope face that is loose hanging or creates a safety
hazard to the public must be removed or stabilized to the Engineer s
satisfaction during or on completion of the section of slope The
Contractor shall exercise extra care in the slope scaling work and shall
avoid over steepening the slope face that may cause instability of the
slope face If during the slope scaling work the Contractor encounters
unstable slope conditions that may constitute a potential slide
immediately notify the Engineer
8 Blocks of rock or debris that hang up on the slope during the slope
scaling operations shall be removed upon completion of the first pass of
slope scaling The Contractor shall continue scaling of the slopes until
the slope scaling has been completed to the satisfaction of the Engineer
The Engineer will inspect the slope faces to determine whether or not
scaling of the slope faces have been completed The Contractor shall
assist and provide a life line for the State s personnel during the
inspection of the work Assistance to the Engineer and provision of a
life line for personnel to inspect the slopes shall be considered
incidental to the contract unit price for slope scaling
9 All debris generated from the slope scaling including the debris
generated from the controlled blasting or trimming of the rock outcrops
designated for removal on the contract drawings shall be the property of
the Contractor and shall be removed from the project site for disposal
The Contractor shall sweep the highway clean of all debris within the
total closure times of the highway before allowing traffic to pass The
Engineer will inspect the cleaned up highway prior to opening of the
highway to the public on a daily basis
10 The Contractor shall maintain equipment on site at all times to
remove debris from the highway to allow the passage of emergency
vehicles in the event that emergency vehicles require passage through
the project area along the highway during the designated closure times
The slope scaling activities shall be temporarily suspended to allow the
passage of the emergency vehicles through the project area along the
highway

netting system and fasten the netting to the boundary ropes with
compression claws

wrench in order to tension the anchored netting to the grouted soil rock
anchors to at least 11 2 kips

Guidelines for Rockfall Protection System Maintenance
INSPECTION AND MAINTENANCE OF ROCKFALL BARRIERS
Rockfall barriers are designed for low maintenance requirements because of 1 the
durability and redundancy of the system components 2 the installations are intended
for remote access slope settings and 3 the available corrosion protection coatings
from the manufacturer help to slow the progress of environmental corrosion
Rockfall barriers are designed to withstand multiple rock impacts within the design
energy class range without need for immediate system maintenance However very
large rock impacts or multiple large rock impacts may effect the future performance of
the system and should be investigated on a timely basis For this reason routine visual
inspections are recommended to periodically review the performance of the system and
evaluate whether rock impacts have damaged some system components and require
repair or replacement The frequency of inspection is usually dependent upon the
environmental setting and the number and magnitude of rock impacts Because the
project site is in a humid tropical forest adjacent to the seacoast strongly corrosive
environment with the anticipated potential for intercepting large rock impacts the
inspections should be performed more frequently to help maintain the desired design
level of rockfall protection
Rockfall barriers require routine periodic visual inspection by a qualified engineer or
engineering geologist to ensure the integrity and desired performance of the overall
system In addition occasional maintenance work performed by a qualified contractor to
repair or replace broken or worn barrier system components following large rock
impacts may be necessary during the life of the rockfall protection system Barrier
manufacturer s ideally recommend routine inspections be performed 1 to 2 times per
year Considering the cost with respect to the remote island location and the difficult
access to reach the protection system on the slope we suggest that routine visual
inspections may be performed at least at about 2 to 3 year intervals and always
following large rockfall impact s However an initial 1 year post installation inspection
is highly recommended to evaluate the system response to installation and possible
new erosion and rockfall conditions that could affect the system The initial 1 year
inspection should be performed to evaluate the operational condition of the system
components following the installation and an initial period of operation The initial and
routine inspections should be performed by a qualified engineer or engineering
geologist using a checklist or procedure tailored to the project conditions The specific
checklist and procedure may be developed from the recommendations provided by the
particular rockfall barrier manufacturer
The routine inspection serves to identify potential rock debris accumulations that may
require timely removal and to inspect for possible changes in the system condition or
the effects of corrosion that may reduce the barrier performance

Barrier maintenance such as the repair or replacement of worn broken components
i e nets wire ropes brake elements and associated mounting hardware may be
required following a large rockfall impact The frequency of barrier maintenance typically
depends on the size and frequency of rockfall events Rockfall barriers are designed to
require minimal routine maintenance especially if no rock impacts are sustained
Where a very low incidence of rockfall activity is experienced inspection of the system
components by qualified persons may be conducted at intervals of about 5 to 10 years
In corrosive environments and where rockfall activity is relatively moderate qualified
persons should perform the thorough inspections at intervals of about 2 to 3 years
Due to the often difficult access condition of most barrier installations owners such as
State Department of Transportation SDOT typically establish a contract with a qualified
engineer or engineering geologist and rockfall specialty contractor to perform the routine
inspections rock debris removal and other system maintenance when needed In
some cases where the barrier is clearly visible from readily accessible locations such
as a roadway or the base of a low height slope the owner may elect to perform the
routine visual inspections themselves
Furthermore where barriers are located at readily accessible locations the owner may
perform the removal of rock debris using construction machinery However a rockfall
specialty contractor who maintains the proper equipment and trained personnel to
conduct work on difficult access slopes should be contracted to perform the removal of
rockfall debris and perform barrier system repair The rockfall mitigation specialty
contractor typically utilizes portable equipment which is transported onto the slope to
perform the potentially dangerous rock removal and system maintenance work
Furthermore the rockfall specialty contractor would typically employ temporary safety
controls to protect downslope persons and property during the performance of the work
It is important to note that the need for maintenance on rockfall impact barriers is
minimal because the barriers are designed to withstand rock impacts within the rated
energy class Therefore it is not necessary to clear the barrier nets if only small
accumulations of rock are contained in the bottom of the nets If large accumulations of
rocks or very large individual rocks are contained in the net and the net is observed to
be sagging or otherwise compromised by excessive loading brake elements fully
deployed or broken it is necessary to remove the entrained material and inspect the
system for signs of worn and broken parts
For this difficult access on slope setting we envision that the rockfall contractor would
need to mobilize drilling equipment to break up the rock into manageable pieces for
manual removal from the net and relocation of the spoils in appropriate stable slope
settings on the hillside

INSPECTION AND MAINTENANCE OF ANCHORED SLOPE NETTING
The anchored netting system is a flexible rock restraint and retention improvement that
is designed to hold back rock mass sliding and toppling mechanisms from the slope
face If the net system is under stress load the nets will deform Net deformation is
minimized by installation of the wire netting over irregular slope surfaces with good

subsoil anchorage and tight slope face contact With the passage of time fractured
rock pieces may loosen separate and press against the netting imparting additional
load If the rocks are large enough the entrained large pieces of loose rock may require
removal to maintain the system effectiveness
Periodic routine inspection of the system is recommended as part of the system
maintenance plan The manufacturer typically recommends annual inspections for the
first two years following installation If two successive inspections demonstrate no
substantial changes to the system the period of time between inspections may be
extended to about 2 years
In consideration of the costs and logistics associated with the remote project location
we suggest that an initial post construction inspection be conducted about 1 year after
system installation Future routine inspections may be performed at about 3 5 year
intervals
The manufacturer recommends additional interim inspections be conducted following
large earthquakes extreme rainfall events and large rock surface impacts emanating
from upslope areas These interim inspections may be performed as needed to evaluate
possible damage resulting from possible major erosion and or earth movements
In general routine inspection of the anchored netting may comprise the following
Overall system condition with respect to corrosion and damage
Condition of potential problem slope areas where rock slippage is
noted
Damage to system anchorage components
Damage from erosion and earth movement
Vegetation re growth condition
Comparison with previous documented potential problem areas
Documentation of any alterations to the system as a result of repair
The inspection findings should be documented with photographs to allow comparison
with previous inspection information The inspection should include observations
pertaining to local erosion and rock decomposition that may require local net
re tensioning resulting from a shift of the rock mass Post construction vegetation
re growth should also be evaluated to determine if vegetation maintenance may be
required Vegetation maintenance may include trimming or removal of large trees and
surface root systems that may adversely affect the netting and anchorage system
