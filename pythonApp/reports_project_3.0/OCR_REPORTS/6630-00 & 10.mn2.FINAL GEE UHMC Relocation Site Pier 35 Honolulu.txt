SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our borings in the vicinity of the proposed new box culvert alignment (Boring Nos. 1 
and 2) encountered about 7 feet of fill consisting of dense to very dense silty sand with 
gravel underlain by loose gravel and soft to very soft  lagoonal deposits of silty clay 
between 10 and 30 feet below ground surface.  This clay horizon grades to stiff at 30 feet 
extending to the maximum depth explored of about 41.5 feet below the existing ground 
surface. 
The ten, 5-foot deep exploratory borings (Boring Nos. 3 to 12) drilled for pavement 
subgrade characterization encountered fill materials of medium dense to dense sand with 
some gravel. 
We encountered groundwater in Boring Nos. 1 and 2 at depths of about 7.2 and 
7.7 feet below ground surface, respectively.  We did not encounter groundwater in the 
shallow borings within the existing pavement area. 
The project involves designing a box culvert to replace the existing CRM-lined open 
drainage ditch at the southern portion of Pier 35 near its boundary with Pier 34, and 
evaluating the existing pavement at the site for its relative suitability under the anticipated 
use and loading conditions. 
Based on the results of our geotechnical exploration and analysis, it is our opinion 
that ground stabilization by jet grouting should be implemented below the new box culvert 
to reduce the effects of the ground settlement caused by the new fills.  Also, it is our 
opinion that the existing flexible pavement structure within the proposed internal access 
roadway appears to be adequate to accommodate the anticipated traffic load at the site.  
The text of this report should be referred to for detailed discussion and specific 
design recommendations for the box culvert bedding and new pavements.

SECTION 1.  GENERAL 
1.1 
Introduction 
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration 
performed for the proposed University of Hawaii Marine Center (UHMC) relocation project 
at Pier 35 near the intersection of North Nimitz Highway and Alakawa Street in Honolulu on 
the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  The project location and general vicinity are shown on the 
Project Location Map, Plate 1. 
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical recommendations derived 
from our field exploration, laboratory testing, and engineering analyses.  These 
recommendations are intended for the design of the planned construction of a new box 
culvert and pavement structures including evaluation of the existing pavements. The 
findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the 
end of this report. 
1.2 
Project Considerations 
The existing University of Hawaii Marine Center (UHMC) is at Pier 45 of Snug 
Harbor off Sand Island Access Road at the former Kapalama Military reservation in 
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. Based on the information provided, it is desired to 
relocate the existing UH Marine Center to a new site at Pier 35 to allow the redevelopment 
of Snug Harbor (and the former Kapalama Military Reservation) into a new container 
terminal. Based on the information provided, UHMC at Snug Harbor provides port facilities 
and services in support of UH marine research programs in Hawaiian coastal waters, the 
Pacific and throughout the world. The existing UHMC has shore side assembly and 
laboratory facilities dedicated to oceangoing activities; libraries of marine scientific samples 
and data; electronic instrumentation development, testing and maintenance; servicing and 
storage for oceangoing research equipment and instrumentation; staging for large 
equipment such as manned submersibles, and towed subsurface electronic mapping 
systems; buoy construction, fish aggregating devices, instruments, sensors and staging for 
ocean side experiments.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lava from the 
Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be 
middle to late Pliocene in age, and the younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late 
Pliocene to early Pleistocene in age.   A long period of volcanic inactivity followed during 
which time erosion formed deep gullies in the Koolau shield. Volcanic activity resumed with 
a series of lava flows followed by cinder and tuff cone formation known as the Honolulu 
volcanic series.  
The project site lies on the coastal plain of Southern Oahu which was built on the 
eroded flanks of the Koolau Volcano. Koolau forms the eastern two thirds of the island of 
Oahu.  
During the Pleistocene Epoch, sea levels fluctuated in response to the cycles of 
continental glaciation.  As the glaciers grew and advanced, less water was available to fill 
the oceanic basins such that sea levels fell below the present stands of the sea.  When the 
glaciers melted and receded, an excess of water became available such that the sea levels 
rose to above the present sea level. 
The processes of erosion and deposition caused the top of the basaltic lava flows to 
be highly weathered and mantled by residual soils.  Urban development and reclamation 
projects within the last century at the southern coastal plains of Oahu have brought the 
project site to its present form.

SECTION 3.  DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
In general, the primary consideration of our geotechnical exploration is to provide 
relevant design parameters for the proposed UHMC relocation site improvements including:  
Determining relevant soil parameters for the design and construction of a  
box culvert to replace the existing concrete-lined  open drainage ditch at the

3.1 
Box Culvert 
Based on preliminary information, it is planned to replace the existing open drainage 
canal with a box culvert to allow paving over this drainage channel for the planned 
container storage area and truck turn around route. Presently, the open drainage channel 
changes direction about 50 feet into the property from a west southwest direction to a 
southwesterly direction towards two 48-inch diameter RCP pipes that pass under the 
existing road linking Piers 34 and 35  and daylights under the wharf. We assume that the 
new box culvert will maintain the same invert elevations as the existing open drainage 
canal. 
Up to about 4 to 5 feet of fills will be placed to raise the low-lying area at the south 
side of the existing canal where the proposed truck turn-around route and portion of the 
new 48-stall parking lot are planned. The weight of these fills as well as the weight of the 
box culvert and backfill will induce consolidation settlements on the order of up to 5 inches 
in the soft deposits below the site. In order to reduce the settlement of the box culvert, as 
much of the fill as possible should be placed and allowed to settle prior to the box culvert 
installation. We estimate that the remaining settlement on the box culvert may be on the 
order of 2 to 4 inches. If these settlements are not tolerable, jet grouting of the soft soils 
under the box culvert are recommended.

3.1.1 Culvert Bedding 
Based on the stratigraphy disclosed in Boring Nos. 1 and 2 relative to the anticipated 
depth of invert of the new box culvert (assumed in this report to follow the gradient of 
the existing drainage canal being replaced), we envision that the proposed box culvert 
will rest on soft to very soft clay and loose to very loose gravel with its invert estimated 
somewhere around 8 to 9 feet below existing ground surface. Due to its proximity to the 
waters edge, we anticipate that groundwater will be affected by the prevailing tide 
conditions. 
For preliminary design purposes, we recommend over-excavating the soft and/or loose 
materials at the bottom of the box culvert trench and replacing with a minimum of 
24 inches of No. 2 Coarse (ASTM C 33, No. 2 gradation) wrapped with non-woven filter 
fabric, such as Mirafi 180N or equivalent. We also recommend granular bedding 
material (ASTM C33, No. 67 gradation) consisting of No. 3B Fine above the 
stabilization layer up to 1 foot above the box culvert. 
A typical box culvert section is shown on Plate 3. These measures are intended to 
stabilize the anticipated soft clay and very loose gravel deposits under the box culvert 
alignment.

3.1.4 Excavation Support 
We anticipate trench excavation depths of about 9 to 11 feet below the ground surface 
will be required for the installation of the new box culvert. Based on these anticipated 
depths and the observed relative depth of groundwater in our borings, groundwater is 
anticipated to be encountered in the trench excavation. Contractors proposing to work 
on this project should be encouraged to examine the site conditions and include 
dewatering options during installation of the new box culvert. 
We anticipate the use of shoring may be necessary.  The excavation support and 
shoring system used must comply with applicable safety requirements. The contractor 
should be solely responsible for the adequacy and safety of the shoring installation. 
The contractors representative should be on-site at all times during excavation and 
construction work, for the opportunity to promptly observe changing or unforeseen 
conditions such as; high groundwater, inappropriate construction sequence or 
techniques, etc., which may affect the shoring stability. 
Excavated soils should not be stockpiled closer than a horizontal distance equal to the 
depth of the excavation from the edge of the excavation to reduce the potential for 
excessive ground movement. 
It is important to install adequate shoring and to maintain it tight against the excavation 
walls with proper bracing during construction. The properly braced shoring is essential 
to reduce the potential for appreciable lateral movements of the adjacent ground into 
the excavation, which may result in potential settlement or distress to adjacent 
structures or other improvements. 
It must be noted that some minor movements of the shoring system and the adjacent 
ground may still occur due to changes in earth stresses during excavation. Due to the 
complexity of the stress changes, it is difficult to accurately estimate the magnitude of 
movement. The magnitude also depends greatly upon workmanship, such as how 
quickly and tightly the shoring and bracing supports are installed, the subsurface

3.1.5 Jet Grouting 
To limit the effects of settlement of the very soft clay materials under the box culvert, 
we recommend the use of jet grouted columns.  For preliminary design purposes, jet 
grouted columns approximately 3 feet in diameter by 35 feet deep alternately spaced at 
14 feet on center under each side of the box culvert is recommended.  A schematic 
plan and section is shown on Plate 4. 
Jet grouting is a technique utilizing a special drill bit and injection monitor with radial 
horizontal nozzles to produce stabilized soil-cement columns.  The jet-grouting 
technique is a process that produces soil-cement columns by pumping neat cement 
grout slurry through horizontal jets injected at high pressures.  The horizontal jets of 
cement grout slurry cuts and mixes the surrounding in-situ materials with the neat 
cement slurry grout as the drill bit is slowly rotated and withdrawn to form a soil-cement 
column.

five selected boring locations to obtain indicative subsoil strength characteristics such 
as bearing ratio and bearing value. The tests were performed from the top of the base 
course through the subgrade to a depth of 5 feet.

3.4.3 Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements 
In general, fills and backfills (above groundwater) should be moisture-conditioned to 
at least 2% above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 
8 inches in loose thickness, and compacted to at least 90 percent relative 
compaction. Fills and backfills within 3 feet of the pavement grade elevation should 
be compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction. Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum 
dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with ASTM D 1557. Optimum 
moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. Compaction should be accomplished by sheep foot, vibratory 
rollers, or other types of acceptable compaction equipment. Water tamping, jetting, 
or ponding should not be allowed to compact the fills.

3.4.4 Anticipated Settlement Due to Consolidation of Soft Clay Under New Fills  
Vertical loads from new fills will result in settlement due to consolidation of the soft 
deposits below the site; therefore, new fills should be placed as soon as possible 
and the settlements should be monitored to determine when construction of surface 
improvements may commence. 
We recommend installing at least five settlement monitoring gauges in this area prior to 
filling operations. The gauges should be monitored periodically by precise leveling on a 
weekly basis until the settlements reduce to tolerable levels before paving over the 
area.  
We anticipate the narrow strip of unconsolidated soft silty clay soils under the existing 
canal would be subjected to consolidation under the anticipated 4 to 5 feet of fill.  We 
estimate that the backfill over the existing drainage canal will induce settlements up to 
5 inches depending on the thickness of the compressible material that lies underneath.  
We recommend filling this area in stages to reduce settlement of the new box culvert. 
Fills adjacent to the existing canal should be placed as soon as possible to induce 
settlement below the new box culvert. The box culvert construction should be delayed 
until the settlements reduce to tolerable levels. Since additional settlements will occur 
due to the box culvert installation and backfill we recommend allowing time for the soft 
zone to settle before paving over the area. We recommend installing at least 
four settlement monitoring gauges along the canal prior to filling operations to be 
periodically monitored by precise leveling until the settlements reduce to tolerable 
levels before paving over the area.

3.6.3 Drainage 
Retaining walls should be well drained to reduce the build-up of hydrostatic pressures. 
A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide zone of permeable material, 
such as No. 3B Fine gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation), placed directly around a 
perforated pipe (perforations facing down) at the base of the wall discharging to an 
appropriate outlet or weep holes.  As an alternative, a prefabricated drainage product, 
such as MiraDrain or EnkaDrain, may be used instead of the drainage material. The 
prefabricated drainage product also should be hydraulically connected to a perforated 
pipe at the base of the wall. 
Backfill behind the permeable drainage zone may consist of compacted on-site 
materials or free-draining compacted fills, where specified by the designer. Unless 
covered by concrete slabs, the upper 12 inches of backfill should consist of 
low-expansion, relatively impervious materials to reduce the potential for excessive 
water infiltration behind the walls. 
3.7 
Utility Trenches 
Based on preliminary information, the new facility will include basic utilities, such as 
water, power and sewer connections. In general, good construction practices should be 
utilized for the installation and backfilling of the trenches for the new utilities. The contractor 
should determine the method and equipment to be used for trench excavation, subject to 
practical limits and safety considerations. In addition, the excavations should comply with 
the applicable federal, state, and local safety requirements. The contractor should be 
responsible for trench shoring design and installation. 
In general, we recommend providing granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of 
open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation) under the pipes for uniform support. 
Free-draining granular materials, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C 33, No. 67 
gradation), also should be used for the initial trench backfill up to about 12 inches above the 
pipes or groundwater level to provide adequate support around the pipes. It is critical to use 
this free-draining material to reduce the potential for formation of voids below the haunches

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling 
two 41.5-foot deep borings along the box culvert alignment and ten 5-foot deep borings within 
the existing and proposed pavement locations.  The approximate locations of the borings 
drilled are shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.  The borings were drilled using a truck-mounted 
drill rig equipped with continuous-flight, solid-stem augers. Dynamic Cone Penetration tests 
were also performed in selected borings for pavement subgrade characterization. 
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural 
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing in 
the laboratory.  Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified Soil Classification 
System as shown on Plate A-0.1.  Graphic representations of the materials encountered are 
presented on the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-12. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified California 
sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained 
from the drilled borings in general accordance with ASTM D 1586, Penetration Test and Split-
Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler using the same 
hammer and drop.  The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the second and third 6 
inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration Resistance” on the Logs of Borings 
at the appropriate sample depths. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the 
field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive 
strength of the soil sample.  Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) tests (ASTM D 6951) were performed at 
five boring locations to obtain indicative subsoil strength characteristics such as California 
Bearing Ratio and corresponding bearing value for pavement evaluation. The tests were 
performed using a Salem Dual Mass Dynamic Cone Penetrometer from the top of the base 
course or bottom of the concrete slab through the subgrade to a depth of at least 5 feet. 
The test results are discussed in the text of this report.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D 2937) determinations 
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample 
depths. 
Three Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification.  The test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth.  Graphic representation 
of the test results is provided on Plate B-1. 
Three Sieve Analysis (ASTM C 117 & ASTM C 136) tests were performed on selected 
samples from the borings to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the surface fills and to aid 
in soil classification.  A graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate 
B-2.  
Two Moisture and Density Relationship tests (ASTM D 1557) were performed on bulk 
samples collected as follows:  Bulk-1 for soils under the existing pavement area and Bulk-3 for 
the exposed surface soils taken in the vicinity of the proposed box culvert.  These tests were 
performed to determine the optimum moisture content and maximum dry density as an aid in 
determining the relative compaction of the soils during construction. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
Two laboratory CBR tests (ASTM D 1883) were performed to measure the shearing 
resistance of the near-surface fills under controlled moisture and density conditions to evaluate 
its suitability as subgrade or base course for pavements. The results are presented on Plates 
B-5 and B-6. 
A one-dimensional Consolidation test (ASTM D 2435) was performed on a selected soft 
clay sample to measure its deformation under various surcharge loads. The results of this test 
is used to estimate the amount of settlement that the fill soils over the unconsolidated materials 
(such as under the existing canal) may undergo under the proposed surcharge loads. The test 
results are presented on Plate B-7. 
A Soil Resistivity test (ASTM G 57) and a pH value determination (ASTM G 51) were 
performed on selected soil samples to evaluate the corrosivity of the underlying soils within the 
box culvert. The resistivity of the surrounding soil environment plays a significant role in the 
corrosion of underground structures i.e.; high resistivity soils are generally not as corrosive as 
low resistivity soils. The resistivity of the soil is one of many factors that influence the service 
life of a buried structure. The test results are presented on Plate B-8.
