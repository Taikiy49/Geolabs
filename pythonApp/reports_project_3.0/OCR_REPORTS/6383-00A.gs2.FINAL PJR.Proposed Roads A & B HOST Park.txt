SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
New pavements will be constructed as part of the Roads A and B at HOST Park
project Based on our field exploration we anticipate the majority of the pavement
subgrade soils for the roadways generally will consist of basaltic gravels and basalt rock
formation We understand that Queen Kaahumanu Highway that connects to Roads A
and B does not have a permeable drainage layer in the pavement section Since the
main highway does not have a permeable drainage layer and because of the relatively
permeable surface conditions at the site the use of a permeable drainage layer was not
considered
Based on the traffic data provided the R value of the subgrade soils and the
economic analyses performed a rigid pavement structural section was most economical
for Roads A and B The recommended pavement structural section for Road A
consists of 8 5 inches of Portland Cement Concrete PCC placed on 6 0 inches of
Aggregate Subbase ASB For Road B the recommended pavement structural section
consists of 8 0 inches of PCC placed on 6 0 inches of ASB
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore special attention should
be given to the surface drainage of the pavements As a minimum the pavement surface
should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures
The text of this pavement justification report should be referred to for detailed
discussion and specific recommendations for design of pavements

Introduction
This pavement justification report presents the results of our analyses performed
for the design of new pavements for the Roads A and B at HOST Park project in the
District of Kona on the Island of Hawaii The project location and general vicinity are
shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1

geotechnical recommendations derived from our analyses for the design of pavements

Kaahumanu Highway Based on information provided the new Roads A and B will

Regional Geology
The Island of Hawaii is the largest island of the Hawaiian Archipelago and covers
an area of approximately 4 000 square miles This island was formed by the eruptive
activity of five major shield volcanoes Kohala long extinct Mauna Kea some activity
during recent geologic time Hualalai last erupted in 1801 1803 and Mauna Loa
and Kilauea both active
The project site is on the western flank of Hualalai Shield Volcano which had
been built up by the successive accumulation of pahoehoe and a a lava flows
originating from the northwestern rift zone At the project site the basalts encountered
were relatively fresh and unweathered and are estimated to be of an age on the order
of a few hundreds of years or less The lavas exposed at and near the project site were
mainly of the pahoehoe form with some clinker areas
Cavities or voids are encountered commonly in pahoehoe lava Cavities form
when the lava is still in a molten state and represent both lava tubes intra flow cavities
and inter flow cavities blisters and pockets Lava tubes form when molten lava drains
from the cooling flow leaving a hollow tube like structure which may extend for a large
distance longitudinally along the flow Inter flow cavities are generally smaller in
horizontal extent

Subsurface Conditions
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling two borings
designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 at the project site The borings were extended to a
depth of about 15 feet below the existing ground surface In addition the excavation of
one test pit was performed at the site The approximate locations of the borings and

2 5 feet thick The fill layer consisted of medium dense gravel with sand The fill layer

depth explored of 15 feet below the existing ground surface A void and clinker layer
was encountered within the basalt rock formation in one of the borings The excavated
test pit generally encountered very hard basalt rock formation from the surface to about
0 5 feet depth
We did not encounter groundwater in the borings and test pit at the time of our
field exploration However groundwater levels at the project site may vary and be

considered in the pavement analyses The flexible pavement sections presented herein

the Hveem Stabilometer method developed and used by the California Department of
Transportation Caltrans

the design procedures described in the Portland Cement Association Thickness Design
for Concrete Highway and Street Pavements

Subgrade Preparation Below Pavement Section
We anticipate the majority of the new roadway pavements will be constructed at
or near the existing grades or in a fill condition In general the area within the contract
grading limits should be cleared and grubbed thoroughly at the on set of earthwork
Vegetation debris deleterious materials and other unsuitable materials should be
removed and properly disposed off site or disposed in a designated area to reduce the
potential for contamination of the excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas
designated to receive fill or future improvements should be over excavated to expose
stiff and or dense materials The resulting excavation should be backfilled with
aggregate subbase materials The excavated soft and or organic soils should be
properly disposed off site
After clearing and grubbing the future pavement areas should be excavated
where necessary to the pavement subgrade level i e bottom of the aggregate
subbase layer In areas where the pavement subgrades expose dense clinker materials
or basalt rock formation the subgrades should be proof rolled with a minimum 15 ton
vibratory roller for a minimum of eight passes to help detect and collapse near surface
cavities Loose areas or cavities disclosed during clearing and proof rolling operations
should be backfilled with well compacted granular fill materials The proof rolling
operation also should be performed at the finished subgrades in cut areas The
vibratory drum roller should travel at a speed of about 200 to 300 feet per minute Due
to the potential for presence of cavities lava tubes blisters pockets etc some
variation in the over excavation and backfill quantities should be anticipated

Fill Materials
We have assumed that the recommended pavement section will be underlain by
structural fill materials 6 inch minus granular fills in the fill areas In general structural fill
materials should consist of well graded granular materials generally less than 6 inches
in maximum dimension with sufficient fines to prevent the occurrence of voids in the
compacted mass

Compaction Requirements
Conventional compaction testing is generally not practical in fills composed of
rocks boulders and or cobbles Instead a testing program to evaluate the number of
passes by a compactor needed to achieve the desired level of compaction should be
conducted at the start of the grading phase of the project under the observation of a
Geolabs representative Based on this testing program the number of passes may then
be used as the field criterion for adequate compaction For preliminary estimating
purposes compaction may be accomplished by using a large vibratory drum roller having
a static weight of at least 15 tons traveling at a speed of about 200 to 300 feet per minute
The roller should pass over the fill surface at least eight times for structural fill
Aggregate subbase materials required for the new pavements should conform to
the requirements stipulated in Subsection 703 16 of the State of Hawaii Standard
Specifications for Road and Bridge Construction 2005 HSS The aggregate subbase
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in
8 inch level loose lifts and compacted to no less than 95 percent of the maximum
density
Asphalt concrete base ACB material should consist of asphalt treated basaltic
aggregate placed in a layer not to exceed 6 inches in compacted thickness and
compacted to at least 92 percent of the maximum theoretical specific gravity determined
in accordance with AASHTO T 209 ASTM D 2041

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions along the roadway improvements were explored by
drilling and sampling two borings designated as Boring Nos 1 and 2 The borings were
advanced to a depth of about 15 feet below the existing ground surface using a
truck mounted drill rig equipped with auger and coring equipment In addition the
subsurface conditions at the site were explored by excavating and sampling one test pit
designated as Test Pit No 3 to a depth of about 0 5 feet below the existing ground
surface The test pit was excavated using a Komatsu 200 excavator The test pit was
extended to refusal conditions for the equipment used The approximate boring and test
pit locations are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings and test pit were classified by visual
and textural examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the drilling
operations on a near continuous basis Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A 0 1 Graphic
representations of the materials encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings
Plates A 1 and A 2 and the Log of Test Pit Plate A 3
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D 2113 Diamond
Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a
hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the hollow core of the
bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description Rock cores were
