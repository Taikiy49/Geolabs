In accordance with our fee proposal dated May 11, 2004, we have evaluated the
subsurface and groundwater conditions within the proposed building footprint for the new
Honolulu Ford Dealership in the Kapalama area of Honolulu on the Island of Oahu,
Hawaii.
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our findings and recommendations pertaining to the design of the
under slab drainage system for the new building at the project site. The findings and
recommendations are intended for design input only and are subject to the limitations
noted at the end of this report.

the project site. Due to the shallow groundwater conditions encountered at the project
site, it is desired to evaluate the site and subsurface conditions and provide

PURPOSE AND SCOPE
The purpose of our geotechnical engineering exploration was to obtain additional
information pertaining to the subsurface and groundwater conditions within the building
footprint at the project site. The subsurface information was used to formulate a summary
of the soil and/or groundwater conditions for the design of an under slab drainage system
for the proposed development. To accomplish this, we conducted an exploration program
consisting of the following tasks and work efforts:
1. Review of the construction plans and geotechnical report entitled
“Eoundation Investigation, Honolulu Ford, King and Houghtailing Streets,
Honolulu, Hawaii, TMK: 1-6-03” prepared by Ernest K. Hirata &
Associates, Inc. dated November 4, 2002.
2. Mobilization and demobilization of a truck-mounted drill rig and two
operators to and from the project site.
3: Drilling and sampling of seven borings extending to depths of about 21.5
and 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface for a total of
approximately 155 lineal feet of exploration.
4. Coordination of the field exploration and logging of the borings by a
geologist from our firm.
5. Laboratory testing of selected soil samples obtained during the field
exploration as an aid in classifying the materials encountered and
evaluating their engineering properties.
6. Analyses of the field and laboratory data for the formulation of
geotechnical recommendations pertaining to the design of an under slab
drainage system for the proposed development.
7. Preparation of this report summarizing our work on the project and
presenting our findings and recommendations.
8. Coordination of our overall work on the project by a senior engineer from
our firm.
9, Quality assurance of our work and client/design team consultation by a
principal engineer from our firm.
10. Miscellaneous work efforts such as drafting, word processing, and clerical
support.

EXISTING SITE CONDITIONS
The above property is located on the southeast side of Houghtailing Street,
between its intersection with North King and Olomea Streets in the Kapalama area of
Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The site was occupied previously by one and
two-story residential dwellings along the southeastern side of the property. At the time
of our field exploration, the project site has been cleared and grubbed of vegetation, and
the previously existed dwellings have been demolished and removed.
Our field observations indicated that the project site is in the process of being
graded to the design finished subgrade elevations at the time of our field exploration.
However, we understand that groundwater was encountered at or above the finished
subgrade elevations of the project site during the foundation excavation work. A pond
of groundwater was observed within the building excavation to achieve the finished
subgrade elevations at the project site.
SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by Ernest K. Hirata &
Associates, Inc. in 2002 by drilling seven exploratory test borings extending to depths of
about 13.5 to 40.5 feet below the then existing ground surface. Our current field
exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling seven additional borings,
designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 105, 107 and 108, extending to depths of about
21.5 and 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. Boring No. 106 was eliminated from
our field exploration work due to difficulties in accessing the boring location at the time of
our field exploration. The approximate locations of the borings drilled (from the 2002
exploration by Ernest K. Hirata & Associates, Inc. and our current field exploration) are
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
In general, our field exploration disclosed that the project site is underlain by alluvial
deposits, consisting of stiff to very stiff silty clays and clayey silts, interbedded with
coralline detritus materials, consisting of loose to medium dense silty gravel, extending to
the maximum depth explored of about 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface.
Groundwater was encountered in our drilled borings at depths ranging from about
1.3 to 15.2 feet below the existing ground surface during the course of our field
exploration. These depths generally correspond to Elevations +3.5 to +14.5 feet Mean
Sea Level (MSL). It should be noted that groundwater levels are subject to change due to
rainfall, seasonal precipitation, artesian conditions, surface water runoff, and other factors.
Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered from our current field exploration are
presented in Appendix A. Laboratory tests were performed on selected soil samples, and
the test results are presented in Appendix B.

FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in the borings, the principal
geotechnical considerations for the performance of the building foundation and
slab-on-grade of the project include proper subsurface drainage requirements to take into
consideration the shallow groundwater conditions at the site. Detailed discussion of our
geotechnical recommendations for the design of the under slab drainage system are
presented in the following sections of this letter report.
Under Slab Drainage System
Based on the foundation investigation report prepared by Ernest K. Hirata &
Associates, Inc., we understand that conventional spread footings and mat foundations
were recommended to support the new building planned at the project site. In addition,
the spread footings and mat foundation should be supported on 18 and 12 inches of
select granular fill, respectively. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 pounds
per square foot (psf) may be used in the design of the shallow foundation system as
described above. Based on our review of the construction plans, thickened concrete
slabs-on-grade at the building column locations and the wall footing locations were used
in the foundation design of the new building. The finished floor elevation of the building
(lowest floor level) was set at +16 feet MSL.
Based on the conditions exposed at the project site during construction, we
understand that groundwater was encountered at or above the finished subgrade
elevations of the project site during the foundation excavation work. A pond of
groundwater was observed within the excavations to achieve the finished subgrade
elevations at the project site. Due to the shallow groundwater conditions encountered
at the project site, it was desired to provide supplemental geotechnical
recommendations pertaining to the design of an under slab drainage system to mitigate
the potential adverse effects relating to the shallow groundwater conditions.
Based on the seven borings drilled at the building location and the ground
elevations provided, we estimate that the groundwater elevations encountered in the
borings drilled ranged from about +0.5 feet MSL to +14.5 feet MSL. The depths to the
groundwater levels and the corresponding elevations, as measured in the borings drilled
during the course of our field exploration, are summarized in the following table. The
groundwater surface generally slopes down from the northwest side of the building (high
groundwater elevation) to the southeast side (low groundwater elevation).

Based on the above information, we have set the bottom of the under slab
drainage layer at +12.5 feet MSL. It should be noted that the drainage trenches (about
Elevation +11.75 feet MSL) should be located approximately at the center between the
column grid lines.
The bottom of the drainage layer should be located at Elevation +12.5 feet MSL
or lower such that the granular fill below the bottom of footings are not low points in the
subsurface drainage system. In this manner, the trench drains oriented in a
mauka-makai direction (the short direction of the building) may be used to provide for

positive drainage of groundwater rising into the drainage layer. The portion of the
perforated pipes located within the building footprint may be flat; however, the
perforated pipes should be sloped at about 5 percent grades for the last 10 feet of the
pipe before tying into a collector drain pipe at the end.
It should be noted that the select granular fill below the footings will need to
consist of crushed rock granular fill (ASTM C 33, No. 67 gradation or similar materials)
to facilitate placement of the select granular fill in the wet. Because the groundwater
elevation within the building footprint may be as high as +14.5 feet MSL or higher,
performing earthwork in these conditions would not be practical without dewatering the
groundwater. Due to site constraints associated with disposal of the dewatered effluent,
we believe that placement of the crushed rock drainage fill layer may be performed in
the wet. For that reason, we recommend that two layers of non-woven filter fabric
(Mirafi 180N or equivalent) be provided on the excavated subgrade prior to the
placement of the crushed rock granular fill. A schematic of the Typical Under Slab
Drainage System Detail is shown on Plate 3 of this report.
The excavated subgrade should be relatively firm prior to placement of the
non-woven filter fabric. A representative from Geolabs should observe and/or probe the
subgrade prior to the placement of the non-woven filter fabric. In addition, care should
be exercised during the placement of the crushed rock granular fill to avoid tearing
and/or puncturing the non-woven filter fabric. As mentioned previously, the crushed
rock granular fill may be placed in the wet; however, the crushed rock granular fill
should be densified using a vibratory plate compactor or similar vibratory compaction
equipment at the top of the crushed rock granular fill a minimum of six passes.
LIMITATIONS
The findings and discussions submitted in this letter report are based, in part, upon
information obtained from the field borings. Variations of subsurface conditions between
and beyond the borings may occur, and the nature and extent of these variations may not
become evident until construction is underway. If variations then appear evident, it will be
necessary to re-evaluate the findings and discussions provided in this report.
The locations of the field borings indicated in this report are approximate, having
been estimated by taping and pacing from the building corners staked out in the field by
Hawaiian Dredging Construction Company. Elevations of the field borings were
surveyed by Hawaiian Dredging Construction Company and transmitted to our office on
April 5, 2004. The physical locations and elevations of the borings should be
considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used.
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict
the approximate boundaries between soil types and, as such, may denote a gradual
transition. Water level data from the borings were measured at the times shown on the
graphic representations and/or presented in the text of this report. These data have been
reviewed and interpretations made in the formulation of this report. However, it must be

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by others in 2002.
Our current field exploration consisted of drilling and sampling seven additional borings,
designated as Boring Nos. 101 through 105, 107 and 108, extending to depths of about
21.5 and 26.5 feet below the existing ground surface. The borings were drilled using
truck-mounted drilling equipment equipped with continuous flight augers. The
approximate locations of the borings drilled (previous and current field explorations) are
shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2.
The materials encountered in our borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist, who monitored the drilling operations on a
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System, as shown on Plate A. Graphic representations of the
materials encountered in our borings drilled are presented on the Logs of Borings,
Plates A-1 through A-7.
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling
of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer
falling 30 inches. In addition, some samples were obtained from the borings drilled in
general accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1586, Penetration Test and
Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration sampler
using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the sampler the
second and third 6inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.
Pocket Penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in
the field. The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined
compressive strength of the sample. Results of the pocket penetrometer tests are
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content (ASTM D 2216) and Unit Weight determinations (ASTM D 2937)
were performed on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of
soil properties. The results of these tests are presented on the logs of borings at the
appropriate sample depths.
Six one-inch Ring Swell Tests were performed on relatively undisturbed samples
of the clayey soils obtained from the current field exploration to evaluate the swelling
potential of the subsurface soils. The results of the tests are summarized on Plate B-1.
Two Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D 4318) were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits. The test results are summarized on the
Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentations of the test
results are provided on Plate B-2.
Two Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C 117 & C 136) were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil
classification. Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution of the two samples is
provided on Plate B-3.
Two unconfined Compression tests (ASTM D 2166) were performed on selected
soil samples to evaluate the unconfined compressive strength of the soil sample. Results
of the unconfined compression tests are presented on Plates B-4 and B-5.
Two Consolidation tests with time rates (ASTM D 2435) were performed on
samples of the potentially compressible soils to evaluate the compressibility
characteristics of the materials encountered. Results of the consolidation tests are
presented on Plates B-6 and B-7.
