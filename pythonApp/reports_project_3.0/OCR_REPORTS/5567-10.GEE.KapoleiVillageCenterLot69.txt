2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basalt and basaltic lavas from
the Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to
be middle to late Pliocene in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island
The younger Koolau Volcano is estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene
Ice Age in age and forms the majority of the eastern two thirds of the island As
volcanic activity in the Waianae Volcano ceased lava flows from the Koolau Volcano
banked against its eroded eastern slope forming a broad plateau known as the
Schofield Plateau
Physical and chemical weathering followed by erosion of this plateau generated
sediments which were transported to the coast In the project vicinity and to the south
these sediments accumulated and interbedded with marine sediments and coral algal
reef formations to form a sedimentary wedge The thickness of the sedimentary wedge
ranges from zero in the area of the Interstate Route H 1 Highway to over 1 000 feet at
Ewa Beach This wedge forms the Ewa Plain and serves as the confining formation or
caprock over the artesian basal aquifers of southern Oahu Deposition of sediments
has continued from earlier geologic time through the present Agricultural development
within the last 100 years has brought the project area to its present form

SECTION 3 0 DISCUSSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on our field exploration the Kapolei Village Center site is generally
underlain by medium stiff to very stiff clay and loose to very dense coralline deposits
Our laboratory tests indicate that the near surface clayey soils exhibit high shrink swell
characteristics when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents Therefore
special attention should be given to the site preparation recommendations for the site
grading design to account for the highly expansive soil conditions
In order to provide an economical source of select granular fill material for the
mass grading operations we believe that it would be feasible to exploit the coralline
materials encountered in the southern portion of the site by excavating a series of
borrow pits The excavated coralline material may then be used to cap the expansive
soils present in the remaining areas
The resulting borrow pits may be utilized as retention basins during construction
and may be backfilled only when required to develop the borrow pit areas Backfilling
could be accomplished with less expensive imported materials or the borrow pits may
be used to dispose surplus expansive clay soil from grading operations in the northern
portion of the site Special considerations will be required for backfilling if borrow areas
are used for expansive soil disposal
Field observations during our exploration indicated that shrinkage cracks have
developed on the existing ground surface across the project site The existing shrinkage
cracks if left unattended may increase further in size under prolonged dry weather
conditions Shrinkage cracks adjacent to the building foundations are potentially
detrimental since water can readily infiltrate into the subsurface through the crack
openings and soften the subsoils around the building foundations thus affecting their
performance Therefore we recommend scarifying the existing ground
moisture conditioning and compacting again during the site preparation operations

3 1 Site Preparation
Our field observations indicated substantial shrinkage cracks within the surface
clay soils at the existing ground surface In general shrinkage cracks start to develop in
the ground due to the lack of maintenance of the soil moisture content as the soils start
to dry out under the sun and dry weather conditions The development of shrinkage
cracks in the ground was apparently intensified by the prolonged dry climatic conditions
in the Kapolei area resulting in large fluctuations of the soil moisture content
Based on our field observations shrinkage cracks up to about 1 inch wide and
12 to 18 inches deep were observed Therefore we recommend scarifying the existing
ground surface a minimum depth of 12 inches moisture conditioning to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture content and compacting to at least 90 percent
relative compaction prior to commencement of the site work grading operations to
remove the zone with shrinkage cracks

Site Preparation
At the on set of earthwork areas within the contract grading limits should be
cleared and grubbed thoroughly Vegetation debris deleterious and other
unsuitable materials should be removed and disposed properly off site or
stockpiled in a designated area to reduce the potential for contamination of the
excavated materials
Soft and yielding areas encountered during clearing and grubbing should be
over excavated to expose firm natural material and the resulting excavation should
be backfilled with well compacted engineered fill The excavated soft and or organic
soils should be properly disposed off site and or used in landscape areas where
appropriate Contract documents should include additive and deductive unit prices
for over excavation and compacted fill placement to account for variations in the
over excavation quantities
After clearing and grubbing the existing ground surface should be scarified to a
minimum depth of about 12 inches moisture conditioned to at least 4 percent
above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 85 percent relative
compaction For pavement subgrades the minimum relative compaction should be
95 percent Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed
as a percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in
accordance with ASTM D 1557 Where shrinkage cracks are observed after
compaction of the subgrade we recommend thoroughly moistening the soils to
close all cracks or the subgrade should be scarified moisture conditioned and

Fills and Backfills
The fill materials within 3 feet below finish subgrades structural fill should consist
of non expansive select granular material such as crushed coral basalt gravel or
cinder sand Structural fill material is not required for subgrade consisting of
coralline materials The fill material should be well graded from coarse to fine with
particles no larger than 3inches in largest dimension It should also contain
between 10 and 30 percent particles passing the No 200 sieve The material
should have a laboratory CBR value of 20 or more and should have a maximum
swell of less than 1 percent when tested in accordance with ASTM D 1883
Free draining materials and highly permeable materials are not recommended for
use as the non expansive select granular fill The fill material should also be free
of vegetation and deleterious materials In general the on site coralline material
should be suitable for this purpose
The on site cut materials generated from excavations into the underlying coral rock
formation may be used as general fill below 3 foot structural fill materials provided
that they are screened of the over sized materials greater than 6 inches in large
dimension and processed to contain sufficient fines to produce a well graded
material This requirement is to facilitate future utility trench excavations Over size
rock fragments may be disposed of in deep fill areas or broken down to

Fill Placement and Compaction Requirements
Fills and backfills should be moisture conditioned to about 2 percent above the
optimum moisture placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to at least 90 percent relative compaction
The compaction requirement for the non expansive select granular fill under the
roadways and parking area within 2 feet from finish grade should be increased to a
minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a
percentage of the maximum dry density of the same soil established in accordance
with ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry
weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
Compaction should be accomplished by sheepsfoot rollers vibratory rollers
multiple wheel pneumatic tired rollers or other types of acceptable compaction
equipment

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture Content ASTM D 2216 determinations were performed on selected soil
samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties The test results
are presented on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths
Two one inch Ring Swell tests were performed on remolded samples to evaluate
the swelling potential of the near surface soils The test results are summarized on
Plate B 1
Two Atterberg Limits tests ASTM D 4318 were performed on selected soil
samples to evaluate their liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Test Pits at the appropriate sample depths
Graphic presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 2
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 3 through B 5
