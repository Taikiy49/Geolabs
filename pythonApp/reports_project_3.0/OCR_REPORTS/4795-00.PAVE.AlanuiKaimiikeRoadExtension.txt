SECTION 1 0 GENERAL
1 1 Introduction
This pavement justification report presents the results of our analyses for the
design of new pavements for the Alanui Ka im ike Road Extension project located in the
Kaunakakai area on the Island of Molokai Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the
project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and presents our
geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our analyses for the design of
pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein are subject to the
limitations noted at the end of this report
1 2 Project Considerations
The project site is located in the Kaunakakai area on the Island of Molokai Hawaii
We understand that it is planned to extend Alanui Ka im ike Road beginning from
approximately 363 feet from the intersection between Kamehameha V Highway and
Alanui Ka im ike Road The roadway will extend toward the northwest to a dead end at
the intersection between Kakalahale Street and Kalohi Street The approximate location
of the new roadway extension alignment is shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2
Based on the information provided the roadway extension will be approximately
1 300 feet long and 36 feet wide with one traffic lane in each direction We understand
that fills up to about 7 feet high and minor cuts on the order of about 1 foot thick will be
required to achieve the design finished grades for the roadway construction

2 1 Regional Geology
The Island of Molokai was built during the early to middle Pleistocene Epoch by
basaltic lava flows from two shield volcanoes East Molokai Mountain and West Molokai
Mountain The project site is located on the Hoolehua Plain between the East and West
Molokai Domes The Hoolehua Plain or isthmus of Molokai is composed of lava flows
from the East Molokai Volcano banking against the older West Molokai Volcano
During the Pleistocene Epoch Ice Age there were many sea level changes as a
result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world As the great
continental glaciers accumulated the level of the ocean fell since there was less water
available to fill the oceanic basins Conversely as the glaciers receded or melted global
sea levels rose because more water was available The land mass of Molokai remained
essentially stable during these changes and the fluctuations were eustatic in nature
These glacio eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea which were both higher
and lower relative to the present sea level on Molokai
The lower sea stands caused streams to carve valleys in the sediments and reef
deposits Subaerial exposure of the sediments and calcareous materials caused
consolidation of the soft deltaic materials and lagoonal deposits and induration of the
calcareous reef materials In addition renewed subaerial erosion of the upper areas of the
volcanic dome deposited terrigenous alluvial soils under relatively high energy conditions
The higher sea level stands caused the accumulation of deltas and fans of terrigenous
sediments at correspondingly higher elevations in areas protected by fringing reefs and
beach stands During periods of no significant sea level changes continued stream action
extended the alluvial deltas and fans seaward
2 2 Climatic Conditions
The Island of Molokai is located in the tropics in the Pacific Ocean Molokai is
characterized by mild temperatures throughout the year moderate humidity persistent

northeasterly trade winds significant differences in rainfall within short distances and
infrequent severe storms The temperatures on the Island of Molokai typically range
between 60 and 85 degrees Fahrenheit
The annual rainfall on the Island of Molokai averages about 20 to 30 inches The
rainfall may be as much as fifteen times the average annual rainfall over the upper
mountain slopes and crests and may be less than one third the average annual rainfall in
the lowland areas The rainfall is usually seasonal and the summers are dry In the
wetter regions the rainfall comes from both winter storms and trade wind showers
The project site is located in the Kaunakakai area on the Island of Molokai Hawaii
The average annual rainfall near the project site ranges between 20 and 25 inches The
precipitation at the project site may be considered moderate

3 1 Methodology of Pavement Design
The procedures used in determining the new pavement sections generally
follow those described in Chapter 3 of the Pavement Design Manual dated March 2002
The pavement design manual was prepared by the State of Hawaii Department of
Transportation Highways Division Material Testing and Research Branch In general the
design procedures for flexible pavements are based on the Hveem Stabilometer method
developed by the California Department of Transportation Caltrans The design
procedures for rigid pavements are generally based on the design procedures described in
the Portland Cement Association article entitled Thickness Design for Concrete Highway
and Street Pavements

Preparation Below Pavement Section
Based on the grading plans we understand that the majority of the roadway
extension will be in a fill condition Fills on the order of about 1 to 7 feet thick will be
required for construction of the roadway extension In general the area within the contract
grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and grubbed at the on set of earthwork
Vegetation debris deleterious material and other unsuitable materials should be removed
and disposed of properly off site or disposed of in a designated area to reduce the
potential for contamination of the excavated materials Soft and or yielding areas
encountered during clearing and grubbing below areas designated to receive fill or future
improvements should be over excavated to expose firm or dense materials The resulting
excavation should be backfilled with aggregate subbase materials The excavated soft
and or organic soils should be properly disposed of off site
After clearing and grubbing the pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a
depth of at least 8inches moisture conditioned to 2 percent above the optimum
moisture content and compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction
Relative compaction refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage
of the maximum dry density of the same soil determined in accordance with AASHTO
Test Method T 180 ASTM D 1557 Optimum moisture is the water content
percentage by dry weight corresponding to the maximum dry density
If the subgrade soils are pumping during subgrade preparation the pumping
subgrade may be stabilized by cement treatment to reduce the potential for pumping
subgrade conditions As a guide stabilization by cement treatment may consist of one
sack of cement for approximately 25 square feet of subgrade area Geolabs should be
contacted to evaluate the pumping subgrade conditions and to evaluate whether the
pavement sections need to be revised and or modified based on the exposed subgrade
conditions
Where shrinkage cracks are noted after compaction of the subgrade we
recommend that the soils be scarified and re prepared as recommended above

In general the excavated on site materials may be re used as a source of general
fill material Imported fill materials if required should consist of low expansive select

3 7 Compaction Requirements
Fill and backfill materials should be moisture conditioned to 2 percent above the
optimum moisture content placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose thickness
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction The compaction
requirement for fills placed within the pavement areas should be increased to a minimum
of 95 percent relative compaction

3 8 Pavement Drainage
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to
saturation of the base subbase and or subgrade soils Therefore the pavement surface
should be sloped and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off
the pavement to appropriate drainage structures Surface water ponding should not be
allowed on the site during or after construction
lf a permeable base layer will be provided in the pavement section we envision that
an edge drain will be needed to collect and daylight the infiltrated water into the pavement
structural section from the untreated permeable base layer The edge drain should consist
of a 6 inch PVC perforated pipe with perforations facing down surrounded by an
untreated permeable base A permeable separator non woven filter fabric conforming to
Section 648 of the HSS should be placed around the untreated permeable base to reduce
the potential for migration of fines into the drainage material In addition a permeable
separator should also be placed between the untreated permeable base and aggregate
subbase Care should be exercised when placing the subbase base and or construction
of the surface course to avoid damaging the edge drain collector pipe The edge drains
should be daylighted into appropriate drainage structures for proper discharge

pits designated as Test Pit Nos 1 through 6 extending to depths of about 3 to 10 feet
below the existing ground surface The approximate locations of the excavated test pits
for the project are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 1 and 2 2 The test pits were
excavated using a rubber tire back hoe
The materials encountered in the test pits were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our geologist who monitored the test pit operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Detailed descriptions of the materials encountered are
presented on the Logs of Test Pits Plates A 1 and A 2
