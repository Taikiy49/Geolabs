Dear Mr. Winebrenner: 
As requested, Geolabs, Inc. provided geotechnical engineering observation 
services during the drilled shaft construction in support of the Wind Turbine Generator 
(WTG) tower base stabilization for the above project. This letter report summarizes the 
results of our field observations during the drilled shaft construction. 
In accordance with the executed “Design Subconsulting Agreement,” dated 
December 10, 2018, with Condon-Johnson & Associates, Inc., we observed the 
installation of 126 drilled shafts for wind turbine generators WTG-3, WTG-7, and 
WTG-11 at the Kawailoa Wind Farm complex in Haleiwa on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii.  
The project location and general vicinity are shown on the Project Location Map, Plate 
1. 
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
The Kawailoa Wind Farm is located approximately 5 miles east-northeast of 
Haleiwa Town on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. The windmill generators are arrayed along 
the upper foothills on the northwest flank of the Koolau mountain range. Ground 
elevations at the site generally range from approximately +500 to +1,200 feet Mean Sea 
Level (MSL). The subsurface soils encountered at the project site generally consisted of 
reworked residual soils and saprolites. 
We understand that the intent of the project construction is to improve the 
stability of the above WTG tower foundations. The improvements include the 
construction of six subsurface buttresses/secant walls which are referred to as spokes. 
These spokes are uniformly spaced in a radial array around the base of each WTG

DRILLED SHAFT INSTALLATION 
Condon-Johnson & Associates, Inc. was the general contractor and Hawaiian 
Dredging Construction Company performed the drilled shaft installation services for the 
project. Overall, the contractor installed a total of 108, 3-foot diameter drilled shafts and 
18, 6-foot diameter drilled shafts for the stabilization of WTG-3, WTG-7, and WTG-11, 
from January 22 through April 17, 2019 at the wind turbine locations shown on the Site 
Plan, Plate 2, and the Typical Foundation Plan, Plate 3. The as-built diameters and the 
lengths of the drilled shafts installed for the project are summarized on the Drilled Shaft 
Installation Summary, Plates 4.1 through 4.6. Representatives from Geolabs observed 
the drilled shaft excavation, placement of reinforcing cage, and concrete operations on 
a near-continuous (full-time) basis during the installation of the drilled shafts. 
The drilled shafts directly in contact with the tower base (Shafts A1 through F1) 
and the larger drilled shafts at the far end of each spoke (Shafts A7 through F7) were 
reinforced with rebar cages and filled with a 4,000 pounds per square inch (psi) 
concrete mix. All the intermediate drilled shafts numbered 2 through 6 are unreinforced 
and generally filled with a 1,000 psi lean concrete mix. 
In general, the drilled shafts were installed using three different drill rigs including 
a SOILMEC R-625, an IMT rotary drill mounted on a CAT AF-160 tractor, and a Bauer 
BG-24H. These rigs were equipped with 36-inch and 72-inch diameter augers, digging 
buckets, and clean-out buckets.  
After the drilled holes were extended to the required depths and cleaned out, a 
Geolabs representative observed the bottom of the excavated shafts to determine that 
the bottom was relatively clean and free of debris from the drilling operation. Loose 
materials at the bottom were cleaned out with the appropriate tools prior to the concrete 
pour. 
Upon completion of the drilled shaft excavation and prior to concreting, 
pre-fabricated rebar cages equipped with sled-type PVC centralizers were aligned and 
lowered into the drilled shafts located at the inner and outer ends of each spoke (Drilled 
Shafts numbered 1 or 7). The cages were suspended in place with wires supported on 
wooden beams. No rebar cages were installed for the intermediate drilled shafts (Drilled 
Shaft numbered 2 through 6). 
Concrete was supplied and delivered on-site by HC&D, LLC. The concrete 
supplied for the reinforced drilled shafts generally had a slump ranging from about 7 to 9 
inches with a 28-day design compressive strength of 4,000 psi. Concrete supplied for 
the intermediate drilled shafts generally had a slump of about 5.5 to 8.5 inches and a
