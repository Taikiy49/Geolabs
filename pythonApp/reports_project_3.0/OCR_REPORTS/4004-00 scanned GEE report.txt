SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Based on site observations and our field exploration we
believe there is a global slope stability issue at the project site
that the Owner should be made aware The proposed Goto
residential site is located in an area where there is evidence
of previous ground movement In addition the driving forces
of ground movement at the project site appear to be beyond
the limits of the property boundary and therefore beyond the
control of the Owner of the subject site
Based on the global stability issue at the site we believe that
construction at the project site will likely be subjected to
subsequent ground movement and inherent risks Therefore
it may be prudent for the Owner not to build at the project site
However if the Owner chooses to proceed with construction
at this site the Owner should be aware of the inherent risks
associated with construction on a site with previous ground
movements The constructed residential structure will likely
be subjected to ground movements in the future and some
distress to the structure within the life of the residence
It should be emphasized that the recommendations provided
herein are to help in reducing some of the effects of ground
movements on the residential structure only Our
recommendations are not directed at stabilizing the hillside or
reducing the slope movements that may occur at the site
because the global stability issue affects more than this lot
and extends beyond the limits of this property
In general it is our opinion that a shallow foundation system
consisting of continuous footings structurally connected to
provide some rigidity to accommodate some amount of
differential movements may be used to support the proposed
new residence An allowable bearing pressure of up to

INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed Goto Residence at 992 Hao Street in the Aina Haina area on
the island of Oahu Hawaii The general location and vicinity of the project site are shown
on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes the findings from our field exploration and laboratory
testing and presents our geotechnical engineering recommendations derived from our
analyses for the proposed project These recommendations are intended for design input
only and are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling two
borings to a depth of approximately 20 feet below the existing ground surface using a
portable drill rig The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Engineer who monitored the drilling and sampling
operations on a near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed
visually and by testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance
with the Unified Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Logs of the materials
encountered in the borings are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 and A 2
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
Penetration sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches The blow counts
needed to drive the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths
Core samples of rock materials encountered at the site were obtained by using
diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM Standard Practice
D 2113 83 Diamond Core Drilling for Site Investigation Core drilling is a rotary drilling
method that uses a hollow bit to cut into the rock formation The rock material left in the
hollow core of the bit is mechanically recovered for examination and description
Recovery REC is used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative
quality of rock masses Recovery is defined as the actual length of material recovered
from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt If 3 7 feet of material is
recovered from a 5 0 foot core run the recovery would be 74 percent and would be
shown on the Logs of Borings as REC 74

The Rock Quality Designation RQD is also a subjective guide to the relative
quality of rock masses RQD is defined as the percentage of the core run that is sound
material in excess of 4 inches in length without any discontinuities discounting any drilling
induced fractures or breaks If 2 5 feet of sound material is recovered from a 5 0 foot
core run the RQD would be 50 percent and would be shown on the Logs of Borings as
RQD 50 Generally the following is used to describe the relative quality of the rock
based on the Practical Handbook of Physical Properties of Rocks and Minerals
The rippability of a rock mass is a function of the relative hardness of the rock its
relative quality brittleness and fissile characteristics A dense basalt with a high RQD
would be very difficult to rip and would probably require more arduous methods of
excavation

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
One Atterberg Limits test ASTM D 4318 was performed on a selected sample of
the soils to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification The test
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depth Graphic
presentation of the test results is provided on Plate B 1
One sieve analysis test ASTM C 136 was performed on a selected sample of the
soils to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil classification
Results of the test are presented on Plate B 2
