SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration indicates that the project site is generally covered with a thin 
surficial fill layer underlain by a thick deposit of very loose lagoonal material.  The lagoonal 
deposit was underlain by coralline materials with alluvial soils at greater depths extending 
to the maximum depth explored of about 64.5 feet below the existing ground surface.  We 
encountered groundwater in the borings at 4 and 4.2 feet below the existing ground surface 
corresponding to elevations of +1.5 and +1.3 feet MSL, respectively. 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered and our liquefaction analysis, it is 
likely the underlying very loose lagoonal soils will have a factor of safety of less than 1.0 
against liquefaction based on the design earthquake.  Based on the potentially liquefiable 
soils at the project site and structural loads provided, we recommend supporting the Spider 
Monkey Exhibit and Bird Holding structures on a deep foundation system consisting of 
micropiles.  The micropiles should extend through the loose lagoonal deposit and into the 
underlying coralline materials. We recommend designing each micropile based on an 
allowable compressive load capacity of 10 kips. To achieve the allowable compressive load 
capacity with a factor of safety of 2.0, we estimate that each micropile would consist of a 
minimum grout bulb diameter of 5.5 inches and would need to be embedded to a depth of 
about 40 feet below bottom of pile cap.  The micropile length is based on a minimum 5-foot 
long load bearing grout bulb (bonded zone) developed within the coralline materials with 
35 feet of permanent casing (unbonded zone) within the lagoonal deposit. 
We understand that the use of a micropile foundation system to support the new 
structures during a moderate seismic event may not be desired and the risk of potential 
damage to structures supported on a shallow foundation in the event of liquefaction may be 
acceptable.  Therefore, shallow foundation recommendations for non-liquefaction condition 
is provided. 
Based on the above and the anticipated relatively light structural loading, we believe 
that the new structures may be supported on shallow foundations consisting of spread 
and/or continuous strip footings. An allowable bearing pressure of up to 2,000 psf may be 
used for the design of footings bearing on the re-compacted on-site materials (minimum 
90 percent relative compaction). It should be noted that soft and/or loose subgrade soils 
may be encountered at the footing subgrades due to the relatively low elevation of the 
finished grades and groundwater near the ground surface. If soft/loose soils are 
encountered at the footing bottom, a stabilization layer (minimum 18 inches thick) should be 
provided below the footings to provide uniform support of the foundations. The stabilization 
layer also should extend a minimum of 18 inches laterally beyond the edges of the footing.

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of the two 
extinct Waianae and Koolau shield volcanoes. The older Waianae Volcano forms the bulk 
of the western third of the island while the younger Koolau Volcano forms the majority of 
the eastern two-thirds of the island. It is believed that the Waianae Volcano became 
extinct while the Koolau Volcano was still active, and its eastern flank is partially buried 
below Koolau lavas in Central Oahu. 
The project site is on the southern flank of Koolau Volcano, and its geomorphology 
and subsurface conditions are directly related to the glacio-eustatic fluctuations of the sea 
level during the Pleistocene Epoch and the genesis of the Honolulu Coastal Plain. The 
subsurface conditions in the Waikiki area are dominated by fossil Pleistocene Age 
coral/algal reef deposits and related deposits laid during high stands of the sea, which 
are intercalated with terrigenous sediments. The base of the stratigraphic section in this 
area is Koolau Basalt. 
During the later Pleistocene Epoch (Ice Age), many sea level changes occurred 
as a result of widespread glaciation in the continental areas of the world. These glacio 
eustatic fluctuations resulted in stands of the sea that were both higher and lower relative 
to the present sea level on Oahu. About 15,000 years ago, a relatively rapid rise in sea 
level occurred. During that rise, the valleys in the project area were drowned. In the last 
10,000 years or so, the sea level has adjusted to its present stand. Terrigenous and 
marine sediments have continued to accumulate in low-energy estuarine or lagoonal 
environments resulting in thick deposits of loose and/or soft sediments along the coast in 
the areas that were formerly valley and drainageways. 
In the early part of this century, the Waikiki area consisted of low, marshy areas. 
As the City of Honolulu grew and the Waikiki area urbanized, man-made fills were placed 
to reclaim the marshy areas and lagoons. The fill was placed upon lagoonal deposits 
consisting of silty sands with coral fragments. The lower clayey materials were deposited 
during periodic influxes of non-marine alluvial debris, which apparently smothered the 
coral reef development. Land development and reclamation projects within the last

Seismic Design Considerations 
Based on the International Building Code, 2006 Edition (IBC 2006), the project site 
may be subject to seismic activity and seismic design considerations will need to be 
addressed. The following subsections provide discussions on the seismicity and soil 
profile type for seismic design. 
2.4.1 Earthquakes and Seismicity 
In general, earthquakes throughout the world are caused by shifts in the tectonic 
plates. In contrast, earthquake activity in Hawaii is primarily linked to volcanic activity; 
therefore, earthquake activity in Hawaii generally occurs before or during volcanic 
eruptions. In addition, earthquakes may result from the underground movement of 
magma that comes close to the surface but does not erupt. The Island of Hawaii 
experiences thousands of earthquakes each year, but most are so small that they 
can only be detected by sensitive instruments. However, some of the earthquakes 
are strong enough to be felt, and a few cause minor to moderate damage. 
In general, earthquakes associated with volcanic activity are most common on the 
Island of Hawaii. Earthquakes that are directly associated with the movement of 
magma are concentrated beneath the active Kilauea and Mauna Loa Volcanoes on 
the Island of Hawaii. Because majority of the earthquakes in Hawaii (over 90 percent) 
are related to volcanic activity, the risk of seismic activity and degree of ground 
shaking diminishes with increased distance from the Island of Hawaii. The Island of

3.1 
Micropile Foundations 
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered at the project site and our 
analysis, it is likely the loose, granular lagoonal soils will have a factor of safety of less 
than 1.0 against liquefaction based on the design earthquake. Based on the potentially 
liquefiable soil at the project site and structural loads provided, we recommend supporting 
the Spider Monkey Exhibit and Bird Holding structures on a deep foundation system 
consisting of micropiles. 
A micropile consists of a small diameter (usually less than 12 inches) drilled and 
grouted pile with steel reinforcing. The micropile foundation typically is constructed by 
drilling a borehole, placing reinforcing steel in the hole, and grouting the borehole. 
Micropiles are desirable because they can be installed readily in access restrictive

permanent casing (unbonded zone) within the lagoonal deposits.

3.1.1 Downdrag Loads 
In the event of seismically induced liquefaction, settlement of the compressible 
deposits will occur at the project site. When the loose soil deposits consolidate, or 
move downward relative to the micropiles, the consolidation or downward 
movement will create negative skin friction forces, generally known as downdrag 
forces, acting on the micropile shafts. The magnitude of the downdrag force

depends on the thickness of the liquefiable layer, relative density of the potentially 
liquefiable deposits, and the micropile size. 
The allowable compressive load capacity of the micropiles has been reduced by 
the expected downdrag loads in the micropile foundation analyses. Based on our 
foundation analyses, we estimate the downdrag load imposed on the micropiles 
due to settlement of the very loose lagoonal deposits may be on the order of about 
1 kip for the 5.5-inch diameter micropiles. 
In addition, placement of fill materials required to achieve design grades also may 
induce settlement of loose compressible deposits. The ground settlement would 
result in downdrag forces acting on the micropile shaft supporting the new 
structures. If additional fill is required to achieve design grades, the micropile 
recommendations presented herein should be re-evaluated. 
3.1.2 Uplift Load Resistance 
Uplift loads imposed on the micropiles may be resisted by shear along the grout 
bulb (bonded zone) and the adjacent soils.  In general, an uplift load capacity of 
up to 10 kips per pile may be achieved with a bonded zone extending a minimum 
of 5 feet below the bottom of casing.  The uplift load capacity provided is intended 
for transient load applications only.  Therefore, the uplift load capacity should not 
be further increased for transient load applications.  For sustained uplift loads, we 
recommend reducing the uplift load capacity provided above by 50 percent to resist 
sustained uplift loads.  The capacity of the pile member in tension should be 
checked by the project structural engineer. 
3.1.3 Lateral Load Resistance 
It should be noted that the micropile foundation system provides low lateral load 
resistance due to the relatively small diameter of the micropile.  Therefore, the 
lateral load resistance contributed by the vertical micropiles should be neglected.  
Lateral loads imposed on the new structure foundations may be resisted primarily 
by passive earth pressure resistance against the vertical faces of the pile cap and 
grade beams and by shear acting along the sides of the pile caps and grade 
beams.

Settlements of the micropile foundations will result primarily from elastic

settlement of the micropile foundations to be less than 0.5 inches.  We believe that 
these settlements are essentially elastic and should occur as the loads are applied. 
3.1.5 Micropile Load Test Program 
It should be noted that the micropile load capacity is highly dependent on the 
drilling procedures and the grouting methods employed by the contractor to install 
the micropile.  Therefore, the micropile load capacity may vary considerably 
between different contractors and micropile foundation systems.  In order to 
determine whether the contractor’s methods of micropile installation are adequate 
and to determine the ultimate load capacity, we recommend performing one 
pre-production load test. In general, the purpose of the pre-production load test on 
a sacrificial micropile is to fulfill the following objectives: 
To examine the adequacy of the methods and equipment proposed 
by the contractor to install the micropiles to the depths required 
To assess the contractor’s method of drilling and grout injection 
To develop criteria for determination of the bonded zone and 
evaluate the ultimate bonded stress 
In general, the pre-production load test should be performed in accordance with 
ASTM D1143. Based on experience, we believe the load test should be conducted 
no earlier than 7 days after completion of the micropile installation to allow the 
grout adequate time to cure. Two additional micropiles may be used for reaction

3.4.4 Drainage 
The backfill behind the wall structure should be well-drained to reduce the build-up 
of hydrostatic pressures. A typical drainage system would consist of a 12-inch wide 
zone of permeable material, such as open-graded gravel (ASTM C33, No. 67

connected to a perforated pipe at the base of the wall.

potential for water infiltration into the footing subgrade. The relatively impervious 
material should be compacted to no less than 90 percent relative compaction.

the potential for formation of voids below the haunches of pipes and to provide adequate 
support for the sides of the pipes. Improper backfill material around the pipes and 
improper placement of the backfill could result in settlement of the backfill and damage to 
the pipes.  Where groundwater is encountered, the bedding should be wrapped on all 
sides by non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent). 
We envision that soft and/or loose lagoonal deposits may be encountered at or 
near the invert elevations along some of the utility lines planned for the project.  If soft 
and/or loose soils are encountered, we recommend that a subgrade stabilization layer 
consisting of 24 inches of No. 2 Coarse Aggregate (ASTM C 33, No. 4 gradation) wrapped 
in a non-woven filter fabric (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) be provided below the bedding 
layer for uniform support.  The stabilization layer should also extend beyond the sides of 
the pipe a minimum width of one fourth the outside diameter of the pipe or 12 inches, 
whichever is greater. 
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to 
the finished subgrade may consist of approved on-site soils or select granular fill material. 
The select granular fill and/or on-site backfill materials should be moisture-conditioned to 
above the optimum moisture content, placed in level lifts not exceeding 8 inches in loose 
thickness, and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction to reduce the 
potential for appreciable future ground subsidence. The finished subgrade of the trench 
backfill below the pavement grade should be compacted to no less than 95 percent 
relative compaction.

Corrosion Potential 
Design of metallic substructures, such as electrical conduits or other metallic 
piping, should consider the effects of the corrosive environment on the substructure.  
Resistivity is generally recognized as one of the most significant soil characteristics

regarding the corrosivity of the soil to buried metallic objects. In general, the lower the 
resistivity, the greater the potential for corrosion of the buried metallic structure. 
Conversely, the higher the resistivity, the less likely the soil will contribute to corrosion of 
metallic objects.  
A set of corrosivity testing, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity (ASTM 
G57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), was performed 
by our office and TestAmerica Laboratories, Inc. on a selected soil sample obtained from 
our field exploration. A summary of the corrosivity tests is presented in Appendix B, 
Plate B-6.  
Based on the results of corrosivity laboratory testing, the subsurface soils at the 
project site exhibit a minimum resistivity value of approximately 2,900 ohm-cm, 
corresponding to a corrosion rating of 2 (very corrosive) to buried metallic structures in 
accordance with Board of Water Supply of Honolulu Water System External Corrosion 
Control Standards, 1991. Therefore, metallic substructures (such as piping) for protection 
against the potential for corrosion should be properly designed.

Field Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and sampling 
two exploratory borings, designated as Boring Nos. 1 and 2, extending to depths of about 
64.5 and 60 feet below the existing ground surface, respectively. The borings were drilled 
with a portable drill rig equipped with 4-inch OD solid-stem augers and HQ coring tools. The 
borings were drilled at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan, Plate 2. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and 
textural examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice 
for Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by testing 
in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, Standard 
Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil Classification 
System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the soil 
classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described on the Soil Classification Log 
Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on 
the Logs of Borings, Plates A-1.1 through A-2.2. 
Some soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with 
ASTM D1586, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch O.D. 
standard penetration sampler using a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, 
some relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch O.D. modified 
California sampler with the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the Logs of Borings indicates the number of blows required for the 
specific sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the 
Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Core samples of the rock materials encountered at the project site were obtained by 
using diamond core drilling techniques in general accordance with ASTM D2113, Diamond 
Core Drilling for Site Investigation. Core drilling is a rotary drilling method that uses a hollow 
bit to cut into the rock formation. The rock material left in the hollow core of the bit is 
mechanically recovered for examination and description. Rock cores were described in 
general accordance with the Rock Description System, as shown on the Rock Log Legend, 
Plate A-0.3. The Rock Description System is based on the publication “Suggested Methods 
for the Quantitative Description of Discontinuities in Rock Masses” by the International 
Society for Rock Mechanics (March 1977). 
Core Recovery may be used as a subjective guide to the interpretation of the relative 
quality of rock masses, where appropriate. Core Recovery is defined as the actual length of 
material recovered from a coring attempt versus the length of the core attempt.  For

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D7263) determinations 
were performed on selected soil and samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation 
of soil properties. The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate 
sample depths. 
Five Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil 
samples to evaluate its liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test 
results are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths, and 
graphic presentation of the test results are provided on Plate B-1. 
Four sieve analysis tests (ASTM C117 & ASTM C136) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification.  Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate B-2. 
Two Direct Shear tests (ASTM D3080) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the shear strength characteristics of the materials tested. The test results are 
presented on Plates B-3 and B-4. 
Four Uniaxial Compression tests (ASTM D7012 Method C) were performed to 
evaluate the uniaxial compressive strength of the coral and sandstone formations 
encountered. The test results are shown on the Log of Boring at the appropriate sample 
depth. The test results are presented on Plate B-5. 
One set of Corrosion tests, including pH (ASTM G51), Minimum Resistivity 
(ASTM G 57), Chloride Content (EPA 300.0), and Sulfate Content (EPA 300.0), was 
performed by our office and TestAmerica Analytical Testing Corporation on a selected 
soil sample obtained from our field exploration. The test results are summarized on 
Plate B-6.
