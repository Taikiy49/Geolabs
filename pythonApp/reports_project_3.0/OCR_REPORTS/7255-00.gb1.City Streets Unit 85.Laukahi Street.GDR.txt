PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS 
 The project is at Laukahi Street in Honolulu on the Island of Oahu, Hawaii. We understand 
it is proposed to rehabilitate/reconstruct Laukahi Street from the intersection with Kalanianaole 
Highway to the vicinity of the security gate for Waialae Iki Subdivision.  A total of approximately 
8,700 linear feet of roadway will be rehabilitated or reconstructed.  Laukahi Street is a two-lane 
roadway with grades of generally moderate to steep inclination. 
Borings and corings were drilled to determine the existing pavement thickness and 
composition and to investigate subgrade conditions for the design of pavement 
rehabilitation/reconstruction options. The design of the pavement rehabilition/reconstruction 
will be done by others.  An Overall Site Plan showing the boring and coring locations is presented 
on Plate 2. Site Plans presenting individual boring locations are provided as Plates 3.1 
through 3.9.

REGIONAL GEOLOGY 
The Island of Oahu was built by the extrusion of basaltic lavas from the Waianae and 
Koolau shield volcanoes.  The older Waianae Volcano is estimated to be middle to late Pliocene 
in age and forms the bulk of the western third of the island.  The younger Koolau Volcano is 
estimated to be late Pliocene to early Pleistocene (Ice Age) and forms the majority of the 
eastern two-thirds of the island.  Waianae became extinct while Koolau was still active, and its 
eastern flank was partially buried below Koolau lavas banking against its eastern flank. 
The project area is on the southern flank of Koolau and is composed of basaltic rock 
built by the extrusion of the lavas of the Koolau Volcanic Series.  These rocks are generally 
characterized by flows of jointed, dense vesicular basalt with interbedded thin clinker layers.  
For the last 1 to 2 million years, in-situ chemical weathering of the Koolau lava flows has 
occurred, forming a mantle of residual soils, which grade to saprolite with depth.  In general, 
saprolite is composed mainly of silty material and is typical of the tropical weathering of 
volcanic rocks.  The saprolite grades to basaltic rock formation with depth.  Grading operations 
for the developments in the area have brought the site to its present form. 
SOIL SAMPLING AND FIELD TESTING 
The borings and corings were drilled using truck-mounted drill rig equipped with 
continuous flight augers and coring equipment. Our geologist classified the materials 
encountered in the borings by visual and textural examination in the field and observed the 
drilling operations on a near-continuous basis. These classifications were further reviewed by 
visual observation and laboratory testing. Soils were classified in general accordance with 
ASTM D2487, Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Rock encountered in the 
borings/corings was described in general accordance with the Rock Description System, as 
shown on the Rock Log Legend, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials 
encountered in the drilled borings are presented on the Logs of Borings and Corings, Plates A-1 
through A-18.  
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings/corings in general accordance with 
ASTM D3550, Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch OD 
Modified California sampler, and ASTM D1586 by driving a 2-inch OD standard penetration 
sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS 
Our field exploration program consisted of drilling and sampling nine borings, 
designated as B-1 through B-9, at selected locations along Laukahi Street. The borings generally 
extended to depths of about 1 to 5 feet below the existing pavement surface. In addition, the 
boring exploration was supplemented by coring the existing pavement section to depths of 
about 1 to 2.2 feet below the existing pavement surface at nine selected locations, identified as 
C-101 through C-109.  The approximate boring and coring locations for this project are shown 
on the Site Plans, Plates 3.1 through 3.9. 
In addition, Dynamic Cone Penetrometer testing was conducted in B-1 through B-4 and 
B-6 through B-9 to estimate the CBR value of the subgrade soils.  This instrument is typically 
used to assess material properties of the pavement subgrade soils. The 17.6-pound DCP can be 
used to estimate the strength characteristics of fine and coarse-grained soils, granular 
construction materials and weak stabilized or modified materials. The 17.6-lb DCP cannot be 
used in highly stabilized or cemented materials or for granular materials containing a large 
percentage of aggregates greater than 2 inches.  The results of the DCP testing are shown on 
the DCP logs in Appendix C.

The pavement structure at Laukahi Street consisted of about 3 to 8 inches of asphaltic 
concrete (AC) underlain by base/subbase material about 3 to 21 inches thick.   We did not 
encounter base/subbase material in B-1.  In general, the base/subbase material below the AC 
encountered in the borings/corings between B-1 and B-8 consists of medium dense to dense 
sandy coralline gravel.  The base/subbase material encountered below the AC in the 
borings/corings between C-108 and C-109 consists of medium dense to very dense silty basaltic 
gravel.  Below the base/subbase material, residual soils and highly weathered basalt were 
encountered to the maximum depths explored.  The residual soils generally consist of dense 
silty gravel/sand and stiff silty clay.   
We did not encounter groundwater in our shallow borings (maximum depth of 5 feet 
below the pavement surface) at the time of our field exploration. However, it should be noted 
that groundwater levels are expected to fluctuate depending on seasonal rainfall, time of year, 
surface runoff, and other factors 
Results of the Dynamic Cone Pentrometer Tests are presented in Appendix C. 
Photographs of the asphaltic concrete cores are provided in Appendix D.  
LABORATORY TESTING 
Laboratory testing was performed on selected soil samples obtained during the field 
exploration as an aid in classifying the materials and evaluating their engineering properties. 
The tests performed are described below. 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations were 
performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties. 
The test results are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil samples to 
evaluate the liquid and plastic limits and to aid in soil classification. The test results are 
summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. Graphic presentation of 
the test results is provided on Plate B-1.

Ten Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & C136) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the near-surface soils and to aid in soil classification. 
Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plates B-2 and B-3. 
Eight laboratory California Bearing Ratio tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
selected bulk soil samples to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of the soils. The 
test results are presented on Plates B-4 and B-11. 
LIMITATIONS 
The geotechnical field and laboratory test data submitted in this geotechnical data 
report are based upon information obtained from the field exploration points.  The field 
exploration locations are approximate, having been estimated by pacing and measuring from 
visible features shown on the Topographic Survey Map transmitted by Austin Tsutsumi & 
Associates, Inc. on October 20, 2015. Elevations of the field data points were interpolated from 
the spot elevations shown on the same map. The boring and coring locations and elevations 
should be considered accurate only to the degree implied by the methods used. 
The stratification lines shown on the graphic representations of the borings depict the 
approximate boundaries between soil and/or rock types and, as such, may denote a gradual 
transition. Water level data presented in the text herein were based on measurements 
recorded in the borings at the times shown on the graphic representations of the borings. It 
must be noted that fluctuation may occur due to variation in tide, rainfall, temperature, and 
other factors.
