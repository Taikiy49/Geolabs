SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
Our field exploration generally encountered about 2 to 4 inches of AC over variable 
base course thicknesses underlain by alluvial soils consisting of stiff to hard silty clays and 
clayey silts extending to the maximum depth explored of about 3.5 feet below the existing 
pavement surface. It should be noted that beach deposits consisting of medium dense sands 
were encountered in the borings drilled at the DT Fleming Park (B-101 through B-103) and 
Honokowai Beach Park (B-203). We did not encounter groundwater in the shallow drilled 
borings at the time of our field exploration. 
In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement areas 
would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing pavements should be 
performed. Existing pavements showing minor distresses and severity are generally 
rehabilitated with cold-planing and an AC overlay. Reconstruction of the existing pavements is 
usually performed for those areas where the observed distresses are likely caused by failures 
in the underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials. 
Based on our pavement condition survey, we recommend rehabilitating the parking lot 
pavements with minor distresses, consisting mainly of fatigue cracking, with an AC overlay 
consisting of cold-planing 2 inches of the existing AC surface and replacing with 2 inches of 
new AC overlay.  For parking lot pavements exhibiting moderate to severe distresses that are 
likely caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials, we 
recommend reconstructing these failed pavement areas with a new pavement structural 
section consisting of 2 inches of AC on 10 inches of aggregate base course. New pavement 
sections are recommended for DT Flemming Park, Hanakao’o Park, and Lahaina Civic Center 
(Upper Parking Lot). 
We recommend proof-rolling the bottom of the new pavement structural section with a 
minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of 6 passes or recompacting to a minimum of 
95 percent relative compaction.  A layer of reinforcing geogrid (such as Tensar TriAx Grid, TX7 
or equivalent) on a layer of permeable separator (Mirafi 180N or equivalent) should be 
provided below the new pavement structural section.  The reinforcing geogrid would serve to 
enhance the R-value of the subgrade materials by providing stabilization and reinforcement to 
the underlying base/subbase/subgrade materials. 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the base, subbase, and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement surface 
should be sloped, and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry surface water off the 
pavement to appropriate drainage structures.

recommendations presented herein are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this

SECTION 2.  SITE CHARACTERIZATION 
2.1 
Regional Geology 
The Island of Maui is comprised of two major volcanoes, the older Tertiary Epoch 
(West Maui) and the more recent Pleistocene Epoch (East Maui), also known as Haleakala 
(Pleistocene Epoch).  The Isthmus of Maui is a narrow, gently sloping plain located 
between these two volcanoes.  The Isthmus of Maui was created by lava flows from 
Haleakala Volcano ponding against West Maui Volcano.  The gently sloping plains were 
subsequently filled with alluvium washed down from the slopes of West Maui and East Maui 
(Haleakala) Volcanoes.  The erosional processes are dominated by the detachment of soil 
and rock masses from the mountain walls, and the soil materials are transported downslope 
toward the isthmus primarily by gravity as colluvium.  Once these materials reach the 
stream in the central portion of a valley, alluvial processes become dominant, and the 
sediments are transported and deposited as alluvium. 
The project site is located on the western flank of the West Maui Volcano, which 
forms the west side of the Island of Maui.  The West Maui Mountains have been deeply 
incised by erosion depositing large quantities of unconsolidated recent sediments along the 
coastal regions. The basalt formations underlying the project site are part of the Wailuku 
Volcanic Series, which consist of a thick series of thinly-bedded a’a and pahoehoe lava 
flows dipping seaward at angles of about 10 to 20 degrees. These lava flows are known to 
be highly permeable and can readily dispose of surface water runoff in dry well 
applications.

2.2.1 DT Fleming Park 
The existing DT Fleming Park parking lot within the project limits generally consists of a 
two-way drive aisle between the parking stall areas.  The approximate limits of the DT 
Fleming parking lot under consideration for this project are shown on the Site Plan, 
Plate 2.1. 
The shoulder areas beyond the edge of pavement generally consist of short cut grass, 
trees, walkways, signs and divider rail and posts. The surface soils along the shoulder 
areas below the vegetation generally consist of brown sandy silt to tan coralline sand 
with silt. The existing parking lot alignment generally slopes down from west to east.  
Based on our site reconnaissance, the existing pavements at the parking stalls along 
the south side of the parking lot appear to be in fair condition with few minor signs of 
developing structural defects. Pavement deterioration is restricted to light alligator 
cracking of low to moderate severity. The ride characteristic of the pavement was 
generally fair.

with moderately to highly distressed areas. Alligator and alligator block cracking were 
observed and found to be generally moderate to high in severity. The poor areas 
consist of elongated, severely distressed zones of alligator block cracking with a few 
depressions of up to about 3 inches were observed in the northeastern and central 
portions of the parking stalls. However, no rutting of the pavements within the drive 
aisle was observed. Longitudinal cracking along paving joints were also observed with 
gaps varying from about 0.5 to 1.5 inches. Open potholes and patched potholes with

In general, the type and severity of the distresses exhibited in the pavement areas 
would determine whether rehabilitation or reconstruction of the existing pavements should be 
performed. Existing pavements showing minor surface distresses are generally rehabilitated 
with cold-planing and an AC overlay. Reconstruction of the existing pavements is usually 
performed for those areas where the observed distresses are likely caused by failures in the 
underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials. 
Based on our pavement condition survey, we recommend rehabilitating the parking lot 
pavements with minor distresses, consisting mainly of fatigue cracking, with an AC overlay 
consisting of cold-planing 2 inches of the existing AC surface and replacing it with 2 inches of 
new AC overlay.  For parking lot pavements exhibiting moderate to severe distresses that are 
likely caused by failures in the underlying base, subbase, and/or subgrade materials, we 
recommend reconstructing these failed pavement areas with a new pavement structural 
section consisting of 2 inches of AC on 10 inches of aggregate base course.

3.1 
Subgrade Strength Characterization  
Dynamic Cone Penetration (DCP) tests were performed in all nine parking lot 
locations in general accordance with ASTM D6951 to obtain indicative subsoil strength 
characteristics such as bearing ratio and bearing value. The tests were generally performed 
through the subgrade between depths of about 2 and 5.5 feet below the existing pavement 
surface. 
The Dynamic Cone Penetrometer device consists of two 16mm-diameter stainless 
steel rods with the lower rod containing an anvil, a replaceable 60o pointed tip and depth 
markings. The upper rod holds a 17.6-pound drop hammer with a 22.6 inch drop distance. 
At the beginning of a test, the cone is “seated” by dropping the hammer until the widest part 
of the cone is just below the testing surface at which point the initial penetration is recorded 
as 0. The operator then lifts and drops the hammer one or more times depending on the 
strength of the soil at the test location. Following each sequence of hammer drops, a 
penetration reading is taken. The process is continued until the desired depth of testing is 
reached or the full length of the lower rod is buried. 
Data from our DCP testing is presented as the penetration index (PI) which is the 
penetration distance of the cone for each drop of the hammer expressed in millimeters per 
blow. The penetration indices obtained from our tests were correlated with common 
pavement design parameters such as California Bearing Ratio (CBR) and bearing value. 
The penetration indices are presented on the DCP field logs shown in Appendix B.

Recompacted Existing Base/Subbase/Subgrade 
The existing base/subbase/subgrade soils under the new pavement structural section 
should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches, moisture-conditioned to near the optimum 
moisture, and proof-rolled with a minimum 10-ton vibratory drum roller a minimum of six (6) 
passes or recompacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction.  Relative compaction 
refers to the in-place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry 
density of the same soil established in accordance with AASHTO T180 or ASTM D1557. 
Optimum moisture is the water content (percentage by dry weight) corresponding to the 
maximum dry density. 
A Geolabs representative should monitor the pavement subgrade preparation to 
observe whether undesirable materials are encountered during the excavation and 
scarification process and to confirm whether the exposed soil conditions are similar to those 
encountered in our field exploration.  CBR tests and/or field observations should be 
performed on the actual subgrade soils during construction to confirm that the above 
design section is adequate.

3.4 
Surface Drainage 
One of the primary distress mechanisms in pavement structures is pumping due to 
saturation of the subbase and base course and/or subgrade soils. Therefore, the pavement 
surface should be sloped to drain and drainage gradients should be maintained to carry 
surface water off the pavement to appropriate drainage structures. Surface water ponding 
should not be allowed during or after construction. Development of good shoulder drainage 
along with a program to prevent obstructions in the drainage structures is strongly 
recommended to reduce the potential for pavement deterioration or premature failure of the 
pavements.

Test Boring Exploration 
The subsurface conditions at the parking lot sites were explored by drilling and 
sampling a total of twenty-seven borings to depths ranging from about 2.5 feet to 3.5 feet 
within the existing pavement surface. The approximate locations of the borings drilled are 
shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 2.9. The borings were drilled using a 
truck-mounted drill rig equipped with continuous-flight solid-stem augers.. 
Our geologist classified the materials encountered in the borings by visual and textural 
examination in the field in general accordance with ASTM D2488, Standard Practice for 
Description and Identification of Soils, and monitored the drilling operations on a 
near-continuous (full-time) basis. These classifications were further reviewed visually and by 
testing in the laboratory. Soils were classified in general accordance with ASTM D2487, 
Standard Practice for Classification of Soils for Engineering Purposes (Unified Soil 
Classification System), as shown on the Soil Log Legend, Plate A-0.1. Deviations made to the 
soil classification in accordance with ASTM D2487 are described in the Soil Classification Log 
Key, Plate A-0.2. Graphic representations of the materials encountered are presented on the 
Logs of Borings, Plates A-1 through A-27. 
Relatively “undisturbed” soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general 
accordance with ASTM D3550, Ring-Lined Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 3-inch 
OD Modified California sampler with a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches. In addition, some 
samples were obtained from the borings drilled in general accordance with ASTM D1586, 
Penetration Test and Split-Barrel Sampling of Soils, by driving a 2-inch OD standard 
penetration sampler using the same hammer and drop. The blow counts needed to drive the 
sampler the second and third 6 inches of an 18-inch drive are shown as the “Penetration 
Resistance” on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths. The penetration 
resistance shown on the logs of borings indicates the number of blows required for the specific 
sampler type used. The blow counts may need to be factored to obtain the Standard 
Penetration Test (SPT) blow counts. 
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples in the 
field.  The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the unconfined compressive 
strength of the soil sample.  Pocket penetrometer test results are summarized on the Logs 
of Borings at the appropriate sample depths.

A P P E N D I X   B 
Dynamic Cone Penetration Tests 
We explored the subsurface conditions at the parking lots by performing Dynamic 
Cone Penetration (DCP) tests at 27 boring locations (3 locations per parking lot), extending 
to depths of about 2.8 to 5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface.  The approximate 
locations where DCP tests were performed are shown on the Site Plans, Plates 2.1 through 
2.9.  
The test locations were augered down to a depth of 2 feet below the existing 
pavement surface.  DCP tests were then performed in general accordance with 
ASTM D6951, Standard Test Method for Use of the Dynamic Cone Penetrometer in 
Shallow Pavement Applications.  The DCP apparatus consists of a Salem Dual Mass 
Dynamic Cone Penetrometer and the tests were conducted to a maximum depth of about 
5.5 feet below the existing pavement surface. Results are shown on the Logs of Dynamic 
Cone Penetration Tests, Plates B-1 through B-27.

Laboratory Tests 
Moisture Content (ASTM D2216) and Unit Weight (ASTM D2937) determinations 
were performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil 
properties.  The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the 
appropriate sample depths. 
Four Atterberg Limits tests (ASTM D4318) were performed on selected soil samples 
to evaluate the liquid and plastic limits.  The test results are summarized on the Logs of 
Borings at the appropriate sample depths.  Graphic presentations of the test results are 
provided on Plate C-1. 
Three Sieve Analysis tests (ASTM C117 & ASTM C136) were performed on selected 
soil samples to evaluate the gradation characteristics of the soils and to aid in soil 
classification.  Graphic presentation of the grain size distribution is provided on Plate C-2. 
Nine laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests (ASTM D1883) were performed on 
bulk samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the pavement support characteristics of 
the soils.  The test results are presented on Plates C-3 through C-11. 
Six Modified Proctor Compaction tests (ASTM D1557) were performed on bulk 
samples of the near-surface soils to evaluate the dry density and moisture content 
relationships.  The test results are presented on Plates C-12 through C-17.
