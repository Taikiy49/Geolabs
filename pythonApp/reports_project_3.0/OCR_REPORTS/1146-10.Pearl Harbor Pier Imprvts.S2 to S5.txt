INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of a supplementary soils
investigation performed at the proposed Piers S2 S5 improvement
project sites U S Navy Project No P 084 at Pearl Harbor Submarine
Base Pearl Harbor Oahu Hawaii The general area of the project
sites is shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1 These sites are
shown in greater detail on the Site Plan Plate 2
PROJECT CONSIDERATIONS
It is our understanding that the proposed project will consist
of installing new sheetpile bulkhead dredging harbor bottom and
extending Piers S4 and S5 in addition to demolishing Piers 2 and 3
and installing an addition to electric substation J 15
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1146 10 Page 2
It is further understood that the harbor bottom will be dredged
to a depth of 35 feet below Mean Lower Low Water or Elevation 65 U S
Navy Datum MLLW 100 feet U S Navy Datum all elevations
mentioned in this report will be referred to this datum plane and to
a line 25 feet in front of the quay wall
The existing sheetpile behind the quay wall fronting Building 650
will be replaced with new sheetpile bulkhead tied back with anchors
In addition Piers S4 and S5 will be extended another 25 feet and
two new dolphins will be installed at the end of the pier extension
This supplementary soils report provides additional design
recommendations for the above items which were not included in our
soils investigation report
SCOPE OF SERVICES
The purpose of this investigation is to explore the subsurface
conditions and to provide recommendations for the harbor dredging new
tieback sheetpile bulkhead and pier extension constructions The
scope of our investigation consisted of the following

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
Geology
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a
complex series of deposition and erosional events brought on by glacio
eustatic sea level changes and post erosional volcanic activities
during the Pleistocene Following the cessation of activity of the
Koolau Volcano alluvial materials were deposited in a deltaic or
estuarine environment in the area of Pearl Harbor
The sea level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 300 feet
below and rising about 95 feet above the present sea level
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 1146 10 Page 5
Concurrent with a rise in sea level during late Pleistocene era
eruption of the Salt Lake Makalapa member of the Honolulu Volcanic
Series mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash
which consolidated to form volcanic tuff locally called mudrock
layers Subsequent erosion dissected and thinned those mantles of
tuff
During the Recent epoch as sea level rose to its current stand
the area was covered with soft and loose lagoonal marine deposits
Those deposits were subsequently covered by man made fill placed
during the middle portion of this century for land reclamation pro
jects during the construction of the new existing Naval Submarine Base
Sheetpile Bulkhead Site
The subsurface conditions in the new sheetpile bulkhead area were
explored by drilling 6 borings to depths between 30 0 and 65 0 feet
below the existing ground surface In general the conditions encoun
tered consisted of a surface layer of dense granular fill 4 to 8 feet
thick underlain by loose lagoonal deposit of coralline sand and
gravel layer approximately 5 to 15 feet thick Below the sand and
gravel deposit was stiff alluvial silt and clay which were underlain
by a thick stratum of competent tuff except at the northern end of
the site where the tuff thinned to approximately 5 feet in thickness
Underlying the tuff stratum was a layer of stiff alluvial silty clay
which was in turn underlain by another older layer of tuff

Pier Extension Site
Based on the results of our three off shore borings the
subsurface conditions at the proposed pier extension areas consisted
of very thick harbor sediment very soft dark gray organic silty clay
occurring at about Elevation 64 feet This sediment was a little
over 70 feet thick and extended to depths of Elevation 7 to 9 feet
The consistency of the sediment increased slightly from very soft to
soft at about Elevation 45 feet It graded to medium stiff at about
Elevation 5 feet
The soft harbor sediment was underlain by relatively thick
alluvial deposits to Elevation 55 8 feet the maximum depth
explored The alluvial deposits consisted mainly of very stiff to
hard brown clayey silts and silty clays with pockets of gravels
cobbles and boulders

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Dredging
For the proposed dredging an attempt was made to evaluate its
effect to the existing quay wall piling foundations An effort was
made to locate previous driving records for the quay wall piling
between Piers S2 and S4 However we are unable to locate the as
built pile records and it is our belief that these old records may
not likely be available Therefore we were unable to fully evaluate
the dredging effect on the quay wall piling foundation
Judging from the pile driving records for the period from 1931
through 1933 for other piling work in the vicinity the quay wall
piles were probably of 16 inch square reinforced concrete piles and
most likely driven by a hammer with a rated energy of lbs
5000 1b ram with 3 feet stroke It is estimated that these piles
carry dead loads of up to about 20 tons on the front row and 35 tons
for the back row
On the Navy Drawing No Z N15 112 dated only
the estimated pile lengths for construction were shown The lengths
indicated were 35 feet for the front row piling and 30 feet for the
back row piling Assuming that the subsurface conditions under the
quay wall were as depicted on Plates 4 through 6 we believe that
previous piles were unable to penetrate any Significant depth into the
volcanic tuff most probably they terminated on top of the tuff
GEOLABS HAWAII
although some might have penetrated a few feet into the tuff Thus
we believe that the actual pile lengths should have been about 20
feet instead of 30 to 35 feet as estimated and that the underlying
volcanic tuff layer is essentially intact
According to various Navy drawings the area between Piers S3 and
S4 had been dredged several times excavating up to 25 feet below the
1922 harbor bottom near the existing quay wall The steepest dredged
11 as shown on Section BB Plate 4 It appears that this slope has
changed very little since the 1970 sounding and no known instability
has been reported
Assuming that the quay wall pilings terminated on top of the
volcanic tuff and that the tuff layer remains intact we believe that
the proposed dredging to a Elevation 65 feet up to a horizontal
distance of 25 feet in front of the quay wall as depicted in Plate 7
may be made For the piles terminating on top of the tuff layer we
believe that a lateral confinement of at least 7 feet should be
provided Therefore to provide such a confinement we recommend that
the slope should not be excavated below a plane rising at 2
H V from the control point at Elevation 65 feet at 25 feet in front
of the wall This dredging limitation is also presented on Plate 7
Although the volcanic tuff is relatively thin in the Boring 10 area
we believe that the very stiff clay and silt underlying the tuff can
provide adequate support for the quay wall after the proposed dredging

Sheetpile Design Pressures
For the design of the bulkhead sheetpiles we recommend that the
pressure diagram presented on Plate 8 be used These pressures
include a surcharge load of 50 p s f on the ground surface
As the existing quay wall appears to offer little lateral
Support its contribution is ignored in the design pressure diagram
For tieback force design we recommend that the bottom of
sheetpile be assumed to be hinged at Elevation 85 feet
The above design parameters are developed based on conditions at
Boring 9 where very loose silty gravels were encountered Conditions
at Borings 10 and 11 are also analyzed and the results are found to be
less critical

Sheetpile Bulkhead Construction
During our boring exploration numerous utilities were
identified As a result the upper five feet was hand excavated prior
to drilling and 11 to avoid damaging the underground
utility lines
Toning for borings on the landside of Building 650 also disclosed
numerous underground utility lines These conditions will not only
increase the cost of future sheetpile and tieback anchor installations
but will also reduce the allowable lateral bearing on deadman anchors
if used
Based on our findings from the borings we believe that the new
sheetpiles can be terminated after being driven 3 to 4 feet into the
upper volcanic tuff layer It is not necessary to drive the sheetpile
below the dredge bottom because the moderately hard volcanic tuff
layer should be stable and would provide an adequate support for the
sheetpile and the quay wall after dredging

Recommendations for sheetpile installation criteria should be
developed after the actual selections for sheetpile and driving hammer
are made
From experience we believe that driving the lbs per
sq ft Z shaped sheetpiles by a Mckiernan Terry Model 9B3 rated
energy at lbs to practical refusal would be adequate
Refusal is defined as not more than one quarter of an inch of
sheetpile penetration over a period of 60 seconds of driving at the
maximum rated hammer energy
Tieback System
Two types of tieback anchor systems are reviewed These consist
of inclined tieback rods anchored in the underlying volcanic tuff
and horizontal tieback rods with deadman anchors on the landside of
Building 650
In view of the difficulty to install horizontal tie rods through
conjested utility systems and the existing buildings inclined rock
anchor tieback scheme will be more cost effective than the horizontal
tieback with deadman anchors Nevertheless the design criteria for
both schemes are presented below

The inclined tieback rock anchors are schematically shown
on Plate 10 We believe that the allowable bond between
the cement grout and the volcanic tuff should not exceed 50
p S i The maximum anchor force should be limited to 85
kips per rod The effective horizontal component for a tie
rod inclined at 45 degrees below horizontal line will then
be about 60 kips
The installation of the tieback anchor generally should
consist of the following
a Drill a pilot hole and install a 4 inch diameter
casing such as a PVC pipe at least 10 inches into the
volcanic tuff Such casing length will probably vary
from 20 to 30 feet
b Place a smaller inch minimum NX size casing
through the large outer casing and set the smaller NX
casing at least 8 inches further into the tuff Then
drill a inch minimum diameter open hole at least
another 17 feet into volcanic tuff for an 85 kip rock
anchor force

c Clean the NX size open hole in the anchor zone then
insert anchor rod of suitable size and grout the lower
17 foot anchor zone
d After the grout has gained strength proof tension the
anchor bolt to 125 of the working load
e Release the proof load and grout the remainder of the
hole The larger outer casing may consist of PVC pipe
to be left in place with the grout to provide further
corrosion protection
The inclined tieback anchor rods should be carefully laid
out to avoid damage to the existing foundation piles of
Building 650
There will be a vertical component from the anchor force
acting on the sheetpiles Unless this added anchor load is
distributed by a stiff waler this load may be concentrated
onto the particular sheetpile at the tieback location
Generally we recommend that the vertical component be
distributed over at least three sheetpiles As mentioned
earlier all sheetpiles should be driven into the volcanic
tuff to develop adequate end bearing support

B Deadman Anchor System
This scheme will use buried deadman anchors on the landside
of Building 650 and thread tie rods through the building
foundations Because of the conjested underground
structures and utility lines this scheme will be more
difficult Except for the above considerations the
deadman anchor system design is very simple
For deadman design our recommended design pressure diagram
is shown on Plate 11 We believe that the face of the
deadman should be at least 20 feet away from the Building
650 foundations to minimize interference with the
building Generally this horizontal distance will place
the passive earth pressure wedge of the deadman anchor
outside the active wedge zone of the sheetpile
We recommend that the top of the deadman be embedded at
least 2 feet below the street grade Some provisions
should be made to limit any future trenching operation in
front of the deadman anchor to avoid loss of passive
resisting earth pressures

It is anticipated that the bottom of the deadman anchor
excavation will be below the prevailing groundwater level and
that dewatering may be necessary In addition any soft or loose
soil encountered at the bottom of the excavations should be
removed to a minimum depth of 18 inches and the over excavation
backfilled with coarse crushed rock such as No 3B Coarse
material
Other Considerations
Wave Protection
After the dredging in front of the quay walls the loose
sand and gravel material underneath the quay walls may be
eroded and the existing as well as the new sheetpiles may
be exposed Therefore provisions to protect the sheetpile
against wave action may be needed
Seal Under the Quay Wall and Sheetpiles
There will be a structural discontinuity between the
existing quay wall and the new sheetpile This may lead to
loss of fill through the widening of gaps or cracks We
believe that some protective measures such as filling the
discontinuity with lean concrete should be provided

Pier Extension
It is our understanding that the existing Piers S4 and S5 will be
extended 25 feet Our borings drilled in this extension area did not
disclose any readily identifiable end bearing zones Therefore
friction piles are considered We recommend piles be driven into the
very stiff silt and clay with pile tips at elevations ranging from 35
to 40 feet U S Navy Datum Since long piles will be required for
the proposed pier extension we recommend that inch octagonal
prestressed concrete piles be used For such a pile driven to
Elevation 35 feet a design pile capacity of 50 tons per pile dead
plus live loads may be used
These piles should be driven with a hammer delivering at least
of energy per blow Piles may be stopped when
encountering a penetration resistance of 45 blows per foot after
penetrating below Elevation 20 feet or reaching Elevation 45
whichever is higher The penetration resistance criteria should be
modified according to the actual hammer used
GEOLABS HAWAII
In general splicing in the very soft silty clay harbor sediment
should be avoided if possible because of the following reasons
1 The lower pile section may sink off during splicing This
will make splicing of batter piles particularly difficult
2 The lower pile section may accidentally slip off if heavy
hammer blows are applied during driving subsequent to
splicing
3 A conventional sleeved splice joint will not transfer bending
or tension stresses
However because of practical limit of transporting very long
precast prestressed concrete piles we believe that the conventional
splicing sleeves can be adapted provided that a careful construction
procedure be followed In addition bolts may be included in the
splice sleeves to prevent the lower pile section to slide out of the
splice during driving
We recommend that the lower pile sections be casted at 70 feet
long and that the upper pile sections be casted at 75 to 85 feet
GEOLABS HAWAIi
W O 1146 10 Page 19
The shorter lower section is an attempt to achieve on one hand a
deep embedment of splice joint below the mudline and on the other
hand to provide a sufficient length of the lower pile section in the
very soft mud to remain temporarily stable for splicing However a
special securing device may be necessary to hold the lower batter pile
section for splicing One consideration could be to hang the butt end
of the batter pile on vertical piles during splicing
We believe that no test pile program is necessary However we
recommend that the pile installation be observed by the soils engineer
who should assess the actual driving conditions and make modifications
as deemed necessary
Dolphin Piles and Fender Piles
For the new dolphin fender piles we believe that wooden piles
will still be suitable The length of the dolphin piles should be
such that the tip of the piles will be at Elevation 10 feet if
moderate amount of long term axial load on the piles are required If
no long term load is applied on these piles we suggest that dolphin
piles be embedded about 50 feet below the mudline Thus tip
elevations will be about Elevation 15 feet with pile length about
100 feet for batter piles
The length of fender piles should be about 75 feet having an
embedment of about 30 feet below the mudline with tip at about
Elevation 35 feet

All wooden piles should be driven with a lighter hammer and we
recommend that a hammer with a rated energy of 1lbs per
blow be used

Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the new sheetpile bulkhead
site were explored by drilling 6 land borings and those at the
pier extension site were explored by drilling 3 off shore
borings at the approximate locations shown on the Site Plan
Plate 2 A truck mounted drilling rig was used to drill the
first land through 10 and a trailer
mounted rig was used on the last of the land borings The
trailer mounted rig was later placed on a raft and was used to
drill the three off shore borings
The soils encountered in the borings were classified by
visual and textural examination in the field by our engineer
who continuously monitored the drilling operations The classi
fications were further reviewed by visual inspection and testing
in the laboratory The soils were classified in general confor
mance with the Unified Soil Classification System A graphic
presentation of the materials encountered is presented on the
Boring Logs Plates A 1l through A 9 3
In the borings soil samples were obtained by driving a
barrel sampler with a 140 pound hammer falling 30 inches In
tube sampler were used The blow counts used to drive the
sampler the 12 inches are shown on the log of borings at the
appropriate sample depths The rock core samples were obtained
by using double tube NWX size coring barrel

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
em ee eae
Moisture content and unit weight determinations were
performed on selected samples as an aid in the classification
and evaluation of soil properties The results of these tests
are presented on the boring logs at the appropriate sample
depths
To further identify soil characteristics additional
tests were performed These include 5 Atterberg Limit tests
and 2 Sieve Analyses The results of these tests are presented
on Plates B 1 through B 4
Three direct shear tests were performed on undisturbed
samples to evaluate the strength characteristics of near surface
granular fill materials on the sheetpile bulkhead site The test
results are presented on Plates B 5 through B 7
Four Unconsolidated Undrained Triaxial Compression Tests
were performed on select undisturbed samples to evaluate the soil
strength characteristics of the soil All triaxial compression
tests were performed using a multi stage procedure in which the
next higher confining pressure was applied after the sample
approached the peak compressive strength under the on going
confining pressure
Five Unconfined Compression Tests were performed on
slightly disturbed and undisturbed samples to further evaluate
the soil strength characteristics The peak compressive strength
values for triaxial and unconfined compression tests are
summarized on Plate B 8
One Consolidation test was performed on selected
undisturbed sample to evaluate the settlement characteristics of
the soft sediment under the pier extension site The test
results are presented on Plate B 9
Three bond strength tests were performed on selected
volcanic tuff core samples to examine the bonding strength
between the concrete and tuff To prepare the testing specimen
concrete was casted onto a selected rock core samples as shown on
Plate B 10 The samples were then cured for 7 days in water and
load tested for bond strength The test results are presented
also on Plate B 10
