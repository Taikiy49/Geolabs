INTRODUCTION
This report presents the results of our geotechnical engineering exploration
performed for the proposed FY99 Improve Family Housing Phase 4 project within
Hickam Air Force Base AFB in Honolulu on the island of Oahu Hawaii The general
location and vicinity of the project site are shown on the Project Location Map Plate 1
This report summarizes our findings and geotechnical engineering
recommendations resulting from our field exploration laboratory testing and engineering
analyses These recommendations are intended for the design of foundations site
preparation and pavements only The findings and recommendations presented herein
are subject to the limitations noted at the end of this report

The proposed FY99 Improve Family Housing project is located along Tinker
Avenue west of the Main Gate at Hickam AFB in Honolulu on the island of Oahu
Hawaii Based on the information provided by USArchitects Inc it is proposed to
provide improvements to 36 housing units in three areas Area A consists of 3 buildings
two of which are Type Building Nos 185 and 187 and a Type
Building No 186 Area B consists of 3 buildings of Type Building Nos 104

had no major renovations since construction The current proposed improvements will

REGIONAL GEOLOGY
The island of Oahu is composed largely of the weathered remnants of two extinct
shield volcanoes Waianae and Koolau The older volcano Waianae forms the bulk of
the western one third of the island while the younger shield volcano Koolau forms the
majority of the eastern two thirds of the island It is believed that Waianae became extinct
while Koolau was still active and its eastern flank is partially buried below Koolau lava in
central Oahu
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3920 00 Page 5
The geologic conditions underlying the site are the result of a complex series of
depositional and erosional events due to glacio eustatic sea level changes and post
erosional volcanic activities during the Pleistocene Epoch The Koolau volcanic shield
was built during the early Pleistocene Epoch by thinly bedded basaltic lava flows
Following the cessation of activity of the Koolau Volcano alluvial materials were
deposited in an estuarine and deltaic environment in the area of Pearl Harbor The sea
level fluctuated several times receding perhaps 300 feet below and rising about 95 feet
above the present sea level Concurrent with a rise in sea level during the late
Pleistocene Epoch eruption of the Salt Lake Makalapa member of the Honolulu Volcanic
Series mantled the older alluvium with thick deposits of volcanic ash which consolidated
to form volcanic tuff locally called mudrock layers Subsequent erosion dissected and
thinned those mantles of tuff
During the Recent Epoch as the sea level rose to its current stand the area was
covered with additional sediments consisting primarily of soft and loose lagoonal marine
deposits Land development and reclamation projects in the past 40 to 60 years have
added man made fills bringing the site to its present configuration

SUBSURFACE CONDITIONS
The subsurface conditions at the project site were explored by drilling and
sampling three borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 extending to a depth of
about 20 feet below the existing ground surface Three bulk samples designated as
Bulk 1 through Bulk 3 of the near surface soils were obtained to evaluate their physical
and pavement support characteristics The approximate locations of the borings and
near surface bulk samples are shown on the Site Plans Plates 2 and 3
In general the majority of our borings throughout the project site encountered
surficial fills overlying volcanic tuff In Boring Nos 1 and 2 the fills consisted of a surficial
layer approximately 1 5 feet thick of stiff to very stiff clayey silts and silty clays overlying
very dense gravelly sand extending to a depth of approximately 4 feet below the existing
ground surface Underlying the fills a layer of very stiff severely weathered tuff
approximately 1 5 feet thick was encountered grading to hard volcanic tuff to the
maximum depth explored of 20 feet below the existing ground surface
In Boring No 3 the surficial fill layer consisted of stiff silty clay to a depth of
approximately 1 5 feet below the existing ground surface Underlying the fill hard
volcanic tuff was encountered to the maximum depth explored of 20 feet below the
existing ground surface
Groundwater was encountered in one of the three drilled borings at the time of our
field exploration In Boring No 1 groundwater was measured at about 14 feet below the
existing ground surface Due to the proximity of the project site to the shoreline it is likely
that the groundwater levels measured in the borings during our field exploration will be
GEOLABS HAWAII

affected by tidal fluctuation In addition the water levels at the project site may be
influenced by seasonal precipitation and storm surge conditions

DISCUSSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Our field exploration disclosed that the project site is generally underlain by stiff to
very stiff silty clays overlying very dense sandy gravels and hard volcanic tuff Our
laboratory tests indicated that the near surface clayey soils generally exhibit moderate to
high shrink swell expansion when subjected to fluctuations in the soil moisture contents
Based on the generally stiff to very stiff subsoil conditions encountered at the project site
we envision that shallow spread and or continuous footings may be used to support the
proposed new additions anticipated for the subject project
However in order to reduce the potential for structural distress to the new slabs
resulting from swelling of the on site expansive soils we recommend that the
near surface soils be capped with a minimum of 18 inches of non expansive select
granular fill material below the slab cushion In addition special attention should be given
to the recommended procedures for subgrade preparation of slabs and pavements to
reduce the potential shrink swell effects of the near surface soils Detailed discussion of
these items and our geotechnical engineering design recommendations are presented in
the following sections of this report
Foundations
Based on the subsurface conditions encountered in our exploration we believe
that a shallow foundation system consisting of spread and or continuous footings may be
used to support the proposed structures An allowable bearing pressure of up to
This allowable bearing pressure is for dead plus live loads and may be increased by
GEOLABS HAWAII

one third 1 3 for transient loads such as temporary wind and or seismic forces Spread
and continuous footings should be designed with minimum widths of 18 and 12 inches
respectively
In general footings should be embedded a minimum of 18 inches below the lowest
adjacent finished grade New footings located adjacent to the existing structures should
be lowered to the same depths as the existing building footings to avoid surcharging the
existing foundations The actual depths of the existing footings should be confirmed in
the field during construction by the project geotechnical engineer Where footings are
located adjacent to below grade structures or utilities the footings should extend to a
depth below an imaginary 45 degree plane projected upward from the bottom of the
below grade structure or utility This requirement is necessary to avoid surcharging
adjacent below grade structures with additional structural loads and to reduce the
potential for foundation settlement We estimate that total footing settlements under the
anticipated design loads are on the order of less than 1 inch with differential settlements
of approximately inch
Lateral loads acting on the structure may be resisted by frictional resistance
developed between the bottom of the foundation and the bearing soil and by passive
earth pressure acting against the near vertical faces of the embedded portion of footings
A coefficient of friction of 0 3 may be used between the base of the footings and the
near surface soils encountered in our borings Resistance due to passive pressure may
be calculated using an equivalent fluid pressure of 300 pounds per square foot per foot of
depth p c f This assumes that the soil around the footings is well compacted Unless
covered by pavements or slabs the passive resistance in the upper 12 inches below
finished grade should be neglected
Footing excavations should not be allowed to dry before placing concrete If
shrinkage cracks appear in the footing excavations the soil should be thoroughly

moistened to close all cracks and recompacted to provide a firm base prior to concrete
placement
Footing excavations should be observed by a representative of Geolabs Hawaii

bearing materials and embedment depths If unsuitable materials are encountered in the
footing excavations these materials should be over excavated to expose firm soils and
replaced with compacted engineered fill or the footing bottom should be extended down
to underlying competent material
Slab On Grade
As previously indicated the project site is generally underlain by moderately to
highly expansive clayey soils Unless slabs on grade supported by these expansive soils
are properly designed there is a potential for future distress to the residential structures
resulting from wetting and subsequent swelling of the expansive clayey soils
To reduce the potential for structural distress resulting from swelling of the on site
expansive soils we recommend that near surface expansive soils be capped with
non expansive select granular fill material a minimum depth of 18 inches below the slab
cushion The non expansive select granular fill should extend laterally at least 2 feet
beyond the perimeter of the proposed structures and pavement areas The upper
6 inches of capping fill outside the limits of the structures and pavement areas should
consist of non expansive impervious material to reduce water infiltration into the
underlying select granular fill material as shown graphically on Plate 4
Prior to placement of the non expansive select granular fill the clayey subgrades
should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches moisture conditioned to at least
2 percent above the optimum moisture and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent
relative compaction The non expansive select granular fill material should provide a
capping layer of moist soil above the underlying potentially expansive subgrade soil
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3920 00 Page 10
thereby reducing the loss of moisture from the clayey subgrade soils In our opinion the
reduction of drying and subsequent wetting of the subgrade soils should reduce the
impact of shrink swell effects on the slab caused by potential heaving of the underlying
expansive clayey soils when wetted The non expansive select granular fill should be
moisture conditioned placed in level loose lifts not exceeding 8 inches and compacted to
not less than 90 percent relative compaction
For more uniform support interior slabs should be supported on a minimum 4 inch
thick layer of cushion fill consisting of No 3B Fine gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation
To reduce moisture infiltration a plastic vapor barrier should be placed above the cushion
fill A 2 inch thick moist fine sand layer may be considered above the plastic membrane
to aid in the curing of the concrete The thickened edges of slabs adjacent to any
unpaved area should be embedded at least 12 inches below the lowest adjacent grade
Flexible floor coverings such as carpet or sheet vinyl should be considered since
they can better mask minor slab cracking It is also recommended that interior walls be
designed to incorporate some flexibility to accommodate some amount of possible ground
movements
Where the slabs will be subjected to vehicular traffic such as the carport slabs a
6 inch layer of base course is recommended in lieu of the 4 inch cushion fill layer vapor
barrier and 2 inch sand layer The base course should consist of crushed basalt
aggregate compacted to a minimum of 95 percent relative compaction The 18 inch
non expansive select granular fill layer recommended for slab on grade construction
should also be provided for the carport slabs
Exterior concrete walkways may be required for the proposed project We
recommend that a minimum 4 inch cushion layer consisting of base course be provided
below the concrete slab compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction
GEOLABS HAWAI
W O 3920 00 Page 11
Contraction joints should be provided at intervals equal to the width of the walkways with
expansion joints at right angle intersections
Areas adjacent to slabs should be backfilled tightly against the slab and graded to
divert water away from the slab to reduce the potential for water ponding around the
foundations
Site Preparation
Based on the existing topography and the proposed finish grades minimal site
grading work will be required for the construction of new improvements at the subject site
In general the area within the contract grading limits should be thoroughly cleared and
grubbed at the on set of earthwork All vegetation debris rubbish and other unsuitable
materials should be removed and disposed of off site
Slabs and foundations of the existing structures that are to be demolished should
be completely removed Any over excavation resulting from the demolition should be
backfilled with compacted select granular fill material Existing utilities to be abandoned
should be removed and the resulting excavation should be properly backfilled with select
granular fill material moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture placed in
8 inch loose lifts and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities
that are to be abandoned in place under the proposed structures should be backfilled by
pumping lean concrete under low pressure
After demolition clearing and grubbing all areas beneath future new slabs should
be over excavated to provide at least 18 inches of compacted non expansive select
granular fill material below the bottom of the slab cushion If zones of soft loose or
Saturated soils are encountered at the over excavated subgrade level further
over excavation may be required to expose the underlying firm material The resulting
over excavation should be backfilled with compacted select granular fill material
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3920 00 Page 12
The over excavated subgrade surface should be scarified to a minimum depth of
8 inches moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content
and compacted to not less than 90 percent relative compaction Relative compaction
refers to the in place dry density of soil expressed as a percentage of the maximum dry
density of the same soil established in accordance with ASTM D 1557 91 test
procedures Optimum moisture is the water content percentage by dry weight
corresponding to the maximum dry density
Slab subgrades should be kept moist and free of shrinkage cracks until covered by
the capping fill slab cushion fill or concrete Where shrinkage cracks are noted after
compaction of the subgrade we recommend that the soil be re scarified and prepared as
recommended above
Non expansive select granular fill material required for the capping fill layer under
the slab areas should consist of imported granular material such as select borrow
crushed coral or basalt gravel The material should be well graded from coarse to fine
with no particles greater than 3 inches in largest dimension The material should have a
laboratory California Bearing Ratio CBR value of 20 or higher and a swell potential of
1 percent or less when tested in accordance with ASTM Test Designation D 1883 It
should also contain between 10 and 30 percent passing the No 200 sieve
Any imported fill material should be tested and approved by Geolabs Hawaii prior to
delivery to the project site for its intended use
Pavement Design
Asphalt concrete pavements are anticipated for the new parking areas at the
subject site The anticipated subgrade soils for the proposed new pavements as
encountered in our borings generally consist of the stiff clayey soils Based on our field
exploration and laboratory testing the anticipated subgrade soils generally exhibit
moderate to high shrink swell potential when subjected to moisture fluctuations
Therefore we recommend that the subgrade soils be properly prepared and capped with
GEOLABS HAWAII
W O 3920 00 Page 13
a minimum of 6 inches of select borrow subbase as presented below to reduce the
potential for distress resulting from the swelling of the potentially expansive subgrade
soils Based on the laboratory test resuits the near surface clayey soils at the site have a
CBR value of about 12 with a swell value of about 1 percent We anticipate light to
medium traffic for the parking areas Based on the above assumptions we recommend
that the following pavement section be used for preliminary design and budgeting
purposes
Flexible Pavements
2 Inch Asphalt Concrete
6 Inch Base Course 95 Percent Relative Compaction
6 Inch Select Borrow Subbase 95 Percent Relative Compaction
14 Inch Total Pavement Thickness on Moist Compacted Subgrade
The pavement subgrade soils should be scarified to a minimum depth of 8 inches
moisture conditioned to at least 2 percent above the optimum moisture content and
compacted to not less than 95 percent relative compaction The select borrow and base
course should consist of crushed basalt aggregate compacted to a minimum of
95 percent relative compaction
Laboratory CBR tests should be performed on samples of the actual subgrade
soils encountered during construction to confirm the above recommended design section
The recommended pavement section assumes that good drainage will be provided for
areas adjacent to the pavements
Paved areas should be sloped and drainage gradients maintained to carry all
surface water off site Surface water ponding should not be allowed anywhere on the site
during or after construction Where concrete curbs are used to isolate landscaping in or
adjacent to the pavement areas we recommend the curbs extend a minimum of 2 inches
into the road subgrade level to reduce migration of landscape water into the pavement
section Alternatively a subdrain system could be constructed to collect excessive water

from landscaping irrigation For long term performance we recommend a subdrain
system be considered adjacent to paved iandscaped areas
Utility Trench
We envision that new utility lines such as sewer and water lines may be required
for the project In general a granular bedding consisting of 6 inches of No 3B Fine
gravel ASTM C 33 No 67 gradation is recommended under the pipes Free draining
granular materials such as No 3B Fine gravel should be used for the trench backfill up
to about 12 inches above the pipes to provide adequate support around the pipes and to
reduce compaction of the backfill thus reducing the potential for damaging the pipes
The upper portion of the trench backfill from the level 12 inches above the pipes to
the top of the subgrade or finished grade may consist of select granular materials The
on site clayey soils may be used as trench backfill in landscape areas only The backfill
should be moisture conditioned to above the optimum moisture content placed in
maximum 8 inch level loose lifts and mechanically compacted to not less than 90 percent
relative compaction to reduce the potential for future ground subsidence Where trenches
are below pavement areas the upper 2 feet of the trench backfill below the pavement
finish grade should be compacted to at least 95 percent relative compaction

Existing utilities to be abandoned shouid be removed and the resuiting
excavation should be properly backfilled with select granular fill material
moisture conditioned to above the optinum moisture placed in 8 inch level loose lifts
and compacted to a minimum of 90 percent relative compaction Utilities to be

Drainage
The finished grades outside the buildings should be sloped to shed water away
from foundations and to reduce the potential for ponding It is also advised that gutter
systems be installed around the buildings and that the discharge be diverted away from
the foundation and pavement areas Excessive landscape watering near the foundations
and slabs should also be avoided Planters next to foundations should be avoided or
have concrete bottoms and drains to reduce water infiltration into the subsoils
These drainage requirements are essential for the proper performance of our
foundation recommendations since ponded water could cause subsurface soil saturation
and subsequent heaving or loss of strength The foundation excavations should be
properly backfilled against the walls or slab footings immediately after setting of the
concrete to reduce water infiltration Drainage swales should be provided as soon as
possible and should be maintained to drain all surface water run off away from the slab
and foundations

APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
APPENDIX A
Field Exploration
The subsurface conditions at the site were explored by drilling and sampling three
borings designated as Boring Nos 1 through 3 to a depth of about 20 feet below the
existing ground surface using a truck mounted drill rig equipped with continuous flight
augers The approximate locations of the borings are shown on the Site Plan Plate 2
The materials encountered in the borings were classified by visual and textural
examination in the field by our Geologist who monitored the drilling operations on a
near continuous basis These classifications were further reviewed visually and by
testing in the laboratory Soils were classified in general conformance with the Unified
Soil Classification System as shown on Plate A Graphic representation of the materials
encountered are presented on the Logs of Borings Plates A 1 through A 3
Soil samples were obtained from the drilled borings in general accordance with
ASTM Test Designation D 1586 84 Penetration Test and Split Barrel Sampling of Soils
sampler with a 140 pound hammer failing 30 inches The blow counts needed to drive
the sampler the last 12 inches of an 18 inch drive are shown on the Logs of Borings at
the appropriate sample depths
Pocket penetrometer tests were performed on selected cohesive soil samples
retrieved in the field The pocket penetrometer test provides an indication of the
unconfined compressive strength of the sample Results of the pocket penetrometer
tests are summarized on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample depths

APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
APPENDIX B
Laboratory Testing
Moisture content ASTM D 2216 and unit weight determinations were performed
on selected soil samples as an aid in the classification and evaluation of soil properties
The results of these tests are presented on the Logs of Borings at the appropriate sample
depths
Two ring swell tests were performed on selected samples to evaluate the swelling
potential of the subsoils The test results are summarized on Plate B 1
Three laboratory California Bearing Ratio Tests ASTM D 1883 were performed
on bulk samples of the near surface soils to evaluate the pavement support
characteristics of the soils The test results are presented on Plates B 2 through B 4
